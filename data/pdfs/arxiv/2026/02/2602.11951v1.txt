Abstract—DNA data storage offers a high-density, long-term alternative to traditional storage systems, addressing the exponential growth of digital data. Composite DNA extends this paradigm by leveraging mixtures of nucleotides to increase storage capacity beyond the four standard bases. In this work, we model composite DNA storage as a multinomial channel and draw an analogy to digital modulation by representing composite letters on the three-dimensional probability simplex. To mitigate errors caused by sampling randomness, we derive transition probabilities and log-likelihood ratios (LLRs) for each constellation point and employ practical channel codes for error correction. We then extend this framework to substitution and insertion–deletion (ID) channels, proposing constellation update rules that account for these additional impairments. Numerical results demonstrate that our approach achieves reliable performance with existing LDPC codes, compared to the prior schemes designed for limited-magnitude probability errors, whose performance degrades significantly under sampling randomness.
Index Terms—DNA storage, composite DNA, multinomial channel, substitution, insertion-deletion, sampling randomness.
I. INTRODUCTION
The rapid growth of data generated by smartphones, smart homes, IoT devices, and other platforms is driving an exponential increase in digital information, projected to reach 384 zettabytes in 2028 [1]. As a result, the risk of a data storage crisis is rising, since traditional devices and methods cannot keep pace with this demand. To address this challenge, DNA data storage systems were introduced as early as the 1960s [2]. The core idea is to store binary data by encoding it into synthesized strands of DNA and later decoding it back. The practicality of DNA storage was demonstrated in the early 2010s through large-scale proof-of-concept experiments [3], [4], showing that it offers a high-density solution with remarkable durability [5], [6]. In such systems, data is encoded using the four DNA bases {A, C, G, T } to construct strands through DNA synthesis, which are then stored. To retrieve the stored data, a sequencing process is applied, where the strands are read and the original binary data is recovered.

Current synthesis and sequencing technologies are costly, and during the synthesis phase multiple copies of each DNA strand are inherently produced, resulting in significant redundancy. When reading (i.e., sequencing) these strands, one can exploit this redundancy by considering the multiple copies rather than treating them as duplicates to be discarded. To exploit this redundancy, composite DNA letters were introduced in [7]. In a composite letter, instead of using a single base from {A, C, G, T }, information is distributed across multiple strands as a mixture of nucleotides at specific positions, thereby extending the alphabet of standard DNA storage beyond the four bases. A composite letter is characterized by a quartet of probabilities {pA, pC , pT , pG}, representing the expected frequency of each nucleotide across the copies. Since these probabilities sum to 1, only three values are sufficient to define a point in the 3-dimensional probability simplex.
Different aspects of composite DNA storage systems have been studied in the literature. In [8], the channel impairments are modeled as limited-magnitude probability errors, and sphere packing and Gilbert-Varshamov bounds are derived, along with code constructions for this model. In [9], coding for composite DNA is explored under substitutions, strand losses, and deletions, and non-asymptotic upper bounds on the code size are obtained. In [10], the problem of selecting nucleotide mixtures that maximize decoding probability is addressed, and the expected number of reads required for reliable decoding is analyzed. For the multinomial channel, [11] proposes an optimization algorithm to determine the capacity-achieving input distribution using the Blahut-Arimoto algorithm. Extensions to combinatorial composite DNA, where shortmers replace base nucleotides, are studied in [12], which proposes encoding schemes and investigates their properties. A related work [13] considers asymmetric errors in shortmerbased systems, where some shortmers may be missing in some reads, and develops error-correcting codes for such scenarios.
In this study, we consider a DNA data storage system with composite letters to exploit the redundancies inherent in the synthesis and sequencing processes. Following the approach in [11], we first define the multinomial channel for such systems and demonstrate a natural relationship between the constellation diagram of a digital modulation scheme and

composite DNA letters, represented on the three-dimensional probability simplex. Because of the randomness in DNA sampling, the observed frequencies may deviate from the original constellation points. To address this, we propose the use of practical error correcting codes, in particular, lowdensity parity-check (LDPC) codes, and derive the posterior probabilities of each constellation point for a given channel observation under sampling randomness, thereby enabling the computation of log-likelihood ratios. We further extend the proposed approach to commonly observed error models in DNA storage systems: substitution and insertion-deletion (ID) channels, and introduce a constellation update policy to account for these errors. Overall, the proposed framework provides a practical method that leverages the increased capacity of composite DNA while enabling robust error protection with existing LDPC codes.
The paper is organized as follows. In Section II, we introduce the system model and the underlying multinomial channel for composite DNA data storage using channel coding based error protection. Section III presents the encoding and decoding structures, along with the composite letter mapping and LLR computation for the multinomial composite DNA channel. In Section IV, we extend the proposed approach to the substitution and ID channels. The performance of composite DNA data storage with different LDPC codes is provided in Section V, and the paper is concluded in Section VI.

II. SYSTEM MODEL

We consider a DNA data storage system with composite letters, as illustrated in Fig. 1. The input is a binary message sequence m = [m1 m2 . . . mK ], where each bit takes the value 0 or 1 with equal probability. This sequence is encoded using a channel code with rate R = K/N to generate the codeword c = [c1 c2 . . . cN ], which is then mapped to composite DNA letters. A composite letter is defined as a mixture of the four standard DNA nucleotides {A, C, T, G} in specified ratios at a given strand position. These composite letters exploit the redundancies inherent in DNA synthesis and sequencing, and are constructed by reading n copies of a DNA strand at a specific index. The resulting channel is referred to as a multinomial channel, whose output is a quartet of probabilities {pA, pC , pT , pG} representing the expected frequency of each nucleotide in the n copies.

III. ENCODING AND DECODING STRUCTURES FOR COMPOSITE DNA WITH CHANNEL CODING
In this section, we present the encoding structure, composite letter mapping, LLR calculation, and decoding process of the proposed coding scheme for composite DNA storage systems. The goal is to exploit the increased storage capacity of composite DNA letters while ensuring robustness against the randomness introduced by the sampling process.

A. Encoding of the Binary Input Sequence
Consider a binary input sequence of length K, denoted by m. This sequence is encoded with a channel code of rate R = K/N to generate the codeword c. For example, when LDPC codes are employed, the generator matrix G has dimensions K × N and the encoding process can be expressed as

c = m · G.

From the perspective of DNA synthesis, many DNA strands are generated according to this mapping. For example, for the input bits ‘100’ in Table I, the synthesis process produces strands such that half contain nucleotide ‘A’ and the other half contain nucleotide ‘C’.

B. Mapping to DNA Composite Letters
As described in Section II, mapping the codeword c to composite letters is equivalent to mapping it onto threedimensional constellation points. Specifically, each L-bit segment of c is associated with a constellation point ρs ∈ ∆L, as defined in (1), where the corresponding input segment is denoted by us, for all s ∈ {1, . . . , 2L}.

Finally, note that the mapping of constellation points can be further optimized: the values of ρs,i may be chosen based on the number of DNA copies n, as discussed in [11].

B. Insertion–Deletion Errors
We consider insertion–deletion errors in each DNA strand under the following channel model: after each nucleotide of a strand, with probability pi a random nucleotide is inserted

(each equally likely, with probability pi/4), or the nucleotide is deleted with probability pd, and no insertion/deletion takes place with probability 1−pi −pd, independently of each other. Thus, this model accounts for both insertions and deletions.
Similar to the substitution channel, when the strand passes through the ID channel, the transition probability in (4) and the LLR in (9) may no longer reflect the true likelihood of the constellation points. Therefore, we update the constellation points accordingly.

V. NUMERICAL RESULTS
In this section, we evaluate the error rate performance of composite DNA with sampling randomness, substitution errors, and insertion–deletion errors. For error correction, we employ three binary LDPC codes with different rates: 1) A rate R1 = 0.3235 LDPC code based on the 5G standard, obtained from the first base graph with parameters expansion factor Zc = 12 and set index iLS = 1. The corresponding parity-check matrix H1 has size 552 × 816 with K = 264 and N = 816. 2) A rate R2 = 0.5 LDPC code from the WRAN (IEEE 802.22) standard, with parity-check matrix H2 of size 240 × 480, where K = 240 and N = 480. 3) A rate R3 = 0.75 LDPC code from the IEEE 802.11 standard, with parity-check matrix H3 of size 162×648, where K = 486 and N = 648. While we focus on the use of LDPC codes, the same framework can be applied with other coding schemes, such as polar codes or convolutional codes.

As expected, the BLER is higher than in the samplingonly channel, since insertions and deletions both introduce errors and reduce the effective number of usable strands. Moreover, compared to the substitution channel, ID errors are more disruptive because insertions and deletions cause index shifts. A single insertion or deletion event affects not only the modified position but also all subsequent bases. This leads to correlated errors across positions, unlike substitution errors, which remain independent across nucleotides. Consequently, the overall performance degradation in the ID channel is more apparent. Nevertheless, the results demonstrate that composite DNA storage remains feasible under ID errors as well. Future extensions could explicitly incorporate strands with varying nucleotide counts, rather than discarding them, to further improve performance.
We conclude the paper with a comparison with the results in [8], which considers limited-magnitude probability errors for composite DNA systems and provides both outer and inner bounds, along with practical error-correcting code constructions. The underlying channel model is based on limitedmagnitude errors and can correct at most a fixed number of t symbol errors along a strand of composite letters. On the other hand, under the sampling randomness considered in this work, the majority of composite letters may deviate from the designed constellation points. Therefore, under the error definition in [8], our channel model would classify most positions along the strand as composite letter errors. As a result, the coding scheme proposed in [8], while effective for limited-magnitude probability errors, performs poorly in our setting due to the fundamental difference in the channel model.

VI. CONCLUSIONS
We study composite DNA data storage systems, which
increase the capacity of DNA storage by using mixtures of
composite letters. Using an analogy to digital modulation,
we consider composite letters as 3-dimensional constellation
points on the probability simplex and define the transition
probabilities for the multinomial channel model. We propose
using practical channel coding schemes to handle sampling
randomness for this model and show how to obtain LLRs for
use in channel decoding. We validate the approach via numeri-
cal examples using practical binary LDPC codes, demonstrat-
ing that even codes not specifically designed for composite
letters can provide high performance in composite DNA data
storage systems. Furthermore, the proposed approach is also
extended to account for substitution and insertion–deletion
errors. Future work may incorporate additional error types
(e.g., strand losses, nucleotide absorptions) and address the
design of specific codes.
