I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are capacityapproaching error-correcting codes widely used in modern communication and storage systems, including Wi-Fi, optical/microwave links, and 5G enhanced mobile broadband (eMBB) channels [1],[2]. However, under iterative decoding, LDPC codes suffer from the error floor phenomenon[3]. This behavior is mainly caused by certain harmful substructures in the Tanner graph, known as trapping sets, among which elementary trapping sets(ETSs) are regarded as the most detrimental [4],[5].
Considerable effort has been devoted to eliminating trapping sets in Tanner graphs. As approximating the minimum trapping-set size is NP-hard [6], exhaustive enumeration lacks reliable worst-case guarantees. Consequently, structural constraints on Tanner graphs are widely adopted to exclude small ETSs. Restrictions on short cycles—such as 8-cycles in girth8 quasi-cyclic LDPC codes [7, 8] or short cycles with chords [9, 10] have proven effective in improving minimum distance and error floor performance, with further bounds derived using Tura´n numbers of theta graphs [11] and variable-regular degree constraints [12]. The structure of this paper is as follows: Section II introduces the essential definitions and notations required for our analysis. In Section III, We determine the Tura´n numbers for several special graphs, including theta graphs, dumbbell graphs, and short cycles with chords. This mathematical foundation allows us theoretically to show the effect of eliminating various small ETSs. II. PRELIMINARIES
A. Graph theory
A graph G = (V, E) consists of a non-empty vertex set V and an edge set E as unordered pairs of vertices. Two vertices u and v are adjacent, denoted as uv ∈ E if they are connected by an edge. The neighborhood of a vertex v is defined as NG(v) = {u ∈ V | uv ∈ E} which is the set of vertices adjacent to v. Similarly, for a subset S ⊆ V , its neighborhood is denoted by N (S). The subgraph induced by S is defined as G[S] = (S, E′), where E′ = {vw ∈ E | v, w ∈ S}. A graph without loops or multiple edges is simple. A simple graph is complete, denoted Kn, if there is an edge connecting every pair of its n vertices. Additionally, a simple graph is bipartite if its vertices can be partitioned into two sets V1, V2 with no edge within the same set. A bipartite graph is complete, denoted by Km,n, if every vertex in V1 is connected to every vertex in V2, with m and n vertices in each part respectively. In particular, the complete bipartite graph K1,n−1 is called an n-vertex star graph, denoted by Sn.
The degree of a vertex u is defined as dG(u) = |NG(u)|, representing the number of vertices adjacent to u. The complement of G is defined as G = (V, E), where E is the complement of the edge set E. For an n-vertex graph G

The graph Pk = (V, E) with V = {x0, x1, x2, · · · , xk} and E = {x0x1, x1x2, · · · , xk−1xk}, where all vertices xi are distinct, is called a path of length k. The vertices x0 and xk are the endpoints of the path P . We say that two or more paths are internally disjoint if they have no common vertices except the endpoints. The distance d(u, v) between u and v is defined as the length of a shortest path connecting u and v. The diameter of a graph G is defined as diam(G) = max{d(u, v) | u, v ∈ V (G)}. A cycle of length k, denoted by Ck, is a path that starts and ends at the same vertex x0 = xk with all other vertices in the sequence being distinct. The girth of a simple graph is the length of its shortest cycle, denoted by g. Definition 1. A theta graph, denoted by θ(a, b, c), is formed by three internally disjoint paths with the same pair of endpoints, of length a, b, c respectively, where 1 ≤ a ≤ b ≤ c.
Definition 2. A dumbbell graph, denoted by D(a, b; q), is formed by two vertex-disjoint cycles Ca and Cb connecting via a path of length q. In particular, when q = 0, D(a, b; 0) indicates that Ca and Cb share one common vertex.
If a graph G does not contain a subgraph isomorphic to H, then G is said to be H-free. We now introduce the concept of the Tura´n number [17].
Definition 3. Let F be a non-empty family of graphs. The Tura´n number ex(n, F) is defined as the maximum number of edges in an n-vertex F-free graph. An n-vertex graph attaining this maximum number of edges is called an F-free extremal graph of order n. In particular, when F consists of a single graph G, we simply write ex(n, G).
For two graphs G and H, we say that G = H if G and H are isomorphic. The disjoint union and join of G and H are denoted by G + H and G ∨ H, respectively. The notation mH stands for the disjoint union of m copies of H. For a graph G = (V, E) and a vertex v ∈ V , G − v denotes the graph obtained from G by deleting the vertex v together with all edges incident to v.
B. LDPC code
A Tanner graph G = (L ∪ R, E) is a bipartite graph derived from the parity-check matrix H of an LDPC code, which is introduced in [18]. In this graph, L and R denote the sets of variable nodes and check nodes, corresponding to the columns and rows of H, respectively. The i-th check node is adjacent to the j-th variable node if and only if H(i, j) = 1. The Tanner graph is variable-regular if every variable node v ∈ L has the same degree dL(v) = γ.
For a lifting degree p, the parity-check matrix H of a QCLDPC code can be represented by p × p circulant permutation

Definition 4. An (a, b) trapping set is an induced subgraph G(S) generated by S ⊆ L where a represents the size and b is the number of vertices in N (S) with odd degree. Furthermore, if all check nodes have degrees of either 1 or 2, it is called elementary trapping set (ETS).

Definition 6. For a given (a, b)-ETS, let G be its corresponding VN graph. Define D(G) = {(u, v), (v, u) | uv ∈ E(G)}. The system matrix Asys is a |D(G)| × |D(G)| matrix whose rows and columns correspond to ordered pairs in D(G). Asys(G)(i, j) = 1 if and only if the second component of ordered pair j equals the first component of ordered pair i, and the first component of j is not equal to the second component of i; otherwise, Asys(G)(i, j) = 0.
The system matrix Asys characterizes the main messagepassing process within the ETS, and its spectral radius ρ(Asys) is an important parameter influencing the decoding behavior. V. SPECTRAL RADIUS ANALYSIS AND SIMULATION
In this section, we further investigate the effects of these structural constraints from two perspectives. First, we analyze the spectral radii of the system matrices associated with ETSs under the proposed structural restrictions. Second, we construct QC-LDPC codes that satisfy these restrictions and evaluate their error-floor performance through simulations.
A. Spectral Radius of ETSs
According to the linear state-space model for ETSs, the spectral radius of the associated system matrix is a key parameter that affects decoding behavior. In particular, a larger spectral radius generally indicates that erroneous messages within an ETS decay more slowly during iterative decoding, thereby making the ETS more harmful in the error floor region. Motivated by this observation, we examine whether the structural constraints proposed in Section IV also reduce the spectral radii of the remaining ETSs.
We first consider Tanner graphs in which no two 8-cycles share a common variable node. In this case, the corresponding VN graph of an ETS contains no pair of 4-cycles sharing at least one common vertex. To quantify the effect of this restriction, we partition all (a, b)-ETSs into two classes, denoted by intC4 (a, b) and indC4 (a, b). The set intC4 (a, b) consists of ETSs whose VN graphs contain one of the subgraphs θ(1, 3, 3), θ(2, 2, 2), or D(4, 4; 0), whereas indC4 (a, b) consists of ETSs whose VN graphs are free of all these subgraphs. For each ETS in these two classes, we compute the spectral radius of its system matrix and then report the corresponding mean and median values.
Using the graph tool ‘nauty’ [21], we enumerated all nonisomorphic VN graphs satisfying the required conditions. Table II summarizes the results for all (a, b)-ETSs with γ = 3, a ≤ 14, and b ≤ 6. For all listed parameter pairs, both the mean and the median spectral radii of the ETSs in indC4 (a, b) are smaller than those of the ETSs in intC4 (a, b). This observation suggests that ETSs whose VN graphs avoid the above substructures tend to be less harmful from the perspective of spectral radius. Therefore, imposing the condition that 8-cycles in the Tanner graph do not share aatleast one variable node can effectively eliminate ETSs with relatively large spectral radii and is thus beneficial to the error floor performance of LDPC codes.
Next, we consider the short cycles with chords Ti, where i ∈ {1, 2, 3}. For each Ti, we divide all (a, b)-ETSs into two sets: FGi (a, b), consisting of ETSs whose VN graphs are Gi-free,

and UGi (a, b), consisting of ETSs whose VN graphs contain Gi as a subgraph. For each ETS in these two sets, we compute the spectral radius of the associated system matrix and the
corresponding mean and median values for each class. The results for all (a, b)-ETSs with γ = 4, a ≤ 10, and
b ≤ 4 are presented in Table III. For each Ti and each listed parameter pair (a, b), the mean and median spectral radii of the ETSs in FGi (a, b) are consistently smaller than those in UGi (a, b). This finding indicates that ETSs containing Gi are generally more detrimental than those that avoid Gi. Consequently, excluding the substructures Ti from the Tanner graph can help reduce the occurrence of ETSs with
relatively large spectral radii, thereby improving the error floor
performance.

VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we derive the Tura´n numbers for several special graphs, including theta graphs, dumbbell graphs, and short cycles with chords, and translate these extremal results into structural constraints on ETSs when the corresponding subgraphs are forbidden in the VN graph.
