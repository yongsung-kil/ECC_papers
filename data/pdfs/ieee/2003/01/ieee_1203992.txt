Optimal Puncturing of Irregular Low-Density Parity-Check Codes

Jeongseok Ha School of Electrical and Computer Engineering Georgia Institute of Technology Atlanta, Georgia 30332–0250 Email: jsha@ece.gatech.edu

Steven W. McLaughlin School of Electrical and Computer Engineering Georgia Institute of Technology Atlanta, Georgia 30332–0250 Email: swm@ece.gatech.edu

Abstract— In this paper we consider rate compatible puncturing of low density parity check (LDPC) codes. We present a general density evolution-based procedure which finds the optimal puncturing of a base code. We show that puncturing can be performed across a range of rates and code lengths in a manner that produces punctured codes with good thresholds. This allows one to implement a single optimal LDPC code of a low rate that can be punctured across a wide range of rates without loss of threshold performance. Simulation results show that the error floors of the codes do not degrade after puncturing.

I. INTRODUCTION

In this paper, we consider rate compatible punctured low-

density parity-check (LDPC) codes, namely one would like to

have a single LDPC code which, when punctured in a rate-

compatible way, remains good across a range of punctured

rates. We present a way to puncture LDPC codes which does

not disturb the optimality of the base code, and where the

resulting punctured codes maintain threshold optimality across

a range of rates. We focus mainly on asymptotic thresholds

of the punctured LDPC codes instead of practical issues

such as code performance with a short block length, number

of iterations to achieve a saturated performance and finite

precision effects due to quantization.

For the puncturing problem, we shall set up the problem as

follows. Variable nodes of a bipartite graph can be grouped

in accordance with their degrees. Thus, all coded symbols

have the same degree in a group denoted by Gj where
2 ≤ j ≤ dl and dl is the maximum left degree. We shall randomly puncture a proportion πj(0) of the symbols in Gj, where πj(0) is determined in a systematic way to optimize performance. We define p(0) to be the total puncturing fraction,

number of variable nodes). We extend the distribution pair,

III. DESIGN OF PUNCTURING DISTRIBUTIONS FOR BASE R = 1/2 AND 1/3 CODES

In this section, we design puncturing distributions with two good edge degree distribution pairs in [2] and [3] for 1/2-rate codes that have the maximum variable (left) degrees of 10 and 20, respectively. For a broader range of coding rate variations, we also design puncturing distributions for a rate 0.33 code that has the maximum variable degree of 30. The design goal is to maximize the puncturing fraction, p(0) for a given Eb/N0. We can formulate the problem as simple linear optimization programming using the results in Section II.
We optimize puncturing fractions, p(0) from 0.05 to 0.45 in increments of 0.05 to achieve the coding rates, R, from 0.5263 to 0.9091 with the degree distribution pair in [2],

λ(x) = 0.25105x + 0.30938x2 + 0.00104x3 + 0.43853x9,
and ρ(x) = 0.63676x6 + 0.36324x7.

In Fig. 2, we depict the puncturing proportions, πj(0)’s, all but one of which have smooth changes with respect to the
rate changes, indicating that the codes can be punctured in a rate-compatible fashion. The abrupt changes of π4(0) result from the small magnitude of λ4, which means the puncturing of coded symbols in G4 does not have much influence on
the overall performance. To verify the analytic results and the

linear optimization algorithm, we also design the puncturing distributions with a combination of the Discretized Density Evolution (DDE) [3] and Differential Evolution (DE) [6]. DE searches for an optimum vector with a combination of a genetic and a hill climbing algorithms. The DDE algorithm models the message-passing decoder more accurately, although the algorithm lacks analytic insight and needs more computations. We compare the results from the combination of GA and the linear optimization (denoted as LP) with ones from DE with DDE (denoted as DE) in Fig. 3. The optimizations with DE proceed in three different ways to avoid possible local minima. In the first trial (denoted as TR1), we search the puncturing distribution over the U4 space without initial values where U = [0, 1] . In the second and third trials (denoted as TR2 and TR3), we use the puncturing proportions, πj(0)’s obtained from LP as initial values that are perturbed with Gaussian and uniform noise, respectively. The comparisons show that there is no difference between the results from the three different trials with DE. However, we can see some discrepancies between results from LP and DE, although the results have the same trends with respect to the coding rates. The discrepancies are more distinctive at higher degrees, which comes from the fact that node distribution is inversely proportional to the degree, (λj = αλj/j and α > 0). Thus, the lowest degree puncturing proportion, π2(0) is the most important, which has a good match with the results from DE in Fig. 3.
To see the code performance of the intentionally and randomly punctured LDPC codes, we compute the gaps between the asymptotic Eb/N0 performances (thresholds) and the capacity of the BPSK signal with respect to the coding rates. The asymptotic Eb/N0 performances are evaluated with DDE. We observed that there is less than 0.1dB difference between the performances of the punctured LDPC code with the puncturing distributions from LP and DE. Although the performance difference between the LDPC code with puncturing distributions from LP and DE is relatively small, we are more interested in the ultimate performance of the punctured LDPC codes. Thus, we describe the performance of the punctured LDPC code with the puncturing distributions from DE.
The simulation results with the puncturing distributions from DE are shown in Fig. 4. The results show that the base code has a 0.2dB gap from the capacity and an additional 0.1dB gap permits us to vary the coding rate up to 0.72. The changes of coding rate up to 0.84 and 0.92 are obtained at the expense of 0.2dB and 0.4dB additional gaps, respectively.
To verify the design rule, we implement the LDPC code and evaluate required Eb/N0’s for a BER of 10−4 with the random and intentional puncturing patterns. The implemented code has a code block length of 131072, a coding rate of 0.5 and no 4 cycle loops in the parity check matrix. We depict the gaps between the Eb/N0’s and the capacity (denoted as Simulation) in Fig. 4. The gaps of the implemented code and asymptotical thresholds from DE show a consistency. Good puncturing distributions can be also designed with a combination of Discretized Density Evolution (DDE) and Differential Evolution (DE).
We design puncturing distributions for two half-rate LDPC codes that have the maximum variable (left) degrees of 10 and 20, respectively and evaluate the performances of the LDPC codes. The performance of the punctured LDPC code is measured by the gaps between the asymptotic thresholds and the capacity of BPSK with respect to coding rates. In the experiment with the first LDPC code, the gap of the base code is 0.2dB and an additional 0.1dB gap permits us to vary the coding rate up to 0.72. The LDPC codes are also implemented with code lengths of 131072, and the performances of the implemented codes are evaluated. We observe that the asymptotic thresholds are good lower bounds of the implemented LDPC codes.
For a broader range of coding rate variations, we design puncturing distributions for an LDPC code having a coding rate of 0.33 whose maximum variable (left) degree is 30. The gap of the base code is 0.1dB and additional 0.1dB is needed to increase the coding rate up to 0.8. The changes of coding rate up to 0.9 and 0.985 are obtained at the expense of 0.2dB and 0.3dB additional gaps, respectively.
Through the work done in this paper, we show the existence of good puncturing distributions for LDPC codes and propose a simple but effective design rule. This work is focusing mainly on theoretical aspect of the problem but can be further studied for designing practical rate-compatible punctured LDPC codes.

IV. CONCLUSIONS
We investigate good puncturing distributions for RateCompatible Punctured-Code (RCPC) with Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes. The asymptotical performances of punctured LDPC codes are analyzed with Gaussian Approximation. The analytic results give us an insight into the convergence of punctured LDPC codes and enable us to predict the asymptotic performance of the punctured LDPC codes. From the analysis, we can see that the residual puncturing probabilities during iterations depend only on the structure of the punctured LDPC codes specified by a three-tuple degree distributions λ(x), ρ(x), π(0)(x) instead of SNR, which simplifies the recursive expression of the updated mean, m(uk) to a steady-state equation.
