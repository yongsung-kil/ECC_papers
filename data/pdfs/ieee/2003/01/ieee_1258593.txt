Near Shannon Limit Quasi-Cyclic Low-Density Parity-Check Codes
Shu Lin, Lei Chen, Jun Xu, and Ivana Djurdjevic Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering
University of California, Davis Davis, CA 95616
E-mail: shulin, leichen, junxu, idjurdje@ece.ucdavis.edu

Abstract— This paper presents two classes of quasi-cyclic lowdensity parity-check codes which perform close to the Shannon limit. The construction of these codes is based on decomposition of circulant matrices constructed from finite geometries.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes were first discovered by Gallager in early 1960’s [1]. These codes were recently rediscovered and shown to form a class of Shannon limit approaching codes [2]–[6]. A regular LDPC code is defined as the null space of a sparse parity-check matrix H over GF(2) that has the following structural properties: (1) all the rows have weight ρ; (2) all the columns have weight γ; (3) the number of 1-components in common between any two rows (or any two columns), denoted λ, is either 0 or 1; and (4) both ρ and γ are small compared to the code length. Property (3) ensures that the Tanner graph [7] of an LDPC code is free of cycles of length 4. The parity-check matrix defined above is said to be (γ, ρ)-regular and the code generated by it is called a (γ, ρ)-regular LDPC code. Properties (2) and (3) ensure that for every code bit, there are γ parity-check sums orthogonal on it [8] and hence the minimum distance of the code is at least γ + 1. If the column (or row) weights of H are not constant, then the null space of H gives an irregular LDPC code. We call an LDPC code whose paritycheck matrix H possesses property (3) a narrow-sense LDPC code, or an LDPC code with λ ≤ 1. For simplicity, the paritycheck matrix H is said to be a matrix with λ ≤ 1. We can enlarge the class of narrow-sense LDPC codes by removing property (3). With this removal, we have a broad-sense LDPC code. The Tanner graph of a broad-sense LDPC code may contain cycles of length 4.
This paper presents an algebraic method for constructing regular LDPC codes. This method is based on decomposition of circulant matrices constructed from finite geometries. Codes constructed based on this method perform very well with the sum-product algorithm (SPA) compared to computer generated LDPC codes. Most importantly, these codes are quasi-cyclic and hence their encoding can be implemented in linear time with linear shift-registers. This advantage is not shared by computer generated random LDPC codes.

II. CIRCULANT DECOMPOSITION AND QUASIC-CYCLIC LDPC CODES

A circulant is a square matrix for which every row is the

cyclic-shift (one place to the right) of the row above it and the

first row is the cyclic-shift of the last row. For such a matrix,

every column is the downward cyclic-shift of the column on

its left and the first column is the downward cyclic-shift of

the last column. The set of columns (reading from top down)

is the same as the set of rows (reading from right to left).

The null space of a circulant gives a cyclic code [8]. Given

a circulant, it is possible to decompose it into an array of

circulants of the same size. Then the null space of this array

gives a quasi-cyclic (or circulant) code [9].

Consider an n × n circulant G with column (or row)

weight δ. Since the column and row weights of a circulant

are the same, for simplicity, we say that G has weight δ. For

with weights w1, w2, . . . , wt, respectively. Let g1 be the first

III. NARROW-SENSE QUASI-CYCLIC LDPC CODES
Circulants with λ ≤ 1 can be constructed from Euclidean geometries. Consider the m-dimensional Euclidean geometry, EG(m, ps) over the field GF(ps) [8], [10]. This geometry consists of points, lines and hyperplanes. A point is simply an m-tuple over GF(ps) and hence there are pms points in the

geometry. The pms points in EG(m, ps) simply form the vector space V of all the m-tuples over GF(ps). The all-zero m-tuple
is called the origin of the geometry. A line is simply a one-
dimensional subspace of V or a coset of a one-dimensional subspace of V . Therefore, a line consists of ps points. There are p(m−1)s(pms − 1)/(ps − 1) lines in EG(m, ps). Any two points in EG(m, ps) are connected by a line. Two lines are either disjoint or they intersect at one and only one point. A µdimensional hyperplane (commonly called a µ-flat) is simply a µ-dimensional subspace of V or a coset of a µ-dimensional subspace of V . A µ-flat consists of pµs points. Let EG∗(m, ps) be the subgeometry obtained from EG(m, ps) by removing the
origin and all the lines passing through (or containing) the origin. Then EG∗(m, ps) consists of n = pms − 1 non-origin points and (p(m−1)s − 1)(pms − 1)/(ps − 1) lines not passing
through the origin.
The field GF(pms) as an extension field of GF(ps) is a realization of EG(m, ps) [8]. Let α be a primitive element of GF(pms). Then 1 = α0, α1, α2, . . . , αn−1 represent the n = pms − 1 non-origin points of EG∗(m, ps). The incidence vector of a line L in EG∗(m, ps) is defined as an n-tuple, vL = (v0, v1, . . . , vn−1), over GF(2) whose components correspond to the n points in EG∗(m, ps), where vi = 1 if and only if αi is a point on L and vi = 0 otherwise. The weight of vL is ps. For 0 ≤ i < n, let v(Li) be the vector obtained by cyclically shifting vL i places to the right. For i = 0, v(L0) = vL(n) = vL. It can be shown that v(Li) is the incidence vector of another line in EG∗(m, ps). Furthermore, for 0 ≤ i, j < n and i = j, v(Li) = v(Lj). This says that vL and its n − 1 cyclic shifts give n incidence vectors of n different lines in EG∗(m, ps). A vector with such property is
said to be primitive [11]. The incidence vector of any line in EG∗(m, ps) is primitive. As a result of the cyclic and primitive properties, the incidence vectors of the lines in EG∗(m, ps) can be partitioned into K1 = (p(m−1)s −1)/(ps −1) cyclic classes, denoted Q1, Q2, . . . , QK1 , each consisting of n incidence vectors. The n incidence vectors in a class can be obtained by cyclically shifting any vector in the class n times.
For each cyclic class Qi, we can form an n × n circulant Gi with the vectors in Qi as its rows. This results in a class of n × n circulants G = {G1, G2, . . . , GK1 }. The weight of each circulant Gi is ps, which is small compared to its size n = pms − 1 for m ≥ 2. Hence, each circulant is a sparse
matrix. Since the set of columns of a circulant is the same as
the set of its rows (read from right to left), the columns of
each circulant Gi are also the incidence vectors of lines in EG∗(m, ps). It follows from the structural properties of lines in EG(m, ps) that no two rows (or two columns) either from the same circulant or from two different circulants in G can
have more than one 1-component in common. Consequently,
each circulant in G is a sparse matrix with λ ≤ 1.
The circulants in G can be used for constructing quasicyclic LDPC codes with λ ≤ 1. For 1 ≤ k ≤ K1, let {G1, G2, . . . , Gk} be a set of k circulants from G. Arrange the circulants in this set in a row H = [G1 G2 · · · Gk]. For a

IV. BROAD-SENSE QUASIC-CYCLIC LDPC CODES

Sparse circulants with λ > 1 can be constructed based on
flats of an Euclidean geometry. Here we consider construction based on the 2-flats of the subgeometry EG∗(m, ps) of the m-dimensional Euclidean geometry EG(m, ps) with m ≥ 3. There are (pms −1)(p(m−1)s −1)(p(m−2)s −1)/(p2s −1)(ps − 1) 2-flats in EG∗(m, ps), each consisting of p2s points. These 2-flats do not contain the origin of EG(m, ps). If two 2-flats in EG∗(m, ps) are not disjoint, then they intersect either on a
point or on a line [8], [10]. If they intersect on a line, they have ps points in common. Let F be a 2-flat in EG∗(m, ps). The
incidence vector vF of F is an n-tuple over GF(2) defined
in exactly the same manner as the incidence vector of a line in EG∗(m, ps), where n = pms − 1. The weight of vF is p2s. Again, the incidence vector of any 2-flat in EG∗(m, ps)
is primitive [11]. Therefore, cyclically shifting the incidence
vector of any 2-flat n times gives n incidence vectors of n different 2-flats in EG∗(m, ps). Consequently, the incidence vectors of the 2-flats in EG∗(m, ps) can be partitioned into

Example 3: Again we consider the subgeometry EG∗(3, 23) given in Example 1. This subgeometry consists of 511 2-flats. The incidence vectors of these 2-flats form a single 511 × 511 circulant G with weight 64. The null space of G gives a broad-sense (511,448) cyclic LDPC code with rate 0.8767 and minimum distance exactly 9. The Tanner graph of this code contains 3,605,616 cycles of length 4! With such a large number of cycles of length 4, one would expect that this code cannot possibly perform well with iterative decoding. After two iterations, the decoding would become highly correlated. Surprisingly, simulation shows that this short code performs extremely well with iterative decoding as shown in Fig. 3. At the BER of 10−6, it performs only 2.3 dB away from the Shannon limit and no error floor is observed above BER of 10−7. Suppose we choose column splitting factor t = 16 and row splitting factor c = 2. Decompose the 511 × 511 circulant G into a 2 × 16 array Z of thirty-two 511 × 511 descendant circulants, each having weight 2. Then Z is a 1022 × 8176 matrix with column weight 4 and row weight 32. The null space of Z gives a broad-sense (8176,7156) quasi-cyclic LDPC code with rate 0.875. The Tanner graph of this code contains 9198 cycles of length 4. We see that decomposition of G drastically reduces the number of cycles of length 4. The error performance of this code is also shown in Fig. 3. At the BER of 10−6, it performs only 1 dB away from the Shannon limit with sharp-drop waterfall error performance without error floor above the BER of 10−7. For comparison, the error performance of an equivalent computer generated MacKay code is also included. The Tanner graph of the MacKay code is free of cycles of length 4. We see that the broad-sense quasi-cyclic code with 9198 cycles of length 4 performs equally well compared to the MacKay code.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we have presented two classes of quasiccyclic regular LDPC codes. Besides their easily implementable encoding, codes in these two classes perform close to the Shannon limit with iterative decoding. A very interesting and maybe significant discovery is that we have constructed algebraic codes which have many short cycles in their Tanner graphs but they perform amazingly well with iterative decoding and close to the Shannon limit. This discovery contradicts the common belief that for a code to perform well with iterative decoding, its Tanner graph must be free of short cycles (i.e., with a relatively large girth). Its significant implication is that in search for good and easily encodable LDPC codes, we do not have to focus on construction of codes that have large girths, which is not very easy. In fact codes with large girths do not necessarily perform well with iterative decoding if they have poor minimum distances. Poor minimum distances in general result in high error floor which is not desirable for systems requiring low bit error rates. The discovery opens up a wider avenue for searching of good and easily encodable LDPC codes and makes construction of LDPC codes more interesting and challenging.
In the paper, we have only considered construction of quasicyclic codes based on circulants constructed from Euclidean geometries. However, circulants can also be constructed from projective geometries and experimental designs.
