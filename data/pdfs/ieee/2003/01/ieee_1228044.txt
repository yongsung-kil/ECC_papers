Abstract - This paper presents a combinatoric
method for constructing low density parity check (LDPC) codes. Two classes of LDPC codes are constructed and they perform well with iterative decoding. Codes in one class are quasi-cyclic which can be encoded in linear-time with simple shift-registers.
I. CONSTRUCTION
Let B = [bi,j] be a binary Q x p matrix. Let 9 =
{GI,G P.,..,G K }be a class of sparse k x n matrices which
satisfies the following constraint: no two rows (or two
columns) either in the same matrix Gi or in two different matrices Gi and Gj have more than one “1-entry” in common
(called the row-column constraint). Construct an a k x p n matrix H as follows: (1) replace each l-entry in B by a matrix in
Q; (2) replace each 0-entry in B by a k x TZ zero matrix; and
(3) all the non-zero submatrices in a row or in a column are distinct (called the replacement constraint). Then H is also sparse matrix. The null space of H gives an LDPC code. The above construction of LDPC codes is referred to as a superposition construction in which B is called the the base matrix
and the matrices Gi in Q are called the constituent matrices.
If the base matrix B satisfies the row-column constraint, then H also satisfies the row-column constraint. In this case, the null space of H gives an LDPC code C,, whose Tanner graph is free of cycles of length 4. We refer to this code as a narrow-sense LDPC code. constituent matrices of B are regular matrices, then H is a
regular matrix and C,, is a regular LDPC code. If the base matrix B does not satisfy the row-column con-
straint, then H may not satisfy the row-column constraint. In this case, the null space of H gives an LDPC code c b a whose Tanner graph may contain cycles of length 4. Such a code is referred to as a broad-sense LDPC code. For a broadsense LDPC code, its Tanner graph can be divided into /3 subgraphs. Each subgraph is free of cycles of length 4 due to the row-column constraint of the constituent matrices in
Q. Cycles of length 4 can only exist between two subgraphs.
Cycles of length 4 do not necessarily prevent a code from performing well with iterative decoding as commonly believed. In fact, many broad-sense codes have been constructed and they performed amazingly well with iterative decoding.
