SNR Mismatch Effects in LDPC Coded Magnetic Recording Channels

Weijun Tan and J. R. Cruz
School of Electrical and Computer Engineering The University of Oklahoma Norman, OK 73019 {wjtan, jcruz}@ou.edu

Abstract – Signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) mismatch has been found to have a great impact on the performance of low-density paritycheck (LDPC) coded magnetic recording channels. While an inappropriate SNR mismatch degrades the performance dramatically, a properly selected optimum SNR mismatch can improve it significantly. In this paper we analyze the causes of this phenomenon and find optimum values for the SNR mismatch for specific magnetic recording systems using both density evolution analysis and Monte Carlo simulations.
Keywords – Bahl-Cocke-Jelinek-Raviv (BCJR) algorithm; lowdensity parity-check (LDPC) codes; magnetic recording channels (MRCs); message-passing algorithm; SNR mismatch
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have been shown to be particularly well suited for magnetic recording channels (MRCs) [1]. The decoding algorithms, explicitly, the BahlCocke-Jelinek-Raviv (BCJR) algorithm [2] for the equalized PR channel and the belief-propagation (BP) algorithm for the LDPC code [3] are believed to be the determining factors of their excellent performance. One requirement for using these algorithms is knowledge of the variance of the noise. However, this variance is not always known, although there are means to estimate it. The problem is further compounded by the fact that even if the variance of the noise were perfectly known, the system performance may not be optimum. More specifically, if the variance of the noise used by the two a posteriori probability (APP) decoders is changed by some amount, either up or down, the system performance may improve. This phenomenon is referred to as the SNR mismatch in the literature [4], [5] and has been observed in our simulations for LDPC coded MRCs.
SNR mismatch for LDPC codes was first reported in [6] for MRCs with erasures. It was observed that when a certain number of bits are erased, the codeword cannot be corrected unless the SNR is mismatched, and furthermore, with an appropriate mismatch the performance can be dramatically improved. This observation motivated us to look into the mechanism behind it and how to take advantage of it in a practical system.

II. PROBLEM FORMULATION
The system model for our investigation is quite simple. Specifically, an LDPC code is used on an extended partial response class IV (EPR4) channel or on an MRC equalized to an EPR4 target. For PR channels, we usually assume that only additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) is present, and the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) is defined as SNR = 10 log10 (E / N 0 ) , where E is the energy of the PR channel, and N 0 is the power of the noise; while for an MRC, both white electronic noise and media noise, which is modeled as first-order transition jitter, are considered, and the SNR is defined as SNR = 10 log10 ( A2 / N 0 ) , where A is the amplitude of the Lorentzian or Lorentzian-Gaussian pulse response of the channel.
The decoding system consists of two APP decoders: the BCJR decoder [2] for the channel and the BP decoder for the LDPC code [3]. In this paper we consider the case in which the BCJR decoder only runs once, which is the case of most practical interest, because of the small increase in performance and the large computational complexity required to run it more than once. Extending this work to iterative channel detection and LDPC decoding is straightforward.
A uniform random interleaver is employed between the LDPC encoder and the channel. Its role is to make the channel density identically distributed for all codewords such that the density evolution algorithm can be used. This interleaver is not used in the simulations.
The implementation of the BCJR and BP decoding algorithms requires knowledge of the variance of the noise σ 2 = N 0 / 2 . Let the noise variance used by the decoder be κσ 2 , where κ is the SNR mismatch factor, and the corresponding SNR mismatch is defined as

V. SNR MISMATCH FOR LDPC DECODING
Given the insensitivity of the BCJR channel detection to SNR mismatch, we can conclude that it is the LDPC BP decoding algorithm that mostly affects the SNR mismatch performance. However, we must emphasize that this does not mean that the channel detection is irrelevant. Actually the LDPC decoder is sensitive to the input channel density produced by the soft channel detector.
First let us look at LDPC coded AWGN channels. For this linear system, we can assume that the all-zero codeword is transmitted without loss of generality. It is well known that the channel log-likelihood ratio (LLR) density is a Gaussian function with mean 2 / σ 2 and variance 4 / σ 2 , i.e., fu0 = N (2 / σ 2 ,4 / σ 2 ) . In summary, the channel density has two important properties: i) is Gaussian distributed, and ii) its variance is twice the mean. Or, in other words, the varianceto-mean ratio (VMR) equals two.
The combination of these two properties is called the consistency condition in [7]. Simulations have shown that for this system any SNR mismatch is detrimental. This motivated us to investigate whether the violation of either or both of these two properties causes the SNR mismatch effect.

A. Gaussian Channel
First we consider channels that have perfect Gaussian channel density. For baseline AWGN channel, we assume the channel density to be exactly known and with Gaussian density

For such channels with different VMR, we use the density evolution as described in Section III to compute the SNR threshold. Since this threshold is a typical parameter that features the asymptotic behavior of an LDPC coded system, we believe that it can highlight some essential properties of this system. For m=1.7~2.3, we computed their SNR thresholds, as plotted in Fig. 2. The curve for the standard AWGN channel with m=2 confirms the observation that LDPC codes on AWGN channels should not use any SNR mismatch. For m=1.9 and 2.1, despite the fact that the SNR threshold is minimum when no SNR mismatch is used, the differences between no SNR mismatch and a 1-dB mismatch for m=1.9 and a -1-dB for m=2.1, compared to those for m=2.0, are small. For m=1.7 and 2.3, it is clear that a 1-dB and a -1-dB mismatch improves the SNR threshold slightly.

From the above analysis, we can conclude that the VMR is one of the major contributing factors to the SNR mismatch effect. Over-estimation or under-estimation of the SNR, respectively, improves the SNR thresholds when the VMR is larger or smaller than two. Furthermore, the more the VMR deviates from two, the larger the SNR mismatch.

B. EPR4 Channel with Erasures
Next we attempt to use a non-Gaussian channel density to see how it affects the SNR mismatch effect. Since the channel density of PR channels with AWGN is usually very likely Gaussian with VMR>2, we will use the EPR4 channel corrupted by erasures as an example of a non-Gaussian channel density. In addition, we will consider a channel density with a VMR<2 to exclude the SNR under-estimation effect due to VMR>2.
Erasures in MRCs are due to some physical mechanisms such as media defects or thermal asperity. It is assumed that a certain percentage of the bits in the codeword are erased. Since the noise is still present, the system performance is degraded substantially. From the standpoint of the channel density, the erased part of the codeword generates a section of LLRs that carry no useful information, and the average channel density is no longer Gaussian, instead, it has a small side pedestal on the left tail of the distribution.
In this paper, 3% of the bits are assumed to be erased and only AWGN is present. Similarly as in (4) we use simulation to obtain the average channel density. Plotted in Fig. 3 is an example of such a channel density. There is an obvious side pedestal due to the erasures. We use such channel densities as input to the LDPC decoder to find out the influence of nonGaussianity on the SNR mismatch effect.

VII. CONCLUSION
In this paper we addressed the effect of SNR mismatch on LDPC coded MRCs. First we illustrated using simulations that the BCJR channel detection algorithm is actually not sensitive to SNR mismatch. When hard-decision decoding is used, the BER is almost independent of the SNR mismatch. Its only role is to scale the channel density, which is input to the LDPC decoder and affects the system performance.
Then we investigated the causes of the SNR mismatch effect using SNR threshold analysis based on density evolution. From the observation of an AWGN channel density, we conjectured that Gaussianity and VMR might be causation agents. Subsequently we used two hypothetical channels: the Gaussian channel and the EPR4 channel with erasures, as testing samples to exhibit the influence of these two properties on the SNR mismatch effect. Results show that these two properties are responsible for the SNR mismatch. Generally speaking, if the channel density is approximately Gaussian, and the VMR>2, the SNR should be under-estimated; while when the VMR<2, the SNR should be over-estimated. The effect of Gaussianity is more complex, and therefore no general rules are available.
Based on these conclusions, we used the BER estimation technique derived from density evolution to compute the optimum value of SNR mismatch for MRCs. It was expected that these values could be used as guidance in practical systems. The results show that the SNR mismatch can be computed very accurately using density evolution techniques. The values found for both AWGN and media noise dominated systems are highly consistent with the simulations, namely, -3 ~ -2 dB and -10 ~ -8 dB, respectively.
