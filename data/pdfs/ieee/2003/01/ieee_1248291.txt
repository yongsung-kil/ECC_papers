1. Laser Struction and Device Fabrication Raman fiber amplifiers will be one of the important components in future high capacity long-haul communication systems. In our prior work [I] we demonstrated high power 14xx nm, InGaAsPilnP ridge waveguide diode lasers with CW output powers up to 1 W.These lasers were designed as optical pump sources in Raman amplifiers. In this paper, we describe our latest laser improvements along with advancements in bath fiber-optic module packaging and wavelength combining in Raman pump blocks. As reported previously [I], the InGaAsPilnP laser s t r u m " were grown by low-pressure metal organic chemical vapour deposition (LP-MOCVD) on n-lnP substrates. +ternary InGaAsP campounds of different compositions were used as the material for the confinement, barrier and campres-
sively strained QW layers. In OUT latest devices,
we have reduced the number of quantum wells from three to two. This change leads to a reduction in the optical waveguide losses and a commensurate increase in the operating efficiency. Additional improvements were also made in the
refinement of the ridge waveguide fabrication technology This refinement includes the utilization of the dry etching technology for the initial step of dual-channel ridge fabrication and the optimisation ofboth the dielectric and metals deposition procedures. As with our previous designs, single-mode operation is maintained for a ridge width from 3 to 5 by accurate control of the channel etch depth. The channel depth control is aided by the use of a grown-in etch-stop layer. In order to investigate the optical losses and threshold in our laser structures we used "as cleaves' FabryPerot lasers with differing cavity lengths from I mm to 3 mm. The devices are mounted p-side up on aluminium nihide (AIN) submounts using Au-Sn alloy solder. In our other experiments, the front and rear facets of the devices were coated with low- and high-reflective coatings, respectively. Additionally, p-side down Au-Sn soldering on AIN-submounts was the procedure of choice used for routine characterization, lifetime testing, and module mounting. In super high power operation, the lasers are mounted p-side down directly on standard copper C-blocks using In solder. In turn the C-blocks are screwed to a copper heatrink whose temperame is maintained by a thermo-electric cooler.
