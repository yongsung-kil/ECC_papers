Optimization of Codes for the Joint Viterbi Detector Decoder (JVDD)
1,2Sari Shafidah Bte Shafiee, 1Chan Kheong Sann, 2Goh Wang Ling
1Data Storage Institute (DSI), Agency for Science, Technology and Research (A*STAR), Singapore 2Nanyang Technological University, Singapore

Abstract— The joint Viterbi detector decoder (JVDD) was recently proposed as an alternative to iterative detector/decoder systems (also known as turbo equalizers) used in communication applications today. Despite being conditionally optimal over coded additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN)/inter-symbol interference (ISI) channels, the JVDD suffers from an exponential growth in the number of survivors when used with existing codes such as random low-density parity check (LDPC) codes. This paper proposes a class of JVDD codes designed for the JVDD which mitigate this undesirable trait. This class of codes consists of 4 code designs - 1) fixed row weight, 2) fixed column weight, 3) proportionate row weight and 4) proportionate column weight. While the proposed JVDD codes do effectively bring about a reduction in complexity and enhance the performance of the JVDD, the design parameters of these codes require optimization. In this paper, a design of experiment (DOE) technique that employs the response surface methodology (RSM) is used as a tool to optimize the parameters of each of the proposed codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
Iterative detector/decoders, also known as turbo equalizers, are widely used in communication applications today [1]. The iterative system typically consists of a Bahl, Cocke, Jelinek and Raviv (BCJR) [2] or soft output Viterbi algorithm (SOVA) [3] detector concatenated with a low-density parity check (LDPC) decoder [4]. Despite being widely adopted, the iterative detector/decoder model is sub-optimal. As such, recent advancements in detection and decoding have inclined towards the area of joint detection and decoding.
The joint Viterbi detector decoder (JVDD) was recently proposed in [5] as a scheme in which the detection and decoding are done together on a single trellis structure. It is a novel technique that jointly detects and decodes transmitted sequence in a single step. The JVDD is conditionally optimal over a coded additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN)/intersymbol interference (ISI) channel. However, this proposed detector/decoder system exhibits a high computational complexity when used with error-correction codes that are not specifically designed for the JVDD, such as random LDPC codes. In [5], a novel code coined the Gaussian distribution linear diagonal (GDLD) was designed specifically for the JVDD. With this code, the JVDD exhibited a significant reduction in complexity and reaped a gain in performance over the conventional iterative detector/decoder. There is however a shortcoming in the original design of the GDLD code. A large horizontal offset of the main diagonal of the code is required to ensure adequate column weight for the last few

bits, and thus good performance. This however translates to a high complexity of the JVDD algorithm. To address this issue, 4 novel code designs are proposed in this paper. They consists of fixed row weight, fixed column weight, proportionate row weight and proportionate column weight codes. The 4 newly proposed codes (which can be classified in general as varying gradient (VG) codes) and GDLD codes are members of the JVDD class of codes: they are specifically designed with the JVDD algorithm in mind. GDLD codes can be considered a subclass of VG codes where the gradient of the main diagonal does not change, but is shifted by an offset. With VG codes, there are 2 parameters that allow the diagonal to start at a horizontal offset dx and end at a given vertical offset dy.
Although the JVDD can achieve a better performance and lower complexity with VG codes, the design parameters of each of the 4 types of VG code need to be optimized to jointly attain the best performance with the lowest complexity. In this paper, we take a design of experiments (DOE) approach in identifying the optimal combination of the design parameters of each of the 4 VG codes that gives rise to an optimum combination of high performance (low frame error rate (FER)) and low complexity in some sense that we will define. We adopt a DOE [7] technique using response surface methodology (RSM) [8] in our optimization study.
II. JOINT VITERBI DETECTOR DECODER
The JVDD algorithm aims to return a trellis survivor that constitutes a legal codeword with the smallest path metric through a joint detection and decoding approach. This is achieved via two key steps in the JVDD algorithm 1) metric thresholding and 2) parity checking, as depicted in Figure 1. Metric thresholding is first carried out by splitting survivor paths at every node in the trellis and assigning the usual Viterbi branch metric based on the Euclidean distance to each one. The metrics of each survivor path are compared and survivors with metrics larger than the minimum metric by a user-defined threshold value, τ , are discarded. In addition to the metric thresholding step which occurs at every trellis node, parity checking is carried out at specific parity check nodes in the trellis. These nodes correspond to positions in the parity check matrix, H, of the last ’1’ in some row of H. At these positions, all the bits required to perform the check become available and the syndrome can be computed. Survivors that fail the syndrome checks are discarded while the others are retained.

In the JVDD algorithm, the parity check nodes help to moderate the number of survivors in the trellis, by removing those survivors that violate a given syndrome check. Prior to the start of the parity checking stage, the number of survivors accumulates in the trellis. The later the onset of parity checking, the greater is the survivor growth in the preparity checking stage. It is therefore of interest to note that when codes such as random LDPC codes are used in a JVDD system, the number of survivors in the trellis accumulates exponentially for a large portion of the trellis. This is attributed to the fact that in the H matrix of a random code, the last ’1’ in each row of H are clustered towards the end of the matrix. This is depicted by the red pixels of Figure 2(a). A class of JVDD codes were thus proposed such that the last ’1’ in each row of H are distributed more evenly throughout the matrix.
III. JVDD CLASS OF CODES
The JVDD class of codes include the GDLD and VG codes, both of which are linear block codes. The VG codes are proposed in this paper as a more generalized version of the GDLD codes and it consist of 4 types of code design - 1) fixed row weight 2) proportionate row weight 3) fixed column weight and 4) proportionate column weight.
A. Gaussian Distribution Linear Diagonal Code
A GDLD code is designed such that the last ’1’s in each row of its H matrix follow a linear diagonal as shown in Figure 2(b) to enable the onset of parity checking in the JVDD algorithm to occur earlier and at a more regular interval as compared to the random LDPC code. This is achieved by defining a linear diagonal across H and placing the last ’1’s of each row on the diagonal. The remaining bits in each row are then placed to the left of the diagonal, with a single-sided Gaussian distribution of variance σ2. The number of bits to be placed to the left of the diagonal is given by the row weight, wrow, of the code. In the original formulation of GDLD codes [5][6], the linear diagonal was offset to the right by a certain number of pixels, dx, without changing the diagonal’s gradient. The purpose was

to prevent the last few bits in the codeword having too few checks protecting them, in the bottom right corner of H. If the diagonal were to pass through the bottom right corner of H, the resulting code performs poorly as the last few bits would be lightly protected, or not at all. Hence the necessity of the offset. For GDLD codes where the gradient of the diagonal does not change, small values of the offset result in these unprotected columns at the end of the codeword while too large values result in a delay in the onset of parity checking, leading to a higher computational complexity for the JVDD.
B. Varying Gradient Code
A VG code is an extension of GDLD code, in that it splits the offset value into 2 components: dx, a horizontal offset at the top left corner and dy, a vertical offset at the bottom right corner of the H matrix. With this modification, the gradient of the linear diagonal is now allowed to vary such that the last columns in H are well protected and the onset of parity checking is allowed to occur earlier. In addition to the modification to the offset parameter described above, dx is defined in the same way as that for the GDLD whereas the definition of wrow and σ varies according to the specific type of VG code, of which there are four:
1) Fixed Row Weight Code: wrow and σ are defined in the same manner as that for GDLD codes. The only difference between this code and GDLD code is in the variable gradient of its main diagonal.
2) Proportionate Row Weight Code: Alternatively, instead of assigning a constant value to wrow, the number of ’1’s in each row of H can be defined as a percentage of the row width, drow. Here, drow refers to the distance between a point on the diagonal and the 1st column of H for a given row. With such design, the presence of any overly dense regions that are seen in H matrices of fixed row weight codes can be avoided.
3) Fixed Column Weight Code: For fixed and proportionate row weight codes, the ’1’ bits are added to the H matrix on a row by row basis to the left of the diagonal. Alternatively, these bits can also be added on a column by column basis below

V. SIMULATIONS AND RESULTS
In order to perform the optimization, FER and Complexity values for the JVDD have to be obtained experimentally through computer simulations. First the code with 4 values of the control parameters being tested is generated at a fixed codeword length, N, and code rate, R. The generator, G, and H matrices are obtained and a coded ISI channel is simulated with the code. The JVDD receives the output of the ISI channel model and the detected user bit sequence is subsequently obtained and FER computed. The average number of survivors in the JVDD is also returned by the algorithm. A DOE table is constructed consisting of all variations of the 4 control parameters and the resultant cost function for those values.

A. Comparison of optimized JVDD codes
For each of the proposed VG codes, a comparison was first done between a Gaussian and a uniform distribution that is used to determine the location of the bits in the H matrices during the code design process. 8 types of codes were optimized in total (4 different VG codes each designed with either a Gaussian or uniform distribution). The optimization results indicates that both distribution types give rise to codes with an almost identical performance and complexity. This is reflected in Figure 5(a) where the distribution types are compared for proportionate row weight and proportionate column weight codes. The optimized codes obtained using Gaussian distribution turns out to have a large σ value. With a large σ, the Gaussian distribution approaches a uniform distribution which therefore explain the near identical performance seen. Either distribution can thus be used for the design of JVDD codes. The remaining part of this paper however uses the Gaussian distribution only. Figure 5(b) compares the 4 optimized VG codes designed with Gaussian distribution. On this plot, the optimum proportionate row weight is shown to be the best as it drops the fastest and the lowest. The optimized proportionate column weight code does almost as well. The optimized fixed row weight/fixed column weight codes displayed the worst performance out of the 4 VG codes. The H matrices of these optimum codes are illustrated in Figure 6 and their design parameters are listed in Table I. The optimum H matrices for proportionate row weight/proportionate column weight code displayed a good distribution of ’1’s that is not too dense or too sparse and adequate protection of bits in every column.
B. Performance and complexity of JVDD with optimum codes
Having optimized the VG codes, the performance and complexity of the JVDD is then evaluated with the 2 best performing VG codes (proportionate row weight and column weight) and the GDLD code over varying SNR and R. In Figure 7(a), the performance of JVDD with these codes (at N=512/1024 and R=0.90) are simulated at the same level of complexity but over various channel SNR. On this plot, the performance of the conventional iterative detector/decoder with random LDPC code of wcol=4 is included as a performance benchmark for the JVDD. VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, VG codes are proposed as a better alternative to the existing GDLD codes initially designed for the JVDD. The VG codes consist of fixed row weight, proportionate row weight, fixed column weight and proportionate column weight codes.
