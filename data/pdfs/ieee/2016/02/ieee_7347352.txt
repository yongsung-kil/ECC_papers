Finite-Length Algebraic Spatially-Coupled Quasi-Cyclic LDPC Codes
Keke Liu, Member, IEEE, Mostafa El-Khamy, Senior Member, IEEE, and Jungwon Lee, Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—The replicate-and-mask (R&M) construction of finite-length spatially-coupled (SC) LDPC codes is proposed in this paper. The proposed R&M construction generalizes the conventional matrix unwrapping construction and contains it as a special case. The R&M construction of a class of algebraic spatially coupled (SC) quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes over arbitrary finite fields is demonstrated. The girth, rank, and time-varying periodicity of the proposed R&M SC QC LDPC codes are analyzed. The error rate performance of finite-length nonbinary algebraic SC QC LDPC codes is investigated with window decoding. Compared to the conventional unwrapping construction, it is found through numerical simulations that the R&M construction resulted in SC QC LDPC codes with better block error rate performance and lower error floors. With a flooding schedule decoder, it is shown that the proposed R&M algebraic SC QC LDPC codes have better error performance than the corresponding LDPC block codes and random SC codes. The R&M construction of irregular SC QC LDPC codes is demonstrated. It is shown that low-complexity regular puncturing schemes can be deployed on these codes to construct families of rate-compatible irregular SC QC LDPC codes with good performance.
Index Terms—LDPC codes, algebraic LDPC codes, quasi-cyclic codes, spatially-coupled LDPC codes, girth, PEG algorithm, ratecompatible codes, HARQ.

Quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes [10] have been standardized for various communication systems and are appearing in recent data storage products, due to their low complexity and highly parallelizable encoding [11] and decoding [12]. Extensive simulation results [13], [14], [15] have shown that well-designed QC LDPC codes can perform as well as (or even better than) unstructured random LDPC codes. Simulation results have also shown that non-binary protograph LDPC block codes can outperform their binary counterparts [16].
Due to the merits of both QC LDPC codes and SC LDPC codes, this paper addresses the construction of SC LDPC codes with a QC structure, over arbitrary finite fields. Such codes are called SC QC LDPC codes. There are not many prior works addressing the construction of SC QC LDPC codes. Mitchell et al. presented a technique that improves the upper bound on the minimum Hamming distance of members of the QC sub-ensembles [17]. The progressive edge growth (PEG) algorithm [18], which usually requires a significantly long computer search, has been modified to construct binary SC QC LDPC codes [19]. Recently, M. Hagiwara et al. proposed a construction of SC QC LDPC codes for quantum error correction [20], which is, to the best knowledge of the authors, the only existing work that constructs SC QC LDPC codes using a deterministic approach. However, this construction is specific to a small class of special matrices, and is not flexible enough for arbitrary code design.
Besides the above-mentioned works, a natural method of constructing SC QC LDPC codes is to unwrap a QC LDPC block code [21], [2]. We will refer to such codes as unwrapped SC QC LDPC codes. The unwrapping construction can preserve many structural properties of the underlying block code, such as the girth and the minimum distance [21], [22]. However, we discovered that with window decoding (WD), unwrapped SC QC LDPC block codes could start to show error floors at a block error rate (BLER) of 10−2, which is the operating BLER of many wireless communication systems. This is undesirable since the throughput of many communication systems, especially those deploying hybrid automatic repeat request (HARQ) mechanisms, is determined by the BLER rather than the bit error rate (BER). This issue will be addressed in this paper.

The crux of the R&M construction is replicating the parity-check (PC) matrix of an LDPC block code and masking it with a designed masking matrix. Compared to the conventional unwrapping approach, we show that the proposed R&M construction provides more flexibility in the selection of the code parameters, such as the rate and degree distributions, and the optimization of the constructed codes. The R&M SC LDPC codes can also be guaranteed to have certain girth properties. The conventional unwrapping technique is shown to be a special instance of our new construction approach. Then, we combine our new R&M construction approach with the classical algebraic construction of QC LDPC codes [13], [14], [15], [23] to present a general algebraic construction of SC QC LDPC codes over arbitrary finite fields. In contrast to the previous PEG-based constructions [19], our construction of SC QC LDPC codes avoids time-consuming computer search. Compared to the construction in [20], our construction results in a much larger class of SC QC LDPC codes with a more flexible parameter selection. We illustrate the R&M construction of different SC QC LDPC codes by examples. It is shown that the constructed regular R&M SC QC LDPC codes outperform both the corresponding LDPC block codes and random SC codes with the flooding schedule decoding (FSD) [24]. For the case of WD [9], [8], [25], it is shown that regular R&M SC QC LDPC codes outperform the corresponding unwrapped SC QC LDPC codes, especially at the error floor region. We also show that the R&M SC QC LDPC codes are robust to puncturing, and hence we construct families of rate-compatible (RC) SC QC LDPC codes suitable for wireless HARQ systems with low complexity regular puncturing. For that, we also demonstrate how to construct irregular SC QC LDPC codes with the R&M construction. To our knowledge, this is the first time irregular SC QC LDPC codes are constructed.
This paper is one of few works addressing the finite length construction of non-binary spatially coupled LDPC codes and investigating their performance on the binary input additive white Gaussian noise channel (BI-AWGNC). This work shows that SC codes can have a better error-rate performance than their block code counterparts at finite length. This work has been partially motivated by their promising asymptotic length performance. The asymptotic performance of non-binary SC codes has been studied on the binary erasure channel (BEC), where it has been shown that the threshold saturation phenomenon also holds for non-binary SC LDPC codes on the BEC [26]. We numerically showed in [1] that the threshold saturation phenomenon also holds for (particular ensembles of) non-binary SC LDPC codes on the BI-AWGNC using a flooding-schedule decoder. Specifically, it has been numerically shown that for the (3,6)-regular block code ensemble, as the spatial-coupling length increases, the belief propagation threshold of the spatially coupled non-binary code over GF(22) approaches an upper bound on the MAP threshold of the corresponding non-binary block code on the BI-AWGNC [1]. Moreover, it has been recently shown that WD of non-binary SC codes, on both the BI-AWGNC and the BEC, results in a negligible degradation in their decoding thresholds compared to the FSD if the window size is sufficiently large [25].

The rest of this paper is organized as follows. The unwrapping construction of SC LDPC codes is briefly reviewed in Section II. The general R&M construction of SC LDPC codes is presented in Section III, and the general algebraic construction of SC QC LDPC codes over arbitrary finite fields is discussed in Section IV. Certain characteristics of the R&M constructions are also proved in Sections III and IV. Various examples for the R&M construction of regular, irregular, and rate-compatible SC QC LDPC codes are shown in Section V, together with their FSD error rate performance. Their WD error rate performance is investigated in Section VI. Concluding remarks are made in Section VII.

In this section, we present a systematic construction of SC LDPC codes, referred to as the replicate and mask (R&M) construction. The proposed R&M construction is based on masking a replicated version of an LDPC block code using a masking matrix that is closely dependent on the target SC protograph. (The relation between the masking matrix and the SC protograph will be clarified in Section III-A.) Compared to the matrix unwrapping techniques, our R&M construction has more flexibility in the choice of the underlying block code, and has more degrees of freedom in optimizing the constructed SC LDPC codes. B. A General Guideline for Choosing the Masking Matrix and Underlying Block Code
The R&M process gives the PC matrix Hsc of a resultant SC LDPC code that can be specified entirely by the LDPC block code PC matrix H, and the parameters of the masking matrix W. In this subsection, we analyze how the selection of masking matrix W and the PC matrix H affects the girth and periodicity property of the resultant SC PC matrix Hsc.
In this paper, we mainly consider the construction of a SC LDPC code whose PC matrix is an array of permutation matrices and/or ZMs of the same size, say Q × Q, which is of the greatest interest in practical application. In this case, as explained in Section III-A, the masking matrix is determined by the design target parameters of the SC code, including SC protograph and lifting size, etc. Given the masking matrix, the selection of the block code PC matrix H will determine the final PC matrix Hsc of the SC code. A. Algebraic Construction of QC LDPC Codes Based on Finite Fields
Algebraic tools, such as finite fields, have been shown to be very effective in constructing QC LDPC codes with excellent overall performance [13], [15], [23], [14], [29]. In most algebraic constructions of binary QC LDPC codes, the PC matrix of a code is an array of circulant permutation matrices (CPMs) and/or ZMs over GF(2). Consider a finite field GF(q) and let α be a primitive element of GF(q). The construction of a quasi-cyclic PC matrix Hqc starts with a matrix B over GF(q) (called base matrix). Given an m × n matrix B = [bi, j ] over GF(q), Hqc can be constructed directly from the base matrix B by a (q − 1)-fold dispersion [13] of B as follows: for each entry bi, j in B, if bi, j = 0, then replace bi, j by the (q − 1) × (q − 1) ZM, otherwise let bi, j = αl with 0 ≤ l < q − 1, then replace bi, j by an (q − 1) × (q − 1) CPM whose first row has a single 1-component at the l-th element.
It follows from the above construction process that Hqc is uniquely specified by the base matrix B. It has been proved that a necessary and sufficient condition for the Tanner graph of Hqc to have a girth of at least 6 is that every 2 × 2 submatrix of B contains at least one zero entry or is non-singular [23]. This constraint on the 2 × 2 submatrices of B is referred to as the 2 × 2 submatrix constraint (SM-constraint). We call a matrix that satisfies the 2 × 2 SM-constraint over GF(q) a 2 × 2 SMconstrained matrix. Therefore, the construction of Hqc whose Tanner graph has a girth of at least 6 can be reduced to the construction of 2 × 2 SM-constrained base matrix B over GF(q). A detailed coverage of algebraic constructions of 2 × 2 SMconstrained base matrices can be found in [13], [15], [23], [14] and references therein.

B. A General R&M Algebraic Construction of SC QC LDPC Codes
Suppose B is an m × n 2 × 2 SM-constrained matrix over GF(q). The (q − 1)-fold dispersion of B results in an m × n array Hqc of (q − 1) × (q − 1) CPMs and/or ZMs. The null space of Hqc gives a QC LDPC block code whose Tanner graph has a girth of at least 6. As explained in Section III-A, if Hqc is chosen to be the PC matrix for the block code used in the R&M construction described in Algorithm 1, we can construct a binary matrix Wqc = [wqc,i, j ]0≤i<s(q−1),0≤ j<t (q−1) as the masking matrix, which is a s × t band diagonal array of (q − 1) × (q − 1) all-one matrices and/or ZMs, where s and t are two positive integers. Then, an SC QC LDPC PC matrix Hsc,qc, which is a band-diagonal array of (q − 1) × (q − 1) CPMs and/or ZMs, can be constructed by applying the R&M method described in Algorithm 1 using the block code PC matrix Hqc and the masking matrix Wqc. Since Hqc is constructed using the algebraic approach, the above process combines the R&M construction and the algebraic methods, which gives an algebraic construction of SC QC LDPC codes.
We can also observe another equivalent construction, which applies the R&M method on the base matrix. Note that Hqc can be completely specified by B. Consider a masking matrix Wbase obtained by replacing each (q − 1) × (q − 1) all-one matrix and each ZM in Wqc by 1 and 0, respectively. Firstly, we can perform the R&M process with the block code base matrix B and masking matrix Wbase. This results in a banddiagonal base matrix Bsc over GF(q). Then, we can apply the (q − 1)-fold dispersion on Bsc which gives exactly the same SC QC LDPC PC matrix Hsc,qc as mentioned above. To summarize, an equivalent R&M algebraic construction of SC QC LDPC codes is presented in Algorithm 2.

This subsection addresses the construction of ratecompatible (RC) structured SC LDPC codes suitable for incremental redundancy HARQ applications. The construction of rate-compatible LDPC codes for HARQ applications has been studied (cf. [36] and references therein) and that of RC SC LDPC codes has been recently addressed [39], [40]. In this subsection, we investigate the performance of RC algebraic SC QC LDPC codes of practical length (say 6k information block length in bits) constructed based on finite fields.
It is worth noting that punctured LDPC codes, to be transmitted on AWGN channels, must have good performance on both the AWGN and erasure channel. One of our motivations to construct structured SC LDPC codes is to benefit from the universality property of SC LDPC codes, where SC codes universally achieve the capacity of general BMS channels under belief propagation decoding [41]. Hence, RC families of SC LDPC codes obtained by puncturing mother codes are expected to be capacity achieving. Also, the girth properties enforced by the QC structure and the proposed R&M construction, will aid the recovery of the punctured nodes. Moreover, systematic puncturing schemes often result in optimized puncturing patterns which may have to be stored at the receiver and is not practical for mobile systems.
Hence, we show that RC SC QC LDPC codes can be derived by regular puncturing, which is similar to the approach adopted with 3GPP turbo codes, with minimal additional receiver space and computational complexity. VII. CONCLUSION AND REMARKS
This paper addresses the construction and analysis of finitelength binary and non-binary spatially-coupled (SC) quasicyclic (QC) LDPC codes. An ‘replicate-and-mask’ (R&M) algebraic construction of SC QC LDPC codes from an algebraic QC LDPC block code is proposed. It is shown that the R&M construction generalizes the conventional unwrapping construction of SC LDPC codes. Certain properties of the constructed R&M SC codes such as the girth, rank, and block periodicity are proved. It is verified, by numerical simulations on the AWGN channel, that the constructed algebraic R&M SC QC LDPC codes have better performance than their counterparts, such as the unwrapped algebraic SC QC LDPC codes, SC PEG LDPC codes, and block LDPC codes. R&M constructions of structured irregular SC QC LDPC codes are demonstrated and shown to have comparable performance to the 3GPP turbo codes in the waterfall region, as well as better performance at the error floor region. Also, this paper considers the design of rate-compatible families of algebraic SC QC LDPC codes suitable for incremental redundancy. It is shown that the constructed R&M SC LDPC codes are robust to regular puncturing on the AWGN channel. Performance comparisons with window decoding and flooding schedule decoding illustrated the potential decoding latency reductions and coding gains that can be achieved by adopting the proposed algebraic R&M SC QC LDPC codes.
