Abstract—Spatially coupled low-density parity-check block codes (SC-LDPC-BCs) are a class of LDPC codes in which certain ensembles achieve capacity under iterative belief propagation decoding. Prior work has considered these codes mostly from a protograph perspective. Following this viewpoint, our work presents new protographs for the design of protograph-based SCLDPC-BCs. A reciprocal channel approximation (RCA) analysis of the terminated protographs yields the asymptotic performance limits of the ensembles in terms of their iterative decoding thresholds. Our protographs possess the properties of linear minimum distance growth rate and excellent thresholds.
We consider the following channels in our work: binary-input additive white Gaussian noise channel (BI-AWGNC), binary erasure channel (BEC), and binary symmetric channel (BSC). Our work focuses on obtaining good designs, with two classes of ensembles being asymptotically regular, for all the three channels while minimizing rate loss.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, invented by Gallager [1], perform remarkably well with a suboptimal iterative decoding algorithm. The convolutional counterpart of LDPC codes, investigated by Felstro¨m and Zigangirov [2] and others, are called LDPC convolutional codes (also spatially coupled LDPC convolutional codes). Works of Tanner et al. [3], Pusane et al. [4], and others study LDPC convolutional codes, their properties, and how they compare against classical convolutional codes with maximum likelihood (ML) decoding and LDPC block codes with iterative decoding.
The terminated versions of LDPC convolutional codes, called spatially coupled LDPC block codes, have been of great interest lately due to their capacity-achieving thresholds. Lentmaier et al. [5] first observed in their work that terminated codes with an almost regular degree distribution have much better iterative decoding thresholds when compared to their fully regular LDPC block code ensemble counterparts. Moreover, the threshold of the terminated codes was close to the ML decoding threshold of the corresponding regular block codes. Most prior works design and analyze a certain class of spatially coupled LDPC ensembles; that is, based on protographs. The idea of using a protograph to construct an LDPC code is to obtain a large Tanner graph with the same computation graph as that of a small Tanner graph, which is otherwise called a protograph (for example, see Fig. 1). We propel forward in the same manner and design new protographs that have reduced rate loss and good rate-threshold tradeoffs over various channels. The protographs that we obtain possess the property of linear minimum distance growth rate. We also apply the threshold analysis to compute thresholds for protographs over the binary symmetric channel (BSC), which has not been considered in prior works.
The paper is organized as follows. Section II introduces the required notation and definitions. Section III contains our new protographs, their iterative decoding thresholds obtained using reciprocal channel approximation (RCA), and comparisons of rate loss and rate-threshold trade-off with various other ensembles in literature. We conclude in Section IV.
II. PRELIMINARIES AND NOTATION
The notation and terminology used in this paper follow the recent work of Mitchell et al. in [8]. An LDPC code is a linear block code with a sparse parity-check matrix H. A Tanner graph, attributable to the work of Tanner in [9], is a bipartite graph that corresponds to the parity-check matrix of an LDPC code. If H is of size m×n, then the Tanner graph is equivalently made up of m check nodes and n variable nodes that constitute the two “parts”. The rate r of the code satisfies r ≥ (n − m)/n, with equality whenever H is full-rank.
Definition 1 (Protograph and base matrix): A protograph, or a projected graph, is a small Tanner graph with nc check nodes and nv variable nodes. The design rate of a protograph is R 1−nc/nv. First described by Thorpe in [10], protographs can be used as “templates” to construct large LDPC codes. We

Definition 2 (Lifting): Given a protograph, we define the two-step copy-and-permute process, also known as lifting, to obtain an LDPC code as follows. The protograph is first replicated M times, yielding M disconnected copies of the graph. Then, the connections to the check nodes of the set of M edges (across the M replicas) obtained from an edge of a variable-check node pair in the original protograph are permuted among the M copies of the corresponding variablecheck node pair. The same is performed independently for every set of M edges. M is called the lifting factor.
The second step of lifting connects the M copies of the protograph such that the resulting LDPC code has the same degree distribution as the protograph. The code has M nc check nodes and M nv variable nodes and a rate r ≥ R.
Definition 3 (Ensemble of a protograph): With the lifting factor being M , the set of all LDPC codes obtainable via all possible edge permutations (in the second step) from a protograph is collectively called the ensemble of a protograph.
There are several reasons as to why it is convenient to design LDPC codes using a protograph. Foremost is that the iterative decoding threshold analysis for the ensemble can be performed at the level of the base matrix. Papers that have pioneered this approach include the works of Divsalar et al. [11], Dolecek et al. [12], and Chen et al. [13]. Second is that it is natural to use a protograph to design practical and efficiently implementable LDPC codes. A class of codes called quasi-cyclic LDPC (QCLDPC) codes is relevant in this context as its designs often involve protographs (see, for example, work of Fossorier [14] and others). Lastly, protograph ensembles are amenable to the study of minimum distance growth rate, girth, asymptotic weight spectra of codewords, and asymptotic spectra of noncodewords such as stopping sets, trapping sets, and absorbing sets. Works that perform these analyses include the ones of Abbasfar et al. [15], Divsalar et al. [11], Smarandache and Vontobel [16], Dolecek et al. [12], and Mitchell et al. [8].
A. Edge Spreading to Obtain an SC-LDPC Base Matrix
There are two variants of SC-LDPC codes: spatially coupled LDPC convolutional codes (SC-LDPC-CCs) and spatially coupled LDPC block codes (SC-LDPC-BCs). We focus on the design of base matrices for the latter. The design procedure can be captured using a formal definition as follows. The definition encompasses all protograph designs discussed in this paper.
Definition 4 (Edge spreading for spatial coupling [8]): Consider matrices B(t), t ∈ Z each of size bc × bv. These matrices may represent protographs themselves. Let w > 0 be an integer that denotes the coupling width of the ensemble. Suppose variable node vj(t) in B(t) is connected to ci(t) through Bi,j(t) edges (Bi,j(t) is the element in the ith row and jth column of B(t)). We connect the Bi,j(t) edges emanating from variable node vj(t) at time t over w + 1 check nodes

ci(t), ci(t + 1), . . . , ci(t + w). This edge spreading operation is then independently performed over all t ∈ Z in the same or

a different manner to obtain a convolutional protograph.

C. Iterative Decoding Thresholds and Wave-like Convergence
The asymptotic analysis of iterative decoding, first derived by Richardson and Urbanke in [17] as density evolution (DE), gives the threshold or the minimum channel parameter required for an ensemble to achieve reliable transmission. The analysis tracks the average bit-error probability of variable nodes and finds the channel parameter required for the average bit-error probability to approach zero with increasing iterations. This is done assuming an infinite graph drawn with the degree distribution of the underlying ensemble. At largeenough block-lengths and iterations, codes from the ensemble, on an average, will have an average bit-error probability close to zero at any channel parameter greater than the threshold.

III. NEW PROTOGRAPHS AND THEIR ITERATIVE DECODING THRESHOLDS (INCLUDING RESULTS FOR BSC)

A. Thresholds Over BSC: C(J, K, L) Ensemble
The C(J, K, L) ensemble (Definition 8) has been discussed extensively in the literature. C. Protographs with Multiple Edges Between Node Pairs
The symmetric ensemble that we introduced and analyzed for simplicity, which is asymptotically regular, has a good performance in terms of its thresholds over all the three channels. We now allow for having multiple edges between variable-check node pairs with simple edge spreading methods. Allowing multiple edges increases the density of the base matrix while the design rate remains unchanged, and one would expect that this process leads to better thresholds.
Protograph SC-LDPC-BC ensembles with multiple edges have been considered previously in the works of Ashrafi and Pusane [21] and Ashrafi et al. [22], with 1/2 as the design rate. In the former work, the authors search for the asymptotically regular ensemble with the best threshold among all possible ensembles with a variable node degree of 3 (almost all check nodes have a degree of 6), and this is done for the cases of w = 1 and w = 2. the best irregular ensemble with an upper bound of 9 on the variable node degrees, and this is done again for w = 1 and w = 2. The search for the best ensemble increases quickly in complexity with the increase of w and/or the maximum variable node degree. The works mentioned here use DE with window decoding to find the thresholds. In our work, we consider the full-block analysis of the ensembles and obtain thresholds using RCA for the case when w = 1.
We set our target design rate R = 1/2. The code search problem can be described as follows. Let B(t) = B, and let the edge spreading be identical for every t. With w as the coupling width, we need to find matrices B0, B1, . . . , Bw that yield the best threshold value for a value of L that is large enough and a set number of maximum iterations of the RCA algorithm. The resulting base matrix will have the same structure as B[0,L−1] in (1), but with matrices B0, B1, . . . , Bw that possibly have values greater than 1. Fig. 5 depicts what we intend to achieve. The protograph corresponding to B has 1 check node and 2 variable nodes. The conditions on the degree distribution of this protograph determine the search space. Let B have d1 and d2 edges between the check node and the two variable nodes respectively. Given this, we need to figure out the values d11, d12, d21, d22 that yield the best iterative decoding threshold asymptotically.
We consider BI-AWGN channel. The results we present here are for L = 64 and 100000 iterations. We determined, through an exhaustive search, the best ensembles under two constraints:
1) When d1 = d2 = d ≤ 10, which forces the ensemble to be asymptotically regular, the best threshold obtained is channel SNR∗ = 0.2161dB for d11 = 4, d12 = 1, d21 = 3, and d22 = 2. Note that the Shannon limit for R = 1/2 is 0.187dB and for RL = 0.4922 is 0.083dB. Fig. 4 shows the rate-threshold trade-off for this (sky blue). We obtained for this ensemble ε∗ = 0.4972 and p∗ = 0.1088.
2) Fixing d2 = 2 and allowing d1 ≤ 10, the best threshold possible is channel SNR∗ = 0.1974dB for d11 = 8, d12 = 2, d21 = 1, and d22 = 1. We obtained for this ensemble ε∗ = 0.4983 and p∗ = 0.1096.
The second case above shows the advantage of allowing multiple edges between a variable-check node pair in the protograph. The threshold obtained is not far away from the Shannon limit and the rate loss is minimal.
IV. CONCLUSION
The paper presented spatially coupled LDPC block code ensembles that are capacity-achieving with good rate-threshold

trade-offs. The paper also considered the problem of designing
base matrices with multiple edges between variable-check
node pairs when the coupling width w = 1. For future work, we intend to analyze the case w = 2 and investigate whether a
single ensemble achieves the best threshold over all the three
symmetric channels considered in this paper.
