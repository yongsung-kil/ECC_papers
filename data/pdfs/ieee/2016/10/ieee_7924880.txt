Analysis and Elimination of Short Cycles in LDPC Convolutional Codes 
Ziqin Su, Qiaoyong Qiu, Hua Zhou

Abstract-Time-invariant low-density parity-check convolu­
tional codes (TI LDPC-CCs) can be represented by a polynomial­
domain parity-check matrix derived from the corresponding 
quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC block codes (LDPC-BCs), while time­
varying (TV) LDPC-CCs can be obtained by unwrapping the 
parity-check matrices of LDPC-BCs. The cycle enumerators for 
TI and TV LDPC-CCs are compared. Based on the analysis of 
the graphical structures of short cycles in HT (D), we introduce 
a method of designing the polynomial syndrome former matrix 
HZ; R(D) for LDPC-CCs. It eliminates short cycles and shows 
improved decoding performance on an additive white Gaussian 
noise (AWGN) channel with lower bit error ratio (BER) curves. 
Keywords-LDPC; convolutional codes; LDPC-BC 
I. INTRODUCTION 
Low-density parity-check convolutional codes (LDPC-CCs), 
also known as spatially-coupled codes, were first introduced 
in [1]. They outperform the corresponding LDPC block codes 
(LDPC-BCs [2]) with better iterative decoding threshold [3] 
and equivalent computational complexity [4]. They are suitable 
for practical implementation with continuous transmission and 
variable length by appropriately terminating the codes [5]. 
LDPC-CCs are defined by the semi-infinite syndrome for­
mer matrix denoted by HT which is the transposed version of 
the time-domain parity-check matrix. In terms of the structure 
of the syndrome former matrix, LDPC-CCs can be separated 
into two categories, i.e., time-invariant (TI) and time-varying 
(TV) ones. TI LDPC-CCs can be derived from Quasi-Cyclic 
(QC) LDPC-BCs [6] and protographs [7], [8], while TV 
LDPC-CCs can be obtained by unwrapping the time-domain 
syndrome former matrices of LDPC-BCs [1], [9]. 
The sum-product algorithm (SPA), is a type of belief prop­
agation (BP) decoding scheme, applys Bayes' rule locally and 
iteratively on the Tanner graph of an LDPC code to calculate 
the approximate marginal a posteriori (MAP) probabilities of 
it. If the Tanner graph of the code contains no cycles i.e., the 
graph is of tree structure, then it can be proved that the SPA 
computes marginal posterior probabilities exactly. However, 
LDPC codes of interest contain cycles in the Tanner graphs, 
which degrades the decoding performance because a message 
sent by a node along a cycle propagates back to it after 
several decoding iterations, which destroys the assumption of 
independence among messages of the SPA and prevents the 
SPA from converging. In addition, short cycles forming the 
so-called trapping sets results in an error floor. Intuitively, 
the larger the girth (length of the shortest cycle), the better 
the decoding performance will be. However, due to the finite 
block length and the practical requirement for structured codes 
(e.g., regular or partial regular QC LDPC codes), there are 
many cycles unavoidable. 
In terms of the decoding performance of LDPC-CCs, dis­
tance spectrum (free distance and enumerators for low weight 
codewords) and cycle properties (girth and enumerators for 
short cycles) are two important coding characteristics, which 
are related to the existence of an error floor and the conver­
gence speed of the SPA [10]. In [9], [11], and [12], cycle 
properties of TV and TI LDPC-CCs have been independently 
analysed. In this paper, we first compare the work on the cycle 
enumerators for TI and TV LDPC-CCs. It verifies that the 
cycles property of TV LDPC-CCs is superior to that of TI 
LDPC-CCs. Then, we introduce a method of designing TI 
LDPC-CCs by modifying the polynomial syndrome former 
matrix of a given TI LDPC-CC, which successfully eliminate 
the short cycles in the Tanner graph. The proposed code has 
the same syndrome former memory as the given code, and 
the simulation results show that the it improves the decoding 
performance with lower BER curves. 
The rest of the paper is organised as follows: In Section 
II, we review the definition of the TI and TV LDPC-CCs. 
Section III explains how a cycle is formed in the polynomial 
syndrome former matrix and gives the formal definition of 
cycles. Section IV compares the cycle enumerators between 
TI LDPC-CCs and TV LDPC-CCs. In Section V, a method of 
eliminating short cycles by modifying a given TI LDPC-CC 
is presented. Finally, Section VI concludes the paper.

than once. The length of the shortest cycle is called the girth 
of an LDPC-CC and is denoted by g.

V. A METHOD OF ELIMINATING SHORT CYCLES IN 
TIME-INVARIANT LDPC-CCs 
In this section, we introduce an approach to eliminate short 
cycles by modifying the polynomial syndrome former matrix 
of a given LDPC-CC which is obtained from the corresponding 
QC LDPC-BC. We have demonstrated in Section III that 
the generation of a cycle depends on the power subtractions 
between any two monomials from the same column of UT (D). 
Intuitively, reducing the diversity of the subtractions (the 
amount of different subtractions in value), to some extend 
can prevent the accumulative subtractions sum (ASS) equaling 
zero. On the other hand, large ensemble of different substations 
between monomials in the same column provides more options 
for the ASS, which increases the probability to make ASS 
be zero. Note that, subtractions mentioned here can be either 
positive or negative, it depends on the vertical direction of the 
edge passing. 
Given a K x J polynomial syndrome former matrix of an 
LDPC-CC derived from the corresponding QC LDPC-BC, a 
cycle-reduced version U%R(D) is obtained by modifying the 
matrix UT (D) according to the steps as follows: 
• Step 1: select the column with the syndrome former 
memory ms in UT (D) as the first column of U%R(D). 
• Step 2: copy the first column of U%R(D) and cyclically 
shift it upwards by one position. Set the shifted column 
as the second column of U%R(D). 
• Step 3: copy the second column of U%R(D) with the 
power of each monomial subtracted by the syndrome 
former memory ms. Set this modified column as the third 
column of U%R(D). 
• Step 4: apart from the columns selected in Step 1, choose 
the column with the maximum power in the rest of 
UT (D) and repeat the above three steps as the following 
columns of U%R(D) until the number of columns of 
U%R(D) reaches J. 
Note that, an alternative order of the J columns of U%R(D) 
defends the same code. The modified code with U%R(D) has 
the same syndrome former memory as the original unmodified 
one defined by UT (D). 
As a tool, we use the cycle tracking algorithm (details in 
[11]) to calculate the number of cycles in UT (D) and U%R(D) 
for several codes as shown in Table IV. For example, in terms 
of the (3,5,21) code defined by the 5 x 3 polynomial matrix 
with syndrome former memory ms = 21, the enumerators for 
cycles of length 8, lO, and 12 are eliminated from 11, 62, and 
351 for UT(D) to 4, 30, and 264 for U%R(D), respectively . 
Similarly, decreasing in cycle enumerators are observed for 
the (5,6,29), (3,6,29), and (3,5,57) codes. 
Though the proposed method can eliminate short cycles, 
the structure of U%R(D) also shows its disadvantage. As 
the value of ms increases, the code defined by UT (D) itself 
does not contain short cycles with length smaller than 12, and 
the reduction in large cycles of U%R(D) becomes trivial. In 
addition, the modified structure generates some extra cycles 
of length 10 when the method is applied to the (3,5,126), 
(3,5,134), and (3,5,204) codes. These extra cycles of length 
10 are structurally unavoidable due to the correlation of 
columns in U%R(D). The structure of the unavoidable cycle

VI. CONCLUSION 
In this paper, the cycle enumerators for time-invariant (TI) 
and time-varying (TV) LDPC-CCs are compared. It shows 
that the TV codes are superior to the TI ones with respect 
to the short cycle enumerators. Based on the analysis of the 
graphical structure of short cycles, we introduce a method of 
designing the matrix H%R(D) by modifying the polynomial 
syndrome former matrix UT (D) of a given LDPC-CC. When 
the code defined by UT (D) contains short cycles, the proposed 
modified code with U%R(D) can eliminate short cycles and 
improve the decoding performance on an AWGN channel with 
lower BER curves. The future work will focus on the method 
of designing robust polynomial structure for LDPC-CCs using 
the cycle tracking algorithm, e.g., 1) improve the ability of 
eliminating the large cycles; 2) searching for an LDPC-CC 
with the cycle enumerators minimised (or optimised).
