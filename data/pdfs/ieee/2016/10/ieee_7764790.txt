The Design of Optimized Fast Decoding Protograph LDPC Codes
Thuy Van Nguyen† and Hieu T. Nguyen‡ †Falculty of Infomation Techonology, Posts and Telecommunications Institute of Technology, Hanoi, Vietnam
‡Department of Electrical Engineering, Faculty of Engineering Science and Technology The Artic University of Norway NO-8505 Narvik, Norway Email: thuynv@ptit.edu.vn, hieu.nguyen@uit.no

Abstract— This paper presents a simple method of designing a protograph-based LDPC codes which has excellent performance with a small number of decoding iterations. In addition to designing LDPC codes based on the iterative decoding threshold as before, we add one more dimension into the design process which is a predefined number of decoding iterations. Simulation results show that the proposed codes have better performance than state-of-the-art protograph codes while the number of decoding iterations is small.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] have been an exciting topic in the coding community due to their capacity approaching performance. In most practical applications, quasi-cyclic (QC) structured LDPCs [2] whose parity check matrices are built by using circulant structures, are widely used because of their linear encoding and decoding complexity. One type of QC-LDPC codes is protograph codes [3] whose parity check matrices are built from a protograph, a small graph that contains a few number of nodes. Protograph codes have demonstrated very good thresholds with low encoder complexity as well as fast decoding [4], [3]. Because of their unique simplicities and representation, protograph codes have been received a great attention and have been investigated for many communication applications recently [5].
When designing of a LDPC code, there are two fundamental issues that need to address, i.e. good performance (both in water fall and error floor regions) and low decoding complexity. Currently, most of LDPC designs were only focused on finding the codes with iterative decoding thresholds that are as close to the capacity limits as possible based on either density evolution [1] or EXIT chart [6]. By using these methods, resulted codes usually need a large number of decoding iterations [3], [7] in order to provide satisfactory performance. Such high decoding iteration number essentially produces a high complexity decoder, which limits the applications of protograph codes. To address this issue, we add an additional dimension to design a code with a good performance using a small number of decoding iterations. This design dimension is represented by a predefined number of decoding iterations.
In this paper, we propose a design framework that can produce good protographs with the constraint of a given number of decoding iterations. The issue is particularly important

to many practical applications, in which their resources are limited. Our design approach is based on modified PEXIT chart [6] with an extra constraint on a predefined number of decoding iterations. We found that the optimized codes based on both number of decoding iterations and decoding thresholds have better performance with a small number of decoding iterations than the current design methods in the literature.
We briefly review past works on protograph code designs in the following. One of the first protograph designs was proposed by Divsalar et al. [3] in which authors had designed several family of good protograph codes. These codes have typical minimum distance that grows linearly with blocklength, and iterative decoding thresholds that approache the Shannon limits on binary-input additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels. Then Nguyen et al. [7] extended the works of Divsalar [3] to a more general framework that produced a better protograph code and, in the first time, generated ratecompatible protograph codes in a wide range of rates, all are capacity approaching. One of the closest works to our results is a recent work of Uchikawa [8], in which the author designed non-punctured protograph codes having good performance in the small number of decoding iteration regimes.
Our contribution is a design method that produces an optimized protograph code with a predefined number of the decoding iterations. The design will be a guideline to produce the best protograph codes for delay sensitive applications.
The paper is organized as follows. Section II introduces the concept of a protograph code and its iterative decoding threshold behavior in two types of protograph codes: punctured and non-punctured codes. Section III describes the protograph design process for these two types of protograph codes. Section IV provides numerical results of proposed codes in the AWGN channel. Finally, Section V concludes the paper.

A protograph-based LDPC code, so-called a protograph code, is a larger derived graph built by applying a “copy-and-permutation” operation on a protograph. To estimate the performance of a LDPC code, an iterative decoding threshold of a protograph is one of the most important parameters of LDPC codes. This is the minimum channel quality that supports reliable iterative decoding of asymptotically large LDPC codes built from the protograph. Currently, most of the LDPC code designs focus on optimizing this decoding threshold, producing LDPC codes that usually require a large number of decoding iterations to have good performance. However, practical systems usually have limited bandwidth and power resources which cannot support such a large number of decoding iterations. As a result, practical applications of capacity-approaching codes are limited. In the following, we are going to investigate two main types of highperforming protograph codes in the state-of-the-art, which are two subjects studied in this paper: punctured and nonpunctured protograph codes.

A. Punctured Protograph
One of the most popular protograph designs was proposed by Divsalar et al. in [3] in which they invented the family of accumulate-repeat-4-jagged-accumulate (AR4JA) codes, which have typical minimum distance that grows linearly with block size, and iterative decoding thresholds approaching the Shannon limit on binary-input additive white Gaussian noise (BI-AWGN) channels. The structure included a degree-one variable node connected with a highly connected punctured variable node. Extending this structure, Nguyen et al. [7] proposed a reference design to produce a protograph code which has the lowest iterative decoding threshold and linear minimum distance growth property which facilitates the low

error performance. This design produced one of the best performing protograph codes so far in the literature [7], [10]. Since these codes were mostly optimized for iterative decoding thresholds, they required a large number of decoding iterations in order to have the best performance. B. Nonpunctured Protograph

These above capacity approaching protograph codes used punctured codes. These untransmitted (punctured) nodes might slow down the decoder convergence, resulting a large number of decoding iterations needed to decode successfully. Keeping that argument in mind, Uchikawa [8] studied a design of non-punctured protograph codes that has better performance in a small number of decoding iterations than the rate-1/2 NND code reported in [7]. The idea of the design proposed in [8] was using the non-punctured protograph structure, and was optimized based on decoding threshold only the same as in [7], [3]. C. Number of Decoding Iteration vs. Decoding Threshold
In this subsection, we investigate the behavior of decoding threshold of protograph codes with respect to the number of decoding iterations. Decoding threshold reflects a limit at which an iterative message passing decoder is able to decode codewords successfully if the codeword length is large enough. If the codes have minimum distance linear growth property [3], [7], the decoding threshold could be a good measure to evaluate the code performance in comparison with other protograph codes.

III. PROPOSED PROTOGRAPH CODE DESIGN
In this section, a simple design approach is proposed to search for a good performing protograph which possesses a low iterative decoding threshold in AWGN channel with a predefined number of iterative decoding iterations. An iterative decoding threshold of a protograph with a predetermined number of decoding iteration is the minimum channel quality that supports reliable iterative decoding with a predetermined number of decoding iterations of asymptotically large LDPC codes built from the protograph. This new iterative decoding threshold is computed based on PEXIT method [6] with the predefined number of iterations. In our method, we only focus on graphs that provide good coding properties, such as a low

decoding threshold and the linear minimum distance growth property [3] that guarantees no error floor if random circulants are assigned when constructing the protograph code. In the following, we follow the design guideline described in [7], but with a predetermined number of iterations. In this paper, we use the two above protograph structures for our studies.

A. Punctured Protograph

As pointed out in [7], [3], a good protograph code, which has the decoding threshold close to the capacity, would have a degree-1 variable node, a high-degree punctured node, and a fraction of degree-2 variable nodes. We study the same structure as used in [7] which had 7 variable nodes, and 4 check nodes. Using the search space approach the same as the method used in [7] with the constraints of maximum 3 parallel edges and linear minimum distance growth property, we find the new code that is optimized to work with the maximum iteration number of 20 iterations only. The matrix contains 32 elements each showing how many parallel edges connect the respective variable node (column) and check node (row). Optimization over these 32 variables is computationally complex, therefore a search space reduction is needed to have a feasible solution.

Our protograph codes are derived from protographs in two lifting steps. First, the protograph is lifted by a factor of 4 using the progressive edge growth (PEG) algorithm [12] in order to remove all multiple parallel edges. Then, a second lifting of 64 or 85 for 8 × 4 proto-structures, and 85 for 7 × 4 structure respectively, using the PEG algorithm. This algorithm was applied to determine a circulant permutation of each edge class to avoid short-length cycles occurred with the desired code block length. In this section, protograph codes are simulated with the information blocklength of approximately 1024 bits. The decoder is a standard message passing decoder, in which the maximum number of iterations is set to 20. LLR clipping and other decoding parameters are set according to [13].
FER performance of our two new codes is shown in Figure 5. No error floor is observed down to FER 10−4. The figure shows that Code-3 outperforms Code-1 with a gaps of approximately 0.5 dB. These two codes have the same protograph structure. The same story with nonpunctured protograph structure, Code-4 is 0.23 dB better than Code-2. Furthermore, Code-4 has a gain of approximately 0.72 dB over Code-1. Performances of these codes are compared at F ER = 4 × 10−4. These performance gains highlight the effectiveness of our design method in the coding with a small number of decoding iterations. V. CONCLUSION This paper presents a simple method for designing protograph codes with the predefined number of decoding iterations. This is another domain that needs to be optimized in order to produce a good protograph code for practical applications with limited complexity and power resources.
