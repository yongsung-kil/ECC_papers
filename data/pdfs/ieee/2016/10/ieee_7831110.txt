Abstract—We design and implement a variable-rate nonconcatenated LDPC code with an adaptive power-consumption decoder using 28nm CMOS technology. Experimental results obtained from a fabricated 256Gb/s optical transceiver demonstrate that the proposed LDPC achieves a NCG of 11.4dB at BER=10−15 without error-floor.
I. INTRODUCTION
The telecommunications industry is migrating from static networks with little flexibility to software-defined optical networks (SDON) [1]. The motivation for this paradigm shift is that large increases in traffic cannot be supported by static increases in network capacity. Supporting SDON requires transceivers that support adaptation of at least the following parameters and functionality: modulation format, symbol rate, channel spacing, and forward error correction (FEC) overhead and coding gain [2]. By adapting these and other parameters it is possible to achieve excellent power dissipation/reach/data rate tradeoffs. The same transceiver can operate in metro, long haul, ultra long haul and submarine links with optimal performance and high power efficiency. Towards this end, FEC codes with medium-high net coding gains (NCG) (e.g., N CG = [9 − 13]dB) at a bit error rate (BER) of 10−15 and variable overhead (OH) (e.g., OH = [7−60]%) are mandatory (e.g., see [2]). The coding gain of soft-decision FEC (SDFEC) with iterative decoding increases as the number of iterations, Ni, increases. On the other hand, latency and power consumption grow with Ni. The number of iterations must be selected to provide a good tradeoff between complexity and performance (e.g., 5 to 15 iterations). For a given performance, the optimal (minimum) value of Ni depends on the channel conditions. The value of Ni required to achieve a certain performance reduces as the optical signal-to-noise ratio (OSNR) increases. Furthermore, the number of iterations may vary at a given OSNR as a result of different noise realizations. Therefore Ni should be dynamically adjusted to minimize power consumption and increase the FEC throughput (e.g., see [3] and references therein). Several techniques to stop the decoding process have been reported in the past literature [4]. However, experimental demonstration of their effectiveness to reduce power consumption in CMOS transceivers has not been discussed in previous work. This topic is important since power consumption of LDPC decoders depends not

only on the number of iterations, but also on other practical aspects such as cell placement and routing of the complex interconnections.
In this paper we present experimental results of a variablerate Regular Column Partition (RCP) Quasi-Cyclic (QC) NonConcatenated LDPC code with an adaptive decoding algorithm implemented in 28nm CMOS technology. NCGs as high as 11.4 dB can be achieved, while power consumption is adaptively optimized according to channel conditions. This NCG is 0.4 dB better than the triple concatenated FEC proposed in [5]. Furthermore, the overhead is programmable, supporting 20, 18.18, and 16.66% with codeword lengths of 24576, 26624, and 28672 bits, respectively. Our measurements at 256 Gb/s with 16QAM demonstrate that power consumption can be optimized with the proposed strategy achieving, e.g., a reduction of ∼35% when the pre-FEC BER reduces from 0.025 to 0.012.
II. ADAPTIVE POWER CONSUMPTION LDPC DECODER
As we mentioned, several techniques to stop the decoding process have been reported in the past. Typically, these schemes are built upon two critera:
C1. Stop when the decoder has converged or a maximum number of iteration has been reached.
C2. Stop when the syndrome is zero or a maximum number of iterations has been reached.
Criterion C1 avoids iterations once the soft-decision outputs are stable. This fact minimizes the probability of high values of Ni. When the errors in the received codeword can be corrected by the FEC, criterion C2 minimizes the number of iterations. On the other hand, when the errors in the received codeword cannot be corrected, the maximum value of Ni will be reached in C2-based decoders. We propose to use a combination of C1 and C2 since it provides the best approach to minimize the number of iterations and power consumption. In particular, the proposed LDPC decoder is designed to compute an average number of iterations per codeword (N ) of 9.6 at its BER threshold of 0.025. Since some received codewords will require more than N iterations, an input queue buffer is included to store the arriving codewords while the current one is being processed. The number of codewords to store is chosen to keep the error floor due to buffer saturation lower than 10−20. This approach achieves the performance of 29 iterations with hardware required to compute 9.6 average iterations. When inactive clock cycles take place due to early termination under good channel conditions, zero-data is inserted inside the data path to avoid unnecessary switching activity, and the hardware can be partially turned off.
Fig. 1 shows the top-level architecture of the decoder where V N , CN , and P stand for variable-node, check-node, and permutation blocks, respectively. This is a recursive partialparallel architecture based on the RCP technique proposed in [6]. To simplify message scheduling, recursive check-nodes and non-recursive variable-nodes are used. This is particularly useful here because the high rate of the implemented codes requires parity check matrices with significantly more columns than rows; i.e., more variable-node computations than checknode computations per iteration are required. Two codewords are simultaneously decoded to avoid inactive clock cycles resulting from the pipeline delay inside the processing loop; consequently increasing by 50% the achievable throughput. The latter is possible because the input stage of the checknode (CN -a) can process one codeword while the output stage (CN -b) is processing a second codeword. The two interlaced codewords decoding strategy not only avoids the pipeline penalty but also reduces by a factor 6 the timing requirement between the input and output stage of the check-node. This allows the use of a message serialization technique between the two stages which reduces the interconnection congestion caused by the feedback loop.

IV. CONCLUSIONS
A variable-rate LDPC code with adaptive low-power LDPC decoder has been designed and implemented in an ASIC using 28nm CMOS process. The fabricated FEC is able to provide an excellent power dissipation/reach/data rates tradeoffs as required by next-generation SDON.
