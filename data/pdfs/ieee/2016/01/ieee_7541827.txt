Information Bottleneck Graphs for Receiver Design

Email: {jan.lewandowsky,maximilian.stark,bauch}@tuhh.de

Abstract‚ÄĒA generic design method for low complexity receivers is presented. The method pairs factor graphs and the Information Bottleneck method in one framework. Consequently, the method is called Information Bottleneck Graphs. The main idea of Information Bottleneck Graphs is optimizing the flow of relevant information through the signal processors. In contrast to most topical receivers with high precision signal processing units, Information Bottleneck Graphs yield receivers purely working on unsigned integers. All signal processing degenerates to lookup operations in tables of integers. Information Bottleneck Graphs are exemplarily applied to develop a complete coherent receiver including analog-to-digital conversion, channel estimation and decoding of Low Density Parity Check codes that only works on unsigned integers. This receiver uses recently introduced discrete decoders for Low Density Parity Check codes.
I. INTRODUCTION AND MOTIVATION
Limited hardware resources and a required low power consumption restrict the applicability of complex signal processing algorithms in practical communication systems. In particular real time decoding of Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) codes is a bottleneck in wireless or fiber optical systems. While iterative belief propagation decoding offers very good performance [1], belief propagation decoding has some serious implementation issues. As one of the most fundamental issues soft decision algorithms, such as belief propagation decoding, require processing of log-likelihood-ratios (LLRs). LLRs are real numbers and need high resolution signal processing in the receivers hardware. In belief propagation decoders the processing of LLRs is computationally expensive due to required computation of mathematically complex node operations. As a consequence in practice often approximations for the exact node operations [2] are used on the expense of a performance degradation. Therefore, it is desirable to design receivers with high performance but coarse quantization. Recent progress on discrete LDPC decoders [3], [4], [5], [6] motivates consideration of completely integer based receivers: In discrete LDPC decoders all passed messages are unsigned integers and even more importantly mathematical operations are replaced by lookup tables with reasonable size. Surprisingly, the performance of discrete LDPC decoders for additive white Gaussian noise channels with quantized output is very close to belief propagation decoding [6]. The idea behind discrete LDPC decoders is optimizing the information flow [5] between the nodes of the decoder. In this paper we extend the idea of discrete LDPC decoders to a new generic method for design of complete receiver chains that focusses on the flow of relevant

information. As a further contribution we introduce a compact graphical model to visualize the flow of relevant information in a graph. The Information Bottleneck method [7] addresses the problem of representing an observed random variable Y by a coarsely quantized variable T . Conventional signal processing approaches try to approximate Y as precisely as possible with respect to a certain error measure, e.g. the mean squared error. In contrast, the Information Bottleneck method designs a compression such that the mutual information between T and an appropriately defined relevant random variable X is maximized. Various iterative algorithms for implementation of the Information Bottleneck method exist in the literature [7], [8]. Typically their output is a compression mapping Y ‚ąí‚Üí T that in the scope of this paper can be interpreted as a function implemented in a lookup table. The paper is organized as follows: Section II summarizes prerequisites from the theory of factor graphs and the Information Bottleneck method. Section III introduces the new idea of Information Bottleneck Graphs. Information Bottleneck Graphs extend factor graphs [9] by visualizing the flow of relevant information. As an example an Information Bottleneck Graph for a discrete receiver including analog-to-digital conversion (ADC), channel estimation, detection and LDPC decoding for Rayleigh fading channels is derived. A numerical performance evaluation and a comparison to a state-of-the-art receiver are provided in section IV. Section V concludes the paper.
II. PREREQUISITES
A. Factor graphs and the sum-product algorithm
A factor graph is a graphical visualization of the factorization ‚Ą¶(x0, x1, . . . , xM‚ąí1) = n ŌČn(Xn) of a global function ‚Ą¶(x0, x1, . . . , xM‚ąí1) into a product of local factors ŌČn(Xn), where all Xn ‚äā {x0, x1, . . . , xM‚ąí1}. All arguments xl of the global function are represented by variable nodes, typically drawn as a circle, while all local functions are visualized by factor nodes normally represented by a square. The factor nodes are connected to a variable node by an edge only if the respective variable is an argument of the corresponding local factor [9]. Factor graphs are for example applied in LDPC decoding using the sum-product algorithm [9], [10].

V. CONCLUSION
Information Bottleneck Graphs were introduced as a new generic receiver design method. Information Bottleneck Graphs pair factor graphs with the Information Bottleneck method and visualize the flow of relevant information in a factor graph. Furthermore, they inherently describe a lookup table based implementation of the designed system. In an exemplary application, Information Bottleneck Graphs were used to design a purely discrete and integer based receiver chain for Rayleigh fading channels including ADC, channel estimation, detection and LDPC decoding. The design of the involved lookup tables is very sophisticated and computationally expensive. The complexity is dominated by the design of the discrete LDPC decoder using the discrete density evolution scheme from [6]. We emphasize that lookup table generation can and should be performed offline. In contrast to construction complexity, the actual resulting receiver implementation is of very low complexity. The performance of the proposed fully discrete receiver can compete with state-of-theart implementations with high precision signal processing.
