Submarine Transmission Systems Using Digital Nonlinear Compensation and Adaptive Rate Forward Error Correction
Amirhossein Ghazisaeidi, Ivan Fernandez de Jauregui Ruiz, Laurent Schmalen, Patrice Tran, Christian Simonneau, Elie Awwad, Bogdan Uscumlic, Patrick Brindel, and Gabriel Charlet
(Top-Scored)

Abstract—We report on a full C + L-band erbium-doped fiber amplified (EDFA) submarine transmission experiment of 178 wavelength-division multiplexed channels of 49-GBd polarization multiplexed 16QAM signals, achieving 54.2 Tb/s after 6600 km, with a record per-channel average net bit rate of 304.5 Gb/s. Digital backpropagation and time-domain perturbative nonlinearity compensation were alternatively applied to all channels and their respective benefits, in terms of throughput increase, reach increase, and complexity, were addressed. Multiple-rate spatially coupled low density parity check forward error correction codes with a novel rate optimization algorithm were employed. The power consumption of the power feeding equipment of our EDFA-only amplification scheme was analyzed and compared with that of hybrid EDFA Raman amplification. We also provided numerical and theoretical performance analysis of nonlinear uncompensated/compensated systems.
Index Terms—Adaptive multi-rate FEC, digital backpropagation, PDM 16-QAM, perturbative nonlinear compensation, rate optimization, spatially-coupled LDPC, submarine transmission.
I. INTRODUCTION
S UBMARINE point-to-point fiber-optic wavelength division multiplexed (WDM) transmission systems comprise the backbone of the global data traffic. The throughput of these systems has been evolved from less than 1 Tb/s in 2000 to more than 50 Tb/s in 2014 research lab demonstrations [1]–[25] (cf., Fig. 1(a)). This achievement was due to the fortunate synthesis of a few technological advancements. On one hand the return to optical coherent detection allowed for high spectral efficiency advanced modulation formats, like 4, 8, or 16-quadrature amplitude modulation (QAM), and root-raised cosine (RRC) Nyquist pulse-shaping, as well as digital compensation of fiber chromatic dispersion, which, in particular, enabled dispersion-unmanaged

The most well-known NLC algorithm, which serves also as a reference benchmark to evaluate the performance of other NLC algorithms is digital backpropagation (DBP) [31]–[33]. Although DBP is prohibitively complex for hardware implementation, the more recent perturbative NLC (PNLC) [34]–[35], seems a promising candidate for future generations as its gain is only slightly inferior to DBP, but its implementation complexity is much lower. Employing some kind of NLC is indispensable in future fiber-optic transmission systems in order to approach the so-called “nonlinear Shannon capacity” [30].
As to per-channel symbol-rate, the general tendency in system evolution has been to constantly opt for higher rates, mainly in order to reduce the number of transponders per fiber, hence reducing the cost per bit. Note that at constant bandwidth efficiency (i.e., at constant symbol-rate to channel spacing ratio) single-channel NLC gain grows with increasing symbol-rate [26]–[29]. So, if NLC is present we have a second good reason to go for higher symbol-rates.
Since numerous WDM channels across the full C + L-band have different levels of performance, multi-rate FECs have been used in [1] and [21]–[25] to improve the overall system throughput. The idea is simple: we group the WDM channels according to their pre-FEC Q-factor and apply stronger FECs (i.e., ones with lower rate, equivalently, higher overhead) to the groups with lower performance.
In this work we report on a recent 178-channel C + L-band EDFA-only WDM submarine transmission experiment using 49 GBd 16-QAM channels with 50 GHz spacing, and with adaptive multi-rate SC-LDPC codes. Thanks to the availability of high sampling rate programmable digital-to-analog convertors (DAC), the net per-channel average bit-rate in this experiment for the reach 6600 km was 304.5 Gb/s. To the authors’ knowledge, this is the highest demonstrated net bit-rate in a full C + L-band transoceanic (i.e., > 6000 km) experiment to date. We also demonstrate average bit-rates of 290.4 and 270.8 Gb/s for 7920 and 9240 km reach. Fig. 1(a) illustrates the evolution of the single-mode submarine per-fiber throughput since 2000. III. MULTI-RATE FEC
In this work, like in [1], [22], and [24] we use a family of 52 left-terminated weakly coupled SC-LDPC codes with syndrome former memory equal to 1, and with code rates rm = 0.39 + 0.01 m, m = 1, . . . , 52. This is equivalent to overheads between 10% and 150%. For more details on the SC-LDPC codes please refer to [1], [24], and [37]–[43]. Note that contrary to [15], [16], [19]–[21], [23], and [25] we do not use costly iterative de-

mapping. For performance characterization of this code family, please refer to [1, Figs. 2, 6, and 11].
The Q-factor of WDM channels across the full C + L-band is not flat. In order to increase the system throughput we can apply a multi-rate FEC strategy adapted to the WDM channels performance, where WDM channels are partitioned into various groups according to their Q-factor. We apply FECs with lower rates (higher overheads) to groups with lower mean Q-factor and FECs with higher rates to groups with higher mean Q-factor, respectively. In order to maximize the throughput increase due to this adaptive multi-rate technique, an optimization problem must be solved. In [1] and [24] we introduced a heuristic algorithm that finds a local maximum. Here we introduce a novel algorithm that provably finds the global maximum. In the following sections, first we rigorously state the optimization problem and then describe the algorithm.

B. Rate Optimization Algorithm
The optimization problem stated in Section III-A has a geometric interpretation, which helps us in finding an efficient algorithm [1]. Let’s suppose for each of the N WDM channels, the maximum code rate that successfully decodes (i.e., without residual bit errors) that channel is found. The maximum rate can be found either by using the rate versus Q2 characteristic curve of the FEC family, or else by actually decoding each channel with different members of the FEC family. Note that if a FEC with rate ri successfully decodes a channel, then a FEC from the considered family with rate rj < ri does so as well, since the performance of the FECs in the proposed family is a monotonic function. Hence, the maximum rate can be found by doing a bisection search over R. For each channel we need six trials to find the maximum code rate among the 52 members of R. IV. RESULTS
In this section first we describe the setup. Then, we present experimental results followed by some numerical results.
A. Setup
Fig. 3(a) illustrates the transmitter test comb. We have used eight tunable laser sources (TLS). The TLSs were divided into even and odd groups, each group including four sources. Even and odd groups were separately modulated by two polarizationmultiplexed IQ modulators (IQ-Mod). Each IQ-Mod was driven by a DAC operating at 88 GSamples/s. IQ-Mod outputs passed through polarization scramblers (PS), finally, PS outputs of odd and even groups were coupled together to make the modulated test comb.
The programmable DAC memories were loaded by 49 GBd PDM-16QAM signals with RRC pulseshaping with roll-off 0.01. Decorrelated binary De Bruijn sequences of length 215 were multiplexed to generate transmitted symbols.
The test comb was multiplexed with a comb of loading channels. We had 87/91 C-band/L-band distributed feedback laser (DFB) sources in the loading comb. All TLS and DFB lasers were spaced at 50 GHz. Loading sources in each band were modulated by dedicated polarization multiplexed IQ-Mods and DACs, (not shown in Fig. 3 for the sake of brevity). While measuring C-band, a wavelength selective switch (WSS) was employed to cut a tunable notch of 400 GHz width. The eight TLS’ of the test comb were fit into this notch, while the eight DFB lasers falling in the notch window were turned OFF to avoid any unwanted crosstalk. Thus, while doing C-band measurements we had: 8 C-band TLS + 79 C-band DFB + 91 L-band DFB lasers. While measuring the L-band we had: 8 L-band TLS + 87

The recirculation loop is shown in Fig. 3(b). It consisted of 12 spans of 55 km ultra low loss Corning Vascade EX3000 fiber, with 0.157 dB/Km loss and with 150 μm2 effective area. Fiber loss was compensated by C and L-band EDFAs. The total launched power was 22 dBm (19 dBm per band). For a test channel in the mid C-band this was about 1 dB above the nonlinear threshold. However, loop equalization was done naively by simply equalizing the measured channel powers by an optical spectrum analyzer and reconfiguring two inloop WSSs; therefore, all WDM channels did not have the same NLT.
The transmitted signal was received after 10, 12 and 14 loops, corresponding to 6600, 7920, and 9240 km. The received signal was sampled by an 80 GSamples/s scope with 33 GHz analog bandwidth. The sampled signals were recorded and processed offline.
In the first round of processing, conventional chromatic dispersion compensation, CMA, Fourier transformed-based frequency estimation, and maximum likelihood carrier phase estimation were applied to the recorded signal. Cycle slips were handled by adding 0.5% pilot tones. This overhead was taken into account for net throughput calculations. The recovered symbols were processed by the FECs of the SC-LDPC family. For each channel and each wavelength, the FEC with maximum rate which could successfully decode the measured waveform was found by performing a bisection search over the FEC family. This information was used to construct the characteristic function G(.) (cf., Section III-B). Then we let K = 1, . . . , 52, and for each K, the optimal rates were found and the net throughput was computed.
In the second round of processing, chromatic dispersion compensation was replaced with FDBP, and all the other steps were repeated. Inside FDBP, we used the estimated value of chromatic dispersion in the first round.
In the third round, chromatic dispersion was used, but this time PNLC was applied to the recovered symbols after carrier phase recovery. For each estimated value of chromatic dispersion and each distance, first LUT coefficients were recalculated as per (3), and then PNLC was applied as per (2), where ρ was separately optimized for each channel.

C. Power Consumption Issues One of the main limitations of current submarine transmission
systems is the delivered electrical power of the offshore PFE to

supply EDFA pumps. We developed a simple model to estimate the PFE delivered power of our experimental test-bed, as if it were a truly deployed system. Fig. 8(a) illustrates the model.
We assumed a submarine system composed of eight identical copies of our test-bed, to emulate four bidirectional pairs. The current is supplied by a copper cable, with a resistance of 1 Ω/km, and the same length as the fiber cable. All pumps are assumed to be in series. In the laboratory, the output optical power of the EDFAs were varied from 18 to 23 dBm by varying their pump currents. These currents as well as voltage drops across pump lasers were recorded. We note that, on the average L-band amplifiers consume 44% more power than C-band amplifiers. An additional 1.5 V voltage drop per pump, for control and supervisory purposes was assumed. This model was used to estimate the PFE electrical delivered power as a function of the total C + L-band optical power at EDFA outputs. The resulting curve for 6600 km transmission is shown in Fig. 8(b). At 22 dBm total optical power, the estimated power delivery of the EDFAonly scheme was calculated to be 16.1 kW, which is equivalent to 37 pW/b/s if we assume the net throughput was 54.2 Tb/s. We did not repeat the analysis for hybrid EDFA-Raman, but relied on the assessment of [21] where the power requirement of the hybrid scheme was estimated to be twice as that of an EDFA-only system with the same span length. Thus, at 22 dBm had we switched to hybrid EDFA-Raman scheme, we would have required 32.2 kW. This operation point is also shown on Fig. 8 by a red star. Note that with the current technology, PFE delivered powers greater than 20 kW are extremely challenging.

V. CONCLUSION
In this work we reported on a full C + L-band EDFAonly WDM submarine transmission experiment of 178 PDM16QAM channels at 49 GBd, achieving 54.2 Tb/s after 6600 km, with a record 304.5 Gb/s per-channel net bit rate. Adaptive multi-rate SC-LDPC FEC codes were used. We proposed a new optimization algorithm to assign code rates to WDM channels. Moreover, we alternatively applied two nonlinearity postcompensation algorithms, i.e., digital backpropagation (DBP), and time-domain perturbative nonlinear compensation (PNLC) to all channels, and for the first time to our knowledge, experimentally compared their respective benefits in terms of throughput increase, and reach increase. Later on, we addressed the required delivered power of the PFE of submarine transmission systems, highlighting major benefits of EDFA-only over hybrid EDFA-Raman schemes for submarine transmission systems. Finally, we employed nonlinear noise interference model, together with experimental back-to-back Q-factor versus OSNR characterization and our spatially-coupled LDPC code rate versus pre-FEC Q2 characteristic curve, to theoretically analyze the performance of our test-bed with or without nonlinearity mitigations. Although many simplifying assumptions were made, the analytical results were not far from experimental results, and could explain the observed general trends regarding the mean
