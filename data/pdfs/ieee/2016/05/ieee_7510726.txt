Towards an Analytical Model of NAND Flash Memory and the Impact on Channel Decoding
Hachem Yassine1, Justin Coon1, Mohamed Ismail2, and Henry Fletcher1
1Department of Engineering Science, University of Oxford, UK, hachem.yassine@eng.ox.ac.uk 2Toshiba Telecommunications Research Laboratory, Bristol, UK, Mohamed@toshiba-trel.com

AbstractŌĆöThe underlying models of sources of noise in flash memory are exploited to compute an accurate distribution of the threshold voltage of a cell after cancelling cell to cell interference (CCI). We analytically express the mean and variance of this voltage solely as a function of number of P/E cycles, retention time and data eliminating the read overhead of the adaptive estimation methods. To reduce the computational difficulty in hardware, we approximate the accurate distribution by a moment matched Gaussian mixture and we validate this approximation by comparing the soft decision decoding performance of both models. To gain more error correction advantage, we model the residual CCI as an additional Gaussian noise term.
I. INTRODUCTION
With their continuous scaling down in feature size, flash memories are becoming less reliable. Devices are becoming denser, which amplifies cell to cell interference (CCI). Four and eight-level cells (MLC, TLC) are being employed. These advancements increased the storage capacity but shorten the deviceŌĆÖs endurance significantly [1]. This accelerating deterioration motivates the use of advanced techniques to ensure low bit error rates throughout an extended lifetime of the device, such as error control coding (ECC).
A major difficulty in implementing ECC in flash memory is the presence of non-stationary sources of noise that vary with the number of performed program/erase (P/E) cycles and the retention time [2]. As well as being non-stationary, these sources of noise are data dependent since programming a cell to a higher voltage makes it more susceptible to charge leakage [3] . For hard decision decoding, this means that decision boundaries need to be adapted regularly to the change in the noise characteristics. While for soft decision decoding, an efficient adaptive method is needed to estimate the threshold voltage distribution at every stage of the deviceŌĆÖs life to maintain accurate soft information [4].
Previous research gave insights into the threshold voltage characteristics of flash memory and it has been reported from extensive analysis of real device measurements that a Gaussian mixture describes very well the threshold voltage distribution of a cell [2], [5], [6]. Each sequence of bits (i.e. state) follows a Gaussian distribution with a different mean and variance that change with the number of P/E cycles [5]. Recent experiments suggested that a Normal-Laplace mixture captures the behaviour of the device better when the device is cycled heavily more than the nominal number of cycles set by the manufacturer [6]. Regardless of which distribution

is a better fit, since the parameters of this distribution are non-stationary, it is essential to have an efficient method to adaptively estimate them. Histogram based estimation is the best known of such methods: the gradient descent and the Levenber-Marquardt methods were applied in [2] to search for the eight parameters that minimize the squared Euclidean distance between the theoretical MLC Gaussian mixture model and the histograms built from measurements, the estimation is invoked only when too many decoding failures occur. In [6] the parameters of the MLC Gaussian mixture model (8 parameters) and the Normal-Laplace mixture model (20 parameters) were estimated by minimizing the KullbackLeibler Divergence between the model and the histograms, these parameters were fitted to an analytical function of the number of P/E cycles without considering retention time. In [7], a progressive read algorithm was employed to estimate the parameters of a Gaussian mixture SLC model where each read is chosen based on the results of the previous reads. These methods typically require a large number of reads to build the histograms and storage space to store the estimated parameters.
Another common aspect of these works is that they took an agnostic view on the models of the non-stationary sources of noise in the device. They approached the issue as an estimation problem of the evolving parameters of the distribution taking into consideration the read constraints. The authors in [8] introduced a realistic device based model that has only five parameters. They ignored the effects of CCI but stated that it can be cancelled and that the residual CCI can be added to the system as an additional noise term. In [9] histograms were used to estimate these parameters, but again this work did not consider how the parameters of individual sources of noise evolve.
Apart from the work to characterise the overall threshold voltage distribution, extensive work was done on the physics of the device to statistically characterise the physical phenomena leading to noise in flash (e.g. random telegraph noise (RTN), retention noise). The authors of [3] put forward the probabilistic models of each of the major sources of noise and interference and expressed how their parameters evolve with number of P/E cycles, retention time and data. Because of the complexity of CCI, the distribution of the threshold voltage according to this work is hardly tractable without making many simplifications of the model as done in [4].

The well-defined noise subcomponents are then combined to express the accurate threshold voltage distribution of a cell when being read. A moment matched Gaussian mixture approximation of the accurate distribution was proposed and validated by comparing the soft decision decoding performance when using the two distributions. B. Cell to Cell Interference

In this work, the all-bit line structure [17] is adopted and it is assumed that word lines are programmed sequentially in an increasing index order. This limits interference to that coming from the three direct neighbours on the next wordline. When a new cell is programmed, charge is injected and its threshold voltage changes, thus establishing a capacitance with adjacent cells on the previous word-line. As charge gets trapped or tunnels back through the oxide layer, this charge and voltage will alter again, which will have a knock-on effect, again through capacitive coupling, on adjacent cells. Following the same logic, it is more realistic to assume that the CCI suffered by a cell contributes to the shift it induces into another cell through interference. This means that CCI propagates from word-lines with higher indexes up towards those with lower indexes, making higher index word-lines less CCI contaminated than those with lower index. V. CONCLUSION
In this paper the extensive experimental literature on modelling the major sources of noise in flash memory was exploited to propose a realistic complete model that accounts for these sources including CCI. A first order canceller was employed to cancel CCI while the residual CCI was modelled as an independent Gaussian noise of which the mean and variance were analytically expressed. The output threshold voltage distribution was analytically expressed along with its moments solely as a function of number of P/E cycles, retention time, data and device tuning. Through simulations it was shown that this distribution can be approximated by a Gaussian mixture with moments matching those of the accurate model. According to this model, the need of an adaptive estimation algorithm is eliminated potentially reducing read times and storage space on the device.
