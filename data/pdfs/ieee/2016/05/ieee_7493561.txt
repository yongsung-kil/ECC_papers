17x Reliability Enhanced LDPC Code with Burst-Error Masking and High-Precision LLR
for Highly Reliable Solid-State-Drives with TLC NAND Flash Memory
Tsukasa Tokutomi and Ken Takeuchi Chuo University, Tokyo, Japan, E-mail: tokutomi@takeuchi-lab.org

Abstract— Highly reliable LDPC ECC is introduced to improve the reliability of solid-state drives (SSDs). Although conventional AEP-LDPC ECC [3] is 12× highly reliable than BCH ECC, its error-correction capability is degraded due to the burst-errors and inaccurate log-likelihood ratio (LLR). To improve the reliability of TLC NAND flash, this paper proposes the burst-error masking (BEM) and program-disturb merged LLR estimation (PMLE). The first proposal, BEM eliminates the burst-errors by recording the error-location to the table. The second proposal, PMLE calculates the ratio of program-disturb errors to data-retention errors. As a result, more precise LLR is obtained. By combining BEM and PMLE, the SSD lifetime is extended by 17× and the table size overhead is reduced by 81%.
Index Terms—Low-density-parity-check (LDPC), triple-level cell (TLC) NAND flash, reliability, solid state drive (SSD)
I. INTRODUCTION NAND flash memory based solid-state-drive (SSD) can operate with low-power and higher read performance. Since reliability of NAND flash is degraded as the scaling of memory cells, stronger error-correcting code (ECC) is implemented in the SSD controller. Moreover, 3-bit/cell triple-level cell (TLC) NAND flash has been commercialized to increase the bit-density. However, errors of 2Xnm TLC NAND flash is increased by over 10× due to the narrower read margins compared with 2bit/cell multi-level cell (MLC) NAND flash [1]. Conventional BCH ECC cannot ensure the TLC NAND flash’s reliability. Therefore, low-density-paritycheck (LDPC) code is expected to apply for NAND flash [2]. Although LDPC can correct more errors than BCH, multiple reads with different VRef sensings are necessary to get the analog cell VTH information and calculate log-likelihood ratio (LLR) [2]. To realize a quick LDPC decoding as fast as BCH, advanced errorprediction (AEP-) LDPC has been proposed [3]. Since AEP-LDPC estimates LLR without the analog VTH, multiple VRef sensings are not necessary and the read speed of SSD becomes comparable with SSDs with BCH. Fig.1 shows the overview of the LDPC iterative decoding. As the iteration cycles increase, errors are gradually corrected and the original data is recovered. However, LDPC error-correction may fail when burst-errors exist [4]. In NAND flash, errors are randomly distributed when the write/erase (W/E) cycle is small. However, this paper shows for the first time that when the W/E cycle increases, burst-errors increase and LDPC fail to correct errors. In addition, this paper describes that inaccurate LLR degrades the error correction capability of LDPC [5]. Conventional AEP-LDPC assumes that program-disturb error is negligibly small during the long-term data retention. In reality, a small amount of program-disturb errors remain. As a result, the conventional LLR becomes inaccurate during the long-term data retention. To eliminate burst-errors and realize a more accurate LLR, this paper proposes the burst-error masking (BEM) and program-disturb merged LLR estimation (PMLE). Proposed BEM records the burst-error location to the table. By referring to the error-location table, burst-errors are eliminated in the next read cycle. Moreover, proposed PMLE improves the accuracy of LLR by calculating the ratio of program-disturb (P.D.) error to data-retention (D.R.) error. By combining BEM and PMLE, the SSD lifetime improves by 17times.

II. BACKGROUND OF THIS WORK Fig. 2 shows the measured P.D. errors and D.R. errors of 2Xnm TLC NAND flash. In P.D. errors, the VTH of memory cells increases due to the undesired electron injection to the floating gate. In D.R. errors, the VTH of memory cells decreases over time due to the stress induced leackage currect (SILC) or de-trapping of electrons from the tunnel oxide. Fig. 3 shows the relation beween errors and LLR. Since LLR is calculated based on the bit-error rate (BER) [5], error pattern of NAND flash should be modeled precisely. Fig. 4 shows the comparison of soft-decoding LDPC [2] and AEP-LDPC [3]. To acquire the analog VTH and caclulate LLR, soft-decoding LDPC requires typically 7VRef sensing for each VTH state. For TLC NAND flash, 49VRef sensing (7 × 7VRef sensing) are neccesarly. In contrast, AEP-LDPC estimates LLR based on the pre-determined tables, which store the relation among target-cell VTH, W/E cycle, data-retention time, temperature and inter-cell capacitive coupling. As a result, total read speed is 2.3× improved by reducing the VRef sensing to 21 (3 × 7) times.
III. BURST-ERROR MASKING (BEM) Fig. 5 shows the relation between the acceptable BER of LDPC and average burst length when code-rate = 2/3. The burst length describes how many burst-errors occur at the consecutive logical addresses. In Fig.5, the number of total errors is the same and the average burst length is changed based on the Gilbert-model [6]. As shown in Fig. 5, the acceptable BER of LDPC drastically decreases as the average burst length increases. LDPC becomes unable to correct errors as more burst-errors happen. On the other hand, BCH is not sensitive to burst-errors because the error correction capability of BCH is determined only by the number of total errors and does not depend on the error pattern. Fig. 6 shows the measured dependencies of burst-length vs. error count of 2Xnm TLC NAND flash when the data-retention time is 122days. As the W/E cycle increases, the measured bursterrors increase. What’s worse, the burst-length also increases. The measured maximum burst-length is as many as “8” when the W/E cycle is 6k. That is, 8 consecutive errors are observed. Fig. 7 shows the measured fail address map. Both single-bit error and burst-errors are evenly distributed over all addresses and no dependencies for the bit-line addresses (column addresses) are observed. Note that these burst-errors are “soft” errors and can be recovered if the memory cell is erased again. The error addresses are different for each W/E cycle. Fig. 8 shows the measured cumulative error count vs. offset of errors when the W/E cycle is 4k. The offset is defined as the distance between errors in logical address space and means the degree of burst-errors. When the offset is “1”, errors are categorized to burst-errors. When offset is “2”, errors are more likely to become burst-errors in the next read cycle and the others are assumed to be random errors. In Fig. 8, errors whose offset is “l, 2” account for 2.5% of the total errors when the data-retention time is 1day. When the data-retention time is 122days, errors whose offset is “1, 2” increase to 12.1% of the total errors. Thus, burst-errors increase over time and should be eliminated to improve the error correction capability of LDPC. To eliminate both random and burst-errors, error-masking (EM) has been proposed (Fig. 9) [7]. Once D.R. errors occur, the errors remain and are not recovered afterwards unless the memory cell is erased and programmed again. In the EM, when errors are observed during the read, the error-location is recorded in the

error-location table. At the next read cycle, by referring to the error-location table, pre-recorded errors are eliminated. EM can effectively eliminate errors. However, when more and more errors occur at the high W/E cycles or long data-retention time, the table which stores the location of all errors becomes unacceptably large (Table 1).
To reduce this problem by 81%, this paper proposes the bursterror masking (BEM) (Fig. 10). To save the table size, proposed BEM records only the location of burst-errors which cannot be corrected by LDPC. During the read when errors are observed by the ECC decoder, the errors are distinguished between random errors and burst-errors by measuring the offset. The random errors are efficiently corrected by LDPC ECC. The location of only the burst-errors are recorded in the error location table by the runlength encoding [8]. During the next read cycle, these burst-errors are removed and the proposed LDPC effectively corrects random errors. By removing the burst-errors by BEM and correcting the random errors by LDPC ECC, the best balance of high reliability and small table size is achieved (Table 1).
Figs. 10 and 11 show the case study of the proposed BEM. At first, the read data is transferred from the TLC NAND flash to the SSD controller and the ECC decoding is executed. By comparing the read data, which include errors, and corrected data, the error location is obtained. Next, the SSD controller calculates the offset between errors, N, and searches for burst-errors. To achieve the best balance of the high reliability and the small table size, the definition of burst-errors can be flexibly optimized by adjusting the threshold, NOffset. By comparing the measured offset, N, and NOffset, the SSD controller judges whether errors are random or burst. After burst-errors are detected, the location of random error is marked as “0” because BEM does NOT store the information of random errors. Lastly, error-locations are stored to the table after the compression. For example, in case of NOffset = {1}, the errors whose offset is 2 and over are eliminated from the error-location table. As a result, only consecutive burst-errors are recorded. In case of NOffset = {1, 2}, consecutive errors and every other errors are recorded in the table. If NOffset increases, although the correctable errors increase, the table size also increases.
Fig. 12 shows the measured relation between the reliability improvement and the table size overhead. When NOffset = {1, 2}, the proposed AEP-LDPC with BEM extends the acceptable dataretention time by 7-times compared with the conventional AEPLDPC. Compared with the conventional EM, the table size overhead decreases by 81%.
IV. PROGRAM-DISTURB MERGED LLR ESTIMATION (PMLE) Fig. 13 shows the problems of the conventional BER calculation and inaccurate LLR. Gray-code is assumed for the mapping between VTH states and page information [9]. Conventional AEPLDPC estimates BER by considering both P.D. and D.R. errors during the short-term data-retention. In contrast, during the longterm data-retention, P.D. errors are assumed to be negligibly small and D.R. errors are dominant. At the long data-retention time, all errors are assumed the D.R. errors which decrease the memory cell VTH. For example, when the stored data is the “C” state, the 2nd page BER from “B” to “C” is assumed to be negligibly small, such as “10-7”. However, even in the long-term data-retention, P.D. errors remain and “B” state can shift to “C” state. Since LLR is calculated based on BER, if LLR is inaccurate, error-correction may fail. To precisely estimate LLR, this paper proposes the programdisturb merged LLR estimation (PMLE). PMLE models both P.D. and D.R. errors during both short-term and long-term dataretention. In Fig. 13, 2nd page BER from “B” to “C” is assumed “10-2” and thus the P.D. errors from “B” to “C” are corrected. Fig. 14 shows the comparison of conventional and proposed LLR

estimation. For example, if the memory cell VTH is slightly larger than the read reference voltage, VRef, the correct LLR should be for example, “-4.6”. Since absolute value of LLR is small, errors will be successfully corrected during the next LDPC decoding cycle. In contrast, because the conventional LLR does not consider the P.D. errors, the absolute value of LLR becomes large, for example, “-12”. As a result, errors are not corrected.
Fig. 15 shows the case study of LDPC decoding. In Fig. 15, the correct data is all “0”. If the data read from TLC NAND flash contain errors (“1”), the absolute value of LLR becomes small and LDPC decoder considers that the data is likely to be erroneous. In contrast, if the absolute value of LLR is large, LDPC decoder assumes that the data is correct. In case of P.D., since conventional AEP-LDPC assumes the absolute value of LLR is large, the LDPC decoder judges that this data is definitely correct. Because LLR is assumed large by mistake, more and more errors are generated and become uncorrectable by repeating the LDPC iterations.
Fig. 16 shows the measured P.D. errors vs. D.R. errors. In case of the short-term data retention, for example, when the dataretention time is 1day, P.D. and D.R. errors are similar. In contrast, at the long-term data retention, for example, when the dataretention time is 122days, D.R. errors become dominant but 16% of P.D. errors are remained. Thus, even at the long-term data retention, P.D. errors should not be ignored in calculating LLR.
Fig. 17 shows the flow chart of the proposed PMLE. Similar to the conventional AEP-LDPC, target-cell VTH, W/E cycle, dataretention time, temperature and inter-cell capacitive coupling are considered to calculate LLR. At first, the data-retention time (TRetention) is estimated by referring to the TRetention table [3]. For the long TRetention, P.D. /D.R. error estimation unit is newly added in PMLE. By referring to the P.D. /D.R. error table, BER of P.D. and D.R. is calculated based on the total BER. The P.D. /D.R. error table stores the relation between the ratio of P.D. error to D.R. error, W/E cycle, data–retention time and temperature. The additional table size is assumed as 3kByte, which is negligibly small compared with 16GByte 2Xnm TLC NAND flash memory (<10-5%). Then, cell-error rate (CER) which includes the inter-cell capacitive coupling effects is calculated. Lastly, CER is converted to LLR and LDPC decoding is executed.
Fig. 18 shows the measured reliability improvement of PMLE. By calculating LLR of P.D. and D.R. error, acceptable dataretention time and BER improve by 1.7-times and 1.2-times, respectively. Fig. 19 shows the measured LDPC error-correction results. By combining proposed BEM and PMLE, the acceptable data-retention time is extended by 17-times. Assuming a decoding throughput of 10Gbps [10], LDPC decoding time decreases by 56% compared with conventional AEP-LDPC when correcting the same errors. That is, the proposed LDPC is more than twice faster than the conventional AEP-LDPC.
V. CONCLUSION TableI summarizes this work. By eliminating burst-errors, proposed BEM extends the acceptable data-retention time by 7times. Moreover, acceptable data-retention time is 1.7-times extended with PMLE. Furthermore, by combining BEM and PMLE, the SSD lifetime improves by 17-times.
