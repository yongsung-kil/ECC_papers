On the LLR criterion based Shortening Design for LDPC Codes
Hui Wang, Qingchun Chen, Yong Zhang Email:wanghui 07@126.com, qcchen@swjtu.edu.cn, zykido@163.com. School of Information Science and Technology, Southwest Jiaotong University
Chengdu, Sichuan, 610031, China

Abstract—In shortening based coding design, a certain amount of known information bits (for instance, all 0 bits) will be inserted into each information packet for encoding and be removed before transmission. Since they are known, the decoder can assume a complete codeword in decoding, even those known bits are not delivered at all. Basically, shortening provides a framework to generate a set of more powerful codewords with shorter size and lower rate from one mother LDPC code. In this paper, a novel log-likelihood ratio (LLR) criterion is proposed to identify those shortening information indices for LDPC codes. Firstly, the LLR criterion is introduced to show how to determine the LLR reliability metric for each individual information bit via a simple noise-free decoding step. Then it is proposed to determine the shortened information indices based on the LLR ordering. The relationship between the proposed shortening scheme and the known approaches are highlighted as well. Finally, the irregular QC-LDPC codes in IEEE 802.11ac standard is utilized as an example to verify the proposed LLR criterion based shortening design. And our analysis unveils that the proposed shortening scheme provides an efficient shortening scheme for LDPC codes with lower computational complexity but better reliability performance.
Index Terms—Shortening, reliability criterion, LDPC codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
A DAPTIVE coding plays an important role to realize an efficient wireless communication system. As a capacity approaching code, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes has been adopted as the standard channel coding scheme by various specifications, such as IEEE 802.16e [1] , IEEE 802.11n [2] and IEEE 802.11ac [3]. In these technical standards, only several LDPC base-matrices are assumed. In order to fulfill different application requirements, adaptive LDPC code generation become highly desirable. Basically, there are three typical approaches to generate adaptive LDPC codes from a given parity-check matrix, namely, puncturing, extending and shortening. In puncturing, some parity bits of the LDPC codeword will be removed to generate a set of codewords with higher code rates. While in extending, a set of LDPC codewords with larger size but the same code rate can be generated by means of expanding the given parity-check matrix. Unlike both puncturing and extending approaches, a certain amount of known information bits (basically all 0 bits are assumed) will be inserted into each information packet

for encoding and be removed before transmission. Since they are known, the decoder can assume a complete codeword in decoding, even those known bits are not delivered at all. In [4], shortening is applied to Turbo product codes (TPCs). A hybrid ARQ scheme was investigated for LDPC codes via shortening in [5]. It is shown that shortening provides an effective complementary approach to the puncturing and extending scheme in adjusting both the code rate and the code length for a given mother codeword.
The less short girths criterion was proposed in [6] to derive the shortening scheme for the regular QC-LDPC codes. It is shown that the number of short cycles, which dominates the achieved performance of regular QC-LDPC codes, can be effectively reduced via shortening. For irregular QC-LDPC codes, the degree distribution tends to be a more important factor [7]. The largest extrinsic sum (LES) algorithm with lowest column degree (LCD) criterion and the smallest-rowvariance-priority (SRVP) algorithm was presented respectively in [8] and [9] to determine the shortening scheme for irregular QC-LDPC codes. LES algorithm will pick the variable nodes with the lowest degree as candidates at first; in the second step, that one with the maximal extrinsic sum in candidates will be selected to minimize the decreasing ratios of check degrees; lastly, if there are multiple candidates with the same maximal extrinsic sum, that one which gives rise to the lowest threshold via PEXIT analysis [10] will be selected as the shortening index. In the SRVP algorithm, all information columns will be equally evaluated to determine the lowest degree columns and the SRVP criterion is used, too. It is shown that severe performance degradation phenomena may present when too many information bits are shortened in the shortening design. In order to avoid the negative effect by shortening, the simulationbased error-floor lowering scheme was proposed in [11] for systematic LDPC codes, wherein information bits that form the dominant trapping (or stopping) sets of systematic LDPC codes will be selected through two proposed bit-selection algorithms and, by assigning known values to those bits. It is shown that the error floor performance can be remarkably improved. In all previous shortening designs, some factors that affect the achieved performance of LDPC codes are utilized, and the examples include girth, degree distribution and trapping set analysis (or criterion). However, it is known that, the performance of LDPC code is not determined by one unique factor, but by a number of structural properties. For instance, the minimum distance (or minimum weight),

Instead, the LLR output in the iterative log-domain message-passing decoding procedure provides a direct but comprehensive measure to assess the performance of LDPC codes. The absolute value of LLR represents the reliability of the information bit, and it is used in [12] to estimate the bit error rate (BER) performance. In this work, we proposed to use the LLR-based criterion for data shortening. Firstly, the noise free decoding is utilized to determine the relative reliability ordering. II. LDPC CODES AND SHORTENING
A. Irregular QC-LDPC codes
It is shown in [15] that the properly constructed irregular LDPC codes can approach the Shannon limit more closely than the regular LDPC codes [14]. A regular LDPC code is a linear block code whose parity-check matrix HM×N contains exactly wc 1 s in each column and exactly wr = wc(N/M ) 1 s in each row, where wc M . The degree of each v-node is wc and the degree of each c-node is wr. The code rate R = (N − M )/N is related to these parameters via R = 1−wc/wr. If HM×N is low density, but the number of 1 s in each column or row is not constant, then the code is irregular LDPC codes. For irregular LDPC codes, all v-node degree and c-node degree distribution can be specified by the following distribution polynomials of λ(x) and ρ(x), respectively,

B. Shortening

Shortening is used to flexibly generate codes with shorter length and lower code rate based on a given parity check matrix. Let k denote the total number of information bits to be shorten. And the shortening procedure can be briefly summarized by the following steps.

• Insert k zeros into the k pre-defined shortening information bit indices of each information packet with length K for LDPC encoding;
• Encode from each modified K information packet to obtain all parity check bits;
• Remove all k 0s which are inserted into the K information portion of the LDPC codeword before transmission.

A. LLR Ordering and LLR Criterion
Before we introduce the LLR criterion, let us review the symmetry conditions in [7]:
1) Channel symmetry: For a binary-input memoryless channels, x and y are input and output of the channel, if the conditional probability p(y|x = +1) = p(−y|x = −1), the channel is symmetry.
2) V-node symmetry: Flipping the sign of all incoming messages at a variable node results in a flip of the sign of all outgoing message.
3) C-node symmetry: Let e be an edge emanating from check node c. Then flipping the sign of i incoming messages arriving at node c, excluding the message along edge e, result in a change of the sign of the outgoing message along edge e by (−1)i.

In order to validate the LLR reliability ordering, especially to highlight the relationship between the proposed LLR reliability ordering and other known fact that people believe in. We use three irregular QC-LDPC codes in IEEE 802.11ac, namely, (648, 324) code, (1296, 648) code and (1944, 972) code, as examples. Based on the sorted |LLR| results of all information bits, one can readily conclude that all information bits corresponding to the same base matrix column will have the same |LLR| values with the proposed noise free decoding procedure. And this conclusion complies with the fact that these information bits have the identical structural properties. In Table I, the symbol of a(b) is utilized to indicate that the information bits of column a of the base matrix have the degree b. From Table I, we can also arrive at the following conclusions. B. LLR criterion based Shortening
Based on the sorted LLR reliabilty ordering result, the k least reliable information indices will be selected as the shortening indices. And the LLR criterion based shortening design for IEEE 802.11ac standard LDPC code can be summarized by the following steps.
1) Perform the noise free decoding to obtain the LLR reliability ordering S = {s1, s2, . . . , sk} = {s1, s2, . . . , sK }, K = kZ for the given irregular QCLDPC code. Every si corresponds to Z bits, their |LLR| values are the same.
2) Determine k information bits to be shortened according to specific application requirements. k can be arbitrary value around the coding lengths defined by the standard.
3) Select the first k information bit indices from the LLR reliability ordering S = {s1, s2, . . . sK } as the shortening indices.

We compare the achieved performance of different shortening patterns. Here the LES algorithm [8], the SRVP algorithm [9] and the standard shortening method in 802.11ac [3] are included for comparison.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we propose a new shortening design based on the LLR criterion. Unlike other criterion, always the most critical information bit indices that dominate the LDPC codes can be easily sorted out by using the proposed LLR reliability ordering analysis. And both the degree distribution, girth and decoding convergence threshold analysis, together with the simulation results are presented to show that, the LLR criterion based shortening design will sort out the critical information indices with the similar degree distribution or girth characteristics, which previous shortening design believe that dominate the achieved performance, but also unveils some more inherent and comprehensive factors that dominate the complicated performance characteristics of the general LDPC codes. In addition to the shortening design, it must be noted that, the proposed LLR criterion can also be applied in the puncturing and extending design for LDPC codes, which will be left for the next step analysis.
