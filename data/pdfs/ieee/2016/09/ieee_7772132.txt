AbstractтАФWe consider a special family of spatially coupled
low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes known as LDPC convo-
lutional codes and propose a design technique based on symbolic
hyper-graphs aimed at achieving good codes with small syndrome
former memory order. We define conditions on the minimum
syndrome former memory order that must be imposed in order to
avoid short local cycles in the codes Tanner graphs and to achieve
good minimum distance. Code design examples are provided,
accompanied by error rate performance assessments based on
Montecarlo simulations.
Index TermsтАФConvolutional codes, low-density parity-check
codes, spatially coupled codes, symbolic hyper-graphs.
I. INTRODUCTION
Convolutional codes (CCs) exhibit interesting structural
properties, like a number of low-weight codewords linearly
increasing with the frame length. They are characterized by a
generator memory order ЁЭСЪand a syndrome former memory
order ЁЭСЪтДО, related to the periodic nature of their generator
and parity-check matrices [1]. Traditional CCs are typically
decoded over a trellis with soft-decision decoding algorithms
and computational complexity increasing as 2ЁЭСЪ[2]. A sliding
window decoder can be implemented whose window length
is in the order of ЁЭСЪperiods. LDPC CCs are a class of
modern CCs and a special family of spatially coupled (SC)
LDPC codes [3]. Their decoding is performed through belief
propagation (BP) algorithms working on the code Tanner
graph, having complexity linearly growing with ЁЭСЪтДОand with
the average parity-check matrix column weight [4]. A sliding
window BP decoder can also be implemented to reduce
complexity. When the decoding window is as large as a few
times ЁЭСЪтДОperiods, the sliding window decoder performance
approaches that of an ideal decoder working on the whole
frame. Therefore, having a small syndrome former constraint
length is a desirable feature from both the complexity and
latency standpoints. With respect to classical CCs, LDPC CCs
achieve higher minimum distance and are able to approach the
channel capacity [3], [5]. However, short cycles in the Tanner
graph must be avoided to let BP decoders work well and very
long codes are needed to approach the asymptotic performance
predicted by density evolution (DE) [6].
Traditional approaches to the design of LDPC CCs start
from good quasi cyclic (QC) LDPC block codes, which
represent the equivalent of the tail-biting version of the cor-
responding CCs [7]тАУ[13], and do not aim at achieving small
syndrome former constraint lengths. We have recently shown
that they indeed do not yield the smallest ЁЭСЪтДОfor a given
performance [14]. Better results can be obtained by directly
designing the syndrome former matrix HЁЭСЗ
0 which forms the
semi-infinite parity-check matrix of an LDPC CC [14], [15].
Nevertheless, the alternative representation based on a ЁЭСР├ЧЁЭСО
polynomial parity-check matrix H(ЁЭСе) helps us to classify dif-
ferent families of codes and to find the structure of unavoidable
codewords, thus fixing upper bounds on the minimum distance
[8]. In [16] we proposed to use a tool called symbolic graph,
which helps to enumerate the short cycles corresponding to a
given H(ЁЭСе), and to find lower bounds on the values of ЁЭСЪтДОthat
are needed to eliminate them, up to unavoidable ones. Such an
approach was used in [16] only for a particular family of low-
rate LDPC CCs. In this paper, we extend it to more general
code structures and rates by introducing a generalization of
symbolic graphs that we call symbolic hyper-graphs.
The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. In
Section II the new concept of hyper-edge is introduced. In
Section III the problem of satisfying the row-column constraint
is faced. In Section IV we find the conditions to leave only
unavoidable cycles in some code structures yielding good
minimum distance. Section V provides some code design
examples together with the relevant error rate performance
assessments and Section VI concludes the paper.

III. ROW-COLUMN CONSTRAINT
The presence of length-4 cycles in the code Tanner graph
deteriorates the LDPC decoder performance [4], therefore
the so-called row-column constraint must be imposed on the
parity-check matrix in order to avoid the occurrence of such
short cycles. For given weights of the columns in H(ЁЭСе), it is
possible to find lower bounds on the value of ЁЭСЪтДО(coincident
with the largest difference between any two exponents of
the entries of H(ЁЭСе)) that is needed to meet the row-column
constraint. Despite in this paper we focus on regular codes
with constant parity-check column weight ЁЭСд, these bounds can
be easily extended to the case of irregular codes with column
weights ЁЭСдЁЭСЧ, ЁЭСЧ= 1, 2, . . . , ЁЭСО. Before presenting these bounds,
let us introduce the following two types of separations (i.e.,
differences between exponents) in H(ЁЭСе):
тИЩHorizontal separations are computed between the expo-
nents of each polynomial (hence, monomials do not yield
any horizontal separation).
тИЩVertical separations are computed between any two pair
of exponents along each column, at different rows, and
correspond to hyper-edges.

VI. CONCLUSION
We have extended the approach based on symbolic graphs
to the study of more general classes of LDPC CCs. By
using this tool, we have devised new bounds on the syn-
drome former memory order required to avoid cycles up to
a given length and achieve good minimum distance. We have
provided practical code design examples to give evidence of
the effectiveness of the proposed approach in finding good
performance-complexity tradeoffs.
