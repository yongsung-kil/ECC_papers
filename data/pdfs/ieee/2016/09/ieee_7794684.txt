Analysis and Design of Rate Compatible LDPC Codes

Fulvio Babich, Matteo Noschese, and Francesca Vatta DIA, University of Trieste
Via A. Valerio 10, I-34127 Trieste (Italy) e-mail: {babich, vatta}@units.it, matteo.noschese@phd.units.it

Abstract—The main concern in nowadays digital communications standards, such as IEEE802.11n, 802.16e (Wi-MAX), 10GBaseT Ethernet, and Digital Video Broadcasting (DVB), is the need of adaptive and flexible communication techniques. The request for higher efficiency, both in bandwidth use and power consumption, increases the need for limit-achieving, flexible techniques of channel coding. Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) Codes are very interesting because of their high performances and potential flexibility introduced by puncturing.
In this paper, the design of rate-compatible LDPC is addressed. A class of punctured LDPC codes is defined through their equivalent parity check matrices. A formal analysis is provided, based on a simplified approach on the decoding belief propagation algorithm, i.e., considering a Gaussian approximation for message densities under density evolution. A suitable design criterion for the puncturing patterns is then addressed.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) codes are a class of channel block codes first introduced in 1960 by Robert Gallager in his doctoral dissertation. The thesis was later published in 1963 [1]. Due to the technical limitations of the time and the complexity of decoder design, LDPC codes were ignored for almost 30 years. The only contribution during this time was Tanner’s generalized LDPC definition and graphical representation, presented in his 1981 paper [2] and later called Tanner graph. In 1993, with the introduction of Turbo codes, the search for capacity-approaching channel codes became a primary focus of communication engineering. LDPC codes experienced a comeback thanks to the work of Mackay [3] and Luby et al. [4]. These works showed that LDPC performance can approach the Shannon limit as well as Turbo codes if not better. It has also been shown that punctured LDPC codes can achieve capacity-approaching performaces as well [5]. LDPC codes quickly gained a significant role in digital communications: they were included in modern communication standards such as IEEE802.11n, 802.16e (WiMAX), 10G-BaseT Ethernet, and Digital Video Broadcasting (DVB).
A primary field of innovation in nowadays digital communications is the development of adaptive communication techniques. The request for efficiency increases the need for limitachieving, flexible channel coding techniques. LDPC codes fit these requirements well. Flexibility, i.e. rate adaptability, can be obtained though different pairs of encoders and decoders, which is often unacceptable due to complexity. It can also

be realized by puncturing a low rater mother code, resulting in only one pair of encoders and decoders for each mother code. If the parity bits punctured in a lower rate code are also punctured in a higher rate code of the ensemble, the rate-adaptive channel code ensemble is called rate-compatible. There are two types of approach to rate compatibility: code extension [6], [7] where a high-rate ”daughter” code is extended to achieve lower rates, and code puncturing. In this paper, rate-compatible LDPC codes are obtained by progressively constructing a puncturing pattern for a low-rate mother code. A formal analysis of this class of punctured LDPC codes is provided, by determining the equivalent parity check matrices for the punctured LDPC codes. More insight is given by addressing a suitable puncturing design criterion, using a simplified analysis of the decoding algorithm, i.e., considering a Gaussian approximation for message densities under density evolution [8]. To this end, a puncturing design procedure is derived, able to provide a computationally low cost quality assessment for puncturing patterns.
The paper is organized as follows. In the next section, a description of the class of rate-compatible punctured codes is given, deriving their equivalent parity check matrices. In Section III, the proposed puncturing design criterion is outlined. In Section IV, the results of this design are presented. Finally, Section V summarizes the results of the paper.
II. A CLASS OF RATE-COMPATIBLE PUNCTURED LDPC
CODES
The concept of rate-compatible puncturing has been presented for the first time in [9], where a particular family of convolutional codes, called in the paper rate-compatible punctured convolutional codes, is obtained by adding a ratecompatibility restriction to the puncturing rule. For LDPC codes, when performing rate-compatible puncturing, the Tanner graph associated with a higher rate code is always a subgraph of all the Tanner graphs associated with lower rate codes of the family.
A LDPC code can be fully represented by its parity check matrix or, equivalently, by the associated Tanner graph. The equivalent parity matrix for a punctured code can be easily determined, given the mother code parity matrix and the puncturing pattern. Note that puncturing a bit from a LDPC codeword is the same as eliminating a variable node from the correspondent Tanner graph. Being k the degree of the bit

B. Puncturing design procedure
In order to optimize the degree distribution for an irregular LDPC code, ∆t = f (s, t) − t should be maximized. The O(t−1) term can be neglected. Using this result, a design criterion for the puncturing pattern can be derived which, being related to the stability conditions, mostly influences the error floor behavior of the punctured code. Specifically, ∆t depends on the irregular LDPC code parameters i1, λi1 , and ρj, where λi1 is the first non-zero coefficient of the polynomial λ(x), i1 is its index, and ρj is the j-th coefficient of the polynomial ρ(x).

The algorthm takes a genetic approach to puncturing. With

each iteration, the NB bit positions that are more often set to 1 in the selection of best patterns are chosen and maintained

until the output pattern has the correct weight (i.e., number of

1s). From those positions, new patterns are generated and the

procedure is iterated.

It should be noted that the algorithm operates in a “greedy”

way. The “maintained” bit positions that produces the best

quality assessment are chosen at each iteration of the algorithm

and, once chosen, the are kept for the following iterations

of the algorithm. As such, it may not produce the optimal

solution, but goes through a number of suboptimal solutions.

V. CONCLUSIONS
This paper was written with adaptive digital communication systems in mind. The design of rate-compatible puncturing patterns for LDPC codes has been addressed. A class of punctured LDPC codes was formally defined and an overview of their characteristics was given. A rate-compatible approach was taken into account for possible future uses in Incremental Redundancy Hybrid Repeat Request (IR-HARQ) schemes.
A formal, structural analysis of the punctured LDPC code was proposed. A computationally low cost quality assessment procedure was presented and formally justified, thanks to the work of Chung at al. [8] upon which the assessment is based.
