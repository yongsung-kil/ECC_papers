Detector for MLC NAND Flash Memory Using Neighbor-A-Priori Information
Chaudhry Adnan Aslam, Student Member, IEEE, Yong Liang Guan, Member, IEEE, and Kui Cai, Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract— Cell-to-cell interference (CCI), arising from parasitic coupling-capacitance between adjacent cells, is a major factor for the degradation of cell threshold voltage in today’s flash memory chips. In this paper, three novel postprocessing detection schemes that exploit the a priori information of neighboring/interfering cells for mitigating the CCI effect in multilevel cell NAND flash memory are presented. The proposed schemes are referred to as the Even-A-Priori (Even-AP), the All-A-Priori (All-AP), and the All-AP-coupling-capacitance ratio (CCR) detectors. The main idea is to remove the CCI component from the interfering cells before CCI cancellation from the victim cell. Specifically, the mean CCRs along the victim cell’s vertical and diagonal directions are estimated to enable more accurate CCI cancellation. Performance analysis and simulation results show that the channel signal-to-noise ratio performance can be improved by up to 2 dB at a cell storage capacity of 1.8 bits/cell, which is significantly improved compared with some prior-art detection schemes.
Index Terms— Cell storage capacity, cell-to-cell interference (CCI), channel detector, error performance, NAND flash memory.
I. INTRODUCTION
T HE usage of NAND flash memory has grown tremendously over the past several years due to low cost, high storage capacity, and portability. The application areas of NAND flash memory range from consumer electronics (such as mobile phones, digital cameras, and MP3 players) to more specialized enterprise data applications and largescale data centers. This has become possible mainly because of the advanced manufacturing processes, which have pushed the node-scaling to its limits, producing sub-20-nm CMOS transistors, and advanced read/write processes, such as the multilevel cell (MLC) technology that enables more than 1 bit to be stored in a single memory cell.
On the bleak side, as the flash memory cells are scaled-down and integrated closer to each other, several reliability issues have emerged which compromise the overall performance of flash memory system [1], [2]. With cell shrinking, there are now fewer electrons that can be stored

Besides, for m-bit per cell flash technology, it requires 2m voltage levels to distinctly represent the m-bit data symbol. Adjusting these voltage levels, in an already contracted voltage-window, further reduces the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) margin and consequently degrades the system error performance. The other problem that stems from node-scaling is the increase in parasitic coupling-capacitance between the adjacent memory cells. This circuit-level impairment introduces the cell-to-cell interference (CCI) phenomenon inside the flash cell array, which is well recognized as one of the major source of cell’s threshold voltage distortion [3]–[5]. It leads to data reliability problems and complicates the design of channel detector and encoder/decoder units inside the flash memory controller.
In this paper, we address the data reliability issues associated with CCI and present some channel detection schemes for its mitigation. As the MLC concept and nodescaling are reaching the limits, it has become evident that the conventional, the so-called hard-decision decoding, error-correcting codes (ECCs) are no longer feasible. The Bose–Chaudhuri–Hocquenghem code is one such example of hard-decision code, which has been extensively used for the flash memory [6], [7]. In this scenario, more stronger soft-decision ECC is seriously considered, such as the lowdensity parity-check (LDPC) codes [8]–[10]. However, to fully reap the benefits of soft-decision LDPC codes, more accurate flash channel detectors are inevitably required, which motivate further research into the flash channel detector design.
To mitigate the CCI effect, there have been several schemes reported in the open literature. All these existing schemes can be generally placed into two separate categories, namely, the preprocessing and postprocessing schemes, as shown in Fig. 1. The preprocessing schemes are applied before/during the cell programming operation, whereas the postprocessing schemes are implemented after the memory sensing operation. The contributions in [11] and [12] are two quite similar preprocessing techniques, in which the CCI strength is estimated at the time of cell programming and subsequently subtracted from the desired write/verify voltage levels. However, these schemes cannot remove the CCI effect from the erasedstate cells. Besides, a constrained coding scheme has been presented in [13], wherein certain voltage levels of neighboring cells are forbidden, such that a wider gap between adjacent voltage distributions is ensured. An extensive effort has also been devoted toward the design of CCI postprocessing detection schemes. In [12], [15], and [16], the channel detector is designed by adaptively applying the read-voltage/quantization levels. Besides, another postprocessing detection scheme is presented in [17], where a closed-form expression for cell’s threshold voltage distribution is obtained to compute the loglikelihood-ratio (LLR) information (i.e., bit reliabilities) for LDPC decoding. However, all these postprocessing schemes overlook to remove the CCI component from the readback voltage signal, which subsequently result into suboptimal error-rate performance. More effective postprocessing schemes are presented in [11] and [18], where the detrimental CCI component is estimated and removed from the readback voltage signal. Likewise, there are some trellis-based detection schemes, such as reported in [19] and [20], for the optimal detection and mitigation of CCI component. However, these schemes are more computational extensive as they require the calculation of branch-metric values for the entire trellis structure to produce the soft-input information for the stored data symbols. Therefore, the trellis-based detection schemes, though optimal in terms of error performance, may not be best suited for the time and power sensitive flash memory chips.
A. Our Contributions
In this paper, we present three postprocessing detection schemes for the cancellation of CCI component for 2-bit per cell flash memory.1 Here, we follow the even–odd bitline architecture, in which the even bitline cells are programmed first followed by the odd bitline cells. However, our scheme can easily be modified for the all bitline architecture. The proposed work is inspired by the prior-art Post-Comp detector [11] and suggests further improvements. According to [11], the CCI strength is a deterministic quantity and can be represented in terms of voltage-shift of neighboring (interfering) cells and the coupling-capacitance-ratios (CCRs) between the victim and interfering cells. Therefore, to accurately estimate the CCI component, the individual factors that constitute the

II. FLASH MEMORY OVERVIEW AND CHANNEL MODEL
A. Basics of MLC NAND Flash Memory
The flash memory cells are made of floating-gate transistor. These memory cells are physically organized into an array of rows and columns, where individual rows and columns are connected with the wordline and bitline terminals, as shown in Fig. 2. The memory array is logically divided into several memory blocks, where each block is further subdivided into multiple memory pages. The basic unit of memory read or write operation is a memory page, whereas the erase operation is performed on blockwise basis. A memory cell can either be represented with the erased state, where no electrons stored over the floating gate, or with the programmed state, where electrons are tunneled through the oxide-medium and stored

The electron tunneling is performed through the cell programming operation, which configures the threshold voltage of a memory cell to a particular write/verify voltage level. III. CCI POSTPROCESSING DETECTOR DESIGN

A. Post-Comp [11] Detector

The Post-Comp [11] scheme is a symbol-based detector in
the sense that the CCI cancellation is performed on cell-by-
cell basis. The basic idea is to estimate the CCI strength, Iˆ(i, j), based on the readback voltage of neighboring cells, and subsequently remove this quantity from victim cell’s readback
voltage, V(i, j), as

V(i, j ) = V(i, j ) − Iˆ(i, j )

(10)

where V(i, j) is the cleaned (CCI removed) voltage signal. According to [11], the CCI strength for the odd and even
bitline cells can be estimated as

Iˆ(oid, jd) = μγxy [V(i+1, j −1) − Xe] + μγy [V(i+1, j ) − Xe]

+ μγxy [V(i+1, j +1) − Xe]

(11)

Iˆ(eiv,ejn) = μγx [V(i, j −1) − Xe] + μγx [V(i, j +1) − Xe]

+ μγxy [V(i+1, j −1) − Xe] + μγy [V(i+1, j ) − Xe]

+ μγxy [V(i+1, j +1) − Xe].

(12)

Comparing the CCI estimates (11) and (12) with actual CCI expressions (4) and (5), respectively, we observe that the
random variables X (initial voltage level) are replaced with V (readback voltage), and X¨ and γx|y|xy are replaced with their respective mean values Xe and μx|y|xy.
We make two observations from the Post-Comp [11] detector. First, the readback voltage V is not an accurate estimate
of the initial threshold voltage X. It is because the readback
voltage includes the noise and interference effects as given by (8). Second, the mean values μγy and μγxy for couplingcapacitive ratios along the vertical and diagonal directions
are not constants but random variables. In the proposed neighbor-a-priori detection scheme, we will address these
two issues and replace the readback voltage and couplingcapacitive ratios with more accurate estimates.

V. PERFORMANCE AND COMPLEXITY ANALYSIS

To investigate the coded error performance for the proposed detection schemes, we simulate a binary quasi-cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) [29] code with uniform column-weight of 5. VII. CONCLUSION
In this paper, three postprocessing CCI cancellation schemes, namely, the Even-AP, the All-AP, and the All-AP-CCR, for MLC NAND flash memory are presented. The proposed schemes are implemented over the even–odd bitline architecture in this paper, but they can easily be extended for the all bitline architecture. In the Even-AP detector, the even bitline cells are cleaned using the a-priori information from the odd bitline interfering cells. In the All-AP detection scheme, the odd bitline cells are cleaned first, based on the a-priori information from the next wordline cells, followed by the even bitline cells. In the All-AP-CCR scheme, the All-AP detector is extended by estimating the mean values for CCRs along victim cell’s vertical and diagonal directions. For the All-AP-CCR scheme, the estimation of mean CCRs can be performed in the offline mode, avoiding the need for runtime computations. All the proposed schemes yield substantial improvement in the flash channel capacity and coded error performance.
Due to further node scaling, future flash memory chips may experience the CCI effect not only from the immediate neighbors but also from the distant neighbor cells. In that situation, the proposed detection schemes to clean the interfering cells, using (17) and (18), and then to remove the CCI effect from the victim cell will become even more important. This will be an interesting topic for future investigation.
