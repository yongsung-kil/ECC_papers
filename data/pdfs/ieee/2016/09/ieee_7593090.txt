Abstract-In this paper we consider the problem of designing 
LOPC codes to be used in bit turbo-receivers, where an outer 
binary (turbo) decoder iterates with a bit-detector computing 
LLR of coded bits from the channel outputs. This receiver 
structure has been proposed for several scenarios, including 
systems for high order modulations, MIMO , lSI , and Multi 
User systems. It has been shown that the bit turbo-receiver 
provides performance close to the optimal receiver if the bit­
detector EXIT chart "matches" that of the outer code. Motivated 
by the observation that most bit-detector EXIT charts exhibit 
an almost linear behavior we then introduce the "slope" of 
the EXIT chart as a new parameter for the LOPC design. A 
family of binary LOPC codes having the possibility of tuning 
not only the rate, but also the slope of their EXIT chart, as well 
as the decoder complexity, is then an ideal candidate for being 
considered in future advanced transmission systems where the 
bit turbo-receiver will replace the more common BICM receiver 
that does not consider possible iterations with the bit detector. 
In this paper we provide an efficient algorithm for the design of 
LOPC codes ensembles with excellent performances with those 
constraints and derive some conclusions. It is shown that with 
a given performance threshold the complexity of LOPC decoder 
actually decreases when increasing the target slope, confirming 
the already observed fact that bit turbo-receiver requires weaker 
outer codes. 
I. INTRODUCTION AND MOTIVATION 
The investigation of very powerful capacity achieving cod­
ing techniques over the AWGN channel has been subject of 
investigation in literature for decades by now. The state of the 
art adopted in most standards is based on the BICM approach. 
In BICM powerful LDPC binary codes with variable rate are 
coupled with a suitably optimized constellation set. At the 
receiver, no iteration between decoder and detector is usually 
assumed. Although not optimal, this approach has been proved 
to be the best compromise between performance, complexity 
and flexibility. This approach indeed, over the AWGN channel, 
provides performance very close to the information theory 
limits in a wide SNR range while keeping reasonably low the 
receiver complexity. Other coding schemes based on parallel or 
serial concatenation, or more recent one based on non binary 
codes or convolutional ("spatially coupled") LDPC codes do 
not show, on the AWGN channel, performance improvements 
large enough to justify a radical change in this approach. The 
adoption of one coding scheme w.r.t. others on the AWGN 
channel is then justified by background compatibility and 
historical reasons rather than to technical reasons. The study

Regarding the outer code, since no other observation is 
available for coded bits, the area below the decoder EXIT

code itself. The slope of the outer code EXIT chart is still 
defined as in (1). However, since for the outer code input and 
output messages are swapped, 5 = 0 corresponds to the step 
function EXIT chart. 
In [1]-[3], it is shown that in order to have convergence 
of the bit turbo-receiver with the highest possible coding 
rate, the binary decoder should exhibit an EXIT chart that 
"matches" the inverse of the detector EXIT chart. When 
the matching condition is satisfied bit turbo-receiver provides 
reliable decisions with the maximum possible coding rate 
Rc = 1 - 1H(ClY) corresponding to the optimal receiver. 
When using a Gray-like mapping (solid line in Fig. 2) the 
bit-detector slope is almost zero and BICM capacity almost 
coincides with the modulation constrained capacity itself. In 
this favorable situation BICM receiver (with no iterations) 
is almost optimal. Good binary LDPC codes designed for 
adaptive coding and modulation systems in most standards are 
actually designed to match EXIT chart of Gray like mappings 
and consequently their EXIT chart are designed to be as close 
as possible to the step function (5 = 0).

On the other hand, using different bit labelings, the EXIT 
chart of the detector is not anymore flat and exhibits a linear 
behavior (Fig. 2, dashed line, see also [2], [5]-[9] for other 
examples). When employed with these types of bit-detectors 
LDPC codes previously designed (S = 0) cease to be optimal 
and iterations between detector and decoder also provide small 
benefit (see Fig. 3). The maximum rate for such type of 
binary decoders to ensure the convergence of the bit turbo­
receiver still coincide to the BICM capacity of the detector, 
which can be in this case considerably smaller than the the 
modulation constrained capacity. Under the assumption of a 
linear behavior of the EXIT charts the rate loss is half of the 
EXIT slope mismatch between bit- detector and decoder. 
In order to maximize the performance of a bit turbo-receiver 
it is then necessary to use an outer decoder with a given 
matching rate but also a matching EXIT chart. The cited papers 
dealing with optimization of bit turbo-receiver in different 
applications scenario usually used the matching criterion for 
tailoring the used codes to the application at hand. While this 
design approach is optimum, it lacks of the flexibility that is 
necessary to construct physical layers capable of dealing with 
a multiplicity of scenarios. 
In the next section we will find a binary LDPC code family 
with a suitable simple mechanism for tuning not only its 
rate but also the slope of its EXIT chart and its decoding 
complexity. The availability of such flexibility in the binary 
encoding scheme will allow to implement practical capacity 
approaching bit turbo-receivers schemes for the multiplicity of 
scenarios that can be casted as bit-interfered channel scenario.

V. CONCLUSION 
In this paper we motivated the introduction of a new 
parameter, beside the rate and the decoding complexity, for 
the design of binary encoders to be embedded into bit-turbo 
receiver for the general class of bit-interfered channel scenario. 
We named this parameter the slope the EXIT chart. The slope 
of the binary code should be matched to that of the bit-detector 
to get optimal performance from the bit-turbo receiver. 
We then introduced an algorithm to design left degree 
distributions of RQR-LDPC code ensembles with excellent 
performance and tunable slope and complexity.

possibility of tuning also the bit-detector EXIT chart. This
