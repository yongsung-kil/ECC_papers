Abstract-In this paper, we investigate the complexity of differ­ ent algorithms for LP decoding for short BCH and LDPC codes. Two approaches have gained particular interest: The simplex algorithm and the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM). For the adaptive LP decoding algorithm, we propose a special implementation of the dual simplex algorithm that uses the same simplex tableau in every linear program of the adaptive scheme. We compare this algorithm with another variant of the adaptive decoder and two variants of the recently proposed ADMM regarding the required arithmetic operations. These are important measures for design decisions for future hardware implementations.
I. INTRODUCTION
In optimization theory, integer programs, such as the ML decoding problem, are usually solved by branch-and-bound algorithms, which rely on efficient algorithms that solve the linear relaxation of the integer program. For ML decoding, its linear relaxation is the so-called LP decoding problem, which was introduced by Feldman et al. [1]. Since the repeated solving of the LP decoding problem is the main effort in branch-and-bound ML decoders [2], it is crucial to implement the LP decoder efficiently.
One approach for solving LP decoding problems is the use of the simplex algorithm. It is the most important solver for linear programs and an exact method, rather than relying on iteratively improved approximations [3]. In [4], Zhang and Siegel present an efficient adaptive algorithm for solving the LP decoding problem. It builds up the linear program by adding successively constraints and applying the simplex algorithm repeatedly to the growing problem. Only those constraints that were violated by the last LP solution are added to the next LP. In this way, the number of constraints is kept very low. For this adaptive scheme, a special dual simplex algorithm is proposed in [5], that lowers the number of iterations of the simplex algorithm significantly. In [2], the adaptive scheme is used in a very fast ML decoder.
A second general approach for LP decoding algorithms are iterative methods. Especially, the ADMM algorithm, an optimization technique from convex optimization [6], was studied in recent years. For LP decoding, ADMM was first proposed in [7]. The main computational etlort of the ADMM is the euclidean projection to the so-called parity polytope. For this purpose, Barman et al. developed a special two-slice representation for the points of the parity polytope [7]. The
etlort of the projection is O(dlog d), where d is the check

node degree. In [8] the projection is sped up to an expected
time of 0(d) by reducing it to a projection on a simplex. In
[9] an efficient projection has been proposed, that is based on the cut-search algorithm in [4]. In [10] the error correction is improved by adding penalty functions that penalize fractional bit variables to the objective. The efficiency of ADMM is improved for irregular codes by using different penalty pa­ rameters for different variable node degrees in [11]. In [12] decoding time is lowered by omitting unnecessary projections. Furthermore, the convergence of ADMM can be improved by changing the order of the computation schedule in an iteration to a horizontal layered scheduling [13]. Parallelization aspects of ADMM and its comparison to the sum product approach are considered in [14].
In this paper, we investigate implementations of simplex­ based and ADMM-based LP decoding algorithms with respect to their suitability for a future hardware implementation. To this end we will compare the required operations of these decoders for short LDPC and high-density BCH codes. In this comparison, we separately consider operations which have a ditlerent complexity in hardware. In Section II, we repeat some basics of the ADMM and optimization theory. In Section III, we propose a special simplex algorithm for the adaptive scheme in [4]. Some complexity issues of our analysis are addressed in Section IV. In Section V, our numerical results are presented. This is followed by a conclusion.

III. EFFICIENT LP DECODING ALGORITHMS
In this section, we propose a special simplex algorithm for the adaptive scheme in [4]. Additionally, we mention the second simplex-based algorithm and the two ADMM versions which we consider in our numerical comparison.
In our comparison, the two simplex-based methods (tableau­ and LU-based) rely on the adaptive scheme by Zhang and Siegel [4]. In a previous work [5], we showed that a dual bounded version of the simplex algorithm is very efficient in the adaptive scheme. It reduces the number of iterations in comparison to the usual primal simplex algorithm by a factor of 15-20. Hence, we consider two implementations of the dual bounded simplex algorithm.

A. Dual Bounded Simplex Algorithm with Large Tableau
The idea of the standard primal simplex algorithm is to move from one feasible basic solution to another feasible basic solution until an optimality condition is fulfilled. The dual bounded simplex algorithm works in a reversed way. It starts in a basic solution that fulfills the optimality condition

IV. COMPLEXITY ANALYSIS
The purpose of our complexity analysis is to prepare a future hardware implementation. As a complexity metric we are interested in the number of required arithmetic operations and their type. In hardware implementations the complexity of an arithmetic unit is highly dependent on the type of operation (addition, multiplication, division). Therefore different types of operations are considered separately. Due to their computa­ tional efficiency, fixed-point numbers are considered in many applications instead of floating-point numbers.
For fixed-point implementation in hardware the division is the far most complex operation, followed by multiplication. Addition and subtraction are very similar and have a compara­ tively low complexity. Furthermore, comparisons between two numbers are also implemented as subtraction in hardware and therefore count as subtraction. Comparisons with the constant "0" have negligible hardware complexity and do not count. Since the commonly used two's complement number format is used, taking the absolute value requires the implementation of an adder and is therefore counted as addition. In the simplex algorithm there are many multiplications with entries of the constraint matrix A that consists of all added cuts. Every
cut can only have entries from { - I,0, I}. In the ADMM pI
method from [9], the projection also consists of multiplications
with elements that can only be -l or 1. For multiplications
with operands -1, 0, +1, a hardware multiplier is not required.
Multiplications with 0 and + 1 are straightforward, multiplica­
tions with -I correspond to inversion of the sign, which again is implemented mainly by using an adder in hardware. So the
main complexity of a { -1, 0, 1}-multiplication is an addition.
The ADMM and the simplex-based algorithms require only basic arithmetic operations, i.e., division, multiplication, ad­ dition and subtraction. The only exception is the stopping criterion of the ADMM, where two sums of 12-norms are computed. This requires the computation of square roots. Since square roots are very complex operations in hardware, we avoid them by using the infinity norm in the stopping criterion. In our numerical experiments, this change of the norm did not influence the error correction rate. Moreover, to reduce the number of divisions in the simplex algorithm (see, e. g., line 5 of Algorithm 1) and in the ADMM pI projections, they were replaced by multiplications, if possible. For example, comparisons of the form "a-12. < !a!:4;2 were rewritten as ala4 < a3a2.
To decrease the number of iterations in the ADMM al­ gorithms, a range of values were tested for the parameters
(p, ) (Xi . For the best pair (p, ) (Xi , the overrelaxation parameter
was optimized by testing different values for this parameter. The maximum number of iterations in the ADMM algorithms were chosen such that its error correction rate is very close to the LP decoding error rate. B. Short B CH Codes
A completely different behavior can be seen for the dense codes. Here, we chose the (127,64), (63,45) and (63,36) BCH codes. The required numbers of operations are shown in tables IV to VI. One can see that the simplex algorithms outperform the ADMM versions significantly with 6-30 times less operations in the different "operation categories". Both simplex algorithms need very few multiplications and divisions in all codes.
The reason for the low complexity of the simplex algorithms for BCH codes can be explained as follows: The dual bounded simplex algorithm can efficiently use the hard decisions of the received input LLRs as a starting solution. This hard decision vector forms an infeasible, but basic solution, and thus forms a valid starting solution. The dual simplex then always moves from one basic solution to a neighboring basic solution. In contrast to the simplex algorithm the ADMM is an iterative gradient method, that is not restricted to basic solutions, but may require more iterations even if the (hard decision) starting solution is close to the LP solution.
In case of the short BCH codes (where SNR is typically required to be quite large), the hard decision vector already is very close to the exact LP solution. In many cases it is even a neighboring vertex to the LP solution. This fact can be exploited more efficiently by the simplex, which would terminate after one iteration, compared to the ADMM, which converges to the LP solution more slowly.

VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper we have compared two variants of the simplex algorithm (Tableau and LU version) with two versions of ADMM ([9], [8]) regarding their required arithmetic opera­ tions. For LDPC codes mostly the ADMM algorithms have better complexity. The first ADMM projection method needed

less operations over the second projection. For short and dense BCH codes, the simplex algorithms largely outperformed the ADMM algorithms and should be considered for future hardware implementations.
