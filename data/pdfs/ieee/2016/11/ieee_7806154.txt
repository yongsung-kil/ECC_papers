Designing and Encoding QC-LDPC Codes using Matrices over Commutative Rings

Ishai Ilani
SanDisk | a Western Digital Brand Ishai.Ilani@Sandisk.com

AbstractÔÇö This paper uses tools from matrix algebra over commutative rings to simplify the design of parity-check (PC) matrices for Quasi-cyclic (QC) low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. These tools are also used for computing the ranks of PC matrices and developing low complexity encoding methods for QC-LDPC codes. The motivation for this work stems from the need to develop regular QC-LDPC codes with extremely low error floor, and high throughput in both encoding and decoding for use in flash memory devices.
KeywordsÔÇö Adjugate matrix, array of circulants, commutative rings, quasi-cyclic (QC) low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, discovered by Gallager in 1962 [1], were rediscovered and shown to form a class of Shannon-limit-approaching codes in the late 1990s [2], [3]. These codes are known to achieve very good error performance. Ever since their rediscovery, design, construction, decoding, efficient encoding, performance analysis, and applications of these codes in digital communication and storage systems have become very intense. (cf. [4]).
One class of LDPC codes that draws attention in recent years is the class of quasi-cyclic (QC)-LDPC codes. Welldesigned QC-LDPC codes have been shown to perform as well as computer-generated random LDPC codes in terms of bit-error performance, block-error performance, and error floor.
LDPC codes are typically defined by their parity-check matrices. The parity-check matrix of a QC-LDPC code is given as a block matrix where each non zero block is a sparse circulant of the same order [5], [6].
This paper is devoted to the subjects of low complexity design and low complexity encoding of QC-LDPC codes without compromising on decoding performance. The motivation for this work stems from the need to develop regular QC-LDPC codes with extremely low error floor, and high throughput in both encoding and decoding for use in flash memory devices. This paper addresses the issues of design and encoding. The main results of this paper are: (1) A low complexity method for generating full rank PC matrices for QC-LDPC codes, and (2) A low complexity encoding

method for encoding QC-LDPC codes.

The encoding methods of this paper save approximately

25% in encoding complexity over [9], and an even more

significant gains are achieved over the results of [4] and [8].

The improvement in the design of QC-LDPC codes follows

from a theorem regarding Projections Preserving Invertability,

denoted the PPI theorem. This theorem relates the rank of a

parity-check matrix of a QC-LDPC code and the rank of its

proto-matrix. For binary LDPC codes, the proto-matrix is the

matrix obtained from the PC matrix by collapsing invertible

circulants to 1, and non-invertible circulants, (zero blocks), to

0. In general, the PPI theorem applies whenever the order of

the circulants is an integer power of the characteristic of the

field. The PPI theorem may also be used for computing the

rank of a given parity-check matrix comprising of circulants of

the corresponding order without performing a full Gaussian

elimination process. A different approach for computing the

rank of a parity-check matrix comprised of odd sized

circulants was suggested in [7]. The methods of [7] are based

on a Galois Fourier Transform, and require arithmetic in an

extension field GF(q) of GF(2), while the methods of this

paper only use arithmetic over GF(2). As noted above our

results apply to other characteristics, and are not limited to

characteristic 2.

III. DESIGN OF QC_LDPC CODES VIA PPI THEOREM
The PPI theorem may help greatly in designing QC-LDPC codes which are determined by a parity-check matrix H. For the popular class of QC-LDPC codes H may be a mzÓÁênz matrix over GF(2), but may also be considered as a block matrix of order mÓÁên where each block is a zÓÁêz circulant matrix. The columns of H are partitioned to two sets. The first set is associated with the information bits of the code, and the second set is associated with the parity bits of the code. A popular design requirement is to design H of full rank, such that the parity portion of H is invertible. Other code theoretic constraints may be applied. For example it may be required to avoid certain structures leading to short cycles in the Tanner graph representation of the code. If the designer of the code sets z=2l then the conditions of the PPI theorem are satisfied and the designer can be assured that as long as wt(H) is full rank so is H. The individual circulants in H may be tweaked as long as invertability of the circulants in the parity portion of H is preserved, (i.e. invertible circulants are replaced by invertible circulants and non-invertible circulants are replaced by noninvertible circulants).
