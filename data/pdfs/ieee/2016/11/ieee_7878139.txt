LDPC Codes Based on Hamming and BIBD Codes

Huan Ma and Lijun Zhang School of Electr. and Inform. Eng, Beijing Jiaotong Univ.
Beijing, 100044, China Email: ljzhang@bjtu.edu.cn

Abstract—A hybrid construction for LDPC codes based on (14, 8) extended Hamming code and (7, 7, 3, 3, 1) BIBD is proposed. The two short codes are used as components in the parity-check matrix of LDPC codes due to elegant decoding performance and structure properties. The new construction algorithm results in LDPC codes with high code rate, and graceful performance using sum-product algorithm (SPA). Experimental results show that hybrid codes constructed with progressive edgegrowth (PEG) algorithm and (14, 8) extended Hamming code or (7, 7, 3, 3, 1) BIBD perform well. Code with PEG algorithm and (7, 7, 3, 3, 1) BIBD is 2 dB away from Shannon limit in AWGN channel.
Index Terms—dispersed matrix, (14, 8) extended Hamming code, hybrid construction, PEG algorithm, Rayleigh fading channel, (7, 7, 3, 3, 1) BIBD
I. INTRODUCTION
LOW density parity-check (LDPC) codes were proposed by Gallager [1] in 1962. They have been one kind of significant coding schemes in field of telecommunication and received tremendous attention owing to the nature of near Shannon limit with low-complexity decoding. For LDPC codes there are many construction approaches. One class is computer-based algorithm [1-4], such as PEG algorithm [3], dedicating to maximizing the girth of Tanner graph [5]. Another relies on finite mathematics [6-10]. The notion of hybrid construction is developed in earlier work on demonstrating the theory [11]. It involves a combination between an algebraic method and a random method. Based on this observation, a (14, 8) extended Hamming code and a (7, 7, 3, 3, 1) BIBD are selected to be components of LDPC codes in the hybrid construction algorithm.
As for decoding algorithm SPA, its advantage is the low decoding complexity yet the inaccuracy of message passing is a problem when Tanner graph has 4-cycles. Instead, traditional maximum likelihood decoding (MLD) can guarantee the accuracy of message but has the defect of high complexity. Therefore, it is desirable to select codes for which SPA and MLD offer similar performance. Moreover, for the iterative decoders of LDPC codes, 4-cycles have a negative influence. To clarify the relationship of the codes with 4-cycles and 4-cycles free, progressive edge-growth (PEG) algorithm is adopted to generate base matrix to construct codes without 4cycles. Gallager original method is a contrast with code length longer than 3,000. The (14, 8) extended Hamming code and (7, 7, 3, 3, 1) BIBD are conducive to constructing LDPC codes of less 4-cycles. The most attractive point is that the decoding performance of the former for SPA is roughly the same as MLD [12] and the two decoding algorithms for the

latter is very similar. This is the reason why to select these two codes in the construction. Also in this paper, the performance comparison between hybrid codes based on PEG algorithm and PEG codes with codes length within 2,000 is also presented.
II. HYBRID CONSTRUCTION ALGORITHM
A. (14, 8) Extended Hamming Code (EHC)
A parity-check matrix of LDPC codes may be more directly understood by representation of Tanner graph [5] which gives the connection relationship of variable nodes and check nodes. As a common case that four elements of some two rows and two columns share the 1-component, called 4-cycles. This means that message of iterative decoding becomes correlated after two iterations. However, there are many strategies used to break 4-cycles and column splitting is an effective way [12].
Let c0, c1, · · · , and cn−1 denote the columns of the paritycheck matrix H. Consider splitting each column to the uniform metric columns. The length of new column is the same as that of the original one so that 1-components in the original column are distributed in the new column. A regular column weight distribution is described as follows. Assume an integer q (2≤q≤γ) divided by γ, which is the column weight of H, γ = q×γext + b (0≤b<q) where q and b are quotient and remainder, respectively. γext is the column weight of the splitted matrix. Each column ci is split into q, ci,1, ci,2, · · · , ci,q. Column weights of the first b columns ci,1, ci,2, · · · , ci,b are γext +1 and that of the last q −b columns ci,b+1, ci,b+2, · · · , ci,q are γext. The γ 1-components of ci are assigned to ci,1, ci,2, · · · , ci,q via a cyclic strategy. In the first cyclic round, the first ‘1’ of ci is assigned to ci,1, the second ‘1’ is assigned to ci,2, · · · , and the qth ‘1’ is assigned to ci,q. In the second cyclic round, the (q + 1)th ‘1’ of ci comes into ci,1, the (q + 2)th ‘1’ of ci comes into ci,2, · · · , and the 2qth ‘1’ comes into ci,q. Go on like this until all ‘1’s are assigned to the q new columns.
Column splitting leads to a new parity-check matrix of qn columns Hext with the following three structural properties
• Row weight is σ;
• Column weight is γext and γext+1 while γ=nq, or γext otherwise;
• Ones-overlap of two corresponding positions of any two columns is at most one.

B. (7, 7, 3, 3, 1) BIBD
Balanced incomplete block designs (BIBDs) [13] form a significant branch of combinatorial designs. A special subclass of BIBD designs can be used to construct RC-constraint matrices or arrays of 4-cycles free via circulant permutation matrices (CPM) extension.
Let X = {x1, x2, . . . , xm} be a set of m elements. A BIBD of X is defined as a set containing ν elements in X. B1, B2, . . . , Bn are referred to as blocks of ν-subset of X which satisfy the following three conditions:
• Each element of X appears ρ times in n blocks;
• Every two elements appear simultaneously μ time(s) in n blocks;
• ν is much less than m.
BIBD is characterized by five parameters mentioned above, m, n, ν, ρ, μ, which is also called (m, n, ν, ρ, μ)-BIBD. For a special case with μ = 1, there has one and only one block including every two different pairs. In general, any two blocks have exactly one element in common. Without listing all the blocks, a BIBD can be efficiently illustrated by an m×n matrix ψ=[ϕi,j ]
• The rows of ψ correspond to m elements in X;

• The columns of ψ correspond to n blocks;
• If the ith element xi appears in the jth block, the element located at crossing of ith row and jth column is set to ‘1’. Otherwise, the element is set to ‘0’.

The matrix meeting the above requirements is called incidence matrix with row weight ρ and column weight ν.

Suppose X = {x1, x2, x3, x4, x5, x6, x7} of seven elements. There are seven blocks, B1 = {x1, x2, x4}, B2 = {x2, x3, x5}, B3 = {x3, x4, x6}, B4 = {x4, x5, x7}, B5 = {x5, x6, x1}, B6 = {x6, x7, x2}, B7 = {x7, x1, x3}. Seven
blocks compose a BIBD, its incidence matrix

C. Algorithm description
The hybrid construction algorithm mainly involves the combination of two codes. It consists of three (or four) steps.

Firstly, generate a base matrix H1 as one of component codes. Then the (14, 8) EHC or (7, 7, 3, 3, 1) BIBD serves on the other. Finally, design an appropriate mapping rule between the two codes. Beyond that, if column weight of code is two, it is necessary to append a row of unit matrices in the end of the matrix acquired from the third step.

In term of base matrix, three methods are applied to verify the hybrid codes. The first two are computer-based algorithms. One is PEG algorithm [3]. It relates to two sets of nodes, a set of rules and a given variable-node degree sequence. Edges are added to a variable node one at a time utilizing an edgeselecting procedure. This progressive edge addition to one variable node is called edge-growth. The process is carried out from the node with the smallest degree to that with the largest degree due to the frailty of low-degree variable node. This procedure will not stop until the variable node achieves its specified degree. The relationship between this variable node and check nodes being directed are specified according to the rules. As a consequence, the algorithm will terminate after degrees of all variable nodes are achieved.

The other one is Gallager original method. It involves a μ×μρ submatix Hs with column weight 1 and row weight any arbitrary integer ρ [1]. The contiguous ρ 1-elements locate from iρ − (ρ − 1) to iρ for ith row. Then other γ − 1 (assume
that the column weight of Gallager code is γ) submatrices are generated by column permutations with Hs and a μγ×μρ Gallager code is obtained when γ submatrices are organized
in rows.

III. ALGORITHM ANALYSIS
With respect to combination, a rule is made as follows. Replace each 1-component of every column of base matrix

with the corresponding row of the (14, 8) EHC or (7, 7, 3, 3, 1) BIBD and 0-component is substituted for a 14-tuple or 7tuple all zero elements. Not surprisingly, if the size of the base matrix is m×n, an m×14n or m×7n matrix can be obtained. The code rate is easily got for (14n − r)/14n or (7n − r)/7n , where r is the number of the parity-check symbols. Note that the number of rows for (14, 8) EHC and (7, 7, 3, 3, 1) BIBD are all 7, which indicates a restrict that base matrix must have the property of column weight consistent with row number the two codes. By coincidence, the two codes have same row number and this is a convenience that the base matrix designed for one can also apply to the other.

Simulation results given in the following figures show the bit error rate (BER), frame error rate (FER), and average iterations for PEG codes and hybrid codes listed in TABLE II over Rayleigh fading channel with BPSK modulation using SPA. A maximum of 200 iterations is asked to ensure sufficient convergence.
In Fig.3 and Fig.4 are shown the performance comparison between PH∗s and GH∗s, PH∗s and PEG codes. The PH∗s outperform much better than GH∗s, especially for Eb/N0 larger than 13 dB. They have little difference with PEG codes whereas PH∗s show better while the Eb/N0 reaches 15 dB around. Furthermore, PH∗s attain the BER of 10−5 as the Eb/N0 is about 14 dB. From Fig.3, another message is sent that the (14, 8) EHC could be applied to acquire long LDPC codes without PEG algorithm since the performance of PH∗s are not worse than PEG codes. The hybrid construction is likely to cost less time to generate codes with very long length.

V. CONCLUSION
A method to construct LDPC codes base on a (14, 8) extended Hamming code and a (7, 7, 3, 3, 1) BIBD is

presented. With this method, a series of LDPC codes with high code rates can be constructed. If the base matrices are selected properly, elegant decoding performance can be achieved. Simulation results show that the hybrid codes constructed based on PEG algorithm and (14, 8) EHC take on marginally better performance than PEG codes, and far better than that based on Gallager original method. Hybrid code with identical PEG code and (7, 7, 3, 3, 1) BIBD also outperforms that with base code formed by difference set. As to the average number of iterations, PH∗s have faster convergence ability as the numbers of iteration times is restricted within about 15. The simulation for codes of length exceeding 3,000 once again proves the graceful performance of hybrid codes with PEG codes.
