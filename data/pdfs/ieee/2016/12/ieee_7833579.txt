Construction of QC-LDPC Codes Based on Generalized RS Codes with Girth Larger than 6

Rong Sun, Yu Tian, Jingwei Liu State Key Lab of ISN, Xidian University, Xi’an, Shaanxi, China, 710071 Email: rsun@mail.xidian.edu.cn, 530004960@qq.com, jwliu@mail.xidian.edu.cn

Abstract—In this paper, an approach for constructing Quasicyclic(QC)-LDPC codes with girth larger than 6 based on Generalized Reed-Solomon(GRS) codes is presented. The proposed construction method in conjunction with masking, results in codes whose Tanner graphs have girth 8 or even larger. Experimental results show that, with iterative decoding algorithms, the constructed QCLDPC codes perform well.
Index Terms—QC-LDPC, GRS codes, masking, girth.
I. INTRODUCTION Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are currently the most promising channel coding scheme to achieve Shannon capacity for different channels. LDPC codes were firstly discovered by Gallager in the early 1960s [1] and then rediscovered in the late 1990s [2]–[4]. Since then, great efforts have been given into their design, construction, decoding algorithms, performance analyses, and applications. Among various types of LDPC codes, QC-LDPC codes are commonly preferred in practical applications due to their advantages in encoding and decoding implementations. There are two major approaches in the construction of QC-LDPC codes, graph-theoretic [5] and algebraic approaches. QC-LDPC codes constructed algebraically using mathematical tools such as finite fields, finite geometries and combinatorial designs, in general, give better performances in terms rate of decoding convergence and error-floors. Especially, LDPC codes with large minimum distances can be constructed easier via algebraic methods [6]–[15]. In this paper, we present a generalized framework for construction of QC-LDPC codes. In our work, we firstly propose a generalized framework for constructing the base matrix based on GRS codes. We further show that the constructed codes based on GRS codes have girth larger than 4. We then show that this algorithm with masking matrix could keep the Tanner graphs with girth at least 8. Experimental results show that the codes

constructed using the proposed method perform well and have low error-floors.
II. PRELIMINARIES In this section, we will introduce the basic knowledge of the QC-LDPC codes based on algebraic construction and the masking matrix. The algebraic construction of LDPC codes was firstly put forward in [4] and the masking matrix was proposed in [5].

Suppose we replace a nonzero entry in W (m, n) by the zero element in GF (ps). In the matrix Hdisp(m, n) constructed by W (m, n), the replacement results in replacing a (ps − 1) × (ps − 1) CPM in Hdisp(m, n) by a (ps − 1) × (ps − 1) ZM, which is referred to as masking.

III. CONSTRUCTION OF QC-LDPC CODES FROM GRS
In this section, we first give the construction method of QCLDPC codes based on GRS codes. Then we give the constraints for without length-4-cycle and without length-6-cycle, based on which, using the masking technique, we can obtain different QCLDPC codes for different requirements.

A. Construction of GRS codes

MDS (Maximum Distance Separable) codes, which GRS codes belong to, are a class of linear codes whose minimum distance is maximum. The definition of GRS codes is as follows [15].
Let β = (β1, β2, ..., βn), where βi is an element of GF (ps), and if i = j, βi = βj. Let v = (v1, v2, ..., vn), where vi, 1 ≤ i ≤ n are the nonzero elements (but not necessarily distinct) in GF (ps). All the vectors which satisfy (v1F (β1), v2F (β2), ..., vnF (βn)) are defined as [n, k, n − k + 1] GRS codes over GF (ps), which we call GRSk(β, v). F (x) is a polynomial of degree d(d < k) with coefficients chosen from GF (ps), which we call information polynomial. It has already been proved that the minimum distance of the GRS code is at least n − k + 1 [16].

Definition 2. Let W be the base matrix by which the paritycheck matrix H is constructed. If the code constructed from H has girth at least 8, we call that W satisfies the without length6-cycle constraint.

Theorem 2. A necessary and sufficient condition for W satisfies without length-6-cycle constraint is that the determinant of an arbitrarily 2 × 2 submatrix Wsub(2, 2) in the matrix W is nonzero and an arbitrarily 3 × 3 submatrix Wsub(3, 3) in the matrix W satisfies the equation (13).

Proof: Sufficiency: Depending on the Theorem 1, the determinant of an arbitrarily 2 × 2 submatrix Wsub(2, 2) in the matrix W

Necessity: It can be directly observed that the base matrix W without length-6-cycle must meet the equation (13), and the character of the base matrix W without length-4-cycle has already been proven in the Theorem 1.

E. Construction of QC-LDPC Codes based on GRS Here, we give the construction method of QC-LDPC codes
based on GRS. Using this method, we can obtain different QCLDPC codes for different requirement.
Construction method of QC-LDPC codes with girth larger than 4 or larger than 6 based on GRS is as follows:
• Step1: Decide the finite field GF (ps) and find a primitive element α of the field.
• Step2: Find β, v, and γ0 in the finite field. Form the base matrix WGRS from the parameters.
• Step3: Select part of the base matrix WGRS (r rows and c columns) and get the matrix Hdisp(r, c) with code length n = c∗ps and information length k = r ∗ps. If constructing codes with girth larger than 4, stop here, else go to step4.
• Step4: Search the masked base matrix Wmask(r, c) using Algorithm 1 and get the masked matrix Hmask(r, c).

Let GF (28) with p = 2 and s = 8 be the field for code construction and α be a primitive element of the field. Let β = (α−1, α−2, ..., α−(ps−2)), v = (α1, α2, ..., α(ps−2)) and γ0 = 1. We select the elements of matrix WGRS from the 1st to the 4th rows and the 8th to the 16th columns as base matrix WGRS(4, 8) to construct a (2048, 1024) QCLDPC code. Masking the base matrix to a masked base matrix Wmask, we can construct a (3, 6) regular (2048, 1024) code with girth larger than 6 and an irregular (2048, 1024) code with girth larger than 6. The BER (Bit Error Ratio) and FER (Frame Error Ratio) performances of these three (2048,1024) QC-LDPC codes are given in Fig.2 and Fig.3 respectively. We also give the performances of CCSDS (2048, 1024) LDPC codes for comparison. These codes are modulated using BPSK and transmitted via AWGN channel and decoded using an SPA (SumProduct Algorithm) decoder with maximum 50 iterations.
From Fig.2 and Fig.3, we see that the decoding performances of the codes using masking with girth larger than 6 are better. The performance of the generated irregular QC-LDPC codes with girth larger than 6 is almost similar to that of CCSDS codes. At the BER of 10−6, the performance gap is about 0.4dB between irregular (2048, 1024) QC-LDPC codes using masking and (2048, 1024) QC-LDPC codes without masking.
