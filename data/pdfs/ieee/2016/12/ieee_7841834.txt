Extrinsic Dispersion Transfer Chart for Finite
Length LDPC Codes Design
Ahmed Abotabl and Aria Nosratinia
Department of Electrical Engineering, University of Texas at Dallas, Richardson, TX 75083-0688 USA,
E-mail: ahmed.abotabl@utdallas.edu;aria@utdallas.edu.
Abstract—The most popular methods for the analysis of LDPC
codes, e.g. density evolution and EXIT charts, apply to infinite
length ensembles. Finite length analysis is less common, among
the notable examples are scaling law analysis and the EXIT
band chart. The scaling law is limited to the waterfall region
and furthermore is limited to the final error rates and does
not give insight into the progression and dynamics of belief
propagation decoding. The EXIT band chart was based on
empirical considerations and does not enjoy a strong underlying
theory. The developments of this paper are motivated by the
recent results by Polyanskiy, Poor and Verdu showing that the
rate penalty due to finite blocklength depends on the channel
dispersion. We propose tracking the dispersion between the
transmitted bits and the log-likelihood ratio across the decoder
iterations to capture the effect of the codeword length. Based
on the proposed analysis, a simple lower bound on the block
error probability as a function of the blocklength is derived. The
bound is corroborated by simulation.
I. INTRODUCTION
Density evolution [1], [2] and EXIT chart [3]–[5] allow the
analysis of iterative decoding at the limit of infinite block
length. EXIT chart analysis is for the most part useful for
the AWGN channel as it tracks only mutual information, in
contrast with density evolution which tracks the probability
distributions of the log-likelihood ratio (LLR). Variations of
both techniques have been used in the design of LDPC codes.
The present paper concentrates on finite block length analy-
sis. Amraoui [6] proved that the average performance of LDPC
codes in the binary erasure channel follows a simple scaling
law. The study of finite LDPC codes in the AWGN channel is
more complicated. Lee and Blahut [7] proposed the EXIT band
chart, using empirical observation of the variance of EXIT
curves under finite block length, which was modeled with
a Gaussian distribution. However, this method did not enjoy
an analytic basis and does not lend itself easily to providing
guidance for the design process.
The maximum achievable rate for a given blocklength and
error probability was first studied by Strassen [8] for the
discrete memoryless channel and was recently generalized
for the AWGN channel by Polyanskiy, Poor and Verdu [9],
showing that the rate penalty due to finite blocklength is
characterized by:

Motivated by this result, we study and characterize the vari-
ations in the EXIT curves. The idea is to track the information
density, the random variable whose expected value yields the
mutual information between the LLRs and the transmitted
bits. Since the EXIT curves track the mutual information,
the variations in EXIT curves due to the lack of hardening
(itself due to short block length) are essentially nothing but
an incomplete hardening of information density at finite block
lengths. Therefore, we propose tracking the variance of the
information density, i.e. the channel dispersion. We show the
effect of the processing of both the variable nodes and the
check nodes on the dispersion. Subsequently, we derive a
lower bound on the average block error probability for any
given LDPC code ensemble as a function of the blocklength
𝑛. The bound is corroborated with simulation.

Evaluating the channel dispersion of the output LLR of
a check node is more complicated. However, a Gaussian
approximation of the output LLR is still practical even for
short blocklength codes. This is because the practical length
of short codes is at least several hundreds. Using this Gaussian
approximation, the dispersion of the output LLR of a check
node can be obtained using empirical simulations. Fig. 4 shows
the dispersion of the output LLR of the check node decoder
versus the variance of the input LLR. The dispersion of the
input LLR is also shown for comparison. The figure shows that
the check node degree has a different effect on the dispersion
than the variable node degree since increasing the check node
degree does not reduce the dispersion but it just delays it’s
decay. In other words, the check node decoder increases the
dispersion at the end of the decoding and this increase is
delayed further with increasing 𝑑𝑐. These two figures show
that it might be favorable to have a high variable node degree
and a small check node degree.
C. Dispersion Evolution
The results imply that as the variance of the LLR increases,
the dispersion increases at the check node decoder but de-
creases at the variable node decoder. Towards the very end of
successful decoding, the dispersion decreases in both variable
node decoder and check node decoder. Fig. 5 shows the
conventional EXIT chart for a regular LDPC code with 𝑑𝑣= 3
and 𝑑𝑐= 4. For every EXIT curve, another curve that is one
tenth of the dispersion away from the original curve. This
figure shows an indication about how the dispersion evolve
with the iterations.

IV. ERROR ANALYSIS
The main concern with the infinite blocklength analysis is
the threshold of the code, in other words, the minimum SNR
at which the decoder will be able to decode the transmitted
codeword. However, in finite blocklength transmission, there is
always a probability of error and the waterfall of the probabil-
ity of error with the SNR becomes more gradual than abrupt.
Analyzing the probability of error for finite blocklength is very
challenging specially with iterative decoding algorithms.
The channel dispersion was shown to capture the effect
of blocklength on the maximum achievable rate at a given
permissible probability of error [9]. In the same way, the
probability of error can be approximated as follows

The LLR at the variable node before the decoder starts
it’s operation is equivalent to the received signal, and hence,
the mutual information as well as the dispersion between the
transmitted bit and the LLR can be used to determine the
probability of error under maximum likelihood decoding. With
the belief propagation decoding, the mutual information and
the dispersion between the transmitted bits and the LLR at
the variable nodes can be used to calculate the probability
of error using (14) if these LLR values are the input to a
maximum likelihood decoder. Therefore, a lower bound on the
probability of error can be given by (14) for a given mutual
information and dispersion.

results for the average performance of an ensemble with the
same specifications show that the lower bound is a very good
approximation to the probability at higher number of iterations.

very close to the simulated probability of error at high values
of 𝐸𝑏/𝑁𝑜.
V. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
In this paper we proposed a new EXIT analysis that captures
the effect of finite blocklength for iterative decoding in LDPC
codes. Motivated by the recent results in the maximum achiev-
able rate of finite blocklength codes, the metric that was used
during the paper to study the finite blocklength effect was the
channel dispersion. The evolution of the channel dispersion
through the variable node and the check node decoders were
studied. It was shown that the channel dispersion varies
increases in the beginning of the iterative decoding and then
decreases as the decoding reaches its successful end. The
complete characterization of the probability of error remains
an open problem for future investigation.
