Construction of Quasi-Cyclic LDPC Codes via Masking With Successive Cycle Elimination
Hengzhou Xu, Dan Feng, Rui Luo, and Baoming Bai, Member, IEEE

Abstract— In this letter, we study the construction of quasi-cyclic (QC) low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes by array dispersion and masking. We first propose a class of arrays of circulant permutation matrices (CPMs) of arbitrary size obtained by dispersion. Based on this class of arrays of CPMs, a class of QC-LDPC codes is constructed. For these resulting codes, we analyze their cycle structure and derive the numbers, which indicate how many cycles pass through each CPM in their parity-check matrices. Following the indication numbers with the order from largest to smallest, we replace the corresponding CPMs with zero matrices (ZMs) of the same size one by one until a targeted masking termination condition is satisfied. Then, the resultant arrays of CPMs and ZMs give a large class of QCLDPC codes. Numerical results show that the codes constructed perform well over the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel when decoded with the sum-product algorithm.
Index Terms— LDPC codes, quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes, dispersion, masking, girth.
I. INTRODUCTION
Q UASI-CYCLIC low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes [1] have advantages of easy hardware implementation of encoding and decoding, fast convergence of iterative decoding, and low error floor. Therefore, lots of research effort has been expended on the construction [2]–[4], encoding [5], [6], decoding [7], and analysis [8] of QC-LDPC codes.
A QC-LDPC code is given by the null space of a sparse parity-check matrix H that consists of circulant permutation matrices (CPMs) of the same size. If H has column weight γ and row weight ρ, then the null space of H gives a (γ , ρ)regular QC-LDPC code. If row and column weight of H are not both constant, then the resultant code is irregular. Tanner graph [9] is a simple and common way to describe LDPC codes in coding theory. There is a one-to-one correspondence between Tanner graph and the parity-check matrix H of an LDPC code. If a cycle in the Tanner graph of H passes through a variable node or check node in a CPM of H, we say this cycle passes through the CPM or say the CPM is on this cycle. The length of the shortest cycle is called girth of the Tanner graph and we denote it by g. In the iterative decoding, the exact log-likelihood ratios (LLRs) would be yielded in the first

Thus, LDPC codes with large girth and small number of short cycles have good performance [10]. A technique, known as masking [2], can be used to drastically reduce the number of short cycles and possibly enlarge girth.
In this letter, we focus on the construction of QC-LDPC codes. QC-LDPC codes, in general, are constructed on the basis of algebraic tools, such as finite fields [11], partial geometries [12], combinational designs [13], and graph theory, such as protograph [14], [15]. Recently, a strong connection between algebraic-based and protograph-based LDPC codes had been investigated [16].
Motivated by the work of [17], we construct QC-LDPC codes based on array dispersion and masking in this letter. Different from [17], we design the masking matrix by employing successive cycle elimination and the size p of circulants of QC-LDPC codes is extended from the prime number to the positive integer. More specifically, our construction can be divided into four steps. First, we construct a class of arrays of CPMs of arbitrary size by array dispersion, then a class of QC-LDPC codes is obtained. Second, we analyze the cycle structure of the resulting codes, and propose an algorithm for calculating the numbers which indicate how many cycles pass through each CPM in the parity-check matrices. Third, based on the indication numbers, we design an appropriate masking matrix for the constructed QC-LDPC code. Finally, on the basis of the parity-check matrices of constructed codes and corresponding designed masking matrices, we can construct a class of QC-LDPC codes. Numerical results show that the constructed codes perform well under iterative decoding over the AWGN channel.

IV. CONSTRUCTION OF QC-LDPC CODES BASED ON ARRAY DISPERSION AND MASKING
A. Design of Masking Matrices
Consider a γ × ρ array H of CPMs of size p × p. By replacing a set of CPMs of H with ZMs of size p × p, a γ × ρ array Hmask = M ⊗ H of CPMs and ZMs is obtained. This replacement is called masking [2]. Hmask is referred to as a masked matrix. The masking matrix M of size γ × ρ is defined as
M = mi, j 0≤i≤γ −1,0≤ j ≤ρ−1,
where mi, j = 1 if the row-i and column- j entry of Hmask is a CPM, and mi, j = 0 if the row-i and column- j entry of Hmask is a ZM. Obviously, the Tanner graph of Hmask contains much fewer short cycles than that of H.
Many masking matrices have been constructed by computer search under certain criteria or rules [10], [17], [18], [19]. Here we design the masking matrix by maximizing the number of reduced short cycles and possibly increasing the girth value for a given γ × ρ array H of CPMs and/or ZMs. We first employ Algorithm 1 to compute the indication number of each CPM in H on short cycles, and then replace the CPM in H, which corresponds to the largest indication number, with ZM of the same size. Repeat the above process until a targeted masking termination condition is satisfied. Hence, short cycles are successively eliminated in the design of masking matrix. V. CONCLUSION
In this letter, we first constructed a class of QC-LDPC codes by array dispersion.
