Guaranteed Error Correction of Faulty Bit-Flipping Decoders under Data-Dependent Gate Failures

Abstract—In this paper we analyze the effect of hardware unreliability to performance of bit-flipping decoders of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. We apply expander arguments to show that the simple parallel bit flipping decoder, built partially from faulty gates, can correct a linear fraction of worst case channel errors, when gate failures are correlated and dependent on the switching activity of logic gates. In addition, we provide a lower bound on the guaranteed error correction of LDPC codes with left degree of at least eight.
I. INTRODUCTION
Due to huge density integration increase, lower supply voltages, and variations in technological process, complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor (CMOS) and emerging nanoelectronic devices are inherently unreliable. It is widely accepted that future generations of circuits and systems must be designed to deal with unreliable components. Recently, there has been a surge in interest in error control schemes based on low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes that can ensure fault-tolerance in unreliable hardware.
Most of the relevant research had been aimed to the asymptotic density evolution analysis of faulty iterative messagepassing decoders of LDPC codes under independent failure model (see [1] and reference therein). In contrast to the perfectly reliable decoders, asymptotic analysis of faulty decoders showed that arbitrary small error probability can not be achieved even if code length tends to infinity [2]. However, density evolution is restricted to the independent failure models [2] and the above conclusion do not necessary holds for the practically important data-dependent gate failure models. Recently, we studied the bit error rate performance of harddecision decoders under data-dependent gate failures in a number of papers [3]–[5].
Here we investigate the error correction capability of faulty bit-flipping (BF) decoders under data-dependent gate failures, which appear in sub-powered electronic circuits, where low supply voltage levels cause incorrect switching of logic gates [6], [7]. Treating these failures represent a major challenge in new energy-efficient nano-electronic devices.
Guaranteed error correction of LDPC codes and iterative decoders have been studied in numerous papers, but with rather limited results. Zyablov and Pinsker [8] analyzed the parallel BF algorithm and proved that asymptotically, almost all codes in the regular code ensemble with left degree γ ≥ 5, can correct a fixed fraction of errors, which means that exist some α, 0 < α < 1 for which the decoder can correct αn worst

case errors, where n is the code length. Sipser and Spielman [9] proved that both serial and parallel bit-flipping algorithms can correct a fixed fraction of errors if the underlying Tanner graph is a good expander. In the later work Burshtein [10] generalized their results and proved that a linear number of errors can be corrected by the parallel bit-flipping algorithm with almost all codes in (γ ≥ 4, ρ > γ)-regular ensemble. The expander graph arguments can be also used to provide guarantees of the message passing algorithms, at it was shown by Burshtein and Miller [11] and linear programming shown by Feldman et al. [12]. Recently, Chilappagari et al. [13] provided another look on the guaranteed error correction of the bit-flipping algorithms. They found the relation between the girth of the Tanner graph and the guaranteed error correction capability of an LDPC code.
In this paper we show that expander graph arguments can provide guarantees for the error correction under datadependent failures, which represents the first results on guarantee error correction of faulty decoders. We prove that all expander codes with expansion higher than 7/8γ can correct a fixed fraction of worst case errors. Additionally, we extend results presented in [13] and prove that the error correction of faulty BF decoders can also be guaranteed when Tanner graph of a code has sufficient girth, for all codes with γ ≥ 8, which represents a lower bound on guaranteed correction capability of the faulty BF decoders. Long version of this paper is under review in IEEE Trans. Inf. Theory. [1].
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. In Section II we give a brief description of the faulty bit-flipping decoder and the error model used in our analysis. In Section III the guaranteed error correction of faulty BF decoders is investigated. Section IV includes the numerical results. Finally, some concluding remarks and future research directions are given in Section V.

The cardinality of U , denoted as |U |, represents the order of the graph, while |E| defines the size of the graph. A set of neighbors of a particular node u is denoted as N (u). The number of neighbors of a node u, denoted as d(u), is called the degree of u. The average degree of a graph G is d¯ = 2|E|/|U |.

The girth g of a graph G is the length of smallest cycle in G. A bipartite graph G = (V ∪ C, E) is a graph constructed from two disjoint sets of nodes V and C, such that all neighbors of nodes in V belong to C and vice versa. The nodes in V are called variable nodes and nodes from C are check nodes. A bipartite graph is said to be γ-left-regular if all variable nodes have degree γ, and similarly, a graph is ρ-right-regular if all check nodes have degree ρ.

B. Data-Dependent Gate Error Model
Due to supply voltage reduction, switching of a gate output is prolonged and the signal is sampled or used in the next stage before it reaches a steady value. Recently, Amaricai et al. [7] investigated the probabilistic nature of gate switching for subpowered CMOS circuits. They proposed several fault injection models in CMOS circuits in which errors are added only when the gate output changes.
In this subsection we define the probabilistic gate-output switching model (GOS), in which the logic gate switches incorrectly with a probability that depends on a supply voltage, temperature and considered gate delay. This model was shown to have reduced complexity with minor degradation of accuracy when compared to more complex models that take into account the fact that different input patterns cause failures with different probabilities.
Let zi be the correct output of a logic gate used in the i-th decoding iteration. Due to unreliability of the gate, the actual output is zi ⊕ ξi, where ξi ∈ {0, 1} is the error induced in the i-th iteration. In the GOS error model the probability that a XOR gate fails to switch at the i-th iteration is Pr{ξi = 1|zi−1 = zi} = ε¯, where ε¯ > 0. On the other hand, when the gate output is unchanged during two consecutive time instants, the gate output is always correctly computed as assumed in [6] and [7], i.e. Pr{ξi = 1|zi−1 = zi} = 0.
III. ERROR CORRECTION OF THE FAULTY BF DECODER

In the previous analysis we assumed that XOR gates are unreliable, but not the MAJ gates. If we allow MAJ gates to be prone to data-dependent gate failures, the error correction cannot be guaranteed. This follows from the fact that in the worst case scenario correction of every variable can be annulled by the MAJ logic gate failure.
Note that the decoder’s correcting capability depends not only on the expansion property of its Tanner graph, but also on the number of XOR failures in the first iteration (CXOR). For too many XOR gate failures during the first iteration, the decoding process will not converge to a correct codeword. Recall from the GOS error model that CXOR depends on the XOR gates failures at time instant prior to the first decoding iteration. We do not have any control over the number of XOR gate failures before decoding has started, but there is a practical way to overcome this, and force CXOR to be zero. Before we start decoding a new codeword we can force all transistor-level transient processes in the decoding circuitry to reach a stationary state, so that there are no transitions at gate outputs nor accumulated errors, prior to the start of decoding. Practically, this can be done by slightly slowing down the clock in the first iteration and letting the signal level stabilize. Since the clock is slower, there are no timing errors and the XOR computations are reliable, which yields CXOR=0.
We next compare our results with the results from [9] where a reliable decoder was considered. It can be observed that the presence of the XOR gate failures reduces the number of errors that can be tolerated by the bit-flipping decoder. For example, when the Tanner graph has the expansion of (7/8+ ), the perfect decoder can correct 9/16αn errors, which is two times higher than the error correction capability of the faulty decoder. In the limiting case = 1/8 the number of correctable errors is upper bounded by 3αn/8, which is only the 3/8 of the number of errors correctable by the decoder built from reliable components.
The problem of explicit construction of expander graph, with the expansion arbitrary close to γ (called lossless expanders), was investigated by Capalbo et al. in [14], where it was shown that the required expansion 7/8 + achieve graphs with left-degree γ = poly(log(γ/ρ), 8/(1 − 8 )). This proves the existence of a expander code that can tolerate a fixed fraction of errors under data-dependent gate failures.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper we showed that the concept of guaranteed error correction can be expanded to faulty bit-flipping decoders for codes with high left-degrees. Additionally, we show that high graph expansion is required for guaranteed error correction of the faulty bit-flipping decoders. These requirements usually leads to complex decoders realizations and improving

the required condition represents a major challenge. However, our results present the first known results regarding the guaranteed error correction of LDPC decoders made of unreliable components. The expander arguments have proven to be suitable for the analysis of the message passing decoders and it would be challenging to continue Burshtein and Millers work [11] on the irregular LDPC codes, for the case when node operations are unreliable.
