High Rate LDPC Based Decoder Architectures with High Speed ADC for C-RAN Optical Fronthaul

Ao Li, Vahid Meghdadi, Jean-Pierre Cances and Christelle Aupetit-Berthelemot

XLIM, UMR CNRS 7252/University of Limoges, 16 rue Atlantis, 87068 Limoges Cedex, France e-mail: ao.li@xlim.fr

Abstract вҖ“ In this paper we study the design of LDPC decoding scheme for very high throughput optical communications for a full self-seeded optical architecture dedicated to cloud radio access network (C-RAN). The objective of this paper is to demonstrate the potentiality of Bit Flipping (BF) algorithms. It is shown that the Gradient Descent Bit Flipping (GDBF) algorithm with a little improvement is suitable for high speed applications. With a simple high-speed two-bit Analog to Digital Convertor (ADC) we show that we can obtain a BER of as low as 10-9 for an input BER of 10-4. Furthermore, the proposed decoder achieves a BER of at least 10-7 after just one iteration. It is necessary for real time applications to reduce the decoding delay. We propose a simple FPGA implementation that gives 500 Mb/s. The guidelines are also suggested to increase the throughput up to 2.5 Gb/s, required by CRAN applications.
Keywords: LDPC FEC; C-RAN; hard decision; fronthaul; FPGA
I. INTRODUCTION In this digital age, we are witnessing an unprecedented rise in bitrate demand in order to fit the various multimedia uses increasingly preferred by users who want connections ubiquitous (anywhere, anytime with any device). In the field of radio communications, deployment of the current radio architecture in order to meet the demand is not feasible for the operator, as this will result in operating costs and implementation extremely expensive. This situation requires a new radio architecture, which, in addition to the above constraints, should take into account future radio standards. It is in this context that a new innovative architecture called Cloud-Radio Access Network (C-RAN) is proposed [1]. In traditional architecture the basic unit (BBU: Base Band Unit), which is responsible for digital processing of signals, is located on the antenna site with the radio unit (RRH: Remote Radio Head), which is responsible for the radio operation. In the C-RAN, the BBU is moved from the antenna site to a secure site called Central Office (Central Office: CO) (Figure 1).
Figure 1. C-RAN architecture concept
Moving the BBU from the antenna site to the CO brings up a new segment called fronthaul. This segment uses Digitalized-

Radio over Fiber technology (D-RoF) which is governed by the CPRI specification (Common Public Radio Interface) and OBSAI (Open Base Station Architecture Initiative) [1]. To overcome the expensive solution of using a point-to-point distribution network between the RRH and BBU, WDM (Wavelength Division Multiplexing) is pointed to be an interesting solution. This WDM technology can be based on self-seeded Reflective Semiconductor Optical Amplifier (RSOA) since it brings advantages in terms of cost by using colorless and identical devices at each termination of the network and moreover simplified wavelength operation: the wavelength is automatically and only set by the connection to a channel of a multiplexer associated to a mirror, both of them placed in the infrastructure of the network [2][3][4]. To enhance the transmission performances, Forward Error Codes (FEC) could be developed specifically for this technology. The challenge is to define FEC suitable for the optical fronthaul link in order to achieve throughputs of 2.5 Gb/s and 10 Gb/s.
In this typical context, the objective of this paper is to study the possibilities offered by LDPC (Low Density Parity Check) [5][6] FEC. In the optical transmission, LDPC codes can be good candidates because they can be exploited with high coding rate (typically 0.9). Their performance and cost of exploitation are largely determined by the choice of the decoding algorithm, which is iteratively operated by exchanging messages between the individual processing nodes. Among the various decoding algorithms, BP (Belief Propagation) algorithm and approximate MS (Min-Sum), using flexible decisions, offer the best performance on the AWGN (Additive White Gaussian Noise) channel with binary input [5] [6]. However, the number of necessary arithmetic operations creates limits for high speed telecommunication systems. Architectures with parallel processing allow speeding up the decoding. Other solutions consist in using hard inputs, such as BF (Bit Flipping), WBF (Weighted Bit Flipping), the MWBF (Modified Weighted Bit Flipping) [7][8] and SGDBF (Single Gradient Descent Bit Flipping) [9][10]. These suboptimal algorithms are compared in this paper. Today, we have technological possibilities to sample the received signal at 2.5 G sample per second but the ADC resolution is limited. We show that two-bit ADC can improve the performance significantly. The implementation of the algorithms has been done on FPGA and the LDPC decoder performance has been analyzed.
The first part briefly recalls the principles of LDPC codes with various aforementioned algorithms. LDPC codes performances are presented in the concerned context. Last part shows the FPGA implementation that we propose for the system.

We tested these two methods in our simulations with high rate codes, 0.7 and 0.9. We observe that the mixed-GDBF is preferred to multiple-GDBF or single-GDBF. IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
We perform a set of simulations in which, we counted the number of corrected bits for each iteration. For high-rate codes (0.9 for example), we used first single bit flipping and after some iterations when the number of errors is sufficiently reduced we used multiple-flipping. We observe that the best convergence is obtained when the switching from single to multiple flipping, is done where approximately 4 bits (detected by checking the parity equations) among the 1000 code-word bits are in error. In that case, it is generally sufficient to operate with one more iteration to correct all the remaining errors.
For low rate codes (0.5 or 0.7 for example), we recommend first multiple then single flipping GDBF. It is important to know when we should switch from multiple to single flipping. After a large number of simulations, we observe that when the number of errors is about 5, we should change the flipping method. That means that the multiple bit flipping will work at the beginning, and when the number of error bits is about 5, the single bit flipping begins. Those above algorithms will reduce the latency of the circuit and improve the performance.
The following result will be illustrated and compared with the optimized ЙҒ that we obtained in part III.
According to the result of simulation, for coding rate 0.7, the performance of mixed-GDBF or SGDBF when the input is quantified (use of 2-bit ADC) is degraded compared to SGDBF with soft (floating point) inputs. However, among those algorithms with quantification, multiple then single flipping GDBF gives the best performance.
Figure 6 shows different performance between SGDBF and mixed-GDBF with 2-bit ADC with coding rate = 0.9. VII. CONCLUSION
This article shows the potentiality of LDPC FEC on high throughput transmission for optical fronthaul in the C-RAN context. Different decoding strategies have been presented for high rate LDPC code. In the case of bit flipping algorithms, the effect of two-bit sampling was considered and the performance improvement was given. The best threshold has been determined by simulation. For decreasing the complexity of implemented circuit, we proposed a one-iteration decoder method that achieves a good performance. The correction rate is deeply analyzed. A realistic architecture based on Xilinx FPGA has also been given, where a bit rate up to 2.5 Gb/s could be obtained. As our future work, we are implementing the proposed LDPC decoder on FPGA, which will be inserted in an overall C-RAN demonstrator.
