Layered LDPC Decoding for Turbo-Differential Decoding 
in Presence of Cycle Slips in Optical Communications 
Christian Cabirol, Wolfgang Sauer-Greff, and Ralph Urbansky 
Chair for Communications Systems 
University of Kaiserslautern, 67663 Kaiserslautern, Germany 
Tel: (0049) 631 205 2090, e-mail: Cabirol@eit.uni-kl.de 
ABSTRACT 
In optical communication systems using wavelength division multiplexing, the transmission link can no longer 
be described as an AWGN channel as the co-propagating signals influence each other due to the nonlinearity of 
the fiber. As a consequence of the resulting phase noise, frequent cycle slips may occur in the carrier phase 
estimation which can be counteracted by the use of differential modulation. To mitigate random transmission 
errors, state-of-the-art Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes are for large coding gains. 
The differential decoding penalty due to error doubling can be reduced by iterative demodulation and 
decoding. For AWGN channels, this penalty is almost entirely recoverable. However, cycle slips induce 
contradictions of the received channel sequence and the a-priori information from the LDPC decoder in the 
differential demodulation process leading to non-negligible error floors. We show that using so-called layered 
LDPC decoding, the number of required iterations for iterative demodulation and decoding can be reduced and 
the sensitivity towards cycle slips can be improved. 
Keywords: iterative demodulation and decoding, layered LDPC decoding, differential modulation, cycle slips. 
1. INTRODUCTION
Carrier recovery is of crucial importance for coherent optical transmission systems. Proper tracking of the carrier 
phase is needed for successful demodulation of the received signal. By removing the modulation, e.g. using the 
Viterbi&Viterbi phase estimator [1], this phase tracking can be achieved without insertion of pilots which goes 
along with a loss of net data rate. As the signal constellation is invariant under rotation of 𝜋𝜋2

Typically, the carrier phase fluctuates, caused by to laser phase noise, slowly around a stable operating point. 
However, due to fiber nonlinearity resulting in effects as Cross-Phase-Modulation (XPM), larger sudden phase 
jumps may occur such that the phase estimator changes to a neighboured operating point which causes all 
subsequent received symbols to be detected erroneously. So the initial phase ambiguity is randomly renewed 
during the transmission process, such an event is called a cycle slip (CS).  
As a countermeasure, differential modulation is employed which encodes the information in the phase 
differences of subsequent symbols rather than directly in the phase of the respective symbols. As the phase 
difference of subsequent symbols is independent of the current operating point of the phase estimator, data can 
be recovered in the case of CS. The drawback of using differential modulation is a doubling of the raw symbol 
error rate for random AWGN-induced symbol errors, as each received symbol is involved in the detection of two 
data symbols. This corresponds to a signal-to-noise-ratio (SNR) penalty in the order of 1.2 dB for error rates of 
2% which can be handled by soft-decision forward error correction (FEC) codes with 20% overhead. The 
overhead is mainly spent for LDPC coding and 2% overhead being reserved for an algebraic clean-up code, e.g. 
a Reed-Solomon (RS) code, to remove the potential error floors of the iterative decoding scheme. 
For the AWGN channel, the differential penalty can be recovered by iterating between soft differential 
demodulation (DD) and soft output decoding of an LDPC code [2]. But this is only true for the AWGN channel, 
whereas in the presence of CS studies [3] have shown that the performance of the iterative decoding scheme is 
severely degraded which is attributed to a mismatch of the received slipped sequence and the a-priori 
information sequence from the LDPC decoder. We will show that a change of the decoding scheme of the LDPC 
decoder and employing so-called layered decoding of the LDPC code significantly increases the tolerance 
towards CS. 
In Section 2 we present the system model as it was used in this work. A brief overview of the decoding scheme 
is given in the subsequent Section. The problems of iterative demodulation and decoding on CS channels are 
considered in Section 4. Results of simulations are given in Section 5 and finally, a conclusion is drawn.

At the receiver side, a turbo loop with reliability information feedback using log-likelihood ratios (LLR) from

The model for the optical phase slip channel includes all signal distortions by the physical channel like AWGN 
and dispersion but also all steps of digital signal processing in front of the differential demodulator on the 
receiver side. It is assumed that equalization is perfectly realised and that the carrier phase estimator is able to 
follow the carrier phase perfectly up to uniformly distributed CS. 
3. CODING SCHEME
To mitigate the 1.2 dB SNR penalty due to the differential modulation, iterative differential demodulation and 
FEC decoding is employed. As usual, only the extrinsic part of the output reliabilities of each constituent 
decoder is used as a-priori input to the respective other constituent decoder of the decoding scheme. As 
differential modulation can be seen as a rate one recursive convolutional code, decoding for the differential code 
is done by use of a maximum a-posteriori SISO decoder [4]. 
The iterative decoding process of the LDPC code can be seen as a message passing between variable nodes 
(VN) and check nodes (CN) in a Tanner graph. The variable nodes correspond to the columns of the check 
matrix and the bits of the code word whereas the CN correspond to the rows of the check matrix. The standard 
belief propagation decoding procedure iterates messages between VN and CN, where in one half-iteration all CN 
compute new messages and in the following half-iteration all VN compute new messages. Iterations are 
performed until a valid code word is found or a pre-determined maximum number of iterations 𝑁𝑁𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖 is reached. 
In this work, a regular (3,20) example LDPC code of length 20000 using the scaled min-sum approximation for 
the decoding. By optimizing the degree distribution which results in irregular LDPC codes, the system 
performance could possibly be improved further. However, optimization should be applied to a phase-slip 
channel, which is still an unsolved problem to our knowledge. The number of iterations over the outer turbo loop 
between differential and LDPC decoder was limited to a maximum of 𝑁𝑁𝑜𝑜𝑜𝑜𝑜𝑜𝑜𝑜𝑜𝑜= 15 iterations in all cases. The 
code words were transmitted using QPSK and transferred through a simulated AWGN channel with uniformly 
distributed CS with a probability 𝑃𝑃𝐶𝐶𝐶𝐶= 10−3 like in [3].  
In a previous work [5], we have shown that by using layered decoding of LDPC codes [6] the required number 
of outer iterations to achieve the same decoding performance is reduced by 30%, as can be seen in Fig. 2. Note 
that not only the number of required LDPC decoding steps is reduced but also the number of differential 
decoding steps is reduced by the same factor. This can be attributed to a faster exchange of information between 
CN and VN. Each iteration is divided into a number of sub-iterations according to the number of CNof the code. 
In the decoding process, each CN is updated individually and immediately sends back information to the 
connected VN such that each VN receives several smaller updates in each iteration instead of only one larger 
update as in the case of standard decoding. Thus, layered decoding corresponds to a serial decoding schedule 
considering the CN in contrast to a fully parallel schedule as for standard decoding. By using structured LDPC 
codes, it is also possible to decode several CN in parallel when using layered decoding to meet high throughput 
demands. 
4. ITERATIVE DEMODULATION AND DECODING FOR CYCLE SLIP CHANNELS
In publications like [2] it was shown that iterative demodulation and decoding is suited for a recovery of the 
differential penalty, but the focus is on the AWGN channel without CS. In an analysis of the robustness of the 
scheme towards stressors as CS in [3], a fatal impact of CS on the decoding performance was observed which 
was also visible in own simulations depicted in Fig. 3. The results are visualized as BERin vs. BERout at the input 
and output of the LDPC decoder, respectively, as the distortions due to CS are not taken into account when 
considering only the SNR. After turbo decoding with fixed 𝑁𝑁𝑜𝑜𝑜𝑜𝑜𝑜𝑜𝑜𝑜𝑜, an error floor of almost 10−3 remains for 
𝑁𝑁𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖= 1 and 𝑁𝑁𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖= 2 whereas for 𝑁𝑁𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖= 3, a minimum output BER of 3 ⋅10−6 is reached which 
increases strongly for decreasing input BER. In the error floor, the average number of bit errors in the erroneous 
frames was larger than 1000 in all cases meaning that the clean-up code fails to correct the remaining errors.

Increasing 𝑁𝑁𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖, the LDPC decoder itself gets more powerful and the output BER can be significantly 
reduced for CS channels. So, we propose to use layered LDPC decoding in combination with iterative 
demodulation and decoding for CS channels to make the LDPC decoder as strong as required and 
simultaneously using as few iterations as possible and therefore reduce the overall complexity. 
The steep increase of the output BER for three inner iterations could be attributed to an oscillation process of 
the output BER over iterations. In case of CS events, the estimated symbol sequence and its reliability according 
to the a-priori information originating from the LDPC decoder does not match the received symbol sequence at 
the input of the differential demodulator. This results in a decrease of the output reliabilities of the demodulator. 
Consequently, the LDPC decoder will also output lower reliabilities such that the DD again relies more on the 
channel information corrupted by CS with increased error rates. There is a miss-match of the actual channel and 
the channel model used by the iterative decoder since the DD neglects the existence of randomly distributed CS. 
To overcome this problem, the phase recovery should be included in the iterative decoding process to remove CS 
which seems infeasible due to the increased complexity. 
If the output BER starts to oscillate and the decoder does not converge to a valid code word, it is of great 
interest to stop the decoding process if a minimum BER is reached. Interestingly, it could be observed that for 
fixed 𝐵𝐵𝐵𝐵𝑅𝑅𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖, the iteration with minimum BER in the oscillation process is roughly constant meaning that the 
optimum stopping point in case of an oscillating decoder BER seems to be depending on 𝐵𝐵𝐵𝐵𝑅𝑅𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖. Therefore, 
stopping after a fixed number of outer iterations may result in a low but also in a high number of bit errors per 
erroneous frame.

6. CONCLUSIONS 
Whereas for AWGN channels, the differential penalty can be recovered by an iterative demodulation and 
decoding approach, for scenarios with high CS probabilities, the decoding performance is severely degraded. 
This is due to a CS introduced contradiction of the reliability values from the channel output, the differential 
decoder and the LDPC decoder. By using layered LDPC decoding in combination with an additional stopping 
criterion for the iterative decoding process, we have stabilized the output BER performance to a sufficiently low 
level that after an outer clean-up decoder, no residual frame error events could be found for a CS probability of 
10−3.
