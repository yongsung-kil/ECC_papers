Abstract—Product replacement gives a simple approach for code construction. This paper investigates the use of replacement product graphs for the recursive construction of QC cycle LDPC codes. It is proved that the QC cycle LDPC codes constructed by our approach are of girth 6. Simulation results show that QC cycle LDPC codes constructed by the replacement product perform well over the AWGN channel.
Index Terms—QC cycle LDPC code, girth, replacement product, recursive.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low density parity-check (LDPC) codes first proposed in the early 1960’s [1] and re-discovered in 1996 [2], are the most promising class of linear codes due to their capacityapproaching performance and low decoding complexity. This code are linear block codes with near Shannon limit performance. LDPC codes have been adopted in many practical applications. It is shown that column-weight-two LDPC codes, simply cycle codes, have their own advantages in comparison of LDPC codes with column-weight at least three [1]. Despite the low increase in minimum distance, cycle codes have shown potential in some applications such as partial response channels [3]. Especially, quasi-cyclic (QC) cycle LDPC codes are well suited for hardware implementation using sample shift registers due to the regularity in their parity-check matrices.
LDPC code can be represented through a Tanner graph, that is a bipartite graph composed of variable and check nodes [4], which correspond to the codeword bits and the paritycheck equations, respectively. Cycles, especially short cycles, degrade the performance of LDPC decoders, because they affect the independence of the extrinsic information exchanged in the iterative decoding [1]. The girth of a Tanner graph is the length of the shortest cycle in the Tanner graph. If the girth is four, it is very easy to cause the spread of error information in the iteration, which leads to the decrease of the decoding performance.
Product replacement gives a nice avenue for code construction, in that taking the product of small graphs suitable for coding can yield larger codes [5]. In this paper, we introduce a new recursive construction approach for QC cycle LDPC codes

of girth 6. In our code constructing, the vertices of the result graph of the product replacement are interpreted as rows and the edges are interpreted as columns of parity-check matrix H. Recursive construction can preserve the minimum distance and girth properties of the core matrix and can generate either regular, or irregular LDPC codes which can get based on irregular random bipartite graphs, based on the work of [6]. By choosing component graphs with relatively small degree, we obtain parity-check matrix H of LDPC codes that is relatively sparse.
This paper is organized as follows. Section II describes the replacement product and the QC cycle LDPC codes. Section III introduces the recursive construction of QC cycle LDPC codes based on replacement product. Section IV provides examples of our proposed QC cycle LDPC codes and thier simulation results. Section V concludes the paper.

B. Quasi-cyclic LDPC codes
An LDPC code is defined through its parity-check matrix H. LDPC code can be represented through a Tanner graph consisting of n variable nodes and (n − k) check nodes [4]. Noting by hi,j the element in the i-th row and j-th column of H, there is an edge between the j-th variable node and the i-th check node iff hi,j = 1. The number of edges connected to a node is called degree of that node.
The design of the QC cycle LDPC codes, consist of the following three steps: First, construct a base matrix B. Second, design a shift matrix S by raplacing each ‘-1’ of the base matrix B with a ‘-1’ and replacing each ‘0’ in the m-th row and n-th column of the base matrix B with an integer a(m,n) in the range [0, q − 1]. Third, construct a QC parity-check matrix H by replacing each a(m,n) with a q × q circulant permutation matrix P a(m,n) which cyclically shift the identity matrix I of size q × q to the right by a(m,n) times and replacing each ‘-1’ with a q × q zero matrix. There are many techniques to select shift values of the shift S [8-11] and the construction of the parity-check matrix H from S is very simple and straightforward. Therefore, in the rest of this paper, we will only focus on the construction of the base matrix B.

L: the total number of recursive replacement product, determined by the final size of H. L can be calculated by mH /(m0 × t) (t represents the number of points in G2).
l: the current number of recursive replacement product.
To construct a larger graph, we first generate a small graph, and then use extension to recursively generate larger graphs. In detail, the recursive construction approach includes the following steps.
• Step 1: Input positive integer L ≥ 1 and initialize the l = 0.
• Step 2: Choose two graph G1, G2 and the number of degrees in G1 and the number of points in G2 are equal.
• Step 3: Do the replacement product graph G = G1 G2. • Step 4: Associated with the result graph G is a ml × nl
incidence matrix Bl = [Bi,j] where each row and each column corresponds to a point and a line, respectively. • Step 5: If l < L, l = l + 1, G → G1, go back to Step 2. Otherwise, stop the recursive process and set B = Bl.

V. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we generalized the replacement product resulting in a product for bipartite graphs. By recursive replacement product of small graphs, we get a graph of large size. We proved that the resulting graphs are of girth 6 and can be used as the Tanner graph of base matrix. By replacing the ‘1’s with a circulant permutation matrix. and replacing ‘0’ with a zero matrix, we obtain the QC cycle LDPC codes of girth 6. We evaluate the performance of QC cycle LDPC codes obtained from recursive replacement product of graphs by Monte Carlo simulation over AWGN channel. Simulation results show that QC cycle LDPC codes perform comparably to others cycle LDPC codes.
