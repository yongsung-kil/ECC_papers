Abstract—Spin-transfer torque random access memory (STTRAM) has emerged as a promising nonvolatile memory technology for its fast speed, small footprint and zero standby power. However, the unique and unusual high asymmetric error rates at different memory bit operations, which are proved to be far beyond the efficiency of common error correction codes (ECCs), greatly hinder its applications. In this work, we investigate the potentials of the powerful low-density parity-check (LDPC) code to address the aggravated reliability issue in STT-RAM. We first develop a holistic STT-RAM channel model to quantitatively measure the asymmetric effects during the write and read process for single-level-cell (SLC) and multi-level-cell (MLC) design. We then propose an asymmetric LDPC (A-LDPC) decoding to particularly enhance the asymmetric error correcting capability. An STT-RAM dedicated hardware-favorable soft information, namely asymmetric log-likelihood ratio (A-LLR), is also derived from the proposed channel model. Experimental results show that our A-LDPC can outperform at least two/four orders of magnitude over existing ECCs for combating the asymmetric bit errors in SLC/MLC STT-RAM.
Keywords-spin-transfer torque random access memory (STTRAM), reliability, channel model, asymmetric log-likelihood ratio (A-LLR), low-density parity-check (LDPC) code

II. BACKGROUND AND MOTIVATION
A. STT-RAM Basics Fig.1(a) shows the basic structure of a SLC STT-RAM cell,
including one NMOS transistor and one MTJ (‘1T1J’). An MTJ consists of two magnetic layers (i.e. reference layer and free layer) and a thin MgO insulator layer sandwiched between them. The magnetization of the reference layer is pinned while that of the free layer can be changed by a polarized current. If a current is injected from bit line (source line) to source line (bit line), the magnetization of the free layer will become parallel (anti-parallel) to that of reference layer, representing a high (low) resistance state of MTJ or data ‘1’ (‘0’).
Compared to SLC design, an MLC cell [4] is usually composed of two series-stacked MTJs to store multiple bits in a similar area, as shown in Fig.1(b). The two MTJs in different dimensions can easily represent a 2-bit data by constructing four resistance levels. Because programming these two MTJs requires currents with different amplitudes, a two-step write scheme is usually needed [4]. For example, switching the large MTJ requires a large current to flip both MTJs first, followed by a small current to restore the state in small MTJ (i.e.‘00’→‘11’→‘10’). Based on the programming difficulty of the two data bits in an MLC cell, we name the first bit that is decided by the resistance state of the small MTJ as most significant bit (MSB) while that of the large MTJ as least significant bit (LSB).

current is removed before the MTJ switching process completes (namely, incomplete write) [13]. As discussed exten-

In an MLC cell, however, the write error mechanism is more complicated: besides incomplete write in both MSB and LSB, enlarging the amplitude of the driving current to switch the MSB may accidently flip the LSB, leading to new overwrite error at LSB. Also, the incomplete write failure rate at LSB is severer than that of MSB considering a larger threshold current of LSB. Thus, the average failure rate of LSB is much larger than that of MSB, leading to further asymmetry reliability between MLC bits. Overall, the reliability concerns and associated asymmetric effect of MLC deisign are more remarkable than SLC design.
Read errors of SLC and MLC designs usually consist of read disturbance and sensing error. A read disturbance failure occurs when the enlarged read current flips the resistance states unexpectedly, while sensing error occurs if the sense amplifier cannot or falsely distinguish MTJ states affected by resistance variations. Similar as write process, the high resistance states usually suffers more variations.

C. Limitation of Existing LDPC
An LDPC code [3] is defined as the null space of an M ×N sparse random parity check matrix, where M and N denote the length of check bit and coded bit, respectively. Fig.2(a) shows typical iterative belief propagation (BP) decoding process of LDPC, where messages between check node and variable node are iteratively exchanged. Different from conventional ECCs, an LDPC decoder uses LLR instead of ‘0’/‘1’ itself as its initial decoding information. Here LLR represents the measured reliability degree of the data extracted from the channel, which is a crucial factor to determine LDPC decoder performance. On the other hand, LDPC can conduct both soft and hard decision decodings. Usually the former approach can have better decoding efficiency than that of the later one due to the increased numbers of quantization bits of the initial input message.
Fig.2(b) presents an example of comparing error correction efficiency of a BCH code and a typical LDPC code for an existing MLC STT-RAM design [14]. Here the LLR for LDPC soft-decision decoding is characterized from widely adopted additive gaussian noise model [7]. Our initial results clearly prove that the common LDPC design can be even less efficient than BCH when combating such unique errors in STT-RAM.

III. STT-RAM CHANNEL MODEL

The inefficiency of existing LLR based LDPC for STTRAM asymmetric errors motivates us to investigate the efficient LLR design. In this section, we develop a comprehensive channel model for SLC/MLC STT-RAM write and read operations to capture LLRs that could closely embrace STTRAM asymmetric storage mechanism. Our goal is to unlock the under-represented asymmetric error correcting capability of LDPC code by fully leveraging our optimized LLR.

A. BAC for Write Operation

In general, the write failures of STT-RAM can be categorized as the following two types:

1) The cell keeps ‘0’ and fails in ‘0’→‘1’ flipping during writing ‘1’ process;
2) The cell keeps ‘1’ and fails in ‘1’→‘0’ flipping during writing ‘0’ process.

Therefore, as Fig.3(a) shows, a simple binary asymmetric channel (BAC) model can be developed to characterize the aforementioned failures. Here X denotes the target data to be written into the memory cell, while Ywrite is the actual data stored after the writing operation. The failure rates for writing ‘1’ and ‘0’ are δ1 and ε1, respectively. Note the extreme asymmetric effect is represented by assuming δ1 ≫ ε1.

B. BAC+AGN Channel for Read Operations

The read operation errors can usually be summarized as the following three different types:

1) Read disturbance: An MTJ stores ‘1’ but flips to ‘0’ after injecting the reading current;
2) Sensing error for reading ‘0’: An MTJ stores ‘0’ but is read out as ‘1’;
3) Sensing error for reading ‘1’: An MTJ stores ‘1’ but is read out as ‘0’.

The hardware implementations of the A-LLRs are usually very costly due to the complicated logarithmic circuits, as shown in Eq.(7), Eq.(8) and Eq.(9). As we shall show in Section V, the A-LLRs can be well approximated by the piecewise linear function with efficient basic logic circuits.

B. Aymmetric LDPC (A-LDPC) Decoding

The error correction capability of LDPC code (n,k) is also proportional to the length of pre-coding length k. Here n is the post-coding length with extra redundancy bits. However, unlike the LDPC codes with larger k in wireless communications, the required k in memory design is relatively short (i.e. 512 or 1024 bits), aggravating the corresponding error correction capability. In this work, we designed two short LDPC codes dedicated to SLC STT-RAM and MLC STTRAM, respectively, according to prior works [6][9]: (585,512) and (683,512). We adopt similar hardware implementations for the two codes from [7]. The error correction capability of proposed A-LDPC decoders are boosted by leveraging aforementioned A-LLR and optimized quantization bits for soft decoding [2].
The general A-LDPC decoding process can be summarized as follows:

1) Deriving the A-LLR for each bit stored in STT-RAM cell;
2) Performing A-LDPC soft or hard decoding based on the initial soft information–A-LLR.

VI. CONCLUSION
As conventional ECCs cannot effectively solve asymmetric errors in STT-RAM, in this work, we systematically analyze the possibility of LDPC codes to address such errors. We first propose a comprehensive channel model to capture the asymmetric effects in writing and reading STT-RAM. We then extract the STT-RAM dedicated A-LLR from the channel to unleash the potentials of LDPC codes. Our simulation results show that our A-LDPC design can improve the reliability by 102 (104) over the existing ECCs for SLC (MLC) STT-RAM under the current manufacturing conditions. The synthesized hardware cost is relatively high in MLC design due to its severe reliability concerns. We hope our study can inspire and motivate more in-depth study on the unique STT-RAM reliability issues and the advanced ECC designs.
