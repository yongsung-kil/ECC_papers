A Progressive Edge Growth Algorithm for Bit Mapping Design of LDPC Coded BICM Schemes
Junyi Du†, Jinhong Yuan‡, Liang Zhou† and Xuan He† †National Key Lab on Communication, University of Electronic Science and Technology of China, Chengdu, P. R. China
‡School of Electrical Engineering & Telecommunications, University of New South Wales, Sydney, Australia Email: jydu@std.uestc.edu.cn, j.yuan@unsw.edu.au, lzhou@uestc.edu.cn, xhe@std.uestc.edu.cn

Abstract—In this paper, we consider the design of the bit mapping in low-density parity-check (LDPC) coded bit-interleaved coded modulation (BICM) schemes. We introduce a two-layer bipartite graph to represent the LDPC coded BICM scheme where a new bit mapping graph linking sub-channels to variable nodes (VNs) is added to the conventional Tanner graph. We propose a progressive edge growth (PEG) algorithm to design the bit mapping for the BICM scheme. The design paradigm is to provide more protections to the VNs that are allocated to the sub-channels with the lowest mutual information. We define a novel concept of unreliable depth profile to classify the reliability of the VNs. By connecting more reliable edges to the least reliable VNs, we can significantly improve the reliable edge distribution for the unreliable VNs, thus improve the extrinsic information in the iterative decoding. The proposed bit mapping algorithm is employed for the design of the LDPC coded BICM with 64-QAM and 256-QAM. Simulation results show that the proposed design can considerably improve the error performance, compared to the conventional consecutive bit mapping strategy.
I. INTRODUCTION
The bit-interleaved coded modulation (BICM) scheme with Gray labeling can approach the coded modulation (CM) capacity [1]. Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, discovered by Gallager [2] and analyzed by density evolution [3] and extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) chart [4], were verified to be able to approach the Shannon limit. The joint design of BICM schemes and LDPC codes is important for practical communication systems.
In BICM with M -ary QAM, m = log2 M bits are grouped to select a symbol according to the constellation labelling. The most trivial mapping strategy is to map m consecutive bits to a symbol which is known as the consecutive-bit (CB) mapping strategy [5]. However, in a high order BICM scheme, the minimum Euclidean distance for each modulation level is different and the disparate sub-channel capacities bring unequal error protections (UEPs) [6] for the transmitted bits. Thus, the CB mapping strategy is not optimal. Instead, a properly designed bit mapper, which purposefully maps coded bits to different coded modulation levels, can lead to significant performance improvement for LDPC coded BICM schemes.
The recent works searching for the optimal bit mapping of the irregular LDPC coded BICM schemes are based on the density evolution [7], EXIT chart [8]–[10] and protograph EXIT chart [11]. Although irregular LDPC codes have better performance in the waterfall region, regular LDPC codes are more attractive in some applications due to their lower

error floor and the simpler implementation [12], like in the application of very-large-scale integration (VLSI) [13] and field-programmable gate array (FPGA) [14].
The authors in [12] proposed an ad hoc search algorithm over the column permutations of the LDPC parity-check matrix. It aims to ensure that each check node (CN) connects at most one variable node (VN) allocated to the sub-channels with the lowest mutual information. However, for M -ary QAM (or PSK), the ad hoc algorithm is less effective when the CN degree dc is larger than m/2 (or m). Besides, the complexity of the algorithm is high which makes it impractical when the code length is long.
In this paper, we propose a novel algorithm to design the bit mapping in LDPC coded BICM scheme. A new layer, called sub-channel layer is added to the code Tanner graph. The edge between a VN and a sub-channel means that the specific VN is allocated to the specific sub-channel (equivalent to be mapped to the specific modulation level). Based on that, the bit mapping design is considered as an edge growth process. An efficient progressive edge growth (PEG) algorithm, operated on the modified Tanner graph, is proposed. The classical PEG algorithm [16] aims to construct LDPC codes with large girth by establishing edges in an edge-by-edge manner between VNs and CNs. However, in this paper, we propose the PEG algorithm to design optimal bit mappings by connecting edges between VNs and the sub-channels in the modified Tanner graph.
This paper is organized as follows. Section II introduces the LDPC coded BICM scheme. Section III presents the PEG algorithm to design the bit mapping. Section VI shows and analyzes the simulation results. Finally, some conclusions are drawn in section V.

Definition 1: A VN is regarded as an unreliable node if it is connected to the sub-channel in L, while it is reliable if it is allocated to H. The unreliable VN has a higher probability to obtain a small LLR from the connected sub-channel.
In the update process of CNs in a (dc, dv) LDPC decoder, a CN cj receives LLRs from the connected dc VNs and feeds back recalculated LLRs to the VNs. For a VN vk, the LLR from cj is mainly determined by the minimum LLR that cj receives from the other dc − 1 VNs. Thus, vk receives small extrinsic LLR from cj if one or more of the remaining dc − 1 VNs send small LLRs to cj.
Definition 2: A specific edge (vk, cj) connects vk to CN cj. We call (vk, cj) a reliable edge if all the other dc − 1 VNs connected to cj are reliable. Otherwise we call the edge unreliable.
In the update process of VNs, the VN receives the extrinsic information from the connected sub-channel in the bit mapping graph and CNs in the code Tanner graph, as shown in Fig. 2 (thick lines). For the unreliable VN, while the extrinsic information from the sub-channel is unreliable, the reliable edge can provide reliable extrinsic information to enhance

In this section, we propose a bit mapping which allocates unreliable sub-channels to VNs aiming to minimize the unmatched VN number. Here, |L| · N/m edges need to be added between VNs and sub-channels in L. Assuming T edges have been already added, for the (T + 1)-th edge, the algorithm has two steps.
• Calculate the unreliable depth profile D for each unallocated VN and group the VNs with the largest D as G;
• Take a VN from G, each VN leads to a different unmatched VN number if it is connected to L. Choose the VN with the minimum unmatched VN. If there exist multiple choices, choose one VN randomly.
This process is conducted until |L| · N/m edges are added. There are two main tasks in this process which are detailed in the following.
1) Calculation of D: The classical breadth-first search (BFS) algorithm [17] is adopted to calculate the unreliable depth profile for each unallocated VN. A queue Q, the classical first in first out (FIFO) data structure, is used. For a specific VN vk, dk is denoted as the minimum depth with the nearest allocated unreliable VN, and dk = min(Dk). The algorithm is illustrated in Algorithm 1.
The unreliable depth profile for each unallocated VN is calculated and the complexity is O(N + N · dv). Find the VNs with the largest unreliable depth profile and group them as a set G.
2) Adding the (T + 1)-th edge: With T edges added to L, the unmatched VN number is calculated and denoted as φ. If G has more than one VN, the VN leading to the minimum unmatched VN should be found and the algorithm is illustrated in Algorithm 2.

C. Allocation to sub-channels in H
In this section, we focus on the allocation of H, which consists of reliable sub-channels. For the matched unreliable VNs, they can transmit reliable extrinsic information to CNs with the help of reliable edges. However, for the unmatched VNs, their outputs are still unreliable and the other VNs within the same CN will receive unreliable information.
Definition 6: Each VN vk has a dv-tuple vector Nk = (n1k, n2k, . . . , nkdv ), called the unmatched number profile. nki represents the number of unmatched unreliable VNs in the ith connected CN. Note that, for each VN vk, we assume that nki ≤ nkj , if i < j. The comparison rule for N is the same with D.
The unmatched number profile measures the reliability of the extrinsic information from the CNs. For the unallocated VNs, with a larger unmatched number profile, the received extrinsic information from CNs in the Tanner graph in Fig. 2 is less reliable. The relatively larger capacity sub-channel in H has a more reliable channel input. It should be connected to the VN with a larger unmatched number profile.
In Fig. 2, each sub-channel needs to connect N/m VNs. We first sort the sub-channels in H according to their mutual information increasingly. For the sub-channel CHk with the k-th least mutual information in H, the PEG algorithm has three steps:
• Calculate N for the unallocated VNs; • Sort the unallocated VNs according to their N increas-
ingly; • Connect the N/m VNs with the N/m smallest N to
C Hk . The allocation algorithm has two parts, the calculation of N and the allocation of H. The complexity is O(dv · dc · N + |H| · N/m).

The 64-QAM and 256-QAM with Gray labelling are adopted and the optimized bit mappings are obtained based on the proposed algorithm.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we considered the design of the bit map-
ping for LDPC coded BICM schemes. We first built a new
allocation model based on the Tanner graph and regarded the
bit mapping design process as an edge growth process. We
further divided the sub-channels into two groups according to
their mutual information and found that the unmatched VNs
lead to the degradation of the error performance. We proposed
a PEG algorithm, as well as the unreliable depth profile
and unmatched number profile, to design the bit mapping.
Compared to the traditional consecutive bit mapping, the
designed bit mapping minimized the number of unmatched
VNs and achieved considerable performance improvement.
