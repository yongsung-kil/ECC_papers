The ADMM Penalized Decoder for LDPC Codes
Xishuo Liu and Stark C. Draper, Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract— Linear programming (LP) decoding for low-density parity-check codes was introduced by Feldman et al. and has been shown to have theoretical guarantees in several regimes. Furthermore, it has been reported in the literature—via simulation and via instanton analysis—that LP decoding displays better error rate performance at high signal-to-noise ratios (SNR) than does belief propagation (BP) decoding. However, at low SNRs, LP decoding is observed to have worse performance than BP. In this paper, we seek to improve LP decoding at low SNRs while maintaining LP decoding’s high SNR performance. Our main contribution is a new class of decoders obtained by applying the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) algorithm to a set of non-convex optimization problems. These non-convex problems are constructed by adding a penalty term to the objective of LP decoding. The goal of the penalty is to make pseudocodewords, which are non-integer vertices of the LP relaxation, more costly. We name this class of decoders—ADMM penalized decoders. For low and moderate SNRs, we simulate ADMM penalized decoding with 1 and 2 penalties. We find that these decoders can outperform both BP and LP decoding. For high SNRs, where it is difficult to obtain data via simulation, we use an instanton analysis and find that, asymptotically, ADMM penalized decoding performs better than BP but not as well as LP. Unfortunately, since ADMM penalized decoding is not a convex program, we have not been successful in developing theoretical guarantees. However, the non-convex program can be approximated using a sequence of linear programs; an approach that yields a reweighted LP decoder. We show that a two-round reweighted LP decoder has an improved theoretical recovery threshold when compared with LP decoding. In addition, we find via simulation that reweighted LP decoding significantly attains lower error rates than LP decoding at low SNRs.
Index Terms— LDPC, ADMM, LP decoding, iterative decoding, penalty method, nonlinear optimization.

brings LDPC decoding to the realm of convex optimization. LP decoding has many desirable attributes that message passing algorithms do not possess. First, LP decoding has an “ML certificate” property [1]. That is, if the solution from an LP decoder is an integral solution then it is a maximum likelihood (ML) solution. Second, LP decoding provides theoretical guarantees on decoding success [2]–[6] whereas message passing algorithms are not guaranteed to converge. Finally, LP decoding can outperform message passing algorithms at high SNRs. In particular, simulation results from [7]–[10] indicate that LP decoding achieves lower error rates than belief propagation (BP) at high SNRs. Furthermore, instanton analysis results from [11]–[13] back up these empirical results and suggest that LP decoding achieves a lower error rate than BP decoding at asymptotic high SNRs.
Despite these advantages, a major drawback of LP decoding was initially seen to be computational complexity. LP decoding is not efficient when implemented using generic LP solvers. Thus, some work has been dedicated to developing efficient LP decoding algorithms, e.g., [8], [10], [14]–[18]. Among these works, Barman et al. [10] develops a distributed and scalable approach based on the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM). In [10], the authors exploit the geometry of the LP decoding constraints and develop a decoding algorithm that has a message passing interpretation. Simulation results from [10] reveal a drawback of LP decoding: for all codes simulated in [10], LP decoding has a worse word-error-rate (WER) performance than BP decoding at low SNRs. This drawback makes LP decoding less favorable than BP in these regimes, even if computation complexity were not a concern.
In this paper, we focus on improving LP decoding WER performance at low SNRs. We introduce a class of decoders that we term ADMM penalized decoders; obtained by modifying the objective function of LP decoding. As will be explained precisely in the main text, the WER performance of these decoders can significantly outperform that of LP decoding at low and moderate SNRs. In addition, for all codes we simulated, these decoders can match or outperform BP decoding. At high SNRs, we observe that the WER performance of ADMM penalized decoding is much better than BP decoding, but is worse than LP decoding. While an apparent drawback of our algorithm, we note that ADMM penalized decoding becomes LP decoding when a parameter of the decoder that controls the magnitude of the penalization is set to 0. In other words, we can tune the ADMM penalized decoder as a function of SNR so that it can always match (and often outperform) the performance of LP decoding.
The key idea of ADMM penalized decoding is to introduce a penalty term into the objective of LP decoding. The penalty term is designed to make non-integral solutions more costly.

In particular the value of the penalty is smaller at 0 and 1 than at any point in the interval (0, 1) in between. The choice of penalty is not unique. In this paper, we primarily investigate using the 1 norm and the 2 norm-squared as penalties (we refer to the latter simply as the 2 penalty). In order to obtain a low complexity decoder similar to the one proposed in [10], we apply the ADMM template to the resulting optimization problem. We leverage the efficient algorithm that projects a vector onto the parity polytope developed in [10] (and subsequently improved in [17] and [18]). As a result, based on our software implementations, the ADMM penalized decoder can decode faster than our baseline sum-product BP decoder in terms of average decoding time. We note two interesting connections between ADMM penalized decoding and existing algorithms. First, ADMM penalized decoding fits into the class of constraint satisfaction problems described in [19]. In the sense of [19], ADMM penalized decoding can be considered as having an objective that is the sum of channel evidence and a “soft constraint” specified by the penalty term. The second interesting connection involves BP decoding. It is well known that sum-product BP is related to minimizing the Bethe free energy at temperature equals to one [20]. The Bethe free energy objective is the sum of the LP decoding objective and the Bethe entropy function. ADMM penalized decoding can be considered as replacing the Bethe entropy function with a different, adjustable, penalty term, more suitable to optimization.
Since the high SNR performance of a decoder is crucial for applications such as fiber-optic communications and data storage that require ultra-low error rates, it is important that we study the high SNR error performance of ADMM penalized decoding. Unfortunately, at high SNRs, it is hard to obtain sufficient data via Monte Carlo simulation. To address this problem, we leverage the numerical approach developed in [11], where the concept of instantons is used. Instantons are the most probable noise vectors that cause the decoder to fail. By identifying these noise vectors for a decoder/code pair, one can predict the high SNR behavior of that decoder/code pair (see also [12]). We propose an instanton search algorithm for the ADMM penalized decoder. We show that the instanton information we obtain provides good predictions for high SNR WERs. In addition, our results suggest that the ADMM penalized decoder can suffer from trapping sets, which have been widely studied for message passing decoders (e.g., [21]). This means that performance of the ADMM penalized decoder can be improved by code design techniques that remove trapping sets, e.g., [22].
Unlike LP decoding, it is hard to prove guarantees for ADMM penalized decoding because the latter is trying to minimize a non-convex objective. In order to regain provable guarantees, we focus on the 1 penalized objective and use a sequence of linear functions to approximate this objective. The result is a sequence of linear programs called reweighted LP. We show that for bit flipping channels, reweighted LP decoding has an improved theoretical recovery threshold compared to LP decoding. Further, we observe through empirical simulation that reweighted LP decoding improves LP decoding significantly in terms of

WER performance. However at low SNRs, we observe via simulation that it cannot achieve the WER performance of the “original” ADMM penalized decoding it is approximating.
In the literature, other researchers have sought to improve the error performance of LP decoding. One main focus in the literature has been tightening the polytope under consideration (e.g., [23]–[26]). It is also possible to improve LP decoding by branch-and-bound algorithms such as those proposed in [25] and [27]. We refer interested readers to [28] for a comprehensive review on methods to improve LP decoding. In an inspiring paper [29], Khajehnejad et al. propose solving a second, reweighted LP, if LP decoding fails. This reweighted LP is constructed using fractional outputs of the LP decoder. Khajehnejad et al. not only proved some useful theoretical tools for LP decoding, but also showed via empirical results that their reweighted LP decoding outperforms LP decoding in terms of WER performance. Compared to the algorithm proposed by Khajehnejad et al. in [29], our reweighted LP decoding has two advantages. First, although the theorems we present hold only for bit flipping channels, our reweighted LP algorithm can be applied to the AWGN channel. Empirically, we see that the reweighting improves performance. Second, our algorithm allows multiple rounds of reweighting in order to achieve better error performance, albeit without the guarantees of the first reweighting step.
We summarize our major contributions in this paper:
• We introduce a new class of decoding objectives constructed by penalizing the LP decoding objective. (Section III-A)
• We derive ADMM algorithms for penalized objectives that use the 1 norm and the 2 norm (squared) as the penalty. (Section III-B)
• We observe that ADMM penalized decoding improves LP decoding significantly in terms of both WER and decoding complexity. Further, we observe that for ADMM penalized decoding, the slopes of the WER curves at moderate SNRs are similar to LP decoding. In addition, ADMM penalized decoding achieves or outperforms BP decoding in terms of WER for the codes we simulated. (Section III-D)
• We develop an instanton search algorithm for the ADMM penalized decoder for the AWGN channel. (Section IV-B)
• We show that instanton information can be used to predict the slope of the WER curve at high SNRs. Further, we observe that failures of the ADMM penalized decoder are related to trapping sets. (Section IV-C)
• We develop a reweighted LP decoder that has a theoretical guarantee for bit flipping channels. (Section V-A and V-B)
• We show via simulation that our reweighted LP decoding outperforms LP decoding in terms of WER performance. (Section V-D).

B. ADMM Decoding for a Generic Objective
We now review an efficient algorithm that can be used to solve the two problems mentioned above, namely, the ADMM formulation presented in [10]. We generalize this formulation to optimization problems over the fundamental polytope P, which allows us to reformulate (2) easily. We refer the readers to [30] for a comprehensive description of ADMM. When using ADMM, a crucial subroutine is projection onto PPd j . Barman et al. characterize the geometry of PPd j in [10] and develop an efficient algorithm for projecting onto this convex object. Some recent works improve on the initial projection algorithm and in [17], Zhang and Siegel propose an algorithm based on cuts. Zhang et al. leverage the “two-slice” property developed in [10] to develop a linear time projection routine.

III. USING PENALTY FUNCTIONS FOR DECODING
LP decoding fails whenever it outputs a fractional solution which is called a pseudocodeword. Since pseudocodewords are directly related to the fractional vertices of the fundamental polytope P, it is not surprising that error performance can be improved by tightening the relaxation of P. “Cutting plane” and “branch-and-bound” are two types of techniques that have been used to improve the error performance of LP decoding (cf. [28]).
In this work, we take a different approach to improve LP decoding and propose ADMM penalized decoding. We are partially inspired by [29] because the authors of that paper improve LP decoding not by tightening the constraints, but by properly changing the objective. Our approach is also similar to the well known LASSO algorithm [31] in the sense that the penalty in our approach can be considered as a “regularizer”. However, the objective in our approach is non-convex. Therefore, we use the term “penalty” rather than “regularizer” in this paper.

A. Review of the Instanton Concept

In [38], the authors introduce an instanton analysis for error correction codes. Instantons form the set of most probable noise configurations that cause decoding failures. At high SNRs, instantons dominate decoding failures. Therefore, the minimum norm of the instantons can be used to predict the slope of the WER curve at high SNRs (see also [11], [12]). The generality of instanton analysis makes it a good candidate for analyzing the high-SNR performance of ADMM-PD. One necessary part of this analysis is an algorithm that identifies instantons. The “amoeba” algorithm is used in [38] for continuous channels. Although this approach is generic and can be applied to any decoder, it does not exploit characteristics of a specific decoder. As a result, several specific instanton search algorithms have been developed for different decoders. An instanton search algorithm for BP decoding in the AWGN channel is introduced in [11]. For LP decoding, there is a tight relationship between instantons and pseudocodewords [39]. Based on this relationship, in [39] and [40] the authors propose iterative algorithms to search for pseudocodewords for the AWGN channel.
We first review the definition of an instanton and then rephrase instanton search as a non-convex optimization problem.
Definition 8 (cf., e.g., [12]): Instantons are the most probable configurations of the channel noise that result in decoding failures.

B. Instanton Search Algorithm for ADMM-PD
We first introduce a specific instanton search algorithm for the ADMM penalized decoder that we denote as ISA-PD (instanton search algorithm, penalized decoder). We then describe a generic algorithm, denoted by ISA-R (instanton search algorithm, refining), that is used to refine the outputs of ISA-PD. We note that ISA-PD is already able to produce good instanton search results. However, ISA-R can still refine the results obtained by ISA-PD to find lower-norm instantons. This is demonstrated in Section IV-C.
1) Specific Algorithm for ADMM-PD: In [39], the authors propose an iterative algorithm to find the minimum weight pseudocodeword in the AWGN channel. In each iteration, the algorithm uses an LP decoder to decode a noise configuration that, in the previous iteration, was found to cause an LP decoding failure. The output from the LP decoder is a pseudocodeword, used to calculate the minimum norm noise vector that can produce this failure. The algorithm terminates when it converges or exceeds a maximum number of allowable iterations. This algorithm is executed repeatedly, with random initialization to develop statistics on instantons.
For ADMM-PD, there is no analytical connection between decoder outputs and instantons because ADMM-PD is trying to solve a non-convex problem. Whenever ADMM-PD fails, the output is not necessarily a vertex of the fundamental polytope. D. Weaknesses of ADMM-PD
It is noticeable from Fig. 13 that the minimum instanton norm (not the 1%) stays almost constant for a range of α. This suggests that there might be some common structure that underlies these instantons. This motivates us to study the patterns of these noise vectors.
We observe that for all minimum norm instantons there are only a few non-zero entries. Moreover, every instanton we found has support on trapping set structures, which are widely studied for BP decoders. For the [155, 64] code, we observe instantons with non-zero entries on (5, 3) (plotted in Fig. 14) and (6, 4) trapping sets. For the [1057, 814] code, we observe instantons with non-zero entries on (5, 1) and (7, 1) trapping sets. The (7, 1) trapping set contributes to most of the norm-7 instantons in Fig. 13. It is worth mentioning that pseudocodewords that result from LP decoding failures are also observed to have connections with trapping sets [12]. Building a connection between the fundamental polytope and trapping sets is an important open question.
We comment on two ways in which the above observations can prove useful. First, the results imply that we can leverage trapping set knowledge in the literature to compute the multiplicity of a particular instanton. Second, one can expect that code designs that alleviate trapping set effects should also benefit ADMM-PD.
E. Remarks
We now briefly discuss the intuition gained from our results. We note that the penalized decoder aims to penalize fractional solutions by adding penalties to the objective on a symbol-by-symbol basis. As a result, the decoder more easily gets stuck in some local configurations such as trapping sets. Meanwhile, increasing the penalty coefficient can decrease the significance of channel evidence during the decoding process.

VI. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we introduce the family of ADMM penalized decoders. The key idea is to add a penalty term to the objective function of LP decoding, and then use ADMM to try to solve this problem. We first focus on using the negative 1 and 2 norms as penalty functions and derive ADMM algorithms. Next, we study the performance of the decoder via simulation and an instanton analysis. We find that at low SNRs ADMM penalized decoding can outperform both BP and LP decoding in terms of WER. At high SNRs, ADMM penalized decoding has a worse error floor than LP decoding. This means that the best choice can be to set the penalty to zero at sufficient high SNRs. Finally, we study a linear approximation of the penalized objective and prove error correction guarantees.
We summarize three future directions relevant to this decoder. First, recall that we observe that decoding failures are related to trapping sets for ADMM penalized decoding. This means that we may be able to use the known structure of trapping sets to improve WERs predictions at high SNRs. Some initial efforts for unpenalized (original LP) decoder can be found in [45]. Second, some recent work [48] characterizes the convergence of ADMM for certain

From 2013 to 2014, he was a visiting research student at the University of Toronto, Canada. In 2015, he received the Ph.D. degree from the University of Wisconsin-Madison under the supervision of Professor Stark C. Draper. He is currently with Facebook, Inc.

Stark C. Draper (S’99–M’03–SM’15) is an Associate Professor of Electrical and Computer Engineering at the University of Toronto and was an Associate Professor at the University of Wisconsin, Madison. As a research scientist he has worked at the Mitsubishi Electric Research Labs (MERL), Disney’s Boston Research Lab, Arraycomm Inc., the C. S. Draper Laboratory, and Ktaadn Inc. He completed postdocs at the University of Toronto and at the University of California, Berkeley. He received the M.S. and Ph.D. degrees from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), and the B.S. and B.A. degrees in Electrical Engineering and in History from Stanford University. His research interests include communications and information theory, error-correction coding, statistical signal processing and optimization, security, as well as the application of these disciplines to computer architecture.
Dr. Draper has received the NSERC Discovery Award, the NSF CAREER Award, the 2010 MERL President’s Award, and teaching awards from the University of Toronto, the University of Wisconsin, and MIT. He received an Intel Graduate Fellowship, Stanford’s Frederick E. Terman Engineering Scholastic Award, and a U.S. State Department Fulbright Fellowship. He serves on the IEEE Information Theory Society Board of Governors.
