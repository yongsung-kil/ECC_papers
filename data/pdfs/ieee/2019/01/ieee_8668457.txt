Joint Decoder Design for SSDs
Qing Li

Abstract—Error-correction codes (ECC) and redundant array of independent disks (RAID) are commonly used to protect NAND-based solid state drives (SSD) from serious noise/disturb. In order to tolerate more errors, common solutions are more complex ECCs and RAIDs. Meanwhile, due to the errorcorrection mechanisms and flash translation layer, NAND-based SSDs inevitably generate abundant redundant information. The theme of this paper is to show that instead of designing new error correction mechanisms from scratch, the NANDbased SSD reliability can be greatly improved by utilizing current decoders to explore the inherent information between redundant pages. We first propose a joint decoder to recover multiple pages by utilizing redundant pages within a stripe of ECC/RAID, and then we consider a joint decoder to recover multiple pages by utilizing write-amplified (WA) pages. The simulation results show that with the proposed algorithm, a performance gain of as much as 1.8 dB can be obtained. The theoretical study reveals that the joint decoding model can be studied from the perspective of relay channel model, and this helps to explore how much information can be reliably stored with the RAID/WA system in SSD.
Index Terms—SSD, ECC, RAID, write-amplification, LDPC, density evolution, relay-channel coding.
I. INTRODUCTION
N AND-BASED Solid State Drives (SSD) are used in diverse consumer electronics applications due to their high speed, physical robustness, and non-volatility. However, NAND has quite a few unique noises or disturb mechanisms [17], which pose a serious threat to its reliability. In this work, we propose a novel decoding model, i.e., joint decoder, to improve the SSD reliability by incorporating the redundant information in the decoding process.
We present the necessary background of NAND-based SSD (see Fig. 1) and more details are referred to [3]. A basic unit of NAND-based SSD is a page whose size ranges between 2KB and 64KB. A group of pages (e.g., ∼ 100) form a block. A plane contains multiple blocks (e.g., ∼ 1000). A channel connects multiple planes (e.g., ∼ 32). Several plans (e.g., ∼ 4) is packaged as a chip. A stripe consists of pages on different channels. NANDbased SSDs are commonly accessed through Flash Translation Layers (FTLs) [9]. The core functions of FTL are mapping from Logical Address (LA) to Physical Address (PA), wear leveling and garbage collection, etc.

Due to error-correction mechanisms and FTLs, NAND-based SSDs inevitably generate redundant information. One reason is the error-correction mechanism, where pages are protected by ECCs (error-correction codes) and stripes are protected by RAIDs (redundant array of independent disks) [2]. ECCs and RAIDs form a two-level error-correction hierarchy, where ECCs are first applied to corrupted pages and then RAIDs when ECCs fail (for which we say a hard error occurs). However, when both ECCs and RAIDs fail, the current error-correction mechanism has no way to recover corrupted pages despite redundant pages within one stripe.
In this work, we use xn ∈ {0, 1}n to denote a codeword of length n ∈ N+, and [n, k, d] to denote a linear block code with block length n, code dimension k ∈ N+, and minimum distance d ∈ N+. By abusing the notation, we may skip minimum distance d, and use [n, k] to denote a linear block code with block length n and code dimension k. A linear [m, k, d] (m ∈ N+) code is a RAID if there are k information pages, r = m − k redundancy pages, and it can tolerate r hard errors. For example, consider an array of m SSD pages within a stripe, m − 1 pages carry information, while the m-th is the XOR-summing of the m − 1 information pages. The pattern is repeated for each stripe of pages in planes. Such an architecture is called RAID 4 or RAID 5, and only one hard error is recoverable. RAID 6 is an [m, m − 2, d] linear code and can tolerate two hard errors.
We use Fig. 2 to illustrate this. In Fig. 2, let M0, M1, M2, M3 ∈ N+ be data at a logical addresses LA0, LA1, LA2 and LA3, respectively, xn0 , x1n, xn2 and xn3 be their codewords initially stored at PA0, PA1, PA2 and PA3, respectively. xn0 , xn1 , x2n, x3n are codewords within a stripe, and let xn4 be the codeword stored in a parity page of [5,4,2] RAID (i.e., xn4 = x0n + xn1 + x2n + xn3 ) stored at PA4. y0n, y1n, . . . , y4n are noisy codewords of x0n, xn1 , . . . , xn4 . If there is only one hard error, it can be recovered from other pages of the same stripe. However, if there are two page failures, e.g., y0n and y2n, neither can be recovered with current error-correction mechanisms.

Out-of-place updating leads to abundant copies of identical/similar pages whenever wear leveling, garbage collection, etc. is issued. The ratio between the number of codewords in physical memories and the number of logical codewords issued by hosts is defined as WA (write amplification). WA is an undesirable phenomenon because more amount of information is physically written to the storage media, and it causes serious security issues (see [15], [16], [18]). [7], [30] estimate that WA can be as high as 3 ∼ 13.
We use Fig. 3 to illustrate this. Let M ∈ N+ be data at a logical address LA0 and xn be its codeword initially stored at PA0. Due to out-of-place updates and garbage collections etc, copies of yn may be stored at PA1, PA2, . . . , PAt gradually, only one of which is mapped to LA0 (indicated by the valid arrow in Fig. 3). y0n, y1n, . . . , ytn are noisy codewords of xn at PA0, . . . , PAt, respectively. Whenever a hard error happens in ytn, y0n, y1n, . . . , ytn−1 are also highly likely to contain hard errors as noise/disturbs in NAND-based SSDs are accumulated as time goes by [17]. Again, there is no way to recover corrupted pages whenever a hard error happens despite write-amplified pages with current error-correction mechanisms.
In order to tolerate more errors, common solutions are more complex ECCs (e.g., [20], [32]) and RAIDs (e.g., [1], [10], [21], [23]). The theme of this work is to show that instead of designing new error correction mechanisms from scratch, the NANDbased SSD reliability can be greatly improved by utilizing current decoders to explore the inherent information between redundant pages. In more details, we first propose a joint decoder to recover multiple pages by utilizing redundant pages within a stripe of ECC/RAID, and we consider the joint decoder to recover multiple pages by utilizing write-amplified pages. The simulation results show that with the proposed algorithm,

a performance gain of as much as 1.8 dB can be obtained. The theoretical study reveals that the joint decoding model can be studied from the perspective of relay channel model, and this helps to explore how much information can be reliably stored with the RAID/WA system in SSD. Due to the NAND-based SSD idiosyncrasy, the relay strategy studied here is a new instance of the conventional decode-and-forward strategy [6], where the forwarding message is generated by RAID/WA. Therefore, the proposed joint decoder can be applied to other relay channel applications, where a similar relay strategy is used (e.g., [13], [27]). To the best knowledge of the author, this is the first time that NAND-based SSD is studied from the perspective of relay channel coding setting.
This paper is organized as follows. In Section II, we present necessary preliminaries used throughout the paper. In Section III, we present a joint decoder for ECC/RAID 4 and show that even with a single parity page it is possible to recover two hard errors; we generalize the joint decoder scheme to an ECC/RAID 6 system to accomplish the same task with two parity pages. In Section IV, we study one joint decoder for WA codes. Section VI concludes the paper and suggests problems for future research.
II. LDPC PRELIMINARIES
In this paper, we focus on Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) code, which is widely used in SSDs [32]. For simplicity, we assume the underlying channel is the binary erasure channel for illustrating purpose, and the memoryless binary-input continuous-output Additive White Gaussian Noise (AWGN) channels during analysis and simulation parts, although more accurate channel models exit (e.g., [24], [28]).
An [n, k] LDPC code is defined by the null space of (n − k) × n sparse parity check matrix. Its sparse parity check matrix can be represented by a bipartite graph between (n − k) check nodes in one set and n variable nodes in the other set as shown in Fig. 4. In this work, we only consider regular (dv, dc)LDPC codes, where dv denotes the number of neighbors of a variable node and dc denotes the number of neighbors of a check node. The regular LDPC is easy to analyze [26] and is a good choice of many applications considering the trade-off between its hardware complexity and performance [31].
An LDPC is decoded by iterative message-passing algorithms (see Fig. 4): after each variable is initialized with the input channel message, the decoding messages are iteratively computed

For many channels, LDPC code with message passing exhibits a threshold phenomenon: as the block length tends to be infinity, an arbitrarily small bit-error probability is achieved if the noise level is smaller than a certain threshold. The d LDPC codewords and the two sets of parities form a bilayer LDPC codeword [25] (e.g., Fig. 5): the lower layer corresponds to LDPC codewords within d pages (y0n, y1n, . . . , ydn−1) and their LDPC parity constraints; the upper layer corresponds to the d pages (y0n, y1n, . . . , ydn−1) and the RAID 4 constraint xdn + · · · + xm n −1. In this bilayer LDPC code, the variable node degree is dv + 1, that is the dv edges emanating from the lower check node (i.e., n − k zero LDPC parities constraint), one edge emanating from an upper check node (i.e., the RAID 4 constraint), the lower check node degree is du, and the upper check node degree is d.
The decoding is to do message-passing alternatively with lower/upper bilayer LDPC codes. The bilayer LDPC code is statistically different from the a conventional LDPC code: in the conventional message passing algorithm (see equation (1)), the LLRs from all check nodes at every iteration are the same because the probability that an edge emanating from a check node is connected to a variable node is the same; however, in the bilayer LDPC code, the probability that an edge from an upper check node is connected to a variable node is different from the probability that an edge from a lower check node is connected to a variable node. Therefore, it is necessary to track the LLRs from upper check nodes and lower check nodes for bilayer LDPC code.

IV. JOINT DECODING OF WRITE-AMPLIFIED CODES
In this section, we consider the joint decoder to recover possibly existed write-amplified codes. For simplicity, we assume that the LDPC codes are systematic and the write amplification factor is two. However, the ideas presented here can be generalized to other non-systematic and general write amplification case readily. We further assume that two write-amplified codewords are encoded through different ECCs. This assumption is based on the following two facts: 1) joint decoding of identical codewords is one special case of ECC/RAID 4 joint decoding; 2) the possibility of adopting more than one ECC hardwares to improve both performances and reliability is currently actively investigated, e.g., [22], [29].

B. Joint Decoder of Different WA Codes
In this part, we present a joint decoder design for different WA codes, that is, the two WA codes are encoded through a generator matrix G1 and G2, respectively, and G1 = G2.
y0n and y1n satisfy two sets of parities, n − k zero LDPC code parities individually and k joint extra parities enforced by identical information bits across y0n and y1n. Thus, the two sets of constraints form the following bilayer LDPC code: the lower layer corresponds to the (y0n, y1n) and their LDPC parity constraints; the upper layer corresponds to the k identical information bits constraint (see Fig. 8 for an example).
For bilayer LDPC of different WA codes, the upper layer has only k nodes of degree 2 and they connect to information bits of lower variable nodes; while for bilayer LDPC of LDPC/RAID 4, the upper layer has n nodes of degree 2 and they connect to all lower variable nodes. This difference between bilayer LDPC of LDPC/RAID 4 and bilayer LDPC of different WA codes can be further seen in their theoretical analysis in Appendix A and Appendix B, respectively.

V. SIMULATION RESULTS

Fig. 10. Performance comparison of conventional sum-product decoding and the proposed joint decoding algorithms, where the curves labeled with “con”, “r=2”, “r=3”, “diff”, “coupling”, and “rdp” correspond to conventional sumproduct decoding, the proposed joint decoder of LDPC/RAID 4 with two hard errors, LDPC/RAID 4 with three hard errors, joint decoder of different WA codes, joint decoder of coupling WA codes, and LDPC/RDP with three hard errors, respectively.
Existing RAID 6 [11] codes require two parity page columns to recover from two catastrophic failures (i.e., the whole columns of pages are hard errors in the RAID system). In addition to the catastrophic failures, the recently proposed Partial MDS (e.g., [1]) devotes even more extra global parity pages to recover unexpected hard errors. However, LDPC/RAID joint decoder achieves the same error-correction ability as RAID 6 or Partial MDS code with high probability without extra columns of pages or global pages. The resulting bilayer LDPC codeword consists of existing page level LDPC codewords, therefore, no extra parity bits are traded for the page level LDPC code to achieve better reliability. However, the decoding complexity, latency, and the hardware resources (e.g., RAM memory, decoding engines, etc) of the bilayer LDPC code are increased for better reliability. We leave the hardware optimization work as our future work.

To further verify advantages of our proposed joint decoding schemes, we conduct experiments to compare the bit error rates (BER) of the conventional LDPC decoding algorithm (e.g., [32]) with our proposed joint decoding schemes. The codes used are (3, 6) LDPC codes, of length 800, and are constructed based on [12]. In all the simulations presented, the maximal decoding iteration is 80.
Fig. 10 shows the performance comparison over AWGN channels. The results show the performances of the joint decoder are much better than the conventional independent LDPC decoder. Furthermore, it can be seen that gains of 0.6 dB, 0.8 dB, 1.4 dB, 1.6 dB, and 1.8 dB over conventional independent message passing decoding are obtained at BER of 10−3 for joint decoders of different WA codes, joint decoders of coupling WA codes, joint decoders of LDPC/RAID 4 with two hard errors, LDPC/RAID 4 with three hard errors, and LDPC/RDP with three hard errors, respectively.

VI. CONCLUSION
The main goal of this work is to show how the inherent information within redundant pages can be used in order to improve the reliability of SSDs. We believe that the examples and decoders we gave in this paper are only first ideas for better decoders which use similar ideas and properties from NAND flashbased SSDs to improve the reliability. In particular, these ideas can be used in other error-correction codes as well as other families of erasure codes in order to further improve the reliability.

A. System Model
We model the NAND-based SSD with k RAID parities as
a relay channel [6], that is, k = 1 for RAID 4/5 and k = 2 for RAID 6. Specifically, let X = (X0, X1, . . . , Xm−1) ∈ X m denote the symbol in m NAND page cells at the source, let Y = (Y0, Y1, . . . , Ym−1) ∈ Ym denote the noisy symbol received at the destination, and let W = (W0, W1, . . . , Wm−1) ∈ Wm denote the noisy symbol of X received at the relay to calculate a RAID parity Z = (Z0, Z1, . . . , Zk−1) ∈ Zk, where X , Y, W, Z represent the source alphabet, the noise alpha-
bet at the destination, the noise alphabet at the relay, and the
parity alphabet, respectively. In our setting, the noisy symbol
of Z is accessible at the destination, which can always re-
cover Z. Therefore, for simplicity, we assume the channel between the relay and the destination is noiseless. Let Y × Z = (Y0, Y1, . . . , Ym−1) × (Z0, Z1, . . . , Zk−1) ∈ Ym × Zk denote the symbols received at the destination. The aggregate channel among X, Y, W, Z is denoted as RAID-relay channel, (X m × Zk, p(y × z, w|x, z), (Ym × Zk) × Wm), that is (X m × Zk, p(y, w|x, z), (Ym × Zk) × Wm).
We describe our RAID-relay system as follows. Users j = 0, 1, . . . , m − 1 independently encode messages Mj into xjn. xn = (xn0 , xn1 , . . . , xm n −1) is sent (written) over the channel (i.e., a set of cells). A RAID system receives wn = (w0n, w1n, . . . , wmn −1), reliably decodes xn = (xn0 , x1n, . . . , xm n −1), and sends (writes) relay codeword zn = (z0n, z1n, . . . , zkn−1) to the channel (i.e., a set of another cells). The decoder receives both yn = (y0n, y1n, . . . , ymn −1) and zn = (z0n, z1n, . . . , zkn−1) to assign an estimate Mˆj for j = 0, 1, . . . , m − 1.

while a destination can not without the RAID parity implies a
channel degradation, which we use the following known definition to capture. The relay channel (X m × Zk, p(y, w|x, z), (Ym × Zk) × Wm) is said to be degraded if p(y, w|x, z) can
