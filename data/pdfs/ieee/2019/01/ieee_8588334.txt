ABSTRACT Girth is an important structural property of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. Motivated by the works on the girth of Tanner (3, 5), (3, 7), (3, 11), and (5, 7) quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes, we, in this paper, study the girth of Tanner (3, 13) QC-LDPC codes of length 13p for p being a prime of the form (39m + 1). First, the cycle structure of Tanner (3, 13) QC-LDPC codes is analyzed, and the cycles of length lesser than 12 are divided into five equivalent classes. Based on each equivalent class, the existence of the cycles is equivalent to the solution of polynomial equations in a 39th unit root in the prime filed Fp. By solving these polynomial equations over Fp and summarizing the resulting candidate prime values, the girth of Tanner (3, 13) QC-LDPC codes is obtained. As an advantage, Tanner (3, 13) QC-LDPC codes have much higher code rates than Tanner (3, 5), (3, 7), (3, 11), and (5, 7) QC-LDPC codes, and provide a promising coding scheme for the data storage systems and optical communications.

I. INTRODUCTION Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are a class of capacity-approaching modern codes [1]. Because of low complexity of encoding/decoding [2]–[5] and excellent performance [6]–[8], the applications of LDPC codes have been used more widely. To facilitate the implementation in hardware, LDPC codes always require a special structure, called quasi-cyclic (QC) structure [9], [10], and this class of LDPC codes is the well-known QC-LDPC codes. Furthermore, QC-LDPC codes have fast convergence under iterative decoding [11]. Hence, it is interesting to design/construct good QC-LDPC codes.
The low-complexity decoding algorithms for LDPC codes in general are under the frame of iterative decoding. The effectiveness of the iterative decoder for an LDPC code depends on several structural properties of its Tanner graph [12], such as girth, minimum distance, trapping set, cycle distribution, degree distribution and so on. Short cycles, especially cycles of length 4, degrade the performance of an LDPC code when decoded with the iterative decoding algorithms [1], and the length of the shortest cycle in the

Tanner graph of an LDPC code is called girth, denoted by g. Therefore, girth is an important structural property of LDPC codes [13]–[15]. To design and construct QC-LDPC codes with large girth, many methods and techniques are proposed, e.g., the methods constructed from algebraic or combinatorial tools [16]–[19], the algorithms constructed based on the computer [20], [21], and the masking techniques [22]–[25]. To avoid the cycles of length 4 in the Tanner graph of an LDPC code, a well-known row-column (RC) constraint is usually imposed on its parity-check matrix: no two rows (or two columns) have more than one position in common that contains a nonzero element [1]. In other word, LDPC codes whose parity-check matrices satisfy the RCconstraint have girth at least 6. According to suitably chosen algebraic or combinatorial tools, LDPC codes with girth at least 6 can be easily constructed, but the ones with girth larger than 6 are few. That is, there exist few methods for constructing QC-LDPC codes with a large girth directly, but such a well-known method was proposed by Tanner et al. [26]. It was shown in [9] that fully-connected QC-LDPC codes have girth at most 12. Can QC-LDPC codes proposed by

Later, the girth problems of Tanner (3, 7), (3, 11), and (5, 7) QC-LDPC codes are successively solved [28]–[30]. Research results show that these three classes of Tanner (γ , ρ) QCLDPC codes have the same girth distribution as Tanner (3, 5) QC-LDPC codes. In this paper, we study on the girth of Tanner (3, 13) QC-LDPC codes of length 13p, where p is a prime of the form (39m + 1). We first divide the cycles of length less than 12 into five equivalent classes by analyzing the cycle structure of Tanner (3, 13) QC-LDPC codes. We prove that Tanner (3, 13) QC-LDPC codes have no cycles of length 4, and then the existence of cycles of lengths 6, 8, and 10 can be transmitted into the solution of polynomial equations in a 39th unit root in Fp. We solve these polynomial equations over Fp, and obtain the candidate values of the primes p. By summarizing all the results, the girths of Tanner (3, 13) QC-LDPC codes are derived. Since the code rates of Tanner (3, 13) QC-LDPC codes are much higher than that of Tanner (3, 7), (3, 11), and (5, 7) QC-LDPC codes, and they can be used for applications requiring high code rates, e.g., the data storage systems and optical communications.

Definition 1: Type (j0, j1, . . . , ji−1) is equivalent to type (j0, j1, . . . , ji−1) in Tanner (3, 13) QC-LDPC codes if one of the following conditions holds:
1) There exists some k ∈ {0, 1, 2} such that js = js + k (mod 3), for all s ∈ {0, 1, 2, . . . , i − 1}.
2) There exists some k ∈ {1, 2} such that js = kjs (mod 3), for all s ∈ {0, 1, 2, . . . , i − 1}.
3) There exists some k ∈ {0, 1, . . . , i − 1} such that js = js+k for all s ∈ {0, 1, 2, . . . , i − 1}.
4) There exists some k ∈ {0, 1, . . . , i − 1} such that js = ji−1−s+k for all s ∈ {0, 1, 2, . . . , i − 1}.
Notice that the addition/subtraction operation in the subscript of j is under modulo (i − 1).
According to the equivalent relation in Definition 1, we divide the types of all cycles of lengths 4, 6, 8, and 10 into five equivalent classes as follows.
1) 4-cycle: The types of all 4-cycles have the unique class (0, 1).
2) 6-cycle: The types of all 6-cycles have the unique class (0, 1, 2).
3) 8-cycle: The types of all 8-cycles can be divided into two equivalent classes, i.e., (0, 1, 0, 1) and (0, 1, 0, 2).
4) 10-cycle: The types of all 10-cycles have the unique class (0, 1, 2, 0, 1).
