Implementation of encoder and decoder for LDPC codes based on FPGA

Abstract: This paper proposes a parallel cyclic shift structure of address decoder to realize a high-throughput encoding and decoding method for irregular-quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (IR-QC-LDPC) codes, with a dual-diagonal parity structure. A normalized min-sum algorithm (NMSA) is employed for decoding. The whole verification of the encoding and decoding algorithm is simulated with Matlab, and the code rates of 5/6 and 2/3 are selected respectively for the initial bit error ratio as 6% and 1.04%. Based on the results of simulation, multi-code rates are compatible with different basis matrices. Then the simulated algorithms of encoder and decoder are migrated and implemented on the field programmable gate array (FPGA). The 183.36 Mbps throughput of encoder and the average 27.85 Mbps decoding throughput with the initial bit error ratio 6% are realized based on FPGA.
Keywords: low-density parity-check (LDPC), field programmable gate array (FPGA), normalized min-sum algorithm (NMSA).

1. Introduction
Information reconciliation is necessary for two parties who have two sets of correlated data with a few discrepancies between them. Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, first introduced by Gallager [1] and rediscovered by MacKay and Neal [2], are very popular coding technologies for error correcting in the communication and storage system. Its remarkable performance is very close to Shannon capacity limit [3] under the assumption of having a long codeword length. A lot of research focuses on structure, coding, decoding and application. The application of LDPC codes in quantum key distribution (QKD) has made

significant progress [4 – 7].
If all column weights are the same and all row weights are the same, they are regular LDPC codes. Otherwise, they are irregular LDPC codes [8]. Accordingly, irregularquasi-LDPC (QC-LDPC) and irregular-QC-LDPC (IRQC-LDPC) are two typical kinds of LDPC codes respectively for regular and irregular application. Numerous constructions of effective LDPC codes have been researched [9 – 12]. In this paper, a random cyclic block-type LDPC code is introduced with the IR-QC-LDPC matrix to have almost lower triangular form [13], thus leading to a smaller storage memory for the matrix and a simple recursive iteration method for the encoding algorithm [14].
LDPC codes, ever since their rediscovery, decoded based on belief propagation in the beginning [15 – 17], then based on the sum-product algorithm (SPA) [18 – 26], and the min-sum algorithm (MSA) [27 – 30], achieve amazingly good error performance. Because of complex mathematical operations, the implementation of LDPC codes in hardware is very difficult. The normalized MSA (NMSA), a variant of MSA, achieves a good balance between performance and hardware implementation [31 – 34]. Compared with the SPA, the NMSA can greatly reduce decoding complexity while introducing substantial performance degradation in forms of bit error rate (BER).
Researchers are keeping chasing higher throughput and efficiency. Up until now, most check matrices in the field programmable gate array (FPGA)-based schemes are stored in block random access memory (BRAM) in a conventional style, which reduces the reading and writing efficiency during the message passing and achieves a lower throughput. In this paper, a parallel cyclic shift structure of the address decoder for BRAMs storing the check matrix is presented to optimize the performance of the discrete variable QKD system. Both 183.36 Mbps throughput of the

4.1 Overall architecture

(17) The implemented overall architecture of our proposed IRQC-LDPC decoder based on the FPGA is illustrated in Fig. 2.

Fig. 2 Overall architecture of IR-QC-LDPC decoder

In this architecture, there is a global controller, an input message unit, an iteration decision unit, an address convert unit, a check-node processor (CNP), a variable-node processor (VNP) and a decode decision unit.
The global controller deals with the whole procedure of encoding and decoding. The input message unit receives message information and check information. The decode decision unit will judge wehther the result during iteration is right and outputs the decoding status. The left sub-

architecture can be classified into the following main categories.
(i) Computational logics (registers for pipeline) consisting of CNP and VNP, and format converters (CNP and VNP’s complement to sign-magnitude (C2S) and its inverse (S2C)). The function of these logics is to update the edge messages between the variable nodes and check nodes.
(ii) The RAM controller which deals with the updated

4.2 Memory arrangement

As in the illustration of Fig. 2, there are three RAM-arrays for message storage:
(i) Variable-node initialization RAM-array (init-array) for the initialization message of the original codeword;
(ii) C2V message RAM-array (C2V-array) for updating the variable-node with the check-node message;
(iii) V2C message RAM-array (V2C-array) for updating the check-node with the variable-node message.
In this paper, we realize the quantized NMSA with quantization of 8-bit binary data, so the data width of each RAM is 8. As the expansion factor is z, the depth of each RAM is z according to the architecture of the check matrix H.

4.5 Variable-node processor
The VNP contains two parts: one is variable node initialization processing, and the other is variable node message update processing.
(i) Variable-node initialization processing: after it receives the initial codeword (x) in Bob, the VNP will initiate each bits in x. x is divided into n groups with z bits in each group according to the matrix structure of H. In other words, x can be denoted as

x = {x0, x1, . . . , xn−1}.

(20)

In (20), every xi has z bits. Here we adopt n RAMs to store the initial probability message, and the depth of each RAM is z and the width of each RAM is 8 bits. Then we initiate every bit of xi and store the result into the ith RAM sequentially. We can collaterally store n bits at the same time, thus we need z clocks to initialize the x and store it in n RAMs as Fig. 3.
(ii) Variable node processor: in this module, the VNP should read message from the C2V-array and the init-array,

then the VNP will save the updated message to the V2Carray. There are m × n RAMs in the C2V-array, we can read messages from m × n RAMs at the same time.
Aware of the preponderance of parallel processing of the FPGA, we can process the kth variable node in each ci (i = 0, 1, 2, . . . , n − 1) at the same time. As the initialized message and the C2V message are separately stored in the same size RAM, we can read them simultaneously as shown in Fig. 7.
V2Ci,j,k connects the corresponding Cj,k and C2Vi,j ,k (j ∈ {0, 1, . . . , j − 1, j + 1, . . . , n − 1}), and the relationship between k and k is shown in (18).
The procedure of the VNP is as follows. (i) Read the k th message from the C2V-array and the kth initial message from the init-array; (ii) Sum each C2V value from the same block row excluding the jth column and the kth initial value from the init-array to get the V2C message as (15); (iii) Sum each C2V value from the same block row and the kth initial value from the init-array to get the posterior probability as (16).

7. Conclusions

Fig. 10 LDPC check matrix H with code rate of 2/3

The proposed encoder and decoder have been implemented on an Xilinx Kintex UltraScale XCKU040FFVA1156-2-E FPGA which provides 242 400 look-up tables (LUTs), 484 800 flip-flops (FF) and 600 block RAMs (BRAMs).
The key source generates Alice sifted key and sends it to the LDPC encoder, and generates Bob sifted key and sends it to the LDPC decoder. Then the encoder will send the check message to the decoder after encoding.
The system clock is 25 MHz. Each message frame has an aptotic length of z × (n − m) = 1 296 bits. The frame error rate after decoding is less than 1% if the initial BER is no more than 6%. Under this condition, the error correction efficiency is 1.49 according to (21). The throughput for encoding is 183.36 Mbps while the average throughput of decoding is 27.85 Mbps.

Biographies

He is currently working toward his Ph.D. degree in quantum key distribution at the University of Science and Technology of China, Hefei, China. E-mail: chengkun@live.cn
SHEN Qi was born in 1986. He received his Ph.D. degree in Department of Modern Physics from University of Science and Technology of China, Hefei, China, in 2013. He has been with National Laboratory for Physical Sciences at Microscale and Department of Modern Physics, University of Science and Technology of China, and Chinese Academy of Sciences Center for Excellence and Synergetic Innovation Center in Quantum Information and Quantum Physics, University of Science and Technology of China, as an assistant researcher. His research interests include quantum key distribution, and time-to-digital converter. E-mail: shenqi@ustc.edu.cn
LIAO Shengkai was born in 1983. He received his Ph.D. degree in Shanghai Institute of Technology, Chinese Academy of Sciences, Shanghai, China, in 2010. His research interests include light source, transmission channel, and detection of quantum key distribution. E-mail: skliao@ustc.edu.cn
