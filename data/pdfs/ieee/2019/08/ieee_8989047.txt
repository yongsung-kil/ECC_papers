Small stopping sets in projective low-density parity-check codes

Abstract—It is known that redundant parity-check equations can improve the performance of an LDPC code by reducing the number of harmful substructures in the parity-check matrix. However, it is a difficult problem to design a parity-check matrix in such a way that it avoids substructures that are known to be harmful to iterative decoding while keeping the number of redundant parity-check equations moderate and ensuring other desirable properties. We explicitly give redundant parity-check matrices for c√yclic regular LDPC codes of length n and minimum distance d ∼ n in which there are only n parity-check equations but no stopping sets of size d + 1 or smaller except for those that correspond to the nonzero codewords of the smallest weight. We do this by showing that the well-known projective LDPC codes from the incidence matrices of projective planes PG(2, q) with q even have this property. This result may give insight into how the small number of redundant parity-check equations in the geometric LDPC codes may be contributing to the good performance reported in the literature. We also give a slightly improved upper bound on the size of a smallest generic erasure correcting set.
I. INTRODUCTION
The study of the structures of error-correcting codes is fundamental and has been among the most important areas in coding theory. Indeed, the basic structural information about a given code, such as its minimum distance and weight enumerator, is not only important to engineers but also quite interesting from a combinatorial viewpoint.
While code structures have always been of interest in engineering and discrete mathematics, advances in coding theory have had a nontrivial impact on the focuses of researches in this area. For instance, it has been a central problem to find a large vector space over the finite field F2 of order 2 that avoids nonzero vectors of small weight, that is, a binary linear code of large dimension and large minimum distance [1, chs. 1–2]. While this still remains to be a fundamental problem in coding theory today, the discoveries of the excellent performance of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes in the last two decades led to an influx of studies not on the structure of a particular linear code C but on that of matrices whose kernel is C, that is, its parity-check matrices [2].
One influential discovery in this context is the fact that a linear code with a relatively poor minimum distance can still be practical or even far outperform codes of the same length and size with larger minimum distances when there is

a severe restriction on decoding complexity. LPDC codes are a notable example that exhibits this phenomenon, where their powerful error correction capabilities come from an efficient iterative decoding algorithm whose performance is sensitive to the structure of the parity-check matrix in use. For this reason, it is now of great importance and interest to find special paritycheck matrices that are particularly suited for efficient iterative decoding methods.
Considering the remarkable success of LDPC codes, it may be surprising that we do not yet fully understand exactly what particular substructures of parity-check matrices are responsible for their excellent performance and other characteristics including negative ones such as the existence of error floors [3]. In fact, it seems quite difficult to combinatorially characterize the culprit structures in a parity-check matrix that makes iterative decoding fail, except in the case of the erasure channel, where we do know what kind of combinatorial substructure causes decoding failure under the standard iterative decoding for erasures and how [4, pp. 135-136].
This paper studies the structure of the parity-check matrices for the well-known class of LDPC codes based on projective planes, which was first investigated in the context of iterative decoding in [5]. This class of LDPC codes is all cyclic and one of the earliest examples whose remarkable error correction capabilities over the AWGN channel were discovered in simulations. They are also classic examples in which redundant parity-check equations appear to be, at least in part, responsible for the good performance under iterative decoding [6, p. 569]. As these LDPC codes are particularly well-structured from a combinatorial viewpoint, the substructures of their parity-check matrices have been investigated in classical coding theory [7], [8] as well as in the context of quantum error correction [9], [10].
Our focus is on small stopping sets in a parity-check matrix, which are known to completely determine the performance of the standard iterative decoding for erasures and can potentially have a nontrivial negative impact on iterative decoding over other channels [2, sec. 15.3]. Despite their apparent importance in coding theory, determining the spectrum of the sizes of small stopping sets in a parity-check matrix seems to be quite a challenging problem even for a well-known LDPC code. In the case of projective LDPC codes, while it is known that the size

In this paper, we prove that the well-known parity-check matrix based on a projective plane not only avoids stopping sets of size smaller than the minimum distance d but also contains no stopping sets of size d or d + 1 except those that correspond to the minimum-weight nonzero codewords. More specifically, we show the following.

The standard terminology regarding finite geometries used in the above theorem will be explained in the next section.
It is notable that Theorem 1.1 says that as long as the number of erased bits does not exceed the minimum distance by two or more, the LDPC code from a projective plane does not fail to recover erasures with the standard iterative decoding unless a maximum likelihood decoder also fails. A generic erasure correcting set is a tool for constructing such a parity-check matrix for any linear code, where the standard iterative decoding for erasures works as well as a maximum likelihood decoder even if the number of erased bits may be larger than the minimum distance [11], [12]. In addition to proving the above mentioned theorem on stopping sets in the projective LDPC codes, we improve one of the tightest known bounds on the size of a generic erasure correcting set. As we will see later, this result highlights how few the redundant parity-check equations are in the projective [q2 +q +1, q2 +q −3log2 q, q +2] LDPC code for a parity-check matrix that avoids small stopping sets to this extent.
In the next section, we briefly review the LDPC codes from projective planes and known results on stopping sets. The proof of Theorem 1.1 is given in Section III. Section IV gives the improved bound for generic erasure correcting sets and concludes this paper with some remarks.
II. PRELIMINARIES
In this section, we give a brief review of LDPC codes from projective planes. For the basic facts on LDPC codes and projective geometry in general, we refer the reader to standard textbooks such as [2], [13]. In what follows, we always assume that q is an even prime or prime power, that is, a power of 2.
Points and lines of the projective plane PG(2, q) over Fq are the 1-dimensional subspaces of Fq3 and the 2-dimensional ones, respectively. There are exactly q2 + q + 1 points and exactly q2 + q + 1 lines in PG(2, q). It is easy to see that each line contains exactly q +1 points, while for any pair of distinct points there exists a unique line that passes through them.
A line-by-point incidence matrix of PG(2, q) is the (q2 + q + 1) × (q2 + q + 1) binary matrix H = (hi,j) over F2 such that the rows and columns are indexed by lines and points, respectively, and such that hi,j = 1 if the ith line contains the

jth point and hi,j = 0 otherwise. The duality of PG(2, q) states that swapping the roles of points and lines of PG(2, q) gives rise to a projective plane over Fq again, which means that the transpose of a line-by-point incidence matrix of PG(2, q) is again a line-by-point incidence matrix of PG(2, q) with rows and columns indexed not necessarily in the same order. When seen as a parity-check matrix, a line-by-point incidence matrix of PG(2, q) defines a [q2 + q + 1, q2 + q − 3log2 q, q + 2] LDPC code [9]. Because reordering the rows and/or columns does not essentially change the underlining geometry or LDPC code, we may speak of the line-by-point incidence matrix of PG(2, q).
The LDPC codes from projective planes are known to have various desirable properties such as being cyclic, exhibiting low error floors, and having potentials for use in quantum communications [5], [9], [14]. Their parity-check matrices not only are combinatorially beautiful but also avoid small substructures that are harmful to iterative decoding, which may partially explain their good error correction performance observed in simulations [5], [14], [15].
A stopping set of size s in an m × n binary matrix H over F2 is the set S of indices of a set of s columns of H in which the corresponding m × s submatrix T of H contains no rows of weight 1. In the language of sparse graph codes, it is a set S of variable nodes in which no check nodes are adjacent to exactly one element of S. It is known that the stopping sets in a parity-check matrix completely characterize the behavior of the standard iterative decoding for erasures. In fact, as is the case with codewords of small weight for maximum likelihood decoding, small stopping sets are particularly harmful and the main culprit of decoding failure. For this reason, the size of a smallest stopping set in a given parity-check matrix is called the stopping distance. Problematic substructures for iterative decoding in general are often collectively called trapping sets, to which stopping sets also belong.
The line-by-point incidence matrix of PG(2, q) is known to contain no stopping sets of size smaller than or equal to q + 1.
Theorem 2.1 ( [7]): The (q2 + q + 1) × (q2 + q + 1) line-bypoint incidence matrix of PG(2, q) with q even defines a paritycheck matrix for the projective [q2 +q+1, q2 +q−3log2 q, q+2] LDPC code in which there exist no stopping sets of size less than or equal to q + 1. In other words, its stopping distance equals the minimum distance.
Note that the stopping distance of a parity-check matrix can not exceed the minimum distance of the code by definition. Note also that although it is not difficult to see that any linear code admits such a parity-check matrix, all else being equal, it is more desirable to have this property with fewer redundant rows because of decoding complexity and overhead for implementing a decoder.
For a given linear code C of minimum distance d, the stopping redundancy ρ(C) of C is the number of rows in a smallest parity-check matrix for C such that there exist no stopping sets of size smaller than or equal to d − 1. Theorem 2.1 says that the stopping redundancy of the projective [q2+q+1, q2+q−3log2 q, q+2] LDPC code is at most q2+q+1, which is the length of the code. This sharply contrasts with

IV. CONCLUDING REMARKS
We have proved that the LDPC code formed by the line-bypoint incidence matrix of PG(2, q) with q avoids stopping sets of size beyond the minimum distance as long as they are small

enough. While the previously known results could only show that the stopping distance of the parity-check matrix from a projective plane equals the minimum distance and that the stopping redundancy of the code is at most the code length, Theorem 1.1 proved in the previous section revealed that the desirable avoidance property of the projective planes extends beyond its stopping distance.
It is notable that combinatorial analyses of stopping sets of size beyond the minimum distance of a given linear code are rather scarce in the literature. The general analyses of stopping sets and random ensembles in this direction can be found in [19], [20]. For constructions for parity-check matrices for general linear codes avoiding small stopping sets beyond minimum distances, as far as the authors are aware, the only method in the literature is through the notion of generic error correcting sets introduced in [11]. As we briefly show in the remainder of this paper, it seems difficult for this approach to give as compact a parity-check matrix as the projective LDPC code while ensuring a high level of avoidance of small stopping sets.
Let r, m be positive integers such that r ≥ m. Given a linear code, a set E of coordinates is called correctable erasures if a maximum likelihood decoder can recover all bits in E when they are all erased. A generic (r, m)-erasure correcting set of size f is an f × r matrix A over F2 such that for any parity-check matrix H with r rows for any linear code C of codimension r, the product AH is a parity-check matrix for a subcode of C in which for any correctable erasure E of size at most m, the restriction of AH on E contains a row of weight 1. Roughly speaking, a generic (r, m)-erasure correcting set produces a parity-check matrix containing no avoidable small stopping sets of size at most m from any parity-check matrix for any [n, k, d] linear code with n − k = r. Because we would like a parity-check matrix with fewer redundant rows in general, we are interested in the size F (r, m) of a smallest generic (r, m)-erasure correcting set for given r and m. The known results on F (r, m) can be found in [11], [12], [21]. In particular, the following is the sharpest known upper bound on F (r, m).
