Abstract—In this paper, we develop the density evolution of bit-flipping algorithm (BFA) for regular low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes and analyze the evolution of probability density for the bit-decision error. In contrast to the sum-product algorithm (SPA) and min-sum algorithm (MSA), the density evolution for BFA has remained untouched, which motivates this work. In the developed density evolution for BFA, we first introduce a state variable which is a function of bit-decision result and the flipping function value at each iteration. Then, the state probability is derived, with which the density of bit-decision error is obtained. To confirm the derivations, we design regular LDPC codes and compare the empirical evaluations of bit-error rates and the derived probability of bit-decision errors.
Index Terms—LDPC code, bit-flipping algorithm, flipping function, density evolution
I. INTRODUCTION
LDPC codes [1] have been widely adopted in communication and storage systems due to their outstanding errorcorrecting capability. There have been various decoding algorithms for LDPC codes such as SPA, MSA and BFA among which SPA computes and exchanges messages between variable nodes (VNs) and check nodes (CNs) in the form of log-likelihood ratio or probability. Meanwhile, BFA exchanges messages of single bit, which significantly reduces decoding complexity. In particular, the messages from VNs and CNs are bit-decision results and syndrome values, respectively. The syndrome computation at each CN is performed with the modulo-two addition for the messages from VNs, and the bitdecision at each VN is made with a flipping function which is defined as the sum of the CN messages, i.e. syndrome values, from adjacent CNs. The flipping function value implies the reliability of bit-decision at a VN based on the messages from its adjacent CNs.
The density evolution is a tool which traces the evolutions of probability density functions for messages in an iterative decoding. The density evolution is especially useful in the sense that for a given LDPC code, it allows one to predict the level of channel degradation, called threshod below which reliable communication is achievable. Moreover, it can be utilized to optimize LDPC code ensembles assuming a specific iterative decoding algorithm. While the density evolution for the Gallager’s algorithms [3], SPA, and MSA were already studied in [3], [4], BFA has remained untouched, which motivates this work. The Gallager’s algorithms and BFA seem to have

some similarities. However, while the Gallager’s algorithms exchange messages without flipping its bit-decision result at each VN, BFA keeps updating the bit-decision result during iterations. Due to the bit-flipping operation, the probability for the bit-decision at a VN to be erroneous is not independent and identical across VNs.
This work derives the density evolution of BFA for regular LDPC codes by introducing a state variable and obtaining the state probability. The state variable is defined as a function of the bit-decision and flipping function value and forms a Markov chain as the iterations proceeds. Then, the evolution of bit-decision error at each VN is expressed based on the state variable. This work assumes binary-symmetric channel (BSC) over which bit-error rates of regular LDPC codes are empirically evaluated. Then, the developed density evolution will be confirmed by comparing the bit-error rates and the probability of bit-decision from the density evolution.

IV. SIMULATION RESULTS

In this section, we confirm the analytic results of the density evolution developed in this work by comparing certain probability distributions obtained in both analytically and empirical ways. First, we evaluate the conditional state probability Pr[Ψ( ) = ψ|Z( )] at a crossover probability of = 4.0×10−2 and = 2 given that the VN is correct and erroneous, i.e. Z( ) = 0 or 1, for a regular LDPC code ensemble with dv = 4, dc = 8. Meanwhile, the conditional probability is also empirically obtained for a quasi-cyclic (QC) regular LDPC code with dv = 4, dc = 8 at length N = 16384 which consists of circulants of size Q = 128. A parity-check matrix is constructed with the QC progressive edge growth (QCPEG) algorithm [6]. The evaluation of the state probability is based on observations of 200 received codewords over BSC. The conditional probabilities from the analytic and empirical evaluations are depicted in Figs. 1 and 2, respectively. It is noticed in Figs. 1 and 2 that the conditional probabilities obtained from the different approaches look very close each other, which confirms the results in this work.
Now, we will conduct comparisons between the bit-decision error rates in (2) and a corresponding empirical measure, i.e. bit-error rates for two LDPC codes with (dv = 3, dc = 6) and (dv = 4, dc = 8). The the bit-decision error and bit-error rates are evaluated at each iteration for the comparisons. In particular, the evaluations are carried out for the LDPC codes with (dv = 3, dc = 6) and (dv = 4, dc = 8) at different crossover probabilities, i.e. = 3.5 × 10−2 and = 4 × 10−2, respectively. For the bit-error rate evaluations, we construct two QC LDPC codes at length N = 16384 with the QC-PEG algorithm in which the circulant size is set to Q = 128. In the evaluation of (2), the threshold, θ( ) is determined at each iteration, say , to minimize the bit-decision error rate at the next iteration, i.e. + 1.
