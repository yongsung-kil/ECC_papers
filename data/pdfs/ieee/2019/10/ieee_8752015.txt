Thresholds of Absorbing Sets Under Scaled Min-Sum LDPC Decoding
Marco Ferrari , Member, IEEE, Alessandro Tomasoni , Ramon Marenzi, and Sandro Bellini

Abstract— In this paper, the definition of threshold of elementary absorbing sets is extended to scaled Min-Sum lowdensity parity-check (LDPC) decoding. Based on the analysis of the behavior of scaled Min-Sum LDPC decoders in the Tanner graph of the absorbing set, it is proven that correct decoding is guaranteed with received channel messages above threshold. A fast algorithm for the threshold computation is derived. Many examples of absorbing sets taken from LDPC codes of various variable node degrees are investigated, and it is shown that all of them can be deactivated with low enough scaling factors.
Index Terms— Low-density parity-check codes, error floor, absorbing sets, scaled min-sum decoding, absorbing set threshold, tanner graph.
I. INTRODUCTION
L OW-DENSITY Parity-Check (LDPC) codes are a class of linear block codes well fit for various suboptimal iterative decoding algorithms, i.e., belief propagation (BP) or sum-product algorithm (SPA) [1], min-sum (MS), scaled (or normalized) MS [2], conditional or unconditional offsetMS [3], [4]. Many LDPC codes suffer from the error floor phenomenon, which is a significant change of slope in the word error rate (WER) and bit error rate (BER) curves versus the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). This performance degradation, that in some LDPC codes is caused by a relatively low minimum Hamming distance, is more often due to the suboptimality of message passing decoding in the Tanner graph of the code [5].
Iterative message passing decoding in graphs with loops can fail due to subsets of Variable Nodes (VN) able to lock the decoder to incorrect decisions (non-codewords), since iterative

Absorbing sets (AS) are a particular class of TS defined in [7] as subsets D of VN where every VN has a minority of neighboring CN with odd degree with respect to D. This purely topological characterization enables a decoderindependent search for AS, which can be run on the code graph using analytical [8] or algorithmic methods [9]. In [7] and [10], the authors define also fully AS, which are trapping sets for the binary symmetric channel with iterative bit-flipping decoding, as the corresponding near-codewords are stable competitors of the all-zero codeword. AS also describe the dominant decoding failures of various soft message passing decoders [7], [11], [8]. Elementary TS (ETS), and their subclass of elementary AS (EAS) are those whose induced subgraph contains CN with degree at most 2 with respect to D. Although recent results for non Gaussian channels report non elementary AS as harmful error-prone structures [12], on Gaussian channels EAS are recognized as the main cause of errors [6], [13], [14]. Their search and analysis has catalyzed most of the attention in the last decade [15]–[19].
In [15], the authors showed that a local analysis of iterative decoding inside elementary TS could be carried by a linear dynamical system or state-space model, under some simplifying hypotheses. Considering MS decoding, and assuming that in the Tanner graph the messages outside the TS converge towards the correct decisions according to Density Evolution (DE), the updated extrinsic messages exchanged by the nodes in the TS are given by linear combinations of external and internal messages generated at the previous iteration. This linear model was generalized to the SPA decoder in [16]. The authors applied their model to the AS of the IEEE 802.3an LDPC code, to predict the error floor. In [17], the analysis with the linear state-space model has been further extended to more general TS structures. The authors also considered the effect of messages saturation due to finite precision, assuming that messages entering the TS from the external graph evolve, iteration by iteration, according to discretized DE or simulated distributions. The analysis confirms the empirical observation that the probability of being trapped by a TS strongly depends on the level of saturation of the decoder messages. Similar observations appeared independently in [20].
In our works, we focused our attention on practical LDPC decoders based on MS decoding, with fixed point representation of all messages, as in most practical implementations. The fixed point representation introduces a saturation level, since

messages are clipped to a maximal value, which depends on the quantization choices. In [21], we introduced the saturation in the linear state-space model of the MS decoder. We focused on the analysis of equilibria, looking for sufficient conditions for successful decoding. We defined the AS threshold, that is an AS parameter that discriminates the existence/non-existence of misleading equilibria, i.e., equilibria that are not guaranteed to correspond to correct decoding. Though in general the decoder can be trapped by an AS and reach an equilibrium that does not lead to the correct decisions, in [21] it is shown that if all VN in the AS have channel messages above the AS threshold, the only equilibrium of the system corresponds to maximal extrinsic messages and leads to correct decisions.
The threshold is a real-valued parameter that depends only on the topology of the AS, and is the result of a max min optimization, described in [22]. For AS with negative threshold, the condition that channel messages are above the threshold can be guaranteed choosing a suitable saturation level of the extrinsic messages. Therefore, these AS are deactivated. These results are in agreement with the fact that the error floor level of quantized LDPC decoders is strongly related to the quantization choices [8], [23]. An AS whose threshold is positive cannot be deactivated, and must be eliminated by other means (see, e.g., [24], [25]).
In [26], we delved into the behavior of the MS decoder model introduced in [21] to describe the evolution of extrinsic messages exchanged by VN and CN inside an elementary AS. We studied equilibria, and we showed that for each set of channel messages there exists a minimum equilibrium. We also proved that minimum equilibria can be ranked according to the minimum of the channel messages. As a consequence, the threshold of an AS can be found by tracking the minimum equilibrium versus the minimum channel message, up to a point where the minimum equilibrium corresponds to the maximal value for extrinsic messages. This algorithm is much simpler and faster than [22]. We tested it with the well known Margulis code, whose population of AS is described in [21].
In this paper, the analysis is generalized to scaled-MS decoders. Scaling is a popular means to reduce the SNR loss of MS decoding with respect to BP [2]. The definition of AS threshold is extended to scaled-MS decoding. The theorems in [26] are generalized to scaled-MS decoders and are used to prove that correct decoding inside an elementary AS is guaranteed above its threshold. Finally, a fast algorithm to compute the AS threshold under scaled-MS decoding is derived.
Many examples of AS, with VN-degrees dv = 3, 4, 5, and 6, show that the threshold values for scaled-MS decoders are lower than for the MS decoder. Therefore, beside improving convergence, scaling also lowers the error floor. This fact is confirmed, for instance, by simulation of the Margulis code.
The paper is organized as follows. Section II introduces the notation and the system model for the local analysis of iterative decoding inside an AS. In Section III, the definition of AS threshold is extended to scaled-MS decoding. In Section IV, equilibria of the state-space model are investigated. The two main outcomes of the analysis are the proof of the decoder behavior above threshold and a fast

algorithm for the threshold computation. In Section V, many AS thresholds examples are shown and, finally, Section VI draws the conclusions.
II. SYSTEM MODEL AND BACKGROUND
Let GH = (V, C, B) denote the bipartite Tanner graph [5] of a binary LDPC code with parity-check matrix H. The set of vertices V = {vi} is associated with the bits in the code (columns of H), and the set C = {cj} is associated with the parity checks of the code (rows of H). The edge set B is defined by H; there exists an edge e(i, j) ∈ B connecting vi and cj if and only if Hj,i = 1. Elements of V are called variable nodes (VN) and elements of C are called check nodes (CN). The set of CN N (vi) = {cj : ∃ e(i, j) ∈ B} and the set of VN N (cj) = {vi : ∃ e(i, j) ∈ B} are the neighborhood of the ith VN and jth CN, respectively.

B. Background: EAS and State-Space Models

Consider a subset of VN D ⊂ V and the CN in the neighborhood N (D) {N (vi), vi ∈ D}. Let E(D) and O(D) be the set of neighboring CN connected to D an even or odd number of times, respectively. Let a and b denote the cardinality of D and O(D), respectively. D is an (a, b) absorbing set if every VN in D has more neighboring CN in E(D) than in O(D) [7]. Besides, an AS D(a, b) is elementary if the CN in N (D) are connected to D no more than twice.
Elementary AS allow a simple linear state-space model
for the evolution of the vector x of the extrinsic messages
exchanged inside the AS at each iteration [15], [16], [27]. For
instance, in [17], the sequence of states xk obeys the linear system1

The system matrix A describes the dependence of the state update upon the prior state vector xk−1. The matrix B replicates the channel message received by each VN, inside each message propagated by that VN. The matrix Bex does the same, with the external messages entering D from the outside graph. The average gains g¯k are used to account for the influence of the messages received from the external graph by the CN E(D) in the SPA.
When messages are allowed to take arbitrarily large values, the state-space model analysis focuses on the growth rate of messages xk and λ(kex) [15], [16]. When the messages are saturated to a maximum value, the behavior is quite different. This is analyzed in [17] using probability distributions for λ(kex) whose variance, empirically or analytically computed via discretized DE, vanishes with increasing k.
In [21], as in [15]–[17], we studied the decoder behavior inside D, assuming that iterative decoding converges to the correct decisions in the rest of the graph. We considered MS decoders and we introduced the saturation function in the state-space model, focusing on the analysis of possible equilibria. In this paper, we generalize our approach to scaledMS decoding.
As in [21], to avoid the problem of modelling the dynamical evolution of messages outside D, unlike [15], [16], [27], [17], the following analysis of the iterations is started when the messages uvi→cj are already saturated to +1 for all vi ∈/ D. In this scenario, CN of degree-2 in the AS simply scale and swap the messages over the two internal edges, since they are certainly the least reliable entries to the CN, with no need of CN gain or inversion modelling as in [16] and [27]. On the other hand, the starting value x(0) is unknown, and every possible vector should be taken into account to find sufficient conditions for successful decoding of D. But if there are no starting conditions which lead to a failure, correct decoding is guaranteed independently of the initial evolution of the internal or external extrinsic messages. This is a great simplification especially at very low error probabilities which may be affected by rare events, hardly predicted by models based on average behaviors. In the next subsection, the quasilinear state-space model introduced in [21] is extended to scaled-MS decoding.

VI. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we extended the definition of threshold of elementary AS introduced in [21], to LDPC decoding based on scaled Min-Sum. The threshold of an AS D has been shown in [21] to be strongly correlated with its dangerousness. If all VN in D have channel messages above the threshold, the only equilibrium of the system leads to the correct decisions. For AS with negative threshold, the condition that channel messages are above the threshold can be guaranteed choosing a suitable saturation level of the extrinsic messages. Then, these AS are deactivated.
