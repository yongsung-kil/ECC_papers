DESIGN AND IMPLEMENTATION OF PARALLEL BRANCHES FOR CONCATENATED BCH AND LDPC CODING ON FPGA
Ahmed Mahdy1, Mohamed Helmy2, Mohamed Hassan3 1Arab Academy for Science and Technology and Maritime, Cairo, Egypt, ahmedmahdy189@gmail.com
2Canadian International College (CIC), Cairo, Egypt, mhmmae_m@hotmail.com 3Arab Academy for Science and Technology and Maritime, Cairo, Egypt, mhabdazeem@hotmail.com

ABSTRACT
Recently, satellite services applications are of wide utilization as in radio and television broadcast, voice and data communication, and location services communication. Error correction codes are continuously designed to accomplish reliable communication with low Bit-Error-Rate (BER) in satellite communication link. Modern techniques are using concatenation of different codes, to achieve low BER. This paper presents an implementation for Bose-Chaudauri Hocquenghem (BCH) on FPGA. Also, it provides an implementation for low density parity check (LDPC) on FPGA. Finally, a design and implementation for multiple branches of BCH concatenated with LDPC on FPGA, where the encoder is designed such that two parallel BCH branches are concatenated with one LDPC, while the decoder design is four parallel BCH branches concatenated with one LDPC. This proposed design improves the link reliability and increases the throughput when compared with the serial concatenated coding systems, where the encoder throughput increased from 100 Mbps to 200 Mbps, and the decoder enhanced from 10.5 Mbps to 50 Mbps.
Keywords: BER, BCH, LDPC, Concatenated.
I. INTRODUCTION
The world of communications is witnessing a continuous struggle to attain better performance. The efforts towards attaining a lower Bit-Error-Rate (BER), higher throughput, and lower power consumption are the main goal of any communication system. In this paper, we will discuss two techniques: Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) [1], and Bose-Chaudauri Hocquenghem (BCH) codes [2]. The second-generation Digital Video Broadcast (DVB) [3] standards use a combination of both outer coding BCH and inner coding LDPC. BCH codes are broadly utilized in data protection. In addition, LDPC exceptionally has error rectifying capability. Amongst the existing coding techniques, serial concatenation of codes has the advantage of being a powerful scheme that can increase the error correcting capability of a resulting system and decrease Bit-Error-Rate (BER) as well [4]. In earlier work [6], the design of concatenated code of BCH and LDPC had a problem of degradation of the throughput due to serial concatenation. Accordingly, we aim to improve the degradation of throughput of serial concatenation code, to be suited for real time application. This is done via designing parallel processing of BCH code with LDPC code. The proposed scheme provides parallel processing of BCH code with LDPC to improve throughput besides improving error correction capability. The design of parallel BCH code with LDPC is based on the time delay of a single branch of BCH code. This paper is organized as follows: Section II related work. Section III describes BCH code and its implementation, Section IV explain LDPC. Section V discusses the concatenation of parallel BCH code with LDPC code, then the simulation is presented in section VI. Finally, the conclusion is written in section VII.
II. RELATED WORK
In [7], the author proposed a parallel BCH concatenated with LDPC code with a selection of equal-length BCH codes, the information bits will be divided into N groups, each group will be encoded by their related BCH encoder and the output of every N-BCH codeword will be assembled and get into the LDPC encoder. A typical decoding procedure for such a concatenated coding system is done in the decoder, the concatenation of LDPC codes with accurately selected BCH codes will erase the undesirable error floor. While in [8] the author proposed the same scheme as [7] with a modification of unequal-length BCH codes instead of equal-length BCH. This leads to better error correction capability in the waterfall region in addition to maintaining low error floor. Yet In [9], the author indicates that parallel BCH and LDPC encoders lead to achieving high throughput and high speed using parallel LDPC-IRA. Due to the lack of information to decide the number of parallel branches and information to build interface between BCH and LDPC encoder/decoder, it was our main goal to build the proposed design with two BCH branches and one LDPC at the encoder, then the proposed design defines four BCH branches with one LDPC at decoder, the proposed scheme is used to enhance the throughput and reduce the time delay.

III. BCH CODE

The BCH codes, discovered in 1960 [5], are a large class of error correction codes that uses up a noticeable place in theory and practice of error correction. It is a class of cyclic codes with powerful error-correcting properties and well-known implementation algorithms [6]. The following parameters characterize BCH codes: For positive integer m, such that m ≥ 3 and number of errors required for correction is t. There exists a binary BCH code such that:

•t

Maximum number of errors to be corrected

• n=2m-1

Block Length

• dmin ≥ 2t+1 Minimum distance

• r=n-k ≤ m*t Parity-Che

A. BCH Encoder.
BCH encoder implemented as cyclic codes [2]. Its implementation based on linear feedback shift register (LFSR) [5]. Utilizing the properties of cyclic codes, the remainder b(x) can obtained in a linear (n-k) stage shift register with feedback connections according to the coefficients of the generator polynomial. g(x) = 1 + g1x + g2x2 + ... ……..+ gn-k-1 xn-k-1 + xn-k. Such a circuit is shown in Fig. 1.

Fig. 1: The operation of the encoder LFSR design. For the clock cycle 1 to k, original message bits are transmitted without changing its form (in this process S2 is in status 2), and the parity bits calculated by LFSR (now S1 is on). For cycle k+1 to n, the generated parity bits in the LFSR are transmitted (S2 is in status 1) and the feedback in the LFSR is switch off (S1 off). For BCH encoder (7,4,1), which encodes 4 bits to 7 bits and single error correcting BCH code is designed by organizing LFSR with generated polynomial 1+X2+X3 and implemented using Field Programmable Gate Array (FPGA) [5]. Fig. 2 shows the 3-stage encoding shift registers.

Fig. 2: The 3-stage encoding shift registers. The simulation of the BCH (7, 4, 1) encoding stage using Modelsim is shown in Figure 3. The message bits [0101] is encoded to (0101100) and the message bits [1010] is encoded to (1010011).The 3 clock cycles between the two inputs is the encoding interval.

IV. LDPC CODE
Low density parity-check code (LDPC) is an error correcting code specified by a matrix containing mostly 0’s and only a small number of 1’s, making it sparse. Also Tanner graph [10] can be used to represent the code. A Tanner graph is a graph whose nodes can divided into two sets such that each node is connected to a node in the other set. The sets of nodes in Tanner graph called variable nodes and check nodes representing columns and rows respectively. Fig. 10 is an example of Tanner Graph and represents the same code as the matrix H. It composed of m check nodes and n variable nodes. Variable node Cj is connected to check node Fi if the element hij of H is a 1.

B. LDPC Decoder
The decoding of LDPC depends on an algorithm called bit-flipping; it is a hard-decision message-passing algorithm for LDPC code. The variable node make hard-decision on received bits, and then send them to the connected check node. Each check node XOR the received bits form the variable nodes, then if the result of mod-2 addition of all check nodes is zero, then the received bits is correct, otherwise the check nodes return the XOR bits to the variable node to checks the majority of them and make decision on the received bits. This procedure is iterated until some maximum number of decoder iterations have passed and the decoder gives up, or until all of the paritycheck equations are satisfied [4].
Assuming there is an error in the two messages and the received codeword for both are [0101010100001110] and [1001010001101100], the LDPC decoder detects the error and corrects it so the decoded messages are [01010101] and [10010100]. As mentioned before, we used BCH (7, 4, 1) and LDPC (16, 8), the input to the LDPC is 8 bits, while the output of the BCH is 7 bits so we add ‘0’ bit to the output of the BCH at the left hand side of the BCH codeword to make it possible for the LDPC to encode the BCH codeword while at the decoder the padded bit will be removed after decoding. As shown in Fig. 15, we used two BCH and one LDPC. Because of the 4-bits input of the BCH is series, it will take 4 clock cycles to read the message and 3 clock cycles to encode the message, and 1 clock for LDPC to generate the output. So, the first output take 8 clock to generate codeword, and the next codewords generated after 4 clock.

It is clear that the proposed parallel branch schemes save 4 clock in each generated codeword than the standard single branch scheme, which achieve higher throughput than the traditional one. As shown in Fig. 16 The decoding scheme will use four parallel BCH branches with one LDPC, The LDPC takes 4 CLK to get 8 bit from 16 bit, then the BCH takes this 8 bits and remove the padding and decode in 15 CLK meanwhile, the LDPC will switch to another BCH branch until the first branch finish processing, this process repeated continuously which save the time delay.
To achieve synchronization, the frequency of the BCH must be higher than the frequency of the LDPC, so we will use frequency down conversion to avoid the problem of the synchronization. Implementation of this concatenated part achieved using FPGA. As shown in Fig. 17, the message bits [0101] is encoded to [0010110000001111] and the message bits [1010] is encoded to [0101010001100110].

VII. CONCLUSION
This paper discussed the design and implementation of parallel branches for concatenated BCH and LDPC to improve the performance of single branch BCH concatenated with LDPC. The proposed scheme used two parallel BCH branches concatenated with LDPC at the encoder, while four BCH branches concatenated with LDPC used at the decoder, which enhances the throughput of the satellite communication link. It shows that the encoder saves 4 clock than the single branch and the decoder saves 15 clock to process each codeword. The proposed scheme used 28% of kit resources while serial concatenation used 13% of kit resources. On the other side it increases the throughput where the encoder throughout increased from 100 Mbps to 200 Mbps, and the decoder enhanced from 10.5 Mbps to 50 Mbps. Future research could examine the implementation of our proposed design on a complete wireless communication system as satellite communication system. To simulate the BER curve against the SNR where it will be needed most, as they suffer from high error rate and low SNR, Also using it in cloud networks for the same reason.
