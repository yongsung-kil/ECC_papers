Sliding-Window Processing of Turbo Equalization for Partial Response Channels

Sirawit Khittiwitchayakul, Watid Phakphisut, and Pornchai Supnithi Faculty of Engineering, King Mongkut's Institute of Technology Ladkrabang, Bangkok 10520, Thailand.
Email: {57601155, watid.ph, pornchai.su}@kmitl.ac.th

Abstract— Spatially coupled low-density parity-check (SCLDPC) codes are the attractive candidates for the application requiring a practical constraint on the latency and complexity. In previous works, the turbo equalization consisting the SC-LDPC window decoder and Bahl-Cocke-Jelinek-Raviv (BCJR) detector is used to tackle the inter-symbol interferences (ISIs) in the magnetic recording systems. However, the prior works consider only the SC-LDPC codes which replace the conventional LDPC block codes. In this work, we propose the modification of turbo equalization processing whereby both the SC-LDPC decoder and BCJR detector operate using the sliding window. The results show that the proposed turbo equalization schemes used in the partial response (PR) channel can provide better bit error rate (BER) performances than the conventional turbo equalization.
Keywords— LDPC codes, spatially coupled codes, BCJR detector, sliding-window, turbo equalization.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check block codes (LDPC-BC) [1] with iterative decoding have been demonstrated to achieve the performance approaching the channel capacity in an additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel [2]. In addition, the error-correcting capability of LDPC-BC can be improved when their block size is expanded [3]. Unfortunately, the complexity of LDPC-BC decoder is also increased in a linearly function. In recent years, the spatially coupled LDPC (SC-LDPC) codes [4] also known as LDPC convolutional codes have been proposed. The SC-LDPC codes can be constructed by coupling several LDPC-BC transmitted at different times [5]. The attractive benefit of SC-LDPC codes is that the decoding threshold converges to the maximum a-posteriori (MAP) threshold of underlying LDPC-BC over binary erasure channel (BEC) [6].
Since the edge connections between consecutive code blocks of SC-LDPC codes are limited, the sliding window decoding proposed in [7] can be applied in the decoder. In [7], the SCLDPC codes with sliding window decoding provide lower latency and complexity than those of LDPC-BC. This result indicates that the SC-LDPC codes are the strong candidates for applications requiring capacity-approaching performance and acceptable complexity. For example, in recent works, the SCLDPC codes are considered for the mobile communication standards [8] and the high-density magnetic recording (MR) systems [9]. Especially, in our previous work [10], the SCLDPC codes are employed in the turbo equalization of the MR system. The window decoder of SC-LDPC codes cooperates with Bahl-Cocke-Jelinek-Raviv (BCJR) detector to tackle the inter-symbol interferences (ISIs). However, the sliding window

decoding is only applied for the SC-LDPC decoder. Although the window decoder can provide the output node-by-node, the BCJR detector must start to process only after the entire codewords are decoded by SC-LDPC decoder. Therefore, in this work, we propose “sliding-window turbo equalization (SW-TE) scheme” by modifying the conventional turbo equalization (CTE) scheme [10] with the sliding window decoding of BCJR detector and SC-LDPC decoder. In the SW-TE scheme, the information previously decoded will be used to decode the next window decoding. Consequently, the SW-TE scheme can converge faster than the C-TE scheme, and moreover it also provides the better BER performances. As the number of node updates of SW-TE scheme is much greater than that of the C-TE scheme, therefore, we further propose the reduced-complexity SW-TE (RC-SW-TE) scheme to reduce the number of node updates during the iterative message passing. The results show that the RC-SW-TE scheme can provide the reduction in complexity compared to the SW-TE scheme without any loss in BER performances.
The rest of paper is organized as follow. In Section II, we briefly review the definitions of the SC-LDPC codes, and the sliding window decoding employed in the turbo equalization. Afterward, the proposed turbo equalization schemes are described in Section III. Finally, the simulation results and conclusions are presented in Section IV and V, respectively.
II. SPATIALLY COUPLED LDPC (SC-LDPC) CODES IN TURBO EQUALIZATION
A. Protograph-based SC-LDPC codes A protograph [11] is a small bipartite graph which can be
extended to the derived graph of LDPC codes. It is represented by the base matrix B containing exactly J and K non-zero elements in each column and row, respectively. The derived graph corresponding to the parity check matrix H can be given by replacing non-zero elements in B with the N × N permutation matrix and every zero elements by an N × N all-zero matrix. By the definitions given in [7], we now explain the (J, K)-SC-LDPC code by examining the transmission of L protograph code blocks, v = [v1, . . ., vt, . . ., vL]. An essential feature of SC-LDPC code is that the transmitted code blocks are interconnected to each other [5], where the memory mcc is denoted as the maximal distance between a pair of coupled code blocks. The resulting SC-LDPC code can be viewed as a protograph with base matrix B = [B1, . . ., Bt, . . ., BL]. In order to achieve the coupled code, the edges of the variable node at time t are distributed to the check nodes at times t + i, i = 0, 1, . . ., mcc, known as the edgespreading procedure [5] - [7]. B. Reduced-complexity of sliding window turbo equalization (RC-SW-TE) scheme
Since the BCJR detector of SW-TE scheme are operated using the sliding window, the number of trellis node updates is much greater than that of the C-TE scheme. Therefore, the computational complexity of SW-TE scheme is higher than CTE scheme. Herein, we further propose the “RC-SW-TE” scheme to reduce the complexity of SW-TE scheme. We will ignore to update the information of some trellis nodes during the lth turbo iterative processing. In Fig. 4, we now examine the BER performances of each code block within the window on the PR1 channel, HPR = [1 1]. Here, we set the memory mcc = 2 and the window size W = 8. The ILDPC is set to 20 and the ITURBO is varied from 1 to 6. Due to the characteristic of sliding window decoder, the BER performances of each code blocks within a window are different (increase from left to right). Moreover, the result reveals that the BER performances of each code block will slightly decrease at a higher turbo iteration.

In Fig. 5, we show an example of the LLR exchanges between the trellis nodes and variable nodes during the lth turbo iteration, where the window size is set to 8. At the first turbo iteration as
an initialization phase, all the positions of the trellis and variable nodes are updated. During the 2nd turbo iteration shown
in Fig. 5(a), only the trellis nodes and variable nodes at the
position p = 1, 2, . . ., 7 will be updated, and hence the edges connected between the trellis nodes and variable nodes at the position p = 8 are omitted. Similarly, when the turbo iteration is increased to 4, only the trellis nodes and variable nodes at the
position p = 1, 2 …., 5 are updated as shown in Fig. 5(b). Herein, it must be noted that the target node (p = 1) is always updated at every turbo iteration, therefore, the ITURBO is selected by satisfying the condition ITURBO ≤ W – 1 to avoid the degradation of BER performances.

A. Convergent performances of turbo equalization schemes in PR channels
The purpose of this part is to investigate the convergent performance of C-TE and SW-TE schemes in the PR1 channel. Herein, the turbo iteration ITURBO is varied at the SNR of 4.6 dB. The simulation results in Fig. 6 show that the BER performances of both turbo equalization schemes are improved when the turbo iteration ITURBO is increased. However, for the C-TE scheme, the BER performances will stop decreasing when the turbo iteration ITURBO is above 10. Furthermore, the BER performance of the SW-TE scheme stop decreasing when the turbo iteration ITURBO is increased above 4. Since the information of previously decoded nodes are used to decode the next window decoding, the proposed SW-TE scheme can converge faster than the C-TE scheme. Moreover, the SW-TE scheme provide better BER performance than C-TE scheme.

B. Performance comparisions of turbo equalization schemes in PR channels
In this part, we show the BER performance comparisons of the previous C-TE, proposed SW-TE and RC-SW-TE schemes as shown in Fig. 7. Herein, we consider the BER of 2×10-4 which is sufficient for the communication standard scenarios. Consider at the same turbo iteration of 4, the SW-TE schemes can achieve about 0.5 dB coding gain compared to the C-TE scheme in the PR1 channel, and similar behavior is observed for the PR2 channel. Based on the results given from Section IV-A, all the schemes are compared evidently at the convergent performance of which the turbo iterations of the C-TE and SW-TE (RC-SWTE) schemes are set to 10 and 4, respectively. The simulation results show that the BER performances of the SW-TE schemes are still better than those of the C-TE schemes. The coding gain is about 0.4 dB for the PR1 and PR2 channels. Moreover, the results also reveal that the BER performances of RC-SW-TE are comparable to those of the SW-TE schemes.
