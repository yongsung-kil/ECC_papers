On Finite Alphabet Iterative Decoding of LDPC Codes With High-Order Modulation
Xuan He , Kui Cai , and Zhen Mei

Abstract‚ÄĒ This work aims to apply the mutual information-maximizing finite alphabet iterative decoding (MIM-FAID) method to decode low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes with high-order modulation. To this end, we propose an efficient model, through which our task becomes to design an MIM quantizer for a binary-input continuous memoryless channel (CMC) with log-likelihood ratios (LLRs) as the output. We then properly discretize the LLRs to convert the CMC to a discrete memoryless channel (DMC) such that we can obtain the MIM quantizer by using dynamic programming. Both theoretical analysis and simulation results verify the effectiveness of our proposed method.
Index Terms‚ÄĒ Channel quantization, finite alphabet iterative decoding (FAID), high-order modulation, low-density paritycheck (LDPC) code, mutual information (MI).
I. INTRODUCTION
H IGH-ORDER modulation schemes, such as pulse amplitude modulation (PAM), quadrature amplitude modulation (QAM), phase-shift keying (PSK), and amplitude phase-shift keying (APSK), are widely used in practical transmission systems due to their high bandwidth efficiency. For example, 256-ary QAM is employed by the secondgeneration digital terrestrial television broadcasting standard (DVB-T2) [1]. At the receiver, a symbol-to-bit demapper is needed for computing each bit‚Äôs soft information which will be used for decoding. Since the conventional log-domain maximum a posteriori probability (Log-MAP) demapping algorithm has high complexity, many efforts have been devoted to simplify the max-sum-approximation-based version of the Log-MAP (Max-Log-MAP) demapping [2], [3].
Recently, mutual information-maximizing finite alphabet iterative decoding (MIM-FAID) [4]‚Äď[7] of low-density paritycheck (LDPC) code [8] with binary phase-shift keying (BPSK) modulation attracts a lot of interest. The corresponding decoders are very hardware-friendly. Moreover, they can generally approach and sometimes even outperform the floating-point belief propagation (BP) decoder in terms of error rates. Therefore, it is an interesting problem to apply these decoders to decode LDPC codes with high-order modulation.

In [9], a technique named message alignment was proposed for this problem. In the first step, [9] quantizes the channel so as to maximize the mutual information (MI) between the quantization outputs and the modulation symbols. In the second step, through message alignment, it manipulates the quantization outputs in a way such that for each quantization output, different bits involved in a modulation symbol will have similar log-likelihood ratios (LLRs). However, this technique suffers from severe loss of MI between the quantization outputs and each channel input bit, especially during its first step operation. Instead, in this work, we propose an efficient model for this problem, through which our task is turned to design an MIM quantizer for a binary-input continuous memoryless channel (CMC) with LLRs as the output. However, designing the MIM quantizer seems generally impossible for a general binary-input CMC [10], while it is easy for a general binaryinput discrete memoryless channel (DMC) [11], [12]. With respect to this, we properly discretize the LLRs to convert the CMC to a DMC, and then obtain the MIM quantizer for the DMC by applying the dynamic programming (DP) method [11], [12]. Our model and solution can also be easily applied to multilevel flash memory systems [13], [14].
The remainder of this letter is organized as follows. Section II introduces the preliminary on modulation and demodulation. Section III proposes the problem model. Section IV illustrates how to design the MIM quantizer involved in the problem model. Section V presents the simulation results. Finally, Section VI concludes this letter.
Throughout this letter, the following notations are used. Uppercase calligraphic letters (e.g., X ) denote sets, and their associated uppercase and lowercase letters (e.g., X and x) denote the corresponding random variables and realizations, respectively. For any discrete (resp. continuous) random variable X, denote PX (x) (resp. pX (x)) as the probability (resp. probability density) of X = x. For any two discrete (resp. continuous) random variables X and Y , denote PY |X (y|x) (resp. pY |X(y|x)) as the probability (resp. probability density) of Y = y conditioned on X = x.

III. PROBLEM FORMULATION
We model the problem as shown by Fig. 1. The original CMC with input S and output Yňú is equivalent to m parallel binary-input sub-channels Ch1, Ch2, . . . , Chm, where Chi, 1 ‚Č§ i ‚Č§ m, is the sub-channel that the i-th bit Xi of each modulation symbol is transmitted over [15]. Let E ‚ąą {1, 2, . . . , m} be a random variable, with E = i indicating that the channel input bit X is transmitted over Chi. We have PE(i) = 1/m, i.e., X has equal probability to be transmitted over different sub-channels. Given E = i and Yňú = yňú at the receiver, the demapper computes the LLR Lňú for X by Li(yňú), and then Lňú is quantized into a channel message symbol W ‚ąą W = {1, 2, . . . , K} by a quantizer Qch. Our task is to design Qch : Lňú ‚Üí W to maximize I(X; W ).
We have two remarks regarding this model. First, the quantized channel message W is necessary for the MIM-FAID [4]‚Äď[7]. However, the MIM-FAID methods cannot make use of the information of the specific E associated with each W (i.e., the information that from which sub-channel W is generated). Thus, it is reasonable to design Qch to maximize I(X; W ) instead of maximizing I(X; W, E). V. SIMULATION RESULTS
We investigate the bit error rate (BER) and frame error rate (FER) performance of the mutual information-maximizing quantized belief propagation (MIM-QBP) decoder [4] for decoding LDPC codes with high-order modulation transmission over the AWGN channel. A 3-bit or 4-bit MIM-QBP decoder corresponds to the case that each message symbol alphabet (e.g., W) used by the decoder has cardinality of 8 or 16, respectively. For each MIM-QBP decoder, we will specify its design parameters [4]: qc (number of bits used for the additions for check node update), qv (number of bits used for the additions for variable node update), and ŌÉd (standard deviation of the noise used for designing the decoder).
The simulation results are presented by Figs. 5, 6, and 7. At least 100 frame errors are collected for each simulated signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). For easy reference, each transmitting symbol is mapped from m consecutive bits of a codeword. However, if proper bit interleaving is applied, the BER performance of each decoder can be improved. These results justify that our proposed method is very effective for applying the MIM-QBP decoder to decode LDPC codes with high-order modulation.
We have one more observation from the simulation results: the underlying modulation scheme seems not to affect the relative performance of the MIM-QBP decoder and the BP decoder, i.e., these two decoders almost have the same gap (in terms of SNR) at the same error rate when only the modulation scheme varies. For example, when decoding the (6, 32) LDPC code [18] with a maximum of 10 iterations, the 4-bit MIM-QBP decoder always outperforms the floatingpoint BP decoder by around 0.1 dB (resp. 0.15 dB) at the BER of 10‚ąí5 (resp. 10‚ąí6) for the modulation of BPSK (see [4, Fig. 13(a)]), 4-ary PAM (see Fig. 6), and 32-ary QAM (see Fig. 7). This phenomenon seems reasonable, since changing the modulation scheme should have a similar effect on the MIM-QBP decoder and the BP decoder. Moreover, the occurrence of this phenomenon also implies that this work deals with the high-order modulation as successfully as that of [4] dealing with the BPSK modulation.
