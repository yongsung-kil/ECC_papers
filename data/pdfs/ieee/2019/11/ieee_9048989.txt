First-Then-Second Extrema Selection

Ali Chamas Al Ghouwayel School of Engineering
Lebanese International University (LIU)

Hassan Harb and Emmanuel Boutillon Lab-STICC, CNRS UMR 6285
University of South Brittany (UBS)

Abstract—High speed and low cost sorting architectures are highly desired in many signal processing applications. Recently, the specific sorter that outputs only the first two minimum/maximum values received specific attention due to its major role in iterative channel decoders. In this paper, a high speed and low cost architecture for the selection of the first two smallest values is proposed. A detailed analysis of the elementary sorting units, computational complexity and structural modularity are addressed. Compared to existing architectures, the proposed architecture offers the highest throughput and, when synthesized on 28-nm TSMC technologies, achieves up to 50 % area reduction and up to 30 % critical path reduction. Consequently, the area efficiency, defined as the number of sorted symbols per second per µm2, is increased by a factor ranging from 1.17 to 2. The proposed architecture is suitable for binary and non-binary LDPC decoders as well as for any other application requiring a two-minimum sorting function.
I. INTRODUCTION
Sorting is an important operation in several applications including image compression [1], K-best MIMO detectors [2], turbo product codes [3], binary Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) codes [4] [5] [6] [7], the maximum-likelihood decoder of arithmetic codes [8], turbo decoders [9] and, more recently, Non-Binary LDPC (NB-LDPC) decoders [10]. There is an abundance of proposed sorting algorithms in the literature; however, not all of these algorithms are suitable for hardware implementation. In this section we discuss the most important hardware-implementable algorithms. In terms of speed performance, the best reported are the bitonic and the oddeven merge sorting networks [11], and their simplified version that outputs only the largest M out of N input values [12]. Despite its modular structure and its high throughput, the oddeven algorithm suffers from a high N 2 log2 N computational complexity. Alternative algorithms have been proposed to select M largest values in [13] and [14] where the former suffers from high latency (which depends on M ) and the latter from high computational complexity. The specific case when M = 2 has recently received attention due to its need in several popular applications, of special interest are binary LDPC [15] [16] and NB-LDPC [17] decoder architectures. The current state-of-the-art algorithms that select only the first two minimum/maximum values are proposed in [18], [19] and [20].
In this paper, stemming from [18] [19], a new low cost, high speed architecture called First-Then-Second Extrema Selection (FTSES) is proposed. The architecture is composed of two units: 1) the First Minimum Unit (FMU) that determines the first Extrema value and also preselects a subset of size k =

log2 N of the inputs that contains the second extrema value; 2) the Second Minimum Unit (SMU) that finds the second Extrema among this reduced set. This two-step process allows up to 49 % reduction of the total number of comparators, while keeping the number of multiplexers unchanged. It also offers a reduced critical path in the FMU. An efficient parallel structure of the SMU is proposed to reduce its critical path. The proposed architecture can be extended to extract any M > 2 minimum/maximum values among a total of N input values. In the sequel, without loss of generality, only the minimum extrema will be considered.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. Section II describes the problem statement and the proposed architecture. Section III discusses the experimental results and comparisons. Finally, section IV concludes the paper.
II. PROBLEM STATEMENT AND PROPOSED ALGORITHM
Given a set of N numbers, S = {x0, x1, . . . , xN−1}, the first minimum is defined as m1 = min{S}, and m2 = min{S\xp = m1}, where the \ operator indicates exclusion of only one element representing the first minimum value, with p ∈ {0, 1, . . . , N − 1}.

B. Architecture of N -SU for N = 8
In a modular fashion, an 8-SU architecture is obtained by implementing two 4-SUs in parallel connected to a third stage composed of one comparator-swap, CS03, and two MUX2-1s as shown in Fig. 2. The CS03 receives the two first minimums, m01→3 and m41→7, and sorts them; hence, their minimum value will constitute the first minimum among the 8 input values entering the 8-SU. The maximum value, max(m01→3, m41→7), will be inserted into the set of second minimum candidates, Φ20→7, whose cardinality is equal to log2(8). The key idea in our proposed algorithm is to postpone the determination of the second minimum until the last stage of the sorter in order to reduce the number of comparators to be used in each stage. Thus, instead of determining the local second minimum m20→j−1 among j elements, a local set Φ02→j−1 is formed

progressively containing log2(j) candidate elements at the log2(j)th stage, hence delaying the selection of the second minimum until the last stage of the sorting unit. This reduces
the complexity of an ESU from (2 CSs, 3 Cs, 1 MUX2-1),
needed in the TS approach [18], down to (3 CSs, 1 MUX2-1).
However, more outputs are generated at each stage, since a set of second minimum candidates, Φ02→j−1, is propagated to the next stage instead of only one element representing the local second minimum. The set Φ02→7 at the 3rd stage is obtained according to the following equation:

C. Timing Analysis
The critical path T of the proposed sorting unit architecture can be easily computed as T = TF MU + TSMU , where TF MU and TSMU denote the propagation time of the FMU and SMU respectively. Each of the k = log2(N ) stages of FMU has a critical path equal to TC + TMUX2−1, where TC denotes the time needed to perform one comparison, thus TF MU = k(TC + TMUX2−1). The time TSMU depends on the selected architecture as shown in Fig. 4. For the SMUTS, TSMU = TSMU−T S = log2 k (TC + TMUX2−1). For the parallel structure, TSMU = TSMU−P S = TC + log2 k TAND + TAND + log2 k TOR, where TAND and TOR denote the propagation time of the logic AND and OR gates respectively. By approximating TMUX2−1 ≈ TAND + TOR, and TMUXk−1 ≈ log2 k TMUX2−1, TSMU−P S can be approximated as TSMU−P S ≈ TC + log2 k TMUX2−1.

IV. HARDWARE IMPLEMENTATION
The different designs are synthesized using a TSMC 28-nm standard cell technology. For all our synthesis results, the parameterizable data width has been set to 6 bits. Table II shows the post-synthesis results where different values of N have been considered. As shown, the architecture [19] consumes less power and permits to get an area reduction ranging from 14 % (resp. 32 %), when N =16, up to 31 % (resp. 50 %), when N =512, as compared to the architecture proposed in [18] (resp. [20]). However, this architecture suffers from longer critical path imposed by the SMU. The proposed FTSES architecture based on the SMU-PS structure is less complex with the smallest critical path for all values of N . A slight increase in the power consumption is observed. If we consider the fully pipelined architectures, i.e., a pipeline register is inserted between each two adjacent stages, the critical path of the TS-based architecture [18] would be TC + 2TMUX2−1, while that of the FMU would be TC + TMUX2−1. In this case the SMU should be also pipelined and its critical path will be max{TC , log2 k TMUX2−1}.
In order to compare the efficiency of the different architectures, we evaluated two metrics: Area Efficiency (AE) and Power Efficiency (PE). The AE, indicating the number of symbols sorted per time unit (ns) per area unit (µm2), is defined as: AE = 1/(Nc × C[ns] × A[µm2]), where Nc represents the number of cycles per sorted symbol. Since there are two symbols sorted in each cycle, Nc is equal to 0.5. Similarly, the PE indicates the number of symbols sorted per seconds per µW , defined as: PE = 1/(Nc × C[ns] × P [µW ]).
To better illustrate the efficiency comparison, we have evaluated the AE Ratio (AER) (resp. PE Ratio (PER)) defined as the ratio of the AE (resp. PE) of the FTSES architecture to the AE (resp. PE) of each of the architectures [18], [19] and [20].
In Table II, the three rightmost columns show the numerical values of these efficiency ratios for different input lengths. In terms of AE, the proposed architecture outperforms all the

architectures by a factor ranging from 1.17 up to 2. It is also more power efficient for all values of N as compared to [18], and for N ≥ 64 when compared to [20], but slightly more power consuming as compared to [19].
V. CONCLUSION
The paper has presented the design and implementation of a low-hardware cost and low latency two-minimum value sorting architecture called FTSES. Theoretical complexity and performance analysis of the proposed sorting algorithm as compared to the most important similar existing algorithms have been addressed. Moreover, post synthesis results on TSMC 28 nm standard cell technology have been presented and discussed. For any size of data stream, the proposed architecture offers the highest frequency with an area efficiency ranging from 1.17 up to 2. Future work will consider the generalization of the proposed algorithm to extract the first M (M > 2) minimum values.
