Construction of Girth-8 QC-LDPC Codes Free of Small Trapping Sets

Sima Naseri and Amir H. Banihashemi , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract— In this letter, we propose a new technique to design quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes with girth 8 and variable node degrees 3 and 4. Small trapping sets are avoided in the designed codes by imposing simple constraints on the cycles of length 8 in the code’s Tanner graph. We also derive lower bounds on the block length of the codes that can satisfy such constraints. Simulation results are provided to demonstrate the superiority of the designed codes in comparison with similar codes in the literature.
Index Terms— Low-density parity check (LDPC) codes, quasi cyclic (QC) LDPC codes, error floor, trapping sets, girth.
I. INTRODUCTION
Q UASI-CYCLIC low-density parity check (QC-LDPC) codes are an important class of LDPC codes due to their efficient implementation and good performance. There has been much research on the construction of different categories of QC-LDPC codes, and the design of QC-LDPC codes with low error floors [1]–[8].
Most of the literature on the construction of protograph QC-LDPC codes is devoted to codes with small variable node degrees such as dv = 3 and dv = 4 [2]–[8]. One important reason is that smaller node degrees, for a given rate and block length, correspond to smaller number of edges in the code’s Tanner graph and thus result in a simpler implementation of the decoder and less area and power consumption. Moreover, it is well-established that to have a good waterfall and errorfloor performance, the length g of the shortest cycles of the code’s Tanner graph, called girth, should be at least six. Many designers consider g = 8 as a good choice for the girth, and design girth-8 codes which are free of certain classes of trapping sets, and thus have low error floors, see, e.g., [3], [4], [6], [8]. In this work also, we focus on girth-8 QCLDPC codes with dv = 3 and 4. The Tanner graph of a protograph QC-LDPC code can be viewed as a cyclic N lifting of a base graph (protograph) [7], [9]. In many cases, the base graph is fully-connected1 with no parallel edges. In such cases, if the base graph has n variable nodes and m check nodes, then the parity-check matrix of the protograph QC-LDPC code consists of m row blocks and n column blocks, where each row block (column block, resp.) has n (m, resp.) circulant permutation matrices (CPM) of size N × N .

We use the notation Ipij to denote a CPM that is obtained by circularly shifting the identity matrix IN×N by pij positions to the left for each row. The m × n matrix P = [pij] is called the exponent matrix. In the construction of protograph QC-LDPC codes, one has to determine the exponent matrix of the code whose elements are selected from the set of integer numbers {0, 1, . . . , N −1}. Although different techniques have been proposed to reduce the size of the search space [7], the number of possible choices, which is effectively N mn, can easily become too large to handle. This is particularly the case for high-rate codes, where n is large, or codes with large block length, for which N is a large number. In addition to the large search space, the relationship between the exponent matrix and the trapping set distribution of the code, which determines its error-floor performance, is often complicated. As a result, in many publications, the authors consider only a small number of trapping set structures [3]–[6], or choose a surrogate graphical structure which is easier to enumerate and examine [8]. In this work, we adopt the latter approach. Focusing on the shortest cycles in the Tanner graph of the code, we impose certain constraints on different categories of such cycles and demonstrate that by doing so, many small trapping sets of the code are eliminated. Since these constraints are easy to verify/impose, the proposed technique can be used to construct codes with relatively large values of n or N .
In the existing literature, the most relevant work to ours is [8]. In [8], protograph QC-LDPC codes with dv = 3 and g = 8 are constructed that are free of leafless elementary trapping sets (LETSs) [10], [11] with size a ≤ 8 and the number of odd-degree check nodes b ≤ 3. To eliminate these trapping sets, in [8], a certain category of 8-cycles is avoided. In this work, through a careful examination of the small trapping sets and their relationships with different types of 8-cycles, we impose a less restrictive constraint on 8-cycles compared to that of [8]. This, in general, allows us to find codes that are free of the same range of trapping sets but have a smaller block length compared to those of [8]. Moreover, while the results of [8] are limited to dv = 3, in this letter, we also devise a simple constraint on 8-cycles for codes with dv = 4. The imposition of this constraint ensures the removal of all the LETSs within the union of the following four regions: {a ≤ 11, b ≤ 2}, {a ≤ 10, b ≤ 4}, {a ≤ 9, b ≤ 6}, {4 < a ≤ 7, b ≤ 8}. For both cases of dv = 3 and dv = 4, we also derive a lower bound on the lifting degree and the block length of the codes that can satisfy the constraints imposed on 8-cycles. For dv = 3, the derived lower bound is smaller than that of [8]. In fact, we find codes with dv = 3 that are free of the same range of trapping sets as the codes designed in [8], but with block lengths that are smaller than the lower bound of [8].

the number of odd-degree check nodes b in their induced subgraph, and are said to belong to the (a, b) class. It is often the case that TSs with smaller a and b values are more harmful. It is also well-known that the most problematic TSs responsible for the error floor of variable-regular LDPC codes over the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel are leafless ETSs (LETSs) [10], [11]. (ETSs are those TSs whose induced subgraph contains only degree-1 and degree2 check nodes, and LETSs are ETSs in which every variable node is connected to at least two degree-2 check nodes.) In this work, similar to many existing literature, including [8], we focus on LETSs as the source of the error floor. Different characterizations of LETSs for variable-regular LDPC codes can be found in [10] and [11]. Most relevant to the discussions of this letter is the layered superset (LSS) characterization of LETSs [11], [12]. In this characterization, each and every LETS is characterized as an embedded sequence of LETSs that starts from a simple cycle or a small prime structure [12] and grows one variable node at a time until it reaches the targeted structure. The added variable node at each step makes connections to m degree-1 check nodes of the parent structure, where m ≥ 2, to generate the child structure. This expansion is dubbed dotm in [10].
Consider a Tanner graph G with the set of variable and check nodes V and W , respectively, and let S be a LETS in G. Suppose that the set of degree-1 check nodes in the subgraph of S is denoted by Γo (S). We refer to S as LSS non-expandable (LSS-nE), if every variable node in V \S has at most one neighbor in Γo (S). (We note that for the case of dv = 3, LETSs are the same as elementary absorbing sets (EABSs) [13], and LSS-nE LETSs are identical to fully EABSs [13].) It is clear that the four check nodes of any 8-cycle in a protograph QC-LDPC code with dv = 3 must belong to either two or three of the row blocks. (See Fig. 1(a), (c), (e), (f), (g) and (h).) Correspondingly, we distinguish the 8-cycles as being of Type A or Type B, respectively. V. CODE CONSTRUCTIONS AND NUMERICAL RESULTS

Since [8] is the closest result in the literature to this work,

we first construct codes for the same base graphs as those

considered in [8], i.e., fully-connected 3 × 5, 3 × 6 and 3 ×

10 base graphs. We note that based on the design approach

of [8], to remove LETSs within the range R : a ≤ 8, b ≤ 3,

the minimum lifting degree is 41, 61 and 181, for the three

base graphs, respectively. In [8], the authors found codes with

lifting degrees 41, 63 and 251, for the three cases, respectively.

In comparison, using the technique proposed in this letter, for

the same base graphs, to remove trapping sets in the same

range R, the lower bound of Theorem 2 is 21, 31 and 91,

respectively. Using a greedy column by column search of the

exponent matrix [7], we were able to find codes with lifting

degrees 27, 41 and 165, for the three base graphs, respectively.

(We note that the gap between these values and the lower

bounds of Theorem 2 is mainly due to the fact that the bound

of Theorem 2 is derived only as a necessary condition for the

i.e., Type-B 8-cycles need to be LSS-nE.) These codes are

significantly shorter than those of [8]. In particular, the block

VI. CONCLUSION
We proposed a new technique for the design of girth-8 QC-LDPC codes whose Tanner graphs are lifted from the fully connected 3×n and 4×n base graphs. The proposed technique eliminates certain types of 8-cycles from the Tanner graph of the code and consequently removes a number of dominant classes of trapping sets. Compared to the existing literature, superior codes are designed as a result.

Fig. 5. FER comparison of C1-C4 with some existing codes over the BI-AWGN channel.
To design this code (C4), for the first three rows of the exponent matrix, we used the exponent matrix of a girth-10 QC-LDPC code with N = 61, designed in [7] by lifting the fullyconnected 3×5 base graph. We then searched for the elements of the last row to satisfy the constraint that no 4-cycle or 6-cycle exists in the Tanner graph, and the constraint that there is no 8-cycle with repeated degree-2 check node indices.
The multiplicities of (a, b) LETSs of C4 in the range of a ≤ 11 and b ≤ 8 are shown in Table II. As is evident from Table II, all the LETSs in this range, except for the 8-cycles, are removed from the Tanner graph. For comparison, we have also provided in Table II, the multiplicities of LETSs of a similar QC-LDPC code designed in [15]. This code also has girth 8 and is a cyclic lifting of the fully-connected 4 × 5 base graph with N = 61. The comparison shows a clear advantage of the designed code over the code of [15] in terms of the LETS distribution.
To demonstrate the superior performance of the designed codes, in Fig. 5, we have provided the frame error rate (FER) of C1-C4, decoded by a 5-bit min-sum algorithm [16] with clipping threshold 7.5 and maximum iteration number 50 over the binary-input (BI) AWGN channel. For comparison with C1-C3, we have also given the FER of QC-PEG codes [17] with similar base graphs and lifting degrees in Fig. 5. The FER of the code of [15] is provided for comparison with C4. As can be seen in Fig. 5, the codes constructed here handily outperform the existing codes in the error floor region.
Finally, as an indication of the complexity of the proposed technique, we note that it took the search algorithm 2127, 7190, 24485, and 21173 seconds to find Codes C1-C4, respectively (given the corresponding lifting degrees). The search algorithm was implemented in MATLAB and ran on a desktop computer with 4 GHz CPU and 32 GB RAM. In general, the search complexity reduces with the increase in lifting degree.
