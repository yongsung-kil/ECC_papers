A Modified Shuffling Method to Split the Critical Path Delay in Layered Decoding of QC-LDPC Codes
Alireza Hasani∗, Lukasz Lopacinski†, Steffen Bu¨chner∗, Jo¨rg Nolte∗, and Rolf Kraemer∗† ∗Brandenburg University of Technology Cottbus-Senftenberg, Cottbus, Germany Email:{alireza.hasani, steffen.buechner, joerg.nolte}@b-tu.de
†IHP - Leibniz-Institut fu¨r innovative Mikroelektronik, Frankfurt (Oder), Germany Email: {lopacinski, kraemer}@ihp-microelectrinics.com

Abstract—Layered (or Turbo) decoding of Low-Density ParityCheck (LDPC) codes is considered as a decoding schedule that facilitates partially parallel architectures for performing iterative algorithms based on belief propagation. It has, on one hand, reduced implementation complexity and memory overhead compared to fully parallel architectures and, on the other hand, higher convergence speed compared to both serial and parallel architectures. In this paper, we introduce a general form of shuffling of the parity-check matrix of quasi-cyclic LDPC (QCLDPC) codes which can split the critical path delay in layered decoding and therefore improve throughput by allowing higher clock rates. We also reveal a valuable property of Latin squares QC-LDPC codes which makes them a good candidate for the proposed shuffling method. As a result of that property, no special caution of choosing offset values in the proposed generalized shuffling method is required.
I. INTRODUCTION
Reliability has always been a major concern in telecommunication and even more important in wireless communication systems. In this regard, Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes have been so far used in various standardized communication systems as a forward error correction technique. They are also well-known in the corresponding literature as capacityachieving codes, capable of approaching the Shannon capacity of the channel [1].
Promising functionality of LDPC codes is obtained when decoded by iterative algorithms based on Belief Propagation (BP) [2]. This, however, introduces some challenges when high-throughput communications is in scope, as these decoding algorithms are complex and costly. There have been many workarounds to address this problem, among them layered decoding (LD) schedules [3]–[21] are of high popularity. LD is, in fact, a schedule, specifying how reliability messages are exchanged between variable nodes (VNs) and check nodes (CNs) in the Tanner graph representation [22] of the code.
This paper builds upon the novel LD schedule of [20] and the idea of parallel LD (PLD) architecture of [11]. In particular, we show that both methods represent the same idea, though from different perspectives. The fast-convergence decoding method of [20] is based on a novel shuffling scheme, which shuffles the rows of the parity-check matrix (PCM) of a QC-LDPC

code prior to decoding and gives it a new layered format. By performing LD algorithm on the shuffled PCM, the cyclic property inherent in QC-LDPC codes is exploited in order to simplify decoding and speed up convergence rate. On the other side, the PLD architecture proposed by [11] expresses a seemingly distinct method, along with a novel scheme to split critical path delay in the decoding procedure. The proposed scheme permits to increase the clock frequency and thus throughput. We will first reveal the connection between these two methods and later introduce a general shuffling method that also includes the idea of critical path splitting. In addition, we uncover an appealing property of Latin squares QC-LDPC codes [23], i.e. they will have columns of weight at most one in each layer, even after the primary form of shuffling proposed by [20]. In other words, in the case of Latin squares QC-LDPC codes, no offsetting necessitated by the proposed generalized shuffling method is needed.
The organization of the paper is as follows. We first bring preliminaries in sec. II, providing the fundamentals of QCLDPC codes and their Latin squares subtype, shuffling method of [20] and PLDA with critical path splitting of [11]. Then, in sec. III, the proposed general form of shuffling and the prevalent good property of Latin squares QC-LDPC codes are stated. This section also expresses how LD algorithm is performed on a shuffled PCM in order to enlighten the implementation benefits acquired by the proposed shuffling method. Sec. IV is devoted to implementation concerns and simulation results. Final conclusions are made in sec. V.
II. PRELIMINARIES
Before describing the main idea of the paper, a short introduction on QC-LDPC codes and LD schedule is necessary.
A. QC-LDPC Codes and Their Latin squares Subtype
A linear block code C is called an (γ, ρ)-regular LDPC code if it is the null space of a PCM H with following characteristics [24]: I- each row consists of ρ 1s; II- each column consists of γ 1s; III- γ and ρ are small compared with n (the length of the code) and J (the number of the rows in H); IV- the number of 1s in common between any two columns (or rows) is no

greater than one. The last condition is usually referred to as the row-column (RC) constraint in the literature [25]. A PCM in which rows or columns have different weights but still holds the conditions III and IV produces an irregular LDPC code.
The PCM of an LDPC code is usually visualized by a graphical representation known as the Tanner graph. The Tanner graph of a PCM consists of two sets of nodes. One set represents CNs, i.e. the parity-check sums (or equations) which are, in fact, the rows of H, and the other set stands for VNs that are equivalent to columns of H. CNs and VNs are connected to each other by lines (or edges) according to the location of one entries in the corresponding PCM. Specifically, the lth VN 0 ≤ l < n is connected to the jth CN 1 ≤ j ≤ J if and only if the corresponding entry in the PCM Hl,j = 1.
A circulant is a matrix in which each row is a cyclically rightward-shifted copy of its above row. If a circulant is a permutation as well, i.e. every rows and columns are singleweight, then the circulant is called a circulant permutation matrix (CPM). In the special case, if an RC-constrained PCM is comprised of c × t arrays of b × b CPMs, the resultant LDPC code will be QC too. Among the various methods for constructing QC-LDPC codes, our focus is on Latin squares QC-LDPC codes, that were firstly introduced in [23]. Assuming α and η respectively as the primitive element and an arbitrary nonzero element of GF (q) = {α0 = 1, α1, . . . , αq−2, α−∞ = 0} and also A = {0, 1, . . . , q − 2, −∞} as the index set, we form a q × q base matrix W over GF (q) as

W = [αiη − αj ]i,j∈A.

(1)

The q elements in any row or column of W are distinct and, therefore, each row or column of W is a complete GF (q). Such a matrix is called a Latin squares matrix. Each of the members of the GF (q) can be corresponded to a CPM. The corresponding CPM of a nonzero element of GF (q) is an (q − 1) × (q − 1) identity matrix in which all rows have been circularly shifted rightward equal to the power index of that element. The corresponding CPM of the zero element is simply the (q − 1) × (q − 1) zero matrix. If W is dispersed, i.e. each element is replaced by its corresponding CPM, the resultant matrix will satisfy RC constraint [23] and can serve as the PCM of a QC-LDPC code. We regard HLS(m, η, γ) as the Latin squares PCM containing the upper γ row-blocks of the dispersed W over GF (2m).

B. Fast-Convergence and Memory-Efficient LD
The main decoding techniques that can reveal the capacityachieving feature of LDPC codes are soft-decision and iterative and they are based on BP algorithm [2]. The basic idea of BP algorithm is to pass reliability messages between VNs and CNs successively within iterations to find a codeword that has the maximum probability according to the received soft-decision sequence. Many researchers have been trying over the years to modify this algorithm in different aspects. Dozens of existing works address for instance the issue of computation of reliability messages [2], [26]–[36]. Several

other papers investigate scheduling of the algorithm [37]–[39] which determines in what order the reliability messages should be exchanged between the nodes in the Tanner graph.
LD is a variant of decoding schedule which relies on the layered structure of the PCMs. It can also be viewed as a way to realize a partially-parallel architecture for execution of the BP algorithm. This is in contrast to flood schedule [40] which facilitates fully-parallel architecture for decoding of LDPC codes. In LD schedule, each iteration is split into several subiterations, running over successive layers of the PCM. During each sub-iteration, reliability messages are exchanged between CNs of that layer and their neighbor VNs, and at the end, the updated reliability messages are handed to the next layer. Accordingly, only a subset of CNs and VNs participate in each sub-iteration, and layers are processed successively from top to down the PCM.
The method proposed by [20] has targeted modifying Hqc, the PCM of a QC-LDPC code, in order to make the best use of the cyclic property existing in that. It is then shown that LD on the modified PCM results in a reduction in the number of iterations (or equivalently an increase in the speed of convergence) by 40% without BER loss [20]. In order to achieve this target, the proposed method performs a shuffling on the rows of Hqc which gives it a new layered structure. Specifically, for i = 1, . . . , b, let H(i) be a c × n matrix containing the rows i, i+b, i+2b, . . . , i+(c−1)b of Hqc. In this way, the J = c.b rows of Hqc are partitioned between matrices H(i), i = 1, . . . , b. The shuffled PCM, Hq(csh), is then formed by having matrices H(i), i = 1, . . . , b as its row-blocks. An example of such a shuffling is depicted in Fig. 1-(b), which has been performed on the HLS(2, 1, 4) of Fig. 1-(a). Afterwards, the iterative decoding with LD schedule is performed over Hq(csh) instead of Hqc. In sec. IV, it is clarified how this shuffling can be useful and ease the decoding implementation.
The problem with this shuffling is that it may cause columns with weight bigger than one to appear in the shuffled PCM. This, in turn, gives rise to the complexity of the decoding algorithm and therefore longer data paths in the VLSI implementation. This will be better understood when LD on a shuffled PCM is formulated mathematically in section III.
C. PLD Schedule
PLD schedule of [11] rather proposes to process all layers concurrently, but to process the rows sequentially within each layer. As an example, consider Hqc in Fig. 1-(a). PLD schedule in this example suggests dividing each iteration into four subiterations, equal to the depth of the layers. In the first subiteration, the first rows of all the layers participate in the decoding algorithm and update reliability messages of their own neighbor VNs. In the second sub-iteration, the second rows of all the layers are activated. This continues until the forth subiteration in which the last rows of the layers are used in the decoding process. It is furthermore suggested that an offset value may be opted for each layer, so that the CN processing can start from different rows in each layer. III. MODIFIED DECODING METHOD
When studying the fast-convergence method of [20] and PLD schedule of [11] together, it becomes clear that they both sketch the same idea. In other words, according to the previous discussions, the idea of PLD is in fact equivalent to performing BP decoding algorithm on the PCM which has been shuffled according to the aforementioned method. Moreover, the PLD schedule with critical path splitting can be thought of as a generalized shuffling which is explained in this section.
A. Generalized Shuffling
The shuffling method, described before, can be generalized to include the idea of critical path splitting of [11]. Suppose that a set of integer offset values O1, . . . , Oc related to layers of Hqc is selected, such that 0 ≤ Oj < b, j = 1, . . . , c. For i = 1, . . . , b, let Hg(i) be the matrix made up of i+O1, i+b+O2, i+ 2b + O3, . . . , i + (c − 1)b + Oc rows of Hqc. Consequently, the new shuffled matrix Hq(cg sh) having matrices Hg(i) as its layers will be the generalized shuffled matrix. The offset values are carefully selected according to the Hqc which is to be shuffled, such that in the generated shuffled PCM no column in a layer has the weight of greater than one. This will ensure the least possible path delay in the VLSI implementation of the LD. For example, consider the IEEE 802.16e QC-LDPC code whose base matrix has been shown in table I. The PCM for this code is derived by substituting each non-negative integer with an identity matrix of size 96 cyclically shifted rightward equal to that integer [11]. The appropriate offset values for such a PCM are [0, 8, 0, 12, 84, 0, 88, 8, 0, 16, 0, 80] as determined in [11]. Otherwise, when choosing all the offset values as 0 which is equivalent to performing the primary form of shuffling results in some columns of weight bigger than one in some layers and therefore longer path delay.

B. Shuffling of Latin Squares QC-LDPC Codes
For the Latin squares QC-LDPC codes, the appropriate offset values are simply 0s. This property stems from the fact that the q elements in any column of W in (1) are distinct elements of the GF (q). As a result, when shuffled, the columns in a layer will not have weight greater than one. Consequently, no particular caution is necessary on choosing offset values for Latin squares QC-LDPC codes when they are to be accordingly shuffled.
Once the PCM is shuffled, the LD algorithm can be performed on that. For that, we assume, without loss of generality, the min-sum decoding scheme of [28] as the reference for CN processing. However, any of the many other proposed schemes like the ones in [27], [30], [41]–[45] can be used. IV. NOTES ON IMPLEMENTATION ASPECTS AND
PERFORMANCE EVALUATION
In order to understand how the aforementioned shuffling can be useful, one should note that in Hq(cg sh) each layer can be produced by cyclic shifting of its upper layer if the shifting operation is performed on the t individual sections of a layer independently. For instance, in Fig. 1-(b), H(2) can be derived from H(1) by just rightward cyclic shifting of the t = 4 sections of each row of H(1). Repeating this shifting one more time yields H(3). This property can be
exploited to ease implementation of the algorithm. Let’s remain
focused on Fig. 1-(b) and show how LD can be implemented
in hardware for this example. The memory banks needed to run LD algorithm on Hq(csh) is illustrated in Fig. 2. Three sets of memory banks, i.e. Z-memories, L-memories and Ymemories are required to store Zl(i), Lj(i,l) and Yl(i) values respectively. The size of the Z- and Y-memories is generally 1/b of the size of L-memories, as it is not needed to keep VTC
messages and APP values of all the layers. In fact, it suffices

to keep VTC messages and APP values of just the current layer in each sub-iteration. The VTC messages shown in Zmemories in Fig. 2, are for the first layer related to H(1). The connection between the cells of L-memories and Z-memories, although not shown here, are determined according to (4) by the locations of ones in H(1). At the beginning of each subiteration, after computing new values of Zl(i) and placing them in Z-memories, the values are circularly shifted to the left. In this way, the existing connection between Z-memories and Lmemories remain valid and, in fact, represent the dedicated connections for the next layer. Therefore, the next sub-iteration can be initiated instantly, without the need to redefine the connections between Z-memories and L-memories. In other words, the connections between Z-memories and L-memories are initially specified and set according to H(1) and then remain intact thereafter.
The performance of the BP algorithm with LD schedule remains unchanged when the primary PCM or its shuffled version is used. In order to show that, Fig. 3 shows a comparison between LD schedule on unshuffled and shuffled PCMs in terms of BER performance. The simulation has been performed with four QC-LDPC codes, namely HLS(4, 1, 8), HLS(4, 1, 16), HLS(5, 1, 4) and HLS(5, 1, 8) over an AWGN channel model. It confirms that LD with shuffled PCM can perform as good as an unshuffled PCM and no BER loss is observed. As a result, the LD on shuffled PCM appears as powerful as the LD on the primary PCM, while it is able to be implemented with lower complexity. This is resulted by the fact that it can exploit the cyclic property inherent in QC-LDPC codes to simplify implementation.
V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we establish a connection between the fastconvergence LD algorithm on shuffled PCMs, which was proposed by [20], and the idea of PLD architecture of [11]. We first show the analogy between the two ideas and then introduce the general form of shuffling which includes the idea of critical path splitting and, as a result, allows to increase clock frequency and have higher throughput. In addition, we show an appealing property of Latin squares QC-LDPC codes which makes them a good candidate for shuffling. Due to that property, a Latin squares QC-LDPC code will have columns of
