Abstract—In this paper we propose an efficient and general method to obtain structured generator matrices for QC-LDPC codes. Moreover, we devote particular attention to the family of fully-connected monomial codes and perform a statistical analysis of their low-weight unavoidable codewords, whose weight gives an upper bound on their minimum distance.
Index Terms—Generator matrix, minimum distance, QCLDPC codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes were introduced by Gallager in [1]. After the advent of turbo codes [2], LDPC codes were rediscovered [3] and, since then, they have received a great deal of attention, thanks to their capacity-approaching performance [4]. A family of codes which has been widely investigated is that of quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes. Owing to their structured form, they allow efficient encoding and decoding strategies [5]–[7]. For this reason, QC-LDPC codes find application in modern communication standards, such as IEEE 802.11n, IEEE 802.16e and 5G, and many other scenarios such as, for example, codebased-cryptography, deep space communications and so on.
The classical representation of QC-LDPC codes is based on a structured sparse parity-check matrix H, which is an r0 ×n0 array of p × p circulant matrices. The generator matrix G is usually derived from H through reduction techniques such as Gauss-Jordan elimination, which leads to non-structured, dense generator matrices. However, QC-LDPC codes also allow, under particular constraints on the code parity-check matrix, a sparse [8] and structured [9], [10] generator matrix. The main limit of the approaches proposed in the aforementioned papers is that they do not have general validity, but allow the construction of such a structured G only for some specific families of QC-LDPC codes, such as, for example, array codes [11], QC-LDPC codes based on circulant permutation matrices [12] or difference-based QC-LDPC codes. For some of these code ensembles, the generator matrix proposed in previous literature has not full rank and actually describes a subcode

of the code represented by the given parity-check matrix. We study the reasons beyond this phenomenon and propose some countermeasures.
The main contribution of this work is a general technique to derive the generator matrix of a QC-LDPC code, given its parity-check matrix, based on the computation of the permanent of some submatrices of H. Our method is inspired by the analysis of minimum distance and unavoidable codewords proposed in [12] for QC-LDPC codes based on circulant permutation matrices and generalized in [13] for multi-edge QC-LDPC codes. The proposed method can be applied to any family of QC-LDPC codes, including those whose paritycheck matrix contains non-unitary weight circulant matrices and/or all-zero matrices. Given a parity-check matrix which is an r0 × n0 array of p × p circulant matrices, our analysis, which specializes and contains as special cases those in [9], [10], results in a generator matrix containing k0 = n0 − r0 row-arrays of circulant matrices and some additional rows that compensate the rank deficiencies of the parity-check matrix and of the row-arrays in the generator matrix. A generator matrix in such form yields significant savings in terms of encoding complexity. Furthermore, it follows from the definition of generator matrix that the minimum Hamming weight of the rows of G gives an upper bound on dmin. Motivated by this, we perform a statistical analysis of the weight of unavoidable codewords of QC-LDPC codes.
The paper is organized as follows. In Section II we introduce QC-LDPC codes. In Section III we propose a method to derive a structured generator matrix for QC-LDPC codes. In Section IV we make some considerations on the minimum distance of QC-LDPC codes, based on the previously derived representation. Finally, in Section V we draw some conclusions.

As the authors recognize in [10] for the code ensembles they consider, the row-blocks Gi of a generator matrix as in Fig. 1 may not have full rank. This observation is correct also in our more comprehensive case. If so, the generator matrix G defines a subcode of the code represented by H.
In the following, we derive the conditions that must be fulfilled in order to have G with full rank, which therefore actually describes the code represented by the given H. Let us recall some results on polynomials in F2[x]/(xp + 1) and, thus, on p × p binary circulant matrices.

Theorem 1 Let p > 2 be a prime such that p − 1 is the order of 2 in Fp and let q(x) ≡ up(x) be a non-zero polynomial in F2[x]/(xp + 1). Then, if W(q(x)) is odd, the circulant matrix Q corresponding to q(x) has full rank p, otherwise its rank is p − 1.
Proof: If W(q(x)) is odd, it follows from Lemma 2 that q(x) does not contain 1 + x in its factorization. Moreover, we have assumed that q(x) ≡ up(x) ∈ F2[x]/(xp + 1). As, according to Lemma 3, xp + 1 has x + 1 and up(x) as irreducible factors, then q(x) and xp + 1 have no common factors and, according to Lemma 4, Q has full rank p.
If W(q(x)) is even, then q(x) and xp + 1 have at least a common factor, (x + 1). Thus, the rank of Q is smaller than or equal to p − 1. In order to prove that the rank of Q cannot be smaller than p − 1, we need to prove that up(x) is not a factor of q(x). This is straightforward, as if both x + 1 and up(x) appear in the decomposition of q(x), then q(x) ≡ 0 mod xp + 1. So, gcd(q(x), xp + 1) = x + 1 and, according to Lemma 4, the rank of Q is exactly p − 1.
The following corollary specializes Theorem 1 to an array of p × p circulant matrices or, equivalently, to an array of polynomials in F2[x]/(xp + 1).
Corollary 1 Let p > 2 be a prime such that p − 1 is the order of 2 in Fp and
Gi(x) = [G(i0)(x) Gi(1)(x) . . . G(ir0)(x)],

Proof: The statement is a direct consequence of Theorem 1: if there exists at least a value of j such that W(Gi(j)(x)) is odd, then the corresponding circulant matrix has rank p. It follows that the rank of the whole p × r0p matrix is p.
A straightforward consequence of Corollary 1 is that if G(ij)(x) ≡ up(x), ∀i, j and, for any i, there exists at least a value of j such that W(G(ij)(x)) is odd, then the generator matrix as in Fig. 1 has full rank.
Unfortunately, the above conditions are not verified for some code ensembles such us, for example, the widely investigated fully-connected monomial codes. For this reason, we devote the following section to the analysis of this code ensemble.
