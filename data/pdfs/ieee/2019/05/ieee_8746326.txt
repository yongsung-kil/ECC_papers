Robust Low Density Parity Check Decoding Over Markov Gaussian Channels

1st Der-Feng Tseng Dept. of Electrical Engineering National Taiwan University of Science and Technology
Taipei, Taiwan dtseng@mail.ntust.edu.tw

2nd Shi-Shun Lin Dept. of Electrical Engineering National Taiwan University of Science and Technology
Taipei, Taiwan danturquoise@gmail.com

Abstract—Communications systems suffer from impulsive interference, generally modeled as a memory channel to account for the inherent bursts of impulse even though conventional receivers take into account the additive white Gaussian noise only. In this paper, finite-field based constructions of Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) coding over the Markov Gaussian channel are investigated. In contrast with existing approaches assuming the impulse noise statistics, this paper proposes a modified maximuma-priori method, which forfeits using the statistical knowledge of impulse, to estimate whether or not each transmitted symbol is corrupted by impulse noise. Subsequently, the sum-product algorithm LDPC decoder invokes a clipper to diminish impulsive interference when the channel state is identified to be impulsecorrupted. The bit error rate (BER) performance versus the signal-to-noise ratio for the proposed decoder is conducted using computer simulation. The experimental results in terms of the BER performance justified the robustness of our proposed receiver and also revealed its competitiveness with the optimal benchmark decoder in the hostile environment. Moreover, using the proposed receiver, our calibrated quasi-cyclic LDPC code is shown to slightly outperform its 5G New Radio counterpart under certain circumstances.
Index Terms—Memory impulse noise, Markov Gaussian channel, clipping
I. INTRODUCTION
Impulse noise is a common factor to degrade the quality of service of data transmission in generic communications systems. For instance, simultaneous signal emission from end users in overlapped resources across heterogeneous wireless networks may contribute to impulse noise to each other. It is inevitable to overcome this barrier to support ultra reliable low latency communication services and vehicular ad hoc networks in the next-generation communications system [1]. The inherence of impulse is bursty and the Markov Gaussian channel [2], akin to the Gilbert-Elliott channel, is commonly used to model the memory impulse noise.
It has been reported in [3] that the memory impulse noise significantly degrades turbo decoding performance if the conventional decoder metric, which considers thermal noise as the sole impeding factor, is used unwittingly. Regular low density parity check (LDPC) codes in the Gilbert-Elliott channel were designed and analyzed in [4] by means of density evolution

and a more generalized development of irregular LDPC ones was reported in [5]. An efficient LDPC decoding algorithm can be devised in an analogous manner to overcome memory noise characterized by a Markov Gaussian channel; a study regarding LDPC coded systems over the finite-state Markov channel was conducted in [6], where a hybrid approach combining the forward-backward algorithm and the LDPC decoding was implemented to address memory impulse noise; nevertheless, their work was accomplished by instituting a hard decision decoding, leaving their bit error rate (BER) performance results some room for improvements.
It is commonly known that quasi-cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes boast to be capable of being efficiently encoded by using simple shift-registers with linear complexity. Furthermore, finite-field LDPC codes over the binary-input additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) are known to enjoy low error floors. This paper aims to devise a robust receiver−which lacks statistical knowledge of memory impulse noise−for the QCLDPC code, constructed based on finite fields [7], against bursty impulse noise by harnessing the masking calibration to effectively blunt the detrimental impact from impulse noise as well as exploiting message passing between impulse noise state estimation and channel decoding. Through extensive simulations, this work examines the degree to which the BER performance loss to an excessively refined receiver−which assumes the impulse noise−is induced by the proposed receiver at the expense of reduced complexity (thanks to foregoing the estimation of impulse noise model parameters).
This paper is organized as follows. The problem statement and the system framework are reviewed in Section II, and the details of the proposed receiver scheme are manifested in Section III. The simulation results are presented in Section IV, followed by a conclusion drawn in Section V.
II. SYSTEM MODEL
A brief description of a memory impulse noise model that adopts the Markov Gaussian channel is introduced, followed by a review of finite-field based construction of QC-LDPC codes. After that, the system framework is summarized.

Notably, estimating the statistics of impulse noise is computationally intensive, if not infeasible. To overcome that obstacle to realizing coding gain in the context of memory impulse noise, the proposed receiver, as illustrated in Fig. 1, encompasses two cascaded entities: the channel state sequence estimator, run by the forward-backward (F-B) algorithm, and the revised SPA decoder. In particular, impulse noise statistics are not estimated but rather a clipping operation is implemented to blunt the impulse noise [8], [9]. Noteworthily, the proposed receiver always estimates each noise state sj (1 ≤ j ≤ nc) to be either G or B (the received signal yj (5) either is subjected to be clipped when sj is estimated as B, or remains intact when sj is estimated to be G), regardless of the actual number of noise states that signaling is faced with−a more generalized impulse noise model such as that work in [10] can be straightforwardly applied to our receiver structure.

IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
The code-construction field is GF (512) for the following finite field based QC-LDPC codes and Hqc,disp(8, 16) is employed as a base array for masking. The proposed receiver mechanism is assessed using computer simulations of bit error rate (BER) versus signal-to-noise (SNR) (i.e., Eb/N0 in decibels, where Eb is the energy of an information bit). Quadrature phase-shift keying with gray mapping is employed. The maximal number of decoding iterations is set to be 100. A Markov-Gaussian channel with PB = 0.1 and PBG = 0.025 and the power ratio of the noise between the bad and good channel states R = 100 is considered in the following experiments, if not specified otherwise. It is aimed to demonstrate the efficacy of the calibrated masking (derived from adhering to three key rules outlined in Sec. II-B) in Fig. 2. A (3, 6)-regular masking matrix is yielded to generate a (3, 6)-regular (8 176, 4 088) QC-LDPC code and tested against a randomly constructed masking matrix (denoted by Z1) and their ensuing BER performance results are depicted in Fig. 2. Recall that the optimal receiver uses the full statistical knowledge of impulse noise and, when the masking matrix is deliberately calibrated, poses a slight performance gain over the proposed receiver which painstakingly estimate the noise states prior to activating the SPA decoder (a rough 0.1 dB SNR gap is observed at 10−5 BER for those two solid lines). However, the BER performance result worsens when, despite lacking the perfect knowledge of memory length of impulse noise, the design of a masking matrix neglects to preempt the residual burst of impulse noise: the error floor of the optimal receiver (see the dash-dot line marked by “◦”) emerges as early as at a SNR of 4.3 dB. Moreover, the BER performance gap between the robust receiver and its optimal counterpart grows wider (see those two dash-dot lines) with this negligent masking, given an identical rate of the finitefield QC-LDPC codes.
Recall that the MAP algorithm is implemented as an outer loop for updating the noise state sequence estimation once the most recent information (regarding the a-posterior probabilities of code bits) is fed as its input from the SPA decoder−after the maximal number of iterations within the inner loop is reached. In this view, the computational complexity weighs unbearably on the proposed receiver even for a moderate size of codeword length. A simplified version of our proposed scheme considers executing the outer loop only one time and its BER performance result is shown in Fig. 3 (see the dash line marked by “◃”). As seen there, the performance loss to the receiver carrying out outer-loop iterations (corresponding to the solid line marked by “∗”) is nearly unnoticeable. From this perspective, the F-B algorithm in couple with the (iterative) soft thresholding algorithm reinforces noise state sequence estimation.
An investigation on how the variation of the average bursty length of impulse impacts on BER system performance is studied, given a universal value of PB(dG) −which remains at 0.035−set at the proposed receiver over memory impulse noise channel. In the next experiment, the state transition probability PBG is changed to 0.5 while the remaining system parameter values at our proposed receivers are unchanged. As observed in Fig. 4, the BER performance loss to the optimal receiver (compared with a rough 0.1 dB in Fig. 3) is widened−a slightly more than 0.5 dB gap is induced when the BER is at the level of 10−5, due in part to the enhanced difference between the values of PB(dG) and PBG. However, the BER curves appear to be steeper when compared with those lines in the previous plots−when the SNR increases to 3 dB

and beyond, all of the BER performance results fall below 10−5, strengthening the link between system performance of the LDPC decoding schemes and the degree to which the impulse noise stagnates, even though the probability of impulse occurrence remains fixed in this context.
Finally, the (8 176, 4 088) QC-LDPC is tested against a rate1/2 (but with a slightly different codeword length) irregular (8 448, 4 224) QC-LDPC code [12], [13] and the BER performance results are exhibited in Fig. 5. In the AWGN channel, this 5G NR QC-LDPC code outperforms the finite-field regular QC-LDPC by 0.25 dB when the BER is at the level of 10−5. Nevertheless, in the context of the memory impulse noise, the space between those two solid lines (marked by “◃” and “◦”) corresponding to their optimal receivers, individually shrinks as the SNR grows progressively. Remarkably, the BER performance result induced by the proposed robust receivers, which forgoes assuming the impulse statistics, corroborates that the finite-field QC-LDPC code with painstaking calibration on masking matrix is more robust against memory impulse noise (which contributes to a lower error floor), in spite that−when compared with its own optimal counterpart−the prevalence of a BER performance gap persists for both QC-LDPC codes.

V. CONCLUSION
Quasi-cyclic low density parity check (QC-LDPC) codes constructed from finite field are identified to induce low error floor over additive white Gaussian noise channel. This paper explores robust schemes to counteract impulse noise, pervasive in modern communications systems and inherently bursty with a distinctive feature of strong power, and whose statistics are generally hard to be modeled accurately, without assuming its statistics.
