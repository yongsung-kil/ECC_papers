Abstract—Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are widely used in communication systems. When user messages are encoded by binary LDPC codes and transmitted with higher-order modulations, bits composing the same modulated symbol are generally correlated. Traditional soft-decision decoding algorithms for binary LDPC codes like sum-product algorithm (SPA) and min-sum algorithm (MSA) update messages only based on loglikelihood ratio (LLR) of bits, and correlations introduced by modulation are not utilized. In this paper, symbol-based SPA and MSA (S-SPA and S-MSA) which incorporate symbol-level information in the decoding procedures are proposed to provide an alternative low-cost way to better utilize available information. By narrowing the search space of symbols based on bit-level information, the complexities of the proposed algorithms are independent of modulation order. S-SPA and S-MSA achieve better error-correction performances than SPA and MSA with little additional complexities and S-MSA is friendly to hardware design of LDPC decoder.
I. INTRODUCTION
Since the rediscovery by Mackay and Neal in the late 1990s [1], LDPC codes [2] have been widely used in communication and data storage systems because of their capacityapproaching performances. Due to the complex encoding and decoding procedures of random LDPC codes, structured codes like quasi-cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes are more commonly used in practice.
When messages are transmitted with higher-order modulations such as 64-QAM and 256-QAM, or burst errors exist, non-binary LDPC (NB-LDPC) codes [3] achieve better errorcorrection performances than the binary counterparts. NBLDPC codes can take advantage of the information of each symbol, not just the bit-level information derived from symbollevel information, while lead to more complex decoding procedures.
In this paper, S-SPA and S-MSA are proposed for decoding binary LDPC codes with higher-order modulations to make full use of symbol-level information that was discarded before. Furthermore, we analyze and reduce the complexity of the algorithm, making its complexity independent of modulation order. Finally, considering trade-off between the complexity and performance, we proposed simplified algorithms and evaluate their performance. Numerical results show that both of them achieve better error-correction performances than the corresponding SPA and MSA, respectively.

The rest of the paper is organised as follows. In Section II, we introduce binary and non-binary LDPC codes, their decoding algorithm SPA and MSA, and some basic definitions. In Section III, we describe in details about the proposed symbol-based binary LDPC decoding algorithm S-SPA and S-MSA and provide complexity analysis and optimization. In Section IV, we demonstrate the error-correction performance of the proposed S-SPA and S-MSA. We conclude the paper in Section V.

II. LDPC CODES AND DECODING ALGORITHMS
LDPC codes are defined over Galois field GF(q). q equals 2 for binary codes, and is larger than 2 for non-binary codes. Parity-check matrix (PCM) H of an LDPC code characterizes its property. Each row of H refers to one code constraint that the codeword satisfies. In Tanner graph, columns and rows of H are represented by variable nodes (VNs) and check nodes (CNs), respectively.

When binary LDPC codes are combined with higher-order modulations, a common practice is to apply SPA or MSA based on bit-level LLRs generated by a soft-output demodulator.

In S-SPA, T is chosen as a small constant number 4. Even though the log-sum approximation is applied, the overall complexity of computing V2C messages is still O(2nld). This is because when we look for the T minimum values, we still has to traverse all the constellation points. The bottleneck is selecting the 8 symbols in Ω0 and Ω1 using Nj(ivc)(·) as criterion. The complexity reduction of V2C messages is achieved by narrowing the search space of symbols. One reasonable choice of this value is the sum of bit-level V2C values or bit-level APP messages. In this paper, we consider using the APP valuse to reduce the search space because it determines the posteriori probability of each bit. The smaller the absolute value of the APP, the more unreliable the bit is, i.e. the more it needs to be corrected.
This approach will picks out log2 T minimum values in n APPs, and reduces the exponential complexity to constant amortized complexity, that is, the complexity of selecting symbols in Ω0 and Ω1 will reduce to O(n) and the overall complexity of computing V2C messages will become O(nld).
To further reduce the O(nld) complexity to O(ld), we can set T to 1, while finding the minimum value of APP messages can be avoided. According to hard decision w˜ j the only two possible symbols can be found. The detailed search process is shown in Algorithm 2.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, S-SPA and S-MSA which combine symbollevel information with bit-level information in the LDPC decoding procedures are proposed to provide an alternative way to better utilize available information. By updating V2C messages based on symbol-level LLRs and narrowing the search space of symbols, S-SPA and S-MSA utilize available information better than SPA and MSA with little additional complexity and achieved significant performance gains in high-order modulation. Further reducing the complexity of the S-SPA and S-MSA, and the hardware implementation of S-MSA on Field-Programmable Gate Array (FPGA) and Application Specific Integrated Circuits (ASIC) is a topic for further study.
