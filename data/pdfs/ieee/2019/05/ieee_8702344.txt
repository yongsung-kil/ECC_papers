An Adaptation of Min-Sum Decoder for 5G

Low-Density Parity-Check Codes

Khoa Le Trung∗†, Fakhreddine Ghaffari∗ and David Declercq∗ ∗ ETIS, UMR-8051, Universite´ Paris Seine, Universite´ de Cergy-Pontoise, ENSEA, CNRS, France † Faculty of Electrical and Electronics Engineering, Hochiminh City University of Technology, VNU-HCM, Vietnam
{khoa.letrung, fakhreddine.ghaffari,declercq}@ensea.fr; khoa.le@hcmut.edu.vn

Abstract—This paper presents an adaptation of the MinSum decoders for the Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) used in the enhanced mobile broadband (eMBB) scenario in the 5th generation mobile networks (5G). Starting from the structure of the proposed LDPC codes for 5G where a significant part of the Variable Nodes (VNs) is with degree-1 and is sensitively to be erroneous in the traditional Offset Min-Sum decoder, we adapt the Min-Sum decoding principle to decode these 5G LDPC codes, targeting to improve the error correction performance. The proposed decoder, named Adapted Min-Sum (AMS), processes the core and the extension parts of the code differently using different offset factors. By doing that, the error probability of degree-1 VN is significantly depressed. We show through the simulation performance that the proposed decoder, with small number of quantization bits, can even surpass the floating-point counterpart and approaches the performance of the Sum-Product decoder for several 5G LDPC code lengths and code rates, with negligible additional complexity.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes were used in several communications standards thanks to the powerful error correction capability [1],[2]. Recently, it is adopted in the enhanced mobile broadband in 5G. The two base matrices (BG1 and BG2) have been introduced for 5G supporting the rate compatible and information transmission with scalability [3]. Typically, LDPC decoding algorithms are the iterative message passing decoding where the information (or messages) are iteratively processed and passed between the two groups of nodes: the Variable Nodes (VNs) and the Check Nodes (CNs). Many decoding algorithms are proposed in the literature with different levels of error correction capability and the computing complexity. In floating-point decoding algorithm, i.e., the messages are presented with infinite precision, the Sum-Product (SP) decoding algorithm offers the best performance while the Min-Sum (MS) and its variants (Offset Min-Sum, Normalized Min-Sum) are the low calculation complexity solutions with a considerable performance loss [4]. In the finite-precision decoding, i.e., the messages are presented by a limited number of binary bits, making the decoders more practical in term of hardware realization, the Min-Sum and its variants are used widely. The decoding performance loss of these finite-precision decoders is even more significant compared to that of the floating-point decoding algorithms. These decoders are called quantized decoders such as Quantized Min-Sum, Quantized Offset Min-Sum [5],[6] and many works in the literature have been introduced to improve their performance such as in [7],[8]. This paper focuses on the practical, implementable decoders, i.e., the quantized decoders to improve their performance on the 5G LDPC codes.
Recently, two LDPC base matrices have been introduced, named as the BG1 and the BG2, for the enhanced mobile broadband in 5G [3]. These base matrices are constructed in

such a way that they support the rate compatible and scalable data transmission. These LDPC codes are constructed by a concatenation of a high rate LDPC code and a low-density generating matrix code, which leads to the fact that these codes are dramatically irregular and a significant part of the codes are with the degree 1 [9]. The decoding performance of OMS is better than the MS in floating point decoders. However, it is not always the case in quantized ones, especially with the irregular LDPC codes. For example, the Quantized OMS performance on the WiMax LDPC code is much worse than that of the QMS decoder as seen in [8]. This performance loss may be explained by the fact that the low-degree VNs are more sensitives to be erroneous and that the offset factor in the quantized decoder can not be the optimal one due to the limited bit representation of messages.
This paper presents an adaptation of the traditional MinSum decoder for the 5G LDPC codes to improve the error correction performance. We named this decoder as Adapted Min-Sum (AMS) decoder. The principle of this adaptation is to process differently the core and the extension parts of the code differently using different offset factors. We apply the OMS principle on the VNs for the core of the 5G LDPC matrices to obtain the gain from the offset principle while using the traditional Min-Sum for the extension part to reduce the offset effect on these VNs. It is shown that the additional complexity of the AMS is negligible compared to the traditional OMS while a significant gain in decoding performance is obtained. The AMS performance simulations, on the BG2 matrix with different code lengths and code rates, are shown surpassing that of the floating point OMS decoder and approach to the floating point Sum-Product decoder.
The paper is organized as following. Section II recalls the concept of the LDPC codes and their decoder. The structure of the 5G LDPC codes is also presented in this section. The adaptation of the AMS on 5G LDPC codes and its analysis are introduced in Section III. The decoding performance as well as the complexity analysis are shown in Section IV. Section V concludes the paper.
II. 5G LDPC CODES AND THE LDPC DECODERS
A. Preliminaries
A binary LDPC code is defined by a parity check matrix H(M × N ) where N columns corresponding to N coded bits and M rows corresponding to M parity check equations. The coded bit n (1 ≤ n ≤ N ) is checked by the parity check equation m (1 ≤ m ≤ M ) if the entry H(m, n) = 1 and they are called the neighbors. The neighbors set of the CN m is defined as N (m) and that of the VN n is as N (n). The number of neighbors of a VN (CN) is called its degree, denoted by dv (dc), i.e., dvn = |N (n)| and dcm = |N (m)|. Most of the LDPC codes adopted in the practical applications such

In order to send an information vector with K bits through a communication channel, it is encoded by adding M additional bits to form a vector x = (x1, x2, . . . , xn) (|x| = N ) such that HxT = 0 and x is called a codeword. x is modulated before being sent. In this paper, we use the Binary
Phase Shift Keying (BPSK) modulation method and denote y = (y1, y2, . . . , yn) (|y| = N ) as the received information at the receiver: yn = (1 − 2 × xn) + zn where zn is the Gaussian noise with zero mean and variance σ2. The quantized LDPC decoders take the quantized version of y as their inputs. We denote γ = (γ1, γ2, . . . , γN ) as these quantized values such that γn = [µ.yn] where [η] returns the closest integer of η in the integer set A = (−Q, . . . , −1, 0, +1, . . . , +Q), (Q = 2q−1 − 1), i.e., γn is represented in a length of q bits. µ > 0 is referred as the channel gain factor, which needs to be optimized to have a good error correction performance
of quantized decoders [7]. QC-LDPC provides the possibility
to implement the decoder on a “layered” manner in which
a decoding iteration comprises several decoding layers. In a decoding layer, only CNs belonging to 1 (or some) row(s) of the base matrix update their messages and the VNs connecting
to that CNs update their a-posteriori-probability (APP) (some rows of the base matrix can be included into 1 decoding layer as long as there is no VN connecting to more than 1 CN in the layer). A decoding iteration is finished when all of the CNs of H update their messages. This is called the row layered scheduling. There is also the column layered scheduling where
the VNs of one or some column(s) of the base matrix are
included in one decoding layer. We target the decoders with
row layered scheduling in this paper. The LDPC decoding is

a message-passing process where the messages are iteratively
passed between the VNs and the CNs. The LDPC decoders take γ as its inputs and the iterative process is continuously proceeded until the correct codeword is succesfully found or the maximum number of iterations (denoted by Itmax) is reached. In the former case, a decoding success is declared
while the latter is a decoding failure.

B. The Adapted Min-Sum decoder for 5G LDPC codes
The proposed AMS targets to mitigate the scale effect on the low degree VNs while still benefiting the advantage on the other high degree ones. The principle of AMS is to apply different offset factors to different VNs degree groups. In general, each VNs degree group will be applied a different and optimized offset factor. However, for the simplicity in this work we gather all degree-1 VNs as a VNs group and all the others is the second group, which corresponds to the VNs in the extension part and the core part of the 5G LDPC codes. In AMS, all CNs that are the neighbors of VNs degree-1 will apply the scale factor λ = 0, i.e., apply the CN processing of the traditional MS decoder. For the other CNs, the offset factor λ = 1 is applied as in the OMS processing. When applying the AMS on 5G LDPC code, for example the BG2 matrix, the offset factor can be seen as a function of the decoding layer, λ(l). The CNs which have the degree-1 VNs neighbors are from the layer l ≥ 5. Therefore, the offset factor for AMS on BG2 can be defined as: λ(l) = 1 if l ≤ 4 else λ(l) = 0. The decoding process of the AMS follows similarly to that of the MS/OMS decoding as in Algorithm 1 and the difference comes only from the choice of λ(l) (see in the algorithm footnote). In order to verify the effect of AMS principle, we conducted the same statistics as in Fig. 2 for the AMS decoder and plot the results in Fig. 3. It can be seen in the figure that, although the difference between the error probability of degree-1 VNs and the other high degree VNs is till large, the error probability of these degree-1 VNs have been significantly depressed. The worst VNs error probability is of the degree-1 VN and is around 3.2e−5 compared to 2.4e−2 of the OMS and 1.8e−3 of the MS. The similar behavior is observed on another 5G LDPC codes with different code lengths and code rates. It is expected to improve the general decoding FER or BER (Bit Error Rate).

IV. DECODING PERFORMANCE AND COMPLEXITY
ANALYSIS
Due to the space limit, we decided to put the detail architecture and circuits With Z elements, in the longer version of this paper. However, it can be predicted that the additional complexity is not too much compared to MS and OMS. Indeed, the difference comes only from the CNs circuits where the additional control is added as seen in Fig.4. In each AMS CNU with degree dc, dc multiplexers 2-to-1 and a comparator are required. This additional hardware is negligible to the whole system since only Z CNU are implemented to perform in one decoding layer a row of the base matrix.
The decoding performance simulations of AMS, on the comparison of OMS and MS, are conducted on the 5G LDPC code with K = 520, Z = 52 and the code rates are respectively R = 0.2, 0.25 and 0.3. All quantized decoders are implemented in layer scheduling with L = C and Itermax = 15 The performance of the floating point decoders are also added (Itermax = 15). For the simplicity, the simulation assumes that the codeword is sent only one time without using any hybrid automatic repeat request (HARQ) scheme. It can be seen in general that the AMS offers a very good correction performance, much better than the MS and OMS decoder. The AMS is even better than the floating point OMS decoder on the R = 0.25 and R = 0.3 codes and approaches the performance of SP decoder. By design, the low-rate 5G LDPC code performs better than the higher rate. A limitation of AMS can be seen that the error floor starts around the FER=5e−4. However, it is till better than the conventional MS/OMS and can compensate by using the HARQ.
