Constructions of Type-II QC-LDPC Codes With Girth Eight from Sidon Sequence
Guohua Zhang , Yulin Hu , Senior Member, IEEE, Yi Fang , Member, IEEE, and Juhua Wang

Abstract— In this paper, we consider the constructions of type-II quasi-cyclic (QC) low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes with girth eight from a Sidon sequence. We first derive the necessary and sufficient conditions guaranteeing a girth-eight type-II QC-LDPC code. By combining these conditions with the concept of the Sidon sequence, three classes of type-II QC-LDPC codes are subsequently proposed with girth eight. To the best of our knowledge, the second and the third classes we proposed are the first series of systematic and algebraic constructions for the girth-eight type-II QC-LDPC codes that have rates being greater than a half and, at the same time, have distance upper bounds being not limited to 12. Via simulations, we show the promising performance of the proposed type-II QC-LDPC codes with girth eight. In addition, a set of general bounds and explicit/random constructions without using a Sidon sequence are also presented for the type-II QC-LDPC codes with girth six or eight. We observe that the proposed codes perform better than or equally well as the random QC-LDPC codes from PEG and quadr.-congr. methods, while the novel codes possess both highly structured parity-check matrices and very flexible choices in circulant size.
Index Terms— Circulant permutation matrix (CPM), girth, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, quasi-cyclic (QC), Sidon sequence.

important class of LDPC codes, quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes have recently attracted increasing attention owing to their good performance and relatively low encoding/decoding complexity [1]–[6]. Typically, the PCM of a QC-LDPC code is an N ×M array of P ×P circulants, where a circulant could be a zero matrix (ZM), a circulant permutation matrix (CPM), or a summation of w (2 ≤ w ≤ P ) distinct CPMs. Hence the weights of the three types of circulants are 0, 1 and w, respectively. In the PCM of a QC-LDPC code, if the maximum weight over all circulants is n (n ≥ 1), then the code is called type-n. For example, under special cases n = 1 and n = 2, these QC-LDPC codes are also called type-I and type-II codes, respectively. In particular, a type-I code whose PCM only consists of CPMs is called a classical code.
On the one hand, as girth is an effective indicator ensuring a lower bound on minimum distance, it is desired to be as large as possible for QC-LDPC codes [1], [4], [6]–[8]. Due to the inevitable cycles of length six (6-cycles) in a circulant with weight exceeding two [9], type-I/II QC-LDPC codes are generally preferred. On the other hand, compared with classical QC-LDPC codes, type-I (classical excluded) and type-II codes with the same row/column weight commonly permit larger upper bounds on minimum distance [6], [10], [11], which hopefully result in larger minimum distances for well-designed codes.
For type-II QC-LDPC codes, a couple of constructions ensuring girth greater than four have been designed in [12]–[16]. Although several classes of classical/type-I (J, L) QC-LDPC codes with J ∈ {2, 3} have been proposed with girth up to 12 or more [4], [7], [17], it is still challenging to design the systematic construction method for type-II (J ≥ 3, L) QC-LDPC codes with girth greater than six (i.e., at least eight). In the direction of type-II codes with girth eight, two beneficial proposals for J = 3 have been available in [18] and [19]; however, neither of them guarantees girth eight due to the omission of some patterns of 6-cycles (see Remark 1, Section III-B for details). Besides, the codes obtained by method [19] are limited to a half rate, and those generated in [18] come with small distances (upper-bounded by 12) if their rates are greater than a half.
In our previous work [16], we have proposed to construct type-II QC-LDPC codes by a Sidon sequence, and shown that the girth of the codes is greater than four. As achieving larger girth is commonly preferred for an LDPC code design but at the same time is a challenging task, we are motivated to construct type-II QC-LDPC codes with girth greater than six from a Sidon sequence in this paper. A. Weight Matrix, Exponent Matrix and PCM
Let P be a positive integer. Denote by ZP the set {0, 1, · · · , P − 1}. A weight matrix W is an N × M array of nonnegative integers not exceeding P . An exponent matrix E can be obtained by replacing each positive entry w(i, r) in W with a group of w(i, r) distinct integers (exponents) {ek(i, r)|0 ≤ k ≤ w(i, r) − 1} from ZP , and by replacing each zero in W with a symbol ∞. From E, the PCM H of a QC-LDPC code can be generated by replacing each integer ek(i, r) with a CPM generated from a P × P identity matrix with rows cyclically shifted to the right by ek(i, r) positions, and by replacing the symbol ∞ with a P ×P ZM. The w(i, r) CPMs associated with the w(i, r) integers are completely superposed.
For a classical QC-LDPC code, W is only composed of 1’s, and each entry of E is a nonnegative integer. For a type-I QC-LDPC codes, W is composed of 1 and possibly 0. Each entry of E is a nonnegative integer or the symbol ∞. For a type-II QC-LDPC code, W is composed of 2 and possibly 1 and/or 0. Each entry within E is a pair of distinct nonnegative integers {e0(i, r), e1(i, r)}, a single nonnegative integer e(i, r) or the symbol ∞, which corresponds to a summation of two distinct CPMs, a CPM or a ZM in the associated PCM, respectively.

C. Sidon Sequence
The definition of a Sidon sequence is given by [2] and [22]: Definition 1: A Sidon sequence S over ZP is a sequence of CS nonnegative integers {s0, s1, · · · , sCS−1} such that the sums si + sj (i ≤ j) (mod P ) are distinct. The following three lemmas hold for a Sidon sequence. Lemma 1a [2, Lemma 1]: CS(CS − 1) + 1 ≤ P . Lemma 1b [22, Sect. 4.3]: (αS + β)mod P is a Sidon sequence over ZP for any integer α satisfying gcd(α, P ) = 1 and any integer β. Lemma 1c [16, Lemma 3]: S is a Sidon sequence over ZP for any P ≥ 2[max(S) − min(S)] + 1. For P = q2 + q + 1, P = q2 − 1 or P = p2 − p (p prime and q a prime power), Sidon sequences over ZP with maximum cardinality (q + 1, q and p − 1) can be algebraically obtained by Singer construction [22, Sect. 3.4], Bose construction [22, Sect. 3.3] and Rusza construction [22. Sect. 3.2], respectively. For P distinct from the above three forms, one can use computer-aided methods, such as those in [2] and [23], to search for Sidon sequences.

Throughout this paper, we consider the following setups: (i) a binary input additive white Gaussian noise (BI-AWGN) channel with bipolar signalling is assumed; (ii) the sum-product algorithm (SPA) with a maximum number of iterations of 50 is employed to decode LDPC codes; (iii) at least 50 block errors are collected per simulation point.

Remark 3: Based on the analysis provided in Sections V, VI and VII, we summarize the salient features of the three methods as follows. (i) All the methods can yield G8 type-II QC-LDPC codes, via a Sidon sequence over ZP (P even). (ii) Methods I and II are applicable to the case of column weight of three, while Method III is applicable to the case of column weight of four. (iii) As the underlying weight matrix of Method I is composed of identical weight submatrices, the resultant type-II QC-LDPC code has a minimum distance (i.e., dmin) at most 12 for rates greater than a half. (iv) Both the type-II QC-LDPC codes constructed by Methods I and II have a code length of N KP , while those constructed by Method III have a code length of P multiplied by the number of columns of T.
