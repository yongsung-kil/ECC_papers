Codes

Abstract—The generator matrix of quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes is not, in general, in systematic form. Therefore, finding the message corresponding to a detected codeword at the receiver is not as straightforward as it is in the case of systematic codes. In this paper, we propose two methods for finding the message from the codeword in a QC-LDPC code. The first method is a general scheme which views the problem as a set of linear equations. This method is not realizable with pure digital circuits and may be implemented by having a processor core in the design. The second method is rather an alternative scheme which makes use of a particular structure in generator matrices of QC-LDPC codes in order to find the message from a non-systematic codeword. We examine a large class of nonsystematic Latin squares QC-LDPC codes and one of the IEEE 802.16e standard QC-LDPC codes, and show that they have the required structure to be usable by the algorithm. The latter method is realizable in hardware and can be implemented with a digital circuit consisting of XOR gates.
I. INTRODUCTION
In any wireless communications system, reliability is a crucial issue and forward-error correction (FEC) methods are one of the well-known means to respond to this need and provide desired reliability. Among FEC schemes, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are one of the attractive class of codes which have been proven to be capacity-achieving [1].
In an attempt to find a FEC code suitable for highthroughput communication systems operating with data-rates near 100 Gb/s, various aspects of a code must be carefully examined. High encoding and decoding complexity of powerful codes like LDPC or Turbo codes may appear as an obstacle to use them in high-throughput communication systems. Nevertheless, finding good instances of such codes and optimizing their encoding and decoding procedures have been the focal point of research in this area. Quasi-cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes have been one of the examples of LDPC codes that have gained a large attention and been established as a suitable candidate for high-throughput communication systems. In fact, the cyclic structure of QC-LDPC codes can be exploited to simplify both encoding [2]–[5] and decoding [6]–[9] process of the code.
One of the downsides of QC-LDPC codes is that their generator matrices are not necessarily systematic, and hence, obtaining the message from the decoded codeword at the receiver side is not as straightforward as it is in the case

of systematic codes. In this regard, in this paper, we reveal an unnoticed property in some instances of QC-LDPC codes that partially simplifies their decoding. Due to this property, a message corresponding to a codeword can be simply discovered in a few steps by using a circuit comprising merely XOR gates. Specifically, we describe two methods for finding the corresponding message of a QC-LDPC codeword. The first method is the direct stating of the problem as a linear equation system and then solving it via the conventional schemes. This method, due to high complexity, is not realizable in hardware via pure digital circuits and one needs to include a processor core to perform this task. As an alternative, we propose another method which relies on the inherent structure of QC-LDPC generator matrices, and is capable of finding the message within limited number of steps and with a lowcomplexity digital circuit of XOR gates. As a result, the need for having a processor in the design is eliminated and the entire chain of decoding can be performed by simple digital circuits. We couldn’t prove mathematically that all the QCLDPC generator matrices have the required property to be usable by our method. However, our examination on a large number of Latin squares QC-LDPC codes [10] and the rate1/2 QC-LDPC code of IEEE 802.16e standard shows that they are all compliant with the proposed method.
The paper is organized in the following sections. First, in sec. II, we give a brief introduction to LDPC codes, QC-LDPC codes, their generator matrices and Latin squares QC-LDPC codes. We describe the two proposed methods for finding the message from the codeword in sec. III, along with the results of our examination of various instances of QC-LDPC codes. Finally, we conclude the paper in sec. IV and give possible works in the direction of this subject.
II. PRELIMINARIES
A linear block code C is called a (γ, ρ)-regular LDPC code if it is the null space of a parity-check matrix H with following characteristics [11]: i) each row consists of ρ 1s; ii) each column consists of γ 1s; iii) γ and ρ are small compared with n (the length of the code) and J (the number of rows in H); iv) the number of 1s in common between any two columns, denoted by λ, is no greater than 1 (or equivalently the number of 1s in common between any two rows is no greater than 1). III. PROPOSED METHODS
In this section, first, we explain a general method for finding the message u from the codeword v, corresponding to a QC-LDPC code. Later on, we reveal a handy property existent in some QC-LDPC codes which facilitates a very simple way to find the message from the codeword.

A. Method One

Mathematically, we formulate the problem as

u1G ⊕ u2Q = v .

(4)

In (4), u1, the first (t − l)b bits of u, is already known from v. In fact, it is exactly the first (t − l)b bits of v, because

of the systematic form of G. The only unknown variable in

(4) is then the u2 sequence. To find that, (4) is reformulated

to

u2Q = v ⊕ u1G ,

(5)

which yields u2, if solved. Although straightforward, this method is not realizable in hardware, as implementing a circuit capable of solving (5) is impossible. The only option to implement the method is using processors which is not desirable at all. In general, FEC methods are entirely implemented with pure logical circuits and having to include a processor just to take over a part of the computations is not an accepted strategy. To overcome this problem, we have proposed another method which is realizable by pure logical gates and described in the next subsection.

B. Method Two
Here, we propose another method which is iterative, and able to find the message from the codeword in the case of QC-LDPC codes, with a very lower complexity. However, the proposed method is functional if Q possesses a particular property that is described on the continuation.
The method is done in multiple steps, and during each step, some of the bits of the message are discovered, until the whole message becomes known. The basic idea is that columns of Q with weight one can be used to reveal those bits of the message that pertain to the location of ones in those columns. Once those bits are distinguished, relevant rows of Q can be discarded. Now, with discarding some rows of Q, there may

appear new columns in Q that happen to have weight one. These columns again are able to reveal pertaining bits. This procedure can be continued, given that some columns turn out to have weight one at the end of each step.

To make the procedure more clearer, we assess the method as an iterative algorithm. Lets assume that S(i) represents the
indices of single-weight columns in Q in the i-th iteration and b(i) shows the indices of the location of ones in those columns. In the i-th iteration, find S(i) and b(i). If S(i) is not
an empty set, then the bits of the message u2 whose locations are specified by b(i) can be found by solving

u1G[S(i)] ⊕ u2 = v[S(i)],

(6)

which is equivalent to

u2 = u1G[S(i)] ⊕ v[S(i)].

(7)

Then, the rows of Q with indices of b(i) are discarded
and the algorithm is proceeded to the next iteration. At any iteration and before discovering all the bits of u2, if S(i) turns out empty, then the algorithm halts and declares failure.

Note that implementation of a circuit that computes (7) is very simple and low-complex, as it will be a simple circuit consisting of merley XOR gates.
Although, it may seem that generator matrices hardly have the required property to be compliant with the algorithm, we examined the method on a large group of Latin squares QC-LDPC codes, and also one of the QC-LDPC codes used in IEEE 802.16e standard, all listed in table II. Suprisingly, they are found to be applicable to the proposed algorithm. Although we found not even a single instance of QC-LDPC codes lacking this desired property, we cannot claim that all QC-LDPC codes have this feature. The assuring way to claim this is viable if a factual mathematical proof is provided.

It should be noted that the examined codes form a large spectrum of QC-LDPC codes with various code-rates. Also included in table II is the number of iterative steps that the algorithm for each code requires to complete its function. Moreover, one should note that examining a generator matrix to see whether it has the appropriate structure demanded by the algorithm, is a straightforward task.

IV. CONCLUSION AND FURTHER WORKS
The main idea of this paper was to disclose an attractive property existing in generator matrices of QC-LDPC codes, by that, after decoding at the receiver, the message corresponding to a codeword can be easily found. Although we could not provide a mathematical proof that this property exists in all instances of QC-LDPC codes, we tried it on a large number of codes with various code-rates and found them all having that property. Therefore, a useful extension to this work is a mathematical investigation on the generator matrices of QCLDPC codes to see whether or not this is the typical feature of all the QC-LDPC codes.
