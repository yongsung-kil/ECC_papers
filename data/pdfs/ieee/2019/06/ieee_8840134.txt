Abstract— The problem of maximal correctable error burst length estimation is considered for the probabilistic ensemble of block-permutation low-density parity-check codes. It is noted that this task has polynomial running time. For the number of probabilistic ensembles the simulations are performed showing that the correctable burst length grows linearly with the block size of the block-permutation construction, and with high probability equals to the block size minus one.
Keywords— low-density parity-check codes, block-permutation construction, burst error correction
I. INTRODUCTION Low density parity check codes (LDPC-codes) were proposed by Gallager in 1962 [1] and have been investigated in many of papers at the moment [2-5]. Low density parity check codes are widely used in digital broadcasting systems, telecommunication standards, communication standards these days. Traditionally, used in communication standards decoding methods are focused on correcting single errors, although in real communication systems errors are usually grouped. Therefore, the important task is developing coding schemes, oriented to typical communication channel errors, in particular, on the correction of error bursts (i.e. error combinations in which the first and last erroneous symbol of some sequence are spaced apart by no more than a number of characters, called the packet length). In this regard, it is necessary not only to develop new constructions of low density parity-check codes, taking into account the grouping of errors and effective methods of their decoding, but also to determine the length of the burst which can be corrected by the code.
II. BLOCK-PERMUTATION CONSTRUCTION Low density parity check codes are defined by a paritycheck matrix H that contains a small number of ones. The parity-check matrix is often considered as an incidence matrix for a bipartite graph called Tanner graph. It is known that the

presence of small cycles (of length 4) in this graph has a negative impact on the work of iterative decoders, therefore, the standard requirement for constructing LDPC codes is the absence of cycles of length 4 in the correspondent Tanner graph.

III. ERROR BURSTS CORRECTION In order to determine that the code can correct some set of error vectors, it is necessary that each vector from this set has a different syndrome. Then the code can correct single error bursts of length b if and only if the bursts of length b are in different cosets, i.e. there are no two error vectors e1 and e2 (forming bursts of length not exceeding b) for which

e1HT = e2HT .

(3)

In particular, it means that the maximum length of the burst is b ≤ m −1, where m is the block size in the blockpermutation matrix, because the sum of all columns of any block gives a column of ones. Because e1 and e2 are error packets, the analysis of (2) can be reduced to consideration of
submatrices Hb, which consists of γm rows and no more than
2b columns, taken according to the location of the bursts in e1
and e2. A pair of such submatrices Hb1 and Hb2 , matching to
two bursts, should not give the same syndrome. And it is easy to see that a sufficient condition for this is the linear independence of the columns of these submatrices, what is easy to verify with polynomial complexity. The number of all possible pairs of burst locations is also polynomial, which generally gives a polynomial procedure for determining the burst-correction length.

IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
Experiments have been done to determine the burstcorrection length for block-permutation ensembles, consisting of (γ × ρ) block-permutation matrices with the number of blocks: 3×6 (Figure 1), 3×8 (Figure 2), 4×5 (Figure 3), 4×8 (Figure 4). We considered ensembles of codes that do not have cycles of length 4 in the parity-check matrix and ensembles without taking into account the presence of short cycles. Simulation was performed for each m in “mode 0” (without checking for the presence of a cycle of length 4) and in “mode 1” (the ensemble does not contain matrices with a cycle of length 4). A set of value m was considered for each blockpermutation matrix with the number of blocks 3×6, 3×8, 4×5, 4×8. The simulation results showed that for codes with low
rates (the value ρ is slightly higher than γ) the average length
of the corrected burst is close to the maximum possible value bmax = m −1 (see Figure 3). In addition, the simulation results showed that for prime values of m (and power of two values) the probability that the length of the corrected package will be
equal bmax is close to 1 (see Table I and Table II).

V. CONCLUSION In this paper, we consider the burst-correction capability of random block-permutation LDPC codes ensembles. An experimental assessment of the burst-correction length probability was made for certain ensembles of LDPC codes. It is shown that the length of the corrected burst grows linearly with the block size of the check matrix. The results can be useful in the construction of codes for memory channels, and also for specific practical tasks solution, such as data storage or information security systems.
