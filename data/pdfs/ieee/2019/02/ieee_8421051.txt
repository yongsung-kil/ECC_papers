A Two-Bit Precision Decoding Algorithm for LDPC Codes Over the Binary Symmetric Channel
Sunghye Cho and Kyeongcheol Yang , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—The min-sum algorithm as an approximation of the sum-product algorithm (SPA) for decoding of LDPC codes has much lower computational complexity, but slightly worse performance than the SPA. However, severe performance degradation might occur when it is realized in hardware due to the finite precision effect, especially in two-bit precision. In order to overcome this problem, we propose a two-bit precision decoding algorithm. The proposed algorithm employs one of several decoders adaptively, based on the syndrome weight of the estimated decoder output every iteration. Numerical results show that it has similar performance to the SPA over the binary symmetric channel.
Index Terms—Binary symmetric channel, low-density paritycheck code, min-sum algorithm, error floor, syndrome weight.
I. INTRODUCTION
L OW-DENSITY parity-check (LDPC) codes, first introduced by Gallager [1] and rediscovered by MacKay and Neal [2], are known to have performance near the Shannon-limit over the binary symmetric channel (BSC) or additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel under message-passing (MP) decoding algorithms [3]. One of the most well-known MP decoding algorithms is the sum-product algorithm (SPA) which is optimal in the sense of minimizing the bit error probability for a cycle-free graph [4].
The min-sum (MS) algorithm as an approximation of the SPA has much lower computational complexity, but slightly worse performance than the SPA. However, severe performance degradation might occur when it is realized in hardware due to the effects of finite precision. Many researchers have focused on developing finite-precision MS decoders having low error floor [5]–[7]. Zhao et al. [5] proposed an MS decoder with four-bit precision and showed that it has similar performance to the SPA over the AWGN channel for some codes. Later, Zhang et al. [6] introduced an MS decoder having two different quantizers of which

The decoder has six-bit precision and has a lower error floor than conventional MS decoders employing one quantizer with six-bit precision over the AWGN channel. Recently, Zhang and Siegel [7] proposed an MS decoder using a quasiuniform quantizer. It uses 4-6 bits for precision and has superior performance over the BSC and AWGN channel than an MS decoder using a uniform quantizer. These quantized MS decoders might need at least four-bit precision to get similar performance to the SPA over the BSC.
In order to lower the error floor of an MS decoder, Planjery et al. [8], [9] proposed finite alphabet iterative decoders (FAIDs) over the BSC. In FAIDs, the update rule for variable nodes is defined by mapping functions or tables while the update rule for check nodes is the same as that in the conventional MS algorithm. For LDPC codes with column weight three, FAIDs with three-bit precision may have better performance over the BSC than the SPA. However, finding optimal mapping tables for variable nodes of degree >3 seems very difficult since it requires an extensive search. Recently, Romero and Kurkoski [10] introduced a decoding algorithm which needs 2dv −1 (dc −2, resp.) mapping tables for updating a variable node (check node, resp.) with degree dv (dc, resp.). Note that their algorithm with three-bit or four-bit precision uses different mapping tables at each iteration and it might have better performance than the SPA with float-point operations over the AWGN channel as well as the BSC. However, it does not seem to be applicable to practical systems since it needs a lot of mapping tables.
In this letter, we propose a two-bit precision decoding algorithm for LDPC codes over the BSC. The proposed algorithm employs one of several decoders adaptively, based on the syndrome weight of the estimated decoder output at each iteration. It is applicable to practical systems due to its simplicity. Numerical results show that it has similar performance to the SPA with floating-point operations over the BSC.
The rest of this letter is organized as follows. Section II gives a brief review on binary LDPC codes. In Section III, we propose a two-bit precision decoding algorithm for binary LDPC codes over the BSC. Numerical results are presented in Section IV. Finally, we give concluding remarks in Section V.

III. PROPOSED LOW-COMPLEXITY DECODING ALGORITHM
Tomasoni et al. [11] defined the scaled channel message of a variable node as ρ = A/Bmax where A is a channel message value and Bmax is the maximum value of extrinsic messages when the dynamic ranges for these messages are limited. They also observed that every variable node in an absorbing set is correctly decoded, if it has a scaled channel message above the threshold of the absorbing set. In the particular case that an absorbing set has a negative threshold, it can be deactivated by increasing Bmax sufficiently. In other words, a decoder with a small range of ρ may correct the errors induced by absorbing sets better than that with a large range of ρ. Furthermore, it was also observed in [12] and [13] that the error floor phenomenon is mainly due to a small number of trapping sets or absorbing sets. This means that the decoder output in the error floor region has a small syndrome weight in most cases.
Based on these results, it may be possible to make a hypothesis that the errors induced by small absorbing sets can be corrected by adaptively employing a decoder depending on the syndrome weight ω (1 ≤ ω ≤ M). In order to confirm this hypothesis, the range of syndrome weights is first divided into K segments as (ω0, ω1], (ω1, ω2], . . . , (ωK −1, ωK ] where ω0 = 0 and ωK = M , and then a distinct decoder is employed in each segment. For 1 ≤ j ≤ K, let Ωj (ωj −1, ωj ] be the j-th segment, and Dj the j-th MS decoder with two-bit precision. When the syndrome is not zero at iteration i, the syndrome weight ω is calculated to determine which segment it belongs. Assuming that ω ∈ Ωj , the decoder Dj is applied at the next iteration.
Let x = (x1, x2, . . . , xN ) be the transmitted codeword of C over the BSC and y = (y1, y2, . . . , yN ) the corresponding received vector. Denote e = (e1, e2, . . . , eN ) by the error pattern introduced by the BSC with crossover probability α such that y = x ⊕ e where ⊕ is the componentwise modulo-2 sum.

For 1 ≤ j ≤ K, let Dj = (Sj , Tj , Qj 1, Qj 2) be the j-th decoder with ρj = Sj /Qj 2 for Ωj . Note that ρj is the maximum of the scaled channel messages for Dj . The decoders employed in the proposed algorithm are chosen such that they can correct the errors induced by small absorbing sets when ω is small. For this purpose, their parameters are chosen to satisfy Su ≤ Sv , Tu ≤ Tv , Qu1 ≥ Qv1, and Qu2 ≥ Qv2 for any two decoders Du and Dv (u < v). At least, this choice guarantees that ρ1 ≤ ρ2 ≤ · · · ≤ ρK . The block diagram of the proposed decoding algorithm is shown in Fig. 1.
Compared with a conventional two-bit MS algorithm, additional operations for the proposed algorithm are required to calculate ωˆ = wH (Hxˆt ) and choose a mapping function for the variable node update. But, these operations are so simple that their contribution to the computational and storage complexities is meager. In the particular case that variable nodes have low degree, the variable update can be implemented as a mapping table to further reduce the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm, as in FAIDs [9]. Furthermore, the proposed algorithm hardly increases decoding latency when it has the same maximum iteration number as a conventional two-bit MS algorithm has.
The proposed algorithm adaptively chooses one of K decoders every iteration, while conventional post-processing decoding algorithms [9], [12] fix the order of K decoders and apply them sequentially. The adaptive choice has a potential to make better use of decoder diversity than the sequential choice. As a result, the proposed algorithm has better performance than the conventional algorithms in [9] and [12], as will be shown in the next section. In summery, it has low complexity and low latency, as well as its performance over the BSC is quite similar to that of the SPA with floating-point operations.
IV. NUMERICAL RESULTS
In order to demonstrate how well the proposed algorithm works, we consider two binary LDPC codes as follows:
• Code 1: (155, 64) Tanner code [15] • Code 2: (4096, 3712) QC-LDPC code with column
degree five where QC stands for ‘quasi-cyclic’. We consider only columnregular LDPC codes with odd column degree here, since column-regular LDPC codes with even column degree can not

have absorbing sets with negative thresholds [11]. Their per-
formances over the BSC are simulated under several decoders.
Based on our extensive numerical results, the proposed
algorithm with three decoders has much better performance
than that with two decoders. However, increasing the num-
ber of decoders to more than three does not bring a notable
performance gain. Therefore, we only focus on K = 3, that
is, three decoders for the proposed algorithm in this section.
For simplicity, we also restrict S , T , Q1 and Q2 to positive integers.
The procedure of designing three decoders Dj for j = 1, 2, 3 for the proposed algorithm can be described as follows.
• As a first step, we generate a number of conventional
decoders and choose a decoder having good performance
in the waterfall region as D3 = (S3, T3, Q31, Q32). • Next, D2 = (S3 − x1, T3 − x2, Q21, Q22) is found by
simulations, where Q21 ≥ Q31, Q22 ≥ Q32, both x1 and x2 are integers such that 0 ≤ x1 ≤ min{2, S3 − 1} and 0 ≤ x2 ≤ min{2, T3 − 1}. This choice guarantees that ρ2 = S2/Q22 for D2 is equal to or smaller than ρ3 = S3/Q32 for D3. Additionally, the condition T2 ≤ T3 increases the possibility that the number of extrinsic messages with Q22 in D2 is larger than that with Q32 in D3. These two facts lead to the conclusion that D2 may correct the errors induced by absorbing sets better
than D3. • Finally, D1 = (S2 − x3, T2 − x4, Q11, Q12) is found by
simulations where Q11 ≥ Q21, Q12 ≥ Q22, both x3 and x4 are integers such that 0 ≤ x3 ≤ min{2, S2 − 1}, 0 ≤ x4 ≤ min{2, T2 − 1}. The parameters of D1 can be determined in a similar way as in D2. Alternatively, in the case that dominant trapping or absorbing sets of a
code are known, D1 may be chosen as in FAIDs [9] to correct the errors induced by them. For example, errors
in a (4,4) trapping set in an LDPC code with column
weight three can be corrected by D = (1, 2, 1, 3). • The syndrome weights determining Ω1, Ω2, and Ω3 are
determined by simulations. In most cases, ω1 for Ω1 is set to be equal to or less than 20 since Ω1 is chosen to cope with the errors induced by a small number of
absorbing sets.

V. CONCLUSION
The proposed algorithm adaptively employs one of several
MS decoders with two-bit precision, based on the syndrome
weight of the estimated decoder output at each iteration. It has
low complexity and low latency, as well as its performance
over the BSC is quite similar to that of the SPA with floating-
point operations. It may be interesting to investigate how to
choose optimal parameters for decoders and how well the
proposed algorithm works over other channels, including the
AWGN channel.
