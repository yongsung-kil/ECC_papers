More Accurate Analysis of Sum-Product Decoding of LDPC Codes Using a Gaussian Approximation

Francesca Vatta , Member, IEEE, Alessandro Soranzo, and Fulvio Babich, Senior Member, IEEE

AbstractŌĆö This letter presents a more accurate mathematical analysis, with respect to the one performed in Chung et al.ŌĆÖs 2001 paper, of belief-propagation decoding for low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes on memoryless binary inputŌĆöadditive white Gaussian noise channels, when considering a Gaussian approximation (GA) for message densities under density evolution. The recurrent sequence, defined in Chung et al.ŌĆÖs 2001 paper, describing the message passing between variable and check nodes follows from the GA approach and involves the function Žå(x), therein defined, and its inverse. The analysis of this function is here resumed and studied in depth to obtain tighter upper and lower bounds on it. Moreover, unlike the upper bound given in the above cited paper, the tighter upper bound on Žå(x) is invertible. This allows a more accurate evaluation of the asymptotical performance of sum-product decoding of LDPC codes when a GA is assumed.
Index TermsŌĆö LDPC codes, threshold, Gaussian approximation, density evolution, sum-product algorithm, upper bound.
I. INTRODUCTION
L OW Density Parity Check (LDPC) codes are a class of channel block codes first introduced in 1960 by Gallager [1]. Due to the technical limits of that age, they were scarcely considered for almost 30 years, and were re-invented in the mid 1990ŌĆÖs by, among the others, Luby et al. [2]. Since their performance can approach the Shannon limit, they were quickly included in modern standards such as IEEE802.11, 802.16, 10G-BaseT Ethernet, and Digital Video Broadcasting.
LDPC codes are still under intense study using tools that require the understanding of the convergence behavior of their iterative decoding process. For example, to design irregular LDPC codes a density-evolution algorithm was used in [3], which, unfortunately, may be unattractive because it requires intensive computations. Another approach is to use extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) charts, which are simpler, because they provide a one-dimensional (1-D) analysis, but ignore information about the probability distribution function (pdf) of messages, resulting in a loss in accuracy.
There have been a number of approaches to 1-D analysis of LDPC codes (see [4]), all of them based on the observation that the pdf of the decoderŌĆÖs log-likelihood ratio (LLR) messages is approximately Gaussian. This approximation is quite accurate for messages sent from variable nodes, but becomes less accurate for messages sent from check nodes, unless the check

Despite its limits, the use of the Gaussian Approximation (GA) is very useful when an efficient and low complexity method is needed. See its recent use, e.g., to obtain bounds on LDPC decoding thresholds in [5], rate-compatible puncturing patterns for LDPC codes1 in [8] and [9], analytical Bit-error rate (BER) expressions in [10], and UEP LDPC codes in [11].
In [4], following the GA approach, the iterative message passing between variable and check nodes was described mathematically by means of a recurrent sequence involving a function, called Žå(x), therein defined, and its inverse. Its analysis is here resumed and studied in depth, to obtain tighter upper and lower bounds on it. To show their usefulness, a piecewise approximation of Žå(x) is defined, similar but better than the one in [4]. With this new approximation, better GA thresholds can be obtained, closer to the thresholds evaluated with density evolution than the GA thresholds reported in [4].
Moreover, unlike the upper bound in [4], the tighter upper bound on Žå(x) obtained is invertible by means of the Lambert W -function. Its invertibility allows, first of all, the extension of the stability condition under the GA to variable node degree distributions ╬╗(x) of minimum degree i1 > 2 (see next section): being, in this case, ╬╗(0) = ╬╗2 = 0, this is useful to design LDPC codes presenting a linear minimum distance growth with the block length with probability 1 [12]. These codes unfortunately cannot reach capacity under iterative decoding, since the achievement of capacity requires ╬╗2 = 0. However, in this latter case, the block error probability might converge to a constant [12]. Secondly, the invertibility of the tighter upper bound on Žå(x) allows the computability of the approximated residual word error probability Pl at the l-th iteration, given in [4], and not only of its rate of convergence.
The letter is organized as follows. Section II reviews the GA for irregular LDPC codes and the function Žå(x) defined in [4], and Section III the bounds on it proven in [4]. In Sections III-A, III-B, and III-C a tighter upper and lower bound on Žå(x) are given, and a new approximation of Žå(x) is defined, similar but better than the one in [4]. A more general, w.r.t. the one in [4], stability condition under the GA is presented in Section IV and in Section V it is shown that the tighter upper bound on Žå(x) allows the computability of Pl in the same hypotheses. Section VI summarizes the results.

VI. CONCLUSIONS

In this letter, the derivation of tighter lower and upper
bounds on the function Žå(x) defined in [4] was addressed.
To show the usefulness of these two tighter bounds, a new
approximation of Žå(x) has been defined, with which better GA
thresholds can be obtained, closer to the thresholds evaluated
