Tail-Biting Globally-Coupled LDPC Codes
Ji Zhang, Baoming Bai , Senior Member, IEEE, Shuangyang Li , Student Member, IEEE, Min Zhu , Member, IEEE, and Huaan Li

Abstract— This paper presents a new type of globally-coupled low-density parity-check (GC-LDPC) codes whose base matrix has a cyclic structure in the global part. Therefore, the resulting codes are referred to as tail-biting GC-LDPC (TB-GC-LDPC) codes. We propose two methods to construct TB GC quasi-cyclic LDPC (TB-GC-QC-LDPC) codes. For the first method, we extract a replicated version of a constructed base matrix and mask it with a designed masking matrix. Compared to the conventional construction methods, this method provides more flexibility in code length for TB-GC-QC-LDPC codes. The second method is based on designing the incidence matrix of a special type of packings. Examples show that the constructed TB-GCQC-LDPC codes perform well over the additive white Gaussian noise channel (AWGNC) and the binary erasure channel (BEC). The asymptotic performance of TB-GC-LDPC ensembles over BECs are also analyzed by resorting to density evolution. Moreover, numerical results show that TB-GC-LDPC ensembles can achieve better flooding-schedule decoding (FSD) thresholds than the corresponding GC-LDPC ensembles with a similar structure. With sufficient decoding iterations in the global phase, the local/global two-phase iterative decoding (TPD) thresholds of the TB-GC-LDPC ensembles significantly outperform those of the corresponding GC-LDPC ensembles as well.
Index Terms— Globally-coupled, tail-biting, quasi-cyclic, packings, two-phase iterative decoding.

Each disjoint graph specifies a small LDPC code with an independent parity-check matrix, called the local LDPC code (local part). We denote by Gi the Tanner graph of the ith local LDPC code in the collections where 0 ≤ i < t. Conversely, we refer to the part of graph which includes s global CNs and theirs adjacent edges as the global part. Thus, the s global CNs and the t members of Gi compose the Tanner graph Ggc of a GC-LDPC code, as depicted in Fig. 1. This means that these global CNs provide a high connectivity for each node of local LDPC codes. Benefiting from this special structure and some algebraic construction methods [1], [3]–[7], the GC-LDPC codes not only perform well over AWGNCs and BECs, but also have a fast convergence speed in iterative decoding. However, the high connectivity of global CNs may lead to the generation of many high-degree CNs which are easily involved in cycles for the Tanner graph of a GC-LDPC code [8]–[10]. For GC-LDPC codes, most of structure designs focus on the local part [1], [6], [7], [11] and little work has directly used for designing the global part. In this paper, we use a special cyclic structure, termed as “tail-biting” (see Section II-B), in the global part to reduce the degrees of global CNs without changing their global connectivity characteristics.
It is worth noting that existing GC-LDPC codes are designed based on finite fields, and usually have a quasi-cyclic (QC) structure [8] that ensures highly parallelizable encoding and decoding procedures with low complexity [12]–[18]. However, the size of the circulant permutation matrix (CPM), the block length and degrees of check/variable nodes are limited by the selection of the field size. In [14], Keke Liu et al. presented a technique called the replicate and mask (R&M) construction, which has good performance and provides more flexibility in the selection of code parameters for constructing the algebraic spatially-coupled QC LDPC (SC-QC-LDPC) codes, where every CN in the Tanner graph is connected to the VNs that are confined to a (small) span of consecutive locations. However, the R&M construction can not be directly extended to design algebraic GC-QC-LDPC codes since it is difficult to ensure the row-column constraint for the global part. This requires us to improve the R&M construction based on the “tail-biting” structure.
Another key feature of GC-LDPC codes is the localization. We can use some local iterative decoders to decode a large consecutive sequence when the number of errors is small. A global decoder based on the entire parity-check matrix starts to work as soon as these local iterative decoders fail to decode the received sequence. This iterative decoding scheme is called the local/global two-phase iterative decoding (TPD) scheme [1], [3], [4]. It has been shown that the decoding latency and memory requirements of TPD can be much less than those of the flooding-schedule decoding (FSD) scheme with negligible performance degradation [1], [3], [4], [11], which meets the requirements for optical communication systems, NAND flash memories, and etc [1], [6], [7]. However, existing works only provide a preliminary analysis of the decoding performance of GC-LDPC codes through performance simulations and the decoding convergence analysis of the iterative decoder is not discussed. Here, we present a method based on density evolution (DE) techniques for calculating the FSD and TPD thresholds of GC-LDPC ensembles and TB-GC-LDPC ensembles over BECs, and study whether the “tail-biting” structure have an effect of the iterative decoding performance. In this paper, we will investigate the GC-LDPC code with “tail-biting” structure in the global part and analyze the asymptotic performance of TB-GC-LDPC ensembles for FSD and TPD schemes. The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows.
• We present a special type of GC-LDPC codes whose base matrix has a tail-biting structure in the global part and refer to the resulting code as the tail-biting GC-LDPC (TB-GC-LDPC) code. Based on the structure of TB-GC-LDPC codes, we propose a new method to construct TB-GC-QC-LDPC codes, called the replicate, extract and mask (REM) construction. This construction method not only ensures the RC-constraint in the global part for the parity-check matrix of TB-GC-QCLDPC codes, but also provides flexibility in the code length.
• Inspired by the balanced incomplete block designs (BIBDs) [22], [23], we derive a type of packings [24], [25], called special-packings, to obtain another type of TB-GC-QC-LDPC codes. The girth of this type of TB-GC-QC-LDPC codes is also discussed.

Examples show that the constructed TB-GC-QC-LDPC codes perform well over BECs and AWGNCs. • For both FSD and TPD schemes, we use DE to analyze the asymptotic performance of TB-GC-LDPC ensembles over the BEC. Numerical results show that TB-GCLDPC ensembles can achieve better FSD thresholds than the corresponding GC-LDPC ensembles with the same design rate (i.e., the rate of the code assuming that all constraints are linearly independent [26]) and the same structures in the local part. If the maximum iteration number of decoding in the global phase is large enough, the TPD thresholds of TB-GC-LDPC ensembles significantly outperform those of the corresponding GC-LDPC ensembles. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section II gives a brief introduction of GC-LDPC codes and the definition of TB-GC-LDPC codes. Section III proposes the REM construction method for TB-GC-QC-LDPC codes. In Section IV, we introduce the concept of special-packings and propose a simple construction method of this type of TB-GC-QC-LDPC codes. The girth of this type of TB-GCQC-LDPC codes is discussed in this section as well. Section V analyzes the asymptotic performance of TB-GC-LDPC ensembles over the BEC by means of density evolution and further compares the decoding thresholds of GC-LDPC ensembles with those of TB-GC-LDPC ensembles. Section VI concludes this paper. Notation: We use lowercase letters to denote scalars and boldface capital letters for matrices. We use calligraphic font C, G, X , and B to denote the GC-LDPC/TB-GC-LDPC code, Tanner graph of GC-LDPC/TB-GC-LDPC code, the set of objects in packings or BIBDs, and the collection of blocks in packings or BIBDs, respectively.
II. BACKGROUND
A. GC-LDPC Codes
We now briefly review the structure and the construction method of GC-LDPC codes. As shown in Fig. 1, for a GC-LDPC code, there are t disjoint LDPC codes which are formed a collection of local LDPC codes. There are also s CNs, which connect all the local LDPC codes together, namely global CNs. Thus, the s global CNs and the t members of Gi compose the Tanner graph Ggc of a GC-LDPC code. It is easy to see that Gi and Gj are disjoint if i = j. Furthermore, any two local LDPC codes are connected by these global CNs [1]. It should be noted that there are no additional VNs for Gi in Ggc, 0 ≤ i < t. So the GC-LDPC code can not be viewed as a kind of concatenated codes whose outer codes are local LDPC codes. In general, the t members of {G0, G1, . . . , Gt−1} can be distinct.
The GC-QC-LDPC code can be constructed by operating direct product between two LDPC codes whose parity-check matrices are the horizontal and the vertical component matrices of the corresponding GC-LDPC code, respectively. Such a GC-QC-LDPC code is called a product type GC-QCLDPC code. Furthermore, a GC-QC-LDPC code can also be formed by partitioning and dispersing a base matrix over a

VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we presented a new type of GC-LDPC codes, called TB-GC-LDPC codes. Two methods have been proposed for constructing TB-GC-LDPC codes. The first method was based on dispersing the entries of a base matrix whose global part has a tail-biting structure, over a nonbinary finite into binary CPMs and/or ZMs of the same size. The second method was based on designing an incidence matrix of a special type of packings. Examples showed that the proposed codes perform well on both AWGNCs and BECs.
Moreover, we gave a brief analysis of the asymptotic performance of TB-GC-LDPC ensembles over the BEC. Numerical results showed that TB-GC-LDPC ensembles can achieve better FSD thresholds than the corresponding GC-LDPC ensembles with the same design rate and the similar structure in the local part. By comparing the decoding complexities of FSD and TPD schemes, we observed that if the maximum iteration number of decoding is well-selected, the TPD scheme can significantly reduce the latency and memory requirements with minimal performance degradation.
