Design of Irregular LDPC Codes without Markers for Insertion/Deletion Channels
Ryo Shibata, Gou Hosoya, and Hiroyuki Yashima Dept. of Information and Computer Technology, Faculty of Engineering,
Tokyo University of Science, Tokyo 125-8585, Japan

Abstract—Over the past two decades, irregular low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have been hardly able to decode information corrupted by insertion and deletion (ID) errors without markers. Surprisingly, in this paper, we bring to light the existence of irregular LDPC codes that approach the theoretical limit of the channel with ID errors even without markers. These codes contain high fractions of low-degree check nodes that do not appear in irregular codes for other channels. This motivates us to investigate the contribution of low-degree check nodes to correcting ID errors. The investigation provides the following interesting results: degree-2 check nodes are critical to approaching the theoretical limit even without markers, codes with only degree-3, 4, or more check nodes provide moderate decoding performance, and codes with only degree5 or more check nodes can hardly correct ID errors. Finally, we present simulation results that confirm the excellent decoding performance of the irregular codes without markers.
Index Terms—insertion/deletion error, synchronization error, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, degree optimization
I. INTRODUCTION
In future non-volatile memory devices such as bit-patterned media [1] and racetrack memories [2], synchronization errors occur because of imperfect writing or reading processes. An insertion or deletion of a symbol is one of the manifestations of the synchronization errors. Channels corrupted by insertion and deletion errors have memory, hence the conventional techniques for memoryless channels and additive noise can rarely be used straightforwardly.
To combat insertion and deletion (ID) errors, there are many studies of synchronization error correcting techniques [3] based on algebraic and modern (stochastic) coding theory. For stochastic ID channels, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [4] have been studied in [5]–[9]. The concatenated coding of irregular LDPC codes and markers has been investigated in [6], [7]. The markers are predetermined bit strings periodically inserted into codewords for synchronization recovery; however, they lead to a loss in the coding rate. Recently, spatially coupled (SC) LDPC codes [10], [11] for ID channels have been investigated in [8], [9], and the codes provide considerable decoding performance even without markers.
Meanwhile, over the past two decades, irregular LDPC codes have been hardly able to decode information corrupted by ID errors without markers. In [9], the authors attempted to optimize the codes by using a local hill-climbing approach proposed in [4], but the obtained code exhibited a decoding threshold far from the theoretical limit. An interesting question

is whether there really do not exist good irregular codes for ID channels without markers although the SC-LDPC codes exhibit excellent performance.
In this paper, we utilize a global optimization method called differential evolution [12], [13] to explore the space of all possible solutions. The optimization is performed using Kavc˘ic´’s density evolution [14]. Surprisingly, the optimized irregular codes provide decoding thresholds that approach the theoretical limits even without markers. The excellent decoding performance is also demonstrated by a computer simulation. Moreover, the optimized irregular codes without markers outperform the irregular codes concatenated with markers [7] and the SC-LDPC codes.
Interestingly, the optimized codes without markers include uncommon check distributions with many low-degree check nodes and especially the existence of degree-2 check nodes. To best of our knowledge, these types of check node degree distributions do not appear in irregular LDPC codes for other channels [4], [15]–[17]. Motivated by this nonconformity, we further investigate the contribution of the low-degree check nodes to the excellent decoding performance in ID channels. We show that irregular codes with degree-5 or more check nodes can hardly correct ID errors. More precisely, degree-2 check nodes are critical to approaching the theoretical limit of ID channels, and degree-3 and 4 check nodes provide moderate decoding performance. These results reveal previously unknown facts about the design of irregular LDPC codes for ID channels, which also yield a new aspect of degree profiles of irregular LDPC codes.

C. Decoding Threshold and Code Optimization
Density evolution [4] and EXIT charts [20] predict asymptotic performance (i.e., decoding threshold) of LDPC codes and serve as a tool for designing capacity-approaching degree distribution pairs. Based on EXIT charts, the authors in [6], [7] have designed irregular LDPC codes for the concatenated coding with markers. In [7], the codes were optimized with a given marker coding rate RM. Meanwhile, density evolution based on Kavc˘ic´’s method [14] has been studied in [8], [9]. The authors in [9] attempted to optimize the irregular codes by using a local hill-climbing approach [4], but the obtained code performed poor performance for ID errors.
Because log-likelihood messages from the FB algorithm do not form a Gaussian distribution, we utilize Kavc˘ic´’s density evolution [14], instead of EXIT charts, to analyze and design the irregular codes for ID channels. With the density evolution, we can evaluate the tolerance threshold of the ID probability pth, that is, the maximal pid for which the code will be decoded with zero probability of error.

IV. SIR-APPROACHING DEGREE DISTRIBUTION PAIRS FOR ID CHANNELS
A. Optimization Method
It has been known that degree distribution pairs with a few nonzero consecutive ρi, nonzero λ2, λ3, λdl , and a few wellchosen λi tend to lead to capacity-approaching thresholds [4]. Therefore, the authors in [4] recommend that optimization of degree distribution should start from such a degree distribution pair to reduce search space. Unfortunately, irregular codes with such degree properties do not perform well over the channels with ID errors [9]. In this study, without being tied to the conventional good degree properties, we explore the space of all possible solutions. Our goal is to find SIR-approaching degree distribution pairs (λ(x), ρ(x)) in ID channels with a given coding rate RL and maximal degrees dl and dr. To find the best solution, we use differential evolution [12], which is one of the population-based optimization algorithms. The differential evolution algorithm imitates the step of biological evolution and can approach a good solution regarding the objective function via the iterative process of selection and reproduction. Differential evolution has been used for the optimization of degree distributions for the binary erasure

channel (BEC) [15] and the Rayleigh fading channel [16]. Our degree optimization method is performed based on these differential evolution algorithms with the Kavc˘ic´’s density evolution [14].
Let λ = (λ2, ..., λdl ) and ρ = (ρ2, ..., ρdr ) denote the variable and check degree vectors corresponding to λ(x) and ρ(x), respectively. Z is the population size, G is the maximum number of iterations, F1 and F2 are scaling parameters of mutation, and i-th element of z-th vector vz is denoted by vi,z. The following is a brief explanation of our degree optimization algorithm. For more details of the basic steps of differential evolution and the density evolution, please refer to [12], [14]– [16].
1) Initialization: Set g = 0. For 1 ≤ z ≤ Z, we randomly generate a parent pair of degree vectors (λz, ρz) that satisfy the constraints (6)–(8).
2) Mutation: For 1 ≤ z ≤ Z, a mutant pair (λ˜z, ρ˜z) is generated as follows. For 3 ≤ i ≤ dl−1 and 3 ≤ j ≤ dr,
λ˜i,z = λi,z + F1(λi,r1 − λi,z) + F2(λi,r2 − λi,r3 ),
ρ˜j,z = ρj,z + F1(ρj,r1 − ρj,z) + F2(ρj,r2 − ρj,r3 ),
where r1, r2, and r3 are randomly chosen distinct integers from [1, Z]. As for λ˜dl,z, λ˜2,z, and ρ˜2,z, they are adjusted to satisfy the constraints (6)–(8) [16]. The mutation step generates the g-th trial individuals, where good property of the (g − 1)-th survived individuals are randomly incorporated into the trial individuals. 3) Selection: For 1 ≤ z ≤ Z, we compute the decoding threshold (5) of the mutant pair (λ˜z, ρ˜z). The threshold is computed by the Kavc˘ic´’s density evolution [14]. If the decoding threshold of (λ˜z, ρ˜z) is greater than that of (λz, ρz), replace (λz, ρz) by (λ˜z, ρ˜z). The selection step keeps the best individuals (i.e., degree distribution pair) regarding tolerance to ID errors. 4) Termination criterion: If g < G, set g = g+1, and repeat steps 2)–3). Otherwise, the pair of degree vectors that gives the highest decoding threshold is selected as the solution of the optimization.
In the above algorithm, we use the following parameters: G = 500, Z = 240, and F1 = F2 = 0.5.

VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we have provided the irregular LDPC codes that approach the SIR of ID channels even without markers. We found that the irregular codes for ID channels contain lowdegree check nodes that do not appear in the codes for other channels. Therefore, we then investigated the contribution of low-degree check nodes to the excellent decoding performance in ID channels. We showed that the irregular codes with only degree-5 or more check nodes can hardly decode ID errors, while the codes with degree-2 check nodes can approach the SIR limit. Finally, the simulation results were provided to confirm the excellent decoding performance of the irregular codes without markers.
In our future work, we plan to investigate the role of degree2 check nodes, which lead to the SIR-approaching thresholds, in the decoding process. Also, we also plan to investigate irregular LDPC codes on ID channels that suffer from other types of errors such as those caused by substitutions and AWGN.
