Search for Good Irregular Low-Density
Parity-Check Codes Via Graph Spectrum
Dawei Yin∗, Xiaojing Zhang∗, Xichao Shu∗, Guiying Yan†, and Guanghui Wang∗ ∗School of Mathematics, Shandong University, Jinan, China
†Academy of Mathematics and Systems Science, Chinese Academy of Sciences, Beijing, China Email: daweiyin@mail.sdu.edu.cn, xiaojingzhang@mail.sdu.edu.cn, shuxch15@163.com, yangy@amss.ac.cn, ghwang@sdu.edu.cn

Abstract—Research on the expander code shows that for a regular low-density parity-check (LDPC) code, the Tanner graph’s spectrum determines its properties, such as the minimum distance and the size of stopping sets. In this study, we demonstrate theoretically and experimentally that the performance of irregular LDPC codes is related to the graph spectrum. Our observations may provide an efficient metric to search for good irregular LDPC codes.
Index Terms—low-density parity-check codes, graph spectrum, expander codes
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have been adopted for the 5G New Radio standard due to their capacityapproaching performance and low iterative decoding complexity [1]. Density evolution builds a useful framework for the LDPC code design, which can obtain an ensemble of codes with a threshold near the Shannon limit [2]. To find an optimal realization, we need to simulate the performance of each LDPC code in a sizeable finite probability space. In the previous study, based on heuristic methods, there are many useful strategies, such as the extrinsic message degree (EMD), the approximate cycle EMD (ACE) and the girth distribution [3], [4]. These metrics analyze the local structures of the Tanner graph to search for high-performing LDPC codes.
In this study, we propose a effective method called graph spectrum analysis which is inspired by the study of random codes. It can be complementary to these methods mentioned above. LDPC codes can be seen as codes whose graphs are made up of simple codes with linear-complexity graph realizations, connected by a long, pseudo-random interleaver [5]. Elias shows that almost all randomly chosen codes perform essentially as well as the best codes [24]. Therefore, we can find a good implementation by finding a “sufficiently random” code (with performance close to the ensemble expectation).
Research on pseudo-random graphs from graph theory points out what kind of graphs are random-like, and one of the most critical measurement parameters is the graph spectrum. The term pseudo-random refers to a wide range of ideas and phenomena where non-random objects behave in certain ways like genuinely random objects. Chung, Graham, and Wilson show that many graph structures properties are equivalent to

the notion of pseudo-randomness and call such graphs with these properties quasi-random [6]. They list several seemingly different pseudo-randomness properties that a graph can have and assert that these properties turn out to be all equivalent to each other. Two of them are given as follows.

The property Ct gives an upper bound of the number of short cycles in a quasi-random graph. Simultaneously, the girth distribution method counts the number of cycles in the Tanner graph and proposes that LDPC codes with less short cycles tend to perform better. I. F. Blake and L. Shu estimate the number of short cycles in biregular bipartite graphs by using the method of eigenvalue analysis [25]. The close correspondence between the above conclusions reflects the randomness of LDPC codes and inspires us to consider the relationship between the EIG property (the graph spectrum) and the performance of LDPC codes.
To the best of our knowledge, the combination of channel coding theory and the graph spectrum started with the expander codes [7]. There are many excellent results in constructing LDPC codes by the regular expander graphs [8]– [13]. The graph’s expansion factor determines some properties of the expander code such as the minimum distance, the size of the stopping set and the efficient decodability of the code [22], [23]. At the same time, the expansion factor can be analyzed by the graph spectrum. These existing conclusions of expander codes are based on the regular assumption, but LDPC codes in most application scenarios are irregular.
The simulation of different constructions with the same code length and degree distribution shows that LDPC codes with smaller second-largest eigenvalue tend to have better performance. By defining the expansion parameters in irregular graphs, we give a lower bound of the minimum distance of

II. NOTATION AND DEFINITIONS

This section formally introduces the objects discussed in Section I, along with some other definitions that will be used throughout the paper.
The adjacency matrix of an undirected graph with n vertices is an n × n symmetric matrix whose (i, j)-entry is 1 if there is an edge between vertex i and vertex j and 0 otherwise.
The graph spectrum of an undirected graph G is a set of all eigenvalues of its adjacency matrix A(G). Since A(G) is a real symmetric matrix, the eigenvalues of A(G) are real. We denote these eigenvalues by λ1, λ2, · · · , λn, without loss of generality, we shall assume that λ1 ≥ λ2 ≥ · · · ≥ λn. Different from other papers, we define the spectrum gap as the gap between λ2 and the average degree.

Let v and w be two n-tuples over GF (2). The Hamming distance (or simply distance) between v and w, denoted by d(v, w), is defined as the number of places where v and w differ. V. CONCULSION
The theoretical analysis and simulation results show that the code with smaller second-largest eigenvalue tends to perform better. This result can help us make a preliminary assessment of an LDPC code’s performance and narrow the search space.

It will reduce the running time of simulations and improve the efficiency of the searching process.
We use the second-largest eigenvalue as an evaluation criterion here. Other graph spectrum parameters may also be considered, such as the algebraic connectivity (the secondsmallest eigenvalue of a graph’s Laplace matrix [21]). It reflects how well connected the overall graph is, which has been used in analyzing the robustness and synchronizability of networks. In a regular graph, it corresponds to the secondlargest eigenvalue of the adjacency matrix. So it is also reasonable to consider its effect in irregular LDPC codes. Extending the spectral graph theory application in LDPC code design and finding an essential connection between them will be our future work.
