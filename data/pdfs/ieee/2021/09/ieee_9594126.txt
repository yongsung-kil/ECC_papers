Forward-Error-Correction for Beyond-5G Ultra-High Throughput Communications

Abstract—Forward-Error-Correction (FEC) constitutes a dominant power consuming and throughput limiting component in the end-to-end digital baseband chain for wireless systems. With the progress in THz and Tbit/s link-level technologies towards practical solutions and deployments arguably in beyond-5G and 6G era, the performance and implementation bottleneck due to FEC in these systems becomes increasingly clear. In this paper, we explore FEC performance requirements for various beyond-5G wireless use-cases from a practical implementation perspective. We then provide some recent designs in Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) and Polar Codes, and their decoder architectures that demonstrate significant potential towards achieving throughputs approaching 1 Tbit/s.
Index Terms—Forward-Error-Correction, LDPC Codes, Polar Codes, ASIC design, Ultra-High Throughput Decoders
I. INTRODUCTION Existing 5G standards, with foundations laid in 3GPP Release 15 and 16, are built on throughputs in the excess of 20 Gbit/s peak-throughputs. Several technology evolution observations on the other hand, from data traffic trends to network infrastructure models, put practical wireless technologies that achieve Tbit/s data rates as the next major milestones [1]. A practical and widely deployed Tbit/s wireless technology, which can be realized in THz bands (100 GHz and above), requires substantial advancements on multiple fronts including radio frequency (RF) components, as well as digital baseband. Forward-Error-Correction (FEC), a.k.a channel coding, is a basic component of any wireless link technology, and constitutes the most complex and computationally intense component in the digital baseband chain once advanced FEC schemes such as Turbo-, Low Density Parity Check (LDPC)- or Polar-Codes are used to achieve desired coding gains. Similar to the state-of-the art and previous wireless communication products and generations, FEC will remain a critical component in THz systems, albeit with increased challenges considering the substantially higher throughputs. The additional complexity burden on the digital baseband of communications systems instructs a joint consideration of the error correction capability and its silicon implementation efficiency in terms of e. g., silicon area, power and energy consumption. In the past, progress in microelectronic silicon technology driven by Moore’s law was an enabler of large leaps in throughput, lower latency, and lower power [2]. However, we have reached a point where microelectronics

can no more keep pace with the increased requirements from communication systems. In addition, advanced semiconductor technology nodes imply new challenges such as power density, since the transistor density increases faster than the corresponding power reduction. Thus, channel coding for beyond5G/6G THz and ultra-high throughput systems requires a cross-layer approach, covering information theory, decoding algorithm development, parallel hardware architectures and semiconductor technology. In this paper, we initially motivate the underlying FEC requirements for THz and Tbit/s systems from a practical implementation perspective targeting beyond5G/6G use-cases. Then we summarize some of the recent and promising LDPC and Polar decoder architectures with performances approaching or achieving the presented FEC requirements.

II. FEC PERFORMANCE REQUIREMENTS FOR ULTRA-HIGH THROUGHPUT THZ SYSTEMS

Wireless link-level, or digital baseband design takes various Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) into account, all of which collectively determine the underlying system requirements. Some of the key KPIs for wireless system design include error correction capability, operating frequency band, transmission range, operating conditions (in terms of channel characteristics), latency constraints, transmission direction, Hybrid Automatic Repeat reQuest (HARQ) admissibility, code flexibility (in terms of block-length and code-rate), and cost. FEC design requirements depend on these communication performance related KPIs and also on the hardware implementation KPIs. The most important FEC implementation KPIs can be considered as information bit throughput, area, and power consumption, that are directly related to cost (silicon area, package), as well as area efficiency, energy efficiency, power density and latency [3]. The affordable chip area of the FEC IP block depends on various factors such as technology node (Integrated Circuit (IC) design cost in very advanced technology nodes can be more than $100 million in today’s terms), production/market volume, system prices etc. We assume that the total area of a baseband Systemon-Chip (SoC) is limited to 100 mm2 due to cost issues. A feasible assumption is that a maximum of 10% of this area should be reserved for the FEC IP [3]. Hence, a practical FEC area, as a widely deployable solution for THz/Tbit/s usecases, is assumed to be less than 10 mm2. In the following, we discuss the FEC KPIs, corresponding design challenges, and define a broad performance requirement framework for THz systems and use-cases.
BER KPI: The Bit Error Rate (BER) requirements for THz use-cases are bound to vary significantly depending on the categorization of these under either infrastructure or enduser domains. For instance, IEEE 802.15.3d standard sets very stringent BER requirements of 10−12 for infrastructure type use-cases such as wireless backhaul/fronthaul, and data centers, in contrast to relatively relaxed BER requirement of 10−6 for close-proximity communications with applications in personal-area networks [4].
Throughput KPI: Beyond-5G era is expected to be dominated by new definition of multi-media transportation and usecases, including 360-degree ultra-HD videos, augmented and virtual-reality applications some of which might be realized by novel AR/VR terminals at the end-users. Various detailed analysis and predictions place the throughput requirements in the order of 100 Gbit/s to 1 Tbit/s, up to 50× improvement over 5G peak throughputs [5].
Power and Power Density KPIs: Thermal Design Power (TDP), that is the maximum amount of heat the package/cooling system of a SoC is designed for, sets an upper limit on the power and corresponding power density. Due to cost reasons (package/cooling cost) the power of the FEC IP is typically limited to around 1 Watt, which yields a power density in the order of 0.1 W/mm2 as a feasible value for practical FEC IP implementation.
Energy Efficiency KPI: A total power budget of 1 Watt for FEC IP and 1 Tbit/s throughput requirement results in 1 pJ/bit energy efficiency constraint. Power density and energy efficiency KPIs belong to the biggest implementation challenges. For comparison, 1 pJ is in the same order of magnitude as performing a 32bit floating point multiplyaccumulate (MAC) operation in 14 nm technology or one order of magnitude lower than accessing a single bit from a state-ofthe-art external Dynamic Random Access Memory (DRAM). For a fixed power budget, improving the energy efficiency is equivalent to increasing the throughput.
Area Efficiency KPI: A 100 Gbit/s/mm2 requirement can be directly obtained from corresponding throughput (1 Tbit/s) and chip area (10 mm2) values. Note that minimizing the power density is a trade-off between area efficiency and energy efficiency.

Frequency KPI: A maximum feasible frequency limit for a FEC IP is 1 GHz. This limit exists mainly for two reasons. First, the dynamic power consumption scales linearly with the frequency. Second, FEC IPs are typically implemented as synthesizable IP cores. Managing routing complexity and clock skew beyond 1 GHz in synthesis-based design methodologies becomes extremely challenging and becomes even more difficult with future technology nodes. Achieving 1 Tbit/s throughput under a frequency constraint of 1 GHz implies that 1000 information bits must be decoded in a single clock cycle. Hence, extremely parallel signal processing and corresponding parallel architectures become mandatory.
Code Flexibility KPI: This feature includes flexible adaptation of code length and coding rate during the transmission. In some of the use cases, such as intra-device communications where relatively constant channels are observed, the code flexibility can be low. On the other hand, for use-cases with dynamically varying wireless links, e.g. VR/AR connectivity, the FEC needs to support high flexibility.
Latency KPI: FEC latency corresponds to the duration of one full-codeword decoding at the decoder. The latency constraint directly relates to system-level latency, or latency in the air-interface transmission. It should be noted that throughput and latency correspond to two different KPIs. Achieving low latency is often more challenging than achieving high throughput. For THz applications with stringent application or service level latency requirements, the target FEC latency is expected to be around 25 ns, whereas for latency-tolerant applications, FEC latency can be in the order of 250 ns [3].

III. FEC CANDIDATES FOR ULTRA-HIGH THROUGHPUT THZ SYSTEMS
FEC solutions that achieve throughputs around 100 Gbit/s already exist for some state-of-the-art applications, e.g. optical communications [6]. However, these solutions are mainly restricted to algebraic hard decision decoding techniques, whereas the most powerful FEC techniques exploit soft decision techniques, primarily for LDPC and Polar codes, which are two FEC techniques with outstanding error correction capabilities and that can scale up in throughput. These codes are adopted by many wireless communication standards, i.e. LDPC codes by WiFi, WiMAX, WiGig, DVB-S2, 10Gbase-T and 5G eMBB data channels, and more recently Polar codes by 5G eMBB control channels. towards 1 Tbit/s under 1 GHz frequency constraints requires the decoding of up to 1000 information bits in a single clock cycle. Hence, a very high degree of parallelism of decoding for a practical FEC solution is a must [7]. The achievable parallelism strongly depends on the code itself, the complexity and properties of the decoding algorithms and the underlying architecture. The achievable throughput is proportional to the code block size N , the code rate R, the frequency f , the architectural parallelism and inversely proportional to the number of decoding iterations in the case of iterative decoding algorithms. Efficient high throughput implementations require locality and regularity to minimize power, area and interconnect [7]. Interconnect might largely contribute to the area, delay and energy consumption, especially in advanced technology nodes. Thus, data transfers can be as important as computations. Although channel decoding algorithms for advanced codes are mainly data-flow dominated, they imply irregularity and restricted locality, since efficient channel coding for LDPC codes is grounded on some randomness, i.e. the Tanner graph for LDPC codes. Any regularity and locality in these structures improves the implementation efficiency but has negative impact on the communications performance. Hence, a large parameter set and many trade-offs on code design, decoding algorithms, architectures, and efficient implementations in advanced technology nodes exist.
IV. LDPC DECODERS LDPC decoding with the Belief Propagation (BP) algorithm has an inherent parallelism on check node and variable node level, that makes LDPC codes well suited for high throughput. Exploiting full parallelism on node level achieves throughputs towards 100 Gbit/s. For throughputs beyond 100 Gbit/s, additional frame-level parallelism becomes mandatory. Two techniques exist: 1) deeply pipelining of a single decoder core and/or 2) multi-core decoders. The unrolled LDPC decoder presented in [8] is an example of deeply pipelining on frame level in which the iterations are unrolled and pipelined. This decoder reads and outputs a complete frame/data block per clock cycle. The coded throughput is directly dependent on the maximum achievable clock frequency and latency is determined by the number of pipeline stages. The implementation efficiency and achievable throughput/frequency of these decoders is largely dominated by the routing complexity that is directly proportional to the block size, quantization, check/variable node degree and the number of iterations. In [9] it was shown that such a decoder can achieve a throughput of 588 Gbit/s in a 28 nm FD-SOI technology. An orthogonal approach to increase the throughput is the use of multi-core architectures that was presented in a recent paper [10]. A multi-core LDPC decoder composed of 12 partially parallel layered LDPC decoder cores is introduced that achieves a throughput of 833 Gbit/s in a 12 nm FinFET technology. Each decoder core processes 3 frames in a 3-stage pipeline, yielding a frequency of 1 GHz and throughput of 69.4 Gbit/s for a single core. V. POLAR DECODERS Polar decoding for throughput towards 1 Tbit/s is only feasible for low complexity decoding algorithms such as Successive Cancellation (SC) decoding [12]. SC decoding traverses the Polar Factor Tree (PFT) in a depth-first manner resulting in a sequential decoding procedure [13]. Highest throughput can be achieved by unrolling and pipelining the PFT traversal in a way similar to the iteration unrolling of unrolled LDPC decoders [14]. Such a polar decoder reads and outputs a complete data block per clock cycle alike the LDPC decoder. In contrast to very high throughput LDPC decoders in which routing dominates, the implementation efficiency of unrolled Polar decoders is dominated by the huge amount of data that has to be processed in the decoding pipeline. An ultra-high throughput unrolled Polar SC decoder implemented in a 16 nm FinFET technology is presented in [15]. The design is based on a (1024,854) Polar code (code rate 0.83), generated with Density Evolution algorithm. Advanced optimization techniques were applied on all design levels: minimization of the PFT by exploiting shortcuts, also known as Fast Simplified SC [16], adaptive quantization [17], optimized pipeline stage insertion and clock gating [18]. Figure 2a shows the corresponding layout; a flat design flow procedure was used in the backend [15]. A frequency of 1.2 GHz could be achieved resulting in a coded throughput of 1.2 Tbit/s. This is the fastest reported SC decoder implementation in the literature. The number of pipeline stages is 60 clock cycles yielding a latency of 0.05 µs.

The total area is 0.79 mm2, providing an area efficiency of 1554 (1296 uncoded) Gbit/s/mm2. The power consumption is 1.17 W, that gives an energy efficiency of 0.96 (1.14 uncoded) pJ/bit. The cell utilization is 67.5% showing that routing is not as challenging as in unrolled LDPC decoders, especially when considering the larger block size of the Polar Code (1024 vs. 648 bit). However due to the high frequency, managing hold time violations in the delay lines is very challenging. The presented decoder already fulfills in 16 nm technology all FEC implementation KPIs except power density.
The SC is a low complexity decoding algorithm that enables highest throughput but suffers on error correction performance. Successive Cancellation List (SCL) processes a list of candidates among the PFT nodes [19]. The list is limited to a fixed length, e.g. 2, 4, 8. At the leaf nodes, where bit decisions are made, i.e. new candidates generated, the list has to be sorted to cancel out less reliable candidates. The increase in decoding complexity due to list management and sorting yields an improved error correction performance that can be further increased by use of an additional Cyclic Redundancy Check (CRC). Figure 2b shows the layout of an SCL unrolled decoder with list size 2, implemented in a 22 nm FD-SOI technology for the same code as the aforementioned SC decoder [15]. In this design, an operating frequency of 500 MHz results in a coded throughput of 465 Gbit/s. The number of pipeline stages is 121 clock cycles resulting in a latency of 0.266 µs. The total area is 3.89 mm2, providing an area efficiency of 117 Gbit/s/mm2. The power consumption is 1.94 W, that results in an energy efficiency of 4.16 pJ/bit. Extrapolation of these numbers to more advanced technology nodes, i.e. 7 nm, shows that throughputs achieving Tbit/s are feasible for the proposed SCL decoder layout, and for SCL decoders depending on the list size. We can also explore the trade-off between error correction capability and implementation KPIs for SC and SCL decoders using the outcomes of the SC [15] and SCL decoders described above. Since the aforementioned SC decoder [15] was implemented in a 16 nm FinFET technology, whereas the SCL decoder in 22 nm FD-SOI technology, it is not possible to have a fair comparison between these two presented decoders. Towards that objective, in this work, the SC decoder presented in [15] is re-implemented using the same 22 nm FD-SOI technology. Moreover, the frequency limit is set to the same value (500 MHz) as in the SCL decoder in order to ensure the same throughput values achieved by these decoders. The SC decoder is optimized for this frequency resulting in 41 pipeline stages (82 ns latency), 1.05 mm2 area that gives an area efficiency of 490 Gbit/s/mm2, and power consumption of 0.62 Watt which corresponds to an energy efficiency of 1.21 pJ/bit. In summary, compared with the SC decoder, we observe that the SCL decoder has an increase of ∼4× in area and a decrease of ∼3.5× in energy efficiency. However, the resultant increase in implementation costs is traded-off with an improved error correction performance of ∼0.5 dB. VI. CONCLUSION In this paper, we have explored the performance requirements for forward error correction and advanced channel coding schemes targeting ultra-high throughput (Tbit/s) B5G/6G use-cases. The analysis shows that a practical FEC technology for this operating regime requires very challenging KPIs, particularly under power and energy efficiency constraints while targeting Tbit/s throughput. In order to successfully achieve these KPIs, thereby a practical Tbit/s FEC technology, a cross-layer approach becomes mandatory, since code/decoding algorithms have a large impact on the final implementation efficiency. Moreover, due to the fact that the decoding algorithms largely differ for the two considered coding schemes, LDPC and Polar codes, the challenges are different for each code. The throughput and implementation efficiency of LDPC decoding is strongly dominated by the interconnect complexity. For Polar decoders, the largest challenge is in the managing of the storage. Quantization directly impacts all implementation KPIs for all decoders. Hence, efficient quantization is another important optimization parameter. New approaches such as information bottleneck techniques are promising candidates especially for data transfer dominated coding schemes. Except for SC-LDPC codes, all other coding techniques are limited to small block sizes when targeting Tbit/s throughput. Alike, iterative decoding algorithms are limited to a small number of iterations. However, small block sizes and low number of iterations have negative impact on the error correction performance. Hence, achieving very good error correction capabilities under such constraints is still challenging when high implementation efficiency has high priority.
