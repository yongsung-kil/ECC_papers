Abstract—Channel coding, or Forward Error Correction (FEC), is a crucial technology component in any digital baseband processing. Decoder IPs for advanced coding schemes like Turbo, LDPC, and Polar codes are major sources of power consumption and silicon area in baseband SOCs and largely contribute to the baseband latency and throughput limitations. Although we observe a continuous increase in throughput and lower latency requirements in emerging communication standards, the available power and energy budget does not increase due to, e.g., thermal design power constraints. If we look on the silicon technology progress, the transistor density still follows Moore’s law, but the power improvement largely slows down, that exacerbates the power density problem. Hence, for use cases with high throughput requirements like B5G, power, power density and energy efficiency become a major bottleneck for the successful application of advanced channel coding from a silicon implementation perspective. In this paper we focus especially on LDPC and Polar codes since they are part of many communication standards. Challenges, power and energy optimizations on the different design levels for decoders targeting throughput towards 1Tb/s and various trade-offs are presented.

I. INTRODUCTION

The most important Key Performance Indicator (KPI) for a Forward Error Correction (FEC) decoder Intellectual Property (IP) from a communication point of view is its error correction capability that is defined as the respective Frame Error Rate (FER) or Bit Error Rate (BER) as a function of the Signal-toNoise-Ratio (SNR). Implementation KPIs are information bit throughput Tinf [bit/s], latency [ns], silicon area A [mm2] and power consumption P [W]. Rather, there are conflicting requirements. Energy efficient FEC decoders require that P , Eeff and Pdens have to be minimized, yielding small values for N , I, π and f . However for given code and decoding algorithm a high error correction capability and high throughput requires the opposite value for these parameters, i. e., large values for N , I, π and f . Beyond that, the code and corresponding decoding algorithm largely impact the error correction capability but also I, π and f . E. g., a sequential and compute intensive decoding algorithm limits the maximum π and f . In a similar way the code structure influences these parameters. E. g., an irregular LDPC code has a negative impact on the maximum π and f . These examples demonstrate that many trade-offs on code design, decoding algorithms, architectures and energy efficient implementation in advanced technology nodes exist.
Contributions: In this paper, we present new optimization techniques on algorithmic and architecture/technology layer for energy efficient very high throughput LDPC and Polar decoders:
• For LDPC codes we propose a layered unrolled LDPC decoding architecture and provide a fair comparison with a State-of-the-Art (SOA) unrolled Two-Phase (TP) decoder in a 22 nm FD-SOI technology.
• For the first time, we apply Iteration Control (IC) techniques to an unrolled LDPC decoder architecture and quantify the trade-off between logic/area overhead and power savings in 22 nm technology.
• We present the first in-depth investigation on the tradeoff between spatial and functional parallelism (pipelining) for deeply pipelined architectures under consideration of VDD scaling.
All presented decoders were synthesized and placed and routed using Synopsys Design Compiler and ICCompiler, respectively. Power numbers are calculated with back-annotated wiring data using Synopsys Primetime and Siemens Modelsim.
II. ENERGY EFFICIENT LDPC DECODERS
LDPC decoding is based on an iterative exchange of messages between variable and check nodes in the Tanner graph of the code. The inherent parallelism on node level makes LDPC codes well suited for high throughput requirements. Here, we focus on unrolled decoding architectures for highest throughput that combine full parallelism on node level with unrolling and pipelining of decoding iterations, i.e. π = I [1], [2].
It was already stated that Eeff depends on the number of iterations I just as the power consumption P (π = I for unrolled architectures). So minimizing I is central for energy efficient FEC architectures. There are mainly two approaches in literature for reducing I while retaining the error correction capability of the decoder.
1) Layered scheduling/decoding. The processing of the Tanner graph is serialized and the nodes partially updated resulting in faster convergence of the decoding and thus a lower number of iterations.

2) Dynamic IC. The number of iterations is adjusted at runtime to the varying SNR of the channel.
Both approaches are widely applied in various SOA partialparallel (π < 1) and full-parallel (π = 1) decoding architectures, e. g., [3], [4], that achieve throughputs up to 100 Gbit/s for a high SNR. In this paper, we present for the first time how these techniques can be efficiently applied to unrolled architectures (π = I) and thus for throughputs far beyond 100 Gbit/s.
SOA unrolled decoding architectures, e. g., for Min-Sum (MS) decoding in [1], employ a TP scheduling, i. e., each iteration stage consists of N Variable Node Functional Units (VFUs) and (1 − R) · N Check Node Functional Units (CFUs) to perform the variable, respectively check node operations. VFUs and CFUs are connected via wires representing the connections of the Tanner graph associated with H, i. e., each 1 in H results in Q wires with Q being the bitwidth of the exchanged messages. For I iterations, I VFU/CFU stages and 2 · I interconnecting networks have to be instantiated. Hence, unrolled architectures exhibit large area and power consumption for routing yielding a low cell utilization in the layout and limiting the maximum N that can be processed [5].
Information bottleneck is a promising approach to reduce Q and thus, the number of wires at the cost of an increased node complexity [2]. Here, we introduce layered decoding for the first time as another concept to reduce the routing complexity of unrolled architectures. Since the routing complexity depends on the node parallelism in one iteration stage, we split the H-Matrix into l horizontal layers, where each layer corresponds to one architecture stage. This stage has a reduced check node parallelism and lower routing complexity. In other words, a stage (= one iteration) of the non-layered unrolled architecture is replaced by l stages, where each stage has a lower routing complexity. This is similar to the fullparallel architecture presented in [4], that, however, has not considered iteration unrolling. Note that the increase in stages only increases the number of registers but does not change the total number of wires and variable node calculations, since the node calculations are now distributed over several stages enabling a continuous update of its values. Since layered decoding decreases the number of necessary iterations I for a fixed error correction capability, less iterations are mandatory yielding a further improvement in the implementation KPIs.
We demonstrate the advantage of this new unrolled architecture by the example of the (1032, 860) EPIC LDPC code [6] and implemented a SOA unrolled TP decoder and the new unrolled layered decoder in 22 nm FD-SOI technology. The H-matrix of the EPIC code is partitioned into l = 4 layers, each consisting of 43 rows. To achieve the same clock frequency as the unrolled TP decoder, the layer processing stage is partitioned into three pipeline stages. The top-level architecture for one such layered decoding iteration is depicted in Figure 1. Each layer processor operates on a different code block, indicated with different colors. This problem can be mitigated by 1) evaluating a stopping criterion at each iteration stage in the decoding pipeline, 2) use of clock gating to switch-off unnecessary iteration stages for the current frame, and 3) propagating the decoded Hard Decision (HD) through the switched-off iteration stages to the output of the decoder. Figure 3 depicts the necessary architecture modifications for an unrolled TP decoding iteration (highlighted in magenta). As stopping criterion, we use the Parity-Check (PCHK) of the check node stage, which implies only negligible hardware overhead. From the PCHK signal, that is ’0’ if all parity checks are satisfied, we derive the enable signal for the registers and a write signal for the HD bits, that are, if applicable, written to the channel values pipeline. In this way, additional memory for storing the HD is not required. Writing each HD bit to the Least Significant Bit (LSB) position of the respective channel value minimizes residual register toggling in subsequent decoding stages. Gated registers are highlighted in green. Obviously these modifications come along with some area overhead, throughput degradation and power overhead for the low SNR regions, mainly due to the additional multiplexers for writing the HD.
To quantify these trade-offs, we implemented two 8-iteration unrolled TP decoder with and without IC in 22 nm FD-SOI technology for a 802.11n (648,540) LDPC code. The amount of gated registers in the IC architecture is 80%. Table II shows the final results. On the one hand, we observe 29% increase in area and 8% decrease in the maximum frequency yielding a decrease in the area efficiency. For the most part, this area increase is not a consequence of the logic, but the routing overhead for the HD and the enable signals that result in a lower area utilization (53% vs. 62%). On the other hand, we see a very large improvement in power, energy efficiency and power density with increasing SNR. Voltage overscaling is another efficient technique to reduce the power consumption by exploiting the inherent error resilience of channel decoding, e.g. [13]. But due to space limitations a discussion on this technique is out of scope of this paper.

IV. CONCLUSION In this paper we presented various challenges and new optimization techniques for energy efficient high throughput FEC decoders. We discussed the strong interdependencies of error correction and implementation parameters, its impact on KPIs and the various trade-offs.
