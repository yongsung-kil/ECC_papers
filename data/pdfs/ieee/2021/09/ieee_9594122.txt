Abstract—This paper presents two classes of partial geometries (PaGs) derived from balanced incomplete block designs (BIBDs) of a special type. Based on these two classes of partial geometries, quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes can be constructed. These codes not only perform well over AWGN channels (AWGNCs) but also can correct bursts of erasures over binary erasure channels (BECs) effectively.
I. INTRODUCTION This paper presents two classes of PaGs which are derived from two classes of BIBDs constructed by Bose [1], named as BIBD-PaGs. Based on these BIBD-PaGs, two classes of QC-LDPC codes, called QC-BIBD-PaG-LDPC codes, are constructed. We organize the paper as follows. In Section II, basic concepts and structural properties of PaGs are presented. Section III defines BIBDs in general and shows that BIBDs of a special type are PaGs. In Sections IV and V, two classes of PaGs derived from the BIBDs are presented. Based on these BIBD-PaGs, QC-LDPC codes are constructed. Several well performing QC-BIBD-PaG-LDPC codes are designed for AWGNCs that have a special cycle structure. This structure is used in Section VI to construct optimal codes for correcting phased bursts of erasures over BECs. Section VII concludes the paper with some remarks.
II. PARTIAL GEOMETRIES Consider a combinatorial system composed of a set of N points and a set of M lines where each line consists of a set of points. Such a combinatorial system forms a partial geometry (PaG) if the following conditions are satisfied for some fixed integers r ≥ 2, t ≥ 2, and c ≥ 1 [2]–[5]: (1) Any two points are on at most one line; (2) Each point is on r lines; (3) Each line passes through t points; (4) If a point a is not on a line L, then there are exactly c lines, each passing through a and a point on L. We denote such a partial geometry by PaG(r, t, c) where r, t, and c are called the parameters of the partial geometry PaG(r, t, c). The parameter c is called the connection number. PaGs were first introduced by Bose in 1963 [2]. A simple counting argument [5] shows that PaG(r, t, c) contains N = t((t − 1)(r − 1) + c)/c points and M = r((t − 1)(r − 1) + c)/c lines. If two lines have a common point a, we say that the two lines intersect at the point a; otherwise, we say that the two

lines are parallel. Two lines in PaG(r, t, c) are either parallel or intersect at one and only one point.
Let HPaG(r, t, c) be the line-point incidence matrix of a partial geometry PaG(r, t, c), which is an M × N matrix over GF(2) with rows and columns corresponding to the M lines and the N points in PaG(r, t, c), respectively. Then, HPaG(r, t, c) has constant column weight r and constant row weight t. The rows of HPaG(r, t, c) are called the incidence vectors of the lines in PaG(r, t, c). Since two lines in PaG(r, t, c) are either parallel or intersect at one and only one point, no two rows (columns) in HPaG(r, t, c) have more than one location where they both have 1’s. Such a structural property of HPaG(r, t, c) is referred to as row-column (RC) constraint [6]. The RC-constrained structure of HPaG(r, t, c) ensures that its associated Tanner graph has girth at least 6.

III. BALANCED INCOMPLETE BLOCK DESIGNS

A BIBD is an arrangement of N objects in M sets satisfying the following conditions: (1) Each set contains exactly t different objects; (2) Each object appears in exactly r different sets; (3) Any pair of objects appears in exactly λ different sets. The sets are called blocks. The parameters N , M , r, t, λ satisfy the following two equalities:

tM = rN, λ(N − 1) = r(t − 1).

(1)

The above BIBD is named as an (N, M, t, r, λ)-BIBD. For a BIBD with λ = 1, each pair of objects appears in one and only one block. This implies that two blocks in this BIBD has at most one object in common. Viewing the objects and blocks as points and lines, respectively, an (N, M, t, r, 1)-BIBD is a partial geometry, denoted by PaG(r, t, t) with N = r(t−1)+1 points and M = r(r(t − 1) + 1)/t lines. Each line consists of t points and each point is intersected by r lines. Two points appear on one and only one line.
The line-point incidence matrix HPaG(r, t, t) of PaG(r, t, t) is an RC-constrained M × N matrix over GF(2) with column and row weights r and t, respectively. The rows of HPaG(r, t, t) are the incidence vectors of the lines in PaG(r, t, t) (or the blocks of the (N, M, t, r, 1)-BIBD), and its columns correspond to the points of PaG(r, t, t) (or objects of the (N, M, t, r, 1)-BIBD). The partial geometry PaG(r, t, t) is called a BIBD based partial geometry, denoted by BIBD-PaG.

IV. CLASS-1 BIBD-PAGS AND THEIR ASSOCIATED QC-LDPC CODES
In 1939, Bose [1] constructed several classes of BIBDs with λ = 1 based on prime fields under certain constraints. In this and next sections, we present two classes of Bose-BIBDs and their associated PaGs, denoted by Bose-BIBD-PaGs, from which two classes of QC-LDPC codes whose Tanner graphs have girths at least 6 are constructed.

Let GPaG,1(4, b) be the Tanner graph associated with HPaG,1(4, b) which consists of b(12b+1) variable nodes (VNs) and 4(12b+1) check nodes (CNs). Let Gsub(2, 2) be a subgraph of GPaG,1(4, b) associated with a 2 × 2 subarray Hsub(2, 2) of HPaG,1(4, b). The subgraph Gsub(2, 2) consists of 2(12b + 1) VNs and 2(12b + 1) CNs. Since each CPM in HPaG,1(4, b) is of size (12b + 1) × (12b + 1) and 12b + 1 is prime, Gsub(2, 2) is composed of a single cycle of length 4(12b + 1), i.e., the 2(12b + 1) VNs and 2(12b + 1) CNs of Gsub(2, 2) are on a single cycle of length 4(12b + 1). This local cycle structure is referred to as the 2 × 2 CPM-array cycle structure [7], [8]. With this cycle structure, a QC-LDPC code constructed based on PaG1(4b, 4, 4) can be used for correcting bursts of erasures over BECs which will be presented in Section VI.
The null space of HPaG,1(4, b) gives a QC-BIBD-PaGLDPC code CPaG,1(4, b) whose Tanner graph has girth at least 6 and has the 2 × 2 CPM-array cycle structure. Using the base matrix BPaG,1(4, b) given by (5) with CPM-dispersion, we can construct a family of class-1 QC-BIBD-PaG-LDPC codes. For 2 ≤ d ≤ 4 and d ≤ k ≤ b, let BPaG,1(d, k) be a d×k submatrix of BPaG,1(4, b). The CPM-dispersion of BPaG,1(d, k) gives a d×k array HPaG,1(d, k) of CPMs of size (12b+1)×(12b+1), a d(12b+1)×k(12b+1) matrix over GF(2) with column and row weights d and k, respectively. The null space of HPaG,1(d, k) gives a (d, k)-regular QC-BIBD-PaG-LDPC code CPaG,1(d, k) whose Tanner graph has girth at least 6 and possesses the 2×2 CPM-array cycle structure.

VI. CORRECTION OF BURSTS OF ERASURES
In the following, we show that QC-LDPC codes constructed based on the two classes of BIBD-PaGs presented in the last two sections can be used to recover specific types of bursts of erasures over BECs. For explanation, we consider codes associated with class-2 BIBD-PaGs.
For 3 ≤ k ≤ b, let BPaG,2(2, k) be a 2 × k submatrix of the base matrix BPaG,2(5, b) given by (9). The CPM-dispersion of BPaG,2(2, k) gives a 2 × k array HPaG,2(2, k) of CPMs of size (20b + 1) × (20b + 1). The array HPaG,2(2, k) is a 2(20b + 1) × k(20b + 1) matrix with rank 2(20b + 1) − 1 = 40b + 1. The null space of HPaG,2(2, k) gives a (2, k)-regular (k(20b + 1), (k − 2)(20b + 1) + 1) QC-BIBD-PaG-LDPC code CPaG,2(2, k) with 40b + 1 parity-check symbols. The Tanner graph GPaG,2(2, k) of CPaG,2(2, k) has the 2 × 2 CPMarray cycle structure. The subgraph Gsub(2, 2) of GPaG,2(2, k) associated to a 2 × 2 subarray Hsub(2, 2) of HPaG,2(2, k) is composed of a single cycle of length 4(20b+1) with 2(20b+1) VNs and 2(20b + 1) CNs on it.
Let v = (v0, v1, . . . , vk−1) be a codeword in CPaG,2(2, k) which consists of k sections. For 0 ≤ j < k, the j-th section vj consists of 20b + 1 consecutive code symbols in v which correspond to the 20b + 1 columns of the j-th CPM-columnblock in HPaG,2(2, k). We call the sections in v CPM-sections. Two code symbols in v are said to be neighbors if they correspond to two VNs in the Tanner graph GPaG,2(2, k) of CPaG,2(2, k) which are neighbors (connected by a path of length 2 through a CN). It follows from the 2 × 2 CPM-array cycle structure of the Tanner graph that each code symbol in a CPM-section vi has exactly two neighbors in a different CPMsection vj of v. This neighbor structure is referred to as the adjacency structure of the code CPaG,2(2, k).
Suppose that a codeword v in CPaG,2(2, k) is transmitted over a BEC, and in the transmission of v, erasures occur which are confined to CPM-sections of v. A burst with erasures confined to a CPM-section of v is called a CPM-phased erasure-burst. The length of the longest CPM-phased erasureburst is 20b + 1. If all the 20b + 1 code symbols in a CPMsection of v are erased, the erasure-burst is said to be solid; otherwise, it is not solid, i.e., at least one of the code symbols in the CPM-section is not erased. Two CPM-phased erasurebursts are said to be mutually semi-solid if the total number of erasures is 2(20b + 1) − 1 = 40b + 1, i.e., one CPM-phased erasure-burst is solid and the other contains 20b erasures.

It follows from the adjacency structure of CPaG,2(2, k) that there exists at least one erased symbol v in the i-th CPMsection vi which is a neighbor of an un-erased code symbol in the j-th CPM-section vj of v. Hence, there is a row h in HPaG,2(2, k) which checks v as the only erased symbol. Take the inner product of h and v to form a check-sum Σ. Then, Σ contains v as the only unknown. From Σ, we recover v by taking the modulo-2 sum of the un-erased symbols in Σ. After recovering v, we find another erased code symbol which is a neighbor of an un-erased code symbol or the recovered code symbol v in the two CPM-phased erasure-bursts confined to the CPM-sections, vi and vj, of v. We recover this erased code symbol in exactly the same manner as the recovery of the first erased code symbol v. Continue this recovery process until all the erased code symbols in the CPM-sections vi and vj of v are recovered. The erasure recovery process will never fail as long as there is at least one un-erased code symbol in one of the two CPM-phased erasure-bursts. The above erasure recovering process is referred to as the peeling algorithm [6].
A measure of the erasure-correcting performance of a code is defined as the ratio of the maximum number of erasures that the code can recover to the number of parity-check symbols of the code. This ratio is called erasure-correcting efficiency, denoted by η. An erasure-correcting code is said to be optimal if η = 1. Since the maximum number of correctable erasures confined to two CPM-sections of a received vector in the code CPaG,2(2, k) is 40b + 1 which is equal to the number of parity-check symbols of CPaG,2(2, k), the code CPaG,2(2, k) is optimal, i.e., its erasure-correcting efficiency is η = 1. For k = 3, 4, . . . , b, we can construct a sequence of optimal QC-BIBD-PaG-LDPC codes of various lengths and rates for correcting two random mutually semi-solid CPMphased erasure-bursts.
Example 3. Consider the 5 × 32 base matrix BPaG,2(5, 32) of the partial geometry PaG(160, 5, 5) over GF(641) given in Example 2. Suppose we take the first 16 columns of the top two rows of BPaG,2(5, 32) to form a 2 × 16 matrix BPaG,2(2, 16). The CPM-dispersion of BPaG,2(2, 16) gives a 2 × 16 array HPaG,2(2, 16) of CPMs of size 641 × 641 which is a 1282 × 10256 matrix with rank 1281. The null space of HPaG,2(2, 16) gives a (2, 16)-regular (10256, 8975) QCBIBD-PaG-LDPC code CPaG,2(2, 16) with rate 0.8750 which is capable of correcting two random CPM-phased erasurebursts with a number of erasures up to 1281 which is equal to the number of the parity-check symbols of the code. Hence, CPaG,2(2, 16) is optimal in correcting CPM-phased erasurebursts.
Taking the first 8 columns of BPaG,2(2, 16), we obtain a 2×8 matrix BPaG,2(2, 8). The CPM-dispersion of BPaG,2(2, 8) gives a 2 × 8 array HPaG,2(2, 8) of CPMs of size 641 × 641. The null space of HPaG,2(2, 8) gives a (5128, 3847) code which can correct up to 1281 erasures confined to two CPM-sections of a transmitted codeword.

We can also design a code for correcting either random errors and/or two mutually semi-solid CPM-phased erasure-bursts. For example, consider the (4, 32)-regular (20512, 17951) QC-BIBD-PaG-LDPC code constructed in Example 2. The random error-correcting performance of the code is shown in Fig. 1. For correcting CPM-phased erasure-bursts, we can use any two CPM-row-blocks of HPaG,2(4, 32). The code is capable of recovering up to 1281 erasures confined to two CPM-sections of a received vector.
Class-1 Bose-BIBD-PaGs can also be used to construct QCLDPC codes for correcting CPM-phased erasure-bursts in a similar manner as using the class-2 Bose-BIBD-PaGs.
More generally, any RC-constrained parity-check matrix which is an array of CPMs of prime size possesses the 2 × 2 CPM-array cycle structure. The QC-LDPC code with such a parity-check matrix is optimal in correcting CPM-phased erasure-bursts.
VII. CONCLUSION AND REMARKS In this paper, we presented two classes of partial geometries which are derived from two classes of BIBDs with λ = 1 constructed by Bose [1]. Based on these partial geometries, we can construct QC-LDPC codes for correcting random errors over AWGN channels and/or correcting CPM-phased erasurebursts over BECs. Besides the two classes of Bose-BIBDs with λ = 1, there are other classes of BIBDs with λ = 1. Hence, other classes of BIBD-based partial geometries can be constructed. For example, there is a (441, 1470, 40, 12, 1) BIBD [11]. Associated with this BIBD is a partial geometry PaG(40, 12, 12) with 441 points and 1470 lines. Partial geometries can also be constructed from prime fields or cyclic groups of prime orders [10].
