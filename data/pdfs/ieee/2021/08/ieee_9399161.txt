Hardware-Efficient and High-Throughput LLRC Segregation Based Binary QC-LDPC Decoding
Algorithm and Architecture
Anuj Verma , Graduate Student Member, IEEE, and Rahul Shrestha , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—This brief proposes hardware-friendly QC-LDPC decoding algorithm with layered scheduling based on new logarithmic-likelihood-ratio compound (LLRC) segregation technique. Subsequently, we present hardware-efficient QC-LDPC decoder-architecture based on the proposed algorithm and additional architectural optimizations. This decoder has been designed based on the 5G-NR specifications, supporting codelengths and code-rates in the ranges of 26112−10368 bits and 1/3−8/9, respectively. Performance analysis has shown that suggested LLRC-segregation based decoding algorithm delivers adequate FER of 10−5 between 1 to 6.5 dB of SNR range. Furthermore, proposed QC-LDPC decoder is post-route simulated and implemented on the FPGA platform. It operates at a maximum clock frequency of 135 MHz and delivers a peak throughput of 11.02 Gbps. Eventually, comparison with relevant works shows that our decoder delivers 2.2× higher throughput and 8.3× better hardware-efficiency than the state-of-the-art implementations.
Index Terms—Wireless communication, quasi-cyclic lowdensity-parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes, field-programmable gate-array (FPGA), frame error rate (FER), signal-to-noise ratio (SNR).
I. INTRODUCTION
N EAR Shannon-limit performance and lower-decoding complexity with parallelism of LDPC code [1]–[3] have resulted in its prolific adoptions in various wirelesscommunication standards like IEEE 802.11n/16e, DVB-S2X and many more [4]–[5]. Recently, the third generation partnership-project (3GPP) advocated LDPC codes as one of the standard channel codes (for data transmission) in the physical-layer specifications of 5G-NR standard [6]. It must support high-end performance metrics like peak datarate of 10-20 Gbps, latency ≤ 1 ms, spectral efficiency of 15−30 bits/s/Hz, connection density of 106 devices/km2, and bandwidth of 100 MHz [6]. These contemporary standards demand substantial throughput and shorter latency from the implementation perspective of LDPC decoder.

There are various decoding algorithms like min-sum (MS), offset MS (OMS) and normalized MS (NMS) [7]–[9] for the LDPC codes. Comparatively, OMS algorithm incurs lower data congestion and lesser computation complexity among all [10]. LDPC decoder architecture have been classified based on the degree of parallelism viz. fully-parallel architecture that delivers higher throughput [11], partially-parallel architecture targets hardware/energy efficiency [12], and serial architecture is suitable for lower throughput applications [13]. Huge basegraph (BG) matrix and higher code-lengths [14] exacerbates hardware-efficiency and throughput of LDPC decoder. Routing technique proposed in [15] increases the implementation complexity when applied to higher code-lengths of 5G-NR standard. Similarly, parallel routing network and high-speed architecture for LDPC decoder have been reported in [16] and [17], respectively. Recently, layer merging method for small code-length and high-throughput LDPC decoders of 5G-NR standard have been reported in [18] and [7], respectively.
Our work addresses these issues by adopting algorithmic and architectural approaches to design hardware-efficient and high-throughput LDPC decoder for exact high-end 5G-NR specifications. We present an implementation-friendly QC-LDPC layered-decoding algorithm based on new approach of segregating the channel-LLRs into LLRCs (LLRC-segregation technique) that alleviates the data-congestion of LDPC decoder architecture. Additional logical optimizations have been applied while designing hardware-efficient QC-LDPC decoder-architecture based on the proposed decoding algorithm. Further, comprehensive performance analyses of the proposed QC-LDPC algorithm with the existing ones are presented in this brief. The suggested QC-LDPC decoder has been hardware prototyped on FPGA platform and its implementation results are compared with the state-of-the-art contributions.
II. DECODING ALGORITHMS
A. Conventional Algorithm
The QC-LDPC codes are (n, k) linear block codes generated from sparse parity-check-matrix H(n−k)×n where n and k are codeword and information bits, respectively, such that k/n represents code-rate [7]. Such H matrix is an enlarged version of base graph B matrix which has mb = (n − k)/z and nb = n/z rows and columns, respectively, with an expansion factor z [14]. Decoding algorithm for such QC-LDPC codes is based on the belief propagation principle in which noisy channel-LLRs are iteratively processed between check-nodes (CNs) and variable-nodes (VNs) of the Tanner graph to deliver a set of error-free decoded bits X [3]. III. PROPOSED VLSI ARCHITECTURES
This section presents suggested QC-LDPC decoderarchitecture based on the proposed Algorithm 2. Comprehensive and generic VLSI-architecture of the proposed decoder is shown in Fig. 1. It is complaint to 5G-NR specifications where the size mb ×nb of B matrix is variable and pronounced by various standard code-rates [20]. Thereby, the expansion factor z has been quantified based on z = a×2j where a ∈ {2, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, 15} and 0 ≤ j ≤ 7. The maximum value of z is limited to 384 with a = 3 and j = 7, based on 5G-NR standard [6], [20]. In Fig. 1, the primary inputs are n quantized channel-LLRs L = {l1, l2, . . . , ln} which are routed via 1:z de-multiplexer (DeMUX). Its outputs are buffered (using registers) for every z clock-cycles to accumulate z channel-LLRs which are concatenated into LLRC (Dc) with the bit-width of q × z bits where q denotes bit-quantization of each channel LLR. Such Dc LLRC is directly fed to initial-register memory-bank (IRMB), as shown in Fig. 1. Aforementioned process repeat for nb times and performs initialization process as shown in line 2−7 of Algorithm 2 that generates nb LLRCs of V matrix which are stored in IRMB.
1) Memory Selection Router (MSR): The LLRCs of V matrix are read from IRMB and are fed to MSR. It routes V(j) ∀ j ∈ {1, 2, . . . , nb} LLRCs into a specific combination of S(k) ∀ k ∈ {1, 2, . . . , tb} LLRCs. As shown in Fig. 1, MSR consumes 19 parallel multiplexers (MUXes) of various sizes viz. six 2:1 MUXes, five 3:1 MUXes, and single 5:1, 7:1, 12:1, 14:1, 17:1, 20:1, 23:1, and 30:1 MUXes which are based on the proposed LLRCs-selection routing, as schematically shown in Fig. 2(b) for B6×9 matrix. Conversely, conventional architecture (consuming 19 68:1 MUXes [7]) and automatically-synthesized-design (ASD) of MSR require 90% and 21.3% more hardware resources, respectively, compared to the proposed design. This MSR subtask is mathematically represented in (5) which is line 10 in Algorithm 2.

2) CN & VN Processing Unit (CVPU): This module updates CNs and VNs to compute row AP-LLRCs of D matrix, as illustrated in (8) and line 14 of Algorithm 2. Fig. 1 shows that S LLRCs from MSR are subtracted with e extrinsic LLRCs in VN-LLRC updation (VNU) unit to generate mvc LLRCs, as represented in line 12 of Algorithm 2. Here, e LLRCs are the decompressed (by DCom units & integrator [7]) versions of compressed-extrinsic LLRC (CE-LLRC) of (itr-1)th iteration that is read from the extrinsic memory. Subsequently, mvc LLRCs from VNU unit are passed through cyclic-rotational (CR) unit for the permutation of LLRs. These rotated mvc LLRCs from CR unit are fed to check-node updation (CNU) unit, as shown in Fig. 1. It performs CNLLRC computations (from lines 13 in Algorithm 2) to generate mcv CN-LLRCs and a CE-LLRC, which is comprehensively explained in Section III-A. Furthermore, tb updated CNLLRCs and a CE-LLRC are processed by reverse-CR unit that generates reversely rotated mcv LLRCs which updates AP-LLRCs of D matrix, as shown in line 14 of Algorithm 2. Such updation is performed by tb parallel adders that sum reverse-CR mcv and VN-updated mvc LLRCs, as illustrated in Fig. 1. It shows that reverse CR-unit also computes CE-LLRC for itrth iteration and is stored in extrinsic memory.
3) Memory Writing Router (MWR): This unit routes updated AP-LLRCs D(j) ∀ j ∈ {1, 2, . . . , tb} into V matrix based on line 15 of Algorithm 2. Such MWR architecture has been designed using 25 MUXes of various sizes, viz. three 2:1 MUXes, five 3:1 MUXes, three 4:1 MUXes, eight 5:1 MUXes, four 6:1 MUXes, one 7:1 MUX and one 8:1 MUX. It also has 43 direct signal-mappings based on the proposed technique for 5G-NR standard BG-matrix and the schematic illustration from Fig. 2(d). These 25 outputs of MUXes and 43 direct-mapped signals aggregate to 68 output signals from MWR, corresponding to nb = 68 columns of B matrix that is compliant to 5G-NR specifications [20]. In the conventional design [7], such MWR unit requires 68 19:1 MUXes that incurs higher data-congestion and alleviates hardware efficiency. Comparatively, proposed MWR delivers 92.6% and 42.5% better hardware saving with respect to conventional architecture and ASD of MWR, respectively. Eventually, all nb = 68V(j) LLRCs from MWR are stored in IRMB within single clock-cycle, as shown in Fig. 1.

4) Hard-Decision Process: As shown in Fig. 1, the proposed QC-LDPC decoder consumes two clock cycles to generate updated D LLRCs (from line 18 of Algorithm 2) for each layer of B-matrix. Hence, it requires 2 × mb clock cycles for all the B-matrix rows that is equivalent to single decoding iteration. Consequently, the decoding latency is 2 × itrmax×mb clock cycles where itrmax represents total number of iterations. For the 5G-NR specified B46×68 matrix, computations of 46 and 5 layers are needed for minimum and maximum code-rates of 1/3 and 8/9, respectively. Therefore, our decoder consumes 100 and 920 clock cycles for 8/9 and 1/3 code-rates, respectively, to compute final V AP-LLRCs for itrmax = 10 iterations. Eventually, the hard-decision registermemory (HDRM) stores all sign-bits of n LLRs, extracted from the outputs of IRMB, and sequentially delivers hard decoded-bits, as shown in Fig. 1.
A. Hardware-Efficient CNU-Unit Architecture
The proposed CNU-unit architecture is presented in Fig. 3 that processes cyclically-rotated mvc LLRCs which are referred as R(i) ∀ i = {1, 2, . . . , tb}. These tb number of R LLRCs are logically transformed into z LLRCs via splitter [7] and are fed to z parallel min-sum units (MSUs), as shown in Fig. 3. In each MSU, input LLRC is segregated into tb LLRs and are applied to two’s-complement (TC) to sign-magnitude (SM) units. This brief suggests the logically-optimized 5-bits TCSM-unit using conversion unit (ConU) and a 2:1 MUX, as shown in Fig. 3. This ConU processes only 4-bits magnitude because the sign-bit is identical in both TC and SM formats. The simplified Boolean equations for the outputs of ConU are f3 = g¯3|(g¯2 · g¯1 · g¯0); f2 = (g¯3 · g¯2)|(g¯2 · g1)|(g¯2 · g¯0)|(g2 · g¯1 · g¯0); f1 = g1 ⊕ g0; f0 = g0. Such ConU −consuming only 16 gateequivalents (GEs)− replaces 4-bit adder and an inverter in the conventional design [7] that is equivalents to 21 GEs, incurring a saving of 5 GEs in each TCSM unit. This proposed design of MSU have tb TCSM units in each MSU which is replicated z× in overall CNU unit, as shown in Fig. 3. Hence, overall hardware saving in CNU is 5 × (tb) × z GEs due to optimized ConU logic. After the TCSM conversions, signs and magnitudes of all LLRs are fed to sign-unit (SU) and magnitude-unit

(MU), respectively. Here, SU and MU operations are compliant to (7) and lines 13 of Algorithm 2. The MU generates offset-magnitudes of min1 and min2 (represented as Omin1 & Omin2, respectively), and index of min1 (Index) [21], as shown in Fig. 3. Such Omin1 and Omin2 values are consecutively processed by ConU logics and fed to Min1/Min2-Streamer that routes tb-1 replications of Omin1 with a specific pattern of Omin2 via tb:1 MUX, as shown in Fig. 3. The proposed Min1/Min2-Streamer replaces the conventional logic that needs tb units of equalizers and MUXes [7]. Subsequently, all the updated-CN magnitudes and their corresponding signs (from SU) are concatenated, as shown in Fig. 3. These concatenated LLRs from z replicated MSUs are passed through an integrator [7] for reverse-transformation logical operation to compute tb number of CN-updated LLRCs. On the other side, Omin1, Omin2 and Index from MU, and signs from SU are concatenated to generate a compressed CN updation, referred as aggregated-extrinsic LLR (AE-LLR), that enhances the hardware-efficiency of extrinsic memory. Eventually, all the AE-LLRs from z MSUs are concatenated into a CE-LLRC which is applied to reverse-CR unit that is subsequent module of CNU unit in CVPU, as shown in Fig. 1. Therefore, single MSU incurs a hardware saving of 31.6%, and the proposed overall-QC-LDPC decoder consumes 54% and 18% lesser hardware-resources compared to conventional architecture [7] and ASD of decoder, respectively.
