A Low-Complexity Decoder With Non-Uniform Quantization for Recursive BMST Codes
Shengbo Ai and Shancheng Zhao

Abstract— Recursive block Markov superposition transmission (rBMST) can be used to construct high-performance spatially coupled codes. When used in power-limited scenarios, low-complexity quantized decoders are required for the rBMST codes. In this letter, we introduce an efficient decoder for rBMST codes by incorporating non-uniform quantization (NUQ) into the threshold attenuated min-sum algorithm (TAMSA). The resulting decoder is referred to as the NUQ-TAMSA. We use the hierarchical dynamic quantization algorithm to design the non-uniform quantizer. To show the effectiveness of the NUQ-TAMSA, we carry out comprehensive comparisons. Our results show that, for low-resolution quantization, performance gains as large as 0.3 dB can be obtained with the NUQ-TAMSA. Numerical results also show that, for a wide range of coding rates, 5-bits non-uniform quantization is sufficient for NUQ-TAMSA to approach the performances of the floating-point based TAMSA.
Index Terms— Low complexity decoding, min-sum algorithm, non-uniform quantization, recursive block Markov superposition transmission (BMST) codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
F OR certain applications, very low-power communications are required. To achieve this goal, un-coded transmission or coded transmission with hard-decision decoding were preferred [1]. The invention of turbo codes marked the beginning of the modern coding era. Iterative soft-decision decoding is one of the key techniques to the success of modern coding schemes, including low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, turbo codes, spatially coupled codes, and polar codes. To incorporate modern codes into low-power communication systems, we are required to design efficient quantized decoders. The min-sum algorithm (MSA) is the first class of low-complexity decoder for LDPC codes. Since then, various low-complexity decoders had been proposed in the literature, including attenuated MSA (AMSA) [2], weight bit-flip decoder [3], reliability-based decoder [4], and finite alphabet iterative decoder [5]. To lower the error-floors of quantized MSA, a threshold-based MSA was proposed in [6] and [7].
The recently introduced block Markov superposition transmission (BMST) was promising in the construction of high-performance spatially coupled codes [8]. Block-oriented

superposition was used in BMST to achieve spatial coupling. Existing results showed that the BMST codes have low encoding complexity. Furthermore, BMST codes admit lower decoding complexity than spatially coupled LDPC codes [9]. However, to achieve high performance, BMST codes typically require a large encoding memory. Since the decoding complexities of the BMST codes grow quadratically with the encoding memories, the implementation costs of high-performance BMST codes are typically high [10]. To address this issue, recursive BMST (rBMST) codes was proposed in [9]. The rBMST codes share most merits of the BMST codes but require a much smaller encoding memory to achieve comparable performances. As a result, the rBMST codes may be attractive for communication systems with stringent requirements on computational loads. To the best knowledge of the authors, the design of low-complexity decoders for rBMST codes has not been considered in the literature.
Motivated by the above observation, we are concerned with the design of low-complexity min-sum decoders for rBMST codes. Since low-power consumption is required, we only consider quantized decoder with low-resolution quantization. In [11], Ghassemi showed that decoders with non-uniform quantizer performed better than those with uniform quantizer. Based on the principle of maximizing mutual information (MMI), non-uniform quantization was designed for LDPC codes in [12]. When there is a large fraction of degree-2 variable nodes, a novel decoding method based on reconstructioncomputation-quantization was proposed in [13]. Our preliminary results showed that the performance losses of existing low-complexity decoders were noticeable, especially when the coding rates were low.
In this letter, we introduce an efficient decoder for rBMST codes by incorporating non-uniform quantization (NUQ) into the threshold attenuated min-sum algorithm (TAMSA). The resulting decoder is referred to as the NUQ-TAMSA. The hierarchical dynamic quantization (HDQ) algorithm based on MMI is used to design the non-uniform quantizer. The proposed decoder is different from the TAMSA [7] in the following aspects. Firstly, non-uniform quantization is used, which is shown to be indispensable when the quantization resolution is low. Secondly, two thresholds are used in the proposed decoder. Performance comparisons are carried out to show the effectiveness of the NUQ-TAMSA. The comparisons show that, for low-resolution quantization, performance gains as large as 0.3 dB can be obtained with the NUQ-TAMSA. The comparisons also show that, for a wide range of coding rates, 5-bits non-uniform quantization is sufficient for NUQ-TAMSA to approach the performances of the floating-point based TAMSA.

B. Non-Uniform Quantization
For low-power applications, quantized decoders are preferred. Note that in quantized decoders, all messages during decoding are represented by integers of a given width. Similar to finite alphabet iterative decoder (FAID) proposed in [5], quantization limits bit width of the messages of the iterative decoder. As a result, the storage complexity and the computational complexity of quantized decoder are typically lower than those of the floating-point based decoders, but at the expense of slight performance degradation. We point out that, in floating-point based decoders, the messages passed during decoding are real numbers. While in quantized decoder, all messages passed in the decoder are integers, instead of real numbers. For quantized decoder, quantization is performed

Recently, a sequential thresholds searching (STS) algorithm is proposed to construct a hierarchical dynamic quantizer (HDQ). In this letter, we construct the non-uniform HDQ by maximizing the mutual information (MMI). We first partition the real line into a large number of intervals of equal length, except the leftmost

one and the rightmost one. For convenience, these intervals are referred to as the basic intervals. The boundaries of the basic intervals are the possible thresholds of the proposed quantizer. For an interval of the bits quantizer with δ basic intervals, there are δ − 1 ways to partition it into two intervals. C. The Proposed NUQ-TAMSA
In the proposed decoder, the received signals and the messages during decoding are quantized with the same non-uniform quantizer. Note that when incorporating NUQ into the TAMSA, the decay factor α may not work when the number of bits for quantization is small. In the following, we introduce a simple approach to achieve the function of scaling factor α for quantized decoder. For updating at the check nodes, we use two thresholds τ0 and τ1 (τ0 < τ1). Please refer to (6). In the proposed decoder, for a quantized representation x ∈ [τ0, τ1], F (x) is equal to the quantized representation of the neighbouring quantization point of Q−1(x) with magnitude smaller than |Q−1(x)|. It can be seen that, different from the original TAMSA, two thresholds are used in the proposed decoder. Furthermore, scaling is not used in the proposed decoder. Note that τ0 and τ1 may take different values for different quantization schemes. For a given quantization scheme, thresholds τ0 and τ1 can be selected with Monte Carlo simulation.
Note that the operation F at the check nodes can be implemented with a look-up table of size 2 , where is the number of bits for quantization. As an example, we present in Table I the look-up table corresponding to F when 3 bits quantizer is used.
Similar to other quantized decoders, the addition operation at the variable nodes should also be implemented with look-up tables [15], [17]. Straightforward implementation of the look-up tables will costs a large volume of memory. To reduce the required memory, we follow [15], [17] to use tree-based decompositions to compute additions in this letter. We present in Fig. 3 the tree-based decompositions used in this letter, where (a) corresponds to 5 bits quantization and (b) corresponds to 3 and 4 bits quantization. Note that the decomposition of the 3 and 4 bits quantization is different from that of the 5 bits quantization. Typically, for a given quantization, we may need to re-design the decomposition.
V. SIMULATION RESULTS AND COMPLEXITY ANALYSIS
A. Simulation Results
Error performances of rBMST codes when decoded with different decoding algorithms are presented in this subsection. In our simulations, rBMST codes with basic code length L = 1250, encoding memory m = 3, and decoding delay d = 11 are selected. Entropy stopping criterion with a maximum number of iterations Imax = 50 is employed for all decoders.

Fig. 4. Error performances of an rBMST code of rate 0.5 when decoded with different decoders.
We present in Fig. 4 the bit error rates (BER) of a rate half rBMST code when decoded with the proposed decoder with different NUQ and uniform quantizers (UQ). The repetition code C[2500, 1250] is taken as the basic code. For comparison, we also present in Fig. 4 the error performances of this rBMST code when decoded with belief propagation (BP), floating-point based AMSA with α = 0.75, and floating-point based TAMSA with parameters (α = 0.75, τ = 3.0). From Fig. 4, we have the following observations.
1) The proposed decoder with 5 bits non-uniform quantization is sufficient to approach the performance of floating-point based TAMSA.
2) For the proposed decoder, the NUQ-TAMSA with 4 bits quantization performs 0.3 dB better than the TAMSA with 4 bits uniform quantization. Similarly, the NUQ-TAMSA with 3 bits quantization outperforms the TAMSA with 3 bits uniform quantization by about 0.35 dB. For further illustration, we have also presented the performances of the quantized TAMSA with two thresholds in Fig. 4. It can be seen that the TAMSA with two thresholds performs about 0.1 dB better than the original TAMSA. This result confirms the effectiveness of using two thresholds.
3) The performances of the proposed decoder with 4 bits non-uniform quantization are comparable to those of the floating-point based AMSA.
For further illustration, we present in Fig. 5 the error performances of an rBMST code with rate 2/3. The SPC C[1875, 1250] is taken as the basic code. It can be seen from Fig. 5 that observations similar to the rate half example can be obtained. Particularly, the proposed decoder performs better than other quantized decoders and it performs almost as well as the floating-point based TAMSA. Note that the updating rule in (6) is used for the decoding of the SPC.
Remark: Similar to TAMSA, the proposed decoder may not be suitable for fast varying channels due to the use of threshold.
