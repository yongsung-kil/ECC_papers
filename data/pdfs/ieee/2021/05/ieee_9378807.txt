High-Speed LDPC Decoders Towards 1 Tb/s

Meng Li , Veerle Derudder , Kaoutar Bertrand, Claude Desset, Member, IEEE, and André Bourdoux , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract— Beyond 5G systems are expected to approach 1 Tb/s throughput. This poses a significant challenge to the channel decoder. In this paper, we propose a multi-core architecture based on full row parallel layered LDPC decoder with frame interleaving. Compared with conventional partially parallel layered architectures, the proposed architecture increases the throughput by applying frame interleaving into the pipeline architecture and by using multi-core architectures. Two high rate medium size QC LDPC codes are designed with fast decoding convergence speed for this architecture. Both codes are implemented with single core and multi-core architectures to explore different trade-offs between code design, communication performance and implementation. The four decoders are implemented in 16 nm CMOS FinFET technology with a clock rate of 1 GHz. The placement and routing implementation results show that the single core decoder for the LDPC (1027, 856) code is able to provide 114 Gb/s throughput at maximum 3 iterations with an area of 0.173 mm2 and energy efficiency of 1.56 pJ/bit; the multicore decoder for the (1032, 860) code is able to provide 860 Gb/s throughput at maximum 2 iterations with an area of 1.48 mm2 and energy efficiency of 3.24 pJ/bit. The multi-core decoder achieves the highest throughput in the literature for medium size (1-2k) LDPC codes. When compared with other state-ofthe-art fully parallel high speed architectures, the proposed architectures bring a significant gain both in area efficiency and energy efficiency while keeping the ability to offer flexibility in code rate, number of iterations and early stop.
Index Terms— Beyond-5G, THz communications, error control codes, LDPC, decoder, layered decoding.
I. INTRODUCTION
D ATA rates of 10–100 Gb/s are specified in leading industry standards such as the 5G New Radio, the IEEE 802.11ax Wi-Fi standard and the IEEE 802.11ay millimeter wave standard. Data rates between 100 Gb/s and 1 Tb/s are foreseen in beyond-5G (B5G) systems. Forward Error Correction (FEC) is a core component for any digital communication system. At very high data rates, approaching 1 Tb/s, the FEC implementation is critical in terms of power consumption and silicon area. This paper aims at presenting a flexible high speed Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) decoder enabling the 1 Tb/s regime.

LDPC codes [2], [3], [4] have played an important role in the wireless communication domain over the past 20 years. This is due to their near Shannon limit error correction capability and mature hardware implementations. In recent years, many designs of LDPC decoders were published with throughputs of tens of Gb/s. However, the design of an LDPC capable of hundreds of Gb/s or approaching 1 Tb/s, with low power consumption and small silicon area, while maintaining good BER performance and flexibility is still a significant challenge.
A deeply scaled CMOS technology brings significant implementation gains for digital circuit design [5] in terms of speed, area and power consumption. It is a key driver of wireless communication at higher data rates with improved energy and less area. The commercial 16 nm FinFET technology from TSMC [6] claims around 50% speed gain over the 28nm node, for the same power consumption. However, technology scaling alone is not enough to cover the increased throughput requirements. Code design innovations and new architectures are the key elements to make efficient ultra-high speed LDPC decoders. In [7], the authors explored a multidimensional design space for high speed quasi-cyclic (QC) or architecture-aware (AA) LDPC decoders. The design space includes quantization, decoding scheduling, optimization of check node calculation, parallelism extensions, pipeline stage optimization, frame handling, etc. To design a high throughput decoder, the designer should consider these multi-dimensional optimizations and optimize trade-offs between communication performance and implementation metrics depending on the application.

A multi-Gb/s LDPC decoder has already been achieved in 2007 by using a full parallel LDPC decoder [8] for a (4, 15) regular 600-bit LDPC code in 130 nm technology. Routing and congestion problems were claimed at that time. The stateof-the-art CMOS technology is able to handle full parallel architectures for a short code length (≈ 1000). However, as the code length increases to several thousands, long interconnects create routing congestions which result in a large decoder with low area utilization, poor timing and increased power consumption.
For AA-LDPC codes, a partial row-based or column-based parallel architecture is a good alternative solution. It reduces the routing congestion problem while increasing the throughput when compared to a block serial decoding [9]. Stateof-the-art multi-Gb/s LDPC decoders [10], [11] applied a row-based scheme, extending the bit node parallelism to the code length while keeping the check node parallelism equal to the size of the sub-matrix. [12], [13] applied a columnbased scheme, extending the check node parallelism to the check node size and keeping the bit node parallelism equal to the size of the sub-matrix. Both the row-based and columnbased partial parallel architectures significantly increase the throughput while reducing complexity, compared to a full parallel architecture.
The unrolling architecture increases the decoding parallelism in the dimension of the iteration loop, which significantly increases the throughput and opens the door for 100+ Gb/s FEC decoders: 160 Gb/s LDPC decoder in [14], [15] and 588 Gb/s LDPC decoder in [16]. These unrolling architectures increase the decoding parallelism by unrolling the iteration loop of a full parallel two-phase architecture. The dedicated hardware is repeated N times, where N equals the number of iterations. The unrolled architecture performs one iteration per pipeline stage in one cycle hence this pipelined architecture allows the decoder to process one LDPC frame per clock cycle. To support different rates, the connection complexity between check nodes and bit nodes will increase exponentially for the full parallel architecture. The number of iterations is fixed as the number of instantiated fully parallel single-iteration blocks is fixed for the unrolling architecture. Hence, the unrolling architecture significantly increases the throughput but lacks the flexibility in coding rates and early stop capability.
Frame interleaving is an area and energy efficient method to increase processing parallelism for pipelined architectures. In [17] and [18], this idea has been applied for digital filters. In [19] and [20], the authors applied the row splitting method to increase the hardware utilization for layered CCLDPC decoders. In [11] and [13], it was applied to flooding architectures. In [21], the authors applied this idea on layered architectures targeting both FPGA and ASIC applications.
In this paper, we first detail a full row parallel layered LDPC decoder with only three pipeline stages and frame interleaved scheduling. By introducing different frames into the decoding pipelines, both the decoding speed and hardware utilization are significantly increased. Unlike the fully parallel architecture with iteration unrolling, the decoding parallelism is increased by changing message update scheduling and making the best hardware utilization. II. FLEXIBLE FRAME INTERLEAVED LAYERED DECODER
A. LDPC Codes and Layered Decoding Algorithm
A LDPC code is a linear block code defined by a sparse parity check matrix H with size M × N, where N is the code length and M is the number of parity checks. For a Quasicyclic LDPC code, the parity check matrix is described as an Mb × Nb base matrix Hb, with Mb = M/z and Nb = N/z, where z is the size of a cyclic sub-matrix. In Hb, the value −1 represents a zero matrix and the value above −1 represents the shift vale of an identity matrix. The base matrix of one QC-LDPC code used in this paper is illustrated in Fig. 1, where Nb is equal to 24, Mb is equal to 4 and z is equal to 43.
This type of QC-LDPC codes can be decoded by partially parallel Sum-Product Algorithm (SPA) with layered scheduling [23], in which the parallelism of the bit node and check node functional units are equal to the size of sub-matrix, because a group of information messages can be calculated and passed between bit nodes and check nodes by passing through the permutation network simultaneously. The layered decoding for AA-LDPC codes is described in algorithm 1.
For a block serial LDPC decoder, the a posteriori information vector Q n of size z is initialized with input log likelihood ratios (LLRs) llrn and the extrinsic information vector rmn is initialized with zeros. Each iteration consists of Mb sub-iterations. During each sub-iteration, the a posteriori information Q n passes through a permutation network with a shifting value m,n equal to P(m, n), where P(m, n) is indicated in the base matrix Hb. The check nodes utilize the intrinsic information qmn which is obtained by subtracting the extrinsic information rmn from the a posteriori information Q n to calculate the updated extrinsic information by using SPA or simplified min-sum [24], [25] algorithm. B. Frame Interleaved Full Row Parallel Architecture
The full row parallel architecture [7], [10], [11] was mostly used for multi-Gb/s LDPC decoders for short length LDPC codes, which significantly increased the throughput of partially parallel architecture where both the check node and bit node decoding parallelism are equal to z. In a full row parallel layered architecture, the bit node parallelism is equal to the code length N and the check node parallelism is equal to the size of sub-matrix z. Fig. 2 shows the timing of a full row parallel architecture for the layered decoding with 3 pipeline stages for a LDPC codes with 3 rows in the parity check matrix. A circle with number n means that the pipeline is active and is processing data belonging to the nth layer. A white circle without number means an idle pipeline stage. Different from block serial [9] or row split [19], [20] layered decoding architectures, where the pipeline utilization is high. If the bit node parallelism is equal to N, the signal processing of consecutive layers must wait till the current layer finishes a posteriori message updating to guarantee the fast convergence property of the layered decoding. Based on this constraint, the hardware utilization is very low. For a full row parallel architecture with a 3-stage pipeline, the logic hardware utilization is 1/3. In order to increase the throughput, two more frames can be decoded during the idle pipeline stages for the first frame. Fig. 3 illustrates how three frames are interleaved and processed in the 3-stage pipeline [26]. At each clock cycle, the three pipeline stages are all active and are processing layers for three different frames.
The architecture of the proposed frame interleaved full row parallel architecture is quite close to the full row parallel architecture, but with limited overhead of storage elements and

Unlike the full parallel architecture, the parallelism of the check node functional block is equal to z. At the beginning, the whole frame of LLRs of a given codeword is fed into the APP storage. The extrinsic information rmn is read from the extrinsic storage, then the intrinsic information qmn is calculated based on line 7 in Algorithm 1 and stored. In the second pipeline stage, the intrinsic information is first saturated. The z check node processors use offset min-sum algorithm to calculate the minimum with index and the second minimum value among the Nb extrinsic information values. Many papers explored efficient architectures for check node calculation. [27] and [28] proposed a check node calculation optimization based on decoding schedule optimization. [29] and [30] proposed an efficient parallel network for BP based check node calculation. In the proposed architecture, we use an efficient tree-structure based algorithm [32] to simultaneously determine the first two minimum values. For Nb inputs, the processing latency is log(Nb). The detailed check node functional block can be found in [26]. Different from [26], the routing network has been removed because this architecture is instantiated for the LDPC code with single rate. [31] and [33] proposed further simplifications based on [32] for ultra-high speed applications. In the third pipeline stage, both the extrinsic information and the a posteriori information are updated based on line 10 in Algorithm 1 and stored immediately without any permutation. In order to reduce the length of the data path in the third pipeline stage and simplify the architecture, only a single permutation [34] is used for check and bit message exchange in the 3-stage pipeline. The sign of the a posteriori information is permuted back to column order and stored if the decoder successfully decodes a codeword or reaches the maximum number of iterations. A XOR network calculates the syndrome and indicates whether the decoder gets a correct codeword.

The frame interleaved schedule significantly increases the hardware utilization compared to the conventional full row based layered architecture. First, the three frames in the pipeline stages share the same logic hardware. Secondly, the overhead storage to have three frames running in parallel is small. The intrinsic information, the a posteriori information and the check information are calculated on the fly for the frame processed in pipeline stages 1, 2, and 3, respectively, and only the information belonging to the current processing frame is stored. Only the extrinsic information and the decoded codewords of the 3 frames running in the pipeline stages need to be stored separately, which are indicated in Fig. 4 with symbol F1, F2 and F3. A detailed storage element analysis for the full row parallel layered architecture without and with frame interleaving is illustrated in Table I, in which the data is based on the decoder for LDPC code (1032, 860) from Fig. 1. For each layer, the check node needs 4 bits for the minimum and 5 bits for its index, 4 bits for the second minimum and 24 bit for the sign of 24 intrinsic information values. The onthe-fly calculation method saves 33% storage when compared to replicating all the storage three times.

D. ASIC Implementation Results
The 0.8V TSMC 16nm CMOS logic FinFET Plus 9 track standard cell libraries have been used for implementation. The LDPC decoders have been synthesized with the Cadence Genus tool. Physical implementation was done with the Cadence Innovus tool following all recommendations in terms of layout, power and timing signoff. The power has been estimated by event-driven gate-level simulations. LLR stimuli at Eb/N0 of 3 dB and 6 dB with and without early stop configuration have been generated. After Placement and Routing (P&R), the post-layout Verilog netlist has been simulated with SDF (Standard Delay Format) delay-based full timing annotation to obtain the switching activity information. Finally, power estimation has been performed using the database from place and route and the annotated switching activity information

from gate level simulation. Power estimations have been done under typical operating conditions (0.8V and 25 ◦C) and the power reported is the total power including the leakage power. The clock rate is 1 GHz.
The key placement and routing implementation results for the two decoders are summarized in Table III. The core utilization for the (1027, 856) and (1032, 860) codes are 49% and 53%, respectively. The power numbers are estimated with LLRs created at Eb/N0 of 3 dB and 6 dB, with and without early stop. Taking the decoder for the (1027, 856) code with maximum 4 iterations as example, the power at 3 dB without and with early stop is 523 mW and 432 mW, respectively. Its power at 6 dB without and with early stop is 425 mW and 137 mW, respectively. As the waterfall in BER curves of Fig. 6 and Fig. 7 starts around 3 dB, where most of the frames could not be decoded within the maximum number of iterations, the data is continuously toggling in the power observation window. At 6 dB, the power is reduced even without early stop, because both the sign and absolute value of the variables are toggling less at high SNR. The power estimated at 6 dB with early stop is significantly reduced because pipeline is clock gated when the frame running in the pipeline gets a correct codeword. Based on Fig. 6 and Fig. 7, when the decoder is configured for fewer iterations, there is less than 0.05 dB performance degradation for the fixed point simulation at a BER equal to 10−6. The throughput of the (1032, 860) decoder working at 2 iterations is 108 Gb/s. Its power at 6 dB with 2 iterations is only 254 mW. The throughput for the (1027, 856) decoder working at 3 iterations is 114 Gb/s. Its power at 6 dB with 3 iterations is only 178.7 mW. Taking the throughput into account, the (1027, 856) decoder achieves the best energy efficiency of 1.56 pJ/bit.

F. Architecture Extension for Other Codes
The architecture proposed in this section inherits the flexibility of the layered architecture and can be applied to other QC-LDPC codes. Based on the implementation results

reported above and in [26], three conclusions can be drawn for the codes selection. First, the code length of the QC-LDPC codes should not exceed a few thousands. The current EDA tools and computational resources are pushed to the limit when handling such architecture for long code length. Second, the architecture with additional routing network in check node calculation is able to support different code rates which has already been demonstrated in [26]. However, the throughput scales linearly with one over the number of rows in the parity check matrix, shown in Equ. 1. The throughput for low rates is slow compared with high rates.Combining superimposed layers or orthogonal layers after row or column re-organization is the solution to increase the throughput for low coding rate [27]. Third, this architecture is applicable for a sub-set of the rate compatibility 5G NR LDPC codes, in which the code length needs to be at most a few thousands. Thanks to the orthogonality property of the layers in two parity check matrices of 5G NR codes [38], several layers in the quasi row orthogonal part and pure row orthogonal part of the parity check matrix [39] could be superimposed to increase the throughput. [40] and [41] detail the solutions to support flexibility for different sizes of sub-matrix, coding rates and code length for layered architectures, which would be used for this architecture as well.
III. MULTI-CORE IMPLEMENTATIONS
A. Architecture
In order to achieve Tb/s decoding speed, there are many possible optimization directions: increasing the decoding parallelism, reducing the decoding latency, increasing the clock speed, increasing the code length, etc. For medium size (around 1-2k) LDPC codes, we propose a multi-core (MC) architecture based on the frame interleaved row parallel layered decoder to approach 1 Tb/s throughput.

The decoding latency Q of the proposed frame interleaved row parallel layered decoder is calculated by: Niteration ∗ Nlayer ∗ Clayer . In order to obtain a simple and streaming mode IO, one frame of LLRs is fed into the decoder in each cycle. The number of cores P is determined by Niteration ∗ Nlayer ∗ Clayer /3, where 3 is equal to the number of pipeline stages proposed in the single core architecture. In such a case, the multi-core decoder is able to process one frame per clock cycle with a decoding latency of Q + 2. The IO timing of the multi-core decoder at full decoding speed is illustrated in Fig. 8, in which P equals 3 and D is 9. Three consecutive frames are allocated to a single core.
The top level of the multi-core architecture is shown in Fig. 9. The top level and the cores use the same clock. As each core has 3 pipeline stages and 3 frames are interleaved, the input/output DEMUX/MUX transfers groups of 3 consecutive frames to the individual cores. The control signal of the (DE)MUX is derived from two counters CNT3 and CNTP. Counter CNT3 is triggered by the input enable signal and it triggers counter CNTP once it reaches three. The CNTP counter controls the DEMUX and transfers three consecutive frames to a certain core. When each frame has finished decoding, either after maximum number of iterations or stopped earlier when the syndrome is detected as zero, each codeword is stored. In order to keep a simple IO schedule, the three codewords of each core are fetched consecutively only when the single core decoder reaches the maximum number of iterations. A D cycles delayed input enable signal controls a counter CNT3 at the output. This CNT3 counter triggers counter CNTP every three cycles. The CNTP counter in turn controls the output MUX and fetches the three codewords from each single core. The multi-core architecture is also able to support different throughput values easily by changing the configured maximum iterations. At the output, the length of the delay line will be selected based on the selected maximum number of iterations.

IV. CONCLUSION
An LDPC decoder approaching 1 Tb/s was proposed to achieve the highest decoding throughput for medium code length codes at high energy efficiency. The proposed decoder is a multi-core architecture based on frame interleaved full row parallel layered decoding. The frame interleaved schedule highly increases the hardware utilization and decoding throughput while keeping all necessary flexibility in coding rate, maximum iteration selection and early stop. Both singlecore and multi-core architectures have been implemented for two 1k LDPC codes with coding rate of 5/6. The placement and routing results in 16nm FinFET show the single core provides a throughput close to 100 Gb/s with energy efficiency better than 2 pJ/bit and an area smaller than 0.2 mm2. The multi-core provides 860 Gb/s throughput with energy efficiency of 3.24 pJ/bit and an area of 1.48 mm2. Both the single core and multi-core implementation results provide significant gain in area efficiency and energy efficiency compared with state-of-the-art architectures. The proposed architecture is suitable for medium code size QC-LDPC with high check node degree of layer or super-imposed layers for low rates. Based on the implementation results of two LDPC codes, it is clear that the number of rows in the parity check matrix and the maximum check node degree impact both the throughput and power consumption. Minimizing those two parameters while keeping good communication performance is an interesting path to follow in order to design future LDPC codes suitable for efficient implementation. Keeping the level of orthogonality of different layers in the parity check matrix is also of high interest for low rate LDPC codes. This paper demonstrates that efficient implementation of Tb/s coding throughput for B5G systems is possible with 16 nm FinFET CMOS technology.
