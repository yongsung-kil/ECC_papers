AbstractтАФMilitary communication systems naturally have 
strong requirements concerning the reliability and the 
robustness of their physical layer data transmission schemes. 
Modern channel coding and modulation schemes can meet these 
requirements in general, but their detection and decoding at the 
receiving end requires complex and power demanding high-
precision implementations of digital algorithms, which are often 
not suitable for military applications. This motivates to explore 
novel techniques to build simple detection and decoding 
algorithms with good performance. In this article, we present 
novel results on the recent idea of using a machine learning 
framework termed the Information Bottleneck method to 
replace demanding implementations of the sum-product 
algorithm with very simple quantized message passing schemes. 
We provide a novel explanation, which links the Information 
Bottleneck decoder processing to the sum-product algorithm. 
Moreover, we present a novel Information Bottleneck 
demodulation scheme for quadrature amplitude modulation 
and discuss special advantages of the Information Bottleneck 
system design approach for military applications.

I. INTRODUCTION AND MOTIVATION 
TodayтАЩs military operations require reliable and robust 
digital communication services for their success, as these 
services are key for the information exchange on the tactical 
and the strategic level. Typically, military communication 
services require high robustness and reliability of the 
communication link. Moreover, more and more military 
applications require high data throughputs, for example, to 
establish real time and high quality simultaneous video and 
audio links. The aforementioned demands have to be 
addressed by the application of modern physical layer 
technologies, for example, powerful forward error correcting 
channel codes and higher order modulation schemes. 
Unfortunately, the corresponding detection and decoding 
algorithms at the receiving end are often based on the sum-
product algorithm [1, 2] and their implementations are power 
demanding and complex, hence resulting in bottlenecks in the 
receiver implementation [3]. These bottlenecks especially 
affect military radios carried in the field which are typically 
battery supplied, but require a very long autonomous 
operating time without recharging in military missions. As a 
result, the development of reduced complexity physical layer 
algorithms for detection and channel decoding is of special 
interest for military applications.  
The number of bits used for the signal representation in 
detection and decoding algorithms, also known as the bit 
width, directly relates to their power consumption and their 
implementation complexity [3]. While low bit widths are 
desirable from an implementation perspective, they typically 
offer worse performance than high-precision implementations 
of the optimum signal processing algorithms. A natural trade-
off exists between the bit width used and the resulting 
detection and decoding performance in conventional detection 
and decoding algorithms.  
Several recent works [4-12] consider an information 
centric design method for detection and decoding algorithms 
with low bit widths. A common idea applied in these works is 
to design signal processing algorithms that aim for the 
maximum preservation of mutual information under 
quantization, that is, for a given bit width per sample in the 
hardware. On an information theoretical level, this design 
approach is an instance of the Information Bottleneck method 
introduced by Tishby et al. in [13]. This information 
theoretical method has its origins in machine learning, where 
it has been successfully applied to an enormous number of 
applications already. The aforementioned communications 
related works [4-12] describe applications of several variants 
of Information Bottleneck like design approaches to quantized 
receiver algorithms for detection and channel decoding. All of 
these papers focus on specific applications of the principle, for 
example, the decoding of low-density parity-check (LDPC) 
codes [4-9] and polar codes [10] and the design of channel 
output quantizers and detection algorithms [11, 12]. 
Interestingly, they have in common that the resulting receiver 
algorithms can compete with high-precision implementations 
of the corresponding state-of-the-art signal processing 
algorithms while using very low bit widths, typically between 
four and five bits per sample. This paper continues our 
preliminary works [7, 8] and [12] on detection and decoding 
with the Information Bottleneck method.  
Concisely, the novel contributions of this article are: 
- 
We present a novel Information Bottleneck based 
quantized demodulator for quadrature amplitude 
modulation (QAM) and study its performance in an 
LDPC encoded transmission.  
- 
We explain the relationship between Information 
Bottleneck detection and decoding and the sum-
product algorithm.  
- 
We illustrate that the Information Bottleneck design 
approach for demodulation and channel decoding 
schemes is very generic.   
- 
We discuss why the Information Bottleneck design 
approach is particularly interesting for military 
communication systems.

A key fact is that in the Information Bottleneck based

considered during decoding, but only for the design of

reduced 
mentionably in comparison to the sum-product algorithm, 
which requires the processing of real valued messages and to 
perform very complex factor node updates. 
 
A key message we want to deliver in this paper is that the 
described process to replace the sum-product algorithm is 
generic and can be applied to replace too demanding high-
precision implementations of the sum-product algorithm with 
simple integer message passing schemes that only require 
lookup operations in a generic manner. The existing literature 
already describes this technique for LDPC [4-9] and polar 
decoding [10], channel estimation and detection [11, 12]. In 
the next section, we apply it to a novel demodulation scheme 
for a QAM signal and again highlight the connection to the 
sum-product algorithm to augment our argumentation. We 
also illustrate the remarkable performance of the resulting 
message passing algorithm with very low bit widths.

V. MILITARY ASPECTS OF INFORMATION BOTTLENECK 
DETECTION AND DECODING 
The Information Bottleneck approach for signal 
processing in detection and decoding could be very 
interesting for military applications, especially for mobile 
and handheld radios. Typically, such radios have severe 
energy and power consumption constraints, as they require a 
long autonomous operating time. At the same time, they have 
to use state-of-the-art channel coding and modulation 
schemes that require costly processing for detection and 
decoding to meet range, reliability and throughput 
requirements on the physical layer. The digital baseband

processing for detection and channel decoding is the main 
bottleneck in digital radio receivers [3]. Lowering the bit 
width used for the signal representation has a drastical impact 
on the routing complexity and the area and power efficiency 
of a digital baseband receiver on a chip. As it has been 
illustrated in this article, the Information Bottleneck approach 
to signal processing is a very flexible approach to replace 
demanding high-precision implementations of the sum-
product algorithm with low bit width algorithms, without 
suffering from mentionable performance losses. Hence, it 
could help to increase the autonomous operating time of 
mobile radio devices in military applications.  
Another aspect that we consider in this context is that 
today software defined radios are very important for military 
missions, as modern software defined waveforms can provide 
powerful features and the flexibility required to adapt to 
changing tactical scenarios. The lookup tables designed with 
the Information Bottleneck method are constructed once and 
offline and then stored and used mismatched to their actual 
design parameters. As it was illustrated, they can replace 
detection and demodulation algorithms and channel decoding 
operations with remarkable performance, anyway. On a 
software defined radio, it is very easy to change the signal 
processing chain with Information Bottleneck components by 
just loading a novel set of pre-generated lookup tables. 
Hence, the approach is very practical for software defined 
radios. In our preliminary work [9], we have presented an 
implementation of an LDPC decoder on a software defined 
radio development platform and illustrated significant 
throughput gains in comparison to conventional decoding 
methods. Hence, we are of the opinion that the signal 
processing with the Information Bottleneck method could be 
beneficial for software defined radios that become more and 
more important in military applications. 
Finally, we would like to stress that the general method to 
replace too demanding implementations of the sum-product 
algorithm could also be interesting for other military 
applications, for example, in distributed networks [18]. 
VI. CONCLUSION 
We have proposed a novel demodulation and decoding 
scheme for LDPC encoded QAM signals and studied its 
performance. The proposed scheme offers performance close 
to that of a double precision floating point reference system 
with belief propagation decoding and even outperforms one 
with min-sum decoding while using very low bit widths 
between four and six bits per sample. In the designed system 
no complex signal processing operations, but only lookup 
operations delivering integers are required. In addition, we 
provided a vivid interpretation of the resulting integer and 
lookup operations and compared them with the sum-product 
algorithm in illustrative examples. Finally, we have discussed 
aspects, which make the proposed Information Bottleneck 
signal processing for detection and decoding particularly 
interesting for military applications. From the results 
presented, we draw the conclusion that further research on 
Information Bottleneck based signal processing in the 
military context is promising.
