Segmented Reconfigurable Cyclic Shifter for QCLDPC Decoder
Hing-Mo Lam, Silin Lu, Hezi Qiu, Hailong Jiao, Min Zhang, and Shengdong Zhang School of Electronic and Computer Engineering, Peking University Shenzhen Graduate School, Shenzhen, China
Email: zhangsd@pku.edu.cn

Abstract—In various wireless communication standards, such as Wi-Fi, WiMAX, and 5G standards, QC-LDPC decoders are required to decode a codeword of variable length. Part of the decoder is in idle if the length of codeword is not the maximum. A reconfigurable cyclic shifter is a key element of a QC-LDPC decoder. If the shifter can only shift one input at a time, the QCLDPC decoder can only decode one codeword at a time as well. A segmented reconfigurable cyclic shifter is proposed in this paper to enable a QC-LDPC decoder to parallelly decode multiple codewords. The proposed shifter can be reconfigured into multiple segments of different sizes. Each segment can perform a cyclic shift of different shift values independently. Therefore, the proposed shifter can enhance the QC-LDPC decoder to decode multiple codewords parallelly by inputting another codeword (or codewords) to re-activate the idle hardware. The test chip of QCLDPC decoder with the proposed segmented reconfigurable cyclic shifter has been fabricated in 0.18-μm CMOS technology which can parallelly decode six codewords.
Keywords—5G, segmented cyclic shifter, codeword-parallel QC-LDPC decoder
I. INTRODUCTION
QC-LDPC code has been adapted as a part of standard in various wireless communication systems, such as Wi-Fi [1-2], WiMAX [3], and 5G [4]. All these wireless communication systems require QC-LDPC decoder to decode codewords with variable length. The length of the codeword is linearly proportional to the lifting parameter of the QC-LDPC code. The decoding process of QC-LDPC decoder is carried out by iteratively passing a bundle of messages between check node cluster and variable node cluster. The bundle of messages is cyclically or anti-cyclically shifted before passing between the check node cluster and variable node cluster.
The multi-way cyclic shift network is proposed in [5] which can only support Wi-Fi and WiMAX standards because only three choices of lifting parameter, 27, 54, and 81, are provided. The designs in [6] is constructed based on the Benes network. The disadvantage of this approach is the long delay because there are more logic levels between input and output than other solutions. The design in [6] can support 5G standard. 51 different selections of lifting parameter between 2 and 384 are supported. However, the controller is complicated because the control signals for a huge number of switch elements need to be generated in real time. Solutions [7-8] are built from a barrel shifter. Two shifters are employed, one shifts the input data to the right and another one shifts to the

left. Then a combiner combines the outputs of the two shifters to form cyclic shifted output of input data with variable data size. However, they can only perform a cyclic shift of one input data of variable size at a time. Part of hardware stays in idle if the input data size is not the maximum. The extended barrel-shifter design in [9-11] can perform cyclic shift of multiple input data, yet the data size and shift value must be the same.
In this paper, a segmented reconfigurable cyclic shifter is proposed. The proposed shifter can be divided into multiple segments of different sizes by separating the control signals of each path and re-group them together, thereby enabling the QC-LDPC decoder to parallelly decode multiple codewords.

II. TRADITIONAL RECONFIGURABLE CYCLIC SHIFTER
An example of a traditional reconfigurable cyclic shifter which supports the maximum input data size (ZMAX) up to 8 is shown in Fig. 1. Input IN[4:0] with input data size (Z[2:0]) equal to 5 is assigned to input X[4:0] to perform cyclic shift of 3 positions to the right (CSR[2:0] = 3). The approach of the traditional reconfigurable cyclic shifter is illustrated in Fig. 2a. The cyclic shifter consists of a left shifter which shifts data to the right, a right shifter which shifts data to the left, and a combiner which combines the outputs of both shifters to form the cyclic shifted output of any input data size. Since the input data size (Z[2:0] = 5) is smaller than the maximum input data size (ZMAX = 8), the input X[7:5] and output Y[7:5] are just ignored in the traditional design. The related circuitry is idle during the cyclic shift operation. As shown in Fig. 2a, a logarithmic shifter is selected for the realization of the left and right shifters. Both logarithmic shifters consist of K stages.

K = ceil(log2ZMAX).

(1)

When ZMAX is 8, K is 3. One advantage of the logarithmic shifter is that the K-bit shift value is the natural control signals for the K stages of the shifter. For example, the control signals of Stages 1, 2, and 3 of the left logarithmic shifter are SR_1, SR_2, and SR_3, respectively. SR_1, SR_2, and SR_3 are equal to the values of Bits 0, 1, and 2 of the shift right value (SR[2:0]), respectively. Namely, SR_1 = SR[0], SR_2 = SR[1], SR_3 = SR[2]. The control signals of three stages of the right logarithmic shifter can be determined in the same manner.

III. PROPOSED SEGMENTED RECONFIGURABLE CYCLIC SHIFTER
This section starts with presenting the “path” concept of a logarithmic shifter, then followed by presenting design details of two versions of the proposed segmented reconfigurable cyclic shifters through examples.

Fig. 2. (a) An example design of a traditional reconfigurable cyclic shifter; (b) An example of a path (Path-7) of the right shifter; (c) An example of two-toone selection circuit of Stage 1 Path 0 of the left shifter.

A. The “path” concept of a logarithmic shifter
The realization of the proposed segmented reconfigurable cyclic shifter is based on the “path” concept of a logarithmic shifter. The traditional design cannot divide a logarithmic shifter into multiple segments due to the lack of the “path” concept. The traditional design applies the same set of control signals to all paths of the shifter. The proposed design separates the control signal of each path, and then re-groups the consecutive paths to form a segment by applying the same set of control signals to all paths in the same segment. Therefore different segment can perform different shift values of different input sizes independently. There is an example of a “path" in Fig. 2b, which is the Path 7 of the right logarithmic shifter in Fig. 2a. The set of control signals of Path 7 are the control signals for Stages 1, 2, and 3 of Path 7, which are SL_1, SL_2, and SL_3 in Fig. 2b.
B. Base version of segmented reconfigurable cyclic shifter
The example of the proposed design is illustrated in Fig. 3 and Fig. 4. It is an extension of the example in Fig. 1 and Fig. 2. Recall the example in Fig. 2a, the input data size Z = 5, less than the maximum input data size ZMAX = 8. Therefore, a portion of the cyclic shifter stays in idle during the process of cyclic shift operation. For example, Paths 5, 6, and 7 of the left and right shifters are idle. Input X[7:5] and output Y[7:5] are

thereby ignored. These idle paths now have been re-activated by feeding the second input data IN2[2:0] in Fig. 3. The size of the second input data (Z2) is 3, which needs to be performed cyclic shift 2 positions to the right (CSR2[2:0] = 2). The detailed implementation is shown in Fig. 4. In Fig. 4, the proposed design uses different sets of control signals for different paths. Therefore, the control signals of Stages 1, 2, and 3 of eight paths of the left and right shifters become SR_1[7:0], SR_2[7:0], SR_3[7:0], SL_1[7:0], SL_2[7:0], and SL_3[7:0], respectively. Both the left and right logarithmic shifters are divided into two segments (Segment 1 and Segment 2) with size 5 and 3, respectively, by re-grouping the set of control signals of Path 0 to 4 together to form Segment 1, path 5 to 7 together to form Segment 2. In the example in Fig. 4, red color is used for Segment 1, while green color is used for Segment 2. In order to have a complete cyclic shift function, the control signal of the combiner (S[7:0]) is also re-grouped in the same way. The calculation of the control signals of two shifters of Segment 2 is in the same manner as the example in Fig. 2 by following (2) – (3). After considering the offset of Segment 2, (4) needs to be modified as
S[i] = 1, if i < (SL[2:0] + segment_offsetn) for i = 0,1,.,7. (5)
segment_offsetn is the index of the lowest path of Segment n. In this example, n equals 2 and segment_offset2 equals 5.

C. Alternative sectioned version of segmented reconfigurable cyclic shifter
In 5G standard, the lifting parameter of QC-LDPC decoder (or the input data size of the cyclic shifter) is one of 51 selectable values between 2 and 384. According to (1), the left and right logarithmic shifters have 9 stages. It is too hardware demanding for the controller to prepare control signals of 9 stages of 384 paths of two shifters. The number (Num_CS) of the control signals of two shifters is

Num_CS = stage# × path# × shifter#

= 9 × 384 × 2 = 6912.

(6)

To tradeoff between the flexibility and the hardware complexity, the alternative sectioned version of the segmented reconfigurable cyclic shifter is proposed in Fig. 5, which divides 384 paths of two logarithmic shifters into 24 sections. Each section occupies 16 paths. All paths within the same section use the same set of control signals. The total required number (Num_CS’) of the control signals of two shifters is significantly reduced to

Num_CS’ = stage# × section# × shifter#

= 9 × 24 × 2 = 432.

(7)

Note that the sectioned version of segmented shifter can only simultaneously perform cyclic shift of at most 24 inputs. In this case, the size of each input must equal to or less than 16. If the size of input is less than 16, the un-used portion of a section is wasted due to the same reason as the traditional design. If the size of input is larger than 16, multiple successive sections can be combined to form a bigger segment to perform cyclic shift just by applying the same set of control signals to all sections within the segment.

The example of the sectioned version of segmented shifter is illustrated in Fig. 5, which contains a controller and a shifter core. The shifter core includes two logarithmic shifters and a combiner. In Fig. 5, ‘i’ is path index (from 0 to 383), ‘j’ is the section index (from 0 to 23), ‘n’ is the segment index (starts from 1) as well as the index of input data.

In the example, the input data are IN1, IN2, …, INN with input data sizes (Zn) of 10, 24, …, 24, respectively. They perform cyclic shift right (CSRn) 4, 6, …, 14 positions, respectively. The proposed sectioned version of segmented shifter is divided into Segments 1, 2, …, N according to the inputs. Segment 1 includes Section 0. Segment 2 includes Sections 1 and 2. Segment N includes Sections 22 and 23. The segments and inputs between 2 and N are skipped. Un-used section(s) outside a segment is allowed in the design. IV. COMPARISON AND TEST CHIP MEASUREMENT
The comparison of delay and area with other state-of-thearts is shown in Table I. The comparison is carried out based on the requirement of 5G standard that the maximum supported input data size is 384. The delay of the reconfigurable cyclic shifter is roughly proportional to the number of logic levels between input and output. The area is counted as the total number of MUXs used for the implementation of the shifter. The proposed design has 10 levels of logics, same as [7] and [9]. Although the proposed design needs a greater number of MUXs for implementation, it is the only design which can support parallelly cyclically shift multiple inputs of different data size and different shift value.

A test chip of QC-LDPC decoder with the proposed sectioned version of segmented reconfigurable cyclic shifter is fabricated in a 0.18-μm CMOS technology with size of 3.1 mm × 3.6 mm. The shifter in the test chip realized 96 paths. The QC-LDPC decoder supports lifting parameter up to 96. The design can be easily extended to 384 without loss of generality. The test chip includes two sectioned version of segmented shifters. One is for decoding process, while another one is for Early Termination module to terminate the decoding process once find the valid codeword.
The measurement setup of the test chip is shown in Fig. 6a. The average current 63 mA is measured by repeatedly inputting four codewords into the test chip from FPGA board at 40 MHz and simultaneously decoding four codewords at 100 MHz. The four codewords are with lifting parameters 12, 24, 24, and 12. The expected iteration numbers to complete decoding are 1, 3, 3, and 1, respectively. The DONE output is asserted only when four segments (or four codewords) trigger the early termination condition. The waveform captured by the oscilloscope in Fig. 6a shows that the DONE output is asserted after 47.6 μs of the START pulse. The photograph of the test chip and experimental setup are shown in Fig. 6b.
V. CONCLUSION
The ability of QC-LDPC decoder to decode multiple small codewords in parallel is critical for 5G base stations to support more low-traffic devices and reduce overall hardware costs. A segmented reconfigurable cyclic shifter, which is realized by separating the control signals of different paths of the logarithmic shifter and then recombining them together to form a segment, is proposed in this paper. By using this shifter, QCLDPC decoder, which can decode multiple codewords of different lengths (or lifting parameters) simultaneously. The test chip of the QC-LDPC decoder with the proposed sectioned version of segmented reconfigurable cyclic shifter is fabricated in a 0.18-μm CMOS technology and successfully demonstrates decoding six codewords simultaneously.
