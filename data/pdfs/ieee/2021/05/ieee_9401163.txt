High Energy-Efficient LDPC Decoder with AVFS 
System for NAND Flash Memory 
 
Chao Zhang, Jingtong Mo, Zihan Lian, Weifeng He 
School of Microelectronics, Shanghai Jiao Tong University, Shanghai, China 
Email: hewf@sjtu.edu.cn 
 
Abstract—In 
conventional 
Low-Density 
Parity-Check 
(LDPC) decoders, the real-time processing performance should 
meet its maximum decoding iterations for all packets and the 
work frequency or supply voltage is always fixed at a high level, 
which decreases its energy efficiency. In this paper, an energy-
efficient LDPC decoding architecture with an adaptive voltage-
frequency scaling (AVFS) scheme is presented. According to the 
usage of input packet FIFO related to variable decoding 
iterations, the architecture can dynamically adjust decoder’s 
work frequency and supply voltage to reduce the processing 
energy while meeting its real-time processing requirement. 
Finally, the decoder is implemented with 28 nm CMOS process. 
Experimental results show that our decoder has a throughput of 
1590 Mb/s when the raw bit error rate (RBER) of Flash memory 
is up to 10-2. The power consumption of the decoder can be 
reduced by 25%-62% and energy efficiency can be increased to 
1.3-2.5 times under different AWGN noise. 
Keywords—QC-LDPC, Decoder architecture, AVFS, energy-
efficient. 
I. INTRODUCTION 
 NAND Flash is widely used in portable devices such as 
mobile phone, digital camera and tablet computer for its large 
storage capacity and low power consumption [1]. However, 
high-capacity NAND Flash memory, which employs multi-
level cell (MLC) technique, suffers higher error rate and lower 
reliability [2]. Recently, Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) 
coding technique has gained a large attention to reduce the bit 
error rate of MLC based NAND Flash [3]. Compared to 
conventional BCH codes, LDPC codes with soft-decision 
decoding show much superior performance of error correcting.  
 Quasi-Cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes [4] are essential 
branches of LDPC codes because their regular cyclic structure 
can significantly reduce hardware complexity. The QC-LDPC 
soft-decision decoding needs iterative computation, but the 
iteration cycles are variable for each packet. Although many 
teams have designed  QC-LDPC decoders [5][6][7] with high 
throughput and less hardware overhead, they all fix the supply 
voltage and work frequency of the decoder  at a high level to 
meet maximum iterations. As a result, if actual decoding 
iteration does not reach the maximum, additional energy will 
be consumed, which is unsuitable for NAND Flash that 
expects low power consumption.  
 In this paper, we have designed a rate-0.89 (9216, 8192) 
QC-LDPC code and implemented a decoder with Adaptive 
Voltage-Frequency Scaling (AVFS) system in 28 nm CMOS 
technology. The AVFS system can dynamically adjust the 
supply voltage and work frequency of the decoder according 
to the usage of input packet FIFO. Finally, the experimental 
results show that our decoder has a throughput of 1590 Mb/s 
when the raw bit error rate of Flash memory is up to 10-2. 
Moreover, the power consumption is reduced by 25%-61% 
and energy efficiency can be increased to 1.3-2.5 times of the 
original after applying AVFS scheme.  
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. Section Ⅱ  
firstly reviews the basic theory of decoding algorithm for QC-
LDPC codes. Section Ⅲ presents an overview of proposed 
AVFS decoder, including its working mechanism and circuit 
architecture. Section Ⅳ provides the simulation results and 
comparison with other work. Section Ⅴ concludes this article.

III. PROPOSED  DECODER WITH AVFS SYSTEM 
A. AVFS System Overview  
As shown in Fig. 3, the whole AVFS system consists of 3 
main blocks: voltage and clock generator, main circuit and 
external interface. Firstly, the voltage and clock generators 
aim to generate variable voltage and clock for the main circuit. 
Secondly, the main circuit includes the conventional LDPC 
decoder and our added AVFS FSM whose function is to 
control the DC-DC converter to generate expected supply 
voltage. Finally, two asynchronous FIFOs are designed as 
data interfaces with external circuits. One of them is used to 
store the codewords output from the decoder. The other aims 
to receive initial LLR and feedback FIFO’s usage to AVFS 
system. FIFO’s usage is measured by FIFO’s remaining 
depth that refers to the difference between the amount of data 
written in the FIFO and the number of data already read from 
FIFO. The overall system can be divided into two clock 
domains. The read clock of output FIFO and the write clock 
of soft message FIFO share the same clock whose frequency 
is fixed at 200 MHz, and the rest of the circuits shares a global  
clock that can be adjusted by AVFS system. 
Before the start of each decoding iteration, the AVFS 
FSM calculates an expected FIFO’s remaining depth 
according to current work information (the clock frequency 
and executed decoding iterations). Then FSM compares it 
with actual remaining depth to select next supply voltage. The 
output voltage of the DC-DC converter is sent to the main 
circuit and adaptive clock generator respectively. The 
adaptive clock generator can generate clock signals of 
different frequencies according to different voltages. Fig. 4 
shows detailed timing diagrams of AVFS system’s 
mechanism. When FIFO’s remaining depth exceeds expected 
value, it means that the processing speed is less than the input 
speed. As a result, AVFS system will increase the work 
frequency, otherwise it should be reduced to save power. In 
addition, after decoding has succeeded, if next packet is not 
yet ready, voltage and frequency will also be reduced to save 
static power during the idle period of the decoder. In short, 
our AVFS system makes it more possible for processing time 
to match the time of inputting one packet. 
Fig. 5 shows input FIFO’s remaining depth change and 
corresponding frequency adjustment when the decoder is 
working under a RBER of 6.5×10-3. The length of a packet 
corresponding to our 9216-bit codeword is 13824-bit. The 
decoder reads in 12-bit LLR per cycle, so writing one packet  
into the FIFO costs 1152 clock cycles. Ideally, decoder's 
processing speed is always the same as input rate, thus the 
remaining depth of the FIFO can be no higher than 1152. 
However, when the number of required decoding iterations 
suddenly increase, the decoding processing speed is less than 
the input rate. As a result, the remaining depth of the FIFO 
will have a peak close to 1500. At this time, the AVFS 
mechanism will increase the decoding frequency to 272 MHz 
to speed up the decoding process. In addition, when decoding 
no longer requires so many iterations, the clock frequency 
will be reduced to save power.

B. LDPC Decoder Architecture 
Fig. 6 shows the architecture of proposed LDPC decoder. 
The decoder mainly consists of 128-parallel Bit Node Unit 
(BNU) processors and Check Node Unit (CNU) processors, a 
checker and necessary storage elements. The storage elements 
include V node Memory, Sign Memory and Min memory. The 
conventional cyclic shift circuit is integrated into the read and 
write controller of V node memory. 
V node Memory stores variable node message, and Sign 
Memory stores its sign bit. Min Memory stores the minimum 
and sub-minimum of check node message. It also stores the 
position of minimum and the result of multiplying all variable 
node message’s sign bits. When the decoder starts to work, it 
first loads the initial LLR from soft message FIFO into V 
node Memory. Then BNU and CNU work together to 
complete iterative decoding, during which the hard-decision 
bits are sent to the checker and the decoded codeword (Data 
out) is written into external output FIFO. If the checker 
indicates that decoding has been successful, the codeword 
will be popped from output FIFO and a valid signal will be 
pulled high at the same time to instruct external circuits to 
receive the codeword. BNU completes the functions of 
equation (3), (4), and (5). CNU executes equation (2) and (3). 
The check node message can be recovered by equation (3) 
and (4).

C. Adaptive Clock Generator 
The adaptive clock generator consists of a configurable 
fanout of 4 (FO4) inverter chain and a frequency divider. 
Firstly, the inverter chain works as a ring oscillator which can 
generate a clock signal. The length of the chain can be 
configured as 10, 12, 14, 16, 18, 22, 26 or 30 through 3-bit 
port named SEL. Secondly, the frequency divider is used to 
reduce the frequency generated by ring oscillator to meet 
decoder’s needs, while making clock’s duty cycle closer to 
50%. 
When AVFS system works, the adaptive clock generator 
receives the adjustable voltage from DC-DC converter and 
change the delay of the inverter chain, thereby changing the 
clock frequency of ring oscillator. As a result, the voltage and 
frequency adjustment can be completed at the same time. 
Certainly, it should be noted that before the voltage regulation 
is over, the clock signal should be gated to avoid voltage 
overshoot or undershoot that may cause errors in circuit 
function. 
The most important thing for a clock generator is whether 
it can generate high enough frequency required by the 
decoder. In order to confirm the effectiveness of our clock 
generator, firstly, we set the maximum iteration of the 
decoder to 4 and calculate 4 required work frequency 
according to the fixed time of inputting one packet and 
different processing cycles of corresponding iterations. 
Secondly, we get the real clock frequency of our adaptive 
clock generator from simulation and compare it with target 
frequency. It can be seen from Table Ⅰ that the frequency 
generated by the adaptive clock generator at each voltage is 
always faster than the target frequency, which means this 
designed clock generator can fully meet performance needs 
of the decoder.

IV. PERFORMANCE EVALUATION 
The proposed LDPC decoder is synthesized in TSMC 28 
nm technology. The circuit has been test under different 
intensities of AWGN. For each noise intensity, 10,000 
packets are loaded to the decoder to test the operating power 
and throughput with EDA tools including Hspice and 
Modelsim. Table Ⅱ shows the average power consumption of 
the decoder under different operating voltages. Fig. 10 shows 
decoder’s throughput and power under different RBERs after 
applying the AVFS system. The throughput and power are 
normalized to ones before AVFS system is applied. It can be 
seen that the throughput of decoder has only about 1% loss 
under each RBER, while the power consumption can be 
reduced by 25%-61%. Therefore, our AVFS system is 
effective on improving energy efficiency of LDPC decoder.

V. CONCLUSION 
When the required decoding iterations vary for each 
packet, conventional LDPC decoder fix the supply voltage 
and work frequency at a high level to meet real-time 
processing performance, which decreases energy-efficiency. 
In this paper, we propose a high energy-efficiency LDPC 
decoder with AVFS system. It can dynamically adjust 
decoder’s supply voltage and work frequency according to 
input packet FIFO’s usage. Final experimental results show 
that the decoder has a throughput of 1590 Mb/s when RBER 
is around 10-2. The designed AVFS system can reduce the 
power consumption of decoder by 25%-61% and improve its 
energy efficiency to 1.3-2.5 times of the original under 
different channel noise. Meanwhile, comparing our decoder 
with other work, it also has significant advantages of energy 
efficiency.
