Abstract— With the extensive cost reductions associated with small satellites, low Earth orbit missions are increasingly becoming popular, mostly with universities and the New-Space industry. However, a persistent limitation associated with the smallest satellites is the significant reduction in energy resources that each satellite has at its disposal. This limitation poses a challenge when using advanced communication systems, particularly those employing advanced forward error correction such as low-density parity check (LDPC) codes. To conserve the high computational energy required to decode such codes, we propose a novel early termination stopping criterion for LDPC decoders that is based on detecting the periodicity of syndrome weight oscillations. The technique is independent of the operating signal-to-noise ratio and results in reductions better than 80%, in the computational energy expended on a well-known bit-flipping decoding algorithm. Real world results are presented using an ARM-based microcontroller.
Keywords—LDPC Codes, Small Satellites, PocketQubes, FEC, Early Termination
I. INTRODUCTION
Due to the sharp cost savings associated with launching small satellites into low Earth orbit (LEO), the attractiveness of CubeSats and other small satellites has been increasing steadily. The introduction of the PocketQube (PQ) by Robert Twiggs in 2009 [1], promised to further reduce the cost of accessing space. However, PQs bring with them several challenges associated with their small size and substantial research work is still needed to improve the viability of this class of spacecraft. This paper aims to tackle the constraints revolving around the very limited energy resources available on such small satellites. This is addressed by reducing the energy consumption of the decoding process within a Hybrid forward error-correction (FEC) – automatic repeat request (ARQ) based communication system. This optimisation process is tested out on the UoMB-Lite satellite – a simplified PQ satellite that the University of Malta (UM) Astrionics Research Group is currently developing to demonstrate a number of key technologies [2]. These small satellites have limited available energy, [3] and as such, the use of FEC poses

Gallager [4], in 1962, proposed low density parity check (LDPC) codes along with a decoding algorithm for these codes, coined bit-flipping (BF) hard decision decoding. These codes were then rediscovered by Mackay and Neal [5] and Sipser and Spielman [6] in 1996. LDPC codes proved to be able to operate close to the Shannon limit [7] and when designed properly may outperform turbo codes [8]. Abela [9] investigated the possibility of a FEC scheme based on LDPC codes to be utilised in a PQ satellite by analysing various decoding algorithms, focusing mostly on the BF algorithm and its variants. Eventually, Abela opted for the BF iterative decoding algorithm due to its lower complexity and developed his own BF variant named Reliability Ratio-Based Self-Normalized Weighed Bit-Flipping (RSSN-WBF).
The solar energy that may be collected by solar panels on such a small satellite is limited. Hence, techniques to reduce the energy consumption across all the various satellite subsystems must be adopted. This includes reducing the computational energy consumption of the LDPC decoder. Beomkyu et al. [10] proposed an early termination technique based on two stopping criteria that utilise the decoder’s syndrome weight. Ismail et al. [11] optimised an Adaptive Threshold Bit Flipping (ATBF) decoder by using probabilistic thresholds to determine whether a codeword is decodable.
The drawback of these two methods is that the thresholds used are based on a single / operating point. However, during any satellite pass, the communications distance from a given ground-station, and hence the / will change. At the minimum elevation angle of 10 the UoMB-Lite satellite is designed to operate at an / of 6 dB. The / continues to increase until elevation angle increases to 90 , beyond which, it starts to degrade once again. In this paper we propose a method that reduces the energy consumption of the LDPC decoder that operates with BF decoders, while being independent of the incoming signal’s / .
The paper is structured as follows. Section II provides a background to LDPC codes, as well as the decoder’s energy consumption analysis. The proposed early termination stopping criteria is described in Section III and its performance is analysed in Section IV, both in terms of detection accuracy, as well as energy consumption. Section V concludes the paper.

II. BACKGROUND
A. Satellite Transceiver Architecture and Operation
The transceiver on board the satellite consists of a multichip module (MCM) controller, a single chip transceiver and a power amplifier for data transmission. The MCM in turn consists of an STM32F427ZI microcontroller (MCU), a Lattice MachXO2-7000 FPGA, and an Infineon Smart Card chip. The transceiver is the SI4468 from Silicon Labs while the power amplifier is the RF5110G from Qorvo.
When the satellite is receiving data from the ground station, the communication system cycles through four main operating phases. The first is the Reception (RX) phase, where the transceiver, FPGA and MCU are working together to store the received data into the internal memory of the system. Once all the data has been received, the communication system moves onto the Processing phase. In this phase, all the received data will be processed. Once this is completed, a status frame is constructed and transmitted to the ground station to indicate any frames received in error and the corresponding error type. These are the Framing and the Transmission (TX) phases, respectively.
The energy consumption of these phases depends on the power consumed as well as the time spent in each phase. The power in each phase was measured either experimentally [12] or obtained from the manufacturer’s datasheet. The active time for each phase was then obtained from orbital propagator (SGP-4) simulations [12], resulting in the energy consumption breakdown shown in Table I. Converting the total energy in a single satellite pass to the energy required for each frame requires knowledge of the average number of frames expected to be received. From the analysis described in [12], the average number of frames in a single pass was 401. Thus, the energy required to process a single frame could be computed. The retransmission rate, as outlined in [12], is low: Only 0.1% to 1% of the frames would have to be retransmitted with the LDPC codes used in this paper. Therefore, the overall impact of retransmitted frames on the average energy required per frame can be neglected.
As one can observe, the Processing phase consumes 84.43% of the total energy being used by the communications sub-system. Therefore, the LDPC decoder which accounts for most of the processing phase is a good candidate for optimisation.

The LDPC decoder may result in instances where the outcome of the decoding process is not correct. Han and Liu [13] defined a frame (or word) in error as being one of three types: type I – decoder results in an invalid word; type II – decoder oscillates between different solutions without converging; and type III – decoder converges to a valid codeword but not the transmitted one. The occurrence of these error types depends on the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), hence
/ , as simulated and presented by Lechner [14]. Lechner shows that the fraction of type I error frames decreases as the SNR improves. All three types of frame errors have to be detected as the corresponding decoded words are all in error. This requires an error detection scheme which triggers a request for a retransmission. One error detection technique that may be used is the cyclic redundancy check (CRC) sum that is appended to the transmitted data, and then re-calculated on the receiver side. Hence a mechanism is required to process this CRC sum and request frames that were in error after the error correction and error detection processes. Such a mechanism is referred to as a type I Hybrid ARQ mechanism, as proposed by Comroe and Castello [15].

IV. TESTING AND RESULTS
An STM32F429ZI microcontroller (MCU) with ARM Cortex M4 architecture, 256 KB of random-access memory (RAM) and 2 MB of flash program memory, was used to obtain empirical data. The testing and results section is split into three main parts. The first identifies the performance of the decoder with the proposed PSD early termination stopping criterion in place, the second shows the expected time taken by the decoder to execute one iteration on the selected MCU as well as the power consumption of the MCU, while the last section analyses the overall LDPC bit-error rate performance with and without the early termination stopping criterion.
During these tests an AWGN channel was used with an optimally detected Binary Phase Shift Keying signal transmission.
A. Decoder Performance
The performance was determined through simulation, based on the decoder implemented by Abela [9]. At each
/ level between 0 dB and 8 dB, in steps of 1 dB, the simulation was terminated when 1,000 decodable words and 1,000 undecodable words were generated. However, the simulation was stopped earlier if the number of transmitted words exceeded 1,000,000, so as to limit the simulation time. These conditions provided a statistically significant dataset. Each received word was then classified into one of four categories:
1. A decodable word with early termination; 2. A decodable word without early termination; 3. An undecodable word with early termination; 4. An undecodable word without early termination.
To determine the number of words classified into each of the four categories listed above, each received word was processed until either the syndrome weight reached zero, or

until a maximum of 200 decoder iterations were performed, irrespective of the stopping criterion. During each iteration, the proposed PSD stopping criterion was evaluated to decide whether the decoder should stop or continue decoding the current word. The number of iterations at which the PSD stopping criterion was met was recorded.
The word error rate (WER) performance of the original and proposed decoder (using PSD) was determined using simulation. For the proposed decoder to offer the same performance as the original decoder, the WER difference between the two needs to be negligible. This difference is shown in Fig. 2 as a percentage difference, taking as reference the original decoder. As one can notice, the percentage difference in WER is marginal, with the worst performance change being a 2% increase in the WER at 8 dB for Code 1. This performance degradation is considered to be negligible, indicating that the optimisation process did not significantly impact the WER performance of the codes being considered.
We next analyse the average number of iterations required to terminate the decoding process. Any reduction in the number of iterations executed implies that the original decoder was performing unnecessary iterations that did not contribute to a decrease in the WER. Fig. 3 compares the average number of iterations executed by the decoder with early termination and without. It can be observed from Fig. 3 that the decoder with early termination decreased the average number of iterations significantly, in some cases by as much as 95%. B. Microprocessor Energy Consumption
As indicated in (1), the number of iterations is only one contributor to the energy consumption, with the other two being the MCU’s power consumption as well as the execution time required to process one iteration. To determine the average execution time, the decoder was implemented and executed on the selected MCU. To obtain the energy consumption 200 words were transmitted for each / between 0 dB and 8 dB. For each word, the following tasks were executed:
1. Generate = random number of bits.
2. Encode the random bits to obtain a codeword.
3. Introduce errors in the transmitted codeword according to the operating / , resulting in a received word.
4. Decode the received word obtaining a (hopefully) error free codeword and extracting the = information bits.
5. Compare the number of bit errors between transmitted and decoded information bits.
For each / , the time taken to perform steps 1 through 5 for 200 words was measured. This process was then repeated, however, this time omitting step 4. The time difference between the first and second experiment would yield the time taken by the decoding process (step 4).
For each simulated word, the number of iterations performed by the decoder was recorded such that the average number of iterations performed at each / could be determined. The average time taken to decode one iteration at each / was measured by determining the total number of iterations executed and by keeping track of the total number of words decoded. This time, the processing phase uses the timing values achieved with the proposed PSD stopping criterion. The processing phase now consumes about 19% of the total energy consumed by the communication sub-system, whereas the original decoder consumed around 85% of the total energy. This is a significant improvement, especially for a small satellite as the total energy required in the original decoder was 1.78 J for each received frame, whereas now, the total energy required is 0.34 J for each received frame. This resulted in an average of 80% reduction in the total energy consumed by the communication system in the four operating phases when receiving data.
As mentioned in Section II-C, the energy consumption of the Processing phase as shown in Table II, is based on the average energy required for the three codes. This was done in order to highlight the overall performance benefit of the proposed PSD algorithm irrespective of the code being used. Under the same operating conditions, Code 1, 2 and 3 with the PSD algorithm decreased the energy consumption of the Processing phase by 85.28%, 82.32% and 76.93%, respectively. However, the selection of the appropriate code was not within the scope of this paper due to the dependencies on the typical data payload size that will be used. The analysis on typical payload sizes was not performed as it depends on the implementation of other satellite sub-systems which have not yet been fully developed.
V. CONCLUSIONS
The LDPC decoding process with the proposed novel PSD stopping criterion was implemented on an STM32F429ZI MCU to be used on a PQ satellite. It was shown that the new PSD stopping criterion reduces the energy consumption from 1.78 J down to 0.34 J per transmitted frame. This resulted in the MCU consuming 80% less energy than it consumes with the standard decoder. Such energy savings make the mission feasible.
This energy reduction technique needs to be investigated further with different codes in order to determine the extent to which any advantages are possible with variations in code structure, code length as well as data rate.
Nevertheless, these codes will be practically applied within the UoMB-Sat Lite satellite. According to Abela [9],

the three codes proposed in Table II were the best codes that should be used for this application. Thus, the work in this paper, while probably transferrable to other contexts, was limited to improving the performance in terms of energy consumption for these three codes.
Another aspect that could be investigated is the algorithm that is being used in order to determine syndrome weight periodicity (Step 9 in Algorithm 1). However, optimising this step will only yield minor improvements in the overall energy consumption of the system, since its contribution is already very small.
