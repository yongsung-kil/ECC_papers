ABSTRACT Free-space optical (FSO) communication is crucial for the next-generation 5G+ wireless networks. The FSO links suffer from atmospheric-turbulence-induced bit errors. For the increasing link’s performance, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, complemented by the belief-propagation (BP) algorithm, are an excellent option. The bit error rate (BER) of the LDPC code is characterized by a parameter called the threshold. The threshold is the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), after which the BER falls arbitrarily and becomes close to zero. We derive the threshold for the LDPC codes under the BP algorithm for an uncorrelated flat FSO channel. The determination of the FSO channel threshold is a tedious task as the density of the log-likelihood ratio from the FSO channel cannot be assumed as Gaussian and is available only in a numerical form. It, thus, requires testing different values of SNR as a possible threshold systematically. Therefore, we propose the divide and conquer algorithm. The threshold depends on the degree distributions, channel state information (CSI), and the turbulence level. When CSI is known, we obtain the threshold at an SNR of 8.10 dB in high turbulence for a regular (3, 6) LDPC code. This threshold steps up to 12.48 dB when the CSI is unknown at the receiver. We evaluate the threshold values for various degree distributions (regular and irregular LDPC codes) under high, moderate, and low turbulence levels for both channel models (CSI known and unknown at the receiver). We also confirm the derived threshold values with MATLAB simulations.
INDEX TERMS Channel state information, FSO communication, LDPC code, threshold.

I. INTRODUCTION
F REE-SPACE optical (FSO) communication is a pioneering technology owing to its high data rate in contrast to radio frequency technologies [1], [2]. Besides, FSO communication uses a free spectrum, and thus network operators are exempted from paying license fees. Moreover, this technology has added benefits of low power requirements and installation costs [2].
FSO communication, however, is susceptible to atmospheric turbulence [3], [4]. Turbulent eddies act as lenses of varying refractive indices in the optical signal propagation path, resulting in fluctuations of the received signal intensity. There are well-formulated probability density functions (pdfs) in the literature, namely, lognormal, negative exponential, and gamma-gamma (G-G) [1], [2], to model

the received intensity distribution according to the turbulence levels (low, moderate, and high).
Generally, G-G pdf [5] is broadly adopted in practice as it effectively models all the turbulence regimes. We also use the G-G pdf to model the turbulence levels in our work.
The fluctuations in the received signal intensity affect the receiver’s signal quality and increase the bit errors. The error-correcting codes (ECCs) mitigate the effects of atmospheric turbulence, reduce error probability, and improve the available capacity [6]. Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [7] are the prime examples of ECCs which have received much attention as these codes approach the Shannon limit (within 1 dB) [8]. The LDPC code employs the belief propagation (BP) algorithm (also referred to as the sum-product algorithm) as one of the decoding algorithms.

For an ensemble of asymptotically long length code, the density evolution proposed in [9] analyses the bit error rate (BER) performance of the BP algorithm. The BER follows an inverted-step response where the BER remains high for low signal to noise ratio (SNR) and decreases sharply to zero for high SNR. The point of transition is referred to as the threshold. Using the threshold, we can also estimate the BER for finite length ensembles [10].
The evaluation of the threshold is well known for the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) [11]–[16] and the Rayleigh channels [17]. In [14], the authors study the adaptive threshold evaluation technique based on the bitflipping (BF) decoding technique of LDPC codes. In [15] and [16], the authors derive the threshold based on min-sum decoding algorithms of LDPC codes. The authors in [18] evaluate the threshold for a binary erasure channel under BP and bitwise maximum a-posteriori probability (MAP) decoding algorithms.
In [19], [20], the authors present low complexity upper bounds to estimate the threshold in the AWGN channel. However, to the best of our knowledge, the threshold has not been evaluated so far for the FSO channel. The FSO channel is primarily different from an AWGN channel because the channel coefficients are G-G distributed. Thus, it is evident that these codes have different performance and threshold in an FSO channel. The previous work in [21] evaluates the effect of optical noises on the threshold but ignores atmospheric turbulence. Some recent works on using LDPC for FSO channels involve probabilistic shaping for non-binary symbols [22] and using continuous-variable quantum key distribution [23]. Our work can also support the analysis of these recently published work.
In this article, we derive the thresholds for uncorrelated flat FSO channel. In an FSO channel, the threshold depends upon whether the channel state information (CSI) is known or unknown at the receiver. While we derive the thresholds in both types of channels, we explain the complete methodology for the case when CSI is known at the receiver (in Sections III and IV), and we present only the essential results for the case when CSI is unknown at the receiver (in Section V). In both these cases, the density of log-likelihood ratio (LLR) (see Section III-A) cannot be assumed as Gaussian, which is a standard assumption for an AWGN channel [11], [24], and is only available in a numerical form. It, thus, entails testing different values of SNR as the threshold systematically. Thus, the evaluation of the threshold for the FSO channel requires a divide and conquer algorithm.
We propose the divide and conquer algorithm in Section IV, using which we determine the threshold for both regular and irregular LDPC codes (discussed in Section III). The divide and conquer algorithm uses a parameter, which we define as fo, to reduce the number of computations. The results are presented in Tables 4–7. We see that the threshold increases considerably in an FSO channel depending upon the turbulence regime. For example, in the case of the channel with known CSI, instead of requiring an SNR of 1.16 dB

VII. CONCLUSION In this article, we derive the thresholds of the LDPC codes in an uncorrelated flat FSO channel. For the evaluation of the threshold, we propose the divide and conquer algorithm. We also verify the obtained results through simulations. We see that the numerical results are in accord with the theoretical results. We estimate the threshold values with and without knowledge of CSI under various turbulence levels, such as low, moderate, and high. The threshold values for no knowledge of CSI reportedly require higher SNR than the CSI known model. We also report the threshold values obtained in the AWGN case for comparison. For example, the irregular LDPC code (see, row 1, Tables 5 and 8) requires an SNR of 0.4727 dB to attain the threshold in an AWGN channel. However, when subjected to the FSO channel with known CSI, it requires an SNR of 7.05 dB, 6.53 dB, and 4 dB in high, moderate, and low turbulence regimes, respectively. These threshold values increase by 4.08 dB, 3.24 dB, and 0.94 dB in the respective turbulence regimes when the CSI is unknown at the receiver.
We also show the gap of the obtained threshold values with Shannon’s limit (capacity) to visualize the difference we need to cross over to achieve the capacity. Achieving capacity in the FSO channel requires an optimum degree distribution, and threshold determination is the first step toward that.
