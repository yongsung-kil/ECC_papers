NAND Flash Memory and Its Place in IoT

1st Sorcha Bennett Department of Electrical and Electronic Engineering
Limerick Institute of Technology Limerick, Ireland
sorcha.bennett@lit.ie

2nd Dr. Joe Sullivan Department of Electrical and Electronic Engineering
Limerick Institute of Technology Limerick, Ireland joe.sullivan@lit.ie

Abstract—Data storage requires an answer to the constantly evolving question - how best to store the mammoth amount of data being generated? There is a great diversity of storage solutions currently, ranging from personal USB devices, personal Solid State Drives (SSD) and HardDrives (HD) to Cloud storage and server farms. However, the right storage solution depends on how the data will be used. As Flash memory plays a key role in all the hierarchy levels of Internet of Things (IoT) systems, this paper will lay out a broad discussion on both IoT and Solid State Memory (SSM), followed by an attempt to link these two fields. The topics mentioned under IoT include: Big Data and the Cloud, The Cloud of Things, Flash in the Datacentre, Scalability and Reliability, Edge Computing, Fog Computing and Osmotic Computation. While, topics under SSM include: Nonvolatile Reliability, Errors and Wearout, NOR v NAND, 2D TLC, 3D TLC, QLC and Optane.
Index Terms—Internet of Things, Cloud, Fog, Edge, Osmotic, Paradigms, NAND Flash Memory, Data Storage, Reliability, Endurance, Retention, 2D, 3D, QLC, 3D XPoint, Optane
I. INTRODUCTION
Internet of Things (IoT) as a term was first mentioned by Kevin Ashton in 1999, but the concept was first given voice by Nikola Tesla during a magazine interview in 1926 where he said “when wireless is perfectly applied the whole earth will be converted into a huge brain [1]. McKinsey Global Institute declared in 2015 that the “total potential economic impact” of IoT could be $11.1T USD per year in 2025, with 1.6 trillion devices alone used by humans for health and fitness [2].
This IoT was initially built on a three layer architecture model, based on the TCP/IP model: 1) Application Layer 2) Network Layer, and 3) Perception Layer. However, this was not expansive enough due to the sheer volume of data and so, based on the later OSI network model [3], further layers were added - a Processing Layer and a Business Layer, along with renaming the Network Layer [4]. This was again extended to include one extra layer - a Coding Layer [5], thereby giving:
1) Business - manage applications and privacy of users 2) Application - interact with society 3) Processing - store, analyse and process data 4) Transport - transport data 5) Perception - gather data 6) Coding - assign a unique id to each piece of data

The term Big Data first appeared in 1997, in a paper authored by NASA scientists, where they defined Big Data as data sets which were too large to fit in the main memory [6]. Further definitions vary slightly depending on the industry, but in the main they all agree that it constitutes a very large amount of data. The challenge then, was how best to deal with all this data, and so Cloud Computing was introduced.
This term first appeared in 1996 as part of a business plan drafted in Compaq, where the authors noted that the “emergence of the Internet is driving the migration of communication and collaboration applications into the Internet ‘cloud’ ” [7]. This cloud facilitates a space for users to store data from their personal devices, without any effort on their part, as all the heavy lifting is done by the service providers [8]. The Cloud of Things was the inevitable next step, integrating IoT and Cloud Computing [8], allowing for the effective analysing and storing of so much data [5]. So, the next big question was this - how to physically store this data?
In 2009, NAND Flash memory was touted as one way to prevail over the issues occurring when attempting to store the ever increasing amounts of data in cloud data centres. At that time, even though the cost per bit of Flash was dropping yearon-year in line with Moore’s Law, and Flash devices were becoming ever more sophisticated and affordable, Flash was not considered to be a replacement for traditional spinning hard drives in data centres [9]. However, NAND was seen as a way to improve data latency in the cloud, and was particularly suited to applications such as database management systems (DBMS) and banking functions, giving an edge to cloud providers, as they could use the more expensive Flash in Tier 1/0 of their data centres [10]. From this start Flash has spread throughout the datacentre.
Even though Flash is non-volatile, meaning it will keep data stored without power, and can be written to, and erased, by electrical means [11], it also requires a lot of power to write data to it, and the long-term power cost could be considerable [12]. It was called Flash because the data could be erased very quickly - in a flash [13].
Unlike traditional spinning disks, Flash memory progressively wears out with reprogramming. Most Flash memory interest focuses on its reliability, including endurance and retention. Endurance is a metric for how many program/erase (P/E) cycles, or single uses, a cell can endure before failure [14]. Endurance figures vary between device types, and also

between manufacturers, but they have comprehensively decreased with each iteration - Single Level Cell (SLC) devices are typically specified to last for 100,000 P/E cycles; MultiLevel Cell (MLC) for 5000 - 10,000 P/E cycles; and 2D Triple Level Cell (TLC) for only 500 P/E cycles. This downward trend was halted with the introduction of 3D devices, which stack storage elements vertically, as well as horizontally, thereby providing extra silicon in the storage elements, and as a result the P/E cycles now range from 1000 - 5000 [15], depending on the type of 3D device. However, such devices are more susceptible to data errors, as a result of disturbance mechanisms with neighbouring cells. Retention is a metric for how long a device can keep data, without being refreshed. The standards body JEDEC specifies that if a Flash device is cycled to 100% of its maximum P/E cycle count, then the device is required to keep the data for 1 year, and if it’s cycled to only 10%, then the data must be retained for 10 years [16].
P/E cycling creates significant problems which eventually cause data errors and the wearout (death) of all Flash memory devices [11]. The most common cause of physical degradation and subsequent data loss is due to the electrical stress generated when changing the cell state. This causes threshold voltage (Vth) disturbances, which, in turn, leads to electron leaks [17]. This Vth of a cell is the gate voltage at which it is turned on. Wear Leveling and Error Correction Codes (ECCs) are two methods which help to fight this wearout issue.
Wear Leveling is the process of ensuring that all the blocks get roughly the same level of wear, such that we do not have frequently-updated blocks wearing out prematurely. Attempts are always made to correct any read errors that do occur by employing ECCs, the most common of which were ReedSolomon and Bose & Ray-Chaudhuri (BCH) [18]. However, 3D TLC NAND devices require even more powerful ECC, such as low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, which have the ability to use soft information from multiple reads to help correct errors [19]–[21]. Both wear leveling and ECCs can therefore help a device to withstand the effects of degradation, thereby increasing both the endurance and retention of Flash, and therefore, its reliability.
A serious issue which affects all NAND Flash memory is its vulnerability to disturb errors, particularly read-disturbs, due to the serial architecture of NAND. These errors appear when one cell is read multiple times, which causes its neighbour cells to lose or gain electrons. The logic state of the cell can eventually change because of all these neighbouring operations, and if the Vth changes cause a breach of ECC limits, then a readdisturb error is deemed to have occurred. Efforts can be made to prevent these read disturbs by placing limits on the number of reads before refreshing vulnerable data or by finding blocks that are read the most often and moving the data from there to blocks which are less often read [22], thereby increasing reliability.

II. IOT
A. Edge Computing
One of the fundamental issues with Cloud Computing is the latency when transferring data between the application and the Cloud, along with the huge scale of the data and the network bandwidth required [26]. To resolve this, Edge Computing was defined, whereby resources near the sensors, or devices, would be used for local storage while the data was being initially processed, after which the often smaller data sets would be transferred to the Cloud. Edge devices examples currently being used by many home users include Apple/Android smart watches, Oculus rift helmets, Google Nest and Glasses, Fitbit fitness trackers - all of which generate huge amounts of data. These devices all need proximity access to their application servers, which, conventionally, are stored across a few large distributed data centres.
Research on current architectures being used in Edge computing, and IoT applications which can benefit from using Edge, is often carried out using mobile gaming case studies [27], with a focus on using Edge devices for resourceintensive streaming and latency. These were built on VMbased Cloudlets which run on WiFi access points [28], using multiple different types of edge resources together under one Edge platform [29], and using Edge applications to deliver services, both at the edge, and to and from the Cloud [30]. Cloudlets were developed as an extra, virtual, layer between edge devices and the cloud, considered to be full “datacenters in a box”. They provide both the required low latency and the high bandwidth, along with better security, as the data operations are performed nearer to the users, thereby reducing the risk of data being damaged or lost during transfer [28].

Fog Computing is a paradigm which exists at the edge of the network, as this is where the data is being generated or used, and provides many services which allow communication between the edge of the network and Cloud Computing technology. It is also know as just Fog, “simply because the fog is a cloud close to the ground” [36].
Due to the many stakeholders involved, along with the fact that applications can require access to both the Fog and the Cloud, there is a requirement for facilitating both these technologies to work together. There is also the question of data storage, as the Fog generally collects large amounts of data from devices, with a range of time scales - from seconds up to days. This requires the Fog to support multiple storage types, from almost instantaneous to almost permanent, with the latter being provided by the Cloud [37].
In 2016 the OpenFog Consortium [43] was signed with the OPC Foundation, and various industry stakeholders, in order to “accelerate the adoption of fog computing in order to solve the bandwidth, latency and communications challenges associated with the Internet of Things (IoT), Artificial Intelligence, Robotics, the Tactile Internet and other advanced concepts in the digitized world”, thereby recognising the issues which needed to be overcome with Fog.
C. Osmotic Computing
Osmotic Computing was first proposed in 2016, and was required as the Cloud was too centralised, and not adaptive enough, to deal with IoT needs [44]. It was named ‘Osmotic’ due to the design of the fluid migration of services deployed across multiple layers, and the fact that this movement is similar to osmosis in chemistry, which is ‘the seamless diffusion of molecules from a higher to a lower concentration solution”. Officially, “dynamic management of services and microservices across the cloud and edge datacentres, addressing issues related to deployment, networking, and security, thus providing reliable IoT support with specified levels of QoS”. Osmotic computing allows for many different stakeholders to add value to the IoT service. It splits applications into “microservices”, and uses “lightweight container-based virtualisation technologies” to dynamically deploy them into the cloud and edge infrastructures, depending on constraints at the lower level and requirements at the higher level.
These technologies all have different goals, and so, different requirements, thereby allowing for a range of Flash devices to be useful. In general, SSM can be considered to be the answer to all goals, with devices having a multitude of attributes designed to meet specific challenges. The usage goals need to be matched to these attributes, some of which include the speed of random versus contiguous read, write speed, endurance, retention, price per bit, and drive writes per day.
III. NAND FLASH MEMORY
There are two types of Flash memory - NOR and NAND. As NOR guarantees 100% good bits [46] and supports fast random memory read access, meaning data can be found

anywhere and in any order, it is used to store code and parameter data. While NAND supports mainly serial data access, suited to stream-style data such as multimedia content, it can perform random read and write access, but does struggle [46]. NAND is denser and cheaper than NOR, and so has taken over for use in data storage, memory cards, mobile phones and SSDs - where the cost per bit is critical.
Both NOR and NAND are based on a Floating Gate (FG) technology built on a MOS (Metal Oxide Silicon) Field Effect Transistor or MOSFET. The gate that controls the flow of current in the transistor has its connection to the outside world cut, hence floating. The Voltage at which this current begins to flow is the Vth. A program operation is performed by adding a charge to this FG and the data should then stay stored there until an erase operation is performed [48], [49]. However, the Vth of the cell is changed over time by these operations, which eventually contributes to data loss.
Within the NAND Flash family, there are distinct types of memory. SLC stores 1 bit of data per cell, and is either Programmed (0) or Erased (1), as shown in Fig. 1 (a). MLC stores 2 bits of data per cell in 4 levels, ranging from 00 Fully Programmed to 11 Fully Erased, as detailed in Fig. 1 (b). 2D TLC stores 3 bits of data per cell in 8 levels, ranging from 000 Fully Programmed to 111 Fully Erased, as shown in Fig. 1 (c). QLC stores 4 bits of data per cell in 16 levels, ranging from 0000 to 1111, as displayed in Fig. 1 (d).
A. 2D NAND Planar TLC
A TLC memory device with reduced capacity area and efficient ECC was first proposed in 1997 [50]. In 1995, a method of increasing the density of the NAND Flash cells was proposed [51], using up to 4-bit cells, which would require narrow Vth distributions and high programming speeds. Due to the structure of TLC, with its 8 possible states, there exists a much higher possibility of errors, similar to those from which both SLC [13] and MLC [52] suffer. Quite often the state of the cell is changed from highest to lowest level, rather than one level at a time [53], which puts the cell under the utmost stress, and increases the chances of errors.
This TLC architecture only allows for a certain amount of scaling, before data loss begins to occur. One of the leading causes of this loss is cell-to-cell interference, which is introduced by the constant attempts to reduce the physical cell size [54]. This, along with cost concerns, led to the solution of scaling vertically in 3 dimensions.
B. 3D NAND TLC
3D NAND Flash memory was created by fabricating multiple stacked layers of 2D Planar NAND Flash memory [55], using the same string connections, but storing these strings vertically, rather than horizontally - similar to a high rise building, with each new floor being built on top of the one below. This method allows Flash manufacturers to fit many more layers onto these 3D devices [56] - the physical footprint of 3D NAND Flash devices is not larger than that of 2D Planar NAND, therefore manufacturing costs are not increased. IV. OTHER NON-VOLATILE MEMORY
Micron and Intel developed Quad-Level cell (QLC) NAND Flash memory devices, which have 16 voltage levels, can store 4bits per cell, can have up to 1Tb storage, have 64 layers and provide 33% more density than TLC [63]. QLC Flash memory provides a larger capacity, cheaper device than TLC NAND, but it has even less endurance than TLC NAND [64], [74], due to the higher number of voltage distributions, allowing for less P/E cycles, and more data errors, due to less space between levels and movement of these voltage distribution levels.
Developed by Intel and Micron, 3D XPoint non-volatile memory is the memory inside Intel Optane SSDs [65]. There are various Optane options available on the market, including Optane memory, Optane SSD, and Optane Persistent memory [66]. Research has found that the Optane SSD is a good use for write-intensive applications [67]. 3D XPoint has been shown to have fast access times, which allows applications to have up to three times faster run times, along with larger data sets. Benefits of 3D XPoint SSDs are faster storage for databases and realtime analytic applications [68]. Intel and Micron parted ways after the 2nd generation of 3D XPoint, and Intel recently launched a 144-layer QLC NAND SSD, with up to 30.72Tb storage [74]. Meanwhile, SK Hynix have said that 800+-layer 3D NAND is in our future, albeit in 2030 [75].
Micron claim that 3D XPoint delivers “up to 1,000 times lower latency and exponentially greater endurance than NAND,” [69]. V. CONCLUSIONS
As discussed previously, non-volatile memory has a huge advantage due to the fact that data will still be stored even without power. However, this type of memory requires large amounts of power to store any data, due to the fact that the data must first be erased, before then being written - in effect, performing two operations instead of one. Also, Flash scaling is an issue, as going below a certain physical size, while also increasing the storage capacity, introduces more challenges. Due to these points, perhaps Flash cannot work quickly enough when performing erase/write operations to allow for the use of very low energy, and therefore consuming more power over the lifetime of the device, and thereby shortening the lifetime of the device [59].
Flash memory is being used for a vast array of IoT applications, including “industrial automation and Factory 4.0, connected vehicles, wearables, healthcare, aviation, plus just about any application that begins with “smart”: smart metering, smart homes, smart utilities, smart farms, smart logistics” [61]. In the cloud, Flash memory features in the SSDs which are used for Tier 0 storage, and are faster than traditional storage, such as harddrives. In local or Edge devices, external Flash chips or Embedded Flash is used for code and data storage. Embedded Flash offers performance benefits over external flash chips, as code no longer needs to be copied from an external chip into RAM. Embedded Flash also provides power reductions because external Flash chips consume more power [62].
NAND Flash is considered a good choice for IoT applications that generate a large amount of data, e.g. USB drivers and wearable devices. These generally require cheap storage, and so are the perfect use for NAND Flash, due to its high storage and low cost. SLC is ideal for “high-end, missioncritical” applications, whereas MLC and TLC are better for lower endurance, but more data storage [61]. IoT also has a use for NOR Flash - for applications that require reliability, code storage, Execute-in-Place (XIP), and low power usage [62].
