Algebraic Design of a Class of Rate 1/3 Quasi-Cyclic LDPC Codes

R. Michael Tanner Computer Science, UC Santa Cruz, Emeritus
Washington, D.C. r.michael.tanner@gmail.com

Abstract— An algebraic design procedure is given for constructing rate 1/3 quasi-cyclic LDPC codes with Tanner graphs with girth 6. Parity check matrices consist of 2 × 3 block matrices of circulants of size p, a prime, exhibiting invariance under the action of a subgroup of the multiplicative group mod p. The group invariance converts the problem of avoiding short cycles into that of choosing orbits to solve connectivity constraints. A series of very low density H matrix codes constructed show surprisingly good minimum distances.
Keywords— quasi-cyclic, LDPC, algebraic, graph, girth, diameter, group-invariant, orbits
I. INTRODUCTION
Early development of the theory of error-correcting codes leaned heavily on algebra to define codes of flexible rate and length for which the minimum distance could be proven mathematically, thereby guaranteeing a level of error detection and performance using maximum likelihood decoding, e.g. [1]. Algebraic constructions often pointed the way to design of encoding and decoding algorithms that could perform error correction at least up to the level guaranteed by the code's minimum distance. With the success of turbocodes [2] and iterative decoding algorithms that can exploit soft-decision information, coding research pivoted to study random graphs and conditions for the convergence of belief propagation decoding algorithms on graphs with large girth, e.g. [3]. One key insight from the analysis of density evolution is that decoder convergence will occur closer the Shannon limit when the Tanner graph is irregular, ideally exhibiting an optimized degree profile. Algebraic specifications have been powerful in defining graphs with a large girth, e.g. [4], and a particularly advantageous structure is found in quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes with algebraic concepts employed to define constituent circulants, e.g. [5], [6]. For the sake of the effectiveness of message-passing decoding, a code's Tanner graph must have a girth greater than four; girth four occurs if two parity checks both check the same pair of bits. In any such short cycle, the probabilistic information passed in a message-passing decoding algorithm becomes correlated after only two iteration, leading to inferior performance. Algebraic specifications of the circulant matrices defining the parity check matrix for the QC code can allow a mathematical proof that the Tanner graph has girth six or greater.

This paper gives a procedure for constructing binary QC LDPC codes for which the parity check matrix H is a 2 × 3 block matrix of n × n circulant blocks, producing a code of length N = 3n and rate R≥1/3. While it is possible to construct codes in this class for n a non-prime, the analysis is simplified when n=p, a prime. A key feature is the use of subgroups of the multiplicative group of GF(p) to form orbits of the integers mod p. Specifically, we use the permutation of the integers πb: i => b i mod p, bz = 1 mod p. The circulant matrices in H are defined by polynomials [ hs,t (x) ], s=0,1, t=0,1,2, in which the exponents of hs,t (x) are an orbit under πb. By carefully selecting the orbits, our procedure produces a code whose Tanner graph has girth six and bits of two different degrees. While we have yet to prove a strong lower bound on minimum distance for all the codes in this class, beyond a weak tree bound from the graph, a number of the codes constructed for which minimum distance has been established are remarkably good, e.g., a [111,38,22] code with bits of degrees 4 and 6, and parities of degree 7. Codes and their graphs were analyzed using Magma [7].
We have not yet done simulation studies to see how these algebraic LDPC codes perform in terms of BER with belief propagation decoding on an AWGN channel. Although the determination of the level and slope of an error floor for these codes would require much more detailed analysis (e.g. [8]), minimum distance makes one unavoidable contribution to the error floor at high SNR, and to the error rate when the code is used simply for error detection.
II. PRELIMINARIES
A. Permutation Groups and Automorphisms
As is well known, for any prime p, arithmetic modulo p forms a field, GF(p). The mapping σ: i => i +1 mod p is a permutation of the elements of GF(p) of order p; that is, applying σ repeatedly, p times, gives the identity permutation: σp = I. For GF(p) there exists a primitive element a such that ai mod p, i = 0,..., p-2, are all the distinct non-zero elements of GF(p), and multiplication by a is a group of order p-1. It is easy to see multiplication by any constant permutes the elements of GF(p). If z is a divisor of p-1, then the permutation of the elements created by multiplying any element by b = a(p-1)/z ∈ GF(p) is a subgroup of order z: πb: i => b i mod p is a

For any j ∈ GF(p), the set of field elements formed by repeated application of πb is called the orbit of j under πb. The orbit includes { πb i(j), i=0,..., z-1} = { j, bj, b2j, ..., b(z-1)j}. For any non-zero j, the orbits must have the same number z of distinct elements, because if bsj = btj for 0≤ t<s < z, then, multiplying by the inverse of j, it implies bs = bt, or using the inverse of b, bs-t = 1, but b has order z, a contradiction. (This is one advantage of focusing on circulants of size n=p, a prime: operations on the row or column indices mod p can be interpreted to be on the field GF(p), so every non-zero b has an inverse.) One can take any convenient element in an orbit to be a leader for the orbit for the sake of an enumeration of the orbits, such as the (p-1)/z leaders { a0, a1, a2, ..., a(p-1)/z -1}.
In a p × p binary circulant matrix H with i,j-th entry [hi,j], 0≤ i,j ≤ (p-1), each row is the right cyclic shift by one position of the row preceding it, hi,j = hi-1,j-1, 1≤ i,j ≤ (p-1), and H is defined by its 0-th row. We will hereafter consider the row and column indices of a circulant to be elements of GF(p), so operations on those indices will be mod p. Another way of stating the circulant property is to say that H is invariant under the shift permutation, σ, operating simultaneously on the row indices and the column indices, hi,j = hσ(i),σ(j). H can be characterized by a polynomial h(x) formed from its 0-th row: h(x) = ∑ xj h0,j , summed over j = 0 to (p-1). The i-th row maps similarly to the polynomial xi h(x) mod (xp - 1), the mod (xp - 1) reflecting the cyclic shift. If H is characterized by h(x), its transpose HT is characterized by h(x-1).
The circulant property gives rise to a useful algebra in which that property is preserved under common matrix algebra operations. The sum of two circulants is a circulant, as is the product of two circulants. This idea can be extended to a set of matrices that are invariant under an arbitrary group of permutations operating simultaneously on the row indices and the column indices.
Let P be a permutation matrix that permutes the rows of a matrix H according to permutation π when H is multiplied by P from the left, P H. PT permutes the columns when H is multiplied from the right, so P H PT is the matrix with rows and columns permuted. Note that P PT = I. H is invariant under π, and π is called an automorphism of H, if H = P H PT. The set of all permutations that leave H invariant form a group, the automorphism group, Au, of H.

B. Graph Girth and Diameter
The basic tool used to analyze the Tanner graphs of the constructions is matrix multiplication. The parity check matrix H is considered to be the incidence matrix for the graph, for this purpose a matrix of integers, for which we will use boldface, H. (For a p x p circulant with polynomial h(x), h(x) will be viewed as an element of the ring of polynomials mod (xp -1) with integer coefficients.) The entries of H constitute a count of the walks between a parity node pi of row i and a bit node bj of column j, namely, either 1 if there is a connecting edge or 0 if not. A walk is a sequence of connected edges in a graph, which may include repeated edges. (See [8] for a concise overview of general graph theory.)
It follows that H HT is matrix whose entries count the walks between node pi of row i and node pj of column j in the product. By the same logic, HT H is matrix whose entries count walks between pairs of bit nodes. A cycle of length four in the bipartite graph occurs if there is a parity node from which there are two different walks to another parity node, or similarly two different walks from one bit node to another bit node. Since the parity nodes are fewer in number, we will examine the smaller matrix H HT. The diagonal entries are the number of walks of

The count of walks of length three in the graph are the entries of H HTH (or its transpose, HTH HT), and the count of walks of length r are the entries in the r-fold products H HTH HTH... and HTH HTH... (r terms). The diameter is the smallest r for which all entries in the product are non-zero, because a 0 in the product implies that there is no walk connecting a pair of nodes.
III. CODE CONSTRUCTION
If a QC LDPC binary code is defined by a 1× 3 block matrix H = [ A0 A1 A2] where the At are all sparse circulants, there will always exist a low weight codeword involving just two blocks, obtained by using the polynomials of A0 and A1, for example, interchanged to form a codeword c = [ a1(x-1), a0(x1), 0 ] so that H cT = a1(x-1) a0(x-1) + a0(x-1) a1(x-1) = 0. The proposed code construction cures that deficiency with a 2 × 3 block matrix H.

The "1"s represent the identity matrix with polynomial x0 =1. The parities in a block row will have a degree that is the sum of the weights of the polynomials in that block row, and the bits in a block column will have a degree that is the sum of the weights of the polynomials in that block column.

The orbits that define the four polynomials of H(x) are chosen to assure that no off-diagonal entry greater than 1 will appear in H HT, and thus that girth is six or greater. Among the choices available, one can select the set of orbits to minimize the graph diameter.

The motivation for seeking to minimize the graph diameter is that the diameter corresponds to the number of iterative steps of the decoder required for information at one node to be assured of influencing the results at any other node. Loosely speaking, a graph with a small diameter µ cannot have a high density of short cycles. For µ as small as possible, being able to

reach any other node along paths of length at most µ from a starting node implies that there cannot be a large number of distinct paths that reach the same nodes repeatedly. The paths of length µ must fan out effectively to reach all nodes in the graph quickly. Minimizing the diameter is another way of assuring that the decoding computations are not dominated by correlated information, and this is why seeking to minimize the graph diameter is a useful design heuristic.
Example 1 p = 13
GF(13) has primitive element a = 2 and ai, i=0,...,11, are 1,2,4,8,3,6,12,11,9,5,10,7. Then (p-1)=12 is divisible by z = 3, and we can choose b = 2(12/3) = 3. Multiplying by 3 forms a subgroup of order 3. The elements of GF(13) can be partitioned into four orbits plus the orbit of 0: [0], [1,3,9], [2,6,5], [4,12,10], [8,11,7]. Choosing A(x) = x2 + x6 + x5, B(x) = x1 + x3 + x9 = C(x), and D(x) = x0 = 1 creates a [39,14,10] code that has a graph with girth of 6, diameter 5, and bit degree 4 and parities of degree 7 in the upper block row and 5 in the lower.

To achieve girth of six at n=39 requires using D(x)= 1, but in general we will select an exponent orbit of size z for D(x) also. For this paper, the polynomials of interest have weights of three or greater, and this implies that the girth will be no more than six, as follows. Suppose a circulant matrix A has A(x) = xt + xu + xv . Use the largest z for which this condition can be met, so the Tanner graph for C will have girth six.
Since the polynomials have weight z≥3, the girth cannot be greater than six. Using the largest z possible while assuring girth six aims to reduce the graph diameter. When z is small and p very large, the (p-1)/z orbits are numerous and give many options to choose the necessarily distinct orbits and solve the "orbit packing puzzle." But solving the orbit packing puzzle easily leaves many zeroes in H(x) HT(x), adversely impacting the graph diameter. Achieving a tight packing with a larger z, a tougher combinatorial challenge, empirically has produced codes with a strong minimum distance.

IV. EXAMPLES
For the first four examples below, the minimum distance of our algebraic LDPC is compared to that of the binary code of the same length and rate with the largest minimum distance of any code known, for those parameters. The "Best Known Linear Code" for binary codes are accessible in tables maintained by Magma, using the BKLC function. The two longest codes are outside the range of the BKLC, and we give a BCH code for comparison.

Although we have not yet proven a general bound on the minimum distance of codes constructed with this procedure, we make an intriguing observation: For all the codes we have designed and for which the minimum distance can be established computationally, the minimum weight codeword itself is invariant under the πb permutation. If it could be proven that the minimum weight word must be invariant under known conditions, it would make an exponential leap in overcoming the combinatorial hurdle. For the subcode of Example 5 consisting of the πb invariant words, Magma found the minimum weight codeword, with weight 50, in 0.05 seconds, whereas it estimated 105 years to prove the minimum weight of the code. At present, checking the minimum distance of the πb invariant subcode is a powerful heuristic.
V. CONCLUDING REMARKS
The use of multiplicative subgroups of GF(p) to structure QC LDPC codes opens up a rich landscape of possible designs. Here we have presented a simple code design template and a search procedure to find codes of design rate 1/3, organized into three p×p circulant blocks. The Tanner graphs of the codes have girth six, and examples described have diameters five and four. The density of the H matrix is low compared to the block length. Bits have degrees z+1 and 2z, parities degree 2z+1. Thanks to the effects of the two simultaneous equations in H, the orbit size is very small compared to the code length: z(z-1) < N/6 for code length N. The examples testify that the class contains irregular LDPC codes of practical interest, many with excellent minimum distances. While this paper studied only design rate one third codes, the concepts can be adapted to other rates. Simulation studies are needed to show how message-passing decoders for these very sparse H codes actually perform on binary channels, to rule out any unanticipated phenomena in iterative decoders on these graphs. Additional research is also needed to devise a general technique for bounding the minimum distance of the codes. Remarkably, the minimum distances of some of these codes, when known, are not far from those of the best known algebraic codes.
