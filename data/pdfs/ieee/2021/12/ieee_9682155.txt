Abstract—In this paper, we propose two algorithms to partition the parity check matrices (PCMs) for the layered decoding of quasicyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes, which targets to reduce the hardware complexity and computation delay. We formulate the optimization problem of PCM partitioning, and systematically propose the principle of partitioning to guarantee a block cyclic shift property for different decoding layers. After that, we develop both greedy and enumerative partitioning algorithms, aiming at minimizing the maximum column weight of each decoding layer. We further derive a tight lower bound for the minimum achievable maximum column weight, which can help to early terminate a searching process once the lower bound is achieved. Simulation results show that the proposed algorithms are generally capable of achieving the lower bound or differ by one.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] have been widely adopted in many applications such as satellite communications and data storage systems, for their capacity approaching performance with iterative message passing decoding [2]. To operate the message passing decoding of LDPC codes, a messagepassing schedule is required to determine the order of updating the messages. The well-known message-passing schedule is the flooding schedule [3], where all the variable-to-check (V2C) messages and the check-to-variable (C2V) messages are updated simultaneously and propagated along the edges in the Tanner graph [4]. However, the flooding schedule requires a large parallelism, which leads to high decoding complexity and memory requirement for hardware implementation.
To simplify hardware implementation and reduce the storage memories, the layered schedules were proposed in [5]–[9], which update the messages in a sequential order by using the most recent information. In particular, the row layered schedules in [5]–[7] operate as a sequence of check node (CN) updates while the column layered schedules in [8], [9] perform variable node (VN) updates sequentially. As shown in [6] that the layered schedules can achieve faster convergence speed with significantly reduced memory requirement compared to the flooding schedule. However, the conventional layered schedules, e.g., [5], [6], [8], are initially developed to process only one row/column of the parity check matrix (PCM) at each layer, which causes a reduction of decoding throughput.
To improve the throughput, partial parallelism have been introduced in [10]–[13] for quasi-cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes

[14]. The idea is to process more rows/columns of the PCM in parallel at each layer. More specifically, to decode a QCLDPC code with partial parallelism, the rows or columns of its PCM are divided into several groups/submatrices corresponding to different layers. At each layer, all rows or columns are processed in parallel, and the decoding conducts layer by layer. As shown in [11]–[13], the way of partitioning the PCM can significantly affect the hardware complexity and throughput of the decoder. With respect to that, a block cyclic shift property is exploited in [11] and [12] among different layers to make the hardware efficiently reused between consecutive layers during the decoding process. At each layer, the maximum column/row weight of each layer is further minimized to reduce both the implementation complexity and the logic delay for the computation [11]. Nevertheless, the block cyclic shift property has not been systematically investigated for partitioning PCMs, e.g., the optimal partitioning in terms of minimizing the maximum column/row weight of each layer is not clear. With massive data rate requirement for the communication systems in the fifthgeneration (5G) and beyond, it is challenging for the LDPC decoders to achieve ultra-high throughput with cost-effective hardware implementation. As one of the practical solutions, the PCM partitioning is necessary to be systematically investigated.
In this paper, we propose two PCM partitioning algorithms for the decoding of QC-LDPC codes, which can reduce the hardware complexity and computation delay. We first formulate the optimization problem of PCM partitioning, and systematically develop the principle of partitioning which can guarantee a block cyclic shift property among different layers. Then, based on the developed principle, we derive two (i.e., greedy and enumerative) partitioning algorithms to minimize the maximum column weight of each layer. We also derive a tight lower bound for the minimum achievable maximum column weight. This can be greatly helpful for reducing the search space, since an optimization algorithm can stop searching once it achieves the lower bound. Simulation results on 5G LDPC codes show that both algorithms can generally achieve the lower bound or differ by one.

each row is cyclically shifted one element to the right relative to the preceding row (the preceding row of the first row is the last row). In this paper, we only consider the QC-LDPC codes whose PCMs consist of circulant permutation matrices (CPMs) or zero matrices, where a CPM is a circulant with only a single non-zero entry at each row. As a result, it suffices to describe a CPM by indicating its size and the position of the non-zero

entry in its first row.

The decoder processes all rows of a layer in parallel based on certain message passing algorithms, and operates serially layer after layer.

III. PRINCIPLE OF PCM PARTITIONING
Let H be the PCM of a QC-LDPC code. We assume that:
• H consists of an M × N array of Z × Z CPMs or zero matrices.
• H does not contain zero rows or two identical rows (this is reasonable for practical H).
Suppose that we would like to partition the rows of H into L layers. Moreover, according to Section II-B, it is desired to make consecutive layers have block cyclic shift property and to minimize the maximum column weight of each layer. In this

section, we formulate the partitioning problem and illustrate the principle for solving it.
A. Problem Formulation
Let F be the first layer which is a matrix consisting of some rows of H. Let S be a positive integer. To maintain the block cyclic shift property among different layers, we mean to use φZ(l−1)S(F), l ∈ [1, L] as the PCM for the decoding within the l-th layer while α(F), α(φSZ (F)), . . . , α(φ(ZL−1)S(F)) form a partition of α(H). Therefore, if P 1 is solved, P 2 can also be solved. We now connect P 2 to the classical problem of finding k-cliques in k-partite graphs [16], [17].
Given an undirected graph G = (V, E) with vertex set V and edge set E, a clique D is a subset of V such that any two distinct vertexes in D are connected by an edge in E. A clique of size k = |D| is called a k-clique. A graph is called k-partite graph if the vertex set can be partitioned into k disjoint subsets so that no vertices in a same subset are connected by an edge.
For a given S, denote Ci,j as the j-th element (LS-class) in ΦZLS(Ci), where i ∈ [1, M S] and j ∈ [1, L] by Lemma 4. We construct a graph G = (V, E) with V = {vi,j|i ∈ [1, M S], j ∈ [1, L]}. Meanwhile, any edge (vi,j, vk,l) ∈ E iff i = k and ω(Ci,j ∪ Ck,l) = 1. As a result, G is an M S-partite graph where for i ∈ [1, M S], the i-th partite consists of the vertex set {vi,j | j ∈ [1, L]}.

VII. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we proposed two PCM partitioning algorithms
for the decoding of QC-LDPC codes with row layered schedule,
which aims to reducing the hardware complexity and computa-
tion delay. In particular, we first formulated the optimization
problem of PCM partitioning, and systematically developed
the principle of partitioning to guarantee a block cyclic shift
property among different layers. Then we proposed both greedy
and enumerative partitioning algorithms based on the partition-
ing principle, which aims to minimizing the maximum column
weight of each layer. We also derived a tight lower bound for
the minimum achievable maximum column weight, which can
help to reduce the search space for the optimization process.
Simulation results showed that the proposed algorithms are
effective for partitioning both large and small scale PCMs and
generally achieve the lower bound or differ by one.
