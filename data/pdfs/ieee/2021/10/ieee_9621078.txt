Abstract—In this work, we propose a polar code design which
targets at improving convergence of belief-propagation (BP)
decoding. That is, the designed polar codes can achieve a target
error rate at a smaller number of iterations. In particular, we will
show that reliabilities on some coded bits have stronger effects
on the initial log-likelihood ratio (LLR) propagation during BP
decoding. By utilizing the property, we carefully puncture and
extend polar codes to speed up the convergence of BP decoding.
It will be shown that polar codes based on the proposed design
achieve either a target error rate at a smaller number of iterations
or an improved error-rate performance at a given number of
iterations as compared to existing polar codes.
Index Terms—Polar codes, belief propagation decoding, punc-
turing, extending, fast convergence
I. INTRODUCTION
Polar codes proposed by Arikan [1] have attracted a great
deal of attention due to their capacity-achieving performance
and low-complexity encoding and decoding structure. For
decoding polar codes, a successive cancellation (SC) decoding
was initially proposed by Arikan in the seminar work [1].
However, the SC decoding is performed in the sequential
manner, which often leads to an excessive long decoding la-
tency. Meanwhile, the belief-propagation (BP) decoding which
has been widely adopted in decoding for low-density parity-
check (LDPC) codes, can be performed in the parallel fashion.
Thus, in hopes of reducing decoding latency, BP decoding was
adopted for polar codes [2], [3]. The decoding latency of BP
decoding depends on the number of iterations to achieve a
certain target error rate, and thus, it is highly desirable to speed
up the convergence of BP decoding to reduce the decoding
latency.
To adjust lengths and rates of polar codes, the puncturing
and extending techniques have been adopted [4]–[8]. In this
work, we instead utilize the puncturing and extending tech-
niques for designing polar codes with a faster BP decoding
convergence. In particular, we first demonstrate that reliabili-
ties on some coded bits have stronger and weaker effects, say
strong and weak bits respectively, on the log-likelihood ratio
(LLR) message propagation in the early stage of BP decoding.
By taking advantage of the property, we judiciously puncture
the weak bits and proportionally extending the strong bits in
hopes of faster convergence during BP decoding. The extend-
ing is conducted simply by repeating the strong bits. It will
be shown that by introducing the simple modification of polar
code based on the design rule, a considerable performance
improvement can be achieved.
This paper is organized as follows. In Section II, we first
describe BP decoding for polar code. Later, in Section III, a
design rule of fast convergence will be proposed. Performances
of polar codes based on the proposed design will be evaluated
in Section IV. Finally, we will conclude this work in Section
V.

III. PROPOSED POLAR CODE DESIGN
In this section, we propose a design rule of polar codes
which is tailored for improving the convergence speed of BP
decoding. The proposed design rule utilizes the puncturing and
extending techniques.
There have been studies on puncturing polar codes, and the
schemes in [4] and [7] select punctured bits in the bit-reversal
order and the bit-channel quality order, respectively. While
the schemes appear to have different punctured bit selection
logic, they in common measure the performance degradation
caused by the punctured bit in their own ways, and puncture
the ones leading to the least performance degradation first. The
extension technique in general adds additional columns to the
generator matrix, which increases the code length. However,
for polar codes, the additional columns ruin the structure of
the kernel, which results in losing the recursive structure of
polar codes. To turn around the technical issue, the existing
schemes [5], [6] extend polar codes by repeating some coded
bits.
In the early stage of iterations, the messages from the
left messages, i.e. Lin,i and Lout,i in Fig. 2, dominate the
messages from the other direction since BP decoding gets
started with the messages from the physical channel, i.e.
the ones from the rightmost nodes. In Fig. 2, there are two
incoming left messages, i.e. Lin,1 and Lin,2, which have
different contributions to the left message leaving from the
processing unit, i.e. Lout,1 and Lout,2. Note that Lout,1 is
determined by Lin,1 and Lin,2 via the box-plus operation,
denoted by g in (1). Thus, both Lin,1 and Lin,2 have an
equal contribution to Lout,1. However, for Lout,2, Lin,2 gives
a stronger contribution than Lin,1 since Lin,2 is directed fed to
Lout,2 while the reliability of Lin,1 is diluted by the box-plus
operation. Thus, the analysis of the LLR propagation in the
early iterations gives us an insight that reinforcing Lin,2 allows
BP decoding to propagate the LLR message in a more efficient
way. By extending the insight gained with the processing
unit to the entire factor graph, it can be conjectured that the
reliability of the last bit (n+1, N) is the most influencing one
in the LLR propagation. The insight also explain the reason
why the existing puncturing scheme in [4] punctures the bit
of the lowest index first.
In this work, we propose a simple design rule in which
coded bits with nF lowest indices are punctured and the one
with the highest index, i.e. N is repeated nF times. Then,
the punctured bits will have zero LLR values while the LLR
values of the extended bit, i.e. the coded bit with the highest
index, becomes increased by nF times. Since the proposed
design rule employs only puncturing and repetition, it does
not require additional complexity. However, a meaningful
performance improvement is observed via simulations, which
will be discussed in the next section.
IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
In this section, we present simulation results to evaluate
performances of the proposed designed rule introduced in
Section. III. For the performance evaluations, we consider a
(128,40) polar code with code rate R = 0.31 which is taken
from 3GPP NR [9]. The proposed design rule is applied to the
polar code to get a faster convergency of BP decoding.

bits, i.e. nF , up to five. In Fig. 3, it is noticed that the polar
code with the proposed design rule has the better error-rate
performance with the larger nF value. For instance, at Eb/N0
of 7dB, the proposed design rule with nF = 5 reduces the

to choose a good combination of the maximum number of
iterations and the value of nF .
We believe that the proposed design rule may have more
advantages by collaborating with an early-termination BP de-
coder for polar code such as the one in [10]. By collaborating
with the early termination BP, the average number of iterations
for the BP decoder can be greatly reduced by the proposed
design rule with requiring additional complexity.
V. CONCLUSIONS
In this work, we propose a design rule of polar codes
which aims to improve the convergence speed of BP decoding.
By analyzing the LLR propagation during BP decoding, we
demonstrate that some coded bits strongly contribute to LLR
messages in the early phase of BP decoding. Based on the
insight, we propose a design rule which punctures coded bits
less contributing to the message propagation. Meanwhile, the
design rule strengthens the reliability of coded bit which has
the strongest contribution to the message update in the BP
decoding. The proposed design rule is simple enough not to
require additional complexity. However, it is demonstrated that
the proposed design rule provides meaningful error-rate perfor-
