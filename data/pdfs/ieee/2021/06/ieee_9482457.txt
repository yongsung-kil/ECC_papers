A 336 Gbit/s Full-Parallel Window Decoder for Spatially Coupled LDPC Codes

Matthias Herrmann, Norbert Wehn TU Kaiserslautern
Kaiserslautern, Germany {herrmann, wehn}@eit.uni-kl.de

Max Thalmaier, Markus Fehrenz, Timo Lehnigk-Emden, Matthias Alles
Creonic GmbH Kaiserslautern, Germany {max.thalmaier, markus.fehrenz, timo.lehnigk-emden, matthias.alles}@creonic.com

Abstract—Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes are among the most powerful Forward Error Correction (FEC) schemes and part of many communication standards. Emerging use cases are expected to require data rates towards Tbit/s, which is one to two orders of magnitude higher than the throughput specified in 5G-NR. For throughputs towards Tbit/s, state-of-theart LDPC soft decoders are restricted to short code block sizes of several hundreds to thousands of bits due to routing congestion challenges, forcing a firm limit to the overall communications performance of the transmission system. In this paper, we focus on a relatively new class of LDPC codes, denoted as Spatially Coupled LDPC (SC-LDPC) codes. For the first time, we show that these codes enable efficient, very high throughput ( 100 Gbit/s) decoding of large code block sizes. To this end, we present an SC-LDPC decoder for a length N = 51328 terminated SC-LDPC code with sub-block size c = 640 and coupling width ms = 1, that achieves a throughput of 336 Gbit/s in a 22nm FDSOI technology, which is about 50 times higher than the fastest SC-LDPC decoder in literature. In contrast to existing work, the presented decoder is based on the Full-Parallel Window Decoder (FPWD) architecture. We propose algorithmic and architectural modifications to shorten the critical path and to reduce the routing complexity of the FPWD resulting in higher throughput as well as better energy and area efficiency for the modified decoder. The decoder furthermore outperforms a state-of-the-art reference architecture that was implemented for the same code in the same technology in terms of throughput and latency.
Index Terms—Forward error correction, SC-LDPC codes, high-throughput decoding
I. INTRODUCTION
Forward Error Correction (FEC) is a foundational component in digital baseband processing. For advanced channel codes that use soft-decoding, the FEC constitutes the most computational intense component in the digital baseband chain. Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes are among the most powerful FEC schemes. First described by Gallager in 1962 [1], these codes were nearly forgotten until their rediscovery in the 1990s by e.g. MacKay and Neal [2]. Due to their excellent error correction performance close to the Shannon limit for large block sizes, LDPC codes gained a lot of attention and were eventually adopted in many communication standards, like DVB-S2x, Wi-Fi and, more recently, 3GPP 5G-NR. But not only outstanding error-correction performance led to this success. Digital baseband processing is subject to major restrictions in silicon area, power and power density.

Reduced-complexity decoding algorithms like Min-Sum (MS) paved the way to efficient ASIC decoder implementations. Moreover, code construction techniques like quasi-cyclic (QC) codes enabled decoders to be flexible in the supported code rates, the block lengths and throughput, which opened LDPC codes to a wide range of applications. Emerging use cases and future standards are expected to require data rates towards Tbit/s, which is one to two orders of magnitude higher than the throughput specified in 5G-NR, resulting in new challenges for efficient decoder implementations.
LDPC decoding is based on iterative message-passing algorithms on a bipartite graph, denoted as the Tanner graph of the code. The throughput of an LDPC decoder is mainly limited by the exchange of messages between the two node sets: the check and variable nodes. To provide good error correction performance, the Tanner graph must 1.) be large (corresponding to a large code block size) and 2.) exhibit only limited locality. This in turn challenges an efficient and high-throughput decoder implementation [3]. The fastest state-of-the-art LDPC decoders rely on the so-called unrolled full-parallel decoding architecture [4] [5]. This architecture corresponds to a one-to-one mapping of the Tanner graph into hardware, i.e., all check and variable nodes are instantiated as dedicated functional units and the corresponding edges of the Tanner graph are hardwired. The iterations of the decoding procedure are unrolled and pipelined. In this way, a new block is processed in every clock cycle. While these decoders can achieve very high throughput in today’s technology nodes, e.g., 588 Gbit/s in [5], they are limited to short block sizes in the order of up to 2000 bits due to the low locality of the Tanner graph that results in highly unstructured routing networks and thus severe routing congestion challenges. This limit in block size also limits the achievable error correction performance.
In 1999, Jime´nez Felstro¨m and Zigangirov introduced a new class of LDPC codes [6], that was later denoted as SpatiallyCoupled-LDPC (SC-LDPC) codes [7]. With spatial coupling, a code of almost arbitrary length can be constructed from a much smaller LDPC block code. In the same way, decoders can be constructed from a set of traditional LDPC block decoders that each process a much smaller so-called sub-block. These small sub-decoders are then chained together and coupled accordingly. Hence, these codes have the potential to combine

B. Decoding
In contrast to conventional LDPC block codes, the structural characteristic of the SC-LDPC code enables so-called sliding window decoding. Here, a window W of length W defines a sub-matrix of H[1,L] and thus a subset of variable and check nodes in the corresponding Tanner graph. Throughout the decoding process, the window moves from the upper left to the lower right of H[1,L]. In every processing step t, the respective nodes corresponding to the current processing window W(t) are updated and the window is moved forward by one subblock over the diagonal structure. The decoder only needs to store W sub-blocks and corresponding messages and can start the decoding as soon as the first sub-block is available at the input of the decoder. Since W L the initial latency and the memory requirements are far less compared to a conventional LDPC block code of similar length.

C. High-Throughput SC-LDPC Decoders

In every processing step t a sliding window decoder finishes the decoding of one sub-block corresponding to c code bits. The coded throughput is thus

Θ = c · TP −1,

(2)

where TP denotes the processing time, i.e., the time required by the decoder to finish the processing of one decoding window W(t). For high-throughput decoding, which essentially means to finish the decoding of a window in one clock cycle, this processing time TP is equivalent to the decoder’s critical path, which in turn has two main contributors: the combinational logic and the signal routing. The combinational logic mainly depends on the maximum degrees of the respective variable and check nodes as well the number of iterations performed inside the window. The routing critical path, on the other hand, primarily depends on the window size and thus W and c, as a larger window size results in complex routing networks with long path delays. Furthermore, there is the quantization that impacts both the logic and routing critical path.
Hence, the challenge for high-throughput decoding is to design highly parallel decoders for large sub-block sizes c with minimal processing time TP . As for LDPC block decoders, with increasing parallelism the routing becomes predominant in the critical path and a major concern for high-throughput decoding [5]. The prevailing approach to mitigate this problem in literature is to split the decoding window into several smaller sub-windows that are processed individually on dedicated subdecoders, also simply denoted as processors.
This idea was already proposed by Jime´nez Felstro¨m and Zigangirov in their initial paper on SC-LDPC codes [6]. Here, the decoding window is considered to be composed of multiple row-layers, i.e. the window is split horizontally into multiple

Each processor processes only a single iteration involving one check node group and (ms + 1) variable node groups, i.e., one row-layer in H[1,L]. Since the architecture is pipelined, every processor operates on a different set of sub-blocks [yt...yt+ms ]. A constraint of this layered processing is that the processors cannot perform an update on the same group of variable nodes at the same time. In consequence, the layers cannot overlap and only every (ms + 1)th layer can be processed in parallel. The corresponding processing schedule for a RLPD with 3 processors and an SC-LDPC code with ms = 1 is illustrated in Figure 1 (a). The active layers are highlighted in red colors. The number of processors determines the number of updates a variable node receives and is, thus, equivalent to the number of iterations. In this way the decoding in the RLPD architecture is analogous to row-layered decoding for LDPC block codes [11], except that multiple layers are processed in parallel. This alternating row processing imposes restrictions on the length of the decoding window, which has to fulfill the relation

The memory requirement of the RLPD and the structural latency are thus proportional to the number of processors (iterations) I and the constraint length (ms + 1). This can become a considerable issue for codes with large ms and that require a large number of iterations.

However, here each processor processes a small subwindow that must be at least of size W = 2 · ms + 1. The (ms +1)th group of symbols inside the sub-window is denoted as the target symbols. In this section we propose a modified FPWD for MS decoding with reduced critical path and routing complexity. For reasons of simplicity, we assume in the following a fixed coupling width of ms = 1. However, the presented concepts can be also applied to larger values of ms.

Each processor has to propagate, respectively receive, a total of (c · dc) extrinsic messages to/from its right and left neighbors. For high-throughput decoders with large parallelism and thus large c (see Equation 2), this message exchange results in a complex routing network that becomes a severe throughput bottleneck.
To mitigate these problems we apply a method we denote in the following as check node splitting. B. Iteration Slicing
A decoding iteration in the original FPWD starts with the update of the Check Nodes (CNs) and ends with the update of the Variable Nodes (VNs). We denote this in the following as CN-VN iteration. After each iteration, the computed VN messages are exchanged between neighboring windows/processors and the next iteration starts. In the modified FPWD the messages are not exchanged between VNs and CNs, but between CNs. For the CN-VN iteration this means that the new exchange messages for the next iteration are computed in the CNs, i.e., in the middle of the iteration and not at the end resulting in a slower convergence of the decoding. We therefore propose a different slicing of the decoding steps into iterations, which we denote in the following as CN-CN iteration. The CN-CN iteration starts after the minimum search and the sign computation of the check node operation. In this stage of the processing, the extrinsic messages can be represented only using the first and second absolute minimum min0 and min1, the edge index of the first minimum idx0 and the dc signs of the output messages. The first step in the CNCN iteration is thus to compute the extrinsic messages for the VNs based on the respective signs and minimum values. Then, the VNs are updated and eventually new signs and minima calculated. Note that the CN-CN iteration comprises the same steps as the CN-VN iteration, only in a different order.
The modified FPWD extends the CN-CN iteration by two additional steps for the exchange of messages:
1) Before the CN-CN iteration, the exchange messages are used to update the signs and minima using Algorithm 1.
2) After the CN-CN iteration, new exchange messages are computed by multiplying min0 with the sign of the check node.
As we will show in the results section, the combination of split check nodes and the CN-CN iteration slicing provides the exact same performance as the original FPWD and can thus be considered as a reformulation of the respective decoding

algorithm. Another way to think of this modified FPWD is as a variation of a column-based layered decoder [13] for SCLDPC codes.

IV. RESULTS
In this section we present FEC performance and implementation results of the discussed SC-LDPC decoders. Both the performance evaluation and the implementation were performed for a terminated SC-LDPC code of length N = 51328 with ms = 1 and sub-block size c = 640. The code rate is R = 0.8. The respective sub-matrices H0 and H1 were obtained from [14].
A. FEC Performance
We evaluate the performance of the presented decoders with Bit Error Rate (BER) over Signal-to-Noise-Ratio (SNR) simulations. Each SNR point was simulated with 106 blocks (corresponding to 80 · 106 sub-blocks) transmitted over an Additive White Gaussian Noise (AWGN) channel with Binary Phase Shift Keying (BPSK) modulation. The results are shown in Figure 3. All decoders use the Normalized MS (NMS) algorithm with γ = 0.75.
For better classification of the results, the performance of a (64800, 51804) DVB-S2 block code decoded with 200 iterations is also shown. The DVB-S2 code is comparable to the SC-LDPC code in terms of code rate (R = 0.8) and length (N = 64800 bit). In addition, Figure 3 shows the performance of the SC-LDPC code decoded with a 200-iteration block decoder to demonstrate the maximum achievable MS performance.
As already stated in the previous section the original and the modified FPWD show the exact same performance. Compared to the RLPD, the performance of the FPWD is lower for the same number of iterations. This performance loss is

The slower convergence is to be expected as the exchanged messages in the FPWD, due to the overlaps, always correspond to previous decoding iterations in the neighboring window. This effect was also observed for the Compact Pipeline Decoder Architecture in [15] and can be considered as a cost for the lower structural latency.
B. Implementation Results
Implementation was performed for a 7-iteration original FPWD, a 7-iteration modified FPWD (compare Figure 2) and, as a reference implementation, a 4-iteration RLPD. The respective number of iterations was selected such that all three decoders achieve the same FEC performance (fixed point) of BER = 10−6 at 4.2 dB, which is around 1 dB from the maximum MS performance of the code. All decoders use 4 bits for the representation of channel values and extrinsic messages. The A Posteriori Probability (APP) values in the RLPD are quantized with 6 bits. The performance loss compared to floating point is less than 0.2 dB up to a BER of 10−6.
All three decoders were implemented in a 22 nm FD-SOI technology under worst-case Process, Voltage and Temperature (PVT) conditions (125 °C, 0.72 V for timing, 0.80 V for power). Synthesis is performed with the Design Compiler, Placement & Routing is carried out with the IC-Compiler, both from Synopsys. Power numbers are calculated with backannotated wiring data. The stimuli for the power simulations were obtained at a fixed SNR of 4 dB.

Although scaling factors exist to map data from one technology node to another one, they are only useful for scaling of logic-dominated designs but not for designs that are routing-dominated as it is the case for high throughput LDPC decoders. We therefore focus on a comparison of both implemented FPWD decoders and the RLPD reference implementation.
As a consequence of the reduced routing complexity, the modified FPWD achieves a 33% higher area utilization and thus a better area efficiency compared to the original FPWD. A further side effect of this is a lower wiring load for the logic circuits, which, in combination with the reduced sub-window size (compare Equations 5 and 6), leads to a 40% better energy efficiency as well as a higher frequency of the circuit.
Compared to the RLPD reference implementation, the modified FPWD exhibits an around 15% lower latency. For the selected operating point, this lower latency results solely from the shorter critical path. However, for a higher number of iterations the FPWD additionally exhibits a lower structural latency due to the comparatively smaller window size and thus shorter length of the processing pipeline making the FPWD a promising approach for latency critical applications.
V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we presented a 336 Gbit/s SC-LDPC decoder based on the Full-Parallel Window Decoding architecture. We proposed algorithmic and architectural optimizations to reduce the critical path and the routing complexity of the decoder. As a consequence, the presented decoder achieves around 40% lower (better) energy efficiency and 120% higher area efficiency. Compared to a state-of-the-art Row-Layered Pipelined Decoder that was implemented in the same 22 nm FD-SOI technology, the presented FPWD decoder is superior in terms of throughput and latency.
