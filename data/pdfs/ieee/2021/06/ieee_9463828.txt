Enhanced Partially-Parallel LDPC Decoder for Near Earth Applications
Jing Kang1,2, Bingbing Wang1,2, Yufeng Zhang1,2 and Junshe An1 1National Space Science Center, Chinese Academy of Sciences, Beijing 100190, China 2School of Computer Science and Technology, University of Chinese Academy of Sciences, Beijing 100190, China Email: k naive@163.com, bingice.w@gmail.com, zhangyufeng15@mails.ucas.edu.cn, anjunshe@nssc.ac.cn

Abstract—This paper presents an enhanced partially-parallel low-density parity-check (LDPC) decoder based on Consultative Committee for Space Data Systems (CCSDS) standard for high speed near Earth applications. Architectural level optimization is exploited to reduce hardware complexity and increase decoding throughput. To enchance the parallelism of the partially-parallel decoder, high circulant weight submatrix of the parity check matrix (PCM) are partitioned into several identity matrices with corresponding cyclic-shifted value and processed separately. Furthermore, messages corresponding to adjacent rows of the cyclic-shifted identity matrix are stored in one memory entry, and low-complexity data aligners are designed between memories and processing units to avoid memory access conflicts. Hardware implementation of a (8176,7154) LDPC code on Xilinx Virtex-5 XC5VLX330 FPGA shows that the proposed decoder can achieve a decoding throughput of 1.02 Gb/s at 10 iterations.
Index Terms—LDPC codes; decoder; partially-parallel architecture; near-Earth application; FPGA.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes proposed by Gallager [1] are a class of powerful error-correcting codes. Irregular LDPC codes [2-3] outperform the best-known turbo codes that can approach channel capacity by using iterative message passing (MP) decoding algorithms. Due to its superior error correction performance, LDPC codes are widely used in many communication systems [4-8].
LDPC codes typically use min-sum algorithm (MSA) [9] for iterative MP decoding, which utilizes an approximate approach for check node computation and thus has a lower complexity than sum product algorithm (SPA) [10]. Such a simplification is practical for hardware implementations but incurs a degradation in performance, thus attenuated MSA (AMSA) and offset MSA (OMSA) were proposed in [11] to ameliorate this situation. Different hardware architectures of LDPC decoders based on FPGA have been proposed in the literature [12-19]. The level of parallelism employed can have a large effect on the decoder’s hardware resource requirement and decoding throughput. LDPC decoders with fully parallel architectures [12-14] usually have the highest throughput, but are resource intensive and routing congestion between the processing nodes always exist. Conversely, serial decoders [15] require a minimal hardware resources but suffer from a low throughput that cannot meet the requirements of highspeed communication applications. Partially-parallel LDPC decoders and block-parallel LDPC decoders [16-19] represent

a compromise between hardware resource requirement and decoding throughput. FPGA-based partially-parallel decoders commonly utilize block random access memory(BRAMs) to store intermediate messages exchanged between processing nodes. However, when adjacent rows are computed simultaneously, more than one location of the BRAM need to be accessed, therefore the distribution of values into BRAMs must be chosen carefully and the interface between BRAMs and processing units must be designed specifically, otherwise there will be memory access conflicts and the decoder will reach the performance bottleneck.
Consultative Committee for Space Data Systems (CCSDS) has adopted (8176, 7154) quasi-cyclic (QC)-LDPC codes for near Earth applications [5]. This paper proposed an efficient enhanced partially-parallel LDPC decoder based on the CCSDS standard. Our contributions are as follows:
1) Messages corresponding to adjacent rows of the submatrix are stored in one memory entry and updated simultaneously, which increases the decoding throughput.
2) Low-complexity data aligners are designed between memories and processing units to avoid memory access conflicts.
3) A detailed FPGA implementation of the proposed decoder on Xilinx FPGA are showed, and the comprehensive comparisons with the state-of-the-art designs are provided.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows: Section II summarize the fundamentals of CCSDS LDPC codes, the AMSA for iterative MP decoding, and the typical partially-parallel decoder architecture. Section III makes a detail description of the proposed LDPC decoder. The implementation results are reported in section IV. Section V concludes the paper.

C. Typical Partially-Parallel Decoder Architecture
Consider a regular QC-LDPC code, whose PCM is formed by an array of m×n weight-1 shifted cyclically identity-matrix of size L × L. A typical partially-parallel decoder architecture is shown in Fig.1. Parallel degree is either the number of block rows m or the number of block columns n. In other words, m rows (n columns) from different block rows (columns) are updated simultaneously in the C2V (V2C) message update phase. All rows (columns) of each block are updated serially in L clock cycles. In Fig.1, m check node update (CNUs) with n-inputs, n variable node update (VNUs) with (m + 1)inputs are adopted for message processing, and three memories RAMF, RAMM, RAMC are employed to store received LLRs, intermediate messages and hard decisions, respectively.

B. Internal Architectures of the proposed Decoder
1) Memories: At the input-side of the decoder, Q bits of quantized channel LLRs are received and pass through the channel buffer. The output P × Q-bits are subsequently stored in channel memory bank(CMB), which is a stack of 16 RAMs. To facilitate P data accesses per clock cycle, each RAM has a depth of 511/7=73 memory locations with P × Qbits of word width. The intermediate memory bank (IMB) is employed to store the intermediate messages exchanges between variable nodes and check nodes during the decoding process. Each submatrix in PCM is a superposition of two cyclically shifted identity matrices. To enhance parallelism, we separate such submatrices into two separate cyclically shifted identity matrices and store their messages separately, thus adopting 64 RAMs with depth 73 and word width P × Q. The hard decision memory bank (HDMB) is used for storing hard decisions of the decoder, which is a stack of 16 RAMs with depth of 73 and Q-bits word width. The entire memory is (16 × 42 × 73 + 64 × 42 × 73 + 16 × 7 × 73 = 253.456)kbits.
2) Processing unit: The processing unit contains two modules to perform the crucial process of the decoding. The V2C message update module is responsible for performing (3), which has D + 1 inputs and outputs. D is the column degree, and the additional one input and output are used for intrinsic channel LLR and the estimation of the decoded bit, respectively. The cascaded adders of the V2C message update module is shown in Fig.3. Fig.4 shows the architecture of the C2V message update module, which performs the relevant operations according to (4). Then, most significant bits (MSBs) of the SM-LLRs are fed to sign unit (SU) and magnitudes of the SM-LLRs are processed by the first two minima find module (FTMFM) and attenuated modification module (AMM). The FTMFM employed a recursive tree architecture to find the first two minima as well as the index of the first minimum [21]. The AMM is designed for multiplying the attenuated factor α = 0.75 to the first two minima by performing left-shift, subtraction and interception operation. In SU, all input MSBs are XORed and the computed product bit is further XORed with every input MSBs to produce the sign bits of the updated SM-LLRs. Finally, the updated attenuated min-sum SM LLRs are converted to 2’s complement format by sign-magnitude to 2’s complement (SMTC) module.
3) Data aligner: Conventional QC-LDPC decoders normally sequentially store the intermediate LLRs in the memory. In the C2V message update phase, the memory address generator generates bi,j, bi,j + 1, · · · 0, 1, · · · bi,j − 1, where bi,j is the cyclic shift value of Ai,j of the PCM. In the V2C message update phase, the memory address generator

generates 0, 1, · · · , L − 1, L, where L is the size of Ai,j. To increase the parallelism, we store messages corresponding to P adjacent rows into one memory entry. However, this will cause memory access conflicts since more than one location of the BRAM is accessed. In [17], a switching network and two data buffers are employed to select the target group of data. The switching networks consists of a large number of multiplexers, which consume significant hardware resources, and the design of its output logic is particularly challenging. Therefore, we designed an optimized low-complexity data aligner as shown in Fig.5. The offset of each intermediate RAM is precomputed according to mod(bi,j, P ) and stored in the look-up tables. To achieve data alignment, data read out from RAMs (or data updated by processing units) is spliced with data read out from RAMs (or data updated) of the previous clock, and the location of the output window is determined by the offset value to obtain adjacent P LLRs. Then the adjacent P LLRs can be sent to the processing units (or written back to the RAMs).
4) Controller: The controller controls the entire decoding process. The status of the controller depends on three counters a, b, c. First, the decoder receives channel LLRs and writes them into CMB to finish initialization. Second, in the status of the iteration loop, the controller generates the memory addresses and enable signals to access data in CMB and IMB. These read LLRs are data aligned and sent to processing units, where the C2V message and V2C message are calculated. Then the updated values are data aligned and written back in IMB. Third, if the number of iterations is smaller than the predetermined iteration number, the decoder starts the next iteration of decoding, otherwise the controller finishes the decoding process and jumps to the OUTPUT status to output the decoded bits stored in the HDMB. As shown in TABLE I, the proposed decoder occupies slice look-up-tables (LUTs) at 46294, flip-flops (FFs) at 39103, BRAMs at 88 and achieves a decoding throughput of 1.02Gb/s at 10 iterations at 250MHz. The layered scheduling LDPC decoder in [16] achieves a high decoding throughput of 4.1Gbps, but large size barrel shifters with 511 bits and large memory word with 511×47 bits consume considerable amount of hardware resources and are not suitable for FPGA design. The decoder in [17] achieves a decoding throughput of 172Mb/s, which cannot meet the high-speed satellite communications.The decoder in [18] overlaps the check and variable node updates and process two frames at the same time alternately, which achieves a high throughput but requires complicated addressing schemes and control logic and increases resource overhead (double memory, 18% LUT consumption and 55% register consumption than our design).The proposed decoder reaches an excellent compromise between decoding throughput and hardware complexity, which meets the high speed near-Earth applications.
V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we proposed an efficient enhanced partiallyparallel LDPC decoder based on CCSDS standard. Benefitting from the well-designed decoder architecture, hardware complexity and decoding latency is reduced. By performing parallel operations and using optimized data aligners, decoding throughput is enhanced and memory access conflicts are avoided. The implementation results show that the decoder
