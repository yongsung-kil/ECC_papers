Construction of Time Invariant Spatially Coupled LDPC Codes Free of Small Trapping Sets
Sima Naseri and Amir H. Banihashemi , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract— In this paper, we propose a design technique for the construction of variable-regular time-invariant spatially-coupled low-density parity-check (SC-LDPC) codes with small constraint length and low error floor. The proposed technique reduces the error floor by imposing simple constraints on the short cycles in the code’s Tanner graph, which in turn, result in the elimination of the most dominant trapping sets of the code. In some cases, we also derive lower bounds on the syndrome former memory for satisfying such constraints. The designed codes are superior to the state-of-the-art in terms of error floor performance and/or decoding complexity and latency.
Index Terms— Spatially-coupled low-density parity-check (SC-LDPC) codes, SC-LDPC code construction, constraint length, error floor, trapping sets, short cycles, girth.
I. INTRODUCTION
A LTHOUGH spatially-coupled low-density parity-check (SC-LDPC) codes achieve capacity asymptotically [1], they, similar to their block counterparts, also suffer from error floor at finite block lengths [2]. A number of recent papers have designed SC-LDPC codes with low error floor [3]–[6]. In [3], [4], for given variable node degree dv and check node degree dc of an SC-LDPC code, the authors have designed exponent matrices that achieve a certain girth g with minimum constraint length νs = (m + 1)dc, where m indicates the memory of the code. The memory and constraint length reduction is important as both the complexity of encoding, through shift registers, and that of decoding, using a sliding window decoder, increase linearly with νs and m. The decoding latency also increases linearly with m. The error floor of the codes of [3], [4], was then improved in [5] (for the same values of dv, dc, g and m) by avoiding certain trapping sets (TSs) in the code’s Tanner graph. Most recently, the authors in [6] proposed a technique based on edge coloring to design time-invariant SC-LDPC codes free of some small TSs.
Trapping sets can be categorized into different categories such as elementary TSs (ETS), leafless ETSs (LETS), nonelementary TSs (NETS), absorbing sets (ABS), elementary

ABSs (EABS), and stopping sets (SS). Depending on the channel, the quantization, and the decoding algorithm, a specific category of TSs is responsible for the errors. For example, stopping sets are responsible for the error floor of LDPC codes over the binary erasure channel (BEC) under the belief propagation decoding algorithm [7]. For the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel and the binary symmetric channel (BSC), the problematic structures are LETSs for variable-regular LDPC codes [8]–[10], and the wider category of ETSs for irregular codes [11]. Absorbing sets [12], [13] are known to be the culprits for bit-flipping algorithms [12] and quantized decoders over the AWGN channel [13], [14]. There has been much research on characterization and search algorithms for different categories of TSs, and the computational complexity of finding them. Stopping sets have been studied in [7], [15]–[19], absorbing sets in [13], [19]–[21], and LETSs, ETSs and NETSs have been the subject of extensive studies in a number of publications [8]–[11], [16], [18], [21]–[25]. For the error floor analysis of LDPC codes based on TSs, see, e.g., [26], [27], and the references therein. Regardless of the category of TSs, the most dominant TSs contain short cycles [19]. Asvadi et al. [28] designed quasi-cyclic (QC) block LDPC codes with low error floor by eliminating short cycles that were part of the dominant TSs of the code. In [29], the progressive-edge-growth (PEG) algorithm [30] was modified to improve the ETS distribution of the constructed graph. Other designs that improve error floor performance of LDPC codes by avoiding certain short cycles and their children in the code’s Tanner graph can be found in [5], [31]–[36].
Most of the literature on the construction of SC-LDPC codes is devoted to codes with small variable node degrees such as dv = 3 and dv = 4 [4], [5], [37]–[39]. The advantage of lower node degrees is the lower decoding complexity and memory requirements. Moreover, a vast majority of the SC codes in the literature have girths 6 or 8 [37]–[39]. Such values of girth avoid cycles of length 4 that are known to be the most detrimental to the performance of iterative decoding algorithms. In this work, we consider codes with dv = 3, 4, 5, and girth g = 6, 8. For a given degree distribution, rate and girth, we design variable-regular time-invariant SC-LDPC codes with a small constraint length and low error floor. To lower the error floor, we impose certain constraints on specific short cycles in the Tanner graph of the code, which in turn eliminate a set of most dominant TSs of the code. In some cases, we also derive lower bounds on the memory of codes that satisfy the above mentioned constraints. Although the

techniques used for the derivation of such bounds are similar to those previously used in the existing literature, such as [3], [40], the application of such techniques to impose constraints on specific categories of cycles of a certain length is novel. Such constraints are quite different than the constraints used in [3], [40] that indiscriminately avoid all cycles of a certain length.
Our design technique has advantages over the existing work in terms of both complexity of the design and the performance of designed codes. In particular, compared to the codes designed in the most recent publication [6], our designed codes have significantly lower constraint length for elimination of the same set or a larger set of targeted TSs. Furthermore, many of our designed codes with g = 6 and dv = 4 and 5 have a smaller memory m and constraint length νs, and a better error floor performance compared to the codes in [3], [5], which have the same degree distribution and rate but a larger girth of g = 8. Even for the case of dv = 3, where the designed codes here have a larger memory than those in [3], [5], we demonstrate that for similar decoding complexity and latency, the designed codes can significantly outperform their counterparts from [3], [5], in the error floor region. We also compare our designed codes with QC SC-LDPC codes in the literature that have the same degree distribution, and show that, for the same decoding complexity and latency, the designed codes outperform their QC counterparts.
In general, we note that while increasing the girth of a code (Tanner graph) is often used as a technique to improve its performance (see, e.g., [3], [4]), it is well-known that girth alone is not necessarily the best metric of the code’s performance in either the waterfall or the error floor region [41], [42]. The results presented in this paper also provide further evidence of this for SC codes, by demonstrating that some of the constructed codes with g = 6 can outperform their counterparts with girth 8. This is due to the fact that imposing a larger girth value on a Tanner graph creates constraints that would limit the available degrees of freedom for other constraints such as the removal of TSs. In this context, although increasing the girth removes some of the harmful trapping sets indiscriminately, it also removes some that are benign. Our experiments also show that increasing the girth, i.e., eliminating all the cycles of the shortest length, often comes at the expense of increasing the multiplicity of the other larger cycles in the graph. This could worsen the performance of the code if the most harmful structures responsible for decoding failures contain such larger cycles.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows: In Section II, we provide some preliminaries about SC-LDPC codes and TSs. The proposed techniques for the construction of SC-LDPC codes with dv = 3, 4 and 5 are presented in Sections III, IV and V, respectively. This is followed by design examples and extensive numerical results in Section VI. We end the paper with some concluding remarks in Section VII.

An SC-LDPC code can be represented by a Tanner graph,

where each column (row) of HSC is represented by a variable (check) node. There is an edge between a variable node j and

a check node i in the Tanner graph if and only if the element

in the row i and column j of HSC is equal to one. In practice,

SC-LDPC codes are decoded using iterative algorithms with

a sliding window based on belief propagation [43]. The

decoding window consists of a number of blocks of check

nodes (each block containing γ check nodes) and their adjacent

variable nodes. For each window position, the sliding window

decoder makes a decision on the leftmost block (c leftmost

bits) within the decoding window, and then the window slides

down by one block (γ check nodes), and moves to the right

by one block (c bits). The minimum window size is (m + 1)

blocks, i.e., γ(m + 1) check nodes and their adjacent bits.

To have a small performance degradation compared to the

case of full window size (when the iterative belief propagation

algorithm is applied to the full Tanner graph), one requires a

window size of about 5(m + 1) blocks. This corresponds to

5c(m + 1) bits.

Trapping sets [44], as small substructures of an SC-LDPC

code’s Tanner graph, are the cause of the error floor. We define

a TS as a subset of the variable nodes of the Tanner graph.

Consider a TS S in a Tanner graph G with the set of variable

nodes U and the set of check nodes W , and let G(S) be

the subgraph induced in G by S. If S has “a” variable

nodes and G(S) has “b” odd-degree check nodes, we say S

belongs to the (a,b) class. In this paper, with a slight abuse of

terminology, we sometimes use the term “trapping set” to refer

to G(S). This will be clear from the context. An elementary TS

(ETS) is a TS whose induced subgraph contains only degree-

1 and degree-2 check nodes. A leafless ETS (LETS) is an

ETS in which every variable node is connected to at least

two check nodes of degree-2. The most harmful TSs in the

error floor of variable-regular LDPC codes3 over the AWGN

channel are LETSs [9], [10]. In this work, similar to [6],

we focus on LETSs as the main source of the error floor.

To represent LETSs, we use normal graphs [9]. The normal

graph of a LETS is obtained by removing all check nodes of

degree 1 and their incident edges, and replacing every check

node of degree 2 and their two incident edges by a single

edge in the LETS subgraph. In our design, we use the layered

superset (LSS) characterization of [9], [45]. The LSS approach

characterizes every LETS as an embedded sequence of LETSs

that starts from a simple cycle or a small prime structure [45]

and grows one variable node at a time until it reaches the

targeted structure. This expansion technique is called dotm

in [10], where the new added variable node is connected to

the parent structure through m degree-1 check nodes (m ≥ 2)

to generate the child structure.

Proposition 1: An SC-LDPC code with g = 6 and dv = 3 is free of all LETSs within the range R1 : {a ≤ 5, b ≤ 2}, if and only if all 6-cycles are LSS-nE.
Proof: In a code with dv = 3 and g = 6, there exist three different classes of LETS within the range R1: (4, 0), (4, 2) and (5, 1) [10]. Classes (4, 2) and (4, 0), each has only one structure. These structures are LSS children of a 6-cycle, through the application of dot2 and dot3, respectively. There is also only one structure in the (5, 1) class, which is the LSS child of the (4, 2) LETS structure. The assumption of LSS-nE 6-cycles, thus, ensures that (4, 0), (4, 2) and (5, 1) LETSs are

avoided. On the other hand, it is easy to show by contraposition

that if all LETSs within the range R1 are non-existent, then all 6-cycles are LSS-nE.

Definition 2: A TS S is twice-LSS expandable if S is expandable through two distinct variable nodes using dot

Consider a TS S in a Tanner graph G, where G has a set of variable nodes U , and a set of check nodes W . In order to identify if S is twice-LSS expandable, one can consider the multiset M whose elements are the variable nodes from the set U \S that are connected to the odd-degree check nodes of G(S), i.e., M is the union of the neighborhoods of the nodes in Γo(S), excluding the variable nodes of S, allowing for multiplicity. In this section, we construct SC-LDPC codes with small values of m satisfying the conditions of propositions in previous sections to eliminate different ranges of LETSs for a given degree distribution and girth g. For this, for a given value of m, we use a greedy column by column search of the exponent matrix such that at least one zero entry exists in each column [48]. For each column, if all possible values are exhausted with no success, we backtrack to the previous column and check the rest of eligible assignments for that column. If, for a given m, an exponent matrix satisfying the design constraints is not found within a certain period of time τ , we move on to the next m value. VII. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we designed time-invariant SC-LDPC codes with girths 6 and 8 and variable node degrees 3, 4 and 5, that are free of some harmful LETSs, and have small constraint lengths. Our approach is based on imposing simple constraints on the short cycles in the Tanner graph of the code. Using extensive simulation results, we showed that the designed codes have smaller constraint length, superior LETS distribution, and lower error floors compared to some of their counterparts in the literature. In other cases, where the designed codes have similar or larger constraint lengths in comparison with the state-of-the-art codes, we demonstrated that, for the same decoding complexity and latency, the designed codes still have a superior LETS distribution and error floor. In particular, we showed that our designed codes with a smaller girth could compete with the state-of-the-art with a larger girth.
The bounds derived in this paper on the memory size of SC-LDPC codes are based on avoiding different types of short cycles that are part of the structure of a targeted set of TSs. These bounds in some cases are rather loose. Derivation of tighter bounds on the memory size to eliminate specific TS structures or low-weight codewords would be an interesting topic for future research. Another subject of interest would be to apply the techniques of this paper to design QC-SC-LDPC codes based on a general edge spreading process.

He joined the Faculty of Engineering, Carleton University, in 1998, where he is currently a Professor with the Department of Systems and Computer Engineering.
