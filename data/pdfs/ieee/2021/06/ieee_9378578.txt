ADMM Check Node Penalized Decoders for LDPC Codes
Haoyuan Wei and Amir H. Banihashemi , Senior Member, IEEE

AbstractвҖ” Alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) is an efficient implementation of linear programming (LP) decoding for low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. By adding penalty terms to the objective function of the LP decoding model, ADMM variable node (VN) penalized decoding can suppress the non-integral solutions and improve the frame error rate (FER) performance in the low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) region. In this paper, we propose a novel ADMM check node (CN) penalized decoding algorithm. Codeword solutions which satisfy all parity-check equations will have smaller penalty values than non-codeword solutions, including the non-integral solutions. We discuss the required properties of CN-penalty functions, propose a few functions that satisfy those properties, and study their performance/complexity trade-offs. We also investigate the convergence properties of the proposed algorithm and prove that its performance is independent of the transmitted codeword. Using Monte Carlo simulations and instanton analysis, we then demonstrate that the proposed CN-penalized decoder outperforms ADMM VN penalized decoders in both waterfall and error floor regions. This comes at the expense of some increase in the decoding complexity.
Index TermsвҖ” LP decoding, alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM), binary low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, ADMM penalized decoding, instanton analysis.
I. INTRODUCTION
T HE linear programming (LP) decoding of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes was firstly proposed by Feldman et al. [1] for binary memoryless channels. Then, LP decoding was generalized to non-binary LDPC codes, polar codes and other channels [2]вҖ“[5]. Compared to belief propagation (BP), LP decoding has in general a better error floor performance. Moreover, while LP decoding always converges to a solution, this may not be in general the case for BP, for which oscillations or chaotic behavior may occur [6]. Another important property of LP decoding, that BP lacks, is the вҖңmaximum-likelihood (ML) certificate.вҖқ This property guarantees that an integral solution of an LP decoder is the ML solution. In addition, lending the tools from optimization theory, one can analyze the complexity and the convergence of the LP decoder, and obtain bounds on its error-correcting

performance [7]вҖ“[10]. Solving the LP model by a standard LP solver, however, leads to high time complexity. Therefore, several efficient algorithms were proposed to solve the LP model with lower complexity [11]вҖ“[16]. In [16], the LP decoding model was solved by the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM). The ADMM framework [17] enables the full utilization of the sparseness of the LDPC code and therefore, reduces the complexity of LP decoding significantly. Recent research works on ADMM decoding of LDPC codes mainly focus on two subjects: further reducing the complexity, and improving the error correction performance.
To reduce the complexity of ADMM decoding, most works [18]вҖ“[27] propose to simplify the check polytope projection operation, the most time-consuming and complicated component in ADMM decoding. Among them, projection algorithms in [23]вҖ“[25] have been proven to converge with linear complexity. Some other works devised better scheduling strategies to accelerate the convergence speed. For example, the layered scheduling and the node-wise scheduling were introduced in [28] and [29], respectively. The software implementation of ADMM on multi-core processors was studied in [30] to accelerate the software simulations on powerful workstations.
In order to improve the frame error rate (FER) performance of ADMM decoding at low signal-to-noise ratios (SNR), penalty terms are added to the objective function of the LP model to penalize the pseudo-codewords. To simplify the decoding, the binary constraints for variables are relaxed in the LP model. This introduces non-binary solutions to the feasible region. These non-binary solutions are called pseudocodewords and may have smaller objective function values than the transmitted codewords (see, e.g., [31]). Since the LP model (without penalty terms) is convex, the application of the ADMM technique to this model always outputs a solution that has the smallest objective function value from the feasible region. As a result, pseudo-codewords may appear as the output and degrade the error correction performance. The additional penalty terms increase the objective value of pseudo-codewords and thus add barriers for the pseudocodewords to appear as an output.
Several simple and efficient functions that can be used as penalty terms were given in [32]. These functions take the value of each variable as their argument and penalize the nonintegral variables in the range [0, 1]. The closer the variable to 0.5, the larger the function value would be. For irregular codes, Jiao et al. proposed the idea to assign different penalty coefficients to variable nodes with different degrees [33].

They efficiently optimized all penalty coefficients by differential evolution and further improved the performance of the decoder. In [34], Wang et al. used a segmented function as the penalty term and also optimized the relevant parameters by differential evolution. We call these decoders ADMM variable node (VN) penalized decoders because all of them penalize the invalid solutions with respect to each variable node independently.
In this paper, different from existing ADMM VN-penalized decoders, we propose a novel ADMM penalized decoder with penalty terms related to check nodes (CN). There is one penalty term corresponding to each check node, which is a function of the values of all the neighboring variable nodes of the check node. We thus refer to the proposed decoder as an ADMM CN-penalized decoder. The values of penalty terms for all codewords are the same and smaller than the values for invalid solutions, including binary solutions which cannot satisfy all parity-check equations, and non-binary solutions. Therefore, invalid solutions are penalized more and are thus less likely to result in the minimum objective function value.
The proposed decoding algorithm is different from ADMM VN-penalized decoding in three aspects. Firstly, the number of penalty terms is different. For the former, it equals the number of check nodes and for the latter, it equals the number of variable nodes. Secondly, the number of arguments used in one penalty term is different. For the proposed algorithm, it is the number of neighboring variable nodes of the corresponding check node. For the ADMM VN-penalized algorithm, it is always equal to one. This means that for ADMM CN-penalized decoding, one variable node could be penalized multiple times and several variable nodes could be penalized together. Thirdly, the design philosophies of the two decoders are different. In the proposed decoder, the unsatisfied check nodes are penalized while in the ADMM VN-penalized decoder, variable nodes with non-integer values are penalized. It should be noted that the non-integral variable nodes are also penalized indirectly in the proposed ADMM CN-penalized model. We evaluate the performance of different decoding methods in the low and high SNR regions via the Monte Carlo simulations and the instanton analysis, respectively. Monte Carlo simulations cannot be readily used in the high SNR region due to the excessive time that they take to be completed. The major contributions in this paper are summarized as follows:
1) We propose an ADMM CN-penalized decoding model and a class of check node based penalty functions;
2) We derive the iterative message-passing algorithm for the ADMM CN-penalized decoding model;
3) We analyze the convergence of the ADMM CNpenalized decoding algorithm, show the all-zero assumption holds for the proposed decoder, and discuss the complexity of the proposed decoder with different penalty functions;
4) We show that the ADMM CN-penalized decoder improves the FER performance of the ADMM based LP decoding significantly at low SNR values and in many

cases, has a superior performance compared with the ADMM VN-penalized decoders; 5) We develop the instanton search algorithm for the ADMM CN-penalized decoder and use the instanton information to analyze the performance of the ADMM CN-penalized decoder in the high SNR region.
The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. In Section II, we briefly review ADMM decoding and ADMM VN-penalized decoding. Section III presents the proposed ADMM CN-penalized decoding with different penalty functions. Section IV contains the instanton analysis technique used for the asymptotic performance estimation in the high SNR region. Simulation results are given in Section V, and conclusions are made in Section VI.

This property encourages solutions that are close to 0 or 1 values. In Property 5, the only requirement on a and b is a = b. IV. INSTANTON ANALYSIS

The error correction performance of LDPC codes at high SNR values is difficult to be obtained by Monte Carlo simulations because of the huge amount of calculations and limited computational resources. Different methods have been proposed to estimate the FER performance of BP decoding at high SNR values, such as importance sampling [35]вҖ“[37], and the linear state-space model [38]. These methods are all based on the knowledge of trapping sets which are the sub-graphs of the Tanner graph that can trap the decoder. To estimate the error floor of LDPC codes, another technique, called instanton analysis, was introduced in [39]. Instantons are the set of most probable noise configurations which lead to decoding failures. Therefore, the minimum norm of the instantons can be used to predict the slope of the FER curve at high SNR values [40], [41]. More specifically, the slope of the FER curve for the AWGN channel is given by

exp(вҲ’ ins 22s2/2),

If we were to estimate the actual FER curve, the multiplicity and the curvature factor of the instantons have to be taken into account. As pointed out in [41], one may assume that the multiplicity is equal to 1. The study of the curvature factor is not conducted in the literature and is beyond the scope of this paper.
