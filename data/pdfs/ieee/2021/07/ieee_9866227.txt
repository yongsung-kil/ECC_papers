Combination of Multi-impairments Compensation and Decoding for LDPC-coded CO-OFDM via Deep Learning

Ying Han, Yuanxiang Chen*, Jia Fu, Yongtao Huang, JiaHao Li, Yijun Deng, Kaile Li, Shangjing Lin and Jianguo Yu
State Key Laboratory of Information Photonics and Optical Communications, Beijing University of Posts and Telecommunications, Beijing 100876, China
*chenyxmail@gmail.com
Abstract: We propose a combination scheme of multi-impairment compensation and LDPC decoding based deep learning for LDPC-coded CO-OFDM system. Experiments show it can greatly reduce the BER and decoding complexity in different transmission scenarios.

1. Introduction
With the increasing demand for data service, fiber optic communication, as the basic support of communication, is confronted with unprecedented challenges. The effective methods to increase transmission capacity include high order phase modulation and channel multiplexing. However, with the increase of modulation order and channel multiplexing density, the transmission distance of high-speed optical fiber communication system is also limited. Impairment compensation and channel coding are two effective ways to improve performance. The former improves the signal’s anti-interference ability, while the latter compensates for impairments. Therefore, the integration of the two schemes will produce unexpected results. In recent years, deep learning technology has been widely used in the field of optical transmission to deal with various complex problems. Without relying on specific model, deep neural network (DNN) can perform flexible statistical analysis of complex impairments of optical fiber transmission system [1]. Some researchers found that the DNN can perfectly learn the encoding structure with noiseless code words as training data [2]. Toshiaki thinks that the training samples with noise limit the learning ability of DNN [3]. For this reason, most schemes are a combination of a denoising module and a decoder[4,5]. These solutions have not used deep learning-based decoders.
In this paper, we propose a combination scheme of multi-impairment compensation and LDPC decoding based deep learning for LDPC-coded CO-OFDM system. Firstly, we build a multi-impairment compensation autoencoder (MICAE) to capture and compensate impairments suffered in the optical fiber transmission. The MICAE consists of two DNN with corresponding structures called encoder and decoder, which are respectively applied to the transmitter and the receiver of the transmission system. Through a large amount of effective data training, the AEN can flexibly adjust its structure and accurately capture multiple impairments in the channel. Secondly, we designed a DNN decoder based on MICAE for LDPC decoding. Benefiting from impairments compensation of MICAE, DNN decoder can take full advantage of its features of low decoding delay and bit error rate (BER). We have done extensive experiments to evaluate and analyze the proposed scheme. The simulation results show that the BER performance is better than traditional decoding scheme. And the combination of impairments compensation and LDPC decoding can indeed effectively increase the transmission distance.

As shown in Fig. 1, we redesign the digital signal processing (DSP) module of the LDPC-coded CO-OFDM communication system. During training, the signal impairments require MICAE to find a robust signal expression suitable for transmission in the channel. After training, the MICAE is deployed on the transmitter and receiver of the transmission system. For details, refer to our previous work [1]. The input and output size of the DNN decoder should match the H matrix size. DNN decoder includes three hidden layers, the number of nodes in each layer is 1024, 256, 64, and the activation function is Relu. The output layer uses Sigmoid as the activation function, which can limit the output to (0,1). Each sub-block is composed of dense layer, batch normalization, activation function, and dropout. The input of the training sample comes from the impaired LDPC codeword obtained at the receiver, and the Label comes from the input of the LDPC encoder at the transmitter. During training, the DNN decoder optimizes network parameters and learns encoding strategies by continuously reducing losses. After training, DNN decoder is applied to the receiver of the CO-OFDM system transmission system and is responsible for decoding the demodulated bit sequence.

The simulation schematic of proposed LDPC-coded CO-OFDM transmission system is shown in Fig.2. The transmission signal is generated offline by the DSP module at the transmitter. Different from the traditional method, we have added MICAE and DNN decoder to DSP module. The electrical signal is converted into optical signal by DAC, IQ modulator and other processes. The optical signal is sent into the optical fiber, which have N spans, each consisting of 80 km standard single mode fiber (SSMF) and erbium-doped fiber amplifier (EDFA). Then, the coherent detection will restore the optical signal that has suffered channel impairments into an OFDM frame. The receiver's DSP will reconstruct the transmission signal offline. In the optical transmission system, the linewidth of the transmit laser and local oscillator laser is 100 kHz. We set the channel transmission rate to 10 or 30 GS/s, and the transmission distance to be within the range of 0~1200 KM. In the DSP module, some parameters are set as follows, the subcarrier number is set to 128, the guard interval is 8, zeros is 8, pilots is 10 and cyclic prefix is 16. In this section, we first evaluate the transmission performance of LDPC code in COOFDM systems. The check matrix size of the LDPC code we use is (16, 32). We use the traditional belief propagation (BP) decoding algorithm. As shown in Fig. 3, the BER performance is significantly improved compared with the CO-OFDM transmission system without encoding. When the transmission rate is 10 GS/s and the modulation format is QPSK or 4QAM, the LDPC code can improve the BER performance of system by at least an order of magnitude.
Then we evaluate the transmission performance of CO-OFDM system with the combination of impairments compensation and LDPC code. We add the trained MICAE to the DSP of the simulation system in order to offline compensate for multiple impairments of signal suffered by the optical transmission. The configuration of LDPC and simulation system is the same as the previous experiment. We measure the Q factor performance of the COOFDM transmission system in different transmission scenarios. The experiment results are shown in Fig. 4, where the dotted line represents the Q factor performance of the system after adding MICAE, and the solid line is used as a control, indicating the system that only uses LDPC codes. Obviously, the MICAE-integrated scheme can compensate for the damage suffered by the system and release the superior performance of the LDPC code. We also measured the BER performance of the system, as shown in Fig. 5. The BER of the system can be further reduced by adding MICAE, which fully proves the superiority of our proposed integration scheme.

Finally, we trained a DNN decoder for LDPC code. We replaced the previous BP decoder with DNN decoder. The experiment results are shown in Fig. 5. The BER performance improvement brought by the DNN decoder is close to but not as good as the BP decoder. However, with the increase of the transmission distance and rate, the signal will suffer more serious impairments during the transmission. Therefore, the iterations of the BP decoder will also increase, which causes the consumption of decoding time and the increase of the transmission delay. On the other hand, once DNN decoder is trained, its parameters will not change, and the time cost of decoding is fixed. In conclusion, it is a better choice to use DNN decoder because of the similar BER performance in highspeed and long-distance transmission.

4. Conclusions
In this paper, we propose a combination scheme of MICAE and DNN decoder for LDPC-coded CO-OFDM system. We verified the superiority of the proposed scheme through experiments in different transmission scenarios. With the assistance of MICAE, LDPC decoder can give full play to its performance advantages. When we replace the LDPC decoder with DNN, although the performance drops a little, it is suitable for long-haul highspeed optic transmission scenarios.
