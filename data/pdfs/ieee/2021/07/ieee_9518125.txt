Quasi-Cyclic Protograph-Based Raptor-Like LDPC Codes With Girth 6 and Shortest Length
Farzane Amirzade, Mohammad-Reza Sadeghi and Daniel Panario, Senior Member, IEEE

2021 IEEE International Symposium on Information Theory (ISIT) | 978-1-5386-8209-8/21/$31.00 ©2021 IEEE | DOI: 10.1109/ISIT45174.2021.9518125

Abstract—We consider multiple-edge QC-LDPC codes with a base matrix of large size. We propose a new method, the degree reduction method, to obtain exponent matrices of these codes which considerably reduces the complexity of the search algorithm. We also provide a necessary and sufficient condition to avoid 4-cycles from occurrence in the Tanner graph of codes obtained using our method. Then, we apply our method to quasi-cyclic protograph-based Raptor-Like LDPC (QC-PBRL-LDPC) codes whose base matrices are multiple-edge. Numerical results show that as a consequence of this study we can obtain the minimum lifting degree of QC-PBRL-LDPC codes with girth at least 6. Thus, the lengths of the obtained codes are much smaller than those of their counterpart shortlength codes in the literature.

Index Terms—Multiple-edge QC-LDPC codes, girth, Tanner graph, Protograph-based Raptor-Like LDPC.

I. Introduction

Systems with incremental redundancy (IR) makes pos-

sible an efficient transmission over a time-varying channel.

It adaptively alters an effective data rate relying on the

results of actual transmissions. In such circumstances

rate-compatible (RC) codes are used. In fact, after a

decoding failure for a received high-rate codeword, the

transmitter sends extra codeword bits to the receiver

for another decoding process but now with a received

codeword of longer length. Thus, the rate of the received

codeword is changing through the decoding process. Dif-

ferent categories of high-rate codes result in a diversity in

RC codes, such as RC punctured convolutional codes [1],

RC punctured turbo codes [2] and RC-LDPC codes [3].

Protograph-based Raptor-Like (PBRL) codes [4] are RC-

LDPC codes in which the high-rate part is a protograph-

based LDPC code and all additional bits, which are related

to the low-rate part, have degree 1. A protograph is a small

size bipartite graph whose adjacency matrix is taken as a

base matrix W of protograph-based LDPC codes. A single-

edge protograph corresponds to a W with entries 1 or 0. If

W includes larger values, then the code is multiple-edge.

In order to construct the protograph of a PBRL-LDPC

code with a certain rate, we consider the protograph of

an LDPC code with a highest rate and a certain size

(HRC part) whose base matrix is denoted by WHRC. Then, adding each bit which is related to a 1-degree variable-node

causes a reduction in the code rate. This process of adding

redundant bits continues until we get a PBRL-LDPC code

of our desired code rate. A check-node connected to each 1-

degree variable-node is also connected to variable-nodes of

the HRC part. The bipartite graph between the variable-

nodes of the HRC part and the new check-nodes connected

to 1-degree variable-nodes is the protograph of the low-

rate incremental redundancy code (IRC) whose adjacency

matrix is WIR[C. Thus, th]e base matrix of PBRL-LDPC

Through the process of constructing the protograph of a short length PBRL-LDPC code it is important to keep the number of parallel edges between the variable-nodes of the HRC part and check-nodes of IRC part as minimum as possible [4]. This property avoids detrimental graphical structures, such as short cycles and trapping sets, as much as possible in the Tanner graph (TG). The resulting protograph is lifted using possible edge permutations and if circulant permutations are used, then the resulting code is quasi-cyclic (QC) PBRL-LDPC code.
There are two methods for the lifting process. The first one is a two-stage method [5] where we lift parallel edges with a lifting degree more than the maximum degree of a variable-node in the protograph. This stage yields a singleedge protograph from the original protograph. Then, we apply the lifting process to the new single-edge protograph with a specific lifting degree. The second method uses a direct lifting which is applied to the original protograph.
One of the merits of QC-PBRL-LDPC codes is their importance in 5 generation LDPC (5G LDPC) codes. In fact, among several channel coding schemes, QC-PBRLLDPC codes are accepted for standard 5G LDPC codes [6]. There are two protographs which are adopted for 5G LDPC codes. One of them in [6] is a single-edge protograph with a base matrix of size 46 × 68.
Similar to other types of QC-LDPC codes, when it comes to the lifting process of QC-PBRL-LDPC codes, different features of the TG such as short cycles, girth, that is the length of the shortest cycle, and other detrimental graphical structures like trapping sets are important aspects to consider. In [7] to construct parity-check matrices of QC-PBRL-LDPC codes with girth 6 the two-stage method is used for the lifting process, and to obtain the circulant permutations suitable for a girth-6 TG, the progressive edge growth (PEG) algorithm is applied to the protograph. In [4], the direct method and PEG algorithm are implemented to the protograph to obtain girth-6 QCPBRL-LDPC codes with a small number of 6-cycles.
An advantage of QC-LDPC codes is for checking 2kcycles of the TG using the exponent matrix and Fossorier’s equation [8]. We can consider a QC-LDPC code in terms of its girth, the existence or non-existence of a specific cycle length and thus, trapping sets. However, a rise in the size of W causes an increase in the complexity of checking cycles and makes it difficult the construction of an exponent matrix with a certain girth. For example, constructing the exponent matrix of size 46 × 68 for the single-edge QC-LDPC code used in 5G LDPC codes with a certain girth is challenging. Such problems are even more difficult for multiple-edge protographs of large size.

An (a, b) trapping set is a set of a variable-nodes in the TG which induce a subgraph of the TG with b checknodes of odd degrees and an arbitrary number of even degree check-nodes. An (a, b) trapping set is elementary (ETS) if all check-nodes have degree 1 or 2. Considering ETSs in the TG of an irregular LDPC code is harder than regular ones because of the diversity of ETSs for each a and b. ETSs of a TG with variable-node degrees belonging to {2, 3, 4, 5, 6} are investigated in [17]. There are different methods to remove ETSs of regular LDPC codes such as the edge-coloring technique [18, 19]. Recently, a method to remove a large number of ETSs from irregular LDPC codes with girth 6 was given in [20] which implies conditions to remove 8-cycles with a chord (8-cycle-wc) from the TG resulting from the removal of small size ETSs. V. Conclusion
Quasi-cyclic protograph-based Raptor-Like LDPC (QCPBRL-LDPC) codes have a large size base matrix and constructing their exponent matrices with the smallest lifting degree, specially for multiple-edge protographs, is a difficult problem. We propose a new method which reduces the size of the search space to construct exponent matrices of such codes with the minimum lifting degree. Using our method we provide necessary and sufficient conditions to avoid 4-cycles in the TG of these codes. For future work, we plan to apply our method to avoid 6-cycles and harmful trapping sets in the TG of QC-PBRL-LDPC codes.
