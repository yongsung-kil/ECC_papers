Abstract—In wireless communication, transmission error can be caused by multi-path fading, shadowing, scattering, interference and noise. As a result, errors generated in digital communication can be classified as burst errors, random bit errors, crosstalk and echo. For reliable communication, channel coding is used which adds redundancy to the information bits. Reliable and low latency communication are crucial for future communication systems involving satellite constellations and 5G communication systems, which underscores the importance of channel coding. Various codes exist for channel coding, among which low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, polar codes and tail biting convolution codes are mentioned for 5G ultra-reliable low latency communication and enhanced mobile broadband systems. In the literature, the performance of large block-length codes has been well-studied. But these codes lead to high latency. The design of short block-length codes remains an open problem due to the trade-off between latency and performance. In this paper, we develop a systematic procedure for construction of hybrid quasi-cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes and compare the performance of the proposed approach with random LDPC and existing QC-LDPC codes for short block-lengths and phase shift keying (PSK) signal characteristics. The proposed method exploits Euclidean geometry and circulant decomposition resulting in a QC-LDPC matrix that avoids shorter girths for iterative decoding cycles. We emphasize that the proposed approach is different from the protographic construction method, masking method, identity matrix replacement method, algebraic based construction method, and base graph method used for the construction of QC-LDPC codes.

1. INTRODUCTION
Wireless communication is prone to errors due to a number of phenomena, namely multi-path fading, shadowing, interference and noise. Based on this, the errors generated in digital communication can be classified into burst errors, random bit errors, crosstalk and echo. In order to make communication reliable, channel coding techniques are used which add redundancy to information bits to counter the errors introduced in the transmission.
Error correction coding techniques encompass forward error correction (FEC) and backward error correction (BEC) coding methods. BEC can be implemented in a duplex commu-
978-1-7281-7436-5/21/$31.00 ©2021 IEEE

nication system which generates a retransmission request by the receiver upon detection of an error. FEC avoids allocation of bandwidth for such requests making it more efficient. In FEC, algorithms are implemented to detect and correct the errors. Block codes (BC) and convolutional codes (CC) are the two main classes of FEC. BC divide the message into blocks and then apply an algorithm to add redundancy to generate a codeword for transmission. CC have memory and encode information based on previously encoded information and then transmit the codewords. Some popular BC include Golay codes, Hamming codes, Reed-Solomon (RS) codes, Bose–Chaudhuri–Hocquenghem (BCH) codes and low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes.
Considering modern digital communication systems, namely the 5G communication system and satellite constellations, reliable and low latency communication is crucial making channel coding critical. Multiple codes exist for this purpose, among which tail biting CC (TB-CC), LDPC and polar codes are mentioned for 5G communication systems, specifically for ultra-reliable, low-latency communication and enhanced mobile broad-band (eMBB) communication. There is also a growing interest in channel coding optimization. Channel coding for large block-lengths has yielded impressive results. Moreover, modern schemes such as LDPC and turbo codes provide exceptional performance using less complex decoding algorithms such as iterative decoding as belief propagation (BP) for given block lengths, typically above 2000 bits [1–9]. However, the design for short block-lengths is an open problem due to conflicting requirements of reliability and low latency, that is, powerful codes with large block-lengths offer reliability at the cost of latency. On the other hand, short block codes lead to low-latency at the cost of reliability [9–17]. LDPC codes reduce implementation complexity as compared to turbo codes (TC), TB-CC and polar codes since LDPC codes do not require interleavers, trellis-based decoding algorithms and cancellation lists [3, 4, 10, 13, 14, 16, 18–21].
The construction of LDPC codes can be achieved using a number of approaches, e.g., Gallager LDPC codes, finite geometry LDPC codes, random LDPC codes [22], algebraic LDPC codes [23], protographic LDPC codes based on extrinsic information transfer (PEXIT) chart [13] and incomplete geometry based LDPC codes [24]. Some of the codes generated by these methods can be decomposed into sub-matrices exhibiting cyclic property, that is, rows of sub-matrices are cyclic shifts. Such codes are known as quasi-cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes. Popular QC-LDPC codes include finite geometry QC-LDPC [22], protographic QC-LDPC codes [14, 25] and QC-LDPC codes based on fundamental theorem of arithmetic [26]. It has also been observed that due to the structural properties of QC-LDPC codes, these are easy to implement in comparison to other variants of LDPC [25–34].
In this paper, we present the construction of QC-LDPC codes for short block-lengths by designing the parity-check matrix

2. QUASI-CYCLIC LDPC CODES
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are linear block codes and are competitive with turbo codes (TC) as these also belong to capacity approaching codes. LDPC codes with quasi-cyclic structural properties are known as quasicyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes. The QC-LDPC codes can be described by a parity-check matrix H and lie in the nullspace of the matrix as given by cHT = 0 where c is a codeword. The parity-check matrix for a QC-LDPC code has the following properties.
P1. The number of ones in each row is equal to ρ. P2. The number of ones in each column is equal to γ. P3. The number of indices with ones common between any two columns is given by λ and must not exceed one, i.e. λ ∈ {0, 1}. P4. ρ and γ are small compared to the code length and the number of rows in H respectively. P5. The × n-dimensional matrix, H is low-density because the weight r is much smaller than the matrix dimensions and given by the relation, r = ρ/n = γ/ . P6. The same weight for columns as well as rows of H yields regular QC-LDPC codes and inconsistent weight yields irregular QC-LDPC codes.
QC-LDPC codes are derived from minimal polynomials in Galois fields (GF). The construction of QC-LDPC codes can be understood from the properties of Euclidean geometry (EG) with n-points and -lines. Consider an mdimensional EG over GF (2s) represented as EG(m, 2s) where EG(m, 2s) consists of 2ms total points, with every point an m-tuple over GF (2s). There are two types of LDPC codes based on EG, namely Type-I and Type-II; both codes are dual of each other with HE2 G = (HE1 G)T . Only the Type-I EG LDPC code shows cyclic behaviour. While the Type-II EG code does not show cyclic behaviour, it can be arranged to show quasi-cyclic structural properties. The traditional approach to construction of QC-LDPC codes can be summarized in the following steps [22, 29].
S1. Segregate the columns into K cyclic classes where
2(m−1)s − 1 K = 2s − 1
so that each cyclic class contains 2ms − 1 incident vectors. S2. For each cyclic class, choose a representative row generated by the parity-check polynomial, p(x) from the generator polynomial, g(x) and generate all other rows by cyclically shifting this representative.

S3. The K-circulant sub-matrices can be concatenated to form the (2ms − 1) × (2msK − 1)-dimensional QC-matrix, H.
QC-LDPC codes can also be constructed using masking with successive cycle elimination method [35]. Based on the analysis of dispersion-based circulant permutation matrices (CPMs), the undesired CPM in H is replaced with zero matrices until a targeted masking termination condition is satisfied. The QC-LDPC codes for a given expansion factor (or lifting degree) and fundamental theorem of arithmetic [26] produce three classes of QC-LDPC codes with girth of at least 6. A finite geometry-based QC-LDPC codes are constructed using auxiliary matrix to facilitate identification and elimination of 6-cycles of the block sub-matrices obtained from EGLDPC codes [31]. Based on the randomization and circulant shift method, regular QC-LDPC codes are constructed using XOR operation, uniform distribution of non-zero elements and pairing of rows and columns [30]. The construction of QC-LDPC codes can also be achieved using base graphs (BG) or protographs and by replicating them to achieve larger graphs resulting in two different classes of protographic based QC-LDPC codes, namely BG1 and BG2 [14]. A systematic design of protograph-based QC-LDPC can also be achieved based on the elimination of trapping sets in a protograph [25].

3. CONSTRUCTION OF HYBRID QC-LDPC CODES
The construction of hybrid QC-LDPC (HQC-LDPC) codes is derived from a combination of Type-II EG based QCLDPC codes followed by a circular decomposition approach. QC-LDPC codes have a one-to-one correspondence with their parity-check matrix H so their construction is actually based on the design of the H matrix. Our proposed design of the H matrix is based on sub-matrices: decomposition matrix, permutation matrix and distribution matrix. The decomposition matrix deals with the segregation of non-zero indices of the H matrix derived from EG-based parity-check polynomial, p(x); the permutation matrix relates the non-zero shifted indices of p(x) required to satisfy the decomposition matrix indices. Finally, the distribution matrix distributes the available non-zero values into cyclic sub-matrices to construct a quasi-cyclic form of H. The procedure for the design of the H matrix is as follows.

5. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we presented a systematic procedure for the construction of hybrid quasi-cyclic LDPC codes (HQCLDPC) using three sub-matrices, exploiting Euclidean geometry and circulant decomposition properties. The proposed approach is different from the protographic construction method, masking method, identity matrix replacement method, algebraic-based construction method and base graph method used for the construction of QC-LDPC codes. The bit error rate performance of the proposed HQC-LDPC was also presented which showed that the performance of the proposed HQC-LDPC is satisfactory for short block-lengths, i.e., 256 codeword bits, with respect to modern QC-LDPC error-correcting codes. From an implementation point of
(a)
view, the proposed HQC-LDPC codes are designed in such a way that they can be implemented using existing encoder and decoder used for random LDPC despite very different structural properties of their parity-check matrices.
