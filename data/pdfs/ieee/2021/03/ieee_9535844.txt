Abstract—In this paper, we propose a principle for improving the “bit duplication” method for designing ratecompatible low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. An experiment was carried out in the Matlab to obtain codes of rate-1/4 with different variants for duplicating bits from the original irregular DVB-S2 code of rate-1/2. Experimental results show that different bit duplication variants give different performance, moreover, codes using duplication of that part of the code word, where, respectively, the columns of the check matrix have the majority of the nonzero elements, turn out to be the best.
Keywords—LDPC code, rate-compatibility, Single bit duplication, Double bit duplication, Triple bit duplication
I. INTRODUCTION
In modern telecommunication systems, the problem of reducing errors in information transmission is one of the most important at all stages of their development. To solve this problem, various error-correcting codes have been developed, in which special symbols, called check ones, are used to detect errors and, if possible, correct them on the receiving side of the communication system. Among them, some of the most powerful codes are low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. These codes were first proposed by R. Gallager [1] in 1962. But they were forgotten for many years due to their computational complexity. In recent years, due to the rapid development of computer technology and the rapid growth of the requirements for accurate communication of information, the LDPC codes once again attracted great interest of researchers in this field.
Recently, one of the most interesting aspects in the field of LDPC codes research is the development of multi-rate communication systems using the same encoder/decoder pair. This is due to the fact that depending on the specific conditions of interference in the communication channel, one or another coding rate is used. Typically, under poor conditions of the communication channel, codes with lower rates are used to increase its noise immunity, and otherwise, where there is no strong interference, codes with higher rates are used to reduce time and other resources of the communication system. In such systems, a separate special encoder/decoder pair is used for each coding rate, which increases the cost of software and hardware. For this reason, the so-called rate-compatibility problem appears, that is, to obtain different rates using only one encoder/decoder pair. For this, many techniques have been developed, such as puncturing, row combining and splitting, extending, bit duplication, and others [2]–[12]. All of these methods are aimed at obtaining a set of different rates by changing either the structure of the check matrix or the structure of the transmitted message. Among them, the bit duplication method is very effective and reliable, the features of which are that the structure of the check matrix is unchanged when switching from one rate to another, and only

the structure of the transmitted message changes. And also some parts of the codeword are duplicated to increase the reliability of the bits when transmitting over the communication channel. But this raises the problem of which bits should be duplicated and which should not. Thus, this paper presents the general principle of selecting a part of the codeword for duplication, depending on the structure of the check matrix, to achieve maximum performance.
This paper is divided into the following sections. Section II describes the bit duplication method. Section III explores the principle of bit selection for duplication. Section IV shows the simulation results and section V concludes the paper.

II. BIT DUPLICATION METHOD
Bit duplication (BD) is one of the methods designed to change the code rates, that is, to obtain a set of codes with lower rates from the original code with a higher rate. In this method, some parts of the codeword are duplicated and inserted into the place where constant bits (sequences of zeros), the total length of which is denoted by ld and is calculated by the following formula:

III. THE PRINCIPLE OF BIT SELECTION FOR DUPLICATION
Practically, in all information transmission systems, irregular check matrices are used, where the nonzero elements are unevenly distributed over the columns. In this regard, for the method of duplication bits, density distribution of the nonzero elements in the check matrix also has an important effect on the reliability of the bits when decoding at the receiver. This is theoretically explained as follows.
Let there be a parity check matrix H(8,4) of an irregular LDPC code and a codeword x = (x1,x2,0,0,x5,x6,x7,x8) (Fig.3). Moreover, the check matrix consists of information and check submatrices. In both submatrices, nonzero elements are uniformly distributed across the columns, but in the information submatrix, each column contains 2 nonzero elements, and in the check submatrix, each column contains only 1 nonzero element. Thus, at the receiver, when duplicating information bits, bits x1 and x2 are simultaneously involved in two checks, and the accuracy of their values is improved due to duplication, which also increases the accuracy of other bits involved in these checks. When duplicating of check bits, bits x5 and x6 are involved in only one check, that the accuracy of their values affects only one check equation. Therefore, we can deduce the general principle that when choosing bits for duplication, it is better to choose those that participate in a larger number of check equations.

Thus, three varieties of the bit duplication method are considered, such as single, double and triple. Moreover, the higher duplication order, the better the noise immunity of the duplicated bits. But as the order of duplication increases to a certain number, depending on the structure of the used parity check matrix and the obtained rate, the code performance begins to decrease. Since in this case, the remaining nonduplicated part increases, but the number of check equations is unchanged, which reduces the code efficiency factor, which depends on the ratio of the number of control bits to the number of unmonitored bits.

V. CONCLUSION
This paper proposes the principle of bit duplication for irregular LDPC codes. Based on the proposed principle, different methods of single, double and triple bit duplication are constructed and investigated. The research results show that the best is the method of double bit duplication (DBD3). This principle can be effectively applied to those check matrices where the density of nonzero elements is high in some columns and low in others.
