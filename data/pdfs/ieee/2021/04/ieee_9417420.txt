Machine Learning Based Early Termination for Turbo and LDPC Decoders
Anusha Gunturu, Avani Agrawal, Ashok Kumar Reddy Chavva, Senior Member, IEEE, Saikrishna Pedamalli Mobile Communications R&D, Samsung R&D Institute Bangalore, India Email: {anusha.g, av.agrawal, ashok.chavva, saikrishna.p}@samsung.com

Abstract—Turbo and low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have been chosen in wireless communications because of their near channel capacity performance. However, they consume huge power and also induce delay because of the iterative nature of the decoders. Various types of early termination (ET) techniques have been introduced within these decoders to decrease the power consumption and delay. Recently, machine learning (ML) algorithms are being explored to replace or improve the complex receiver algorithms in wireless communications, as a part of 6G research. In this paper, we propose a novel algorithm to use ML within the turbo and LDPC decoders, to identify the iteration for ET. We show that the proposed algorithm outperforms the improved hard decision aided ET method by 25% ∼ 57%, in reducing the average number of iterations (ANI) of turbo decoder at 10% block error rate (BLER), for multiple modulation schemes. We also show that the proposed ET method outperforms parity check equation ET method of LDPC decoder by 30% ∼ 36%, in reducing the ANI at 10% BLER, for multiple modulation schemes. The proposed method has negligible loss in BLER performance compared to typical implementation of fixed iteration decoders.
Keywords—Early termination, iterative decoding, LDPC codes, machine learning, neural network, turbo codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
Artificial intelligence (AI) in wireless communications has become one of the mainstream directions for the next generation mobile networks, specifically 5G and beyond. Using machine learning (ML) in wireless receiver algorithms is of great interest, as it significantly reduces the complexity of the receiver algorithms, and helps in reducing the power consumption [1]. ML has been widely investigated in multiple receiver algorithms like channel estimation and equalization, link adaptation, channel state information (CSI) feedback, and channel decoding [2] - [4]. However, usage of ML in the early termination (ET) of a channel decoder structure has not been explored in the literature to the best of our knowledge. In this paper, we take a first step towards analyzing an ET technique in an iterative channel decoder using an ML algorithm.
In wireless communications, specifically cellular, channel coding plays an important role in determining the latency and reliability of the link. Turbo and low-density parity-check (LDPC) are the channel coding schemes chosen by third generation partnership project (3GPP), for data channels in long term evolution (LTE) and new radio (NR) 5G cellular technologies, respectively, given their near capacity error correction performance [5]. Turbo and LDPC decoders operate in an iterative manner. The decoding procedure of the turbo codes involves two decoders passing the extrinsic information of the coded bits to each other,

and thereby reach convergence in an iterative manner [6]. Similarly, LDPC decoder exchanges soft information iteratively between variable and check nodes, that correspond to the parity constraints and data symbols of an LDPC codeword, respectively [7], [8]. Reducing the number of iterations in these decoders is of significant interest, given their complexity and time taken to process each iteration.
Multiple ET techniques have been proposed and proven to be efficient in solving the tradeoff between desirable performance and complexity reduction for iterative decoders. ET techniques use two types of metrics as the evaluation criteria. One is soft decision based, which uses log likelihood ratios (LLRs) of the bits as metrics, and the other is hard decision based, which uses hard bit outputs of the decoder as metrics. Some of the well known ET techniques including both soft and hard decision outputs used for turbo decoder are sign change ratio (SCR), hard decision aided (HDA) [9], sign difference ratio (SDR) [10], improved HDA (IHDA) [11], and LLR histogram [12]. Similarly for LDPC decoders, parity check equation (PCE) is the most standard ET technique used, with others being convergence of mean magnitude (CMM), variable node reliability (VNR), and so on [13]–[15]. As it can be observed, multiple methods have been proposed for turbo and LDPC decoders independently. Also, ET techniques for turbo decoder are evaluated for binary phase-shift keying (BPSK) modulation in most of the available literature [9]– [12], and results with 16 quadrature amplitude modulation (QAM) are presented in [16]. Similarly, ET techniques for LDPC are evaluated upto 64-QAM in [17] while most of the other literature exists for BPSK modulation [13]–[15]. Additionally, it can be noted that all of these ET techniques are evaluated for additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel.
In this paper, we propose a novel ET method for the iterative decoder using an ML based algorithm. The proposed ML algorithm takes the soft decision (LLR) based metric as the input at an iteration, and predicts the decoding status of the received codeword at that iteration. The main features of the proposed algorithm are:
• It is applicable for both the LTE turbo and NR 5G LDPC decoders in predicting the iteration for ET.
• The ML based algorithm is trained and tested for its accuracy with 3 types of modulation schemes: quadrature phase-shift keying (QPSK), 16-QAM, and 64-QAM.
• It contains a single neural network that works for all the codeword lengths without increasing any complexity, and also applicable to fading channel models.

II. DECODING METRIC FOR TURBO AND LDPC CODES
We consider turbo and LDPC encoder structures defined by 3GPP, for LTE and NR 5G technologies, respectively [20], [21]. Let IMAX be the maximum number of iterations for the decoder used in the implementation of standard fixed iteration decoder for turbo and LDPC codes.
A. Turbo Codes
Turbo encoder consists of two parallel concatenated convolutional codes and an interleaver. The decoder has two component decoders, and each of them uses Bahl, Cocke, Jelinek, and Raviv (BCJR) algorithm that estimates maximum a posteriori (MAP) probabilities of the LLRs [22]. The two decoders exchange the extrinsic LLRs with each other iteratively until the configured IMAX, and finally give the hard outputs as the decoded bits [6]. Typically, updating the LLRs through both the decoders is considered as one iteration for turbo decoder. In this paper, throughout, we consider each component decoder operation as one iteration to determine the ET, similar to [23].
B. LDPC Codes
LDPC encoders are the set of linear block codes defined by a sparse parity check matrix (PCM), HM×N [7]. The number of columns, N in the matrix is defined by the codeword (c) length, and the number of rows, M is defined by the number of parity check constraints of the codeword. LDPC decoder uses belief-propagation method [8], where in the variable nodes representing the columns, and the check nodes representing the rows of the PCM, iteratively exchange the reliability of each bit, and update the LLRs of the bits at every iteration. The decoding stops when the decoder reaches either IMAX, or all the parity constraints are satisfied as H.cT = 0.

C. Decoding Metric
In [12], LLR histogram obtained from each iteration is used as a metric in evaluating the early stopping criterion of a decoder. This metric achieves reasonable tradeoff between performance and complexity by reducing the average number of iterations significantly, with negligible loss in performance. Also, this metric is independent of the length of the codeword which makes it more efficient in terms of complexity while evaluating large codewords. We use the same metric in the proposed ML based ET method. We limit the range of LLRs to [−M, M ] before obtaining the histogram. This achieves a single network ML model that works for all the codewords having different ranges of LLRs. Further, the histogram values are normalized by the total number of bits in the codeword, N . We use cyclic redundancy check (CRC) to determine if the codeword is decoded successfully at the predicted iteration [15]. CRCsuccess indicates that the codeword is decoded successfully, and the CRC-failure indicates otherwise. From now on, we use CRC status to indicate the decoding process as a success or a failure. Figs. 1a and 1b show the example patterns of pre-normalized LLR histograms limited to M = 1000, for the codewords with CRC-success and CRC-failure, respectively, at iteration IMAX. We observe that the LLR histogram with CRC-success has wide separation between positive and negative LLRs indicating that they are clearly distinguishable, and can be decoded. Whereas, LLR histogram with CRC-failure resembles Gaussian shape with positive and negative LLRs merging towards zero and can not be decoded.

III. PROPOSED EARLY TERMINATION USING ML
Deep neural network (DNN) based ML models implement a learning process based on the previous data, and the corresponding experience associated with it. We propose to use one such DNN based ML model in the ET of iterative channel decoding procedure. In the proposed ET method, a DNN based ML model is embedded in the decoder architecture as shown in Fig. 2. In this algorithm, the decoder updates the normalized LLR histogram at each iteration, and feeds it to the DNN. Based on the normalized LLR histogram, the DNN sends a prediction on the decoding status of the codeword in the form of a CRC. From Fig. 2, it can be seen that the CRC check is run only for the codeword with CRC-success prediction, whereas CRCfailures predictions are terminated immediately.

The proposed ML based ET method involves the following 3 steps in identifying the iteration for ET in an iterative decoder.
• DNN Training : Train the DNN based ML model using the normalized LLR histograms and the corresponding CRC status of the codewords.
• CRC Prediction : Using the trained DNN, predict the CRC status of the codeword at every iteration for terminating the iterative decoder.
• Early Termination: Identify the iteration number for the ET of the codeword using CRC prediction.

C. Early Termination Using CRC Prediction

Once the DNN predicts the CRC status as given in (4),

we use the following conditions to terminate the decoder in

each of the prediction cases.

1) CRC-success Prediction: If the DNN predicts a

normalized LLR histogram as a CRC-success, UE shall

convert the LLRs into hard output bits, and then run CRC

check as shown in Fig. 2. If the CRC check succeeds, UE

terminates running the decoder including the DNN. If the

CRC check fails, then instead of terminating, UE continues

with next iteration to avoid the information loss due to false

prediction.

coder if the DNN predicts an LLR histogram as a CRC-

IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
In this section, we present the simulation results to benchmark the proposed ML based ET method. LTE turbo and NR 5G LDPC decoders are simulated using link level simulators implemented in MATLAB with 3GPP specified channel models : EPA-5Hz, and TDL-A with delay spread (DS) and doppler frequency (fD) set to 43 ns and 5 Hz, respectively [18], [19]. Codewords corresponding to LTE and NR 5G data channels are simulated with 15 resource blocks (RBs) in frequency domain, and 1 subframe in time domain, using the parameters listed in Table I [24], [25]. We used minimum mean-squared error (MMSE) equalizer with ideal channel estimation, for both LTE and NR 5G simulations. Each simulation is run for 2 × 104 codewords with 100 channel realizations. We evaluated multiple MCS indices for these channel models covering QPSK, 16-QAM, and 64-QAM modulation schemes, for both turbo and LDPC decoders. Each MCS index correspond to a code rate of a modulation scheme, and an MCS index combined with the number of RBs gives the codeword length [24].

The genie method is non casual, that assumes the knowledge of the transmitted codeword at the receiver, and knows the exact iteration number to terminate. It is shown in the literature as the limit of any possible ET method.
Fig. 5 shows ANI as a function of MCS index for turbo and LDPC decoders. Each point in the graph is simulated at signal to noise ratio (SNR) corresponding to 10% BLER, which is the typical operating region for LTE and NR 5G users. From Fig. 5a, we observe that the ANI increases with MCS index within a modulation scheme. And this pattern is consistent for all the methods except for fixed iteration, as it is set to be a constant value. Further, all the ET methods outperform the fixed iteration method. We also observe that the proposed ML based ET method for turbo decoder outperforms IHDA and fixed iteration methods consistently for all modulation schemes in terms of ANI reduction. The ANI of the proposed scheme is 44.65%, 37.39%, and 30.36% lower than that of the IHDA method, at MCS indices 6 (QPSK), 12 (16-QAM), and 17 (64-QAM), respectively. Overall, the proposed method requires 25% ∼ 57% lower ANI for turbo decoder, compared to IHDA method, considered across all MCS indices as shown in Fig. 5a.
From Fig. 5b, we observe that the ANI of LDPC decoder doesn’t have any increasing pattern with respect to MCS index like that of turbo decoder in Fig. 5a. However, we notice that the all the ET methods follow similar pattern. The proposed ML based ET method for LDPC decoder outperforms PCE, IHDA, and fixed iteration methods for all modulation schemes in terms of ANI reduction. The ANI of the proposed scheme is 34.52%, 32.04%, and 35.87% lower than that of the PCE method, and 25.77%, 24.6%, and 23.11% lower than that of the IHDA method at MCS indices 6 (QPSK), 12 (16-QAM), and 17 (64-QAM), respectively. Overall, the proposed method requires 30% ∼ 36% lower ANI for LDPC decoder, compared to PCE method, and 23% ∼ 32% lower ANI, compared to IHDA method, respectively, considered across all MCS indices as shown in Fig. 5b. The reduction observed in ANI is significant for both turbo and LDPC decoders, and consistent in all 3 modulation schemes.

This is because the IHDA method terminates many CRC-failure cases early indicating the CRC-success falsely, resulting in lower ANI for IHDA at ∼ 100% BLER. However, practical system’s operating point of interest for LTE and NR 5G data channels is 10% BLER as discussed earlier.
Genie method in both Figs. 6b and 7b show that the ANI increases with BLER initially, and then decrease while reaching towards 100% BLER. This is because of the assumption that the genie method knows the exact iteration to terminate. At higher BLER regions, genie doesn’t decode for most of the codewords, and terminates at iteration 0 as it knows that the codeword cannot be decoded even if it runs till iteration IMAX. Similarly, at lower BLER regions where most of the codewords are successfully decoded, genie stops at the lowest iteration the codeword can be decoded.

V. COMPLEXITY ANALYSIS

The proposed ML based early termination method reduces the required ANI for both turbo and LDPC decoders, as discussed in Section IV. However, it adds additional complexity of evaluating the ET criteria similar to the existing ET methods. Let the multiplication, addition, comparison, and division be considered as the floating point operations (FLOPS), used to determine the computational complexity as given in [27]. The proposed method additionally involves the following computations at each iteration, in comparison to typically implemented fixed iteration method for an N bit codeword.
• Computation of K bin normalized LLR histogram. • Execution of fully connected DNN model. • CRC check at the predicted iteration.
However, the computation of LLR histogram, and CRC check will be common, and similar to that of other ET methods as well at each iteration based on the metric chosen. The additional computations in the proposed ML based method are due to DNN model implementation at each iteration. Execution of fully connected DNN model in Fig.3 requires (KL1+L1L2+L2L3+3L3+K+L1+L2+L3+12) FLOPS [26]. Therefore, approximately a total of 1900 and 2600 additional FLOPS are required for executing DNN

VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we proposed an ET technique for an iterative channel decoder using ML. The proposed method is evaluated for LTE turbo and NR 5G LDPC decoders. We have shown that the proposed ET method outperforms the IHDA and fixed iteration methods for turbo decoder, significantly in terms of ANI reduction, with negligible loss in BLER performance. Similarly, it outperforms the IHDA, fixed iteration, and PCE methods for LDPC decoder, in reducing the ANI. We show that the ML based ET method consistently outperforms at all the ranges of BLER, and across all the modulation schemes: QPSK, 16QAM, and 64-QAM. We have shown that the proposed algorithm outperforms IHDA method by 25% ∼ 57% for turbo decoder, and PCE method by 30% ∼ 36% for LDPC decoder, in terms of ANI reduction at 10% BLER, considered across all the modulation schemes. We further presented the computational complexity for the proposed ML based method, and compared with the execution of one iteration of the turbo and LDPC decoders.
