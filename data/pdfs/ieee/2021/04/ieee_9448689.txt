The Effect of Coupling Memory and Block Length on Spatially Coupled Serially Concatenated Codes
Mojtaba Mahdavi, Muhammad Umar Farooq, Liang Liu, Ove Edfors, Viktor O¨ wall, and Michael Lentmaier Department of Electrical and Information Technology (EIT), Lund University, Lund, Sweden
Emails: {mojtaba.mahdavi, muhammad.umar farooq, liang.liu, ove.edfors, viktor.owall, michael.lentmaier}@eit.lth.se

Abstract—Spatially coupled serially concatenated codes (SCSCCs) are a class of spatially coupled turbo-like codes, which have a close-to-capacity performance and low error floor. In this paper, we perform a comprehensive design space exploration, revealing different aspects of SC-SCCs and discussing various design trade-offs. In particular, we investigate the impact of coupling memory, block length, decoding window size, and number of iterations on the performance, complexity, and latency of SC-SCCs. As a result, we propose design guidelines to make the code design independent of the block length. By introducing a modified window decoding schedule, we are able to demonstrate that the block length and coupling memory can be exchanged flexibly without changing the latency and complexity of decoding and without performance loss. Thus, thanks to spatial coupling, a certain code strength and performance can be achieved by either a very small block length or a large one, while the complexity and latency are fixed. Moreover, our results show that using higher coupling memory with smaller blocks can even improve the performance without increasing the latency and complexity. For all considered cases we observe that the performance of SCSCCs is improved with respect to the uncoupled ensembles for a fixed latency and complexity.
I. INTRODUCTION
Spatially coupled low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have received a lot of attention due to the threshold saturation phenomenon [1]–[3], which implies that it is possible to provably achieve capacity with low-complexity iterative belief propagation (BP) decoding. The concept of spatial coupling has been extended to turbo-like codes in [4], where it has been proven that threshold saturation also occurs for this class of codes. An information-coupled version of the turbo codes from the LTE standard was proposed in [5]. It is worth to mention that the decoding of spatially coupled codes can be done efficiently using window decoding [6]–[10].
While parallel concatenated codes (PCCs) have excellent BP decoding thresholds, they suffer from relatively high error floors that make them less attractive for ultra reliable communication scenarios (URC) like automated driving, reliable cloud connectivity, and tactile Internet, which have low error rate requirements [11]. Serially concatenated codes (SCCs), on the other hand, have lower error floors but their BP thresholds are further away from capacity. In [12] it has been demonstrated that spatial coupling leads to a new tradeoff between error floor and waterfall performance of turbo-like codes. Since spatial coupling provides some new degrees of freedom in code design, in terms of coupling memory, block size and size of the decoding window, the design space becomes huge and we have many more design choices compared to the uncoupled ensembles. Consequently, the tradeoffs between the performance, latency, and complexity and also the relation between different design parameters are not straightforward. But since the required size of the decoding window increases with the coupling memory, this option may not look appealing from a latency perspective and has not been considered in the literature. II. BACKGROUND
This section describes how to build the SC-SCCs and also presents the corresponding encoder and decoder.
A. SCC Encoder
We have employed the SCC encoder as the fundamental core to construct the SC-SCC encoder. Fig. 1(a) depicts the structure of an SCC component encoder, which is made up of two recursive systematic convolutional (RSC) encoders concatenated in a serial manner using the interleaver. The left and right RSC encoders are called the outer and inner encoders with the trellis length of K and 2K, respectively. Fig. 1(b) shows the RSC encoder, which is used throughout the paper. It has the generator polynomial of (1, 5/7) and code rate of 1/2, resulting in an overall code rate of 1/4 for the SCC encoder.
As shown in Fig. 1(a) the outer encoder receives the information sequence, u, of length K bits and produces the K-bit parity sequence pO. Then, the sequences u and pO are multiplexed and permuted to generate the 2K-bit sequence qO. This sequence is encoded by the inner encoder to produce the 2K-bits parity sequence pI . Finally, the output of the SCC encoder is v = (u, pO, pI ), which is referred as code block and will be transmitted through the channel.
B. SC-SCC Encoder
We have built the SC-SCC encoder by coupling m + 1 samples of SCC component encoders as shown in Fig. 2, where m is the coupling memory. Let us consider the encoding process at time instant t to see how the inner and outer encoders are coupled together. As shown in Fig. 2 the outer encoder receives the information bits, ut, and generates the parity sequence pOt . This implies that m + 1 should be smaller than 2K and also divide 2K. D. SC-SCC Window Decoder
Analogously to LDPC codes, the nodes in an iterative message passing decoder exchange log-likelihood ratios (LLRs) along the edges in the graph (see Fig. 3). The main difference is that here the trellises at the factor nodes are more complicated than single parity-check nodes. The inner and outer trellises are decoded using the Bahl-Cocke-JelinekRaviv (BCJR) algorithm, which calculates the maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimate of the bits. We use the compact graph representation of an SC-SCC with m = 1, shown in Fig. 3, to describe the decoding process of SC-SCCs. Let us consider a decoding window of length W blocks, which starts at time t and ends at t + W − 1, as shown by a solid rectangle in Fig. 3. Among these blocks, the first one to be decoded is referred to as the target block, which is located to the leftmost side of the window. For all the blocks with index t = t, . . . , t+W −1, first the inner and then the outer decoder perform decoding as follows.
In each iteration, the inner decoder receives three sequences: the channel LLR values defined in (1), Lch(q˜Ot ), the channel LLR values of corresponding inner parity sequence, Lch(pIt ), and the a-priori LLR values, La(q˜tO), which are obtained based on the previous extrinsic LLRs of the corresponding outer decoders. The inner decoder produces the extrinsic LLRs, Le(q˜tO), and sends them back to the connected outer decoders. Then, similarly, the outer decoder receives the channel LLR values of information sequence, Lch(ut ), the channel LLR values of corresponding outer parity sequence, Lch(ptO), and the a-priori LLRs, La(ut ) and La(ptO), which are obtained based on the previous extrinsic LLRs of the corresponding inner decoders. The outer decoder produces the extrinsic LLRs, Le(ut ) and Le(ptO), and sends them back to the connected inner decoders.
After IW iterations, where IW is the number of iterations per window position, the decoding of the target block, ut, is finished and the window is moved by one block. The same process is done for the next window, which starts at time t + 1 and ends at t + W as shown by the dashed rectangle in Fig. 3, to decode the target block ut+1.

III. DESIGN GUIDELINES FOR FLEXIBLE CHOICE OF BLOCK SIZE AND COUPLING MEMORY
This section presents the design guidelines, which let us to practically use larger coupling memories without increasing the latency and complexity. Moreover, these guidelines enable a fair comparison between different coding scenarios.
A. Using Higher Coupling Memory in a Fixed Latency
A window decoder will perform very poorly if the window size, W , is smaller than m + 1.1 Thus, if a higher coupling memory is needed, W should be increased, which considerably increases the latency as stated in (3). To solve this problem, we propose to reduce the block length, K, and increase the number of blocks per window, W , simultaneousely. As a result, a higher coupling memory can be used without changing the latency.
Fig. 4 shows an example of an SC-SCC scheme with a latency of L = 4096 bits in two cases. In Fig. 4(a), four blocks of K = 1024 bits per window are employed, which implies that the coupling memory cannot be larger than m = 3. On the other hand, the same latency is achieved in Fig. 4(b) by reducing the block length to K = 512 bits and doubling the window size while the coupling memory can be increased up to m = 7. Thus, depending on the block length, different window sizes should be considered to have a fixed latency and relax the limitation of the coupling memory. In Section IV-A, we will demonstrate how to optimize the coupling memory to achieve the best performance in a fixed latency scenario. However, there are some challenges to employ small blocks and large coupling memory, which are addressed in our scheme as described in the next subsections.
B. Continuous Encoding
The classical way of encoding the SC-SCCs is to terminate the encoder after each block [12], i.e. encoder starts and ends in the zero state. The drawback of such schemes is a significant rate loss for small block lengths, K. One of the contributions of this paper is to perform continuous encoding without termination after each block to avoid the rate loss specially for the small K. For this purpose, after encoding of the block at time t, the encoder state is passed to the encoder of the block at time t + 1. Thus, the last state of the encoder at time t is used as the starting state of the encoder at time t + 1. To represent this concept, we have added the state variable nodes to the SC-SCC graph, as shown by double circles in Fig. 3.

C. Performance Improvement of Boundaries
The traditional window decoding algorithm usually works in a block-wise basis [8] as shown in Fig. 5(a). Thus, at time instant t, the computation of α and β in the BCJR algorithm are done in the forward and backward recursions for the block ut. Then, the results at time t are used in decoding of the next block, ut+1. This method works properly for large block lengths, K. However, in case of small block lengths, running the BCJR for a very short trellis results in a poor performance at the boundaries between blocks. This is due to the unreliable states at the start and end of each trellis. Thus, the bits which are close to the boundaries will have a weak protection. To some extent this problem can be resolved by doing more iterations, but the computational complexity will be increased significantly.
To address this challenge, we propose to perform the decoding over the whole window at once. As shown in Fig. 5(b), the α computation is done in the forward recursion over the whole window at once and then β is computed in the backward recursion. Thus, regardless of the value of block length, K, and window size, W , in our scheme the BCJR algorithm is run one time per iteration over the whole window instead of W times per iteration. As a result, since the trellis length becomes large the boundary states are more reliable, which can improve the performance especially for small block lengths.

V. DESIGN TRADEOFFS
So far we assumed fixed latency and complexity in the evaluations. Now we are interested to see the performance gain of SC-SCCs if we increase the latency or the complexity.
A. Performance-Latency Tradeoff
As mentioned in Section IV-A, for a given latency, L, and complexity, the performance can be improved by increasing the constraint length C, i.e., larger m or K. Now, we want to see if for a given constraint length and complexity, it is possible to improve the performance by increasing the latency? More specifically, having considered a fixed K and m, how does the performance change if we make the window size, W , larger? We have investigated this concept and the results are shown in Fig. 10 for two arbitrary block lengths. For a given constraint length and complexity, if we only change the window size (e.g. doubling W ) we will not gain too much in performance while the latency is increased (e.g. twice latency). So, if the targeted application can tolerate the higher latencies, it is better to increase the coupling memory, m, as well to make the code stronger rather than just increasing the window size, W . As a result, this strategy provides the effective use of a certain latency to achieve better performance. It is worth to mention that, the larger window size enables us to employ a higher coupling memory. This is due to the limitations on the coupling memory, i.e. m ≤ W/2 − 1, as described in Section IV-A.
Fig. 11 shows the tradeoff between latency and performance for the scenarios in Table I. In each scenario the latency, L, and constraint length, C, are fixed and the complexity is the same for all scenarios. In this figure, x-axis shows the required Eb/N0 to achieve the BER of 10−5 in all scenarios. The markers, which tend to the lower left corner of Fig. 11 are corresponding to the scenarios with a low latency, L, and good performance. Thus, by considering this tradeoff the proper values of W , K, and m can be obtained.

VI. CONCLUSION We have carried out a comprehensive investigation to evaluate the effect of coupling memory, block length, window size, and number of iterations on the performance, complexity, and latency of SC-SCCs. Several design guidelines were proposed, which provide the flexibility to exchange the block length with the coupling memory and make the code design independent of the block length. We have demonstrated how a higher coupling memory can be used without increasing the latency and complexity. Moreover, we have shown that SC-SCCs can achieve better performance than the uncoupled ensembles with the same latency and complexity.
