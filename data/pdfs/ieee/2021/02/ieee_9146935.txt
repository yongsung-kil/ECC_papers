Improvement of Iterative Decoding With LLR Modulator by Neural Network Using Magnetic Transition Information in SMR System
M. Nishikawa 1, Y. Nakamura 1, Y. Kanai 2, H. Osawa1, and Y. Okamoto1
1Graduate School of Science and Engineering, Ehime University, Matsuyama 790-8577, Japan 2Department of Engineering, Niigata Institute of Technology, Kashiwazaki 945-1195, Japan
We have previously focused on a log-likelihood ratio (LLR) computed as the decoding reliability by a posteriori probability (APP) decoder on the low-density parity-check (LDPC) coding and iterative decoding system for the shingled magnetic recording (SMR). Then, we clarified that the LLR modulator applying a neural network realizes an effective iterative decoding. In this article, we propose a new neural network LLR modulator considering magnetic transition information obtained from LLRs to reduce the influence of intersymbol interferences (ISIs).
Index Terms— Log-likelihood ratio (LLR), magnetic transition information, neural network.

I. INTRODUCTION

V ARIOUS information storage devices are used according to purposes to store a huge amount of information. In particular, solid state devices (SSDs) with lightweight and high-speed access are rapidly gaining for consumers. Hard disk drives (HDDs) are also used for companies such as data centers and cloud storage, and are expected to have even larger capacities as near-line storage. A low-density paritycheck (LDPC) [1] encoding/iterative decoding is used for shingled magnetic recording (SMR) [2] as a signal processing method for realizing higher recording density HDDs, and a more sophisticated signal processing is needed to achieve higher recording densities. For that purpose, we showed that effective iterative decoding can be realized by a neural network log-likelihood ratio (LLR) modulator [3] selecting and modulating likely LLRs from the output sequence of the a posteriori probability (APP) decoder [4] in the LDPC encoding/iterative decoding system. In this article, we propose a new neural network LLR modulator considering magnetic transition information obtained from LLRs to reduce the influence of intersymbol interferences (ISIs). Furthermore, we evaluate the performance of the proposed LLR modulator in the iterative decoding system by the computer simulation and clarify whether the performance can improve.

III. MAGNETIC TRANSITION INFORMATION
Fig. 5 shows the magnetic transition information between the decoding target bit and the bit we consider affecting the decoding target [13], [14]. In Fig. 5, the decoding target is the bit at time k on the track #l. The influence of the ISI is considered by the transition on the track #l shown in Fig. 5(a). The influences of ITI at SMR writing are also considered by the transitions shown in Fig. 5(b) and (c). For example, the transition of Fig. 5(b) indicates that the information on the track #(l −1) partially remains on track #l, and the transition of Fig. 5(c) indicates that the information on track #l is partially erased by the track #(l + 1) [13], [14]. Fig. 6 shows the LLR distributions of APP decoder output sequence depending on the existence of magnetization reversal shown in Fig. 5, where the simulation parameters are set to SNRS,3 = 20.0 dB, iglobal = 0, and isp = 0. Fig. 6(a)–(c) corresponds to the cases in Fig. 5(a)–(c), respectively. The broken and chain lines show the distributions with and without the magnetization reversal, respectively. In Fig. 6(a), there is the big difference in the two distributions at any BAR. On the other hand, in Fig. 6(b) and (c), there is no significant difference in distribution depending on the presence of magnetic reversal at target positions except BAR = 1.0. Thus, we consider that the influence of ITI is sufficiently reduced by the array head and the TD-FIR filter in the R/W system shown in Fig. 1 [10], [11]. In Section IV, we propose the new LLR modulator using neural network to reduce the influence of ISI.

V. PERFORMANCE EVALUATION
Fig. 8 shows the number of correct LLRs among modulation candidate LLRs, where the simulation parameters are set to SNRS,3 = 21.0 dB. Fig. 8(a) and (b) shows the mse performance at isp = 0, iglobal = 0 and isp = 15, iglobal = 25, respectively. In Fig. 8 (a) and (b), the red solid and blue broken lines show the cases of the proposed and conventional LLR modulators, respectively. Also, the marks of circle, triangle, diamond, and inverted triangle correspond to the recording patterns “000” and “111”; “001” and “110”; “011” and “011”; and “010” and “101,” respectively. The conventional LLR modulator means the case without magnetic transition information. As can be seen from Fig. 8 (a) and (b), the correct LLRs after iterative decoding shown in Fig. 8(b) are more than those before iterative decoding shown in Fig. 8(a). Fig. 9 shows the probability that the recording sequence corresponds to {dk} of Fig. 7, where the simulation parameters, lines, and marks are the same as those shown in Fig. 8. Fig. 9(a) and (b) corresponds to those in Fig. 8, respectively. As can be seen from Fig. 9 (a) and (b), the probabilities after iterative decoding shown in Fig. 9(b) are higher than those before iterative decoding shown in Fig. 9(a). Furthermore, we can determine that the proposed LLR modulator has chosen of more correct candidates to modify the LLR for all recording patterns compared to the conventional LLR modulator. In particular, the proposed LLR modulator has chosen the correct LLRs in the case of “010” and “101.” Fig. 10 shows the SNRS,3 to achieve no-errors for BAR, where the simulation parameters are set to isp = 15 and iglobal = 25.
