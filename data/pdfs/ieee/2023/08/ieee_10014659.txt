Rate-Diverse Multiple Access Over Gaussian Channels
Pingping Chen , Senior Member, IEEE, Long Shi , Senior Member, IEEE, Yi Fang , Senior Member, IEEE, Francis C. M. Lau , Fellow, IEEE, and Jun Cheng , Member, IEEE

Abstract— In this work, we develop a pair of rate-diverse encoder and decoder for a two-user Gaussian multiple access channel (GMAC). The proposed scheme enables the users to transmit with the same codeword length but different coding rates under diverse user channel conditions. First, we propose the row-combining (RC) method and row-extending (RE) method to design practical low-density parity-check (LDPC) channel codes for rate-diverse GMAC. Second, we develop an iterative rate-diverse joint user messages decoding (RDJD) algorithm for GMAC, where all user messages are decoded with a single paritycheck matrix. In contrast to the conventional network-coded multiple access (NCMA) and compute-forward multiple access (CFMA) schemes that first recover a linear combination of the transmitted codewords and then decode both user messages, this work can decode both the user messages simultaneously. Extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) chart analysis and simulation results indicate that RDJD can achieve gains up to 1.0 dB over NCMA and CFMA in the two-user GMAC. In particular, we show that there exists an optimal rate allocation for the two users to achieve the best decoding performance given the channel conditions and sum rate.
Index Terms— Gaussian multiple access channel (GMAC), lowdensity parity-check (LDPC) code, extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) chart analysis.

I. INTRODUCTION
A N INTERFERENCE channel has been considered as a main concern in modern wireless systems, such as 4G/5G cellular networks and next generation WiFi networks [1], [2]. A Gaussian multiple access channel (GMAC) is a simple interference channel where two users communicate with one receiver over additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels. The capacity region of the two-user GMAC was characterized in [3] and [4], where the corner points of the capacity region can be achieved by single user decoding, and the points in between can be achieved by time sharing. Also, rate splitting and joint decoding without time sharing have been showed to attain any rate pair in the capacity region [5], [6]. Superposition coding [7], [8], the message passing algorithm (MPA), and successive interference cancellation (SIC) [9], [10] have made contributions as the solutions of GMAC in terms of coding and information theory. Moreover, Han-Kobayashi scheme has been proposed as the best known achievable solution for the two-user interference channel [11]. As a joint unique decoding, simultaneous joint decoding is proposed to recover messages, which is split into private and common part by superposition encoding [12], [13]. Since the recovery of the private part can be simply decoded as in the point-to-point communications, the main challenge lies in the transmission and recovery of the common messages in the receiver, where the interference channel can be treated as a GMAC.
In a GMAC, multiple users communicate with an intended receiver simultaneously. Among the various receivers, SIC has been widely investigated for its simple implementation by decision and cancellation successively [14], [15], [16], [17]. In a SIC-aided GMAC, the receiver has significantly different received signal powers from different users. Thus, the receiver first decodes the message corresponding to the strongest signal and removes this message from its observation. The corner points of the two-user capacity region can be achieved by SIC, while other points can be achieved by time-sharing, rate-splitting, or joint decoding. From a practical coding perspective, capacity-approaching low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes have been designed for joint decoders in two-user GMAC [18], [19], [20], [21]. A more recent study has optimized the power ratio of the two users to minimize the required SNR under the joint decoding in a GMAC [22].
Not until very recently has the notion of physical-layer network coding (PNC) been adopted into the two-user GMAC,

referred to as network-coded multiple-access (NCMA) [23]. The core of PNC is to turn superimposed signals simultaneously received from the users into useful network-coded (NC) messages [24], [25], [26], [27]. As a prevailing decoding in PNC, XOR-based channel decoding (XOR-CD) aims to decode bitwise NC messages of user messages. The first work on NCMA has used XOR-CD and multiuser decoding (MUD) to first decode the NC message and then both user messages [23]. Later, to achieve a high-efficient transmission, an NCMA system that operates with high-order modulations of QPSK and 16-QAM has been developed [28]. To exploit different channel conditions, different modulations for distinct users have been adopted to realize modulation-diverse NCMA with the same coding rate [29]. It was shown that NCMA with BPSK+QPSK has much higher throughput than the modulation-homogeneous case. More recently, a NCMA downlink system for unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) communications has been developed to improve system throughput by NCMA-based superposition coding [30].
In another line of PNC-based GMAC, compute-and-forward multiple access (CFMA) generalizes PNC to multi-user networks by utilizing structured nested lattice codes [31], [32]. The authors have extended compute-and-forward to a GMAC from a theoretical perspective [33]. With the scaled lattice codes, a single-user decoder was used to attain the dominant face of the capacity region of GMAC without time sharing. Following this study, practical off-the-shelf LDPC codes and efficient decoding algorithms have been adopted for CFMA [37]. Similar to NCMA, CFMA first decodes a linear NC combination of two codewords by XOR-CD, and then recovers the individual user codewords. It has been shown that CFMA can achieve some rate pairs on the dominant face of the capacity region, whereas the conventional SIC fails to attain these rate pairs. However, the achievable rate of XOR-CD in a two-user GMAC is limited by the worse user channel even when the other channel is error-free. In this context, CFMA may not perform well over the user channels that are significantly diverse. This is due to the fact that CFMA decodes the NC message by using the parity-check matrix of the better user channel only.
In this paper, we propose a two-user uplink rate-diverse GMAC over diverse channel conditions. We call the user with the better channel condition as the strong user and that with the worse channel as the weak user. This scheme yields flexibility in controlling the individual message rates of different users by utilizing channel state information (CSI), which are known to both the receiver and transmitter, as might be obtained through an error-free delayless feedback path [34]. The proposed system allows the users to adopt different channel codes, i.e., a low-rate code for the weak user and a high-rate code for the strong user. This work is primarily motivated by the observation that the achievable rate of XOR-CD in a two-user GMAC is limited by the worse user channel even though the other channel is error-free, and this limitation prevents the strong user from fully exploiting its superior channel condition. In this context, the goal of this paper lies in the encoder and decoder designs that can fully exploit the

diverse channel conditions in a two-user GMAC. Our main contributions are as follows.
1) For the encoder of the proposed scheme, we design practical LDPC codes for different users by two methods, i.e., row-combining (RC) method and row-extending (RE) method. The core of these methods is that the two channel codes share a common parity-check matrix with a high code rate. In particular, the parity-check matrix of the low-rate code owns a residual matrix alongside with the common matrix, and the parity-check matrix of the high-rate code is embedded in that of the low-rate code.
2) For the decoder of the proposed scheme, we propose an iterative rate-diverse joint user-message decoding (RDJD) algorithm to decode user messages within the low-rate parity-check matrix. The proposed RDJD consists of a joint user channel decoder (JUD) to estimate the joint user message and a residual user decoder (RUD) to estimate the individual user messages. It differs from NCMA and CFMA which first decode the NC message and then recover both user messages based on the decoded NC messages.
3) We analyze the convergence behavior of the iterative RDJD using an extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) chart. Analytical results show that iterative decoding between JUD and RUD provides large gains over the non-iterative one. Our simulations further demonstrate that iterative RDJD achieves significant performance gains over NCMA and CFMA. This gain can be up to 1 dB when the user channels are largely different. In particular, both analytical and simulation results indicate that there exists an optimal rate pair for the two users to achieve the best performance under given channel conditions. Moreover, we show that the proposed RDJD can approach the rate pair on the dominant face of the capacity region without rate-splitting or time-sharing.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section II elaborates the related background for GMAC. Section III puts forth the concept of rate-diverse GMAC and elaborates its encoder and decoder. Section IV investigates the iterative decoding of the proposed scheme by means of an EXIT chart analysis. Section V provides the simulated performance of the rate-diverse GMAC and validates its advantages. Finally, Section VI concludes the paper.

III. PROPOSED CHANNEL CODE AND DECODER DESIGNS
This section develops a pair of encoder and decoder for a two-user GMAC to employ different coding rates according to the channel conditions. First, we develop the channel codes by the RE and RC methods. Second, we propose a novel RDJD decoder to directly decode both user messages rather than to decode the users’ messages one by one, as in CFMA and NCMA.

A. Proposed Channel Code Designs

We suppose that users 1 and 2 are the strong and weak

users, respectively. From an information-theoretic perspective,

the better channel enables a higher reliable rate. Then, it is

desirable to allocate the strong user with a higher code rate

and the weak user with a lower rate. We design two codes for

the two users with the same codeword length but different code

rates. We consider that user 1 adopts a code C1 of rate R1 with a parity-check matrix H1, while user 2 adopts a code C2 of rate R2 with a parity-check matrix H2. Note that R1 ≥ R2. Moreover, we design the codes that satisfy C1 ⊇ C2. That is, the code C2 is a subcode of C1 given the same codeword

Remark: In the P2P communications, the better channel allows a higher transmission rate to achieve reliable communication. In this context, in a two-user GMAC, it is desirable to allocate the strong user with a higher code rate and the weak user with a lower rate. However, the rate allocation cannot work in XOR-CD, since the achievable rate of XOR-CD is limited by the capacity of worse channel. Thus, both NCMA and CFMA cannot fully exploit the different user channel conditions.
B. Proposed Decoder Design
In contrast to CFMA that only relies on Hc to decode the NC vector c = c1 ⊕ c2, our proposed decoder decodes the user messages using both Hc and the residual matrix Ha in H2.

Second, extrinsic information about c2 from JUD is derived as a priori information for RUD, which can estimate c2 within Ha since c2HaT = 0. D. Complexity Comparison With Benchmark Schemes
This subsection aims to compare computational complexity between the proposed RDJD and the benchmark schemes in binary channel-coded GMAC. In the two-user GMAC, CFMA adopts two decoders in its two-step decoding procedure by using modified SPA, while NCMA uses three possible PNC and MUD decoders to recover the user messages [28]. In the proposed RDJD, we use a JUD to decode the joint user message and a RUD to decode the individual user messages. In JUD, the soft information of four different combinations (c1, c2) are iteratively updated within the common parity-check matrix of two users. VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we proposed a rate-diverse GMAC system that allows users to adopt different channel coding rate under different channel conditions. In the GMAC system, we proposed two channel code design methods for the two users such that the users with a good channel and an inferior channel employ

the channel codes of a high code rate and a low code rate, respectively. Moreover, the two channel codes have some rows in their parity-check matrices identical. We then proposed an iterative RDJD as a serial concatenated decoder that consists of JUD and RUD as two constituent codes. The JUD decodes both user messages within the common parity-check matrix, and RUD provides independent information for the inferior channel user protected by the residual parity-check matrix.
Moreover, we used the EXIT chart to show the decoding gain from the iterative decoding between JUR and RUD, which is also verified by the performance simulations. The simulation results showed that the iterative RDJD achieved a large gain over the conventional NCMA and CFMA schemes for both high rate and low rate transmissions. In particular, this gain could be more than 1 dB at relatively high rate cases. Moreover, we found that there exists an optimal rate allocation to yield the best decoding performance for the two users given a constant sum rate. It suggested that the redundancy for JUD and RUD should be optimized to achieve decoding convergence according to different channels in GMAC.
However, from the EXIT chart analysis, we can still observe the large gap between the two EXIT functions of JUD and RUD at some rate pairs, which suggests much room left for optimization of these two codes [49], [50]. Thus, the code optimization for the common and residual parity-check matrices can help to approach the capacity region. We believe that this topic is interesting and deserves further exploration.
