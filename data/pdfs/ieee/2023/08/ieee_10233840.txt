II. SYSTEM MODEL AND ENCODING SCHEME

In this paper, we focus on the outer code in Fig. 1. Therefore, motivated by [4], we consider the simplified system model in Fig. 2, where the encoder and decoder relate to the outer code, the inner code is viewed as a part of Channel-1, and Channel-2 is to randomly shuffle DNA strings with each other.
More specifically, first, the data matrix U ∈ Fk2×w is encoded as X ∈ Fn2×l, where each row of X corresponds to a DNA string in DNA-based data storage systems. Then, the rows of X are transmitted over Channel-1 and received one-by-one in order. For each i ∈ [n], the i-th received row yi ∈ Y indeed corresponds to the inner decoding result of the i-th transmitted row xi ∈ X. Thus, we regard xi (instead of each its bit) as a whole to be transmitted over Channel-1. Moreover, yi can be classified into three cases:
• yi = xi: The inner decoding of xi succeeds. • yi =?: The inner decoding of xi fails to give a valid inner
codeword such that yi is regarded as an erasure error. This case can be caused by either that xi is corrupted by too many errors or that xi is missing during the synthesizing, storage, or sequencing process. • yi ∈ Fl2 \ xi: The inner decoding of xi gives a valid but incorrect inner codeword, i.e., an undetectable error happens. Since xi may be corrupted by insertion, deletion, and substitution errors, yi can heavily differ from xi in terms of Hamming distance.

In this section, we first derive the soft information M = (mi,j )1≤i≤n,1≤j≤w and hard information M = (mi,j)1≤i≤n,1≤j≤w of each bit in V. This allows us to decode each column of V independently, and V (as well as U and X) can be fully recovered if the decoding for each column succeeds. We call it independent decoding scheme. Next, we use an example to show that this scheme is not good enough since it requires the successful decoding of all columns. Finally, we propose an enhanced joint decoding scheme, which measures the reliability of rows of Z based on the independent decoding result and takes the most reliable rows for a further step of decoding.

We find that in Example 2, all the known bits in V˜ are correct. In a general situation, the known bits in V˜ should also be correct with a high probability. We thus assume
P(v˜i,j = vi,j) > 1/2, ∀v˜i,j ∈ V˜ and v˜i,j ∈ F2. (17)
Consequently, for a non-erased correct (resp. incorrect) row in Z with address i ∈ [n], it generally has a relatively small (resp. large) distance from v˜i. This provides a way to measure the reliability of rows of Z. We can then perform a further step of decoding over the most reliable rows of Z under erasure channels. It motivates us to propose an efficient joint decoding scheme, as shown by Algorithm 1. In the following, we give more explanations (including Example 3) for Algorithm 1.
• Lines 1 and 2 are to decode V as V˜ based on the independent decoding scheme.
• Line 3 is to count the number di of different positions (Hamming distance) between zi and v˜i , where i is the address of zi. According to (17), larger di implies a higher reliability of zi.
• Lines 4 and 5 are to take the most reliable rows Zn for a further step of decoding, where these rows are viewed as correct and the other rows of Z are viewed as erasure errors. Thus, the decoding actually works under erasure channels which essentially is to solve linear systems. The SGE is recommended since it works quite efficiently for solving large sparse linear systems [5]–[7]. The SGE leads to U = U if each row in Zn is correct and n is sufficiently large to uniquely determine U (see Example 3).
• Algorithm 1 definitely has a better chance to successfully

recover U than the independent decoding scheme, since it succeeds if the independent decoding scheme succeeds and it may still succeed otherwise.
Example 3. Continued with Example 2. Recall that Z = Y given by (14) before executing Line 4 of Algorithm 1. As a result, Line 2 of Algorithm 1 gives the V˜ in (16). Then, Line 3 gives (di)1≤i≤n = (2, 1, 2, 1, 1, 1). Next, Line 4 rearranges the rows of Z, say Z = [y2T, y4T, y5T, y6T, y1T, y3T]T, where yi, i ∈ [6] is the i-th row of the Y in (14).
In Line 5, n = 1 gives Zn = {y2}. Let V = [(viT)1≤i≤n]T be an estimation of V. We need to determine V for HV = 0, where H is given by (4) and for each i ∈ [6], vi is the data of yi if there exists a unique yi ∈ Zn with address i; otherwise, vi is an unknown (here vi, i ∈ {1, 3, 4, 5, 6} are unknowns). Since the 1st, 3rd, 4th, 5th, and 6th columns of H are linearly dependent, the unknowns of V (as well as U) cannot be uniquely determined. We need to increase n .
n = 2 gives Zn = {y2, y4}. We need to determine V for HV = 0 with vi, i ∈ {1, 3, 5, 6} being unknowns. Since the 1st, 3rd, 5th, and 6th columns of H are linearly independent, the unknowns of V (as well as U) can be uniquely determined and U = U. As a result, Line 5 gives n = 2 and recovers U successfully. In a general situation, the SGE can solve HV = 0 efficiently particularly when the parity-check matrix H is sparse (e.g., for LDPC codes).
To end this section, we discuss the (computational) complexities of both the independent and joint decoding schemes. For the independent decoding scheme, it decodes each column independently. Thus, its complexity is O(wc1), where w is the number of data columns and c1 denotes the complexity for decoding one column. The value of c1 depends on the underlying ECC and decoding algorithm. The joint decoding scheme needs to further perform Lines 3–5 of Algorithm 1. The complexity of Line 3 and 4 is O(wn) and O(n log2 n), respectively. However, the complexity of Line 5 also depends on the underlying ECC and decoding algorithm. For example, if the underlying ECC is LDPC code which has a sparse paritycheck matrix of a total number of θ non-zero entries. Then, c1 is O(θtmax) under message-passing algorithms having max iterations tmax, and Line 5 generally has complexity O(wθ) [5], leading the overall complexity O(wθtmax + wn + n log2 n). Note that O(wθtmax) dominates the overall complexity, indicating that the independent decoding for each column takes up most of time in the joint decoding scheme. Our simulation in coincided with this conclusion.

In this paper, we considered the simplified system model in Fig. 2 for DNA-based data storage. Starting from the rows of Z with the same address, we derived the soft and hard information of data bits in (8) and (12), respectively. These information was used to decode each column of U independently, leading to the independent decoding scheme. Based on the independent decoding result, the reliability of each row of Z can be measured. We then developed a joint decoding scheme which performs SGE over the most reliable rows to recover U. Simulations with the (1296, 1080) LDPC code showed that the joint decoding scheme can reduce the FER by more than 3 orders of magnitude compared to the independent decoding scheme.
