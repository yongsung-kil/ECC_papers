A Deep Learning based Multi-edge-type decoding algorithm for 5G NR LDPC codes
Tianyu Du, Hao Ju, Yin Xu, Dazhi He and Wenjun Zhang Cooperative Media Network Innovation Center (CMIC), School of Electronics Information and Electrical Engineering,
Shanghai Jiao Tong University, China Email: {dutianyu, juhao237, xuyin, hedazhi, zhangwenjun}@sjtu.edu.cn

Abstract—Low-density parity-check(LDPC) code has been selected as the channel coding method by 5G NR because of its excellent error-correcting performance. To further improve the performance of LDPC decoding, this paper proposes a neural normalized min-sum(NNMS) algorithm based on multiedge-type(MET). Based on the LLR convergence analysis of the protograph matrix of 5G NR, the base matrix is divided into several independent regions. Each part is assigned a unique scaling factor at different iterations. To verify the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm, We use two parity-check matrixes(PCM) derived from different base graphs in simulations. The results show that the proposed algorithm performs at most 0.45dB better than BP, 0.37dB better than NMS, and 0.25dB better than OMS, respectively, when the frame error rate (FER) is at 10−5 level over additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels using BPSK modulation.
Index Terms—Multi-edge-type, 5G NR, LDPC decoder, deep learning, model-driven neural network
I. INTRODUCTION
LDPC codes were first proposed by Gallager [1] in 1961 and rediscovered by MacKey and Spielman [2] in 1997. Due to its near Shannon limit performance and low implementation complexity, LDPC codes have been widely studied and applied in the field of wireless communication, such as ATSC 3.0 [3]– [5], DVB [6], [7] and 5G NR [8], [9].
LDPC codes mainly have two decoding algorithms: belief propagation (BP) algorithm and min-sum (MS) algorithm [10]. BP can achieve prominent bit error performance but causes enormous computational complexity, which is not conducive to hardware implementation. On the contrary, MS introduces excellent approximation to update the check node in the BP algorithm, which significantly reduces the decoding complexity, but results in the decline of bit error performance. Then, normalized min-sum(NMS) algorithm [11], [12] and offset min-sum(OMS) algorithm [13], [14] compensate for the excessive approximation problem caused by MS through scaling factor and offset factor, respectively, and achieve a compromise performance between BP and MS. Recently, deep learning has been widely applied to various traditional physical layer communication fields, such as channel coding and massive MIMO. In [20], the author constructs a small neural network to train the scaling factors required for the next iteration after each iteration. In [21], the author implements a model-driven LDPC decoder, which transforms the iterative decoding process of MS into a forward feedback propagation network and gives all edges a trainable scaling factor.
Although these methods have achieved specific results, they are not necessary for 5G NR codes. Because of the characteristic of the PCM structure, it is not necessary to assign neither the same weight to all the edges or different weights to all of them. In this paper, we used a method to analyze the convergence characteristics of various regions in the LDPC PCM and divide the edge of the PCM into different types. Edges of the same type use the same scaling factors, and the scaling factor varies between edges of different types. At the same time, considering that the number of iterations will also impact LLR distribution, various scaling factors are adopted for edges with the same type and different iterations. We then trained these scaling factors using a model-driven deep neural network.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section II summarizes the characteristics of LDPC codes in 5G NR. Section III-A briefly introduces the traditional LDPC decoding algorithm. Then Section III-B presents the LLR convergence characteristics of different regions of 5G NR codes. Based on this property, the min-sum algorithm based on MET is proposed. Next, Section III-C describes the details of using deep learning to search scaling factors. In addition, Section IV shows the simulation results of the MET-NNMS algorithm under multiple 5G NR codes. Finally, Section V concludes the paper.

II. CHARACTERISTICS OF LDPC CODES IN 5G NR

Fig. 2. Scatter plot of BG2.

Because of various communication scenarios of 5G NR, LDPC codes must be able to support different code lengths and rates flexibly. Therefore, the QC-LDPC code is adopted for 5G NR. The diversity of LDPC codes is realized by using multiple base matrices and lifting sizes.
Fig.1 shows the base matrix structure (also known as Base Graph, BG) of QC-LDPC code in 5G NR. It consists of two major parts: [A, B] is the core part of PCM called Hcore; [C, E] is an extension of PCM called Hext. Precisely, the submatrix A corresponds to the system bit. The submatrix B corresponds to the first set of parity bits and is a dualdiagonal lower triangular matrix. The submatrix C is a lowdensity generator matrix (LDGM). The submatrices E and O are the identity and all-zero matrices, respectively.
There are two types of basis matrices in 5G NR: BG1 and BG2. For BG1 and BG2, the number of rows M =46 or 42 and the number of columns N =68 or 52, respectively. The parity check node of the core part consists of a small number of Mcore = 4 for both BG1 and BG2 but varies in the variable node consists of an information variable node with Kb = 22 (BG1) or 10 (BG2). The base matrix can be extended to multiple lengths by setting different lifting sizes Zc, and the dimension of the corresponding LDPC PCM is M Zc × N Zc.
Fig.2 shows the scatter plot of BG2. The row degree of the Hcore part is much larger than that of the Hext part. Considering that B is a dual-diagonal lower triangular matrix, we can preliminarily identify A, B, C, and E as four distinct parts.

IV. SIMULATION RESULT
In this section, we compare the performance of the proposed decoding algorithm with traditional decoding algorithms over 5G NR LDPC codes. Without loss of generality, the LDPC codes in the experiment are generated from the base graph BG1 and BG2, respectively. All coded bits are modulated by BPSK and transmitted over the AWGN channel. The maximum number of iterations is 20. The scaling factor for NMS is 0.6, and the offset factor for OMS is 0.5 for better performance. The scaling factors required by the MET-NNMS algorithm are obtained by the neural network in Section III-C. The network is based on Tensorflow2.0, uses ADAM as the optimizer, and has a learning rate of 0.001.
Fig.5 and Fig.6 show the performance of the proposed decoding algorithm and the traditional decoding algorithm over the BG1-based and BG2-based LDPC code. MET-NNMS performs better than other conventional decoding algorithms. With the required SNR to achieve FER = 10−5 as the standard. For the BG1-based LDPC code, MET-NNMS performs 0.45dB better than BP, 0.37dB better than NMS, and 0.25dB better than OMS. For the BG2-based LDPC code, MET-NNMS performs 0.28dB better than BP, 0.11dB better than NMS, and 0.07dB better than OMS. Since OMS relies on perfect channel estimation for optimal performance, OMS is worse than MET-NNMS in time-variant channels. At the same time, MET-NNMS is not sensitive to imperfect channel estimation.
V. CONCLUSIONS
This paper proposed a MET-NNMS algorithm for 5G NR LDPC codes. For details, the convergence of LLR messages for MSA in different regions is firstly analysed, followed by a division of edges into different types. Other algorithms may assign all edges with the same factors or every edge with a unique factor. As a trade-off, an edge with different types or iterations will have a unique scaling factor in MET-NNMS, which can improve performance without introducing excessive complexity.
