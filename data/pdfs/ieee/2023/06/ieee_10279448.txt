Rate-Adaptive LDPC Codes Obtained from Simplex Codes

Massimo Battaglioni, Marco Baldi, Franco Chiaraluce and Giovanni Cancellieri Dipartimento di Ingegneria dell’Informazione
Universita` Politecnica delle Marche, Ancona (60131), Italy email:{m.battaglioni, m.baldi, f.chiaraluce, g.cancellieri}@univpm.it

Abstract—In this paper we show that, when a binary primitive polynomial can be associated to a sparse Golomb ruler, the simplex code obtained by taking it as the code parity-check polynomial exhibits good distance properties and performance. We define some conditions under which the obtained codes are also Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes, and can hence be decoded through efficient iterative algorithms. We perform code puncturing, leading to a family of rate-adaptive codes, and we predict some of their structural properties in terms of minimum distance and weight distribution. We show that, in addition to having some useful properties, these codes achieve good performance in terms of error rate under LDPC decoding.
Index Terms—Golomb rulers, LDPC codes, Minimum Distance, Simplex codes.

I. INTRODUCTION

Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes are usually de-

signed through random-like approaches, which make their

structural properties hard to predict. A solution to this problem

is that of finding families of classical codes, with somehow

predictable structural properties, which under some conditions

can also be seen as LDPC codes. In this paper we focus on

a special family of punctured simplex codes that can also

be seen as LDPC codes [1]. One of the conditions to be

satisfied in order to comply with the definition of LDPC code

is that the primitive polynomial taken for constructing the codes is a Golomb ruler. Starting from a [2k − 1, k, 2k−1]

cyclic simplex code, where k is the degree of the primitive

polynomial, puncturing is performed by eliminating one row

and one column in the parity check matrix H of the code.

So, k remains constant, whereas the length n of the codeword

is reduced by one unit. This procedure can be repeated, thus

obtaining

a

progressive

in

the

code

In [1], such a puncturing procedure is adopted, yielding

rate-adaptive codes with short blocklength. However, the the-

oretical analysis of the minimum distance in [1] is limited

to primitive polynomials of weight 3 and results are limited

to

In

codes obtained through such a puncturing procedure by gener-

alizing the analysis to primitive polynomials of any weight. So,

our analysis is extended to a much wider range of code rates,

and new bounds and estimates of the code minimum distance

are provided. Moreover, we perform a comparison with well

settled codes having short blocklength, showing that the new finely rate-adaptive codes are competitive.
Existing methods use modular Golomb rulers to design circulant matrices forming the parity-check matrix of binary quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes [4]–[6]. Non-binary LDPC codes based on modular Golomb rulers are designed in [7], [8]. Our approach is different from the above ones, since the codes we propose are: i) based on standard (non-modular) Golomb rulers, ii) binary and ii) non-QC. The removal of the modular constraint yields rate adaptivity of the novel family of codes.
In a nutshell, we design simplex codes, characterized by a primitive parity-check polynomial h(x), in such a way that the binary representation of h(x) is sparse and that the support of this vector is a Golomb ruler. This way, the paritycheck matrix of the code is also sparse and satisfies the row-column constraint (RCC) [9]. Such a parity-check matrix hence describes an LDPC code, which can be decoded by exploiting low-complexity iterative algorithms. The resulting structure of the parity-check matrix lends itself to puncturing, actually yielding a family of finely rate-adaptive LDPC codes whose low-weight codewords can be analyzed. Finally, even though these codes are not cyclic, they inherit the simple timeinvariant encoder circuit of their parent cyclic code. The only choice to make is on the period of the switch devoted to collect either information or control symbols [10].
As a spark for future works, we mention the possibility to consider another form of code puncturing, called constantlength puncturing [10], in which rows of H are eliminated in the central part of the matrix, so maintaining the block length n constant. This procedure enables the construction of a frame, and hence of a convolutional version of the code.
The paper is organized as follows. Section II is devoted to introducing the notation and presenting the theoretical basis of our method. Section III provides some theoretical results helping in the choice of the code parameters. Sections IV and V deal with the design of low and high rate codes, respectively. Finally, in Section VII we draw some conclusions.

Given a polynomial in F2[x], factor of the binomial xN + 1,

h(x) = h0 + h1x + . . . + hkxk,

the generator polynomial g(x) of a cyclic code is obtained by dividing xN + 1 by h(x). The non-null codewords of this
cyclic code can be written in form [13]

t(x) = xjg(x) mod xN + 1, j ∈ [0, N − 1].

If h(x) is primitive, the code obtained by taking it as paritycheck polynomial is a cyclic simplex code of blocklength N = 2k −1. The parity-check matrix H of this code is characterized by 2k − 1 − k rows, where h = T [h(x)] shifts from left to
right, by one position for any row. Notice that a primitive polynomial h(x) of degree k is asymmetric and has odd weight
[14]. Moreover, the following well-known property holds.

Property 1 [13] The polynomial xjg(x) mod xN + 1 can be associated to a pseudo-noise sequence of maximum period N for all j ∈ [0, N − 1], since h(x) is primitive.
From the well-known properties of pseudo-noise sequences [13], it easily follows that, due to Property 1, there exist values of j for which T [xjg(x) mod xN + 1] contains k − 1 consecutive zero symbols and k consecutive ones.

We assess the performance of some short codes, in terms of

Codeword Error Rate (CER), through Monte Carlo simulations

of Binary Phase-Shift Keying (BPSK) modulated transmis-

considered in [15], i.e., an AR3A LDPC code [16], an ARJA

designed through classical approaches, the proposed code has

two main advantages: firstly, the form of its H allows the

analysis of its structural properties (minimum distance), which

makes the performance predictable in the error floor region;

secondly, it derives from a cyclic code, which enables the use

of encoding algorithms with very low complexity.

VII. CONCLUSION
We have studied a family of punctured simplex codes that are also LDPC codes. Their main merits are that they can be encoded using a simple encoder circuit and they are naturally finely rate adaptive. Moreover, their minimum distance properties can be theoretically analyzed and estimated. Finally, they perform very well under iterative decoding, showing comparable CER performance with that of well-known protographbased LDPC codes with same length and rate.
