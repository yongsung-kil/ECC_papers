A Novel Labeling Scheme for Neural Belief Propagation in Polar Codes
Qi Chen, Hao Ju, Yin Xu, Dazhi He and Wenjun Zhang Cooperative Media Network Innovation Center (CMIC), School of Electronics Information and Electrical Engineering,
Shanghai Jiao Tong University, China Pengcheng Laboratory, China

Abstract—Recently, deep learning has been adopted to improve the performance of the belief propagation algorithm in polar codes, namely, the neural belief propagation decoder. By attaching trainable parameters to each edge on a tanner graph, this decoder effectively weakens the effect of short cycles. In this paper, we will show that the decoder can be further improved with our newly-designed labeling scheme. Instead of groundtruth transmitted codewords, our scheme utilizes codewords generated from a high-performance decoder (i.e., noise-aided belief propagation list decoder) to construct loss functions. Besides, to reduce the labeling complexity, the ground-truth transmitted codewords are integrated into the decoder as a list and compared with the other lists with the principle of minimum Euclidean distance. Numerical simulation results demonstrate that the proposed labeling scheme can improve the decoding performance by up to 0.2 dB with the same run time complexity and model size.
Index Terms—Polar codes, deep learning, belief propagation algorithm
I. INTRODUCTION
Polar codes [1] are a class of error-correcting codes that have been chosen to protect the control channels of 5G New Radio (NR) [2]. These codes have the distinction of achieving channel capacity at infinite code length. The Successive Cancellation (SC) decoder was initially introduced for decoding polar codes and is known for its low complexity of O(n log n). However, for code lengths that are short to moderate, the incomplete polarization of virtual channels significantly degrades the error correction performance of the SC decoder. To improve the error correction performance, the SC-List (SCL) decoder was proposed [3]. This decoder has a complexity of O(Ln log n), where L is the decoding list size. Adding an outer Cyclic Redundancy Check (CRC) code is beneficial for identifying the correct message within the decoding lists. Finally, this technique has been shown to be competitive with other modern error-correcting code families such as LowDensity Parity Check (LDPC) and Turbo codes.

Nonetheless, both SC and SCL decoders suffer from high latency as well as limited throughput because of their sequential decoding nature [4] (i.e., they have to decode codeword bits one by one). Compared with SC and SCL, the belief propagation (BP) algorithm demonstrates significant parallelism and is more favorable for low-latency and high-throughput applications. However, BP is appreciably inferior to SCL in terms of error rate performance and thus cannot fulfill the stringent requirement of 5G NR.
Nowadays, deep learning methods have shown incredible performances in various tasks, including computer vision, game playing, and bio-informatics [5]–[7]. One successful case of applying deep learning to improve BP is the neural belief propagation (NBP) algorithm [?], [8], [9]. The NBP decoder uses deep unfolding [10] to attach trainable parameters to each edge in its factor graph to alleviate the inefficiency caused by short cycles in the graph. These parameters are then optimized using the back-propagation algorithm, and significant gains over standard BP can be provided. Nevertheless, there still exists a gap between NBP and SCL, and multiple efforts have been taken to reduce that.
It is shown in [11] that a hypernetwork-aided decoder, akin to the NBP decoder but varying its parameters based on channel outputs, achieves performance very close to the maximum likelihood (ML) decoder. In [12], the authors proposed a CRC-polar BP (CPBP) decoder by exchanging extrinsic information between the factor graphs of polar codes and CRC codes, where the trainable parameters are on the edges of the concatenated factor graph. [13] demonstrates that restricting an NBP decoder with a shift-invariant structure on the weights can enhance the performance of the NBP for CRC. Besides, the combination of decimation and NBP is investigated in [14], where neural networks are utilized to decide the decimation value for the least reliable bit in NBP decoders. To simplify the computation, the research in [15] replaced the multiplicative parameters in NBP with the additive, with negligible performance loss. For the same reason, in [16], NBP uses the same weights across certain edges through parameter sharing, and simulation results reveal that simple scaling with a few parameters often yields the same gain as full parameterization in NBP.

III. PROPOSED LABELING SCHEME
In this section, we detail our proposed labeling scheme for NBP. Firstly, instead of ground-truth codewords, a Na-BPL decoder is utilized to generate labels to train NBP, which efficiently improves its performance. Secondly, to reduce the list number needed, we replace one list in the Na-BPL decoder with ground-truth codewords.
A. Na-BPL based Labeling Scheme
As shown in Eq. (5), The ground-truth information bits, which could be set to all zero according to the symmetry property in [8], are adopted as labels to train the NBP decoder in polar codes. However, from our observation that Pr(uˆGT/y) ≤ Pr(uˆML/y), an ML decoder can generate better labels. In fact, as soon as the bit error rate (BER) of the ML decoder is not zero, some channel outputs essentially can not be decoded correctly. In other words, these channel outputs are mislabeled when using the ground-truth labels, which probably causes over-fitting.
Unfortunately, producing labels with the ML decoder is unfeasible because of the contradiction between its complexity and the requirement of massive data in deep learning. Thus, a

high-performance decoder could be a good substitute. In this paper, considering both performance and complexity, the NaBPL decoder proposed in [17] is applied to generate labels. As illustrated in Fig. 2, apart from performing the original BP, the Na-BPL decoder adds different artificial noise to the received signal on other parallel independent BP decoders, and the final decoding result is chosen according to the principle of minimum Euclidian distance such as:

B. Ground-truth List aided Na-BPL
In our scheme, the closer the Na-BPL decoder is to an ML decoder, the better training labels we can get. It is easy to find that the ground-truth codeword usually has a same result as an ML decoder because the error rate of ML decoders is low. Therefore, as illustrated in Fig. 2 colored with red, we replace the first list in the Na-BPL decoder with the groundtruth codeword, which is then compared with the other lists by the principle of minimum Euclidean distance. When the list size is 1, our scheme is exactly the original scheme, only using ground-truth labels. Thus, this adjustment can ensure that our scheme will not be worse than the original one in [9]. We call this a ground-truth list aided Na-BPL decoder and we should recognize that this decoder can only be used in label generation but not a real communication system because we utilize the ground-truth value. Simulations in Section will show this method can effectively reduce the list size of NaBPL we need. Finally, the overall training scheme is presented in Algorithm 1.
Note that although our method is more complex, all additional computation is offline and the model size is the same. Once the training of this model is finished, it can be used online same as [9] but with better error rate performance.

B. Results
Fig. 3(a) presents the BER performance of NBP with our proposed labeling scheme where the list size is 8, NBP with ground-truth labeling scheme, standard BP and SCL with list size 4. As can be seen, our proposed labeling scheme has a small but consistent improvement in BER over the groundtruth labeling scheme when SNR is high. And compared with the standard BP, about 0.45 dB is improved with our proposed scheme when BER equals to 1e-5. Finally, the proposed scheme is still inferior to the SCL with 4 lists, which achieves nearly ML decoding.
Fig. 3(b) presents the BLER performance of NBP with our proposed labeling scheme where the list size is 8, NBP with ground-truth labeling scheme, standard BP and SCL with list size 4. Compared with BER, we can observe a similar consistent improvement. However, when BLER = 1e-4, it can be seen that about 0.2 dB improvement over the ground-truth labeling scheme is achieved, which is better than that in BER. We infer that this is because our labeling scheme uses the principle of minimum Euclidean distance, which focuses on BLER. Samely, the proposed scheme is still inferior to the SCL with 4 lists, which achieves nearly ML decoding.
Fig. 5 presents the loss values as Eq. 5 or Eq. 7 with the increasing training epochs. We find that the loss values of both the ground-truth labeling scheme and our labeling scheme decrease slowly with the increasing epochs, but the values of the proposed one are constantly lower than the values of the ground-truth one. This reflects our scheme can produce more accurate labels for NBP to learn.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we propose a novel labeling scheme to improve the performance of NBP further. This scheme replaces ground-truth codewords used before with codewords generated from a high-performance decoder (i.e., noise-aided belief propagation list decoder) to construct loss functions.
