ABSTRACT
Turbo codes have been successfully adopted in 4G LTE, which can approach the channel capacity with Bahl-Cocke-Jelinek-Raviv (BCJR) decoding. With the evolution from 4G LTE to 5G NR, there is a demand to design a unified channel decoder that supports both LTE Turbo codes and NR low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. One solution is to employ belief propagation (BP) decoding on the bipartite Tanner graph for both codes. However, although MacKay pointed out that Turbo codes have a sparse parity-check matrix, the existence of 4-cycles in such a matrix severely deteriorates the performance of BP decoding. In this paper, we propose two polynomial-based methods to optimize the parity-check matrix of Turbo codes by improving the sparsity while also removing 4-cycles and even 6-cycles compared to the original matrix. Simulation results show that the improved BP decoding for Turbo codes halves the error-correction performance gap between the original BP decoding and BCJR decoding, which is a promising step towards the unified channel decoder design based on the BP algorithm.
Index Terms— Turbo codes, belief propagation (BP), Tanner graph, parity-check matrix, minimum-weight parity-check.
1. INTRODUCTION
With the evolution from 4G LTE to 5G NR, the standard errorcorrecting codes for data channels have evolved from Turbo codes [1, 2] to low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [3, 4]. However, since both standards will co-exist for a long time, the baseband processors of base stations and terminals have to support both 4G LTE and 5G NR channel codes. As the channel decoder usually accounts for a major share of the processing complexity [5], it is attractive to consider a unified Turbo/LDPC decoder that merges two separate decoders into one reconfigurable decoder, thereby reducing the implementation complexity and improving the hardware efficiency.
In the 1990s, the invention of Turbo codes [1] and the rediscovery of LDPC codes [6] both revolutionized the coding theory field, because Turbo codes with Bahl-Cocke-Jelinek-Raviv (BCJR) decoding and LDPC codes with belief propagation (BP) decoding can both approach the channel capacity. Motivated by studying their similarities, Wiberg revealed that both codes can be regarded as codes on sparse graphs and their respective natural decoding algorithms are both instances of the sum-product algorithm [7]. More specifically, the component convolutional codes of Turbo codes can be represented by a trellis graph with state transitions. The paritycheck matrix of LDPC codes can be represented by a bipartite Tanner graph, in which coded bits and parity checksums are denoted as variable nodes (VN) and check nodes (CNs), respectively. Different factorization forms on the graphs lead to different calculations of their sum-product algorithms [8], which result in different decoding

architectures and implementations. Therefore, almost all current unified Turbo/LDPC decoders integrate both a BCJR decoder and a BP decoder in one architecture [9–11].
In contrast to a unified architecture, research on a wellperforming unified decoding algorithm for both Turbo and LDPC codes is still in its infancy. Initial works have attempted trellisbased decoding for LDPC codes [12] and BP decoding for Turbo codes [13–15]. However, the serial exploration of the trellis with multiple states suffers from high computational complexity and long latency, while BP decoding of standard Turbo codes has very poor error-rate performance. Even though the parity-check matrix of Turbo codes is sparse [16], it inherently contains numerous 4-cycles that severely deteriorate the performance. For the LTE Turbo code with information length 6144 and rate 1/3, BP decoding falls behind BCJR decoding by 6.5 dB at a frame error rate (FER) of 10−3.
In this paper, we strive for improving BP decoding of Turbo codes on the Tanner graph. Inspired by [17], we develop two polynomial-based methods to generate the minimum-weight paritycheck (MWPC) matrix and the generalized parity-check (G-PC) matrix. Their combined generalized MWPC (G-MWPC) matrix shows good regularity and significantly improves the sparsity of the original matrix. Numerical results show that for LTE Turbo codes, BP decoding with a G-MWPC matrix cuts the error-correction performance gap between the original BP decoding and BCJR decoding into half. Compared with BCJR decoding, the improved BP decoding for Turbo codes can be implemented with very high parallelism, which has great potential for a unified Turbo/LDPC decoder design.

2.4. Methods to Improve BP Decoding
It is well known that cycles in a graph introduce correlation between messages, which makes BP decoding unable to achieve the exact performance of maximum likelihood (ML) decoding. When the girth of a graph, i.e., the length of the shortest cycles, is very short (e.g., four), the performance of BP decoding is very poor. Unfortunately, most Turbo codes result in graphs with girth 4. For LTE Turbo codes, the polynomial items U (D) × (D3 + 1) + Z(D) × (D3 + 1) and U (D) × (D3 + D2) + Z(D) × (D + 1) form two kinds of 4-cycles, which are labeled in red and blue in (4). In addition to

the cycles from the RSC code, there are also cycles introduced by the interleaver, but these cycles are inherently present in the original factor graph of the Turbo code. An efficient and fast method to count cycles in a graph is introduced in [18]: Denote the number of edges in a graph, i.e., the number of 1s in matrix H, as NE. Create a Hashimato directed edge matrix E to represent the connections between every two edges in both directions [19], then the size of matrix E is 2NE × 2NE. If the girth of a graph is g, the number of cycles with length g′ (g′ = g, g + 2, · · · , 2g − 2) is tr(Eg′ )/2g′.
Summarized from the study of LDPC code constructions, BP decoding shows good performance when the girth is large and the number of short cycles is small. However, once the coding scheme of Turbo codes has been fixed, the optimization of the parity-check matrix is not a construction problem, but an extraction problem [20]. Namely, we need to extract a sparse matrix under fixed parity-check constraints. There are two efficient methods to sparsify the paritycheck matrix without changing the parity-check constraints.
1) Minimum-Weight Parity-Check (MWPC) Matrix: In 1986, Bossert proposed to use the minimum-weight codewords of the dual code as parity checks for linear block codes [21], and such MWPC matrices were first applied to BP decoding in 1998 [22]. In recent years, the research on decoding based on MWPC matrices has once again become a hot topic, including research on Reed-Muller codes [23] and cyclic codes [24]. The MWPC matrix is essentially generated from the linear row combinations, and all its rows are with the minimum weight. In order to avoid excessive decoding complexity, the decoder can select a part of the minimum-weight parity rows to generate the MWPC matrix, which has a size of M ′ × N and M ′ ≥ N − K. The MWPC matrix can then be represented by

Hmwpc = QH,

(8)

5. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we develop two polynomial-based methods to optimize the parity-check matrix of Turbo codes, and the resulting improved BP decoding with a G-MWPC matrix can remove all 4-cycles and 6-cycles for the LTE Turbo codes. The improved BP decoding not only enhances the FER performance, but also reduces the complexity due to the fewer edges in the Tanner graph. Simulation results show that improved BP decoding can reduce the performance gap between original BP decoding and BCJR decoding by half, which is a promising step towards a unified BP-based channel decoder design. Future works will focus on further reducing the gap and on hardware implementations of the proposed improved Turbo BP decoding.
