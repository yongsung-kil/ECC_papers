LDPC Codes with Low Error Floors and Efficient Encoders
Benjamin Gadat‡, Lyonel Barthe‡, Bala´zs Matuz†, Charly Poulliat∗ ‡ Airbus Defence & Space, Toulouse, France
†Institute of Communications and Navigation, German Aerospace Center (DLR), Oberpfaffenhofen, Germany ∗Univ. of Toulouse, IRIT/INP-ENSEEIHT, Toulouse, France

Abstract—This work presents low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes with low error floors, close to capacity performance and highly efficient encoders targeting high throughput applications such as free-space optical downlinks from low earth orbit (LEO) satellites to ground. We devise a code design strategy to find suitable protograph LDPC code ensembles and discuss an field-programmable gate array (FPGA) implementation of the obtained codes. In addition to having competitive decoding performance, the proposed codes have advantages in terms of encoding complexity. An FPGA prototype highlights significant improvements in resource utilization by a factor of around 10 compared to standardized DVB-S2 and CCSDS solutions.
I. INTRODUCTION
Free-space optical satellite communications gained increasing interest in the recent years as an attractive technology for high data rate communications ranging from Gbit/s to a few Tbit/s. Such data rates are difficult to achieve with radio frequency satellite systems due to the limited availability of spectrum. Particular applications are LEO downlinks, for instance for Earth observation, where large amount of data has to be transmitted in a short time window (typically 5 to 10 minutes) [1]. Due to various channel impairments, data integrity needs to be ensured notably by forward error correction (FEC) codes. Such systems have particular requirements on the FEC such as very low bit error rates (BERs) or highly efficient encoders. In particular, for LEO downlinks the encoder is located on board of the satellite and has to account for the limited power and hardware resources.
A popular class of capacity approaching channel codes with practical decoding algorithms are LDPC codes originally introduced in [2]. They have been recently adopted in the terrestrial 5th generation mobile network (5G) standard for enhanced mobile broadband (eMBB) links [3]. Thanks to their structured parity-check matrix, these protograph-based LDPC codes enable high throughput encoding/decoding (in the order of a few Gbit/s). However, they are tailored to operate at high/moderate BERs and their encoders are not fully designed for low-complexity: the degree-3 column in the parity-part of their parity-check matrix—inherited from the worldwide interoperability for microwave access (WiMAX) standard— increases encoding complexity.
The probably most common class of LDPC codes for satellite communications are those of digital video broadcasting – Satellite 2 (DVB-S2) [4]. The permuted structure of the parity-check matrix and the concatenation with an

outer Bose–Chaudhuri–Hocquenghem (BCH) code increases implementation cost and hampers throughput. An alternative are accumulate-repeat-jagged-accumulate (ARJA) based codes [4] which guarantee low target error rates, but their encoders are not tailored to Gbit/s communications due to the jagged accumulator structure.
In this work, we design LDPC codes with efficient encoders, targeting BERs of 10−12, and close to capacity performance for data links beyond 10 Gbit/s. These requirements are typical for free-space optical LEO downlinks, so the proposed codes have been selected for standardization by the consultative committee for space data systems (CCSDS) [1]. Nevertheless, the codes in this work may find applications in a much broader set of communication systems. The main contributions of this work are as follows. First, we extend an existing algorithm and design a family of protograph LDPC codes with lowcomplex encoders which show an excellent trade-off between error floor and waterfall performance. Second, we discuss an efficient hardware implementation of the encoder, as well as the decoder and verify the design down to a BER of 10−12 through FPGA prototyping. Finally, a comparison with standardized DVB-S2 LDPC codes and ARJA LDPC codes shows notable gains in resource utilization for FPGA, mainly in terms of look-up tables (LUTs) and flip-flops (FFs).

The LDPC codes considered in this work are based on a protograph structure which has many advantages from an

implementation point of view. A protograph is a bipartite graph with a small number of nb variable nodes (VNs) and mb check nodes (CNs) which are connected to each other by edges. A protograph can be represented by an mb × nb adjacency matrix B = [bi,j] where bi,j ∈ Z+0. The protograph serves as a blueprint for a larger bipartite graph of an LDPC code with n = nbQ variable nodes (VNs) and m = mbQ check nodes (CNs), where Q is called lifting factor. The code rate is Rc ≥ 1 − m/n where equality holds when all m constraints are linearly independent. In that case the information length k = m − n. An LDPC code is obtained from a protograph by lifting (or expansion) [5]. In a nutshell, the protograph is copied Q times and the different copies are interconnected with each other. In our case, the interconnections are described by circular permutation matrices yielding quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes. When choosing the interconnections we rely on enhanced versions of the progressive edge-growth (PEG) [6] and approximate cycle extrinsic message degree (ACE) algorithm [7] to mitigate the effect of harmful cycles in the code’s bipartite graph.

III. LDPC CODE DESIGN
The code design is subject to the following constraints which are typical, but not exclusive for optical downlinks:
a) high throughput encoding and decoding (≥ 10 Gbit/s) b) flexibility in the choice of the code rate (0.4 ≤ Rc < 1) c) constant encoded block size n d) decoding threshold close to Shannon limit (≤ 0.5 dB) e) error floor below a BER of 10−12.
We split the code design into two steps. First, we design a high-rate protograph. Second, if lower rates are targeted (i.e., Rc < 3/4), we design an extended protograph by adding additional columns and rows. LDPC codes in 5G make use of such low-rate extensions which were already proposed in [14] following the work by Richardson and Urbanke [15].

C. Proposed code family
Following the preceding procedure, a code family has been designed. The protographs have code rates ranging from Rc = 2/5 to Rc = 9/10 with thresholds reported in Table II. The corresponding protographs are listed in Appendix (punctured VNs are colored). Observe that the proposed family operates within 0.3 − 0.4 dB from capacity.
Codes with n = 30720 bits and different rates have been designed by a two-step QC lifting method with Q = Q1Q2. For implementation reasons, the lifting factor of the second step Q2 was chosen to be a factor of two. We choose Q2 = 128 while Q1 depends on the base matrix size, i.e., Q1 = n/(nbQ2). Frame error rate (FER) floating point simulation results over the AWGN channel (35 decoding iterations, approximate min* algorithm [17]) are shown in Figure 1. They confirm the competitive waterfall performance in comparison with DVB-S2 (n = 64800 bits) and ARJA (k = 16384 bits) codes. They perform within 0.1 dB from existing solutions.

design. The partial-parallel architecture implements data path parallelization within one circulant matrix. The maximum exploitable parallelization factor is driven by the lifting factor Q2. Layered scheduling partitions the parity-check matrix into layers (set of rows). In one decoding step, only edges associated with a specific layer are processed. Convergence of a min-sum based decoder is increased by approximately a factor of 2 compared to a flooding schedule decoder [19]. A lifting factor Q2 = 128 is chosen for all code rates with 128 functional units working in parallel, in order to minimize routing problems [20] and to efficiently size the permutation network based on pipelined barrel shifter primitives to achieve higher clock speed. This design choice also eases data exchange with wide logical bus interfaces based on AXI-4 standards [21]. Since external logical interfaces use word data width with 4, 8, 16, 32, or even 64 bytes, we choose Q2 to be a power of two in order to avoid data alignment issues.
An offset min-sum (OMS) decoder is used [22] which provides good hardware design trade-offs for the target data rates of at least 10 GLLR/s (Giga log-likelihood ratios (LLRs) per second). For the proposed LDPC family, it performs better than plain min-sum or normalized min-sum with a very small silicon overhead. Compared to approximate min*, a performance degradation is observable, particularly for low code rates (of around 0.35 dB for Rc = 2/5). The average throughput rate per decoder core is between 450 MLLR/s and 700 MLLR/s assuming on the one hand a stopping criterion for all code rates and on the other hand a clock frequency of 300 MHz with double buffering methods for input and output interfaces.1 The number of pipeline stages depends on the code rate which is related to the degree of the CNs: in the proposed implementation, it can reach 48 stages for

Off-line rescheduling [18] based on a two dimensional simulated annealing algorithm (one dimension for ordering CNs within a layer, one dimension for ordering layers) is able to resolve all data hazards for all code rates without the use of pipeline stalls. This is also a result of the appropriate sizing of Q2: for the proposed codes it offers a large number of solutions for the re-scheduling with a significant pipeline depth.
Finally, a multi-core parallelization strategy is used to scale the throughput to at least 10 GLLR/s by multiplying the number of decoder cores. The stopping criterion introduces an out-of-order execution: on an AWGN channel, the loss of throughput associated with the use of a basic blocking round-robin method is estimated to be less than 5% for all code rates. Advanced re-ordering buffer scheduling methods typically found in super-scalar general purpose processors [25] can be adapted to reduce this loss of throughput, but are not considered in this case study. To tackle the routing congestion, the decoder cores are grouped into clusters to provide an additional degree of freedom during the floor-planning phase.
B. QC LDPC encoder architecture
All LDPC codes in this work can be efficiently encoded using architectures optimized for matrix-multiplication and back-substitution operations for sparse matrices. The proposed encoder is implemented using a high level of parallelization by cascading three QC LDPC encoders based on partial-parallel architectures [26] as illustrated in Figure 2. It takes benefit of parallel encoding of ARA codes by construction (IRA and precoder) and pipeline parallelism for Raptor-like codes (low-rate extension) through the use of additional memory buffers and multiplexers. A first in - first out (FIFO) is also implemented at the output stage to enable efficient bus transactions sized by the codeword length (burst accesses). Using this architecture, one single encoder core is able to achieve an encoded throughput rate of at least 10 Gbit/s for all code rates with a minimum clock frequency of 380 MHz.

V. CONCLUSION
We introduced a methodology to design LDPC codes with low error floors, iterative decoding thresholds close to capacity, and highly efficient encoders. An FPGA implementation was put in place to validate the performance of the codes. We experience strongly reduced resource utilization for encoding by a factor of around 10 compared to state-of-the-art LDPC codes, while showing competitive decoding performance. As a consequence, the proposed codes have been selected by CCSDS for standardization of optical LEO downlinks.
