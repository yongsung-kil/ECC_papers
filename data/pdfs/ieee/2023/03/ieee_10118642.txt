Learning to Decode Protograph LDPC Codes over Fadings with Imperfect CSIs
Chen Yang, Yanan Zhou, Zhongwei Si, and Jincheng Dai Key Laboratory of Universal Wireless Communications, Ministry of Education
Beijing University of Posts and Telecommunications, Beijing, China

Abstract—Recently a number of low-density parity-check (LDPC) decoding algorithms based on deep learning have been proposed in the literature. However, most of the work has been targeted for additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels. For more practical scenarios, in this paper we investigate the neural-network based min-sum (MS) decoding for protograph LDPC codes in fading channels. Since the wireless channel is complex and varying, accurate channel state information (CSI) cannot be always available at the receiver. We classify the scenarios into three cases with perfect CSIs, imperfect CSIs, and no CSIs. By assigning learnable weights on the edges in the iterative decoding, the proposed neural decoder compensates for the performance loss caused by the error/lack of CSIs. The trajectory-based extrinsic information transfer (T-EXIT) chart is employed as a theoretical tool to select the proper training dataset for the neural network and the proper channel initialization scheme for the receiver. Numerical results in terms of block error rates are provided, which agree with the T-EXIT analysis. It can be seen that the proposed neural MS decoder clearly outperforms the traditional MS algorithm in Rayleigh fading channels. Meanwhile, the proposed decoder shows a good compatibility so that it can be applied to the cases with different accuracy of CSIs.
I. INTRODUCTION
The low-density parity-check (LDPC) code has been adopted as the coding scheme for data channels in 5G New Radio (NR) due to its low latency and high throughput [1], [2]. Invented by Gallager [3] and recovered by MacKay and Neal [4], LDPC code can achieve a performance close to the Shannon limit when using the belief-propagation (BP) decoding algorithm [5]. The min-sum (MS) decoding algorithm [6] was proposed as an approximation to the standard BP with a lower complexity, which has been widely applied in practice.
The decoding performance is affected by the accurancy of the channel state information (CSI). Channel estimation is usually employed to obtain the CSI through the help of training sequences or pilot symbols, which requires additional resources and therefore causes overhead to the system. However, the wireless channel is complex and varying. The accurate CSI cannot be guaranteed all the time. When the CSIs are misestimated, channel mismatch occurs and the decoding performance degrades. For some other scenarios, such as sporadic access of small packets, the CSIs are not

available at all. For these cases with imperfect CSIs or no CSIs, it is important to generate log-likelihood ratios (LLRs) of channel observations properly. For LDPC codes under the Rayleigh fading channel with no CSIs, a linear approximation of the LLRs using the expectation of the channel fading was proposed in [7], which has a low complexity but a low accuracy. A more accurate approximation was proposed in [8] at the cost of increased complexity.
Deep learning (DL) [9] techniques are being used to optimize key areas in 5G [10], e.g., massive MIMO, channel estimation, channel decoding, etc. In particular, for iterative decoding algorithm, due to the similarity between its expansion structure and the neural network, [11] and [12] assigned the learned weights to the edges of the Tanner graph in the BP decoding to weaken the negative influence of the short girths. In [13], a decoder using a neural network to optimize the MS algorithm was proposed, which improved the decoding performance with a low complexity. The aforementioned work mainly focused on the AWGN channel, where the channel coefficients are perfectly known. The fading channel is a vital model in wireless communications where signals suffer from different fading when going through the wireless channel. Therefore, the DL-based decoding in fading channels is worth an investigation. In particular, when the CSIs are imperfect or even unknown, the application of neural-network-based decoding is more challenging. To the best of the authors’ knowledge, this has not been studied in the literature yet.
In this paper, we investigate the neural-network decoding for the protograph LDPC code in fading channels as an extension of [14]. A protograph LDPC [15] code is a structured LDPC code which uses the copy-and-permute operation to any smaller graph to obtain derived graphs of different sizes. In [14], we have implemented a neural-network MS decoder for the AWGN channel, and the method can be extended to the fading channel with perfect CSIs. In this paper, the study is extended to the cases with imperfect CSIs and no CSIs. Learned weights are obtained by utilizing proper training datasets and then assigned on the edges in the iterative decoding. The proposed neural decoder compensates for the decoding performance loss caused by the error/lack of CSIs. For selecting proper training datasets, a trajectory-based extrinsic information transfer (T-EXIT) chart is applied for the evaluation and illustration. III. NEURAL DECODING FOR FADING CHANNELS
Most of previous work on iterative decoding based on deep learning is limited to the AWGN channel. In fact, the neuralnetwork based decoding can be generalized to various channel models, such as Rayleigh fading channels. In this section, we first implement the neural MS decoder for fading channels, and then we classify the cases of perfect CSIs, imperfect CSIs, and no CSIs. The LLR distribution is provided to interpret the advantages of our neural network decoder.

A. Neural MS Decoding

For conventional iterative decoding algorithms, messages are passed between CNs and VNs. Due to the existence of short girths in the Tanner graph, the correlation of the messages increases continuously, affecting the decoding performance. The neural decoding weakens this effect by assigning learnable weights on the message transmitted from CNs to VNs. Especially for the MS algorithm, the loss caused by the min-sum approximation is also compensated. The rule of message updating in the MS decoding algorithm is changed into:

Based on this algorithm, the decoder can be trained as a

non-fully connected neural network according to the Tanner

graph. The input layer and output layer of the network are of size , which is the number of VN nodes. Each iteration of the decoder corresponds to a hidden layer of size , which is the number of edges in the Tanner graph. Each hidden layer contains two sublayers, namely the VN update layer and the CN update layer. The structure of the neural decoder is illustrated in Fig.2.
In particular, due to the encapsulation property of protograph LDPC, the neurons in a hidden layer can be divided according to the “lifting” operation. The edges which derived from the same edge in the base graph are assigned the same weight, which is called parameter-sharing mechanism. With this mechanism, the memory for saving weights can decrease by a factor of times as against conventional demand, and the training complexity is also reduced. Besides, we use the iteration-by-iteration training method, that is, the weight is fixed after each training, and only the last layer of weights needs to be learned at the next training. This training process can make the decoder converge faster. Moreover, when the required iteration number is less than or equal to the maximum number of training iterations, the weights can be directly intercepted from the trained weights free of repeated training.
During the decoding process, we hope that each channel message matches its corresponding codeword, which can be considered as a binary classification in the neural network. Thus we choose the binary cross-entropy of the transmitted codeword and the output of the neural network as the loss function.
In this paper, we use Adaptive Moment Estimation (ADAM) optimizer to modify the weights to get a smaller loss. For a given set of weights, the messages are transmitted based on the neural network decoding algorithm to obtain the corresponding predicted values. And in the process of reverse propagation, the weights will be constantly corrected by minimizing the loss until convergence.
B. Case Classification
We classify the fading channels into the following three cases according to the availability of the CSIs. Compared to the AWGN channel, the initial channel LLRs need to be redefined for the Rayleigh fading channel. Case 1: CSIs are perfectly known.
For this case, we assume that the CSIs are known ideally at the receiver. Since the study in [14] can be extended to this case, we will not discuss it in detail in this paper. Case 2: CSIs are partly known.
For this case, we assume that the channel fading coefficients are estimated with errors. At the receiver, the initial channel LLRs are generated by using the estimated ℎ. The difference between the true ℎ and the estimated ℎ is measured by the mean square error (MSE).
In our model, the channel with imperfect CSIs is represented by three subcases in which the fading coefficients are incorrectly estimated with MSEs at 100, 10−1, and 10−2,

The maximum iteration number of the neural decoder is 20. For each iteration, the weights are trained with 20000 batches. In each batch, 40 examples are generated under 4 different SNRs. And the Adam initial learning rate is set to be 0.001.

C. LLR Distribution Analysis
In the neural MS (NMS) decoder, the weights are directly multiplied on the LLRs passed from the CNs to the VNs. It is interesting to compare the distributions of LLRs with and without the weights.
Without loss of generality, we assume the all-zero codewords. For a fair comparison, both cases have identical decoding initialization and only differ in whether the LLRs of CNs are weighted or not. In each iteration, we collect the LLRs from the outputs of the decoders. We set = 14 and plot the curves for iteration number 1, 5, and 10 in Fig.3.
Fig.3 compares the LLR distribution of MS decoder and weighted LLR distribution of neural MS decoder with imperfect CSIs, where ℎ is incorrectly estimated with MSE at 10−1. The LLR distribution of traditional MS with perfect CSIs is plotted as the benchmark. In the beginning of the decoding, all the LLR distributions are not Gaussian due to the fast fading. However, with increasing iterations, it can be observed that the introduction of weights makes the LLR distribution more concentrated and closer to a Gaussian distribution. This indicates that the introduction of weights corrects the distribution of LLRs in the iterative decoding.

In this section, we first use T-EXIT charts analysis to select the training dataset and the initialization of channel LLRs. On this basis, the block error rate serves as the performance metric to evaluate the proposed neural decoder under the fading channels with imperfect CSIs and no CSIs, respectively.

A. T-EXIT Analysis

iterative decoding performance of LDPC codes. It provides

intuition for the dynamics and convergence of the iterative

decoding by tracking the exchange of extrinsic information

between the VNs and CNs during iterations. Due to the speci-

ficity of protograph LDPC code structure, the traditional EXIT

chart analysis is not applicable. Protograph EXIT (PEXIT)

[18], based on the tracking of the mutual information on

the edges of the protograph, was proposed. However, PEXIT

can only be applied to BP decoding, and it derives the

threshold value under ideal conditions of infinite codelength

and iterations. Obviously, PEXIT is not appropriate for our

neural MS decoder under fading channels.

the decoding performance and convergence of neural MS

C. Rayleigh channel with no CSIs
The BLER results with = 14 when the receiver has no CSIs are given in Fig.7. We still use the BLER curves corresponding to the neural MS with perfect CSIs, and the traditional MS algorithm with perfect CSIs as benchmarks for ease of comparison. The simulation results show that the neural MS decoder trained with Data (II) and initialized with expected ℎ can outperform its traditional MS counterpart by 1.1 dB. This performance comparison is consistent with the results in the T-EXIT chart, and it demonstrates the feasibility and reliability of our TEXIT-guided selection.
Based on the above observations, we conclude that the proposed neural MS decoder can effectively compensate for the performance loss caused by the lack of channel state information. Using the expected value of fading coefficients to generate the received LLRs and to train the weights is applicable for a variety of cases.
V. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper we have proposed a neural-network based MS decoder for Rayleigh fading channels. When the CSIs are imperfect or even non-available, the trained weights compensated for the performance loss due to the error/lack of channel conditions. The T-EXIT chart was employed as a theoretical analysis tool to select proper training dataset for the neural network and proper channel initialization scheme for the receiver. Block error rates have been provided, which verified the analysis of T-EXIT. The proposed neural MS decoder clearly outperforms its traditional counterpart in Rayleigh fading channels.
