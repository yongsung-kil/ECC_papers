MGC: Multiple-Gray-Code for 3D NAND Flash based High-Density SSDs
Yina Lv2, Liang Shi1,2, Qiao Li3, Congming Gao3, Yunpeng Song2, Longfei Luo2, Youtao Zhang4
1Software/Hardware Co-design Engineering Research Center, Ministry of Education 2School of Computer Science and Technology, East China Normal University, Shanghai, China
3School of Informatics, Xiamen University, Xiamen, China 4Computer Science Department, University of Pittsburgh, Pittsburgh, USA {elainelv95, shi.liang.hk, qiaoli045, gaocm92}@gmail.com, {51205901045, 52215901003}@stu.ecnu.edu.cn, zhangyt@cs.pitt.edu

Abstract—QLC (4-bit-per-cell) and more-bit-per-cell 3D NAND flash memories are increasingly adopted in large storage systems. While achieving significant cost reduction, these memories face degraded performance and reliability issues. The industry has adopted two-step programming (TSP), rather than one-step programming, to perform fine-granularity program control and choose gray-code encoding, as well as LDPC (Low-Density ParityCheck Code) for error correction. Different flash manufacturers often integrate different gray-codes in their products, which exhibit different performance and reliability characteristics. Unfortunately, a fixed gray-code encoding design lacks the ability to meet the dynamic read and program performance requirements at both application and device levels.
In this paper, we propose MGC, a multiple-gray-code encoding strategy, that adaptively chooses the best gray-code to meet the optimization goals at runtime. In particular, MGC first extracts the performance and reliability requirements based on application-level access patterns and detects the reliability degree of SSD. It then determines the appropriate gray-code to encode the data, either from host/user application or due to garbage collection, before writing the pages to the flash memory. MGC is integrated in FTL (flash translation layer) and enhances the flash controller to enable runtime gray-code arbitration. We evaluate the proposed MGC scheme. The results show that MGC achieves better performance and lifetime guarantee compared with stateof-the-arts and introduces little overhead.
Index Terms—Storage System, 3D NAND Flash, Gray-Code, Reliability, Performance
I. INTRODUCTION
3D NAND flash memories are increasingly adopted to construct large capacity SSD (solid-state drive) storage due to several advantages, including high density, low cost, and lightweight. With fast technology advances, the number of 3D stacked layers increased significantly in the last decade, from the early 24 layers to the state-of-the-art 220+ layers [32] [60] [26] [30]. In addition, the number of bits per cell also increased from 1-bit-per-cell to the recent 4-bit-per-cell [26] [57]. More layers in a block and more bits per cell are on the roadmap of several flash vendors. For example, 5-bit-per-cell (PLC) and 6-bit-per-cell (HLC) have been announced [22] [63] [1]. While achieving significant chip density increase and cost reduction, 4- and more- bit-per-cell 3D NAND flash memories face non-negligible reliability and performance degradation, which has increasingly become a major design obstacle for further advances [27] [24] [39] [40] [37].

To save data in n-bit-per-cell flash memory cells, we need to program each cell to one of 2n voltage levels such that each level represents a n-bit value. A flash read can differentiate if a cell is above/below a certain voltage level and thus reading data from such cells often involves multiple flash reads. For widely adopted Gray-code (GC) encoding for flash data, we can represent a particular GC choice using the minimum numbers of flash reads for different pages. For QLC, a graycode can be represented as GC(A,B,C,D) where A, B, C, and D are the numbers of flash reads required to fetch the least-significant-bit (LSB), central-significant-bit (CSB), mostsignificant-bit (MSB), and top-significant-bit (TSB) pages, respectively. We have A+B+C+D=24-1=15. For example, GC(1,2,4,8), GC(1,2,6,6), GC(1,4,5,5), and GC(3,4,4,4) are often adopted gray-codes for QLC. Since data read from the flash memory may contain errors, according to LDPC ECC, we may need additional reads to correctly decode the page.
The choice of gray-code has a direct impact on read performance. For example, for GC(1,2,6,6), we may read the LSB page with one flash read but need 6 flash reads to fetch the TSB page. As such, we referred to such a gray-code as UGC (unbalanced gray-code) in this paper. GC(3,4,4,4) is referred to as BGC (balanced gray-code) as we have roughly the same read performance across different pages.
The choice of gray-code also leads to different programming performance. Traditionally, 3D flash adopts one-step programming (OSP) [28] for low density cells at large technology nodes, e.g., for 3D TLC (3-bit-per-cell), it prepares three pages in buffer and writes all three pages to the device cells of one wordline in one programming step. Unfortunately, adopting OSP for QLC cannot guarantee programming reliability. As such, the industry has revived the two-step programming (TSP) to program n pages for n-bit-per-cell flash cells in two steps: a coarse first step to program m pages and then a second step to program the rest of the pages. Such a TSP is referred to as TSP(2m,2n) as we need at least 2m levels to program m pages. TSP was originally proposed and widely adopted for 2D MLC and can effectively enhance programming reliability. The TSPs proposed by flash manufacturers in the past five years are listed in Table I. Not all gray-codes are compatible with all TSPs. Actually, there is a strong correlation — given TSP(2m,2n) and GC(A,B,C,D), if A+B<2m, or A+B+C<2m, we can adopt this TSP to encode the data and program flash cells. For example, we can adopt TSP(4,16) for GC(1,2,6,6) as 1+2<4 so that we can first program two pages of data and then program the other two pages. TSP(4,16) does not support GC(3,4,4,4) as 4<3+4 so that we do not have enough levels in the first step to differentiate the data of the first two pages. As discussed, TSP(4,16) is faster than TSP(8,16) and TSP(16,16) but it does not support all types of gray-codes.
While different gray-codes exhibit different read and program characteristics, SSD products usually adopt one of them, determined by their corresponding flash memory manufacturers. A fixed gray-code lacks the ability to adapt to the changing performance demands at both application and device levels.
In this paper, we propose MGC, a novel multiple-graycode flash for high-density SSDs. MGC can optimize the performance by exploiting the advantages of different graycodes and TSPs. For write performance optimization, we can apply TSP with shorter programming latency. For read performance optimization, we can apply gray-codes with better read performance and higher reliability. For discussion purposes, we use QLC flash as the default storage. The design principle is applicable to other types of high-density NAND flash. In summary, this paper makes the following key contributions:
• We present a detailed performance analysis using different gray-codes. There are two key observations: First, UGC is compatible with fast programming and thus can achieve good write performance. However, it suffers from inferior read performance. BGC has superior read performance but is compatible only with slow programming. Second, UGC tends to have worse reliability with flash wearing.
• We propose a novel MGC-based flash encoding design that enables multiple programming algorithms in the flash controller. An MGC-based flash translation layer (MGC-FTL)

is then constructed to utilize different gray-codes and TSPs for performance optimization, including two schemes: 1) a data access characteristics guided gray-code arbitration to improve performance, and 2) a reliability-stage aware graycode arbitration scheme to tame the reliability degradation. • We evaluate the proposed MGC using SSDsim, extended with the constructed reliability modeling and validated using real testing data on 3D QLC flash chips. Experimental results show that MGC achieves both superior read and write performance throughout the life of flash with little cost.
II. BACKGROUND
A. High-density 3D NAND Flash Memory
3D NAND flash uses floating gate (FG) or charge trap (CT) transistors as flash cells, which can store one or multiple bits of data using the voltage levels of charges inside the cells. Fig. 1 presents an overview of a typical QLC flash chip, which is hierarchically organized as the primary storage medium of high-density flash. Inside a chip (in Fig. 1(a)), there are thousands of blocks, and a single block is composed of many layers. The number of stacked layers in a block has been increased from 24 to the state-of-the-art 220+ [18] [55]. Inside a block (in Fig. 1(b)), each row of flash cells is connected by a wordline (WL), usually spanning 32K-128K cells. All cells in the same column are connected to form a bit-line (BL). There are two selected gates, the selected gate at the drain end (SGD) and the selected gate at the source end (SGS). One WL consists of several pages. The number of pages in a WL is decided by the number of bits per cell. To this end, there are single-level-cell (SLC), multi-level-cell (MLC), triple-level-cell (TLC), and quad-level-cell (QLC) flash. Take QLC flash as an example. Each flash cell contains four bits, and each WL includes four pages. The page size equals the number of cells in a WL, ranging from 4KB to 16KB. The four different bits are called the least-significant-bit (LSB), central-significant-bit (CSB), most-significant-bit (MSB), and top-significant-bit (TSB), from low to high in this paper.

The programming process is realized by the incremental step pulse programming (ISPP) algorithm [62]. For read operation, there are two sets of voltages that should be produced. One is added to the selected WL (Vread), and the other is added to the unselected WLs (Vpass). Then, the data can be read from the BL.
The flash memory faces significant reliability degradation with increasing storage layers as well as bits per cell. There are two reasons. First, by saving multiple bits in one cell, the noise margin between adjacent voltages is reduced. Fig. 1(d) shows the voltage distribution of the 16-level state. Each state has a narrow voltage range such that adjacent states have narrow noise margins and accessing the flash is prone to errors [46] [45] [20]. Second, with increasing 3D stacked layers, the program disturbance and layer-to-layer interference become serious [6] [3] [4], which distort the voltage distribution and introduce errors. The voltage of programmed flash cells may shift, as shown in Fig. 1(d): (1) read/program disturbance shifts the voltage distribution to the right; while (2) flash cells face charge leaking over time, which shifts the voltage distribution to the left. Flash memory uses reference voltages to read the data. If the voltage of a state shifts near/across a reference voltage, reading the cell may contain errors. Even with the extensive integration of LDPC ECC in modern SSDs, flash reliability remains a major design challenge for high-density 3D flash memory.
B. Two-step Programming Algorithm
Due to high program efficiency, 3D flash memory traditionally adopts one-step programming (OSP) [28] for low-density cells at large technology nodes. With fast technology advances, OSP cannot ensure programming reliability for QLC and more bits-per-cell flash. In particular, the voltages of memory cells of WLn are likely disturbed when programming the memory cells of WLn+1. To tame this challenge, the industry has revived the two-step programming (TSP) algorithm, originally proposed and widely adopted for 2D MLC flash, to program 3D flash memory cells [32] [60] [26]. TSP programs a flash wordline in two steps. Fig. 2(a) shows the basic TSP(16,16). First, a wordline, e.g., WLn, is coarsely programmed such that each cell resides in an intermediate state. Two neighboring states may have voltage overlaps, indicating reading such a wordline tends to incur errors. After the first programming step of the next wordline WLn+1, the controller further programs

WLn with each cell being programmed into its final state. The wordline programmed using TSP has a larger noise margin than that using OSP, thereby ensuring better data reliability. However, a major issue of TSP(16,16) is its programming latency, which is on average 1.53× that of OSP [60].
To reduce the programming latency caused by TSP, several optimizations have been proposed to improve the programming speed of the first step. For example, in 2019, Shibata et al. from Toshiba proposed TSP(8,16) (Fig. 2(b)) that programs three pages into an 8-level state at the first step [60]. TSP(8,16) reduces the programming latency by 18% over TSP(16,16). In 2021, Khakifirooz et al. from Intel proposed TSP(4,16) (Fig. 2(c)) [26] that programs two pages at the first step. Compared with TSP(8,16), TSP(4,16) can reduce the programming latency of the first step by 60%, thereby reducing the overall write latency by about 22% [26] [25]. Fig. 2(d) illustrates TSP(2,16) that programs one page at the first step, which may be the fastest TSP. However, TSP(2,16) is unrealistic as programming cells from a 2-level state to a 16-level state shall introduce large inter-wordline interference and thus reliability cannot be ensured. Studies show that interwordline interference remains severe even adopting TSP and the flash manufacturers are developing different program interference reduction techniques when more stacked layers are realized [11]. Studies have shown that partially-programmed flash cells are more vulnerable to cell-to-cell interference and read disturbance than fully-programmed cells [4]. Therefore, TSP(2,16) is not considered in the rest of this paper. Given TSP(16,16) has completed the programming of four pages with a 16-level state in the first step, it only needs to fine-tune the voltage range for each state in the second step. To summarize, among the three TSP schemes TSP(4,16), TSP(8,16), and TSP(16,16), we find that TSP(4,16) has the best programming performance while TSP(16,16) has the best data reliability.

D. Gray-Code and Performance
1) Gray-codes of 3D NAND flash: High-density flash often adopts gray-code to encode multiple bits in one cell, which assigns multiple-bit values to different voltage levels [17]. Table II presents four commonly used gray-codes for QLC flash — GC(1,2,4,8), GC(1,2,6,6), GC(1,4,5,5), and GC(3,4,4,4) [26] [59]. These codes map 4-bit values to 16 voltage levels differently. The four bits in one cell are referred to as LSB, CSB, MSB and TSB, respectively, as we listed in Section 1.
There is a constraint on gray-code selection for a particular TSP. In particular, given TSP(2m,2n) and GC(A,B,C,D), if A+B<4 (m=2) or A+B+C<8 (m=3), we can adopt the corresponding TSP to encode the data and program flash cells. Clearly, all gray-codes are compatible with TSP(16,16). GC(1,2,4,8) is the only one for TSP(8,16) while both GC(1,2,6,6) and GC(1,2,4,8) are compatible with TSP(4,16).

2) Performance and reliability of gray-codes: Adopting different gray-codes exhibits different read and program performance characteristics. For example, to read the LSB page from cells encoded with GC(1,2,6,6), we just apply one reference voltage to differentiate the voltages of cells below or above REFL7/L8 (a reference voltage between L7 and L8). Similarly, we apply two reference voltages REFL3/L4 and REFL11/L12 to read the CSB page. Reading the TSB page is much slower as it requires 6 reference voltage levels. As such, GC(1,2,4,8), GC(1,2,6,6), and GC(1,4,5,5) are all referred to as UGC (unbalanced gray-code). In contrast, GC(3,4,4,4) is referred to as BGC (balanced gray-code) as reading different pages has roughly the same performance. Due to read retries at degraded reliability, overall read performance depends on the number of read retries as well, as shown in Equation 2.

TSP(16,16) supports BGC but has the largest programming latency. To summarize, neither UGC nor BGC can achieve the best read and write performance, making it a dilemma to choose either one of them.
To study the impact of adopting different gray-codes in real applications, we conduct experiments on different applications at different stages. In this paper, we divide the reliability of flash into three stages (e.g., Young/Middle/Old) based on the number of retry operations with LDPC, as described below.
• Young Stage: this refers to the duration that the SSD just starts its service, which reflects the page reliability characteristics of different gray-codes when no retries occur.
• Middle Stage: this refers to the duration when the reliability of pages in UGC blocks is reduced due to the low reliability of MSB/TSB-based pages, while the reliability of pages in BGC blocks is still high, which requires few or no retries to perform a read operation. The real page reliability of different gray-codes is tested in experiments, which shows that MSB/TSB pages of UGC start retrying earlier than other pages (e.g., the TSB page of BGC). We then choose two sets of reliability features (termed Middle-1 and Middle-2 in Table IV) to reflect the reliability differences between graycodes with decreasing reliability, and to facilitate further design of reliability-stage-aware gray-code arbitration.
• Old Stage: this is the duration when the number of retries exceeds the maximum value (e.g., 8 in this paper), the page reliability is further degraded, which cannot reliably store data and rewrite operations may be activated. We define the Old stage to discuss situations where a large number of retries occur on the MSB/TSB pages of UGC blocks.
Here, we present the rationale for defining three reliability stages, referring to existing work [56] and data collected from our real platform. First, retry operation occurs not only under the worst conditions but also under common cases in

modern high-density flash [56]. Second, we used an industrialgrade testing platform equipped with QLC chips to collect the retry number at 1K P/E cycles. The results verify that many retries occurred when reading pages, and MSB/TSB pages of UGC start retrying earlier than other pages of different gray codes. Third, it is common to protect data with more than 3months retention time in high-density flash [30], especially for read-intensive scenarios, indicating that data reliability can be improved by selecting the high reliable gray-code. Based on the above understandings, we comprehensively consider the reliability characteristics of pages with different gray-codes to differentiate the specific stage and give the design principles. Note that SSD duration is mainly at the Middle/Old stages. The duration of the Middle stage is the longest.

IV. THE OVERVIEW OF MGC FLASH
In this section, we propose MGC to enable multiple graycodes in the flash controller to exploit the advantages from both UGC and BGC to achieve the overall best I/O performance. Compared to the commercial SSD products that adopt a fixed gray-code based programming algorithm, MGC improves the read performance by exploiting the better reliability of BGC and reduces the programming latency by exploiting the high-speed programming of UGC. In addition, MGC helps to achieve better flash reliability and thus reduces the rewrites from internal read scrubbing or refresh mechanism [12] [41].
The proposed MGC consists of firmware and software modifications. At the firmware side, MGC enhances the programming process to support TSP(4,16) and TSP(16,16), and the read process to support data encoding/decoding using both UGC and BGC. The data decoding/encoding is to map 4-bit values from/to 16 voltage levels using two different mapping tables, which incurs negligible overhead. Supporting both TSP(4,16) and TSP(16,16) demands two programming sequences, which is hard to enable within one flash block. As such, to reduce implementation complexity, we enable TSP at block granularity, i.e., while both UGC and BGC are enabled, one flash block can only adopt one of them.

2) Hot Read Data Transformation: When integrating different gray-codes in the SSD, there exist three types of flash pages based on their asymmetric read latency:
• Type-1 page: this refers to the TSB or MSB pages in UGC blocks. They tend to have high read latency.
• Type-2 page: this refers to all the pages in BGC blocks. Their read latency is in the middle range.
• Type-3 page: this refers to the LSB or CSB pages in UGC blocks. Their read latency is relatively low.
Given read operations do not update the data, if there is a mismatch between the hotness of the read-intensive data and the location of the data, it might be beneficial to transform the encoding for such data. For example, if data are written in UGC, the read performance for LSB/CSB pages is superior. However, at runtime, we may accumulate more reads to the MSB/TSB pages such that it is beneficial to transform the encoding to BGC. In this work, read hotness is used as the indicator to guide read data transformation while other indicators also can be applied, such as the performance requirement hint from the host system [41]. If their read hotness continue to increase, these MSB/TSB pages (now in the BGC block) may be moved to LSB/CSB pages in the UGC block.
To implement it, we evaluate the tradeoff between transformation overhead and benefit to decide the appropriate read hotness threshold. The evaluation is based on a simple heuristic that a page, once read s times, is likely to be read again at least s times. If the transformation overhead, i.e., writing QLC with P/E cycle reduction and write latency, is less than the benefits from reading faster pages s times, we may trigger such a transformation.
To differentiate read hotness, we set two thresholds, i.e., TUR and TBR, as the read (or read-retry in LDPC) numbers on MSB/TSB pages in UGC blocks (Type-1 pages) and BGC blocks (Type-2 pages), respectively. We collect page-level hotness statistics in the SSD controller. Since the read latency gap between Type-1 pages and Type-2 pages is smaller than that between Type-2 pages and Type-3 pages, TUR is set more aggressively than TBR, which tends to have good read performance improvement. In this paper, TUR is set to 1 while TBR is studied together with the transformation overhead.
3) Garbage Collection and Wear Leveling: One critical component inside SSD is garbage collection, which reclaims invalid pages caused by data updates [69]. For the above graycode arbitration scheme, UGC blocks are prone to garbage collection since there are more invalid pages. During garbage collection, valid data should be collected and programmed to UGC blocks. This is because garbage collection is timeconsuming, and adopting fast programming with UGC can speed up the process. If a BGC block is selected for garbage collection, the valid data should still be stored in the BGC block due to that data in BGC blocks are read-dominate. Actually, BGC blocks are less likely to be garbage collected as

they frequently store read-intensive data. Therefore, traditional garbage collection can be adopted with little modifications.
Another critical component is wear leveling that even the wearing among blocks for lifetime optimization [68] [21] [42]. For the above gray-code scheme, UGC blocks are erased more frequently than that of BGC blocks. In this case, we propose to encode blocks with UGC or BGC in an interleaving way. For example, if a UGC block is erased, it will be used as a BGC block next time during dynamic wear leveling. However, since BGC blocks are rarely erased, we need to select the BGC blocks with the lowest erase counts during static wear leveling.
C. Reliability-Stage Aware Gray-Code Arbitration
As presented in Motivation, UGC is sensitive to reliability degradation while BGC is not. In this part, by exploiting the characteristics at three reliability stages as discussed in Section III, MGC further optimizes the gray-code arbitration as follows. We use an example in Fig. 9 to facilitate the discussion. For each stage, we discuss the gray-code arbitration on three operations: (1) reads; (2) write for the first time; (3) updates.
1) Young Stage Gray-Code Arbitration: For the Young stage, pages programmed with TSP(4,16) and encoded with UGC have superior write performance and comparable read performance to that of BGC. Then, we propose to use UGC as the default gray-code. In this case, the read and write performance are both excellent. More importantly, there is no gray-code transformation cost. As shown in Fig. 9(a), all data are encoded with UGC and programmed with TSP(4,16). That is, there are only UGC blocks in flash memory.
2) Middle Stage Gray-Code Arbitration: For the Middle stage, data programmed with TSP(4,16) and encoded with UGC may introduce retries on the TSB and MSB based pages. Two changes are made compared with the Young stage. First, BGC is chosen as the initial gray-code to encode data for the first writes. Second, the data on MSB/TSB pages in UGC blocks are transformed to BGC blocks when the read counts exceed TUR. As shown in Fig. 9(b), data A-H are written to BGC blocks for the first time (Step ), and then updated to UGC blocks (Steps  and ). Then, data A and C in the BGC block are frequently accessed and the read count exceeds the threshold, but the transformation is not triggered since the reliability of BGC blocks is still high (Step ). However, if the read count of the data D and E stored in the UGC blocks exceeds the threshold, they will be transferred to BGC blocks for read performance optimization (Step ).
3) Old Stage Gray-Code Arbitration: For the Old stage, data encoded with UGC using TSP(4,16) are no longer reliable. In this case, UGC blocks cannot work and their lifetime is stopped at the Middle stage. BGC will still be selected as the initial gray-code for reliability concerns. Since the LSB/CSB pages of UGC blocks are still reliable, we transfer the hot read data to these two pages when they are identified in the BGC blocks. However, due to the low reliability of UGC, only the first step of TSP(4,16) is processed to program the two pages in one WL. Retry Modeling: According to prior work [27] [56] and open reliability model [71], we can develop the retry model like Equation 3 to determine the average number of retry operations (nRET RY ) for each page in a specific stage.
First, to show the RBER (raw bit error rate) of the data page, we defined a variable, α, which reflects the RBER for two adjacent voltage states (RBERSENSE). Then, the RBER of a data page with the number of reference voltages (nSEN SE) can be expressed as α ∗ RBERSENSE ∗ nSEN SE, which should be collected from the real platform.

Second, to show the capability of the equipped LDPC, we use ELDP C to represent the maximum number of error bits in a data page that the LDPC can correct. If the RBER of the currently accessed page is less than ELDP C , the accessed data can be correctly read. Otherwise, retry operations occur until the data is read correctly or the number of retries is exhausted.
Finally, to model the retry process, we use a variable δ to represent the RBER reduction for each read retry. δ is highly related to the retry algorithm (such as a predefined retry voltage table), which varies by flash manufacturers or products with different requirements. Previously, Kim et al. [27] model threshold voltage tuning and soft-decision decoding, effectively reducing the RBER for each read by a retry scale factor. The retry scale factor can determine δ, e.g., setting the retry scale factor to 2 means that each retry can reduce the RBER by 50% for MLC/TLC flash [27]. To evaluate QLC flash, we set a more conservative retry scale factor to 5, which can reduce 20% of RBER for each retry. Then, δ is set to 20%, that is, after each retry, the RBER of the read page can be reduced to 80% of its original RBER. Based on the above definitions, the retry modeling can theoretically be constructed as Equation 3. After calculation, the minimum retry number can be expressed by Equation 4. Then, we can count the retry values (in Table IV(b)) for pages with different nSEN SE under the three reliability stages. The number of retries increases from the Young to the Old stages, and the retries are larger with a higher nSEN SE. First, the pages of all gray-codes are highly reliable and no retries occur when reading (referring to the Young stage). Second, as the reliability degradation, the MSB/TSB pages with UGC start a retry operation when reading. The number of retry values of these pages (nSEN SE=6) is set to 1 (referring to the Middle-1 stage). Therefore, pages with lower nSEN SE have a retry value of 0. Third, as reliability drops further, the LSB pages in BGC blocks start retrying, set to 1 (referring to the Middle-2 stage). Forth, the LSB pages of UGC blocks start retrying, set to 1 (referring to the Old stage). There is a large number of retries on MSB/TSB pages in UGC blocks and rewrite operations are triggered, similar to the Old stage, as shown in Figure 10. Then, at the Old stage, the retry values of MSB/TSB pages in UGC blocks are set to a value greater than the upper limit of retry operations (e.g., 8 in this paper), such as 9. For other pages with different values of nSEN SE, the average number of retries is set based on the above-collected RBER trend and retry modeling (e.g., we select 4 and 6 for the LSB page of BGC and CSB/MSB/TSB pages of BGC at the Old stage, respectively). Overall, the retry values in Table IV(b) are empirically set with reliability trends tested in real chips and stages defined in this paper to understand

the page reliability characteristics of different gray-codes. In practice, the number of retries is complicated and varies for chips from different flash manufacturers or to account for process variations. Due to the limited scope of discussion in this paper, this part will serve as our future work.
Furthermore, considering real SSD scenarios, the number of retries tends to have a Gaussian-like distribution [61] [5]. That is, for the average retry values given in Table IV(b), we use a Gaussian distribution, N (μ,σ2), to determine the actual retries of the accessed page to accurately simulate real scenarios. μ is set based on the average number of retries given in Table IV(b), and σ is set to 1 for simplicity.

B. Experimental Results
To fully demonstrate the benefit of MGC-FTL, we evaluated five schemes, including UGC, UGC RLV, BGC, MGC and MGC Stage. UGC [26] and BGC [32] represent the state-of-the-art single-gray-code based flash. UGC RLV is a read latency variation aware scheme with UGC-based QLC flash [47], which exploits the LSB and CSB pages of UGC blocks for read performance optimization. MGC represents the proposed scheme with the access characteristics guided graycode arbitration. MGC Stage is the enhanced MGC with the reliability-stage aware gray-code arbitration.
In the following experiments, we evaluated the performance, tail latency, flash lifetime and sensitivity studies. We have done the above experiments at all stages, and the results meet our expectations. However, the duration of the SSD is mainly at the Middle/Old stages (as described in Section III) and space limitation, only the results at the Middle/Old stages are discussed. Additionally, in the Old stage, UGC blocks cannot work due to the low reliability of MSB/TSB pages, thus UGC and MGC are not evaluated. Then, the results are normalized to UGC at the Middle stage and BGC at the Old stage. Here, we use the parameters of Middle-2 to evaluate the Middle stage since both Middle-1 and Middle-2 can reflect the worse reliability of UGC as the definition of the Middle stage.

Normalized IO Latency

Read performance: Fig. 11 shows the normalized read latency. In the Middle stage, the read performance of BGC is much better than that of UGC for most workloads. This is because MSB/TSB pages in UGC blocks suffer from reliability/performance degradation. By exploiting the read latency variation among pages in UGC blocks, UGC RLV reduces the latency by 15% compared with UGC. MGC that arbitrates BGC for the coming data can achieve similar read performance to that of BGC. Furthermore, the read latency of MGC Stage is slightly higher than that of MGC by 4.8% due to that several frequently read data in BGC blocks are not transferred to LSB or CSB pages of UGC blocks for overhead consideration. In the Old stage, compared with BGC, the read performance of MGC Stage can be optimized by 7.4% on average. This is because unreliable UGC blocks cannot store data, which are discarded at the Old stage. To sum up, compared with UGC RLV, MGC Stage improves the read performance by 26% on average and up to 51% at the Middle stage. At the Old stage, the read performance of MGC Stage can be improved by 7.4% on average compared with BGC.
Write performance: Write performance is highly related to the adopted TSPs, as shown in Fig. 12. In the Middle stage, BGC/TSP(16,16) introduces 12%-31% higher write latency compared with UGC/TSP(4,16). Such a write performance gap is smaller than the theoretical value due to that the read latency of UGC blocks increases significantly, which will affect the write performance of SSDs due to the read priority policy. Differently, the write performance of MGC and MGC Stage is similar to that of UGC, as we only use BGC for the first write and arbitrate UGC for updates. In the Old stage, BGC and MGC Stage have similar write latency, as we arbitrate

BGC to encode data for the first write and further updates. I/O performance: Fig. 13 presents the normalized I/O
latency, which highly relates to the read/write ratio of the workload (as shown in Table IV(c)). I/O performance depends primarily on read performance optimization for read-intensive workloads (e.g., hm 1) and write performance optimization for write-intensive workloads (e.g., prxy 0). MGC and MGC Stage can achieve good I/O performance improvement no matter whether the workload is read-intensive or writeintensive.
Tail latency: To further understand the latency stability, we collected the tail latency at the 99th percentile of the Middle stage, as shown in Fig. 14. First, BGC can reduce the tail latency by 42% on average compared with that of UGC. Second, UGC RLV only focuses on average performance optimization, where the tail latency is reduced by 12%, on average, over UGC. Some workloads experienced no improvement in tail latency. Third, the tail latency of both MGC and MGC Stage is similar to that of BGC due to that frequently read data are stored in BGC blocks with high reliability, which can achieve stable performance. In conclusion, MGC Stage can reduce the read latency by 39% at two nines compared with UGC.
Lifetime: We then evaluate the factors that impact flash lifetime by collecting the rewrite counts and transformation cost (e.g., read and rewrite operations during gray-codes conversion). First, rewrites are triggered to protect data reliability. If the number of retries during data accessing exceeds a limit, the data should be read and rewritten to other places. If flash reliability is low, too many rewrites may reduce lifetime and even make SSD fail. Second, during gray-code conversion, some pages will be read out and programmed to other WLs.
Fig. 15 shows the write amplification factor (WAF), where a smaller value of WAF means less impact on flash lifetime. From the results, BGC introduces much less WAF compared with UGC. More importantly, compared with existing works and MGC, MGC Stage only produces a little lifetime reduction for three reasons. First, MGC Stage can improve data reliability, which will reduce the rewrites. Second, we adopt a conservative hot read data transformation method to minimize transformation costs. Third, we tend to use BGC at the Old stage to tame the low reliability of UGC, which can make the SSD still work at the Old stage. Also, we collected the capacity reduction at the Old stage, which is represented by the number of UGC blocks used, as shown in Fig. 16. The results show that the reduced capacity is around 0.6%-7.8% and 1.7% on average due to that we only used a small portion of blocks as UGC-based blocks to optimize the read performance. In conclusion, MGC Stage achieves a similar lifetime as BGC.

Fig. 17 shows the normalized read latency and transformation cost for workload wdev 0 at the Middle stage by varying TBR from 2 to 8. A smaller TBR triggers more aggressive migrations. Then, more data are speeded by transferring them to the pages with lower latency. That is, a better read performance can be achieved with a higher migration cost. VIII. RELATED WORK
RBER reduction: The basic idea is to reduce the raw bit error rate (RBER) to enhance flash reliability and lifetime. For example, many studies proposed to reduce RBER, such as the adoption of fine-grained and multi-step programming algorithms [35] [32] [20] [60] [26]. Some prior works proposed applying refresh schemes to control the retention errors, which can obtain higher P/E cycles [10] [44] [15] [51]. Some other works presented to adjust the read reference voltages to reduce the RBER and achieve high read performance [52] [58] [7] [8]. All techniques mentioned above attempt to tame the challenge of RBER reduction. Different from prior work, we proposed to improve the performance of high-density flash memory by exploring the advantages of different gray-codes.
LDPC optimization: Wu et al. [64] proposed to exploit the write interference and retention time to optimize the decoding process of LDPC. Du et al. [16] proposed to carefully select the sensing voltage for LDPC. Li et al. [36] proposed to smartly place the sensing voltage of LDPC. The above works reduce the decoding latency of LDPC by improving the quality of the input information. However, none of them consider the reliability characteristics of gray-codes.
Latency variation optimization: Prior works proposed to explore the latency variation among pages to improve the read

performance. For example, Cui et al. [13] [14] proposed to schedule I/O requests based on the latency variation to improve read performance. Li et al. [34] proposed to exploit the process variation to improve read performance. Lv et al. [47] proposed a latency variation aware data placement scheme to explore the latency difference among pages in a QLC flash to maximize read performance. Our work has further explored the latency variation among different gray-codes and applied the MGC scheme to different reliability stages for lifetime consideration.
Coding optimization: Prior works proposed to optimize the coding of high-density flash memory, such as WOM codes [23] [67] [48]. For example, Jaffer et al. proposed to use WOM codes to gain more program count before the block is erased, which can enhance the flash lifetime [23]. For graycodes, there are also some works that analyze the reliability among different pages. For example, Pletka et al. [59] and lv et al. [47] proposed read performance optimization schemes based on asymmetric gray-codes. Liu et al. [43] studied the QLC program and read mechanisms, and then discussed five program algorithms and four gray-codes to match different program algorithms. Different from the above works, our proposal is the first work on designing multiple-gray-codes for 3D NAND flash-based high-density SSDs to meet the dynamic read and program performance requirements at both application and device levels.
IX. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we proposed and evaluated MGC for highdensity 3D NAND flash memory. MGC enhances the flash controller to enable the selection of appropriate gray-code at runtime, which helps to match the performance characteristics at application and flash levels. Our experimental evaluations show that MGC achieves better performance and lifetime guarantee compared with the state-of-the-arts.
