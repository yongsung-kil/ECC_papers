Optimization of Protograph LDPC Codes via Surrogate Channel for Unequal Power Allocation
Qiwang Chen , Member, IEEE, Yu-Cheng He , Member, IEEE, Chen Chen, and Lin Zhou , Member, IEEE

Abstract— This paper investigates the possibilities of optimizing existing protograph low-density parity-check (PLDPC) codes and designing optimal PLDPC codes on both AWGN and Rayleigh channels under binary modulation with unequal power allocation (UPA) for nonuniform sources. While the binary UPA modulation achieves a power gain, it also results in unequal error protection on the coded bits in systematic codewords during transmission. For irregular codes, it is possible to best protect the information bits by optimizing the classification of variable nodes (VNs). Those VNs represent the coded bits in protograph that allows exploiting jointly the degree irregularity and the power differentiation. An algorithm for UPA-based protograph extrinsic information transfer (UPA-PEXIT) analysis on the Rayleigh channel is proposed with the concept of surrogate channel which circumvents the channel asymmetry problem with UPA. With the RJA code as example, it is shown that classification optimization does improve the decoding threshold when the source entropy is small. To design optimal codes, a UPA-based differential evolution (UPA-DE) algorithm is proposed in conjunction with the UPAPEXIT analysis. A family of optimized codes are illustrated for three typical nonuniform source distributions and compared with the RJA and NND codes by simulations in error rate performance.
Index Terms— Nonuniform source, PEXIT, protograph LDPC codes, surrogate channel, unequal power allocation.
I. INTRODUCTION
N EAR-CAPACITY information transmission techniques have received deep investigations based on coded modulation [1]. For example, bit-interleaved coded modulation (BICM) [2] can approach the Shannon capacity when integrated with low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [3], [4], [5] or polar codes [6]. Most results on coded modulation are achieved under the assumptions that the source information bits follow the binary uniform distribution and the constellation points are symmetric and equiprobable.

When the information sources are nonuniform, a dedicated source encoder is often utilized ahead of channel coding in order to eliminate source redundancy and hence improve transmission efficiency. However, the optimality of such communication systems relies theoretically on infinite code length and unrestricted transmission delay, since source decoding often suffers error propagation and is sensitive to transmission errors. The separate source-channel coding schemes may not be suitable for low-power and lowcomplexity scenarios, e.g., Internet of Things and wireless sensor networks.
To improve both efficiency and error rate performances of communication systems for nonuniform sources, joint sourcechannel coding (JSCC) schemes have been investigated to combine together the tasks of source coding and channel coding, where source redundancy and channel knowledge can be exploited simultaneously by joint source-channel decoding [7], e.g., double LDPC codes system [8], [9] or double polar codes system [10]. Recently, a JSCC scheme with only one encoder/decoder pair has been proposed, where the task of source coding is converted into a specific unequal power allocation (UPA) for binary modulation, and the encoder/decoder pair is dedicated to channel coding with only one code, e.g., Turbo code [11] or LDPC code [12], [13]. Compared with BPSK modulation based on equal power allocation (EPA), the binary UPA modulation can improve substantially the power efficiency. Due to its promising advantage in power efficiency, the binary UPA modulation scheme has been extended to M -ary orthogonal constellations [14] and Gaussian multiple access channels [15].
Although non-systematic LDPC codes can employ source redundancy, their encoding and decoding methods are complex [16], e.g., including a scrambler pre-coder and more decoding nodes. Moreover, it has been shown that the performance of non-systematic LDPC codes is worse than the systematic LDPC code for non-uniform source when UPA is employed [12]. As a class of near-capacity channel codes, protograph LDPC (PLDPC) codes can be encoded by a systematic form and designed with simple structures by using the differential evolution (DE) algorithm [17], [18], [19]. The decoding performances of PLDPC codes can be analyzed in terms of decoding threshold by using the protograph extrinsic information transfer (PEXIT) algorithm [20], [21].

A. Motivation
A fundamental principle of UPA is that more (less) power shall be allocated to less (more) probable coded bits, thus saving power consumption on average. To facilitate the binary UPA modulation for a nonuniform source, systematic encoding is necessary for identifying the locations of the information bits in a codeword. Meanwhile, the parity bits are mapped onto signals with EPA, as they approximately follow the uniform binary distribution regardless of source distributions. In the stage of transmission, the binary UPA modulation causes unequal error protection by classifying the information bits and the parity bits, and thus differentiating their signal powers.
Therefore, in designing and optimizing PLDPC codes under binary UPA modulation for nonuniform sources, there are two key issues to consider as follows:
1) The first key issue is how to carry out the PEXIT analysis for evaluating the decoding threshold of the codes. It should be noted that the PEXIT analysis [21] simulates Gaussian density evolution based on a symmetric AWGN channel model with uniform binary input and EPA modulation (e.g., BPSK). However, the requirement of channel symmetry is unsatisfied in the binary UPA modulation scheme due to nonuniform source distribution and asymmetric channel symbols [22].
2) The second key issue is how to specify a correspondence relationship (CR) between the coded bits in one codeword and the variable nodes (VNs) in the protograph when considering irregular PLDPC codes for the capacity-approaching purpose.
It is well recognized that the CR is related to unequal error protection by exploiting the irregularity of degree distributions in the stage of belief propagation (BP) decoding. The coded bits corresponding to large-degree VNs can be more reliably protected than those corresponding to small-degree VNs.
Under binary UPA modulation, the CR should be optimized by incorporating the irregularity of degree distributions and the classification of the coded bits, in order to best protect the information bits. However, there is no way to analytically optimize the CR with the objective of achieving the best bit error rate (BER) performance regarding the information bits.
B. Contribution
To overcome the channel asymmetry problem in the PEXIT analysis, we introduce the concept of surrogate channel. In the binary UPA modulation scheme for nonuniform sources, only the information bits suffer from the channel asymmetry problem. Therefore, the symmetric AWGN channel model in the PEXIT analysis can be exploited as the surrogate channel for the target UPA channel regarding the information bits.
It has been shown that LDPC codes could similarly perform on distinct channels if the channels have the same mutual information (MI) between input and output [23], [24]. Specifically, a PLDPC code optimized on the surrogate

channel by the PEXIT analysis could also be optimal on the target UPA channel, if the code has the same MI excess to the Shannon limit on either channel [25].
Equivalently, in order to make it comparable to perform the PEXIT analysis on the surrogate channel, we have to count in the difference in the Shannon limit between the target UPA channel and the supposed EPA channel for coping with the information bits from a nonuniform source.
The main contributions of the paper are summarized below: 1) By resorting to numerical simulations, we obtain the difference in the Shannon limit between the target UPA channel and the supposed EPA channel for various nonuniform source distributions. It is shown that UPA can always approach the benchmark Shannon limit of the AWGN channel, whereas EPA always yields a Shannon limit higher than the benchmark. Meanwhile, the gap in the Shannon limit between UPA and EPA is increasing as the source entropy decreases. At high signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) values, both UPA and EPA can approach the maximum MI equal to the source entropy. 2) Based on the above results, we propose a UPA-based PEXIT analysis algorithm, noted as UPA-PEXIT, on the Rayleigh channel under the concept of surrogate channel, where a specific classification of VNs is considered to locate the information bits and the parity bits in protograph. 3) By simulations, we show that the RJA codes [26] with different code rates can be improved in terms of decoding threshold by optimizing the classification of VNs in protograph. 4) To optimize codes for nonuniform sources, we propose a UPA-based DE algorithm, noted as UPA-DE, which operates in corporation with the UPA-PEXIT analysis. For demonstration, we obtain optimal codes for three typical nonuniform source distributions and compare them with the benchmark RJA code in error rate performance on both AWGN and Rayleigh channels. The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. In Section II, we outline PLDPC codes and present the system model of the binary UPA modulation scheme. In Section III, we analyze the MI and the difference in the Shannon limit of the UPA and EPA channels. Then, we present the UPA-PEXIT algorithm using surrogate channel. In Section IV, we show the improvement of the RJA code by classification optimization in protograph. Then, we present the UPA-DE algorithm for designing optimal codes. In Section V, we compare the BER performances of the searched codes and the RJA code by simulations. Finally, the paper is concluded by Section VI.

The protograph can also be expressed as a base matrix B = [bij]m×n, where the rows and the columns are identified by Ci and Vj , respectively; and bij denotes the number of parallel edges between Ci and Vj. In the first step, B is lifted with a specific factor q1, where each element bij is replaced by a q1 × q1 square binary matrix which contains exactly bij “1”s in each row and in each column. In the second step, the resultant matrix is further lifted with a specific factor q2, where each element “1” is replaced by a q2 × q2 circulant permutation matrix, and each element “0” is replaced by a q2 × q2 zero matrix. Let H be the generated LDPC matrix of size M × N . It follows that M = q1q2m and N = q1q2n.
A non-systematic form of H can be converted into
a systematic form by Gaussian elimination method, i.e., H = [I|P T ]. When considering systematic encoding, the generator matrix is constructed in the form that G = I | P of size K × N , where K = N − M , I is the identity matrix of order K, and P is a binary matrix of size K × M obtained by transforming H.

When the source is nonuniform, the information bits and the parity bits after systematic encoding will be transmitted on an asymmetric UPA channel and a symmetric EPA channel, respectively, with respect to the binary UPA modulation. It is thus unreasonable to apply directly the PEXIT analysis.
In order to circumvent the channel asymmetry issue caused by UPA, we propose a UPA-PEXIT analysis method, where the symmetric AWGN channels are still kept for coping with the nonuniform information bits, but they are viewed as the surrogate channels for the target asymmetric UPA channels. To incorporate the power gain of UPA, the gap in the Shannon limit between UPA and EPA channels is counted in calculating the initial MI regarding the information bits. In this way, the code optimality pertaining to the surrogate channels can be retained on the target asymmetric UPA channels [23], [24].

IV. CODE DESIGN AND OPTIMIZATION
In this section, we first show that existing PLDPC codes can be optimized in terms of decoding threshold by finding the best classification of VNs when used for nonuniform sources under the binary UPA modulation. Then, we present the UPA-DE algorithm for designing new PLDPC codes while optimizing the classification of VNs in conjunction with the UPA-PEXIT analysis.

B. UPA-DE Algorithm for Code Design
The objective of protograph design is to find a base matrix that can achieve the lowest decoding threshold at an acceptable error-floor level. It is well-known that introducing an amount of degree-2 VNs in the protograph can improve the decoding threshold. However, the existence of many such nodes will raise the error-floor to an unacceptable level. To reach a good balance, a principle of code design is to have the number of degree-2 VNs not exceeding the total number of CNs, which ensures the linear minimum distance growth property [30].
Another principle of code design is to limit the base matrix size. One reason is that the size of search space for a base matrix is exponentially increasing with the base matrix size. Suppose that the base matrix has size m × n and the maximum entry value is bmax. As the entries of the base matrix could take values in [0, bmax], the search space has size (1 + bmax)mn−1. The more important reason is that a large base matrix size restricts the choice of the lifting factor q when the code length is fixed, making it difficult to design an proper interleaver and avoid high error-floor level caused by trapping sets.
The DE algorithm [18] provides an efficient way to find an optimal base matrix by iteratively improving base matrix candidates, where the decoding threshold is used as the cost function. For code design under the binary UPA modulation for nonuniform sources, we propose the UPA-DE algorithm which is able to evaluate all distinct classifications of VNs by calling the UPA-EXIT analysis.
In the UPA-DE algorithm, we impose three composition constraints on base matrix candidates as follows:
1) All entries take values within the range [0, bmax]; 2) One specific column (the last column in our example) is
fixed with a pattern of weight 2, and any other column

has a weight of at least 3, which satisfies the principle

of linear minimum distance growth; 3) Every row has a weight of at least 2, which

guarantees the minimum construction of base matrix

candidates.

only if matrix B meets all the above composition conditions.

VI. CONCLUSION
Under binary UPA modulation for nonuniform sources, a UPA-PEXIT analysis algorithm based on surrogate channel has been proposed for analyzing the decoding threshold of a PLDPC code over the Rayleigh channel, where the surrogate channel is introduced for coping with the channel asymmetry problem caused by UPA. It has been shown that the classification of VNs in protograph for the coded bits does affect the decoding performances of PLDPC codes, especially when the source entropy is far smaller. Hence, PLDPC codes that are originally designed under BPSK modulation can be improved in decoding threshold by classification optimization in protographs. In order to comprehensively consider the protograph structure and the classification, a UPA-DE algorithm has been proposed in conjunction with the UPA-PEXIT analysis for designing optimal PLDPC codes. For demonstration, several optimal PLDPC codes with different code rates are constructed for three typical source statistics by applying the proposed UPADE algorithm. The superiority of the optimal codes to the RJA code in BER and FER performance is shown by simulations, which is in line with the analysis results in decoding threshold. Moreover, the optimized un-punctured PLDPC codes have better performance than the exsiting punctured PLDPC codes under UPA modulation, especially in low decoding complexity.

Yu-Cheng He (Member, IEEE) received the B.Sc., M.Sc., and Ph.D. degrees from Xidian University, Xi’an, China, in 1986, 1989, and 2002, respectively, all in information and communications engineering. From 1999 to 2000, he was an Exchange Student at Shizuoka University, Hamamatsu, Japan, while working on the Ph.D. degree. Since 2001, he has been an Associate Professor with the State Key Laboratory of Integrated Services Networks, Xidian University. From 2003 to 2005, he completed a two-year Post-Doctoral Fellowship at École Polytechnique de Montréal, Montréal, QC, Canada. He is currently a Professor of communications engineering with Huaqiao University, Xiamen, China. He has authored or coauthored many of journal and conference papers in the areas of channel coding and wireless communications.

Lin Zhou (Member, IEEE) received the B.E., M.E., and Ph.D. degrees in information and communications engineering from Xidian University, Xi’an, China, in 2004, 2007, and 2011, respectively. He is currently an Associate Professor with the College of Information Science and Engineering, Huaqiao University, Xiamen, China. His research interests include LDPC codes, polar codes, capacityapproaching coded modulation systems, wireless communications, and optical communications.
