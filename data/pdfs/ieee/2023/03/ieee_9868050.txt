Performance Evaluation of Burst Error Correction by LDPC Coding and Iterative Decoding System in Magnetic Tape Drive
Yasuaki Nakamura , Taisei Akamatsu, Madoka Nishikawa , and Yoshihiro Okamoto Graduate School of Science and Engineering, Ehime University, Matsuyama 790-8577, Japan

The tolerance for burst error of interleaved low-density parity-check (LDPC) code is evaluated with the cross-interleaved Reed– Solomon code (CIRC) in the magnetic tape drive using a BaFe magnetic tape. We evaluate the interleaved LDPC coding and iterative decoding system with burst error detector (LDPC-BD) system by the required signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) to achieve “No errors” for the burst-like deterioration such as media defects to the CIRC with erasure error correction (CIRC-EC) system by computer simulation. The results clarify that the LDPC-BD system tolerates the longer burst errors than the CIRC-EC system and the LDPC-BD system achieves “No errors” with the 3.0–4.5 dB lower SNR than the CIRC-EC system.
Index Terms— Burst error correction, iterative decoding, LDPC code, magnetic tape drive.

I. INTRODUCTION

T HE tape systems provide high reliability, a long media lifetime, low power consumption, and low total cost of ownership, making them well suited for the cost-effective long-term retention of data for backup [1]. Studies on high-density recording have been conducted to continue increasing the cartridge capacities of magnetic tapes. Above all, the magnetic tapes using low-cost barium ferrite (BaFe) magnetic material are attracting attention as tapes that achieve a 123 Gbits/in2 recording areal density [1]. Furthermore, to meet the future needs for high-reliability information storage, it is vital to be tolerant of burst errors of tape systems.
In this research, we compare the tolerances for burst error of interleaved low-density parity-check (LDPC) code [2] with that of the conventional cross-interleaved Reed–Solomon (RS) code (CIRC) [3], [4] in a magnetic tape drive using BaFe magnetic tape.

An APP decoder calculates the log-likelihood ratio (LLR) as decoding reliability information for the EPR4 channel. The LLR sequence from the APP decoder is input to the burst detector and the sum-product (SP) decoder [2]. The burst detector outputs the burst information composed of the position and length of the burst by detecting the section where the LLR attenuation continues. The iterative decoding with turbo equalization is performed between the APP decoder and the SP decoder. Here, isp and iglobal stand for the maximum number of iterations in the SP decoder and the turbo equalization, respectively. The SP decoder decodes the sequence using the constraint of six-way interleaved LDPC code from LLR with the burst information by controlling the amplitude of LLR not to cause error propagation due to the burst-like signal degradation [9]. After the given iterations of the iterative decoder, the output data sequence is recovered by a hard decision unit. Note that burst detection is performed only the first time, and iterative decoding is performed using the same burst information.

Fig. 2 shows the block diagram of the CIRC-EC system. In the figure, the input data sequence is encoded with 25 272 bytes/unit over Galois field GF (28) under 8 bits as 1 byte [4]. Fig. 3 shows the dataset with C1 and C2 by the RS encoders equipped with 54- and 64-way byte interleavers.

The C1 encoder divides the input data sequence into every 468 bytes by a 54-way byte interleaver shown in Fig. 3, and a 6-byte error correctable parity C1 is applied to the row direction. In addition, a 5-byte error correctable parity C2 is applied to every 54 bytes. Finally, the 64-way byte interleaver reconverts the recording sequence into the input data sequence order. Here, the total coding rate of the CIRC-EC system is 0.823 [4]. After recording, reading, and equalizing, the EPR4 channel output is fed to the Viterbi detector for EPR4 and the burst detector. The burst detector is the same as the LDPC-BD system and outputs the burst information. The erasure correction and the normal EC are performed by C2 and C1 decoders. In this research, the iterative decoding is performed between C2 and C1 decoders up to icirc times, as shown in Fig. 2.

Fig. 10 shows the SP decoder input LLRs before iterative decoding for one unit. The parameters are set to

Lburst = 2.0 × 104 and SNRS = 16 dB. The green circle and red time symbols show the correct LLR and the erroneous LLR, respectively. Fig. 10(a) and (b) shows the SP decoder input LLRs without and with burst detector. As shown in Fig. 10(b), the burst detector sets the LLR at the detected burst position to “0.0,” which corresponds to the least reliability.
Fig. 11 shows the SP decoder input LLRs with the iterative decoding at isp = 10 and iglobal = 10. The symbols show the same as in Fig. 10. As can be seen from Figs. 10(a) and 11(a), the iterative decoding without a burst detector has increased reliability in the erroneous LLR at the burst position. On the other hand, from Figs. 10(b) and 11(b), you can see that the erroneous LLRs outside of burst position are reduced.
Fig. 12 shows the SP decoder output LLRs after iteration. The symbols and simulation parameters are the same as in Fig. 11. As can be seen from the figure, the LDPC system without the burst detector does not improve the LLR, and the erroneous LLR remains. On the other hand, the LDPC-BD system can improve the LLRs and can decode without error.
