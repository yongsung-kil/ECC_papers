Free-Ride Coding for Constructions of Coupled LDPC Codes
Qianfan Wang , Member, IEEE, Suihua Cai , Member, IEEE, and Xiao Ma , Member, IEEE

Abstract— Free-ride coding, as an approach that admits transmission of a few extra bits over a low-density paritycheck (LDPC) coded link without bandwidth expansion, is applied in this paper to construct coupled LDPC codes. Firstly, we present a syndrome channel model and derive the lower and upper bounds on its capacity (referred to as accessible capacity), indicating the feasibility of the reliable transmission of extra bits. Secondly, we present the performance evaluation on both the word error rate (WER) and the bit error rate (BER) for the freeride codes with simple lower and upper bounds. Then we propose three applications of free-ride coding to construct coupled LDPC codes, including implicit globally-coupled LDPC (GC-LDPC) codes, partial product-LDPC codes, and terminated spatiallycoupled LDPC (SC-LDPC) codes, all of which have the figure of merits that they share the same code rates with the basic component LDPC codes. Simulation results show that: 1) the proposed GC-LDPC codes can outperform the component LDPC codes, yielding a coding gain of up to 0.8 dB; 2) the proposed product codes with (3, 6)-regular LDPC component codes of length 1024 can lower the WER from 10−2 down to 10−6 at the SNR around 2 dB; 3) the proposed terminated SC-LDPC codes can perform as well as the conventional terminated SC-LDPC codes but without any rate loss.
Index Terms— Free-ride codes, globally-coupled LDPC (GCLDPC) codes, LDPC codes, product codes, spatially-coupled LDPC (SC-LDPC) codes.

Without termination, the paritycheck matrix of an SC-LDPC code exhibits the semi-infinite convolutional form. However, for practical use and potential “decoding wave” [9], termination is required. This inevitably leads to a rate loss, especially for a finite coupling length L.
The globally-coupled LDPC (GC-LDPC) [10] codes are another class of coupled LDPC codes, where Tanner graphs of L independent LDPC block codes are coupled by the additional globally-connecting check nodes. As a result, the code length can be scaled up (implying promising performance improvement) based on the component LDPC codes, avoiding construction of a completely new longer LDPC code. Since then, the Reed-Solomon based construction [11], the array dispersion construction [12] and the cyclic structure construction [13] for the GC-LDPC codes have been proposed. Notice that the codes proposed in [14] are actually serially concatenated LDPC codes while can be decoded as GC-LDPC codes. A remarkable feature of the GC-LDPC codes is that the decoder of the component LDPC codes is reusable [12]. However, the encoder of the conventional GC-LDPC codes is usually loosely related to those of the component LDPC codes since the juxtaposition of multiple codewords of the component LDPC codes is usually not a codeword of the GC-LDPC code due to the existence of the global parity checks. This somehow brings inconvenience for real design and practical implementation.1 Also notice that the rate loss caused by the additional global parity checks can not be neglected for the finite coupling length L.
The product codes using LDPC codes as the component codes can also be viewed as a class of coupled LDPC codes, where the coupling constraints across multiple LDPC codewords are introduced by the additional column encoding. Typically, the product LDPC codes with high rate algebraic block codes were proposed in [20], [21], and [22], where the algebraic codes are used to alleviate the error floor of the LDPC codes. In summary, the aforementioned three types of coupled LDPC codes can improve the performance of the LDPC block codes but lead to rate loss or rate reduction. For the SC-LDPC codes, tail biting is a straightforward way to avoid such rate loss but usually degrades the performance when compared with the termination [23], [24], [25]. The rate loss can also be compensated (mitigated) by connecting together several individual SC-LDPC code chains [26], [27], [28], [29] or by modifying the structure at the boundaries of the coupling chain [30], [31].
One main objective of this paper is to construct coupled LDPC codes without any rate loss. This can be achieved by using the free-ride coding [32], [33], which is an approach to transmitting a few number of extra bits over the payload coded link. Suppose that a coded link with a well-designed LDPC code is available for transmitting the payload data and that, for some new applications, a few extra bits need to be transmitted. To achieve this, one may build an extra coded link for the extra bits, which leads to bandwidth expansion. If the bandwidth is strictly limited, one may construct a new code with an unaltered length but a higher dimension (to encode jointly the payload data and the extra bits), which causes inconvenience due to the complete reconstruction of the coded link. Fortunately, the extra bits can be transmitted over the payload coded link using the free-ride coding, where the extra bits are encoded by the free-ride codes and then superimposed (XORed) on the payload codewords, costing neither extra transmission power nor extra bandwidth. Furthermore, the extra bits in the free-ride coding can be more reliable than the payload link, resulting in a negligible effect on the payload data. Thanks to these advantages, the free-ride coding has been applied to construct codes [34], [35], [36], [37], enhance age of information in vehicular network [38], [39] and transmit feedbacks of two-way communications [40].
The basic idea to construct coupled LDPC codes without rate loss is to treat the global parity-check bits, the column syndrome bits, or the partial syndrome bits as extra bits (transmitted using the free-ride coding). Aided by the freeride coding, the resulting coupled LDPC codes have the same code rates as their component LDPC codes and can even outperform the corresponding conventional coupled codes, which are the main benefits from applying the free-ride coding to construct the coupled LDPC codes, especially in the scenario where the bandwidth is strictly limited. Different from other approaches to compensating or mitigating the rate loss, the proposed scheme does not change the structure of the component codes, and hence the encoder and the decoder of the component LDPC codes can be reusable. As an extension work of [34] and [35], the main contributions of this paper are as follows.
1) We enrich the original free-ride coding presented in [32] and [33]. Firstly, we define the syndrome channel and derive its accessible capacity with lower and upper bounds, implying that the reliable transmission of extra bits is possible. Secondly, we evaluate the performance

on both word error rate (WER) and bit error rate (BER) for the free-ride codes with simple lower and upper bounds, implying that transmitting a few extra bits has negligible effect on the original LDPC coded link. 2) We present the implicit GC-LDPC codes, where the global check bits are transmitted as the extra bits using the free-ride transmission. This enables reusing the component LDPC encoder, a distinguished feature from the conventional GC-LDPC codes. We present an efficient two-stage decoding algorithm. At the first stage, we perform the free-ride decoding to recover the global check bits and then remove the effects of the (estimated) global checks on the received vector. At the second stage, we perform the local LDPC decoding and, if necessary, the global LDPC decoding. The simulation results show that the proposed implicit GC-LDPC codes can improve the performance of the component LDPC codes, yielding an extra coding gain of up to 0.8 dB, but without any code rate reduction. 3) We present the partial product-LDPC codes, where the column syndromes are transmitted as the extra bits using the free-ride transmission. For decoding, we present a two-stage decoding algorithm. At the first stage, we perform the free-ride decoding to recover the column syndromes and then remove the effects of the (estimated) column syndromes on the received vector. At the second stage, we perform the row decoding (LDPC decoding) and, if necessary, the column decoding (BCH decoding) to recover residual errors in partial information bits. Then we perform the row decoding for the unrecovered rows by exploiting the messages associated with those successfully decoded columns. The simulation results show that the proposed partial product-LDPC codes can reduce the WER of the component LDPC code from 10−2 to 10−6 at the SNR around 2 dB. 4) We present the rate-lossless terminated SC-LDPC codes, where the partial syndromes are transmitted as the extra bits using the free-ride transmission. For decoding, we present a two-stage windowed decoding algorithm. At the first stage, we perform the free-ride decoding to recover the partial syndrome bits and then remove the effects of the (estimated) partial syndrome bits on the received vector. At the second stage, we perform the windowed decoding of the SC-LDPC codes. The simulation results show that the proposed terminated SC-LDPC codes can perform as well as the original terminated SC-LDPC codes, but without any code rate loss.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. In Section II, after reviewing the free-ride coding, we present the syndrome channel model and derive lower and upper bounds on its accessible capacity. We also present the performance analysis of the payload link with the free-ride coding. In Section III, we present three applications of the free-ride codes to code constructions, including implicit GC-LDPC codes, partial product-LDPC codes and rate-lossless terminated SC-LDPC codes. Section IV concludes this paper.

II. FREE-RIDE CODING FOR SYNDROME CHANNELS
A. Review of Free-Ride Codes
The free-ride codes were proposed in [32] and [33], where
a few extra bits can be transmitted over an existing coded
transmission link but without any cost of extra transmission
energy or extra bandwidth. 1) Payload Transmission: Let u ∈ F2k denote the payload
data to be transmitted. Let Cp[n, k] denote a binary LDPC code with length n and dimension k, whose parity-check matrix is denoted by H. The LDPC code Cp[n, k] is referred to as the payload code. For the original payload link (without extra transmission), at the transmitter, u is encoded by the encoding algorithm of Cp[n, k], resulting in an LDPC codeword v ∈ Fn2 . Then the codeword v is transmitted over a memoryless channel, resulting in a received sequence y. At the receiver, upon receiving y, the decoding algorithm of the payload code is performed to recover the payload data u.
2) Extra Transmission: Now we assume that, in addition to the payload data, a few extra bits of length   k need to be transmitted. To achieve this, one may add an extra coded
link, which leads to bandwidth expansion. If the bandwidth is strictly limited, one may construct a new code [n, k+], which leads to inconvenience due to the complete reconstruction of
new codes. Fortunately, as proposed in [32] and [33], the extra
bits can be transmitted over the payload coded link by using
the free-ride coding (see Fig. 1 for reference) without any cost
of extra transmission energy or extra bandwidth. The encoding
and decoding of the free-ride transmission are described as
follows. • Encoding: The payload data sequence u ∈ F2k is encoded by the payload code Cp[n, k], resulting in an LDPC codeword v ∈ F2n. Remark 2: To simplify the design, we provide a guideline for constructing the free-ride codes and the derived coupled codes as follows. Given the payload code, we first determine (by experience via simulating and analyzing, say, randomly constructed free-ride codes [40]) the maximum number of transmitted extra bits, denoted as max, under the constraint that the payload link is not degraded noticeably. Then we find an RM code with dimension  max to construct the free-ride coding to ensure a sufficiently reliable extra transmission. Finally, the total number of coupling check bits is determined, which should be less than or equal to L, where L denotes the coupling length (i.e., the number of payload codes).

III. COUPLED LDPC CODES USING FREE-RIDE CODING
In this section, we will present three types of coupled LDPC codes but without any rate loss, as compared with the component LDPC block codes. The basic idea is to transmit the global check bits for the GC-LDPC codes, column syndromes for the product-LDPC codes or partial syndromes for the SC-LDPC codes as the extra bits using the free-ride transmission, resulting in three new coupled LDPC codes, respectively. Furthermore, for each presented coupled LDPC code, we present the simple lower bound by assuming that the extra bits are perfectly known at the decoder.
At the transmitter, the information sequences (payload data) are encoded by the component LDPC code or the SCLDPC code with the coupling check bits (say, termination bits) being calculated out. Then the coupling check bits are encoded (as the extra bits) by the free-ride codes and superimposed (XORed) on the payload codewords, resulting in the transmitted codewords. At the receiver, a twostage decoding algorithm (from a high-level overview) is performed.2 The first stage performs the free-ride decoding (to obtain the coupling check bits) and removes the effects of the estimated free-ride codewords on the received vectors, and then the second stage performs the decoding of the original code as usual to recover the information sequences.
Remark 3: Taking the original coupled LDPC codes as the benchmark, the complexity increase caused by the RMbased free-ride coding is relatively low compared with payload LDPC codes (or column component BCH codes in product construction). At the transmitter, the free-ride encoding (implemented by binary operations) is required for each block. At the receiver, the free-ride decoding is required before decoding the payload code, where the free-ride decoding consists of the soft syndrome computation (equivalent to one time massage update for all check nodes) and the soft decoding of the RM code (implemented by the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) algorithm as [41] with complexity of O(m log m)). After removing the effect of the estimated free-ride codewords, the payload codes have a similar complexity to the conventional ones.3

IV. CONCLUSION
This paper has made the first attempt to apply the free-ride coding approach to constructions of coupled LDPC codes. The basic idea is to transmit extra bits introduced by additional constraints along with the LDPC codewords in a superposition manner without any rate loss. We present three constructions of coupled LDPC codes: 1) the implicit GC-LDPC codes, which outperform the component LDPC codes, yielding an extra coding gain of up to 0.8 dB; 2) the partial productLDPC codes, which can improve the performance of the component LDPC codes, yielding an extra coding gain of up to 1.0 dB; 3) the rate-lossless terminated SC-LDPC codes, which can perform as well as the conventional terminated SC-LDPC codes. For all code constructions, we have presented the encoding and decoding algorithms as well as concrete construction examples. Taking the original coupled LDPC codes as the benchmark, the complexity increase is caused by the free-ride encoding at the encoder and the free-ride decoding at the decoder, both of which are relatively low-cost. In addition, although almost all numerical results are based on (3, 6)-regular LDPC codes, we point out that the generalization to other LDPC codes with relatively low row weight is straight forward.
