Abstract‚ÄĒLow-Density

codes are two classes of channel codes potentially able to
achieve the channel capacity, and both are used in current 5G
communication systems. Belief Propagation (BP), which is the
main decoding algorithm for LDPC codes could also be used
for decoding polar codes. A fully parallel fully unrolled BP
decoding architecture has been recently proposed for both of
these codes. In this paper, we investigate these two architectures
further and compare the two codes from the viewpoint of Bit-
Error Rate (BER) performance, achieved throughput, latency,
chip area, and also energy efficiency. Toward that, both archi-
tectures have been synthesized in a 28 nm CMOS technology,
and corresponding results are provided.
I.
INTRODUCTION
Forward Error Correction (FEC) is an indispensable part
of today‚Äôs communication systems, and its role in delivering
the required level of reliability and Quality of Service (QoS)
is vital. Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes [1] and po-
lar codes [2] are two important instances of FEC codes which
both have been adopted for 5G communication systems. In
this regard, attempting to achieve a decoding throughput of
hundreds of Gbps or higher has been a mainstream research
endeavor, specifically for these two codes.
The main decoding algorithm for LDPC codes which has
the potential for such a high throughput is the Belief Propa-
gation (BP) algorithm. With (fully) unrolled fully parallel
architectures several works have thus far reported a near
Tbps throughput [3]‚Äď[5], and [6].
On the other side, a polar code‚Äôs defining attribute. i.e.,
channel polarization stems from Successive Cancellation
(SC) decoding [2]. Therefore, SC and its important variants
like SC List (SCL) [7], [8] and SC-flip [9] are more common
for decoding polar codes. However, SC-based decoding tech-
niques are serial in nature and suffer from a low decoding
throughput. Arikan then proposed a BP decoding algorithm
in [10] which is inherently parallel like BP decoding of
LDPC codes, and thus is able to facilitate high-throughput
decoding of polar codes. The first Tbps BP decoder for polar
codes was reported in [11].
In this paper, we aim to have a comparison between BP
decoding of LDPC and polar codes. Since our focus is
high throughput communication systems we study the fully
unrolled fully parallel architecture of each case by which the
ever highest throughput can be reached. As a baseline, we
rely on the two recent works [6] and [11] which report the
fastest decoding throughput, to the best of our knowledge,
for LDPC and polar codes, respectively. In our investigation,
we highlight the differences between the two architectures
which lead to considerable differences in chip area and
consumed energy per bit. We will provide simulation results
of the decoding methods to reveal their Bit-Error Rate (BER)
performance. An important observation from synthesis re-
sults is obtained showing how completely different the two
codes‚Äô complexity are related to the code rate. Therefore,
the pros and cons of the two codes for different code rates
are revealed. The decoding latency of the two codes is also
investigated and compared with each other.
The remaining of the paper is organized as follows. In
section II a brief overview of LDPC and polar codes is given.
In section III the fully parallel fully unrolled BP decoding
architecture of these two types of codes is outlined. Synthesis
and simulation results of the two architectures are provided
in section IV and then the two types of codes are compared
in terms of several factors such as chip area, energy per bit,
throughput, BER performance, clock frequency, latency, etc.
Final conclusions are made in section V.

B. Polar Decoding
To further increase BP decoding throughput of polar codes
successive stages of the algorithm can be unrolled. The
unrolling of a polar BP algorithm has two dimensions: (1)
unrolling stages of an individual iteration, and (2) unrolling
iterations of the whole BP algorithm.
The first dimension is understood by noting that each
iteration of the BP algorithm outlined in section II-B consists
of 2n stages which can be unrolled. However, the data
dependency between stages is a challenge needing to be
addressed. For example, in stages 4 and 5 of an iteration
in BP decoding of a polar code with factor graph of Fig. 2,
messages computed previously during stages 2 and 1 of that
iteration are respectively needed. For the general case, it can
be stated that stages n + 1, n + 2, . . . , 2n ‚ąí1 of an iteration
are reliant on messages computed in stages n‚ąí1, n‚ąí2, . . . , 1
of that iteration, respectively.
The second dimension arises when an unrolling idea
similar to what outlined in section III-A for LDPC codes
is to be applied to BP polar decoding, i.e., decoding succes-
sive iterations of the algorithm with separate but replicated
modules used for a single iteration.
The decoding architecture shown in Fig. 7 was proposed
in [11], which is able to deliver a fully-unrolled BP decoder
for polar codes. This architecture corresponds to the polar
code of length 8 whose factor graph was shown in Fig. 2.
As seen, FFs are responsible for solving the data-dependency
problem
With such a decoding architecture input sequences enter
the decoder in every clock cycle, like the fully unrolled
LDPC codes. However, the initial latency, i.e., the required
time duration for decoding the first sequence, will be equal
to 2n.itemax clock cycles.

CONCLUSION
A fully parallel fully unrolled BP decoding architecture,
one for LDPC codes and another for polar codes were
investigated in this paper. The architectures were synthe-
sized in a 28 nm CMOS technology. For an LDPC code
complexity rises as its rate decreases, leading to a reduction
in clock frequency as well. Therefore, we observed in our
investigation that a 5/6-rate LDPC code can achieve higher
throughput than a 5/6-rate polar code of similar length.
However, the situation is opposite for a 1/2-rate LDPC
and polar code. Another important deduction is that polar
codes fall behind LDPC codes in terms of the core area,
latency, and energy efficiency. From the standpoint of BER
performance, LDPC codes deliver a higher coding gain, yet
with lower quantization precision. Moreover, the undesirable
phenomenon of error-floor is more severe in polar codes than
LDPC codes.
