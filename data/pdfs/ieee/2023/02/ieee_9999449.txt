A Low-Complexity RS-SPC Product Coding Scheme for Optical Systems
Mingyang Zhu , Graduate Student Member, IEEE, Ming Jiang , Member, IEEE, Chunming Zhao , Member, IEEE, and Fan Yu

Abstract— This paper presents a product coding scheme based on binary images of Reed-Solomon (RS) codes and single-paritycheck (SPC) codes for high-speed communications. Utilizing the special selection of components, we propose hybrid softand hard-decision iterative decoding (ID) algorithms for these codes, during which many RS component decoders can be early terminated. The proposed hybrid ID only exchanges ternary messages between component decoders and early stops the RS component decoders satisfying a proposed stopping criterion, thus low hardware and computational complexities are guaranteed. The hybrid ID of RS-SPC product codes not only performs comparable to the soft-decision decoding of low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes and block turbo codes (BTCs), but also takes much lower computational complexity and smaller decoder data flow. We also propose semi-analytical methods to estimate the waterfall and error-floor performances of the proposed scheme, which yield relatively accurate estimated results. The simulation results and error-floor estimations indicate that some good RS-SPC product codes have very low error floors that satisfy the requirements of optical systems.
Index Terms— Forward error correction, product codes, binary image RS codes, low-complexity decoding.
I. INTRODUCTION
H IGH-PERFORMANCE forward error correction (FEC) schemes are required for modern high-speed optical systems to achieve a large coding gain at a target bit error rate (BER) [1], [2], [3], [8]. For a transmission system that supports 100 Gb/s or multiples thereof, two coding schemes are typically used: block turbo codes (BTCs) [5], [13], [29] and low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [2], [12], [17], [30].

BTCs can perform well with hard-decision decoding (HDD), but the soft-decision decoding (SDD) of BTCs no longer has capacity-approaching performance when the overheads are more than 15%, since the number of codewords to consider in Pyndiah’s decoder [5] becomes prohibitively large. Many capacity-approaching BTCs require long component codes to realize small overheads, giving a total blocklength of 100,000 ∼ 1,000,000 bits [4]. Besides, some spatiallycoupled product-like codes, such as staircase codes, braided BCH codes, and block Markov superposition transmission codes [6], [7], [30], [31], also show remarkable coding gains, particularly for the binary symmetric channel (BSC). However, there are still some limitations for the mentioned codes: 1) The performance is limited by HDD, and 2) the decoding latency is a potential problem due to very large blocklengths or decoding windows (e.g., millions of bits).
On the other hand, LDPC codes can fully utilize soft information under belief propagation (BP) decoding. Recently, an LDPC code has been adopted as the FEC scheme in the next-generation passive optical network (PON) standard [17]. In the 10G PON systems [16], the (255, 223) Reed-Solomon (RS) code is used, and the Berlekamp-Massey HDD (BMHDD) of this code can lower the BER to 10−12 with an input BER of 10−3 [9]. Under the soft BP decoding, the LDPC code adopted in the 50G PON extensively improves the errorcorrection ability, e.g., it provides a post-FEC BER of 10−12 with an input BER of 10−2. However, the implementation of a several hundreds of Gb/s soft BP decoder is still a challenge, which is mainly due to the large internal decoder data flow of exchanged messages [6], [12], [13].
Improving performance while also maintaining low decoding complexity is important for the next-generation optical systems requiring very low BER and faster processing speed. In this paper, we construct a new type of BTCs based on the binary images of RS codes and single-parity-check (SPC) codes, called RS-SPC product codes, and propose lowcomplexity two-phase decoding schemes. Specifically, in the two-phase decoding, the local decoding (LD) is to decode individual RS component codes by HDD, and the global decoding (GD) is to decode the whole product code by the proposed hybrid soft- and hard-decision iterative decoding. It is worth noting that conventional BCH- or RS-based BTCs with high-rate components [5], [13] can hardly trust their LD due to miscorrections [13], [26]. A. Local Decoding

To achieve effective LD, component codes with large min-

imum distance are required, which is why LD can be applied

to RS-SPC product codes rather than the conventional BTCs.

In LD, we use BM-HDD to decode every sub-block (i.e.,

RS component code) and freeze the sub-blocks satisfying a

proposed stopping criterion. The freezing operation means that

only the hard decisions of a frozen sub-block participate in

the subsequent decoding (i.e., GD), and the hard decisions

will not be changed. The freezing operation reduces a lot of

unnecessary computations and data flows in GD. However, it is

well-known that BM-HDD may cause miscorrections partic-

ularly for the algebraic codes with small minimum distance

[26, Ch. 7]. If a frozen sub-block is actually miscorrected, the

decoding will fail. Therefore, we propose a simple stopping

criterion based on the so-called ML metric threshold to avoid

miscorrections.

We call the above decoding process the iterative decoding (ID). From the perspective of message passing in ID, we can see there is no soft information exchanged between component decoders, which leads to low complexity. For column decoding, it is clear that the column decoders output binary messages (the hard decisions of Lκ). For row decoding, the situation is a little more complex. Actually, the outputs of row decoders can be seen as ternary messages such as {−1, 0, +1}. We can take ±1 to be the signs for the bits in frozen sub-blocks and take 0 to indicate active sub-blocks, which replace the functions of cˆi and F in (9), then (9) can be computed. Note that L0 used in (9) is initialized from the channel, which does not propagate.
We describe the ID of RS-SPC product codes in Algorithm 1. In the Line 4 of Algorithm 1, we can see that ID will be terminated if no new sub-blocks can be corrected by row decoding. When |F κ−1| = |F κ−2| for κ > 1, the column decoding can not generate a different result, i.e., Lκ = Lκ−1. Therefore, The decoder will be “locked” no matter how many iterations it continues to take. In our simulations, we find that although the maximum number of iterations can be set to Imax = l, the decoder is likely to be terminated at a small κ (e.g., κ = 6). This phenomenon brings both advantages and disadvantages. The good side is that we can set Imax to a very small number to reduce the maximal decoding complexity while the performance will not degrade. However, the bad side is that the best performance of ID is limited by this locking effect, since we can not improve the performance by increasing Imax.

Note that the ID-NACA with σa = 0 is equivalent to the ID. Although artificial noise is introduced in ID-NACA, the decoding can still be seen as ternary message passing decoding since the artificial noise is not exchanged between row and column decoders. Due to the Chase-like characteristic of ID-NACA, the maximum number Imax of iterations for ID-NACA can be sufficiently large to improve the performance. Moreover, even if Imax is large, we observed that the average number Iavg of iterations is still quite small thanks to the proposed early stopping criterion (e.g., Iavg is usually less than 3 at low-BER regions).
In the proposed ID and ID-NACA, Mθ and σa are two decoding parameters that need to be optimized by simulations. Due to the different channel characteristics and scaling schemes for L-values (see the beginning of this section), we should optimize these two decoding parameters according to a specific channel (AWGN or ISI). Let {Mawgn, σawgn} and {Misi, σisi} be the optimized decoding parameter settings for the AWGN and ISI channels, respectively. When we only consider the L-value scaling without considering the channel characteristics, we can find that Misi = 4Mawgn and σisi = 4σawgn due to the L-value scaling. However, due to the special characteristics of the ISI channel, such as more consecutive bit errors, we empirically find that for the ISI channel, the best Mθ and σa usually satisfy Misi > 4Mawgn and σisi < 4σawgn. For high-rate RS-SPC product codes (e.g., rate > 0.7), we empirically verified that σawgn ∈ [0.05, 0.1] and σisi ∈ [0.15, 0.25] are robust settings for σa.
Remark 3: The artificial noise is exploited in ID-NACA, but we do not need arithmetic modules to generate Gaussian RVs on the fly. We can generate the artificial noise offline and store it in look-up tables. For example, in our simulations, we store a length-65536 sequence of the artificial noise and randomly choose one from the sequence when calculating Eq. (11). Recall that only ternary messages are exchanged during ID-NACA and ID, thus we can set the quantization scheme for ID-NACA or ID as Qch = 6 (for channel L-values and artificial noise) and Qex = 2 (for exchanged messages). Therefore, the artificial noise only requires 393 Kbits to store.

VI. ERROR FLOOR ANALYSIS FOR THE CODES OVER THE AWGN CHANNEL
Since many optical systems require very low error rate, the error floor of the coding scheme needs to be noticed. The error floor of an RS-SPC product code is usually caused by some error patterns with a specific structure as well as the miscorrections of BM-HDD. We can construct product codes from large-dmin RS codes for optical systems, then the error floor is completely caused by specific error patterns. VIII. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we propose two constructions of RS-SPC product codes and their decoding algorithms. Thanks to effective LD and the proposed ID/ID-NACA, RS-SPC product codes can approach or outperform the SDD of conventional BTCs in the AWGN or ISI channels, respectively, while with lower decoding complexity than iHDD of conventional BTCs. Moreover, we develop two semi-analytical methods to estimate the waterfall and error-floor performances, both of which yield believable results close to the simulation results. The simulation results and the error-floor estimation method indicate that some good RS-SPC product codes have very low error floors, such as a BER of 10−19. Due to low error floors and low decoding complexity, the proposed coding scheme is appealing for high-speed optical systems.
