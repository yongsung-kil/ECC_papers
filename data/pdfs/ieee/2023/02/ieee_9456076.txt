Boost Sum-Product Performance for Multiuser Detection in mMTC at Millimeter Wave

Tao Huang , Baoliu Ye , Member, IEEE, Bin Tang, Member, IEEE, Lei Xie , Member, IEEE, Sanglu Lu, Member, IEEE, and Song Guo , Fellow, IEEE

Abstract—We consider the multiuser detection (MUD) problem, i.e., how to separate and decode colliding data streams, in the uplink of massive Machine Type Communications (mMTC) at millimeter wave (mmWave). Operating on factor-graphs by passing messages, the sum-product algorithm and its variants are widely applied in many other scenarios. However, in this paper, we find that their performance in mMTC at mmWave could be dramatically degraded due to the ill-conditioned MUD channel gain matrix and the existence of enormous short cycles in their corresponding factor-graphs, which are caused by the limited scattering of mmWave and the sharing of a same codebook for error correction among densely located user equipments. Assuming LDPC codes are used for error correction, we further propose a novel sum-product based approach to dealing with the MUD problem in mMTC at mmWave. It first leverages the propagation characteristics of mmWave to optimize the factor-graph for MUD by removing short cycles based on nodesplit and node-contraction, and then takes a dynamic-programming based method to approximate the messages passing on the resulted factor-graph, which can achieve a higher decoding accuracy. Extensive simulation results show that our approach outperforms the state-of-the-art sum-product based approaches significantly.
Index Terms—Multiuser detection, sum-product, mMTC, mmWave, angular-domain, short-cycle-removing

1 INTRODUCTION
1.1 Background
MASSIVE Machine Type Communications (mMTC) is one of the three major 5G usage scenarios. It refers to wide area IoT use cases consisting of a large number of low cost IoT devices (referred to user equipments, UEs), where the density of UEs can be larger than 106=km2 [1]. In mMTC, many of the UEs will be sensors (e.g., cameras), which require real-time data streaming, leading to an enormous radio resource requirement of the network. As the number of UEs is exponentially growing and new intelligent IoT applications are continuously emerging, today’s sub-6 GHz spectrum is becoming more and more overcrowded.
Millimeter wave (mmWave) technology is a very promising approach to solving the above issue through the usage of massive chunks of high-frequency spectrum. Enabling mmWave in mMTC brings two significant advantages [2]. One is that, the load of low-power and low-cost UEs can be removed from the overcrowded sub-6 GHz spectrum. The other is that, the spectrum usage can be much more efficient by exploiting the directionality property of mmWave communication where

Although existing mmWave hardware/software components have high power consumption and also expensive, great efforts have been made towards improving the practicability of mmWave technology in mMTC, and the adoption of mmWave in mMTC is expected to be realized in the near future [2], [3], [4], [5].
Consider a mmWave-enabled mMTC scenario, where the low cost UEs are equipped with a limited number of radio-frequency-chains (RF-chains) [2], [6], [7], and only a limited number of concurrent data streams can be generated from each UE. In order to meet the high connection density requirement by fully utilizing spatial multiplexing, the base station (BS) which is working at mmWave and equipped with a massive antenna-array should simultaneously serve a large number of UEs in the same time-frequency resource, i.e., enabling Multiuser Multiple-Input and Multiple-Output (MU-MIMO), in both the downlink and uplink. However, in the uplink, due to the mutual interference of digitally modulated data streams emanating from different UEs in the same time-frequency resource, a crucial problem known as multiuser detection (MUD), i.e., how to efficiently separate the superimposed signal and decode each of the colliding data streams of UEs at the BS, should be well addressed.

4.1 Performance Deterioration due to IllConditioned MUD Channel Gain Matrix

In previous work studying the sum-product algorithm and

its common variants for MUD [10], [11], [12], the widely

used channel model is the statistical model, which is an ide-

alized abstraction of spatial propagation characteristics and assumes independent fading between different TX/RX-element pairs. This model is also heavily used in deriving capacity and in the development of space-time coding techniques [8]. However, in mMTC at mmWave where UEs simultaneously transmitting to the BS are densely located, and the number of signal propagation path clusters of each UE is very limited, this statistical model is less accurate, especially for the MUD problem.
