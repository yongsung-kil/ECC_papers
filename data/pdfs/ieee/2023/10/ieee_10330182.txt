Short codes for ADMM-based decoding
Dmitry Artemasov, Anna Fominykh, Alexey Frolov, Pavel Rybin, Kirill Andreev Center for Next Generation Wireless and IoT (NGW) Skolkovo Institute of Science and Technology Moscow, Russia
{dmitry.artemasov, anna.fominykh, al.frolov, p.rybin, k.andreev}@skoltech.ru

Abstract—Modern communication systems are required to operate in different regimes depending on the channel state, size, and purpose of the message. Considering today’s widely used coding schemes, it is known that different code designs and decoding algorithms are optimal for different regimes. From the point of view of technical implementation, it is convenient to have a unified scheme of coding and decoding for the whole range of possible modes. For the solution of this problem it is necessary to consider decoding algorithms based on new principles and code constructions optimized for them. Intensive research in recent years has shown that iterative algorithms based on the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) have good performance and are a perspective direction of research. In this paper, we consider short code constructions optimized for both classical decoding algorithms (successive cancellation and message-passing based algorithms) and ADMM-based algorithms, and compare their performance using numerical simulation methods.
Index Terms—coding schemes, linear programming, LP decoding, alternating direction method of multipliers, ADMM-based decoding, low-density parity-check codes, LDPC codes, beliefpropagation, BP decoding, polar codes, successive cancellation, SC decoding
I. INTRODUCTION
In modern communication systems, channels are distinguished according to their purpose and state, which determines the requirements for rate, delay and reliability of data transmission, which in turn affects both the length and rate of the code used and the choice of decoding algorithm. For example, in 5G communication systems, there is a control channel and a data transmission channel. The first channel requires moderate rate, low latency and high reliability, which leads to the use of short codes with moderate rate and an effective error correction algorithm. The requirements for the second channel range from moderate to high rate, moderate delay and reliability of transmission, which leads to the use of medium to long length codes with relatively high rate and high throughput decoding algorithm. It is known that among the currently widely used code schemes, different code designs and decoding algorithms are effective for different sets of requirements. Therefore, in the 5G standard, it is proposed to use polar codes with successive cancellation list (SCL) decoding algorithm for the control channel, and a family of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes with iterative minsum (MS) decoding algorithm for the data channel. from the point of view of technical realization it would be convenient and effective to have a unified code scheme which would be effective for all sets of requirements (regimes of operation). Obviously, this problem requires development and research of code schemes based on principles different from those widely used in modern solutions. One of such directions is to formulate the decoding problem as some optimization problem (in particular, linear programming problem) and to develop new decoding algorithms and code designs optimized for them, based on principles and methods of optimization theory.
The decoding problem can be reformulated as an integer linear programming (ILP) problem, subsequently addressed through the application of linear programming (LP) relaxation techniques. The inception of LP decoding can be traced back to its introduction by [1], followed by an alternative method proposed by [2] to cast the decoding problem into an LP framework. Comparing LP decoding to its counterpart, Belief Propagation (BP) decoding, reveals several distinctive advantages. Primarily, LP decoding exhibits superior decoding performance, particularly in scenarios characterized by high Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR). Furthermore, another important property of LP decoding is “maximum-likelihood (ML) certificate”, signifying that the integral solution derived from the LP decoding process coincides with the ML solution, underscoring its precision and reliability. Nonetheless, it is essential to acknowledge a notable drawback associated with LP decoding, which is its propensity for demanding substantial computational resources. Addressing this computational complexity challenge, in [3]–[5] it was introduced various algorithmic strategies aimed at streamlining the computational burden borne by LP decoders. Notably, in [5], the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) technique was seamlessly integrated into LP decoders. The ADMM decoder exhibits the remarkable capability of furnishing closed-form solutions during each iterative step, thereby significantly augmenting the overall computational efficiency of LP decoding processes.
Over the past ten years, efforts to enhance ADMM decoding have primarily focused on two main directions. The first one is reducing complexity and time consumption. On each iteration of ADMM decoding a Euclidean projection onto the paritycheck polytope is performed. This step makes the ADMM decoding process quite complicated. So, much research [6]– [11] have been devoted to simplifying this projection step. In

In reference [11], the authors introduced a technique called the “line segment projection”, which made the projection step much faster without needing a lot of iterations. At the same time, some researchers have been looking at other ways to make ADMM decoding more efficient. In reference [12], the authors suggested a different approach called the “check-polytope-free ADMM framework”. This approach was designed for the LP model from [2]. The unique thing about this method is that it can guarantee a linear level of complexity in terms of the length of the codewords.
Another direction aims at enhancing the decoding performance. This endeavor stems from the inherent characteristics of the LP problem, which relaxes the rigid binary constraints, allowing for non-integer values as solutions. Consequently, the optimal solution to the LP decoding problem may not necessarily be an integer; it could be a fraction. The challenge arises when the ADMM decoder generates a solution that has non-integers values, termed a “pseudocodeword”. In reference [13], researchers addressed this challenge by augmenting the LP problem’s objective function with a penalty term. This term functions as a scoring mechanism, favoring integer solutions over fractional ones. As a result, the decoder is more inclined to produce integer solutions. The study in question also delved into various penalty functions, conducting a comparative analysis of their impact on decoding performance. Reference [14] introduced a novel approach by utilizing a segmented function as the penalty term, yielding notable improvements in decoding performance. In the realm of innovation, reference [15] proposed an alternative penalty term that penalizes the check nodes rather than the variable nodes, leading to enhanced decoding efficiency. Furthermore, some researchers sought to elevate decoding performance by introducing novel constraints that serve a role analogous to the penalty term. In reference [16], the authors introduced what is known as ℓp-box constraints, while in [17], an auxiliary variable was introduced, accompanied by constraints designed to ensure that the variable is constrained to be either 0 or 1. These inventive strategies collectively contribute to an elevated level of decoding performance.
In this paper, we investigate the performance of short codes under ADMM-based decoders. First, following the paper [13], we examine the impact of the penalty form for non-integer solutions on the performance of augmented ADMM decoding. We consider the ℓ1 and ℓ22 penalties proposed earlier and introduce a new penalty, ℓ44. Next, following the approach from [15], we propose a novel check-node penalty based on the MS decoding approach and introduce a new MS-assisted ADMM decoding, which demonstrates superior performance. Subsequently, following idea from [18], we optimize a code specifically tailored for MS-assisted ADMM decoding. We then compare its performance with codes optimized for classical SCL and sum-product (SP) decoding algorithms. In the final section of the paper, we present numerical results and conduct an in-depth analysis.

An interesting observation is that as the number of iterations increases, the SP decoder reaches a performance limit, while the MSassisted ADMM decoder continues to significantly enhance its performance. V. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
In this paper, we delve into the performance analysis of short-length codes when subjected to ADMM-based decoding with various penalties, both previously known and newly proposed. Additionally, we compare the performance of these codes with classical SCL and SP decoding algorithms. Unlike previous studies, our approach does not involve optimizing the ADMM decoding algorithm for classical codes or those from established standards. Instead, we deliberately choose codes that are optimized to align with the specific decoding algorithm under consideration. Our findings indicate that the proposed MS-assisted ADMM algorithm exhibits significant performance improvements within certain parameter ranges, and its practical implementation appears to be straightforward.
And although the results presented in Section IV illustrate that ADMM-based decoding falls short of outperforming

message-passing algorithms when a low number of decoding
iterations is employed. However, increasing the number of
iterations allows the ADMM decoder to converge and yield
better results. As mentioned above, convergence rate can
be increased by introducing higher-degree penalties, but it
can negatively affect the convergence stability and result in
worse performance. The question of alternative techniques for
enhancing convergence remains an open area of research.
