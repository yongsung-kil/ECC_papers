A Non-uniform Quantization-based Hardware Architecture for BP Decoding of Polar Codes
Xinyi Gu, Mohammad Rowshan, Yixuan Xie, and Jinhong Yuan, Fellow, IEEE School of Electrical Eng. and Telecom., University of New South Wales (UNSW), Sydney, Australia
xinyi.gu@student.unsw.edu.au, {m.rowshan, yixuan.xie, j.yuan}@unsw.edu.au

Abstract—Belief propagation (BP) decoding of polar codes benefits from the high parallelism in hardware implementation and consequently provides high throughput compared to successive cancellation-based decoding algorithms. As the evolved log-likelihood ratios (LLRs) in the decoding process have a wide dynamic range, the quantization error can degrade the error correction performance of the BP decoder. To reduce the quantization error in a uniform quantization scheme, we may need to use more bits for each LLR value which is not desirable due to the required memory space. In this paper, we design an efficient hardware architecture for non-uniformly quantized LLR messages in BP decoding where the arithmetic operations on logarithmically compressed messages are replaced with the mapping to precomputed results in lookup tables. By employing 5-bit non-uniform quantization, the designed BP decoder architecture reduces the required memory space by 37.5 percent compared to 8-bit uniform quantization while additionally improving the block error rate (BLER) by more than 0.1 dB in high SNR regimes. Compared to uniform quantization, the BLER improvement of the designed architecture is up to 0.4 dB when both quantization schemes use 5 bits for LLRs.
Index Terms—Polar codes, belief propagation, decoding, hardware architecture, quantization.
I. INTRODUCTION
Polar codes can achieve channel capacity under a lowcomplexity successive cancellation (SC) decoding given infinite code length [1]. Since the error correction performance of SC decoder in finite block lengths is not satisfactory, various SC-based and belief propagation (BP)-based decoding algorithms were proposed. Introduced in [2], SC list (SCL) decoding provides a near maximum likelihood (ML) performance with a sufficiently large list size L at the cost of high computational complexity. Moreover, the precoding of polar codes by adding CRC bits [3] or by convolutional transformation [4]–[6] (a.k.a PAC codes) give an additional boost to the performance of polar codes. Nevertheless, their high computational complexity results in relatively low throughput.
Initially employed to decode polar codes in [7], BP decoder is an iterative message passing algorithm [8] that is widely used in the decoding of low-density parity check (LDPC) codes. From a practical standpoint, BP decoders offer a significant advantage over SC-based decoders as the operations on the factor graph (FG) can be executed in parallel, providing the potential for higher throughput and lower latency. hardware architectures of BP decoders have been improved in [9]–[11], [13]–[15] in terms of throughput, reduce latency, and minimize memory requirements.
In this work, our focus is on the quantization of the messages in BP decoding. The choice of quantization scheme for hardware implementation varies depending on the tolerance of the quantization error and the dynamic range of the messages. Non-uniform quantization schemes have been developed for SC-based decoders [16] to reduce memory requirements. As the iterative structure of BP decoders typically requires large memory resources, it can be employed. However, the hardware implementation of a non-uniform quantization is challenging due to introducing additional latency and difficulty in an efficient way of designing it. To the best of our knowledge, there is no hardware implementation of a non-uniform quantizer in the literature for any kind of polar decoder.
The main contribution of this work is the development of an efficient hardware architecture based on non-uniformly quantized messages for BP decoding. In the proposed design, by leveraging lookup tables, the arithmetic operations of the logarithmic mapping are replaced with pre-computed results to preserve throughput in a pipeline-based architecture. To evaluate and verify the design, we use a field programmable gate array (FPGA) platform. The numerical results reveal that under a similar BLER for uniform and non-uniform quantization, the designed architecture can reduce the required memory space by 37.5 percent compared to 8-bit uniform quantization. Compared to uniform quantization, the designed architecture demonstrates a BLER improvement of up to 0.4 dB when both quantization schemes use 5 bits for log-likelihood ratios (LLRs).

The belief propagation (BP) decoder is a widely used softin soft-out decoding algorithm for polar codes, operating iteratively on a (n + 1)-layer of factor graph (FG). Each layer contains N nodes for evolving the LLRs along the FG. We use (i, j), 0 ≤ i ≤ N − 1 and 0 ≤ j ≤ n, for the node representation in the FG, which has different permutations. This work adopts the FG used in [17]. The terms channel LLRs and evolved LLRs refer to the LLRs estimated at the channel outputs and at the nodes during the BP decoding, respectively. The decoding of BP employs two tables L and R, propagating messages in opposite directions. The layers are denoted as Lj and Rj. Initialized with channel LLRs in layer Ln, the left propagating message Li,j is known as a measure of a posterior information. Right propagating message Ri,j stores a measure of a prior information. Instead of computing the conversions in real-time, lookup tables are pre-built, including all the possible results of addition and subtractions for different levels. The top-level architecture of the BP decoder is drawn in Fig. 3 with two inputs and two outputs. The inputs are the channel LLRs Ln and a prior LLRs R0, which are the rightmost and leftmost layers in the FG for the two propagating tables respectively. When either meeting early stopping conditions or reaching the maximum iterations, the BP decoder outputs the decoded sequence and an indicator representing the completion of the decoding.

Since the designed quantization is non-uniform, a LUT-based searching scheme is employed for the summation computation instead of a conventional adder. The unit block for this computation is labeled as “LUT-based Q” in Fig. 4 and the detailed design is presented in Fig. 5, where “mag” and “sign” refer to the magnitude and the sign of the input LLRs, respectively.
For the designed 5-bit non-uniform quantizer, the number of quantized levels is 2(Q−1) = 16. To increase the throughput, an architecture consisting of a set of LUTs is introduced for addition and subtraction, respectively, allowing the input magnitude to be added to or subtracted from all quantization levels simultaneously. At the output, the results of adding to or subtracting from a are obtained in the form of two bit-sequences. Each bitsequence consists of 2(Q−1) · (Q − 1) = 64 bits, which are indexed as [0, 63]. Every 4 bits (as the output of one of the LUTs) in the sequence represent the result of the addition or subtraction operation on a (i.e. one of the 2Q−1 levels). Since the original addition and subtraction operations are replaced by the precomputed LUT-based non-uniform quantized operations, the increase in resource usage is relatively small. The LUT searches are conducted in parallel, resulting in a time complexity of one lookup table search concatenated with two multiplexers, which helps maintain the throughput compared to the conventional uniform quantization. However, the power consumption of this lookup table-based scheme increased as it performs a number of LUT searches in parallel. This design prioritizes better throughput, smaller memory, and improved error performance over energy efficiency.
V. NUMERICAL RESULTS AND DISCUSSIONS
In this section, the verification of the designed BP decoder is discussed. Implementation results are given in terms of throughput, latency, memory, resources, energy consumption, and error correction performance. The Xilinx ZC706 Systemon-Chip (SoC) evaluation platform is used to verify the designed lookup table-based BP decoder for polar codes of lengths 64, 128, 256, 512 and 1024.
We evaluate the BP decoder with the binary phase-shift keying (BPSK) modulation over the AWGN channel. The polar codes are constructed using the universal bit-channel reliability sequence specified in the 5G standard [12]. In Figure 7, we compare the error correction performance of the proposed 5-bit non-uniform quantization scheme with that of the BP decoder using floating-point arithmetic, as well as the results obtained using floating-points, uniform quantization schemes with Q = 5 and Q = 8 for the optimized minsum BP decoder with a scalar [13]. To ensure good error correction performance, the maximum number of iterations is set to 60 for the BP decoder to converge in most cases. It can be observed that the proposed non-uniform quantization scheme not only outperforms the 8-bit uniform quantization but also eliminates the performance degradation caused by the min-sum approximation, approaching the error correction performance of the BP decoder to the performance of floatingpoint LLRs at high SNR regimes. The number of iterations required for the BP decoder to converge varies depending on the noise of the channel, resulting in a variable average number of required iterations. Therefore, the latency and throughput of the BP decoder are SNR-dependent. Since this work does not focus on improving throughput, there are several hardware schemes that can be applied to increase the throughput of the BP decoder.
The block RAM (BRAM) size varies depending on the FPGA model, and its usage determines the memory requirements of the design. The number of required BRAM also depends on the length of the read and write bit sequence for each CC. In the case of BP decoder, the memory usage is related to the LLRs that evolve along the FG of the corresponding code length. Applying the proposed 5-bit non-uniform quantization method can achieve a better error performance than the BP decoder with the uniform quantization scheme and can save memory. Compared with the 8-bit uniform quantization scheme, about 37.5% of the memory can be saved, that is the total required memory for the BP decoder can be reduced by (8 − 5) · 2 · N · n = 6 · N · n bits.
VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we propose a hardware architecture design for the main operations in the BP decoding process for the non-uniformly quantized LLRs. In this design, all the addition and subtraction operations are performed in the logarithmic compressed domain based on lookup tables, i.e., no actual computation is carried out, but the operation is simple mapping to the precomputed results of additions and subtractions. As the quantized messages in the BP decoding process are logarithmically compressed, this design can significantly reduce the memory requirement for BP decoding. The verification of the proposed design on the FPGA platform shows no BLER degradation compared to software implementation while reducing the required resources for the decoder. The designed architecture maintains the throughput of the architecture based on uniformly quantized messages with a relatively small increment in energy consumption. As future work, we suggest employing different quantization schemes for different layers in the BP decoder, as it might reduce the quantization bits for the propagating information in the BP decoder.
