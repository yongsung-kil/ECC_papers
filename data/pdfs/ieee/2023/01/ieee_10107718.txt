BIC Codes: Bit Insertion-Based Constrained Codes With Error Correction for DNA Storage
Seong-Joon Park , Student Member, IEEE, Hosung Park , Member, IEEE, Hee-Youl Kwak , Member, IEEE, and Jong-Seon No , Fellow, IEEE

Abstract—In this article, we propose a new coding algorithm for DNA storage over both error-free and error channels. For the error-free case, we propose a constrained code called bit insertion-based constrained (BIC) code. BIC codes convert a binary data sequence to multiple oligo sequences satisfying the maximum homopolymer run (i.e., run-length (RL)) constraint by inserting dummy bits. We show that the BIC codes nearly achieves the capacity in terms of information density while the simple structure of the BIC codes allows linear-time encoding and fast parallel decoding. Also, by combining a balancing technique with the BIC codes, we obtain the constrained coding algorithm to satisfy the GC-content constraint as well as the RL constraint. Next, for DNA storage channel with errors, we integrate the proposed constrained coding algorithm with a rate-compatible low-density parity-check (LDPC) code to correct errors and erasures. Specifically, we incorporate LDPC codes adopted in the 5 G new radio standard because they have powerful error-correction capability and appealing features for the integration. Simulation results show that the proposed integrated coding algorithm outperforms existing coding algorithms in terms of information density and error correctability.
Index Terms—Bit insertion technique, constrained coding, DNA storage, GC-content, low-density paritycheck (LDPC) code, maxmimum homopolymer run, rate-compatible code.

The problem is that current storages such as hard disk drive and solid-state drive that cannot handle all these data. However, DNA storage can cope with these problems. The main idea of DNA storage is to store digital data as a sequence of DNA nucleotides by mapping binary data to four nucleotides of DNA: adenine (A), cytosine (C), guanine (G), and thymine (T). Due to the extremely small size of DNA nucleotides, DNA storage can store nearly 215 petabytes of data and it can safely store data for more than centuries [2]. Due to these characteristics, DNA storage has been studied actively as an attractive candidate to replace electronic-based media such as hard disk drive and solid-state drive. Furthermore, there are many other applications of DNA storage, such as DNA-based micro-disks [4] that allow selective and multiple accession of data-encoded DNA, and DNA hard drives [5] that allows for easy-writing and easy-reading, rewritable, and secure data storage using Nanopore sequencer.
For the encoding procedure of DNA storage system, long input binary data is partitioned into short binary sequences, which in turn, are converted into quaternary oligo sequences and stored in the form of DNA through the process of synthesis. When encoding the binary data to oligos, they should satisfy some biochemical constraints including the run-length (RL) and the GC-content constraints because violating these constraints can results in a high error rate in the DNA storage procedure. Therefore, constrained coding algorithms that generate oligo sequences satisfying the biochemical constraints are essential and active research area in the field of DNA storage. The ultimate goal of this line of research is to achieve the capacity for DNA storage, which is the theoretical bound of the information density, under the biochemical constraints with simple encoding and decoding algorithms.
For the decoding procedure, the stored oligo sequences are read by a sequencer. Since errors can occur during the synthesis and sequencing procedures, how to perfectly retrieve the original data in DNA storage is also an important issue in the practical DNA storage system. For example, the previous works in [2], [6], and [7] applied Reed-Solomon codes with fountain codes to handle errors in DNA storage system and the works in [8]– [11] applied low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes to correct errors.

A. Related Works
There have been many works on the constrained coding for DNA storage. As a pioneering study, the Huffman coding is used for the RL constraint [13]. Immink and Cai [14] first adopted RL limited codes to satisfy constraints in DNA storage. They also proposed a way to satisfy two constraints by counting GC-AT balanced codewords from chosen RL limited codes and using a look-up table [15], [16]. Erlich and Zielinski [2] proposed the so-called DNA Fountain code where the maximum RL of three and the GC-content [0.45,0.55] are satisfied by applying the Luby transform and screening. They also showed that nearly 215 petabytes of data can be stored in one gram of DNA. Wang et al. [17], [18] proposed a constrained coding algorithm using a finite state transition diagram to preserve the maximum RL of three and the GC-content [0.4,0.6]. In [19], they utilized the randomization and the M -ary mapping table to satisfy the constraints, while considering the error propagation.
Contrary to the aforementioned works, there are few works combining constrained coding algorithms with error-correcting codes. Nguyen et al. [20] combined the constrained coding algorithm with the binary Varshamov-Tenengolts (VT) codes to correct a single edit error while satisfying high information density. Lu et al. [21] designed nonbinary error correction codes with RL constraint using a 48-ary mapping table. Press et al. [22] proposed HEDGES codes that can handle multiple errors while satisfying two constraints in a single strand and showed effectiveness by conducting both in silico and in vitro experiments. Li et al. [23] also proposed multiple error correction techniques using DNA-LR code. Although these existing works handle multiple errors and satisfy RL and GC-content constraints, further improvements in information density and error-correction capability are required to be used in practical DNA storage applications.
B. Contributions
In this paper, we propose an integrated coding algorithm applied across multiple oligo sequences that i) satisfies the RL and GC-content constraints with the capacity approaching information density and ii) corrects errors occurred in the DNA storage system with high performance. The proposed coding algorithm consists of three components that play different roles as shown in Fig. 1 in detail: i) The constrained coding algorithm for the RL constraint, ii) the balancing technique for the GC-content constraint, and iii) the error-correction coding algorithm. We first balance the GC-content of the oligo sequence with the modified Knuth’s balancing technique [20]. Then, for the RL constraint

BIC codes convert the input binary data to oligo sequences satisfying the RL constraint by inserting dummy bits to break the long homopolymer run. The insertion occurs only in the predetermined positions when two simple insertion conditions for adjacent bits are satisfied. We derive the lower and the upper bounds on the average information density of the BIC codes and show the tightness of the bounds by comparing with the numerical simulation results. In spite of the simplicity of the proposed algorithm, the BIC codes nearly achieves the capacity of error-free channel by 99.62% for the maximum RL of three.
Finally, we incorporate 5 G new radio (NR) LDPC codes [24] with the BIC codes to correct errors and erasures in the DNA storage system and satisfy constraints in DNA storage. LDPC codes are capacity-approaching codes that have excellent error performance with low complexity [25]. One of the most important point in the proposed encoding algorithm is that we perform the LDPC encoding for the same bit positions across all oligo sequences (i.e., inter-oligo direction). Thus, we can correct not only substitution errors, but also erasures originating from insertion, deletion errors and oligo dropouts.
In addition, the property that 5 G NR LDPC codes support the rate compatibility as well as a large range of blocklength [24] motivates us to use LDPC codes as error correcting codes for our integrated coding algorithms. In BIC codes, we employ error-correcting codes in the vertical direction (i.e., inter-oligo direction) across the oligos, and the message length of the codes becomes the number of the oligo sequences. Since the number of generated oligo sequences depends on the size of input data, the error-correcting should support various lengths and rate-compatibility. By applying 5 G NR LDPC codes to the proposed balancing algorithm, we are able to cope with the problem of integration between constrained and error-correcting codes, while achieving high average information density and error-correction performance.

II. DNA STORAGE SYSTEM
A. Notations
For simplicity, we define the relation between the nucleotides {A, T, C, G}, quaternary symbols {0(4), 1(4), 2(4), 3(4)}, and binary symbols {00, 01, 10, 11} as follows: A = 0(4) = 00, T = 1(4) = 01, C = 2(4) = 10, G = 3(4) = 11. Let f be the second bit flipping function of quaternary symbol defined as follows: f (0(4)) = 2(4), f (1(4)) = 3(4), f (2(4)) = 0(4), f (3(4)) = 1(4). We assume that input binary sequence of n bits is stored in multiple oligo sequences and every oligo sequence has length k. Let x = (x1, . . ., xn) ∈ {0, 1}n be the whole input binary sequence and Oi = (oi,1, . . ., oi,k) ∈ {0(4), 1(4), 2(4), 3(4)}k be the ith oligo sequence of x for k < n. Also, we represent Oi by the binary sequence Oibit = (oiu,1, oil,1, . . ., oui,k, oli,k) ∈ {0, 1}2 k, where oui,j and oli,j represent the upper and lower bits of the quaternary symbol oi,j, respectively. Let m denote the maximum RL throughout the paper.

C. Characteristics of DNA Storage
Most works for DNA storage assume a fixed length of short DNA sequences (oligos). Also, due to the limitation of current synthesis technology, the length of oligos is limited from 100 nt to 300 nt [1], [2], [7], [33]. Following these approaches, we also assume the oligos length in 100nt 300 nt throughput this paper.
Fig. 1 shows the overall DNA storage system model. There are three steps between the DNA storage channel and the error-correction decoding: Clustering, alignment, and consensus steps. During these steps, similar reads are clustered and aligned together and one consensus sequence is assigned for each cluster. When a sufficient amount of reads are allowed, most of channel errors in oligo sequences can be corrected by these steps, but it results in a high reading cost. Thus, an error-correcting code with high correction capability should be embedded to reduce the reading cost. We define raw base error rate (rBER) as the base error rate in the oligo sequences processed by the sequencing step and intermediate base error rate (iBER) as the base error rate in the oligo sequences after the clustering, alignment, and consensus steps. Usually, iBER is much lower than rBER because the clustering, alignment, and consensus steps can also correct errors. It is noteworthy that the constrained coding and the error-correction coding can be seen to undergo a combined logical channel consisting of the DNA storage channel and the clustering, alignment, and consensus procedures as shown in Fig. 1. Thus, we evaluate the performance of the proposed coding algorithm under the channel condition with error probability iBER. According to the reports [32] and [34], the range of rBER of the practical DNA storage system is between 0.005 and 0.006 and iBER is assumed to be lower than rBER.
There are three types of errors in DNA storage channel: Substitution, insertion, and deletion. From the extensive experiments [31], [32], and, [33], it is known that the substitution error probability is larger than insertion and deletion probabilities by one order. Also, during sequencing, a small fraction of oligo sequences can disappear, which is called the dropout [2] and it is treated as erasures in terms of decoding. Thus, the coding algorithm for DNA storage should be able to handle the dropout.

III. BIC CODES FOR ERROR-FREE CHANNEL
BIC codes are proposed to break a long homopolymer run by inserting run stopping bits. Before the constrained encoding step, we randomize the input binary sequence by applying XOR operation with a random sequence. Then, we present the encoding and the decoding procedure of the BIC codes.
A. Encoding Algorithm
For the BIC encoding, a dummy bit, called the run-stopping bit (RSB), can be inserted only in predetermined binary positions, which are fixed regardless of the input sequence. We define the predetermined binary positions as the binary runstopping positions (RSPs) and the corresponding quaternary positions mapped from the binary RSPs as the DNA RSPs. For the ith binary sequence Oibit, the binary RSPs are the collection of every 2mth lower bit starting from oli,m+1 and the set is represented by Q = {oil,m+1, oil,2m+1, . . ., oli,βm+1}, where β k/m − 1 . Accordingly, the DNA RSPs are {oi,m+1, oi,2m+1, . . ., oi,βm+1}.
For the BIC encoding, the window of size 2m nt (2m < k) is considered. For the oligo sequence Oi, we consider the wth window Wi,w = (oi,m(w−1)+1, . . ., oi,m(w−1)+2m), for 1 ≤ w ≤ β. The encoding is performed in a sequential manner by sliding the window by m nt for each oligo. After encoding Oi of k nt, we move to Oi+1 for the next encoding. Fig. 3 shows an example of the oligo sequence with the first and second windows for m = 3, where the DNA RSPs are blue-hatched and the binary RSPs are gray-filled. When the DNA base oi,m+1 = o1,4 is changed to satisfy the RL constraint, there is a possibility that the converted base would be the same as the next base. Then, the RL constraint may not be satisfied in the next window. Therefore, the window should slide by m to include oi,m+1 again and decide oi,2m+1 to satisfy the RL constraint.
Fig. 4 shows the encoding procedure of the input binary data to generate the binary sequences Oibit’s. The filled and hatched blocks correspond to the binary RSPs. Specifically, a filled block contains a RSB when the insertion occurs and a hatched block contains the next bit of the input sequence. Starting from the first

Without loss of generality, we consider the first window Wi,1, whose DNA RSP is oi,m+1 to introduce two conditions for the RSB insertion.
(COND. 1) At least m consecutive bases are identical in the set Wi,1 \ {oi,m+1} = {oi,1, . . . , oi,m, oi,m+2, . . . , o2m}.

input binary sequence.

In this case, one can insert a dummy bit both in oi,4 = {oiu,4, oil,4} to satisfy the RL constraint, but it is not efficient because we can store an additional input data in oil,4 if oiu,4 = 1. Therefore, we insert a dummy bit in oil,4 only if (COND. 1) and (COND. 2) both are satisfied.

V. B-BIC CODES WITH LDPC CODES IN ERROR CHANNEL
For practical use of DNA storage, errors caused during the synthesizing and the sequencing procedures are important issues to be addressed. Since the constrained code itself cannot correct errors, the error-correcting code is required and should handle all three types of errors (substitution, insertion, deletion). In this paper, we propose an integrated coding algorithm enabling both constrained coding and error-correction coding by combining B-BIC codes with 5G NR LDPC codes. The integrated coding algorithm can correct substitution errors and erasures caused by dropout and insertion/deletion errors. This section first introduces some characteristics of 5G NR LDPC codes, and then shows the encoding and the decoding algorithms of the proposed algorithm.
A. Characteristic of 5G NR LDPC Code
5G NR LDPC codes are designed to support the rate compatibility as well as a large range of blocklength by various lifting factor Z, shortening, and puncturing [24]. These features are utilized for the integrated scheme shown in Fig. 6. We perform LDPC encoding in the vertical direction (i.e., inter-oligo direction) across the oligos, and the message length of the LDPC codes becomes the number of the oligo sequences η. Since the 5G NR LDPC codes support a wide range of messages length from around 20 to 8448, it can handle the problem that η depends on the input data pattern. Thus, depending on the input data, the user can choose an adequate message length of the code. For example, when the message length is 8,448 and the DNA length is 200 nt, the maximum, average, and minimum data sizes that the proposed algorithm can store are 408.38 KB, 403.23 KB, and 340.31 KB, respectively. Note that error-correction schemes instead of LDPC codes can be concatenated with the B-BIC codes as long as they support variable code lengths.
Another appealing point of the proposed integrated coding algorithm is that the 5G NR LDPC codes in the inter-oligo direction can efficiently handle insertion and deletion errors as

well. If insertion or deletion errors occur in a oligo, the oligo sequence will have a different length from the original. It is assumed to discard the oligo sequences of different lengths and it would incur erasures in the horizontal direction, similar to dropout.
However, for the vertical LDPC codes, these burst errors are regarded as a single erasure error for each codeword and can be corrected by the strong correction capability of LDPC codes. Likewise, the inter-oligo direction coding algorithm allows the proposed algorithm to handle dropout in DNA storage. Even if an oligo sequence is erased entirely by dropout, it results in a single erasure for all vertical codewords, which can be corrected by LDPC codes.

VII. CONCLUSION
In this work, we achieved improvements in both information density and error-correction capability by using B-BIC codes and 5G NR LDPC codes. From the perspective of constrained coding, the proposed B-BIC codes nearly achieved the capacity in terms of information density. To improve error-correcting capability, we employed 5G NR LDPC codes, one of the most powerful error-correcting codes that nearly achieve the capacity. The error-correction encoding is performed in the inter-oligo direction to correct both errors and erasures in DNA storage. Also, since 5G NR LDPC codes are defined in a wide range of block lengths, it is possible to deal with variable error-correction encoding input data lengths resulting from the B-BIC encoder.
In addition, we successfully combined two capacityapproaching coding schemes without additional loss of information density and error-correction performance. As a result, simulation results showed that the proposed algorithm achieved a high information density and a superior error-correction capability, which were not accomplished in previous works. This is expected to make a significant contribution to the design of a practical DNA storage system.
