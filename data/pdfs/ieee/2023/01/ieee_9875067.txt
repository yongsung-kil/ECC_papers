Low Computational-Complexity SOMS-Algorithm and High-Throughput Decoder Architecture for QC-LDPC Codes
Anuj Verma , Graduate Student Member, IEEE, and Rahul Shrestha , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—This article proposes a simplified offset min-sum (SOMS) decoding algorithm for the QC-LDPC codes. It is an implementation-friendly algorithm based on a new logarithmiclikelihood-ratio (LLR) grouping technique that alleviates the computational complexity of the QC-LDPC channel-decoder. This work also presents a parallel and hardware-efficient architecture of the QC-LDPC decoder based on the suggested SOMS algorithm. Additional architectural transformations have been carried out to reduce the routing complexity of the proposed decoder and deliver lower latency and higher throughput. Comprehensive performance analysis of the SOMS algorithm has been presented under various scenarios based on the specifications of the 5G-NR standard. It shows that the suggested SOMS algorithm delivers an adequate FER of 10−5 at SNR of 1.3 dB while decoding 16-QAM modulated QC-LDPC code with the code rate of 1/3 and the code length of 26112 bits. Subsequently, our QC-LDPC decoder has been hardware-implemented on the FPGA platform (Xilinx Zynq-Ultrascale+ board) that operates at the maximum clock frequency of 128.36 MHz. It can be reconfigured to support seven different 5G-NR code lengths and code rates that range between 10368−26112 bits and 1/3−8/9, respectively. This FPGA implementation of the proposed QC-LDPC decoder delivers a peak throughput of 13.3 Gbps and latency of 0.77 µs while decoding with 10 iterations. On comparing this work with the reported implementations, the proposed decoder has shown 7.5× higher throughput and 34% better hardware efficiency than the stateof-the-art implementations.
Index Terms—Digital architectures, field-programmable gatearray (FPGA), 5G new-radio (5G-NR), layered scheduling, offset min-sum decoding algorithm, quasi-cyclic low-density paritycheck (QC-LDPC) code.

The latest technological development in the telecommunication sector is the 5G-NR technology that is envisioned to rendering enhance connectivity to users and materialize the digitization of diverse industrial verticals. In recent release-16 of 5G-NR standardization of the physical layer, the third-generation partnership-project (3GPP) has advocated LDPC code as a standard channel code for data transmission [7]. Such standardization must be compliant with the requirements of international mobile telecommunications (IMT)-2020 set by international telecommunication union (ITU) specifications which are profoundly advanced than the IMT-Advanced specifications of 4 G technology [8]. These high-end 5G-NR specifications are distributed over three use cases: enhanced mobile broadband (eMBB) that addresses data-driven cases of accessing multimedia content, massive machine type communication (mMTC) which is characterized by a large volume of connected devices (like NB-IoT applications) with low cost plus longer battery life, and ultra-reliable low-latency communications (URLLC) that target the delivery of critical communication [9]. To support these high-end cases, the 5G-NR standard has a specified peak data-rate of 10–20 Gbps, latency ≤1 ms, spectral efficiency of 15–30 bits/s/Hz, connection density of 106 devices/km2, bandwidth of 100 MHz, etc [10], [11], [12]. Therefore, all the hardware blocks of analog-RF frontend and digital baseband in the 5G-NR physical layer must be designed efficiently to match the aforementioned specifications of high-throughput (for eMBB), enhanced hardware/spectral-efficiency (for mMTC), and low latency (for URLLC). It is a known fact that channel decoding at the digital baseband module of receiver is an extremely hardware as well as power-hungry process that incurs very high latency. From a broader perspective, key challenge lies in the design of high-throughput LDPC decoder architecture with enhanced hardware-efficiency and lower latency. Our work addresses this issue from algorithmic and architectural aspects for the offset min-sum QC-LDPC decoding based on layered scheduling.
There are various decoding algorithms for LDPC codes such as the improved sum-product (SP) algorithm which has lower computational complexity than the SP and the fast Fourier transform-based SP algorithms [13]. Further, the secondminimum approximation min-sum (SAMS) algorithm [14] incurs lower complexity at the level of check-node to variablenode message computations, at the cost of coding loss. Subsequently, the architectural design of the LDPC decoder is classified based on the degree of parallel data-streaming viz. fully-parallel decoder architecture with complex routing network that delivers higher throughput [19], partially-parallel decoder design targets hardware/energy efficient delivering moderate throughput [20], and serial decoder architecture for lower throughput applications where higher latency is tolerable [21]. On the other side, the performance and routing complexity of the LDPC decoder also depend on two scheduling techniques: layered and flooding [23], [24]. A hybrid-NMS algorithm based on layered scheduling, proposed by Zhao et al. [25], delivered double convergence rate in comparison with the flooding schedule based decoding algorithm. On the one side, the LDPC decoder based on the flooding schedule delivers degraded performance, higher throughput, and lower latency at the cost of huge switching complexity [24], [27]. On the other side, a layered schedule based LDPC decoder delivers excellent performance, medium-throughput, moderate routing complexity, and longer latency. However, this routing complexity escalates while decoding the LDPC code with higher code lengths and multiple code rates, which exacerbates hardwareefficiency and throughput of LDPC decoder.
Therefore, one of the major research problems in the design of a high-throughput LDPC decoder is the proliferation of decoding complexity that alleviates its hardware efficiency, throughput, and memory efficiency. There are contributions in the literature that attempt to address such complexity of data-routing like the Euclidian geometry-based technique which is suitable for low-throughput decoder architecture with profound memory requirements [28]. Similarly, the split-row threshold method consumes extra clock-cycles for updating check and variable nodes [29], another Q-based decoder architecture has been proposed for the communication standard like IEEE 802.15.3c [30]. In addition, the path-unrolling method [31] is arduous to design a hardwired-permutational network for higher expansion factor. Such ramification further escalates while decoding LDPC code for large code lengths at multiple code rates [22], as specified by the contemporary 5G-NR standards [7]. These adverse effects have been addressed in our work by proposing a hardware-friendly QC-LDPC decoding algorithm (with layered schedule) that adopts a new simplified technique of grouping and processing the received channel LLRs. This algorithm synchronizes and simplifies the computations carried out by check and variable nodes. On the other hand, it is challenging to design high-throughput LDPC decoder architecture, compliant with the high-end specifications of the 5G-NR wireless standards. Since its achievable throughput is the ratio of code length and latency, higher code length and lower latency are the most preferable factors for the design of high-throughput decoder architecture. Therefore, this work employs a high parallelism methodology to achieve lower latency and high throughput QC-LDPC decoder architecture. However, such methodology surges the hardware consumption; nevertheless, the proposed SOMS algorithm

with lower computational complexity alleviates the hardware consumption. Hence, it results a new QC-LDPC decoder architecture that is hardware efficient and delivers higher throughput. Highlights of our contributions are enumerated as follows.
1) A hardware-friendly simplified offset min-sum (SOMS) decoding algorithm with layered scheduling has been proposed to decode QC-LDPC code. This algorithm alleviates the computational complexity of LDPC decoder. In addition, it renders faster accessibility for the data processing of LLRs between CNs and VNs at higher code lengths and multiple code rates.
2) Various techniques have been incorporated into the proposed LDPC decoding algorithm viz. memory selection technique (MST), cyclic rotational technique (CRT), and memory writing technique (MWT). They primarily mitigate the problem of data overcrowding while performing the selection of LLRs from the memory bank and storing updated LLRs back into the memory.
3) Corresponding to the proposed SOMS algorithm, a new architectures of the QC-LDPC decoder and its submodules are presented in this paper. These architectures are designed based on the IMT-2020 physical-layer specifications of the 5G-NR standard. Additional optimizations of these architectures have been carried out to reduce the routing complexity, lower the decoding latency, and enhance the throughput of the proposed QC-LDPC decoder.
4) Comprehensive hardware utilization and frame-error-rate (FER) performance analysis of the proposed decoder architecture and decoding algorithm, respectively, are presented in this paper. Consecutively, hardware implementation of our decoder architecture has been performed in the FPGA platform and its results are compared with the relevant state-of-the-art implementations. Eventually, real-world hardware testing for functional verification of the proposed LDPC-decoder FPGA-prototype has been carried out.
II. PRELIMINARIES
QC-LDPC codes are the linear block codes constructed using sparse parity-check-matrix H ∈ C(n−k)×n where k represents the number of information bits, n denotes the code length, and k/n is the code rate. Such H matrix is an expanded version of base-graph matrix B where its every element bi,j ∈ Cz×z is a permutational identity or zero matrix with an expansion factor of z. Fundamentally, this base graph matrix B ∈ Cmb×nb proliferates into H ∈ Cm×n parity-check matrix where m = (n − k) = mb×z and n = nb×z[31]. For example, Fig. 1(a) shows the B ∈ C5×4 matrix where each element bi,j is zero/permutational-identity matrix with an expansion factor z=3. Furthermore, it schematically shows the expanded version of the second row from this B matrix for better clarity. On the other side, the decoding algorithm for such QC-LDPC code is based on the principle of belief propagation that iteratively processes the received noisy channel LLRs which are represented by the L vector that comprises such digitally quantized channel-LLRs. Subsequently, computational complexity analysis of the proposed SOMS decoding algorithm is also discussed here.

B. Computational Complexity Analysis
Here, the computation complexity of the proposed SOMS decoding algorithm has been estimated and compared with the computational complexities of other QC-LDPC decoding algorithms. Assume that the average CN and VN degrees of parity-check-matrix H are represented by dc and dv, respectively. Computational complexity and memory requirements of the various QC-LDPC decoding algorithms are quantified in Table I. IV. ARCHITECTURAL CONTRIBUTIONS
This section proposes a high-throughput QC-LDPC decoder architecture based on the suggested SOMS decoding algorithm. In this paper, designs of various decoder submodules are first presented and they are eventually aggregated into an overall QCLDPC decoder architecture. Note that the design specifications of the proposed decoder architecture are compliant to the 5G-NR wireless-communication standard [7]. Standardization of QCLDPC codes for 5G-NR is primarily based on two prototype base-graph (BG) matrices: BG1 and BG2. The expansion factor z for BG matrices has been quantified based on z = a×2j where a ∈ {2, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, 15} and 0 ≤ j ≤ 7. Thus, peak value of z is 384 with a = 3 and j = 7, based on 5G-NR specification [7], [32]. Therefore, maximum information bits supported by BG1 matrix is 22 × 384 = 8448 b (i.e. k = 8448 b) with the code rates (r) ranging between 1/3−8/9 [32]. Since the initial two columns of these BG matrices are extremely congested, they are punctured while encoding and are assigned zero value while decoding [7]. Such puncturing is performed on parity bits to support various code rates of the 5G-NR standard. Therefore, the proposed QC-LDPC decoder architecture has been designed to decode the received channel LLRs corresponding to the maximum value of code length, which is n = 68 × 384 = 26112 b (with 8448 information-bits) that is compliant to the BG1 matrix and supports all the standard code rates and other code lengths of 5G-NR specification.
A. Initialization and Memory Updating Unit
The quantized channel-LLRs L = {l1, l2, l3 · · · ln} are first processed by a submodule, referred as initialization & memory updating unit (IMUU), of the proposed QC-LDPC decoder. The suggested IMUU architecture has been presented in Fig. 4. It accomplishes two subtasks: initialization (presented in lines 3−8 of Algorithm 1) and AP-LLR matrix updation (referring line 35 in Algorithm 1 & Algorithm 4) of the proposed flow-diagram from Fig. 2. In IMUU architecture, 7-bit quantized-LLRs li ∀ i ∈ n are foremost fed to 1:384 de-multiplexer (DeMUX1) whose outputs are buffered by registers (REGs) for 384 clock cycles, as shown in Fig. 4. Subsequently, 384 LLRs at the outputs of REGs are concatenated (‘:’) and denoted by Lc = {l1 : l2 : l3 · · · l384} that has the bit width of 2688 b (i.e. 384 × 7-bit). These processes of buffering and concatenation for 384 clock-cycles repeat 68× in IMUU architecture to obtain initialized A matrix.
The buffered DeMUX1 output Lc is transferred to the dataselection multiplexer-network (DSMN) that decides whether to perform initialization or AP-LLR matrix updation subtask. Here, REGs at the DeMUX1-outputs sequentially deliver 68 Lc

Each of these 2:1 multiplexers is fed with Lc and updated AP-LLRs a(i) (for ith 2:1 multiplexer) values, as shown in Fig. 4. Here, every ith multiplexer of DSMN routes either Lc or a(i) to perform initialization or AP-LLR matrix updation, respectively. E. Overall QC-LDPC Decoder Architecture
The proposed QC-LDPC decoder architecture that aggregates aforementioned submodules (i.e. IMUU, MSESM, VCUM and ACM) with a hard decision register memory (HDRM) is shown in Fig. 8. The decoding process initiates by feeding 7-bit channel LLRs li ∀ i = {1, 2, 3· · ·n} to IMUU where n can range upto a maximum value of 26112 b, based on the 5G-NR specification [7], [32]. Here, IMUU performs initialization and AP-LLR matrix updation to generate these stored LLRs in concatenated form of A(j). These A(j) LLRs and a 12288-bit CE-LLR from VCUM are subsequently processed by MSESM that performs LLRs selection (using MST from Algorithm 2) and updates e-LLR matrix of flow-diagram from Fig. 2. Further, MSESM computes S(j) and e(j) LLRs ∀ j = {1, 2, 3· · ·19}. Such LLRs are routed by VCUM to perform VN-matrix updation, cyclicrotation from (3), and CN-matrix updation to generate V (j), C(j), and a CE-LLR, as shown in Fig. 8. These C(j) and V (j) LLRs from VCUM are processed by ACM to compute a(j) AP-LLRs which are fed back to IMUU for performing the AP-LLR matrix updation using MWT −referring Algorithm 4− that is corresponding to rth layer-processing of B matrix, as shown in Fig. 8. Such layer-processing consumes two clock cycles in IMUU for rth B matrix layer-processing. This updated memory of IMUU is used in the next (r + 1)th layer-processing of B matrix and such layer-processing continues till the last layer of BG matrix which is computed in single decoding iteration.

Therefore, an overall decoder architecture is designed based on the high parallelism property of LDPC codes and also achieves the lower routing complexity or data congestion, as discussed in the aforementioned submodules.
Timing schedule of the overall QC-LDPC decoder architecture has been presented in Fig. 9. Initially, LLRs are fed into IMUU by asserting high value to LLR-writing signal which takes n clock cycles to load the LLRs. Thereafter, LLR-reading signal is asserted high to provide the LLRs which are corresponding to an ongoing BG matrix layer. Consecutively, VN updation, CN updation, and AP-LLR updation processes are started that correspond to various layers of BG matrix, as shown in Fig. 9. These updation processes follow an overlapping message scheme [42] to reduce the latency of decoder architecture. After the computations of AP-LLRs, they are written back to IMUU by triggering high the LLR-writing signal, as illustrated in Fig. 9. Furthermore, the aforementioned processing continues till the last mb layer of BG matrix, and this marks the end of a single decoding iteration. Such processes are repeatedly carried out for 10 decoding iterations. The proposed decoder architecture operates on 46 processing layers for a 1/3 code rate that consumes 92 clock cycles (i.e. 46 × 2 clock cycles) for a single iteration. Therefore, it requires 920 clock cycles (i.e. 92 × 10 clock cycles) to complete 10 iterations. After the 10th iteration, HDRM is activated and stores all the sign-bits of updated AP-LLRs that are corresponding to A(j) LLRs in the MRB of IMUU, as shown in Fig. 4 and 8. However, the LDPC decoder must generate 8448 decoded bits corresponding to information bits, compliant to 5G-NR specifications. Hence, only 8448 LLRs are encapsulated by an initial 22 A(j) LLRs of IMUU to generate the decoded information bits. Therefore, 384 MSBs (corresponding to 384 7-bit updated AP-LLRs) from each A(j) LLR are tapped as a word of 384 bits which is stored in HDRM, as depicted in Fig. 8. It also shows that 22 such 384 b words are tapped and concatenated from A(j) LLRs and stored in HDRM, which has a size of 22×384 bit (i.e. equivalent to 8448 decoded bits). Eventually, these decoded bits from HDRM are fetched out sequentially in every clock cycle.

A. Performance Analysis
This work presents comprehensive performance analysis of the proposed SOMS decoding algorithm from Algorithm 1 based on the specifications that are compliant to the 5G-NR standard [7], as discussed in section IV. For each signal to noise ratio (SNR) value, an extensive Monte Carlo simulation has been performed to decode ≈ 106 transmitted bit, using a binary phase shift keying (BPSK) modulation scheme, via additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel environment. The FER performance analysis of the proposed SOMS algorithm for different bit quantization of input LLRs have been shown in Fig. 10(a). These multiple plots from fixed point simulations are obtained for the bit width range of 5−13 bit while decoding at minimum, moderate and maximum code rates of 1/3, 3/4, and 8/9, respectively, for 10 decoding iterations. In Fig. 10(a), the FER performance of our decoding algorithm gradually improves with the increasing bit width values. However, such up scaling of bit width adversely increases hardware requirement and power consumption of the proposed decoder architecture. Nevertheless, our decoding algorithm with 7-bit quantization delivers an adequate FER of 10−5 at 0.9 dB of SNR for a code rate of 1/3, as shown in Fig. 10(a). Furthermore, Fig. 10(b) shows the comparative FER performance analysis between proposed and conventional [19] QC-LDPC decoding algorithms for all the code rates of the 5G-NR standard. These code rates are ranging between 1/3 to 8/9 and are compliant to the BG1 base matrix of 5G-NR specification [7]. In Fig. 10(b), these FER plots of different code rates 1/3, 2/5, 1/2, 2/3, 3/4, 5/6, and 8/9 are obtained for the 5G-NR specified maximum code lengths (in bits) of 26112, 21888, 17664, 13440, 12288, 11136, and 10368, respectively, for an expansion factor of z = 384. In comparison with the conventional algorithm [19], the proposed SOMS decoding algorithm delivers a minimum coding loss of 0.025 dB and maximum coding loss of 0.34 at 10−4 FER for 1/3 and 8/9 code rates, respectively. The proposed SOMS decoding algorithm is hardware friendly in nature that incurs

lower computational complexity with minor coding loss and it consequently enables the design of hardware efficient architecture for LDPC decoder.

B. Implementation Results and Comparisons The suggested QC-LDPC decoder architecture has been syn-
thesized, post-route simulated, and hardware prototyped in the FPGA platform of the Xilinx Zynq-Ultrascale+ board. This prototype is capable of decoding a maximum code length of 26112 bits with a mother code rate of 1/3 and also supports all

other code lengths and code rates (specified by the 5G-NR standard [7]) with the aid of global controller, as shown in Fig. 8. This proposed QC-LDPC decoder achieves a maximum throughput (ΘT ), expressed as ΘT = (n × max)/(I × LHb × C), of 13.3 Gbps while decoding a code with n = 10368 bits, LHb = 5 base matrix layers at a code rate of 8/9. On the other side, ΘT = 3.64 Gbps of minimum throughput is obtained by this design for n = 26112 bits, LHb = 46 base matrix layers at a code rate of 1/3. Here, I represents the number of decoding iterations (I = 10 iterations in this work), and C denotes the number of clock cycles consumed while processing each base matrix layer (C = 2 clock cycles, as discussed earlier in section IV-E). Our proposed decoder architecture achieves the high-throughput due to its higher code length (n for 5G-NR) and lower latency which is achieved by the balance amount of parallelism technique. However, due to such parallelism, hardware consumption tends to increase. Nevertheless, this adverse effect has been calibrated with the aid of our lower computational complexity SOMS decoding algorithm that results in hardware-efficient decoder architecture. Thus, our QC-LDPC decoder achieves higher throughput with lower hardware consumption.
Static timing analysis indicates that the suggested decoder operates at a maximum clock frequency of max = 128.36 MHz. Further, it has shortest and longest latencies (computed as ΛD = n/ΘT ) of 0.77 μs at 8/9 code rate and 7.17 μs at 1/3 code rate, respectively. Apart from these combinations of code lengths and code rates, there are other such combinations specified by the 5G-NR standard [32] and are also supported by our decoder architecture. For these pairs of code lengths and code rates, Fig. 13(b) and (c) present throughput and decoding-latency values achieved by the proposed LDPC decoder, respectively.

VI. CONCLUSION
This paper presented a new SOMS decoding algorithm with lower computational complexity. Based on it, we presented hardware architecture of the QC-LDPC decoder with additional architectural transformations for parallel processing and lower routing complexity. These contributions resulted in highthroughput decoder architecture and inherent algorithmic qualities reduced its hardware requirement. Thus, these hybrid contributions in algorithmic and architectural domains resulted in new hardware-efficient and high-throughput QC-LDPC decoder for contemporary wireless communication systems. Comprehensive performance analysis and hardware implementation results have proven that our design performs in-par and better than the state-of-the-art algorithms and implementations, respectively. Therefore, the proposed QC-LDPC decoding algorithm and decoder architecture are significant for the deployment of recent and future wireless-communication systems like 5G-NR and 6 G technologies.
