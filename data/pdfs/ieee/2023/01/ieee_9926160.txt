An Asymmetric-Error-Aware LDPC Decoding Algorithm for DNA Storage
Yi Sun , Guojun Han , Senior Member, IEEE, Chang Liu , Yixin Wang , and Yi Fang , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract— With the rapid growth of data, DNA is attracting attention as a promising storage medium due to its durability, large storage capacity, and high bulk density. However, errors occur in DNA synthesis and sequencing processes, with substitutions being the most common errors in DNA storage process. To address this issue, we select low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes as the error correction method to guarantee data reliability. However, unlike traditional data storage channels, errors occurring between nucleotides in the DNA channel are asymmetric. Therefore, traditional belief propagation (BP) decoding algorithm is not sufficient for effective error correction under DNA channel. In this letter, we propose to use two LDPC codes and take error types as additional information for calibrating log-likelihood ratio (LLR) value. In addition, compared with previous works, simulation results show that our proposed decoding algorithm can reduce bit error rate (BER) by 33% ∼ 94%.
Index Terms— DNA data storage, asymmetric substitutions, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, belief-propagation (BP) decoding, bit error rate reduction.
I. INTRODUCTION
W ITH the continuous upgrading of information technology and Internet applications, we have entered an era of explosive data growth. To alleviate the growing gap between explosive data production and current storage capability, the demand of new storage medium has become increasingly urgent. DNA data storage appears as a promising solution that benefits from highly increased density and durability [1]. DNA is a molecule that uses four nitrogenous bases (i.e., A, C, G, and T), and has extremely high storage density [2]. Attracted by these superiorities, several proof-of-principle DNA storage schemes have been implemented [3].
In DNA storage, binary data can be synthesized into DNA sequences for data storage and then can be read using sequencing technologies [4]. DNA-based storage systems remain very sensitive to errors introduced during synthesis

and sequencing [5]. These two operations introduce different error types such as substitutions, insertions and deletions, with substitutions as the most common type of errors [6]. Therefore, in this letter we mainly focus on the substitution errors that occur in the DNA sequencing process.
In order to design efficient error-correction solutions for DNA storage, classic error-correction channel codes such as fountain codes [2], Bose–Chaudhuri–Hocquenghem (BCH) codes [4] and Reed-Solomon (RS) codes [7] have been employed. To further improve the error-correcting capability, low-density parity-check (LDPC) code has been considered to approach the channel capacity [8]. Lu et al. proposed a log-likelihood ratio (LLR) processing scheme based on observed statistics to reduce the reading overhead of LDPC decoding in DNA storage channel [9]. Fei et al. proposed a turbo-like decoding algorithm, referred to as extrinsicinformation-assisted belief-propagation (EBP) decoding algorithm, for a binary LDPC code in a DNA storage channel [10]. This decoder can correct the substitution errors caused by Nanopore sequencing. However, channel information is not fully utilized in the decoding process.
In this work, we present a modified BP decoding algorithm, referred to as asymmetric-error-aware BP (ABP) decoding algorithm, for error correction over the DNA data storage channel. It is known that the decoding performance closely depends on the accuracy of the estimated LLR value, which is iteratively updated in the decoding process. We propose to use two binary LDPC codes for encoding/decoding one quaternary DNA sequence. Furthermore, the extrinsic information are used as additional information to calibrate the LLR value during the decoding process to significantly enhance the decoding performance. In the process, the extrinsic information of the two LDPC codes is exchanged to optimize the LLR value, so that the LLR value can be more accurate to enhance the decoding performance.
The remainder of this letter is organized as follows. Section II introduces the DNA channel model, conventional LDPC decoding and the corresponding LLR calculation. Section III describes the proposed algorithm in details. Section IV presents simulation results and discussions, and the conclusion is presented in Section V.

Here ζ (0 < ζ < 1) is a scaling coefficient which reduces the magnitudes of the LLR informations, because they would be amplified over time. We propose to use a decoding algorithm based on turbo code decoding method [15]. The first LDPC code uses the updated channel information as shown in eq. (15). The extrinsic information of the first LDPC decoding output is used as prior information for the second LDPC decoding, and the transfer of extrinsic information between two decoders forms a circular iterative structure.
As shown in Fig. 1(a), there is a much larger overlap area between symbols T and C. It means that if the received symbols T and C are erroneous, their corresponding transmitted symbols xj have a high probability of being either C or T. Therefore when the received symbol is T or C, if it is different from the estimated symbol, we only consider one error case, which is the transition between T and C. Symbols A and G do not have this characteristic and the transition probability between them is close to 0, so we consider all error cases when the received symbol is A or G. To further improve the algorithm, when the transition probability of the received symbol yj and the estimated symbol xj is close to 0 which only happens between A and G, the auxiliary information is calculated according to the estimated bits xji (i = 1, 2) of another LDPC code.
More specifically, when the received symbol yj is A = (0, 0), if P 1(0) < P 1(1) and P 2(0) < P 2(1), we do not add auxiliary information GCh2j→1,iter to the channel information Ch1j of the first LDPC code. But according to x2j , we determine the corresponding auxiliary information to add. In more detail, CChj2→1,iter is added to the channel information Chj1 of the first LDPC code, because the estimated bit xj2 of the second LDPC code is 1, and the probability that the transmitted symbol is C = (0, 1) is much larger than the probability of G = (1, 1). Similarly, T Ch1j→2,iter is added to the Ch2j of the second LDPC code. The details of the proposed decoding algorithm are provided in Algorithm 1.

IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
A. Comparison With [10]
We apply the algorithms to a rate-0.5 (500,1000) columnweight-3 LDPC code [4]. The maximum number of iteration in the BP decoding is set to 100 [10], and we choose the optimal value of scaling coefficients ζ = 0.5 according to our real world experimental results in the proposed algorithm.
Furthermore, we compare the performances of our proposed LDPC decoding algorithm with strategies in [10]. In Fig. 2, Baseline represents BER performance of the BP decoding algorithm without any improvements. The EBP3 decoding algorithm has the optimal BER performance in [10]. The proposed algorithm is inspired by Algorithm 2 in [10] and has further improvement. Therefore, for convenience of comparison, the Algorithm 2 in [10], considering two error types (T and C), is referred to as the EBP2-TC decoding algorithm. Then we use the same method but take all four error types into consideration, named as the EBP2-ATCG decoding algorithm. On the other hand, when the proposed algorithm considers two error types (T and C) and all error types (A, T, C and G), they are named as ABP-TC and ABP-ATCG, respectively.
We observe that, compared with the EBP2-TC decoding algorithm, at α = 0.03 the ABP-TC decoding algorithm and the ABP-ATCG decoding algorithm can reduce the BER by about 69% and 94%, respectively. Although the EBP3 decoding algorithm is optimal at [10], compared with it, the ABP-ATCG decoding algorithm can still reduce the BER by about 91% at α = 0.03.

C. Discussions
It can be observed in Fig. 2 and Fig. 3 that compared with two error types during decoding when considering all error types, it is not effective for Algorithm 2 in [10]. But can improve the BER performance of the proposed algorithm. There are two reasons for this. One is that from eq. (14), it can be observed that the calculation of auxiliary information is closely related to the channel transition probability. When error occurs, symbols T and C are likely to be transformed to each other with high probability, so the calculated auxiliary information drives the decision to one of these two symbols. When symbol A or G is wrong, the probability of transition to T or C is about 50% (i.e., the entropy of the symbol is close to the maximum), which makes the auxiliary information obtained by eq. (13) unreliable. The other reason is that our proposed algorithm can dynamically estimate the transmitted symbols xj during decoding iterations and then calculate the auxiliary information, while Algorithm 2 in [10] uses the unchanging received symbol yj to calculate. Therefore, compared with it, the proposed algorithm achieves a lower BER when considering all error types.
Moreover, the difference between the proposed algorithm and Algorithm 2 in [10] mainly lies in the message passing mechanism between the two codes. For each iteration, the proposed algorithm has 2n comparison operations more than those in Algorithm 2 in [10], where n is the length of the codeword. On the other hand, it has (1 − )8n multiplication operations for employing auxiliary information and (1 − )6n addition operations less than Algorithm 2 in [10], where is the average symbol error probability. V. CONCLUSION
In conclusion, we have proposed a BP decoding algorithm utilizing the extrinsic information exchange, which can tackle
the asymmetric substitution errors in the sequencing process of a DNA storage system. Specifically, by utilizing the depen-
dency of two-bit errors in nucleotide symbols combined with
the error types, different auxiliary information are added to optimize the LLR values. The simulation results show that
the proposed algorithm significantly improves the decoding
performance. In practice, the Nanopore sequencing is prone not only to substitution errors, but also to insertion and deletion
errors. Therefore, in the DNA storage system, the decoding performance can be further improved by correction of these
two errors. Inspired by this, a more generalized channel model
including all these three errors would be developed in our future work.
