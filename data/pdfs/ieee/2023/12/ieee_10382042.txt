Abstract—This paper studies machine learning (ML) structure for box-plus operation as an element of future error correction based on artificial intelligence (AI) using soft information processing. Box-plus operations are important components for check-node (CND) of error correction codes based on bipartite graphs. We propose an ML structure for box-plus operation and observe the behavior of the structure by investigating all possible structures constructed from 6 neurons in the hidden layer. We utilize activation function: (i) hyperbolic tangent (tanh) function for the hidden layer since it maps any real-valued input to the 1 to 1 range for soft information, and (ii) linear function only at the output layer. We evaluate the performances of the proposed structure using a series of computer simulations. We found that a single hidden layer with many neurons is better than multiple hidden layers with less number of neurons, because the box-plus operation is not too complex. We expect that these results open new ground for the development of future error correction codes based on ML.
Index Terms—soft information, ML, box-plus operation
I. INTRODUCTION
To fulfill the challenges of the rapid increase of realtime and traffic service needs which cannot be addressed by the current fifth telecommunication generation (5G), the 5G-Advanced and 6G generation need to elaborate and exploit new technologies from other fields. Artificial intelligence (AI) within the realm of communication society has witnessed a surge in recent times [1]–[3]. The 6G framework has decided to use the terminology of ”integrated intelligence and communication of which the form can be in the connected intelligence” [4]. Simultaneously, AI became standard for 5G-Advanced in the 3rd Generation Partnership Project (3GPP) on Release 18 [5].
Error correction codes (ECC) have found extensive application in communication systems to facilitate data transmission across noisy or unreliable channels. After the pioneering work by Shannon [6], academia put efforts to develop ECC and their corresponding decoding algorithms to attain or come close to the channel capacity. Developing codes that can reliably transmit information at rates close to the capacity with feasible decoding complexity remains as an active research challenge.

The utilization of soft-in/soft-out decoders that interchange extrinsic information in iterative decoding approaches has led to a notable enhancement in system performance [7]. The conceptualization of soft-in/soft-out decoders as digital filters led Berrou et al. to pioneer the turbo decoding system [8] and influence developments in this field [9]. An iterative soft decision decoding method for linear binary block codes was developed by Lucas [10]. Gallager had already developed an efficient iterative decoding method for his low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes several decades ago [11]. The utilization of Tanner graphs [12] in conjunction with an interleaver is employed to provide a comprehensive understanding of the process involved in parallel and serial concatenated turbo decoding. Other researchers have utilized alternative graphical models, such as Bayesian networks, to describe the iterative decoding technique as a belief propagation algorithm. A graphical model named ”factor graphs” has been put forth, which encompasses both Tanner graphs and Bayesian networks [13].
LDPC codes have been adopted in several recent digital communications standards [14], [15]. The process of iterative decoding in LDPC codes involves the exchange of messages between variable nodes and check-nodes, which produce soft reliability information in the form of loglikelihood ratios (LLRs), which is the logarithm of the ratio of probability P (X = 0) and P (X = 1). The LLRs denote the probability that a bit is 0 or 1 given the received channel values. At the variable nodes, the LLR messages from the channel and neighboring check-node (CND) are combined or summed. Then at the check-node the LLR input is multiplied or product-ed, where the box-plus operation is

The boxplus operation is a key computation in LDPC decoders that ’softens’ the parity check constraints to account for uncertainty in the bit values. Although new coding schemes for 6G are yet to be established, the principle of iterative decoding with sum-product algorithms (SPA) such as LDPC codes is expected to remain as the main investigation. Since AI is one of the main use cases in 5G-Advanced and 6G, we are searching for further development AI for soft information processing for future iterative decoding schemes.
The primary objective of this paper is to study the behavior of neural networks for box-plus operation and propose the most suitable deep learning structure to process soft information for the box-plus operation. By exploring different network structures on 6 neurons, we seek to determine the best structure for solving the problem of the box-plus function using neural networks. In this paper, we present learning for box-plus operations employing soft values as shown in Fig. 2.
A. Related Work
Previous studies have examined several machine learning methods for decoding algorithms [16]–[18], but there has been a lack of emphasis on modeling the boxplus operation specifically. Although there has been significant progress in optimizing neural network weights, there is currently limited study on customizing the structure of neural networks. The objective of this study is to provide a novel perspective on the impact of neural architecture decisions on the performance of box-plus learning models. Our research provides original and detailed analysis of the suitable neural structures for modeling the boxplus operator. While previous studies have attempted to directly approximate the box-plus function, our approach involves utilizing machine learning techniques to acquire representations of this intricate transformation. This study offers a novel approach to integrating coding theory with machine learning by investigating structure neural networks for box-plus operation.

III. THE PROPOSED NEURAL NETWORK STRUCTURE FOR BOX-PLUS OPERATION
In this paper, we utilize a supervised learning method to deal with box-plus operations as shown in Fig. 3. We use framework comprised of two key components: code constructor and code evaluator based on Fig. 2. The code constructor continually acquires knowledge of valid code structures by incorporating performance feedback from the code evaluator. The code evaluator offers precise performance metric assessments for code structures in the context of the specific L-values of box-plus operation. The code structure undergoes continuous enhancement through iterative collaboration between the constructor and evaluator until the performance metric reaches convergence.

The tanh activation function is utilized for the hidden layers in the proposed machine learning model, with the rationale that this approach is well-suited for soft-decoding applications using the L-values. Specifically, the tanh nonlinearity is preferred over alternative activation functions for this particular purpose because it maps any real-valued input to the 1 to +1 range, which corresponds well to probabilities associated with the soft L-values.
Fig. 4 shows the influence of different activation functions on the performance of the machine learning model for the box-plus operation. Based on these results, a tanhlinear configuration of the activation functions is suitable for learning the box-plus operator. The tanh non-linearity in the hidden layers enables effective modeling of the boxplus function, while the linear output activation provides calibrated probability estimates.
In this paper, we also observe the use of bias for boxplus ML. We initially implemented a simple neural network structure only with 2 neurons in the hidden layer. This network was trained on a dataset with mutual information of 0.9. Fig. 5 shows that the neural network model with bias parameters outperforms the one without biases. Given these results highlighting the importance of biases, we incorporate bias terms in all neural network evaluations conducted in this study.

V. CONCLUSION
This paper has studied machine learning structure for boxplus operation using soft information, where all possible ML structures with 6 neurons have been investigated to confirm the best structure. We have utilized the activation functions using tanh and linear functions for the hidden and output layers, respectively. We analyzed the performances of the proposed structure using a series of computer simulations in terms of errors in sign and mean squared error. We found that for 6 neurons, a single hidden layer is better than multiple hidden layers to operate the box-plus function. These elementary results are expected to open the new ground of future error correction codes with soft information based on ML and bipartite graphs.
