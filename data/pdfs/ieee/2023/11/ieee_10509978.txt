A MATE-GDBF Algorithm for Irregular Punctured LDPC Codes and Its Decoder Implementation
Xiao-Juan Huang, Li-Wei Liu, Yen-Chin Liao, Hsie-Chia Chang, and Sau-Gee Chen Institute of Electronics, National Yang Ming Chiao Tung University, Hsinchu, Taiwan Email: x.j.huang1025@gmail.com

Abstract—This paper proposes a MATE-GDBF decoding algorithm and a corresponding decoder architecture for punctured LDPC codes. The proposed algorithm employs majority-vote and tabu-enhancement approaches to improve decoding performance and convergence speed. Additionally, the decoder architecture includes a new flipping access scheme designed to reduce power consumption. The proposed LDPC (9344, 64, 8384) decoder is implemented in TSMC 90nm CMOS process and achieves a 6.39 Gbps throughput at a clock rate of 400 MHz. The decoder’s core area is 0.67 mm2, and its power consumption is 79.6 mW. Overall, the proposed algorithm and decoder architecture offer a promising solution for improving the performance and efficiency of punctured LDPC codes.
Index Terms—low-density parity check (LDPC) code, bitflipping algorithm, punctured LDPC code, erasure decode
I. INTRODUCTION
In the field of NAND flash storage systems, Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes [1] have emerged as the mainstream error correction code due to their excellent performance and distributed computation decoding properties. To achieve reliable performance, high speed, and low power consumption, storage controllers often employ heterogeneous systems comprising min-sum decoders and bit-flipping decoders.
Over the years, Gradient Descent bit-flipping (GDBF) [2] decoders have gained popularity in the field of error correction coding for their low-complexity computation and high throughput design. Various bit-flipping decoding algorithms, including Probability GDBF (PGDBF) [3], Modified GDBF (MGDBF) [4], GDBF with Momentum [5], and UnifiedPenalty GDBF (UP-GDBF) [6], have been proposed to enhance the decoding performance LDPC codes. However, limited research [7] has been conducted on the application of these algorithms to punctured LDPC codes.
Punctured LDPC codes offer significant benefits, particularly in terms of flexible code rates [8], [9], enabling storage systems to adapt to different stages of degrading NAND channel conditions. By utilizing the same LDPC matrix but ignoring the assigned bits during transmission, punctured LDPC codes can improve storage usage efficiency. Despite these benefits, the performance of bit-flipping decoders for punctured LDPC codes received limited attention and required further investigation.

Through this study, we aim to improve the performance of bit-flipping decoding for punctured LDPC codes and develop a low-power decoder architectures. The contribution of this research lies in the proposal and analysis of the MAjority-vote Tabu-Enhancement GDBF (MATE-GDBF) algorithm, specifically designed to address the challenges associated with bitflipping decoding for punctured LDPC codes. Furthermore, we incorporate a queue-based flipping access scheme and flippingvector cache to achieve lower power consumption.
In this paper, we introduce the background and related analysis in section II. The detailed description of the MATEGDBF algorithm and the corresponding hardware architecture are presented in Section III and Section IV, respectively. The implementation results are summarized in Section V. Finally, we conclude our research in Section VI.
II. BACKGROUND This paper focuses on a a 1KB LDPC (9344, 64, 8384) code, where 9344 represents the number of code bits (N), 64 is the number of erasure bits (P), and 8384 is the information length (K). The LDPC code can be visualized as a bipartite graph, with the columns of the matrix corresponding to variable nodes (VNs) and the rows corresponding to check nodes (CNs). In our target code, we utilize 64 nodes (CPM size) as a unit for information exchange. The matrix elements indicate the connections between the two types of nodes, and the number of connections in each column represents the column degree of the LDPC code.

IV. PROPOSED HARDWARE ARCHITECTURE
A. Architecture Overview
Figure 5 illustrates the proposed architecture of the MATEGDBF decoder, which follows a four-stage pipeline design. In Stage0, a read-only memory (ROM) stores memory addresses and barrel shifting coefficients for implementing a shuffle scheduling scheme. Stage1 acts as a data buffer to mitigate timing impacts on the critical path. Stage2 consists of several functional units, including the Erasure Decoding (ED) unit, Energy Computation (EC) unit, and Flipping Decision (FD) unit. In Stage3, the decoder writes the latest data back to memory and updates the syndrome register during the flipping process. To leverage the orthogonal structure of the punctured LDPC code, the proposed decoder employs three clusters of functional units to process three columns of variable nodes as a group. Consequently, the target matrix requires 49 cycles to complete a full iteration.
B. Queue-Based Flipping Access Scheme
The UP-GDBF decoder [6] utilizes a dedicated memory and extra classification logic to establish a link-listed structure for the backtracking flipping procedure. In contrast, our proposed queue-based flipping access scheme only necessitates a dedicated memory and two registers. These registers are responsible for storing a temporary minimum energy value (Global Min) and a writing pointer. Figure 6 provides an illustrative example of the corresponding behavior.

Fig. 6. The behavior of the queue-based backtracking memory
If the temporary minimum of the current group (Group Min) is smaller than the Global Min, the write pointer is reset to zero, and the contents of the queue are overwritten starting from the beginning. If the Group Min is equal to the Global Min, the group index and bitmaps are written to the entries indicated by the incremented pointer. However, if the Group Min is larger than the Global Min, the decoder skips the recording procedure altogether. Since the write pointers always point to the last group index, the proposed decoder can simply decrement the pointer to access the flipping columns.
C. Reuse Tabu-List Memory as Flipping Vector Cache
During the flipping procedure, the previous approach involves accessing the flipping columns and recomputing the energy value for the corresponding flipping vector. In order to reduce power consumption, we propose reusing the original tabu-list memory as a cache (shown in Fig. 7) to store these potential flipping vectors along with a 3-bit column indicator.
In the energy computation phase, the tabu-list memory serves as a repository for the flipping records from the previous iteration. Since the values in the tabu-list entries are no

VI. CONCLUSION
This paper introduces the MAjority-vote Tabu-Enhanced Gradient Descent Bit Flipping (MATE-GDBF) decoding algorithm, which offers one order of performance improvement for adjustable code rates in punctured LDPC codes. The proposed decoder, implemented in the TSMC 90nm process, achieves a high data throughput of 6.39 Gbps at a clock frequency of 400 MHz in the post-layout stage. Remarkably, the erasure decoding module occupies to a 2.3% overhead of the total 0.67 mm2 core area. By employing the queuebased flipping-access scheme and the flipping vector cache, an additional 10% reduction in power consumption is achieved compared to the original architecture. Overall, this research presents a valuable contribution in terms of both algorithmic and hardware improvements, enabling efficient decoding of punctured LDPC codes with adjustable code rates.
