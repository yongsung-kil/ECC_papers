Abstract—A novel class of quasi-cyclic (QC) low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes is explicitly proposed without 4-cycles and 6cycles via the greatest-common-divisor (GCD) framework. From the new codes, a novel type of girth-eight QC-LDPC codes with a dual-diagonal (DD) structure is presented, in which the single undetermined entry in the associated exponent matrix can be determined by simple formulae. The proposed DD codes have similar or better performance compared with DD codes obtained from computer search. Moreover, a lower bound on circulant sizes which guarantees the existence of qualified undetermined entries is also provided.
Index Terms—dual-diagonal, girth, greatest-common-divisor (GCD), low-density parity-check (LDPC) code, quasi-cyclic (QC)
I. INTRODUCTION
It is generally believed that small cycles have a negative impact on the performance of quasi-cyclic (QC) low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. Therefore, constructions of QCLDPC codes without small cycles have attracted great interest due to the advantages of competitive performance and relatively low complexity for codec implementation [1]–[3]. A dualdiagonal (DD) structure [4]–[6] is able to further significantly reduce the complexity of encoders for QC-LDPC codes via direct calculation based on their parity-check matrices. Many methods have been proposed to explicitly (without resorting to computer search) construct QC-LDPC codes without small cycles of length four or lengths up to six (i.e. girth at least eight). Nevertheless, how to explicitly design QC-LDPC codes possessing both girth at least eight and a DD structure is still missing in the literature.
Based on the greatest-common-divisor (GCD) framework [7]–[10], a new construction of girth-eight QC-LDPC codes with large column weight is explicitly proposed in this paper

The novel construction combined with DD structure can produce a class of girth-eight QC-LDPC codes whose exponent matrices are completely determined without using any search procedure. The rest of this paper is structured as follows. Basic definitions and properties of QC-LDPC codes, exponent matrices and the GCD framework are briefly introduced in Section II. In Section III, two types of closely related DD-structures are investigated, and a lower bound on circulant sizes for the second DD-structure is proposed. In Section IV, a new construction of girth-eight QC-LDPC codes is presented under the GCD framework, and the single undetermined entry for the resultant DD-structured codes is explicitly obtained by simple formulae. Performance simulations of the new codes are provided in Section V. Finally, the concluding remark is given in Section VI.
II. PRELIMINARIES
A P × P circulant with weight zero (resp. one) can be defined by the symbol ∞ (resp. an integer e). The symbol ∞ corresponds to a P × P zero matrix. An integer e corresponds to a P × P identity matrix cyclically-shifted to the right by mod(e, P ) positions. All the symbol ∞ and these integers (uniformly called exponents) form an array termed as an exponent matrix. Therefore, for a QC-LDPC code whose paritycheck matrix (PCM) H is composed of P × P circulants with weight of zero or one, the PCM can be uniquely represented by an exponent matrix E and the circulant size P . Thus, the associated Tanner graph of such a QC-LDPC code can be denoted by T g(E, P ). In a Tanner graph, a closed walk without smaller closed walks is called a cycle. Obviously, the length

The GCD framework is an efficient method to produce QCLDPC codes with girth eight. For an increasing sequence of integers, 0 ≤ a0 < a1 < · · · < aJ−1, if each triple [ai, aj, ak] satisfies the condition (ak − ai)/gcd(ak − ai, aj − ai) ≥ L where 0 ≤ i < j < k ≤ J − 1, then the sequence is said to meet the GCD constraint for L [7]. In this situation, the exponent matrix [a0, a1, · · · , aJ−1]T · [0, 1, · · · , L − 1] yields a girth-eight (J, L)-regular QC-LDPC code for any circulant size P ≥ (aJ−1 − a0)(L − 1) + 1 [7].
The following properties regarding the GCD constraint are obvious except for Property 3.
Property 1: Let a be a positive integer and b be a nonnegative integer. Then the triple [0, 1, a · L + b] satisfies the GCD constraint for any L.
Property 2: Let a be a positive integer and b be a nonnegative integer such that a ≥ b. Then the triple [0, L, a · L + b] satisfies the GCD constraint for any L.
Property 3: Let a be a positive integer and b be a nonnegative integer such that 2a ≥ b and a = b. Then the triple [0, L + 1, a · L + b] satisfies the GCD constraint for any L.
Proof : From the conditions 2a ≥ b, a = b, a > 0 and b ≥ 0, it follows that either 2a ≥ b > a > 0 or a > b ≥ 0 holds. (i) First, we consider the case 2a ≥ b > a > 0. As gcd(aL + b, L + 1) = gcd(b − a, L + 1), we have (aL + b)/gcd(aL + b, L + 1) = (aL + b)/gcd(b − a, L + 1) ≥ (aL + b)/(b−a). Because 2a ≥ b, we have a ≥ b−a and hence (aL+ b)/(b − a) > L. Therefore, the GCD constraint is satisfied for any L. (ii) Second, we consider the case a > b ≥ 0. Because gcd(aL + b, L + 1) = gcd(a − b, L + 1), it follows that (aL+b)/gcd(aL+b, L+1) = (aL+b)/gcd(a−b, L+1) ≥ (aL+b)/(a−b). Since a ≥ a−b, we have (aL+b)/(a−b) ≥ L. As a result, the GCD constraint is satisfied for any L.
Property 4: (‘S’-equivalence) The triple [a0, a1, a2] satisfies GCD constraint for L if and only if [0, a1 − a0, a2 − a0] does.
Property 5: (‘R’-equivalence) The triple [0, a1, a2] satisfies GCD constraint for L if and only if [0, a2 − a1, a2] does.

2) A lower bound on P ensuring nontrivial qualified x: Although x = 0 readily ensures girth at least eight, the resultant DD codes often perform far from satisfactory, as illustrated in Section V. A question raises: are there other values of x (rather than 0) which ensure the absence of 4-cycles and 6-cycles? First, let us consider J = 7 and i = (J − 1)/2 = 3. We have the following lemma.
Lemma 1: Let J = 7 and i = 3. Then for a circulant size P greater than P0 = 2(L − 1)(5L − 6), there exist at least P − P0 different qualified values of x.
Proof : (1) 4-cycles: Clearly, there are 2(L − 1) possible 4-cycles related to x.
(2) 6-cycles: As is well known, such cycles can be classified into six different types. Use the letter A ∼ F to denote each type of 6-cycles. Each 6-cycle occurs within a 3×3 sub-matrix of the exponent matrix. Use the notation “Ai” to represent a specific pattern of type A, where the undetermined entry x is located in the (i − 1)/2 -th column of the sub-matrix and in the mod(i − 1, 2)-th point of the vertical line segment appearing in the column in question, where 1 ≤ i ≤ 6. The two types of 6-cycles (A and B) are depicted in Fig. 1 and the rest types (C-F) are shown in Fig. 2.
(type-A): There are three possible patterns: A3, A4 and A6. To be specific, there are L − 1 cases for A3, L − 1 cases for A4 and 3(L − 1)(L − 2)/2 cases for A6.
(type-B): There exist three possible patterns: B3, B4 and B5. Specifically, there are L − 1 cases for B3, L − 1 cases for B4 and 3(L − 1)(L − 2)/2 cases for B5.
(type-C): There are two possible patterns: C5 and C6. To be specific, there are (L−1)(L−2) cases for C5 and (L−1)(L−2) cases for C6.
(type-D): There exist two possible patterns: D4 and D5. Specifically, there are L−1 cases for D4 and 3(L−1)(L−2)/2 cases for D5.
(type-E): There are two possible patterns: E3 and E6. To be specific, there are L − 1 cases for E3 and 3(L − 1)(L − 2)/2 cases for E6.
(type-F): There exist two possible patterns: F5 and F6. Specifically, there are (L − 1)(L − 2) cases for F5 and (L − 1)(L − 2) cases for F6.
Therefore, there are a total of 6(L−1)+10(L−1)(L−2) = (L − 1)(10L − 14) possible governing-equations for 6-cycles. As a result, there are at most P0 = (L − 1)(10L − 12) values of x which lead to cycles with length up to six. Consequently, each of the rest P −P0 values can be selected as x to guarantee the absence of 4-cycles and 6-cycles.

VI. CONCLUSION
A new construction is proposed via GCD framework to produce girth-eight QC-LDPC codes for column weight of seven and any row weight. Based on the novel exponent matrices, a new class of QC-LDPC codes with DD structure is presented, for which the single undetermined entry can be explicitly determined by simple formulae. Numerical simulations show that the proposed girth-eight DD codes defined by formulae are able to perform the same as or even better than the DD counterparts generated by GCD-based random search method.
How to prove Remark 1 and Remark 2 for a general row weight is worthy of further study. Besides, finding girth-eight DD codes with smaller circulant sizes and conducting detailed analysis of the impact of x on the performance also deserve further investigations.
