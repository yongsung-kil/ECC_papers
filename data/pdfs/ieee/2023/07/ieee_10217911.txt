Abstract—For the Belief Propagation (BP) algorithm of lowdensity parity-check (LDPC) codes, existing deep learning methods have a limited performance improvement and it is difficult to train deep-level networks. In this paper, a model-driven quasiresidual network (Quasi-ResNet) BP decoding architecture is proposed for LDPC codes to further improve the performance of standard BP decoding. This method feeds the reliable messages calculated in current iteration into the next iteration based on the shortcut connection, and adjusts the weight of shortcut connection based on the error Back Propagation algorithm of neural network to determine the optimal genetic proportion of reliable messages. The decoding architecture is composed of a model-driven deep neural network (DNN) and shortcut connection. Simulation results show that the decoder can not only unfold more layers quickly compared with the DNN-based BP decoder, but also further improve the decoding performance.
Index Terms—LDPC decoding, neural network, belief propagation, shortcut connection
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) code is an excellent linear block code with high decoding throughput [1] and performance close to Shannon limit. It was first proposed by Gallager in 1962 [2] and has become one of the research hotspots in channel coding technology. Belief propagation (BP) decoding algorithm is a soft decision decoding algorithm based on channel probability information. For LDPC codes with long block length, BP algorithm can achieve decoding performance close to Shannon limit, but for LDPC codes with short and medium code length, conventional BP algorithm obtains poor results.
In recent years, deep learning methods have shown amazing performance in many fields and gradually extended to the field of communication technology. In channel decoding research, neural networks can learn the mapping relationship between received codewords and real transmitted codewords very well, while model-driven neural networks [3] provided guidance for such blind learning based on the introduction of prior knowledge of decoding algorithm. In [4], deep learning was used to improve the BP algorithm for linear codes. The iterative process of BP algorithm was unfolded as a neural

network, and the neural network weights were assigned to the messages transmitted iteratively to “soften” the edges of Tanner graph. The results show that this deep learning method improves the performance of traditional decoding methods of high-density parity check codes by 0.9 dB. Similarly, many deep learning-based methods have been proposed to further improve the performance of traditional decoding algorithms for LDPC codes [5] - [8]. The authors in [5] proposed a BP-CNN decoding architecture by cascading convolutional neural network (CNN) and BP algorithm. Repeated iteration of BP decoder and cascaded CNN can effectively eliminate the channel noise of the input decoder, thereby obtaining performance improvement. In [6], the decoding neural network architecture similar to [4] was applied to 5G ultra-reliable lowlatency communication and also improved the BP decoding performance of short LDPC codes. The selection of the normalization factor in the normalized minimum-sum (NMS) algorithm has a large impact on the decoding performance. Considering that traditional methods usually make selection by virtue of human experience, the authors in [7] used a shallow fully connected neural network to assist the NMS algorithm to learn its normalization factor. In the simulation of BER performance of LDPC codes, the neural network-assisted NMS decoder improves the performance of conventional NMS algorithm by 50% without adding additional complexity. [8] suggested that the iterations of the NMS algorithm are unfolded into non-fully connected neural networks so that modeldriven deep learning methods can be utilized. The authors in [9] proposed two training sample optimization algorithms for improving the robustness of BP neural decoder.
Existing deep learning methods have limited performance improvement and it is difficult to train deep-level networks, namely, the performance of neural network-based LDPC code decoders does not increase as expected when the number of algorithm iterations increases, and it takes more time to train neural networks. Therefore, it is necessary to develop a better decoding neural network that takes into account both decoding delay and decoding performance of neural network. In this paper, based on the principle of residual networks [10], we degrade the convolutional operations on the main path of residual networks to one-dimensional linear operations, and the convolutional operations on the shortcut connections to

II. THE BELIEF PROPAGATION ALGORITHM

Tanner graph is a bipartite graph constructed by parity check matrix of linear code, which describes the information of nonzero elements in parity check matrix. Tanner graph consists of three parts: 1) check nodes (CNs) corresponding to the check equation of LDPC code check matrix, 2) variable nodes (VNs) corresponding to the transmission bits, and 3) the edges connecting check nodes and variable nodes. The messages of BP algorithm are passed through the edges of Tanner graph and then iterated based on flooding (parallel) schedule. For (N, k) LDPC codes, there are N variable nodes and N − k check nodes in the Tanner graph represented by its check matrix. Then, the input layer of BP decoder is a vector of size N , where each component is the log-likelihood ratio (LLR) of the corresponding transmission bit output after passing through the channel, which can be obtained by

III. THE PROPOSED NEURAL NETWORK DECODER
In this section, we present the LDPC neural network decoder with ResNet network structure. We will illustrate how the model-driven quasi-ResNet network based on BP decoding is constructed and provide its training method.

A. Quasi-ResNet LDPC decoding neural network
With the increase of the number of iterations, the reliable information required for decoding may be weakened in the iterative process of BP decoding. One way to strengthen the reliable information is to inherit the historically transmitted messages to some extent, and this idea can be achieved through shortcut connections of residual network. The neural network decoding architecture proposed in this paper is a model-driven quasi-ResNet neural network model, which is composed of feed-forward neural network (FFNN) and shortcut connection, as shown in Fig. 1. The feed-forward neural network consists of an input layer, multiple hidden layers and an output layer. Among them, the input layer is fed with the LLR messages calculated from received signals, which completes the initialization of the algorithm. The hidden layer is composed of selfdefined check node layer (CN-layer) and variable node layer (VN-layer), which is used to implement the iterative update of algorithm. The feedforward neural network is a non-fully connected deep neural network, which is essentially a lattice representation of the Tanner graph. One iteration of BP algorithm is extended to two hidden layers of DNN. In the hidden layers, each neuron in VN-layer represents a variable node in Tanner graph, and each neuron in CN-layer represents a check node in Tanner graph. Each layer of the decoding neural network is unfolded as follows:
• The connection relationship between the ith neuron in output layer and the neurons in penultimate hidden layer (the last CN-layer) is the same as that between the ith VNs and other CNs in Tanner graph.
• The connection relationship between VN-layer neurons and CN-layer neurons in the hidden layer is related to the connection relationship between VNs and CNs in the corresponding Tanner graph, depending on the message scheduling process in decoding algorithm. The input layer acts as an algorithm initialization, which can be regarded as a VN-layer.
• For the neuronal connections between different layers due to ResNet shortcut connection, the connection relationship is the same as that described above according to the symmetry of Tanner graph between different iterations.
For a (N, k) LDPC code, there are N neurons in each of the input layer, VN-layer and output layer of its deep unfolding neural network, and N − k neurons in each of the CN-layer. If the maximum number of iterations of the algorithm is T , the neural network has 2T + 2 layers, including an input layer, an output layer, T variable node layers, and T check node layers.

C. Decoding Block Diagram and Network Training
Based on our proposed neural network decoding architecture, we propose to construct the system transmission model for communication as shown in Fig. 2. The system transmitter uses (N, K) LDPC codes for information transmission, and k bits are encoded in groups to obtain N bits of LDPC codes. Further, the binary message sequence x is encoded to obtain the coding vector u after passing through the encoder and then

transmitted after being modulated by BPSK. The modulated signal is transmitted through an AWGN channel and then reaches the receiver.
In the training phase, the receiver calculates the LLR value as the training sample u based on the received vector y and inputs it into the neural network. The network outputs the negative value of the LLR value and the real transmitted code word uO for calculating the loss function. Fig. 3 gives the variation of quasi-ResNet shortcut connection weights with network training for 5 iterations of BP decoding expansion. The weights on the shortcut connections represent the inheritance rate of historical messages for each iteration. It can be seen from the figure that it is relatively small number when the weights tend to be stable. This indicates that the messages of shortcut connection account for a small proportion in decoding, and the messages with the shorter time step have a greater influence on current iteration.
In Fig. 4, we replicated the state-of-the-art neural network decoder available for LDPC decoding described in [4] [6] as a benchmark test scheme to simulate the BER performance of LDPC codes of code length (576, 432), which we denote as “FFNN”. As shown in the figure, the two decoding methods perform similarly when the number of iterations is insufficient

(5 iterations). However, since the jump link and exponentially weighted moving average of this method strengthen the effect of historical LLRs messages on the final decoding results, the BER curves of this method is smoother. Our method has better BER performance under the same iterations. With a BER of 10−4 for 15 iterations (corresponding to a 32-layer neural network), the quasi-ResNet BP decoder can obtain a gain of about 0.43 dB compared with FFNN decoder. This indicates that our method can converge the LLR messages faster to the most favorable fixed point for decoding.
In Fig. 5, we simulated the LDPC code with a code length of 384 and observed an important phenomenon. As the number of iterations increases, the neural network gain of FFNN decoder gradually disappears and its BER performance decreases and gradually approaches that of conventional BP decoder, while the decoding performance of our method for LDPC codes is still improving. Nevertheless, it should be stated that the improvement of BP decoding performance by proposed method is achieved at the cost of more computational complexity, which comes from the addition and multiplication generated by shortcut connection.
To understand the different decoding behaviors of these two neural decoders, we recorded the variation of loss function with training epoch during the training process, as shown in Fig. 6 ˜ Fig. 8. It can be seen that the network parameters of Quasi-ResNet decoding network can converge quickly in different depths of decoding neural networks, while the training of FFNN BP decoder becomes difficult with increasing network depth. Especially in the network expansion of 15 iterations (corresponding to a 32-layer neural network), the loss function of FFNN decoder has no longer a decreasing trend, which indicates that FFNN decoder network parameters are not updated effectively, and thus its decoding performance is maintained in an untrained state. From this point, it can be seen that the performance improvement of our proposed method partly benefits from the gradient disappearance problem mitigated by shortcut connections during training.
