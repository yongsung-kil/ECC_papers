A family of error correcting codes for automotive applications

Abstract—In this paper we propose a class of error correcting codes for automotive applications. The main merits of the proposed codes are the low-complexity encoding and decoding circuits, and a fine rate adaptability. A special chip for encoding and decoding operations could be designed, with promising market perspectives.
Index Terms—LDPC codes, Minimum Distance, Simplex codes
I. INTRODUCTION
Vehicular communications are becoming more and more determinant for traffic management, motor control, driver assistance, and safety assurance. Intra-vehicle communications [1], or modern solutions for extra-vehicle information transfer, say supported by 5G or Vehicle-to-Everything (V2X) [2], are going to be adopted. The need of data transfer, at a faster and faster speed, is universally recognized [3]. In all these applications, the availability of secure data collection and processing plays an important role. The main degradation suffered by data reproduction is due to external noise, caused by interference from other radio transmissions, electric disturbances, background thermal noise. The overall effect turns out to be a throughput lowering [3]. Error control codes [4] can be efficiently employed to contrast this degradation in a time and spatial redundancy scheme, like Multiple-Input and MultipleOutput (MIMO) systems [5], Forward Error Correction (FEC), as well as Authomatic Repeat-reQuest (ARQ) [6]. The channel code solution we propose in this paper, owing to its high versatility, allow good performance in either error correction and error detection [7]. The purpose of the present paper is to discuss the possibility of adopting a family of error control codes designed for automotive applications, so allowing an easy definition of standards, and giving proper orientations to manufacturers as well as market operators, with the goal of cost saving and quality assurance. The speed of transmissions is rapidly growing, imposing simplicity of construction and hence very short latency. The capability of being adaptable to different applications (e.g. sensor data acquisition, intravehicle control transmissions, vehicle-to-vehicle and vehicleto-infrastructure communications, safety alert message diffusion, and even cyber resilience to possible malicious attacks) is requested. A great variety of situations are to be faced, depending also on the instantaneous state of the transmission

channel. The possibility of employing a unique encodingdecoding scheme for assuring a protection against both external noise and intentional cyber crimes has been investigated, too [8], [9]. In such a double finalization, the codes we propose appear particularly suited, provided that the error capability is properly subdivided between the two goals. In order to satisfy efficiently all these requirements a special chip could be designed.
The paper is organized as follows. Section II contains a brief review of the theory of error control codes, introducing terminology and symbolism. Section III shows the structure of parity check matrix and generator matrix characterizing punctured simplex codes, interpreted as LDPC codes. Section IV is devoted to list some good recommendations for the design of the main code parameters. Three codes of the same family (with an equal number of information symbols), assuming code rates of 1/2, 2/3 and 3/4 are considered. The behaviour of such codes is predicted by Monte Carlo simulations. Section V is devoted to show the possibility to include shortening, besides puncturing, in the code design. Finally, Section VI collects the main conclusions.

The row-column constraint in the parity-check matrix of

an LDPC code expresses the condition of not having four 1-

symbols in the vertices of a rectangular geometry, forming a

4-length closed cycle in that matrix. It is well known that soft-

decision decoding algorithms, like the sum-product algorithm,

exhibit convergence problems when working on parity-check

matrices containing the aforementioned 4-length cycles.

In a digital reproduction the quality can be evaluated looking

at a parameter named the Bit Error Rate (BER), which

expresses the average number of errors on a large amount

of transferred binary symbols. The lower the BER the better

the transmission quality. This goal is reached supporting four

costs [10]: the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR); an increase, by

the factor 1/R, of the transmission speed with respect to a

system without channel coding; the time latency; and circuit

complexity, especially in the decoding procedure.

Error control coding reduces the first cost at the expense of

an increase in the remaining three. In a FEC arrangement,

adopting a soft-decision decoding algorithm [4], applicable

only to certain types of codes, the reduction in the SNR

needed for assuring a target BER value, can be evaluated

by means of a figure of merit called coding gain G. The

asymptotic expression of G, for very small values of the BER,

is G∞ = Rd. Such a compact formula contains a sort of trade off: d is typically larger at low code rates. However,

generally, values of G∞ ranging from 4 dB to 8 dB and

even more are assured, provided that the imposed code rate

R is not too large, and/or the block length n is not too short.

Furthermore, this performance is reached at the expense of an

unavoidable worsening in time latency and circuit complexity.

A practical result is that the smaller the coefficients A(w), for

w = d + 1, d + 2, . . ., the faster the approach to the asymptotic

coding gain G∞. The channel often shows dynamic variations in its SNR,

depending on the particular situation experienced by the

transmission system. In order to follow efficiently such slow

variation, the error control system should be easily adaptable.

Such feature is guaranteed, for instance, employing a unique

circuitry, capable of operating at different code rates and block

lengths. A family of codes of this type is called rate-adaptive.

LDPC codes have gained an impressive success, in the last

about twenty years, mainly owing to these good features:

possibility of a soft-decision decoding with relatively low

decoding complexity, obtainment of very competitive values

of the asymptotic coding gain G∞, availability of wide ranges of code rate values, although generally on the basis of ad-

hoc designs. They have also been suggested especially for

automotive applications [3] in the framework of traditional wireless or radio mobile services, as well as dedicated channel coding schemes.

B. Polynomial description of simplex codes
We also recall the polynomial description [10] of linear codes. In this framework, H and G show binary sequences in their rows, which can be expressed as polynomials preceded and followed by null symbols. When the polynomial is unique, and appears only properly shifted in the matrix rows, the code is called time-invariant. Polynomial codes are defined cyclic when they are time-invariant and, for a certain block length n = N = 2k − 1, any cyclic shift of a code word c(x) along xN + 1 is still a code word. Such a property can be expressed by the equality
c′(x) = xjc(x) mod xN + 1, j ∈ [0, N − 1]. (1)

where c′(x) and c(x) are two different codewords. Over the cyclic length N both the generator and the parity check matrix can be written in time-invariant form: the generator polynomial g(x) shifts along the rows of G, whereas the parity check polynomial h(x) shifts along the rows of H. The relationship between such two polynomials is

xN + 1 = g(x)h∗(x).

(2)

Given a code C for which g(x) and h(x) are the generator and the parity-check polynomial, respectively, we say that C′ is the dual of C if g(x) and h(x) are its parity-check and generator polynomial, respectively.
The family of Hamming codes is characterized by g(x) which is a binary primitive polynomial. A polynomial g(x) of degree k is said to be primitive if it is irreducible and if the smallest integer N for which g(x) divides xN + 1 is N = 2k − 1. The dual of a Hamming code is a simplex code [11], thus characterized by a primitive parity-check polynomial of degree k.
Simplex codes exhibit many interesting properties. We briefly recall some of them.

Property 1 Any non-zero codeword of a simplex code is a Pseudo-Noise (PN)-sequence of maximum period N = 2k −1.

Property 1 implies that all the codewords of a simplex code can be generated by means of a Linear-Feedback Shift Register (LFSR) with k memory elements. In other words, given a certain k, a one-to-one correspondence occurs between the codewords of a simplex code and all the cyclic permutations of the same PN sequence. A PN sequence appears as a completely random sequence, with the well-known binomial behaviour for the distribution of its weight, except for the limitations deriving from the impossibility of having more than k–1 consecutive zero-symbols. Furthermore, over an extremely long observation sample, it shows a periodic repetition. The same shift register can generate 2k − 1 different particular sequences depending on the initially chosen condition of the

C. Puncturing and shortening operations
In order to enlarge the code rate of simplex codes it is possible to apply a procedure called code puncturing, which consists in the removal of the last rows of matrix H. This leads to a decrease of both r and n by the same quantity s. Typically the result is a worsening in the performance, due to a decrease in the minimum distance d and to an increase in the number of low-weight code words. It is easy to verify that this transformation gives rise to a new matrix G, without the last s columns with respect to the original one. Now we can modify the above proposition stating that there is a oneto-one correspondence between the codewords of a punctured simplex code and all the truncated PN sequences having an equal length n, picked from the same mother PN sequence. A further degree of freedom, with the purpose of gaining a very fine rate adaptability, is given by the possibility of applying a procedure called code shortening, which consists in reducing the number of external rows in matrix G. So we obtain a lowering of both k and n, by the same quantity z. In this case we operate with a constant r, and H is transformed losing z external columns. Typically the performance is improved, at the expense of a code rate R reduction.
In general, punctured simplex codes have been already studied, for both error correction [15] and error detection [16], showing excellent theoretical performance, but decoding complexity still represent the main obstacle against their

Recently we have observed that, starting from properly punctured simplex codes, with the additional condition that polynomial h(x) is a Golomb ruler, it is possible to obtain a family of rate-adaptive LDPC codes [17], [18]. IV. OPTIMIZATION OF DESIGN PARAMETERS
In order to characterize a simplex LDPC code, h(x) must:
1) be sparse; 2) correspond to a Golomb ruler; 3) be primitive.
The first two conditions assure the LDPC nature and an efficient and feasible soft-decision decoding process. The third condition guarantees that the mother code (before puncturing) is simplex. Fortunately, Golomb rulers become naturally sparser and sparser with increasing their length L. A great deal of literature treats optimal Golomb rulers, that is those characterized by the shortest L for a given number of marks m. Several theoretical limits have been proposed [20] and three main construction methods have been suggested [21]– [23]. Typically the minimum L exhibits an almost quadratic

increase with m. An important property of Golomb rulers is expressed by the following proposition: a subset of a Golomb ruler is still a Golomb ruler [24]. So we can take a Golomb ruler with length k and m > wh. Then we select many subsets of the set of its marks. Such subsets have wh elements each, and the same length k. To such ensemble of subsets, all able to satisfy the design requirements, we apply a primitiveness test. It is enough to find one primitive polynomial among those tested with the same pair k, wh. If more than one success occurs, it is possible to compare the performance that any choice provides and take the best one. Typically there are no remarkable differences among these attempts. In order to satisfy practical requirements regarding the LDPC decoding algorithm, if we desire to construct a rate-adaptive encoder over a wide range of code rates R, it is necessary to control the value of parameter ⟨wc⟩, generally maintaining it between about 3 and about 4. Taking into account relation (4), it is advisable to subdivide the overall range of values of R into some classes, and repeat the above described procedure, for as many different values of wh.
As an example, let us suppose that for strict automotive requirements the code block must contain k = 282 information binary symbols, and the classes of code rates to be assured are R ∈ {1/2, 2/3, 3/4}. VI. CONCLUSION
Our research has demonstrated the potential of using a family of LDPC codes in automotive applications. These codes offer fine rate adaptability thanks to the possibility of simply applying puncturing and shortening operations. Additionally, they can be encoded and decoded using algorithms whose complexity scales linearly with the code block length, i.e., O(n). Another advantage is that a single encoding circuit can be used for all codes within the same family, as well as for codes with the same maximum block length belonging to different families.
