Cyclic Partial Geometries and Their Associated LDPC and Constant-Weight Codes
Juane Li , Member, IEEE, Yi Gong, Xin Xiao , Shu Lin, Life Fellow, IEEE, and Khaled Abdel-Ghaffar, Member, IEEE

Abstract— Partial geometries form an interesting branch in combinatorial mathematics, and recently they have been shown to be very effective in the construction of LDPC codes with distinct geometric and algebraic structures. This paper presents three specific cyclic classes of partial geometries. Based on these three classes of partial geometries, three classes of LDPC codes and three classes of constant-weight codes are constructed. Codes in these two categories are either cyclic or quasi-cyclic. Designs and constructions of these codes are straightforward and flexible without a need for extensive computer search. It is shown that long high-rate LDPC codes constructed based on the three classes of cyclic partial geometries perform well over the additive white Gaussian noise channel (AWGNC) with iterative decoding algorithm based on belief propagation. They can achieve low error-rates without visible error-floor and their decoding converges rapidly. These LDPC codes also perform well over the binary erasure channel (BEC) and are very effective in correcting phased-bursts of erasures. Based on cyclic partial geometries, a special type of constant-weight codes, called balanced codes, can be constructed. The constant-weight codes constructed based on partial geometries are either optimal or nearly optimal.
Index Terms— Additive white Gaussian noise channel (AWGNC), balanced code, balanced incomplete block design (BIBD), binary erasure channel (BEC), constant-weight (CW) code, LDPC code, partial geometry (PaG), phased erasure burst, quasi-cyclic (QC) code.

In this paper, we investigate three classes of partial geometries with cyclic structure, which are constructed based on finite fields, named as quadruple partial geometries (Q-PaGs), pentagonal partial geometries (P-PaGs), and triplet partial geometries (T-PaGs). II. PARTIAL GEOMETRIES AND BALANCED INCOMPLETE BLOCK DESIGNS
A. Definition and Basic Characteristics of Partial Geometries
Consider a combinatorial system composed of a set of N points and a set of M lines where each line consists of a set of points. Such a combinatorial system forms a partial geometry (PaG) if the following conditions are satisfied for some fixed integers r ≥ 2, t ≥ 2, and c ≥ 1 [1], [2], [3], [4], [5]: (1) Any two points are on at most one line; (2) Each point is on r lines; (3) Each line passes through t points; and (4) If a point a is not on a line L, then there are exactly c lines, each passing through a and a point on L. We denote such a partial geometry by PaG(r, t, c) where r, t, and c are called the parameters and the parameter c is called the connection number. Partial geometries were first introduced by Bose in 1963 [1]. A simple counting argument [4] shows that PaG(r, t, c) contains N = t((t − 1)(r − 1) + c)/c points and M = r((t − 1)(r − 1) + c)/c lines. If two lines have a common point a, we say that the two lines intersect at the point a, otherwise, we say that the two lines are parallel. Two lines in PaG(r, t, c) are either parallel or intersect at one and only one point. All the lines intersecting at a point a form an intersecting bundle of lines. The lines parallel to a line L form a parallel bundle of lines.
Let HPaG(r, t, c) = [hi,j ]0≤i<M,0≤j<N be an M ×N matrix over GF(2) with rows and columns corresponding to the M lines and N points in PaG(r, t, c), respectively. The rows and

columns are labeled from 0 to M − 1 and 0 to N − 1, respectively. The entry hi,j of HPaG(r, t, c) in the i-th row and j-th column equals 1 if the j-th point is on the i-th line of PaG(r, t, c), otherwise, it equals 0. The column and row weights of HPaG(r, t, c) are r and t, respectively. The matrix HPaG(r, t, c) is referred to as the line-point incidence matrix of PaG(r, t, c). The rows of HPaG(r, t, c) are called the incidence vectors of the lines in PaG(r, t, c). Since two lines in PaG(r, t, c) are either parallel or intersect at one and only one point and two points are on at most one line, no two rows (columns) in HPaG(r, t, c) have more than one location where they both have 1’s. Such a structural property of HPaG(r, t, c) is referred to as row-column (RC) constraint and is also known as orthogonal structure [17]. The RC-constrained structure of HPaG(r, t, c) ensures that its associated Tanner graph has girth at least 6.
The transpose HTPaG(r, t, c) of HPaG(r, t, c) is an N × M matrix with rows and columns corresponding to the points and lines of PaG(r, t, c), respectively. This matrix is referred to as the point-line matrix of PaG(r, t, c). The rows and columns of HPTaG(r, t, c) are referred to as the incidence vectors of the points and lines in PaG(r, t, c), respectively.
B. Balanced Incomplete Block Designs
A balanced incomplete block design (BIBD) [1], [3], [13], [14], [15] is an arrangement of N objects in M sets satisfying the following conditions: (1) Each set contains exactly t different objects; (2) Each object appears in exactly r different sets; and (3) Any pair of objects appears in exactly λ different sets. The sets are called blocks. The parameters N , M , r, t, λ satisfy the following two equalities: tM = rN and λ(N −1) = r(t−1). Since a BIBD is characterized by the five parameters N , M , r, t, λ, it is also called an (N, M, t, r, λ)BIBD. For a BIBD with λ = 1, each pair of objects appears in one and only one block. This implies that two blocks in this BIBD have at most one object in common. A BIBD with λ = 1 is also referred to as a Steiner-2 design (or system) [16] in combinatorial mathematics. LDPC codes have been constructed using BIBDs, see e.g., [19] and [20].
Viewing the objects and blocks as points and lines, respectively, an (N, M, t, r, 1)-BIBD gives a partial geometry, denoted by PaGBIBD(r, t, t) with N = r(t − 1) + 1 points and M = r(r(t − 1) + 1)/t lines. Each line consists of t points and each point is intersected by r lines. Two points appear on one and only one line. For a point a not on a line L in PaGBIBD(r, t, t), there are exactly t lines, each passing through a and a point on L. A PaG derived from a BIBD with λ = 1 is referred to as a BIBD-PaG, or a PaG associated with a BIBD. Conversely, a partial geometry PaGBIBD(r, t, t) may be viewed as a BIBD with λ = 1 which is referred to as a PaG-BIBD, or a BIBD associated with a PaG.

IV. LDPC CODES ASSOCIATED WITH Q-PaGs AND PARTIAL GEOMETRIES RELATED TO Q-PaGs
In this section, we present 4 different constructions of QC-LDPC codes based on the lines of PaGQ(4b, 4, 4), which give 4 different types of QC-LDPC codes. We then present some related partial geometries that allow for the construction of other QC-LDPC codes with a wide range of parameters. A. Type-1 Construction
In the first construction, we use the point-line incidence matrix GPaG,Q(b) as a parity-check matrix. Since GPaG,Q(b) is a low-density matrix, the null space over GF(2) of GPaG,Q(b) gives a (4, 4b)-regular QC-LDPC code, denoted by CPaG,Q(b), of length b(12b + 1) with rate at least of (b − 1)/b whose Tanner graph has girth at least 6, which is assured by the RC-constrained structure of GPaG,Q(b). We refer to this code as a circulant-type BB-PaG-QC-LDPC code, in short a CBBPQC-LDPC code. Since no 4b or fewer columns of GPaG,Q(b) can sum to a zero (12b + 1)-tuple, the minimum distance of CPaG,Q(b) is at least 4b + 1.
For 1 ≤ k ≤ b, let GPaG,Q(k) be a (12b + 1) × k(12b + 1) matrix formed by using k different circulants in GPaG,Q(b). The null space over GF(2) of GPaG,Q(k) gives a (4, 4k)regular CBBP-QC-LDPC code CPaG,Q(k), of length k(12b+1) with rate at least of (k − 1)/k. CPaG,Q(k) is a shortened code of CPaG,Q(b). The above construction gives the first type of QC-LDPC codes constructed based on PaGQ(4b, 4, 4).

B. Type-2 Construction
The second type of QC-LDPC codes is constructed by dispersing each entry in the base matrix BPaG,Q(4, b) into a circulant permutation matrix (CPM) of size (12b + 1) × (12b + 1). This is done as follows. We map each entry bi,j into a CPM, denoted by Mi,j, (with columns and rows labeled from 0 to 12b) of size (12b + 1) × (12b + 1). (Note that the entries of BPaG,Q(4, b) are the integers (elements) in GF(12b + 1) = {0, 1, 2, . . . , 12b}.) If an entry in BPaG,Q(4, b) is ℓ, 0 ≤ ℓ ≤ 12b, it is mapped into a CPM of size (12b + 1) × (12b + 1) whose generator (the top row) has its single 1-component at the position ℓ. VII. CLASS-3 PARTIAL GEOMETRIES – T-PaGs BASED ON TRIPLETS
In this section, we present a class of PaGs constructed based on finite fields of characteristic 2. Each line in a PaG of this class contains 3 points. Using these PaGs, QC-LDPC codes, binary and nonbinary, can be constructed. This class of PaGs also gives a class of BIBDs with λ = 1.

A. A Triplet System
Let m be a positive integer greater than or equal to 3, i.e., m ≥ 3. Let GF(2m) be the finite field of characteristic
2 constructed based on a primitive polynomial p(X) over
GF(2) of degree m and let α be a root of p(X). Hence, α is primitive element of the field GF(2m) and the powers of α, namely α0 = 1, α, α2, . . ., α2m−2, give all the 2m −1 nonzero elements of GF(2m), which form the multiplicative group of GF(2m), denoted by GF∗(2m) = GF(2m)\{0}.
Let S be a set of triplets, (αi, αj, αk), with elements from GF∗(2m) which satisfies the following conditions: (1) The three elements in each triplet (αi, αj, αk) are distinct; and (2) αi + αj + αk = 0, i.e.,

S = {(αi, αj, αk) : αi, αj, αk ∈ GF∗(2m), αi ≠ αj ≠ αk,

and αi + αj + αk = 0}.

(27)

The second condition αi + αj + αk = 0 on the set is referred to as the sum-constraint. Since any two elements in a triplet give the same triplet under the sum-constraint, the number of triplets in S is

|S| = 2m − 1 /3 = (2m − 1)(2m−1 − 1)/3.

is odd, 3 cannot be a factor of 2m − 1. Hence, the hypothesis

B. Triplet Partial Geometries
If we view each triplet (αi, αj, αk) over GF∗(2m) in the
triplet system S defined by (27) as a line and each element in GF∗(2m) as a point, it follows from Propositions 1 to 5 that the
triplet system S is a PaG, denoted by PaGT (r, 3, 3), with N = 2m−1 points, represented by the nonzero elements in GF(2m), and M = (2m − 1)(2m−1 − 1)/3 lines, represented by the
triplets in S. PaGT (r, 3, 3) has the following characteristics: (1) Any two points are on one and only one line; (2) Each point is on r = 2m−1 − 1 lines; (3) Each line consists of t = 3 points; and (4) If a point αi is not on a line L, then there are exactly c = 3 lines, each passing through αi and a
point on L. We call this PaG a triplet partial geometry (T-
PaG), which forms the Class-3 PaGs.
For odd m, the lines in PaGT (r, 3, 3) can be grouped into τ1 = (2m−1 − 1)/3 disjoint primitive cyclic classes of lines defined by (29). Each point in PaGT (r, 3, 3) is intersected by 3 lines in each primitive cyclic class. For even m, the lines in PaGT (r, 3, 3) can be grouped into τ2 = 2(2m−2 − 1)/3 primitive cyclic classes and one nonprimitive cyclic class.

(31)

VIII. LDPC CODES ASSOCIATED WITH CLASS-3 T-PaGs
A. Cyclic LDPC Codes Associated With T-PaGs
Since no two rows (columns) in the line-point incidence matrix HPaG,T (r, 3, 3) of PaGT (r, 3, 3) have more than one location where they both have 1-components, HPaG,T (r, 3, 3) satisfies the RC-constraint and has orthogonal structure. The null space of HPaG,T (r, 3, 3) gives a cyclic LDPC code with minimum distance 2m−1 whose Tanner graph has girth at least 6. For large m, it is a very low-rate code but has a very large minimum distance.
For each line (αi, αj, αk) in PaGT (r, 3, 3), we form a polynomial over GF(2) of degree 2m − 2 or less, v(X) = Xi + Xj + Xk, which is called the polynomial associated with the line (αi, αj, αk). The sum-constraint αi + αj + αk = 0 implies that α is a root of v(X). Thus, all the polynomials associated with the (2m − 1)(2m−1 − 1)/3 lines in PaGT (r, 3, 3) are minimum-weight code polynomials of the (2m − 1, 2m − m − 1) Hamming code CHamm [31] with minimum distance 3 generated by the primitive polynomial p(X) which is used to construct the field GF(2m).
It is known that the minimum-weight codewords of a
Hamming code span the code [32]. The dual code of a Hamming code is a maximum length sequence (MLS) code (or a simplex code) [33], denoted by CMLS. The null space of HPaG,T (r, 3, 3) gives the (2m − 1, m) MLS code with rate m/(2m − 1) and minimum distance 2m−1. Therefore, an MLS code is a cyclic PaG-LDPC code which consists of 2m − 1 nonzero codewords of weight 2m−1. It is one-step majority-logic decodable [32] and is capable of correcting

2m−2 − 1 random errors over a binary symmetric channel (BSC) and of correcting 2m−1 − 2 erasures over the binary
erasure channel (BEC). For example, the primitive polynomial p(X) = 1 + X2 + X5 used in Example 7 generates a (31, 26) Hamming code whose dual code is a (31, 5) MLS code with
minimum distance 16.

IX. CORRECTION OF BURSTS OF ERASURES
For an RC-constrained array H of CPMs of size n × n, if n is a prime, then the Tanner graph G associated with H has the following local-cycle structure: the Tanner graph Gsub(2, 2) associated with any 2 × 2 subarray Hsub(2, 2) of H is composed of a single cycle of length 4n, i.e., the 2n variable nodes (VNs) and the 2n check nodes (CNs) of Gsub(2, 2) are on a single cycle of length 4n. Such a local-cycle structure of the Tanner graph G associated with an RC-constrained array of CPMs of prime size was recently proved in [37] and [38] and is referred to as the 2 × 2 CPM-array cycle structure of H (or G). With this local-cycle structure, a QC-LDPC code given by the null space of H or its subarray is effective in correcting random phased-bursts of erasures over the BEC.
In the following, we show that QC-LDPC codes constructed based on the three classes of PaGs presented in Sections III, V, and VII can be used to recover phased-bursts of erasures over the BEC. For explanation, we consider codes associated with P-PaGs.
For 3 ≤ k ≤ b, let BPaG,P (2, k) be a 2 × k submatrix of the base matrix BPaG,P (5, b) given by (18). The CPM-dispersion of BPaG,P (2, k) gives a 2 × k array HPaG,P (2, k) of CPMs of size (20b + 1) × (20b + 1). The array HPaG,P (2, k) is a 2(20b + 1) × k(20b + 1) matrix with rank 2(20b + 1) − 1 = 40b + 1. The null space of HPaG,P (2, k) gives a (2, k)-regular (k(20b + 1), (k − 2)(20b + 1) + 1) CPM-BBP-QC-LDPC code CPaG,P (2, k) with 40b + 1 parity-check symbols. The Tanner graph GPaG,P (2, k) of CPaG,P (2, k) has the 2 × 2 CPMarray cycle structure. The subgraph Gsub(2, 2) of GPaG,P (2, k) associated with a 2 × 2 subarray Hsub(2, 2) of HPaG,P (2, k) is composed of a single cycle of length 4(20b+1) with 2(20b+1) VNs and 2(20b + 1) CNs on it.
Let v = (v0, v1, . . . , vk−1) be a codeword in CPaG,P (2, k) which consists of k sections. For 0 ≤ j < k, the j-th section vj consists of 20b + 1 consecutive code symbols in v which

correspond to the 20b + 1 columns of the j-th CPM-columnblock in HPaG,P (2, k). We call the sections of v CPM-sections. Two code symbols in v are said to be neighbors if they correspond to two VNs in the Tanner graph GPaG,P (2, k) of CPaG,P (2, k) which are neighbors (connected by a path of length 2 through a CN). It follows from the 2 × 2 CPM-array cycle structure of the Tanner graph that each code symbol in a CPM-section vi has exactly two neighbors in a different CPMsection vj of v. This neighbor structure is referred to as the adjacency structure of the code CPaG,P (2, k).
Suppose that a codeword v in CPaG,P (2, k) is transmitted over the BEC, and in the transmission of v, erasures occur which are confined to CPM-sections of v. A burst with erasures confined to a CPM-section of v is called a CPMphased erasure-burst. The length of the longest CPM-phased erasure-burst is 20b + 1. If all the 20b + 1 code symbols in a CPM-section of v are erased, the erasure-burst is said to be solid, otherwise, it is not solid, i.e., at least one of the code symbols in the CPM-section is not erased. Two CPM-phased erasure-bursts are said to be mutually semi-solid if the total number of erasures is 2(20b + 1) − 1 = 40b + 1, i.e., one CPM-phased erasure-burst is solid and the other contains 20b erasures (one code symbol is not erased).
Suppose that, during the transmission of a codeword v in CPaG,P (2, k), two random CPM-phased erasure-bursts occur, one in the i-th CPM-section vi and the other in the j-th CPMsection vj of v. Assume that the CPM-phased erasure-burst in vj is not solid. It follows from the adjacency structure of CPaG,P (2, k) that there exists at least one erased symbol v in the i-th CPM-section vi which is a neighbor of an unerased code symbol in the j-th CPM-section vj of v. Hence, there is a row h in HPaG,P (2, k) which checks v as the only erased symbol. If we take the inner product of h and v to form a check-sum Σ, then Σ contains v as the only unknown. From Σ, we can recover v by taking the modulo-2 sum of the un-erased symbols in Σ. After recovering v, we find another erased code symbol which is a neighbor of an un-erased code symbol or the recovered code symbol v in the two CPMphased erasure-bursts confined to the CPM-sections, vi and vj, of v. We recover this erased code symbol in exactly the same manner as the recovery of the first erased code symbol v. We continue this recovery process until all the erased code symbols in the CPM-sections vi and vj of v are recovered. The erasure recovery process will never fail as long as there is at least one un-erased code symbol in one of the two CPMphased erasure-bursts. The above erasure recovering process is commonly referred to as the peeling algorithm [17].
A measure of the erasure-correcting performance of a code is defined as the ratio of the maximum number of erasures that the code can recover to the number of parity-check symbols of the code. This ratio is called erasure-correcting efficiency, denoted by η. An erasure-correcting code is said to be optimal if η = 1. The maximum number of correctable erasures confined to two CPM-sections of a received vector in the code CPaG,P (2, c) is 40b + 1 which is equal to the number of parity-check symbols of CPaG,P (2, k). Hence, the code CPaG,P (2, k) is optimal, i.e., its erasure-correcting efficiency is η = 1. For k = 3, 4, . . . , b, we can construct a sequence of

X. CONSTANT-WEIGHT CODES ASSOCIATED WITH PaGs
Besides LDPC codes, partial geometries can also be used to construct constant-weight (CW) codes. In this section, we present several classes of CW codes which are constructed based on the classes of the partial geometries presented in Sections III, V, and VII. CW codes in these classes are either cyclic or quasi-cyclic.

A. Constant-Weight Codes
A binary (N, ω, λ) CW code, denoted by Ccw(N, ω, λ), is a set of M N -tuples over GF(2), called codewords, each of weight ω, such that any two codewords agree in at most λ 1positions [18]. Let u and v be two codewords in Ccw(N, ω, λ), ω(u) and ω(v) be the weights of u and v, respectively, and (u, v) be the real dot product of u and v. Then, the distance d(u, v) between u and v is

d(u, v) = ω(u) + ω(v) − 2(u, v) = 2(ω − (u, v)). (35)

An (N, ω, λ) CW code Ccw(N, ω, λ) meeting the upper bound is called an optimal CW code. A CW code of length N is called a balanced code [39], [40], [41], [42] if the weight of each codeword in the code is equal to ⌈N/2⌉. In such a CW code, denoted by Ccw(N, ⌈N/2⌉, λ), each codeword contains ⌈N/2⌉ ones and ⌊N/2⌋ zeros, i.e., the numbers of 1’s and 0’s are equal or approximately equal. A CW code is, in general, not a linear code.
Let HPaG be the line-point incidence matrix of a partial geometry PaG(r, t, c) with N points and M lines, in which each line consists of t points, each point is on r lines, and any two points are on at most one line. HPaG is an M × N matrix over GF(2) with column and row weights r and t, respectively, which satisfies the RC-constraint. It follows from the definition of a CW code that the rows of HPaG give an (N, t, 1) CW code, denoted by CPaG,cw(N, t, 1), of length N with M codewords, each with weight ω = t and λ = 1. The codewords in CPaG,cw(N, t, 1) are simply the incidence vectors of the lines in PaG(r, t, c). Let u and v be two codewords in CPaG,cw(N, t, 1). Since λ = 1, the real dot product (u, v) of u and v is either 0 or 1. It follows from (35) that the distance d(u, v) between u and v is either 2t or 2t − 2. If u and v are the incidence vectors of two parallel lines in PaG(r, t, c), then (u, v) = 0 and d(u, v) = 2t, otherwise (u, v) = 1 and d(u, v) = 2t − 2. We label the columns of HPaG from 0 to N − 1. For 0 ≤ j < N , let Λ(j) be the set of r rows in HPaG that are orthogonal on the j-th position (i.e., j-th coordinate). Then, the distance between two codewords which correspond to two rows in Λ(j) is 2t − 2.
Let GPaG = HTPaG be the transpose of HPaG. Then, GPaG is the point-line incidence matrix of PaG(r, t, c), which is an N × M matrix with column and row weights t and r, respectively. The rows of GPaG give an (M, r, 1) CW code, denoted by CPaG,cw(M, r, 1), of length M with N codewords, each with weight ω = r and λ = 1. The distance d(u, v) between two codewords u and v in CPaG,cw(M, r, 1) is either 2r or 2r − 2. Therefore, associated with a PaG, there are two CW codes, one formed by the rows of its line-point incidence matrix and the other formed by the rows of its point-line incidence matrix.
Since the null space of HPaG (GPaG) or a submatrix of it gives an LDPC code, this LDPC code may be regarded as the dual code of the CW code given by the rows of HPaG (GPaG) or its submatrix. So, from the above developments, we see that CW codes with λ = 1 and LDPC codes constructed based on PaGs are dual in nature.
The three classes of PaGs presented in Sections III, V and VIII and their related PaGs can be used to construct various types of CW codes, optimal or nearly optimal, with cyclic or QC structures.

Using the incidence vectors of lines in the T-PaG, PaGT (2m−1 − 1, 3, 3), constructed using the field GF(2m), we can construct a cyclic (2m − 1, 3, 1) optimal CW code CPaG,T,cw(2m −1, 3, 1) of length 2m −1 with (2m −1)(2m−1 − 1)/3 codewords, each with weight 3. The codewords of CPaG,T,cw(2m − 1, 3, 1) are the minimum-weight codewords of the (2m − 1, 2m−m − 1) Hamming code, which span
the Hamming code. It is well known that Hamming codes
are good for error detection [17], [43]. Therefore, the cyclic (2m −1, 3, 1) optimal CW code CPaG,T,cw(2m −1, 3, 1) is good for error detection.
As shown in Section VIII-A, the null space of CPaG,T,cw(2m − 1, 3, 1) is the (2m − 1, m) MLS code CMLS with minimum distance 2m−1. The MLS code CMLS consists of one all-zero codeword and 2m − 1 nonzero codewords, each with weight 2m−1. The nonzero codewords in CMLS form a cyclic (2m − 1, 2m−1, 2m−2) CW (or balanced) code of length 2m − 1 with 2m − 1 codewords, each with weight 2m−1. If we append a zero bit to the left (or right) of each nonzero codeword in CMLS, we obtain a set of 2m −1 extended MLS codewords. Each of these extended MLS codewords has 2m−1 1-components and 2m−1 0-components, i.e., the number
of 1’s equals the number of 0’s in each codeword. These 2m − 1 extended MLS codewords form a (2m, 2m−1, 2m−2) balanced code CPaG,T,cw(2m, 2m−1, 2m−2) of length 2m with 2m − 1 codewords, each with weight 2m−1. The distance between any two codewords in CPaG,T,cw(2m, 2m−1, 2m−2) is 2m−1.
The incidence vectors of points in PaGT (2m−1 − 1, 3, 3) form a QC-CW code CPaG,T,cw((2m −1)(2m−1 −1)/3, 2m−1 − 1, 1) of length (2m − 1)(2m−1 − 1)/3 with 2m − 1 codewords, each with weight 2m−1 − 1. XI. CONCLUSION AND REMARKS
In this paper, three classes of cyclic partial geometries (PaGs) were presented. The first two classes of cyclic PaGs are constructed based on prime fields under specific constraints, and the third class of cyclic PaGs is constructed based on finite fields of characteristic 2. It was shown that a cyclic PaG in each of the first two classes is a compressed sub-geometry of a mother PaG constructed based on the same field. From a combinatorial design point of view, the three classes of cyclic PaGs and their associated mother geometries are balanced

incomplete block designs (BIBDs), in which two objects are contained in one and only one block. Based on each of the three classes of cyclic partial geometries and their mother geometries, four types of binary QC-LDPC codes, called PaGQC-LDPC codes, were constructed. Simulation results show that the constructed PaG-QC-LDPC codes, especially long high-rate codes, perform very well over the AWGN channel decoded with the min-sum algorithm (MSA) based on beliefpropagation, and they can achieve very low error-rates without error-floors. Simulation results also show that the constructed codes perform very well over the BEC. Besides binary LDPC codes, one class of nonbinary QC-LDPC codes was also presented in this paper.
The Tanner graphs of PaG-QC-LDPC codes constructed in this paper have interesting local-cycle structure. Based on this local-cycle structure, optimal PaG-QC-LDPC codes can be designed to correct erasures, binary or nonbinary, that are confined in any two CPM-sections of a transmitted codeword with a simple erasure-peeling algorithm.
The PaGs presented in this paper also give several special classes of cyclic or quasi-cyclic constant-weight codes, optimal or nearly optimal. Two codewords in such a constant-weight code have at most one position where they both have 1-components. The dual space of such a constant-weight code gives a regular cyclic or QC-LDPC code whose Tanner graph has girth at least 6. The space spanned by the codewords in a cyclic constant-weight code gives a cyclic code.
