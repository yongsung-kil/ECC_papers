LDPC Decoders Prefer More Reliable Parity Bits:

Unequal Data Protection Over BSC

Beyza Dabak∗, Ece Tiryaki†, Robert Calderbank∗, and Ahmed Hareedy† ∗Electrical and Computer Engineering Department, Duke University, Durham, NC 27708 USA †Electrical and Electronics Engineering Department, Middle East Technical University, Ankara, 06800 Turkey
beyza.dabak@duke.edu, tiryaki.ece@metu.edu.tr, robert.calderbank@duke.edu, and ahareedy@metu.edu.tr

Abstract—Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are specified by graphs, and are the error correction technique of choice in many communications and data storage contexts. Message passing decoders diffuse information carried by parity bits into the payload, and this paper measures the value of engineering parity bits to be more reliable than message bits. We consider the binary symmetric channel (BSC) and measure the impact of unequal data protection on the threshold of a regular LDPC code. Our analysis also includes doping where the parity bits are known to the decoder. We investigate BSC with Gallager-A decoder, with a 3-level-alphabet decoder, and with a full belief propagation decoder. We demonstrate through theoretical analysis and simulation that non-equiprobable inputs lead to significant improvements both in the threshold and in the speed with which the decoder converges. We also show that all these improvements are possible even with a simple 3-level-alphabet decoder.
I. INTRODUCTION
Since their introduction by Gallager [1] in 1962, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have found widespread use in wireless communication and data storage. It is worthwhile to consider how to improve decoder performance by directly shaping the reliability of inputs. Here, for unequal data protection (UDP), a specific part of the transmitted/stored data has higher reliability by design. Moreover, doping is to have coded bits that are known to the decoder. In UDP, one prior approach is to protect message bits rather than parity bits [2], but a high rate code will have many more message bits to protect. We choose to protect parity bits rather than message bits of a regular LDPC code. A different approach entirely is to engineer unequal reliabilities by shaping the code, for example, through the choice of degree distribution in multi-edge type LDPC codes [3], or through optimizing code irregularities in order to obtain several classes of protection within the codeword [4]–[6], or through doping to improve performance via creating low-degree check nodes (CNs) [7]. Our approach, instead, is independent of the specific LDPC code construction.
The value of protecting parity bits over message bits was demonstrated empirically in the context of data storage [8]. In magnetic recording (MR), a lexicographically-ordered constrained code [9] was used to protect the parity bits of an LDPC code by mitigating inter-symbol interference. Density gains of up to 16% were observed with respect to an LDPC coded system with no constrained coding and with the same overall rate. The empirical results presented in [8] are the motivation behind this paper as we want to demonstrate the threshold gains of this UDP setup theoretically. The threshold is a measure of the highest channel noise/error level below which, coded error-free transmission is possible for a specific set of code parameters and a decoder [10]. UDP can also be applied in Flash memories and wireless communications [11].

Here, we operate on the binary symmetric channel (BSC) and subject parity bits to lower crossover probabilities than message bits. We employ density evolution (DE) [10], an analytical tool widely used to evaluate asymptotic performance and investigate convergence behavior of iterative decoders. In [12], exact thresholds for Gallager A algorithm are determined for BSC. The methods introduced in [10] and [12] have been widely adopted in the literature [13], [14]. We analyze the BSC with finite alphabet decoders (2-level Gallager-A and 3-level error-and-erasures decoders), also with an infinite alphabet decoder (belief propagation (BP) decoder). UDP and doping provide significant performance gains, even with a simple quantized decoder. Gains jump as we increase the decoder alphabet from 2 to 3 levels; in 3-level decoder UDP is approximately as effective as it is in BP. We achieve up to 38.2% and 42.7% gains for 3-level and BP decoders, respectively.
Our contributions are as follows. First, we derive the DE equations for UDP and doping setups for all decoders (adopting the analysis in [10]). Second, we further analyze the iterative algorithm performance to provide insight into decoder dynamics. In certain cases, we are able to derive closed-forms of the exact threshold, tight closed-form approximations of the threshold in terms of code parameters, or probability approximations that depend on the iteration index. Third, we use the aforementioned DE equations to numerically find the exact threshold for different decoders. Fourth, we experimentally demonstrate the gains of the UDP and doping setups. We also show that, at the same noise level, unequal data protection improves the speed with which the decoder converges.
II. UDP SETUP AND PRELIMINARIES
We introduce the necessary preliminaries. Long version of this section and more details can be found in [15] and [10].
A. UDP Setup We work on regular (dv, dc) LDPC codes, where dv and
dc are the variable node (VN) degree and the CN degree, respectively. In UDP setup, parity bits are more reliable than the message bits, i.e., parity and message bits effectively see two different channels with different reliabilities. Let x be the fixed number of VNs, connected to a specific CN, that are reliable. Thus per CN, there are x reliable VNs and dc − x regular VNs. Since the fraction of parity VNs is dv/dc and the fraction of reliable VNs is x/dc, it is natural to assume that x = dv without loss of generality. We denote the error probabilities of parity bits and message bits by p and p, i.e., parity and message bits are transmitted over two different channels with p and p, respectively. Note that we do not change the code construction in our UDP setup contrarily to other works [3]–[6]. Here, each VN representing a message bit is

These speed of convergence gains are translated into notable power savings in practical systems.
