Abstract—The 
demand 
for 
high-capacity 
satellite 
communications has continuously increased, owing to the need to 
enhance the performance of sensors on earth observation satellites. 
One of the challenges is to realize a high-capacity return link from 
user satellites to ground stations. The Japan Aerospace 
Exploration Agency (JAXA) developed an optical intersatellite 
communication system "LUCAS" in 2020. JAXA aims to 
demonstrate a 1.8 Gbps data relay between a user satellite in low 
earth orbit and a ground station. Currently, the realization of a 
next-generation optical data relay system capable of even higher-
speed communications is being studied. However, a detailed 
performance comparison of channel coding methods for optical 
data relay systems has not been conducted to enable high-speed 
communication within the limits of realistic computation power 
for installation in data relay satellites using optical intersatellite 
communication. Therefore, in this paper, we conduct numerical 
simulations to evaluate the characteristics of communication 
channel coding technologies, such as polar, low-density parity 
check codes, and Digital Video Broadcasting- Satellite-Second 
Generation (DVB-S2) channel codes, when applied to optical data 
relay satellite systems. The results of the simulations indicate that 
the performance of DVB-S2 codes is relatively good. Furthermore, 
a simple log-likelihood ratio correction method for DVB-S2 is 
proposed and shown to be effective.

I. INTRODUCTION 
Non-terrestrial networks are being considered for fifth-
generation (5G) mobile communication systems [1], and various 
use cases using satellite communications are being envisioned. 
By utilizing the wide area and stability of satellite 
communications, it will be possible to expand the coverage of 
cellular systems to include mountains, oceans, the skies above, 
and outer space, which is expected to be an indispensable 
mechanism for realizing a super-smart society. 
A major requirement of this satellite communication system 
is to increase its capacity [2,3]. This is not only because of the 
connection with terrestrial cellular systems but also because of 
the large number of potential users in a wide satellite beam and 
the increasing demand for capacity per user, which has been an 
ongoing issue. This is also required for data relay transmission 
systems that use intersatellite communication [4]. When a user 
satellite orbiting in a low Earth orbit (LEO) transmits data on the 
Earth’s surface observed by remote sensing to a target ground 
station, the LEO satellite may be out of sight of the ground 
station for a long time, depending on its orbital position, 
resulting in a large delay time and loss of real-time observation. 
To solve this problem, the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency 
(JAXA) developed an optical intersatellite communication 
system called the laser-utilizing communication system 
(LUCAS), which was launched in 2020. This is a relay 
transmission system using a geostationary orbit (GEO) satellite, 
which ensures a long communication time between an LEO user 
satellite and a ground station, achieves a large capacity by 
applying optical communication, and can transmit observation 
data with low latency in a short time. In the future, JAXA plans 
to demonstrate a 1.8 Gbps data relay between user satellites such 
as the ALOS series and a ground station [5]. 
Furthermore, JAXA is currently studying the construction of 
a next-generation optical data relay satellite system to support 
the increasing resolution of remote sensing satellites and enable 
data relay from a variety of user satellites. This optical data relay 
system further reduces the size and weight of the onboard 
communication equipment and increases the channel capacity. 
One technical challenge is the construction of a channel coding 
technology, particularly forward error correction (FEC), which 
satisfies these requirements. It is necessary to apply FEC 
suitable for relay systems with light coding and decoding signal 
processing loads, as short a code length as possible, and a high 
coding gain. However, to date, there has been insufficient 
evaluation and comparison of the characteristics of various 
FECs in optical data relay systems. 
Therefore, in this paper, we evaluate the characteristics of 
low-density parity check (LDPC) codes, Bose-Chaudhuri-
Hocquenghem (BCH) codes, turbo codes, and the coupled 
LDPC and BCH codes in the Digital Video Broadcasting- 
Satellite-Second Generation standard (hereinafter called DVB-
S2 codes) [6]. We clarify the transmission characteristics of the 
return link between a user satellite and a ground station by 
numerical simulation and show that the DVB-S2 code with a 
low coding rate is suitable for tolerating the transmission error 
rate of the optical communication link between a user satellite 
and a relay satellite. We also propose a new simple log-
likelihood ratio correction method at the ground station for 
DVB-S2 decoding during relay transmission and demonstrate its 
effectiveness.

provides an overview of the optical satellite data relay system.

II. OVERVIEW OF SATELLITE-BASED OPTICAL DATA RELAY 
SYSTEM 
Fig. 1 shows the system model of the return link 
transmission from a user satellite to a ground station in the 
LUCAS optical data relay system. The purpose of this system 
is to transmit data from a user satellite to a ground station 
immediately and quickly. When a user satellite transmits 
directly to the ground station in LEO, it can communicate only 
four times a day for 10 min each time, owing to the orbital 
displacement of LEO. 
As shown in the figure, LUCAS uses free-space optical 
communication for the intersatellite link between the user and 
relay satellites. The use of optical communication enables 
broadband high-capacity transmission at a user rate of 1.8 Gbps, 
smaller satellite-mounted equipment, lower transmission power, 
and lower power consumption. This is a significant advantage 
for satellite communications, which have severe weight and 
power limitations. In addition, fluctuations in the received 
optical power due to atmospheric turbulence, which is a 
problem in space-to-ground optical communications, are 
virtually nonexistent in intersatellite links in space. In other 
countries, spatial optical communication has been considered 
for feeder links between relay satellites and the ground [7,8]; 
however, in Japan, the probability that optical links will be 
blocked is not necessarily low because of the large number of 
clouds, and Ka-band radio wave transmission is used in 
LUCAS. 
A. Requirements for Next-Generation Systems 
JAXA is currently studying a successor mission to LUCAS 
and plans to construct an optical data relay system that will 
enable higher user data transmission speeds, smaller and lighter 
communication devices, connections to various user satellites, 
and multiple access to multiple users. Various technical issues 
must be solved to realize this system, and this study focuses on 
improving the performance of communication channel coding. 
Specifically, we attempt to identify an FEC method with a high 
coding gain in end-to-end transmission (as shown in Fig. 1), 
short coding length and high coding rate, and light coding and 
decoding signal-processing load. This will contribute to the 
construction of next-generation systems. In the next section, we 
describe the characteristics of the target FEC method and 
problem setup. 
III. CANDIDATE CHANNEL CODING METHODS AND PROBLEM 
SETUP 
Fig. 2 shows the return link transmission model of the optical 
data relay system. Data from an LEO user satellite are encoded 
by a certain type of FEC, digitally modulated, converted to 
optical signals, and transmitted to a GEO relay satellite using an 
optical intersatellite link. The optical intersatellite link is 
assumed to be a binary symmetric channel (BSC) with an error 
probability 𝑝. The optical signal received by the relay satellite is 
converted into an electrical signal, demodulated using hard 
decoding, remodulated, and transmitted as a Ka-band signal to a 
ground station. Various signal processing methods are possible 
for relay satellites, such as FEC decoding with soft or hard 
values, re-encoding with the same or another FEC, and amplify-
and-forward (AF) relay transmission of the received signal in its 
analog value. However, in this study, a hard-decision 
demodulation setting was used to reduce the signal-processing 
burden and storage capacity of the relay satellite. That is, FEC 
decoding is not performed in relay satellite to reduce the load. 
The signals transmitted by the relay satellite are transmitted to 
the ground station via a feeder link. To calculate and compare 
the pure performance of the FEC method, an additive white 
Gaussian noise (AWGN) transmission channel was assumed for 
the Ka-band transmission channel. The ground station decodes 
the received signal using the FEC applied by the user satellite 
and obtains the data. 
For the target performance of the FEC, this study assumes 
that (i) various user satellite connections can be accommodated, 
and (ii) the average signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) of a Ka-band 
feeder link can be made relatively large by expanding the 
antenna aperture of the ground station. Under these assumptions, 
Condition 1 was established.

V. CONCLUSIONS 
In this study, the characteristics of FECs for next-generation 
optical data relay satellite systems were clarified through 
numerical simulations using the polar, LDPC, BCH, turbo, and 
DVB-S2 codes. In addition, a simple log-likelihood ratio 
correction method for DVB-S2 codes was proposed. The results 
show that the overall transmission performance of the DVB-S2 
code is good, and that the hard-decision error rate 𝑝 of the 
optical intersatellite link is acceptable at 𝑝= 0.1 and 0.01 for 
coding rates of less than 0.3 and 0.8, respectively. The 
effectiveness of the proposed log-likelihood ratio correction 
method was confirmed. 
In the future, we will further clarify the relationship between 
FEC parameters, link settings, and satellite signal processing 
burdens suitable for next-generation systems and proceed to the 
study of system design.
