Analysis of Binary and Ternary Message Passing Decoding for Generalized LDPC Codes
Emna Ben Yacoub , Graduate Student Member, IEEE, and Gianluigi Liva , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract‚ÄĒ The performance of generalized low-density paritycheck (GLDPC) codes under binary and ternary message passing decoding (BMP/TMP) is analyzed from a density evolution (DE) perspective. At the check nodes, two types of local decoders are considered, namely optimum a-posteriori probability (APP) soft-input soft-output decoding, and bounded distance decoding (BDD). The purpose is to shed light on the performance loss incurred by BMP and TMP decoding of GLDPC codes with respect to unquantized belief propagation (BP) decoding. A DE analysis for irregular code ensembles is developed for all the algorithms, which allows obtaining the scaling coefficients needed for the variable node operation of BMP and TMP decoders. The stability analysis for the case of bounded distance decoding at the check nodes is derived. The asymptotic DE analysis is confirmed by the finite-length simulation results. For the codes analyzed in this paper, which rely on extended Hamming component codes, the study shows that under BMP decoding, BDD at the check nodes yields almost the same performance as optimum APP check node processing, while under TMP decoding the loss incurred by the sub-optimum BDD at check nodes is within 0.7 dB, when compared with APP decoding at the check nodes.
Index Terms‚ÄĒ Binary and ternary message passing, GLDPC codes, density evolution, bounded distance decoding.
I. INTRODUCTION
THE increasing demand for high data rate has triggered considerable interest in low-complexity and high throughput decoding algorithms. Recently, special attention has been given to the design of iterative decoders where the messages exchanged within the decoder are coarsely quantized. In [1], Gallager presented two message passing algorithms, where the variable and check nodes exchange binary messages. The authors of [2] presented a message passing algorithm, referred to as binary message passing (BMP), where the messages between variable and check nodes are binary and the variable nodes (VNs) exploit soft information from the channel. These decoders were referred to as ternary message passing (TMP), quaternary message passing (QMP), matched quantized min-sum (MQMS), respectively. Sharing several similarities with BMP decoding, reliability-based iterative majority-logic decoding algorithms for low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes were proposed in [6].
Generalized low-density parity-check (GLDPC) codes, introduced in [7], are a class of LDPC codes where the check nodes (CNs) represent more general codes than the single parity-check (SPC) codes in standard LDPC codes. The codes associated to the CNs can be any linear block code and will be referred to as component codes. GLDPC codes offer a trade-off between error floor and waterfall performance due to their good distance and trapping set properties and the powerful block codes used at the CNs (compared to SPC codes employed by the CNs of LDPC codes). This comes at the cost of increasing decoding complexity, especially if optimum softinput soft-output (SISO) decoding is performed at each CN. Several works studied reducing the decoding complexity of GLDPC and product-like codes [8], [9], [10], [11], [12], [13]. In [8], it has been proven that iterative hard decision decoding using extrinsic bounded distance decoding (BDD) at the component codes of spatially coupled GLDPC codes, where all VNs have degree 2, can approach capacity at high rates. The exchange of binary messages between VNs and CNs is particularly attractive for high-throughput applications, since it allows reducing the internal decoder data flow. The method was extended in [12] and [14] such that the VNs exploit the channel reliabilities while the exchanged messages are still binary. In particular, the BMP decoding algorithms introduced in [12] and [14] make use of BDD decoding at the check nodes and follow the approach in [8] to ensure the exchange of extrinsic messages.
In this work, we analyze the performance of GLDPC codes under BMP and TMP decoding. of GLDPC codes. At the CNs, the binary and ternary messages are obtained either by using BDD or by using optimum a posteriori probability (APP) SISO decoding. In the latter case, the component decoder soft-output (i.e., the extrinsic likelihood ratios) is mapped to messages from the desired binary/ternary alphabet. The analysis is limited to GLDPC codes whose CN component codes admit a simple trellis representation, hence enabling the analysis under APP decoding at the CNs. Besides providing asymptotic decoding thresholds for GLDPC code ensembles, the DE analysis plays for BMP and TMP decoding an additional role, as suggested in [2]. In fact, the VN messages are obtained by combing the channel loglikelihood ratio (LLR) with a weighted sum of the incoming CN messages and quantizing the result to obtain a binary or ternary message: the weighting factors used at the VNs can be estimated via DE.
We focus on GLDPC codes with CNs based on (extended) Hamming codes. Throughout the paper, we will consider as a reference model the binary-input additive white Gaussian noise (biAWGN) channel with input alphabet X = {‚ąí1, +1}. The channel output is Y = X +N , where N is Gaussian random noise with zero mean and variance ŌÉ2. The channel quality is defined in terms of Eb/N0, where Eb is the energy per information bit and N0 is the single-sided noise power spectral density.

IV. DENSITY EVOLUTION ANALYSIS
Under BMP and TMP decoding, the DE analysis plays a twofold role: On one hand, it provides a study of the asymptotic decoding threshold achievable by a given GLDPC code ensemble; on the other hand, it provides estimates of the error/erasure probabilities of the messages, that are required to compute the reliability terms in (1) and in (2). We provide a DE analysis for both BMP and TMP under the assumption that the all-ones codeword is transmitted. The analysis works under the tree-like neighborhood condition, hence in the limit of infinite block length [18]. Let p0(‚Ąď) and p(‚ąí‚Ąď1) be the erasure and error probabilities of VN messages at the ‚Ąď-th iteration. Similarly, q0(‚Ąď) and q‚ąí(‚Ąď1) are the erasure and error probabilities of CN messages. Note that, while the threshold definitions below require performing the DE recursions for an infinite amount of iterations, for the numerical implementation of the analysis we employed a fixed maximum iteration count (denoted by ‚Ąďmax).

VI. NUMERICAL RESULTS
We provide next two examples where two GLDPC code ensembles are considered. For the two examples, both iterative decoding thresholds and finite-length simulation results in terms of BER are given, over the biAWGN channel (see Section II.A).
Example 1: Consider the rate R = 0.625 regular GLDPC ensemble with VN degree dv = 2, where all the CNs correspond to the (32, 26) extended Hamming code. Using the derived DE, we obtained the iterative decoding thresholds of this ensemble under BMP and TMP where we set ‚Ąďmax = 200 and applied different decoding methods at the CNs. We observed that for the APP SISO decoder at the CNs, the decoding threshold for TMP improves by 1.82 dB as compared to BMP. For extrinsic BDD [15], the gain of TMP is 1.18 dB. We designed a length n = 8000 code from this ensemble via the progressive edge-growth (PEG) algorithm [22]. For the simulations, we set the maximum number of iterations ‚Ąďmax = 50. The simulation results for BMP (dashed lines) and TMP (solid lines) are depicted in Fig. 1 in terms of BER versus Eb/N0. The results are provided by employing APP SISO decoding (blue lines), BDD, where we replace the j-th entry with an erasure (green lines) [15] and BDD following [8] (red lines) at the CNs, where BDD is implemented in the extrinsic message passing setting. The waterfall performance of the different decoders is in agreement with the thresholds derived via DE. We observe that in this case, when using BDD at the CNs, it is better to replace the j-th entry with an erasure than with the channel message (following the method in [8]).
