Asymptotic and Finite-Length Performance of Irregular Spatially-Coupled Codes

Reza A. Ashrafi, Abdullah Sarıduman, Ali E. Pusane Department of Electrical and Electronics Engineering Bog˘azic¸i University Istanbul, Turkey
Email: {reza.ashrafi, abdullah.sariduman, ali.pusane}@boun.edu.tr

Abstract—The newest family of low-density parity-check
(LDPC) codes, spatially-coupled (SC) codes, is shown to have
several desirable characteristics including low implementation
complexity and close-to-optimal performance over a range of
channels. Furthermore, because of their ribbon-shaped parity-
check matrices, window decoding can be used to decode these
codes, which leads to low-delay implementations. Researchers
have focused on asymptotically regular SC code ensembles and
have examined several aspects of the code construction processes.
In this paper, we concentrate on irregular SC code ensembles.
We evaluate their decoding thresholds over the binary erasure
channel and show that their performance is better than their
regular SC counterparts. It is also shown that the gap between
asymptotic coding thresholds of irregular SC ensembles and the
fundamental Shannon limit gets negligibly small. For the sake
of a better comparison, we have also evaluated the finite-length
error performance of selected regular and irregular SC codes
over the additive white Gaussian channel and it is also observed
that finite-length error performance of these irregular SC codes
outperforms regular SC codes. To further improve the error
performance of these codes and to lower the possible error floors,
progressive edge growth algorithm has also been considered in
the finite-length performance analysis.
I. INTRODUCTION Recently, spatially-coupled (SC) low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have attracted considerable amount of interest in the channel coding literature. These codes are the terminated forms of LDPC convolutional codes, which were introduced in [1]. Thus, SC codes demonstrate some nice characteristics of LDPC convolutional codes, including simple code construction procedure, encoding and decoding simplicity, and better error performance compared to the underlying block LDPC codes. In addition to these features, SC codes exhibit threshold saturation. This means that their error performance, under a suboptimal decoding scheme of belief-propagation (BP), converges to the optimal maximum-likelihood (ML) decoding performance over the binary erasure channel (BEC) [2]. An SC code, as a linear block code, is fully defined by its parity-check matrix H, which still is a sparse matrix, since SC codes are a variant of LDPC codes. Due to the code’s convolutional structure, parity-check matrix H has a ribbonshaped form. This special structure enables us to employ the sliding window decoding, which results in a much lower decoding delay compared to the conventional block decoder. The window decoder has a key advantage in the form of starting the decoding process as soon as a portion of the

codeword is reached at the receiver, while a block decoder has to wait for the entire codeword to be received before starting the decoding process. In this paper, we only consider the window decoding scheme for SC codes.
While researchers mainly focus on (asymptotically) regular SC codes (specifically (3,6)-regular SC codes) in the literature, in this paper, we investigate irregular SC codes, whose variable node degree dv is not fixed and may vary. By means of a belief-propagation-based density evolution (DE) analysis [3], we determine the asymptotic coding thresholds of different irregular SC ensembles over the BEC and show that these irregular code ensembles have higher coding thresholds compared to the regular SC codes. Furthermore, to illustrate their superior error performance over other practical channels, bit error rates (BER) of some of the selected SC ensembles with the highest decoding thresholds over the BEC are also evaluated over the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel via computer simulations. In this analysis, we consider two different algorithms to construct the parity-check matrix H. One approach is based on random permutation matrices, which is less computationally-complex. The problem with this approach is that it may create problematic structure in the parity-check matrix, which results in finite-length performance degradation and error floors. The other approach is the progressive edge growth (PEG) algorithm, which aims at eliminating these problematic patterns out of the parity-check matrix to improve the BER performance of the code [4].
We start in Section II by reviewing the basic code construction method based on protographs for SC codes that are employed throughout the paper. Window decoding and density evolution techniques are also explained in more detail. In Section III, analysis of irregular SC code ensembles, which is the main contribution of this paper, is discussed. Simulation results for BER of selected SC codes over the AWGN channel are presented in Section IV, and finally, Section V concludes the paper.

The DE technique considers the cycle-free graph representation of an LDPC code ensemble and tracks the probability density functions of the exchanged messages over the edges between the variable and check nodes for some rounds of iterations. Maximum number of iterations in our DE analysis is set to 10, 000. This indeed determines the iterative decoding limits of LDPC code families and therefore determines the asymptotic error performance of sparse code ensembles [3]. This analysis can be utilized to determine the iterative decoding thresholds of SC code ensembles using their protograph representation B[L] [7].
Taking the BP equations for variable node and check node updates at each round of iterations over the BEC into account, the iterative decoding threshold ✏⇤ is defined as the largest value of channel erasure rate that allows a successful decoding. In the window decoding scheme, success is defined as reducing the erasure probability of the targeted symbols in the window position below some pre-determined threshold . In this paper, we set = 10 6.
III. ANALYSIS OF IRREGULAR SC CODE ENSEMBLES
We are more interested in SC ensembles with minimal coupling, where the code memory is one or two. There are 16 asymptotically (3, 6)-regular SC ensembles with ms = 1 and for ms = 2, this number is 100 [8]. Among ensembles with ms = 1, ensemble B with B0 = [2 2], B1 = [1 1] has the highest window decoding threshold (✏w⇤ d) over the BEC, which is equal to 0.4844 for window size of W = 4. For ms = 2, ensemble C with B0 = [0 2], B1 = [2 1], B0 = [1 0] (and the ensemble with B0 = [2 0], B1 = [1 2], B0 = [0 1], which is called the mirror of ensemble C) has the highest threshold of ✏w⇤ d = 0.4801 for the same window size.
It is important to note that regular LDPC block codes have a window decoding threshold of ✏w⇤ d = 0.4294. Thus, it is inferred that coupling considerably increases the coding threshold.

IV. BER SIMULATION RESULTS
For convenience, DE analysis and other analytical investigations are performed over the BEC, since it is simpler to derive closed-form mathematical representations on the erasure channel. However, to understand irregular SC codes’ behavior more comprehensively, we also evaluate their BER over the AWGN channel.
We have evaluated the BER of the codes derived from SC ensembles listen in Table V with ms = 1. L is chosen to be 40 and M = 100 and hence, code length is 8, 000. Window decoding based on BP algorithm has been employed and the decoding window size is M ⇥ W = 400. The maximum number of iterations for BP algorithm is set to 100.
To construct the parity-check matrix H from the base matrix B[L], the procedure based on the random permutation matrices has been used. However, as mentioned above, this

method constructs parity-check matrices with built-in problematic structures, and therefore their BER performance may be degraded by exhibiting error floor. To reduce this destructive effect, we also construct parity-check matrices H obtained by employing the PEG algorithm.
The PEG algorithm tries to avoid small cycles by maximizing the girth value of the code [4]. The algorithm establishes edges in the Tanner graph of the code mainly according to two performance measures: minimal impact on the girth and density evolution. The nodes in the graph are connected edge by edge in a way that the dimension of a generated cycle is maximum (if it exists) and the connected check node has the lowest degree among candidate check nodes. In our analysis, PEG algorithm has been slightly modified to be used on SC codes. The available check nodes are restricted according to base matrices and the connections of base matrices are considered to have minimal impact on the girth of the graph.
Figure 1 shows the BER of the SC codes with ms = 1 from Table V over the AWGN channel, where parity-check matrices are constructed via random permutation method. As can be seen, error performance of irregular SC codes outperforms BER of the regular SC code ensemble B. It is also observed that the order of the BER curves is consistent with the results derived by DE technique. It is important to note that error floors are observed on high-SNR regions, i.e., higher than 3 dB, for all of the SC codes. The reason for this behavior lies in the presence of stopping sets that dominate the error performance.
Figure 2 depicts the same comparison, where parity-check matrices are formed by the PEG algorithm. It is seen that error performance of the SC codes improves, which is due to the fact that PEG reduces the number of short cycles and other problematic formations inside the parity-check matrix H. This translates into disappearance of error floors in the figure.
V. CONCLUSION
This paper focuses on irregular SC code ensembles. By searching the code space via employing the DE technique, SC ensembles with the highest asymptotic coding thresholds over the BEC are found and listed. It is shown that their coding thresholds is higher than that of (3,6)-regular SC ensembles and the gap between their asymptotic thresholds and the Shannon limit gets negligibly small. On the other hand, these SC ensembles have larger check node degrees, which consequently results in formation of short cycles and stopping sets inside their parity-check matrices. This leads to performance degradation and error floors on the finite-length error analysis. Thus, we search for the best SC ensemble among all possible ensembles with a fixed check node degree, which is lower than the degree of aforementioned ensembles. For a more comprehensive understanding of their error performance, BER of the latter SC ensembles with ms = 1 over the AWGN channel are also evaluated via computer simulations and it has been shown that their finite-length error performance is also better than the best (3,6)-regular SC code of single memory.
