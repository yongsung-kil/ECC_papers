Advanced Error Prediction LDPC for High-Speed
Reliable TLC NAND-based SSDs
Tsukasa Tokutomi1, Shuhei Tanakamaru1,2, Tomoko Ogura Iwasaki1 and Ken Takeuchi1
1Chuo University, Tokyo, Japan, 2University of Tokyo, Tokyo, Japan tokutomi@takeuchi-lab.org

Abstract—Highly reliable solid-state drives (SSDs) with triplelevel-cell (TLC) NAND flash and Advanced Error-Prediction Low-Density Parity-Check (AEP-LDPC) are proposed. To increase NAND flash’s capacity, bits/cell have been doubled and tripled, which causes reliability to drastically degrade due to narrower VTH margins. Previously proposed Error-Prediction LDPC (EP-LDPC) error-correcting code (ECC) improved reliability for Multi-Level-Cell (MLC) NAND flash [4]. However, in EP-LDPC program disturb is not modeled, so precision is limited, especially in short data retention < 2 days. Here, AEPLDPC is proposed for TLC NAND flash. By considering effects of program disturb, data retention and floating-gate capacitive coupling, the most accurate SSDs can be realized, with high speed read capability. The SSD’s data-retention time increases by more than 12×, decode iterations decrease 57%, and acceptable TLC NAND BER increases by more than 2.8 ×.
Index Terms—Error correcting code (ECC), low density parity check code (LDPC), triple level cell (TLC) NAND flash, memory controller, reliability, solid state drive (SSD)
I. INTRODUCTION
Solid-state drives (SSDs) are fast and durable, but constantly face market pressure to reduce cost. In a typical SSD, several NAND flash chips are managed by a memory controller, which applies software techniques (i.e. wear-leveling, garbage collection) to compensate for technology limitations of NAND flash (i.e. limited write/erase cycles, block erase). ErrorCorrecting Code (ECC) is another major function of the controller. By adding parity bits to the data, ECC algorithms can correct bit errors that occur in the NAND flash. Conventionally, Bose-Chaudhuri-Hocquenghem (BCH) ECC has been used with Multi-Level Cell (MLC) NAND because MLC’s raw Bit Error Rates (BERs) have been acceptably low. However, beyond 20nm, reliability degrades significantly due to stronger neighbor cell coupling/disturb and other interactions [1]. Moreover, the introduction of Triple-Level Cell (TLC) flash [2] substantially reduces cost, but drastically decreases reliability. Therefore, to handle low-cost NAND with high BERs, Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) is considered the most appropriate for next generation ECC [3].
In LDPC, data is iteratively decoded based on additional soft information, also called, log-likelihood rate of error (LLR). In conventional LDPC, shown in Fig. 1(a), this soft information is obtained by extra reads with intermediate references, which provides ideal error correction, but unacceptably long read times. A faster method, ErrorPrediction LDPC (EP-LDPC) was developed for MLC NAND [4] in Fig. 1b. Instead of multiple reads, soft information is stored in error prediction (EP) tables, which are derived from actual measured error rates during various cycling and data

retention conditions. By describing the log-likelihood of error for each VTH state, acceptable BER and chip lifetime is increased. This paper first describes that, although conventional EP-LDPC is effective for MLC NAND, it is not as effective for TLC NAND. Because VTH differences of programmed states are smaller in TLC NAND compared to MLC NAND, TLC NAND has higher error rates and is more sensitive to program disturb errors. Therefore, this paper proposes Advanced ErrorPrediction LDPC (AEP-LDPC) in Fig. 1c, to correct errors caused by program disturb, data retention, as well as floatinggate capacitive coupling. The advantage of AEP-LDPC is, by precisely describing error phenomena, errors correction is more accurate and efficient, with less speed, power or cost overhead.
Table I compares conventional soft-decoding LDPC, LDPC-with-BER-only, EP-LDPC, and the proposed AEPLDPC. In TLC, 3 pages are stored in 1 physical page and there are 8 possible VTH states per cell. Page to VTH states mapping is given in Fig. 2 [5]. In Fig. 1a’s conventional LDPC, soft information from 49 Vref’s, requires 49 reads [6]. In contrast, the other three LDPC methods use only 7 reads, and likelihood of error is estimated for each VTH level based on measured error rates, depending on write/erase cycles and retention time. Furthermore, in EP-LDPC and AEP-LDPC, inter-cell coupling effects are included in cell error rates (CERs) to improve error correction precision. A major improvement of the proposed AEP-LDPC over EP-LDPC is that program disturb effects are also modeled. Generally, VTH with data retention over time, whereas in program disturb, VTH increases during program. When both program disturb and data retention are modeled, performance and short-term (<2 days) reliability is improved.
II. ADVANCED ERROR-PREDICTION (AEP)-LDPC SCHEME
The proposed AEP-LDPC architecture is shown in Fig. 3. During program, data from the host is encoded by the LDPC encoder. Also, cells associated with the 2nd page, the lowest 3 states NcellA_initial, and the highest state N cellB_initial, are counted and these numbers are added to the user data. It is expected that these counts will change with cycling, retention and disturb.
Next, read is described (inside dashed line of Fig. 3), and 3 new units are proposed. In AEP-LDPC, first, retention time is estimated in Unit 1. Unit 2 handles short retention cases. Unit 3 calculates CER parameters for the LDPC decoder, by accessing appropriate EP tables based on short/long retention, endurance cycles, and cell coupling. These CER parameter to the LDPC algorithm provide information about which bits are “most likely” to be errors. In LDPC read, decode is iterative, which means that data variations may be tried several times (Decodeiter) before the original data is successfully recovered.

A key technique of this work is the calculation of CER parameters to the LDPC decoder (Unit 3). In Fig 4, the proposed AEP-LDPC is compared to the conventional EPLDPC. In both methods, the total error rate is divided into individual cell error rates, CERs, and VTH is assumed to shift by only 1 state. However, EP-LDPC assumes that VTH can shift only downwards due to data retention. In reality, immediately after program, the effect of program disturb is stronger than data retention, and VTH is shifted upwards. Therefore, for better accuracy, AEP-LDPC separates data retention into 2 regions, long (when data retention is dominant) and short (which includes both data retention and program disturb possibilities).
In order to estimate the data’s retention time TRetention, BER2nd_page is calculated by re-counting the 2nd page cells and comparing to the initial counts. In Fig. 5a, retention of BER2nd_page is shown, and short/long term effects can be observed, when the BER2nd_page is below/above the Day0 value, respectively. Therefore, in Fig. 5b, if BER2nd_page > BER2nd_page_Day0, the data is considered to be in long term retention and subsequent calculations follow the conventional EP-LDPC method. If BER2nd_page < BER2nd_page_Day0, the short term retention calculation method is used (Unit 2).
More details about BER2nd_page calculation formula and program disturb and retention effects are given in Fig. 6. Since the 2nd-page is set by 2 references, BER2nd_page is calculated by summing the shifted cells in both NcellA and NcellB. Immediately after program, program disturb’s tendency to increase VTH is dominant, and retention effect is negligible. Over time, with data retention, VTH shifts towards lower VTH states, so Ncell_A will increase and Ncell_B will decrease. But during short term retention, when program disturb and retention push VTH in opposing directions, the BER2nd_page calculation formula will underestimate actual errors.
After calculating BER2nd_page, BERTotal, can be estimated, as shown in Fig. 7. In short retention, BERTotal uses the Day0 value. Otherwise, BERs of the other 2 pages are estimated from retention measurements. Fig. 8 shows the relationships of BER3rd_page/BER2nd_page (α) and BER3rd_page / BER2nd_page (β). In Fig. 9, it can be seen that using BERTotal_Day0 as the minimum error rate during short term retention (instead of calculated BERTotal) more closely matches the measured BER.
After BERTotal is estimated, CERs, to include inter-cell coupling, are estimated from EP-tables, which stores cell error ratios for different neighbor cell VTH’s, as illustrated in Fig. 10. These adjustments improves accuracy (compared to LDPCwith-BER-only), because coupling of high neighbor VTH’s can increase the target-cell VTH, which reduces data retention error.
III. MEASUREMENT RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
Fig. 11 shows measured error correction results of conventional BCH ECC, conventional EP-LDPC, and the proposed AEP-LDPC for a code rate of 2/3. Several write/erase NW/E cycles are measured, between 0k to 6k. In Figs. 11b and 11c, Decodeiter shows the iterations needed for successful decode. Clearly, EP-LDPC is more reliable than BCH, but as the cycles increase to 4-6k, errors become uncorrectable. For 6k cycles, AEP-LDPC corrects short retention error more

effectively, due more accurate modeling of VTH shifting up as well as down.
Fig. 12a shows the measured dependence of Decodeiter iterations on data-retention time after 0.5k cycles. Since the number of errors is relatively low in the low cycles and short retention time condition, both EP- and AEP- LDPC decoding methods correct errors within a similar number of iterations. In Fig. 12b, when cycling is increased to 4k, more errors occur during short term retention and the advantage of AEP-LDPC over EP-LDPC is clearer. Maximum decode iterations can be reduced 57%. In long retention times, AEP-LDPC also corrects errors with fewer decode iterations.
Fig. 13a shows AEP-LDPC’s measured improvement of the acceptable data-retention time (the longest time for which ECC decoding is successful) is extended as much as 12× and 3.4×, compared with conv. BCH and LDPC-with-BER-only, respectively. Fig. 13b shows the corresponding acceptable BER, defined as the highest BER with successful ECC decoding. Here, acceptable BER is increased by 2.8× and 1.8× compared with conv. BCH and LDPC-with-BER-only, respectively.
Finally, Fig. 14 shows acceptable BER vs. decode iterations. For less than 8 iterations, AEP-LDPC’s decoding capability is lower than that of BCH, but for more than 8 iterations, AEPLDPC is better than BCH. To evaluate the overhead from iterative decoding, AEP-LDPC decoding time is calculated to be about 8-32μs, assuming a decoding throughput of 10Gbps [7]. Considering TLC-NAND’s access time of 100µs [8] and conventional LDPC’s 49x read requirement (4.9ms), AEPLDPC’s decoding overhead is very reasonable.
IV. CONCLUSION
The key features of AEP-LDPC ECC for TLC NAND in SSDs are summarized in Table II. Compared to conv. BCH and LDPC-with-BER-only, data-retention time is extended by 12× and 3.4×, respectively. Similarly, acceptable BER is increased by 2.8×and 1.8×. Furthermore, compared to EP-LDPC, short retention error correction is provided, and maximum decode iterations is reduced 57%. Thus, a highly reliable SSD can be realized with low-cost TLC NAND flash memory.
