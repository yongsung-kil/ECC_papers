Introduction and Motivation

пВІ The exponential internet traffic growth projections place enormous transmission rate
demand on the underlying information infrastructure at every level, from the core to access networks.
пВІ The 40/100 GbE standard has been adopted recently (IEEE 802.3ba), and the
deployment of 100GbE has been underway at an accelerated pace.
пВІ To meet the ever-increasing capacity demands, 1 TbE rates and beyond (e.g., 4
TbE and 10 TbE) are expected to be standardized in the near future.
пВІ Terabit Optical Ethernet (TOE) will be affected not only by limited bandwidth of
information-infrastructure, but also by its high energy consumption and heterogeneity of optical networks.
пВІ The key enabling technologies for the next generation optical networking
described in this tutorial include advanced FEC (binary/nonbinary regular/irregular LDPC coding), adaptive multilevel/multidimensional coded modulation, and turbo equalization. Additionally, optimum signal constellation design and optimized mapping rules are discussed.
пВІ Here we also describe the hybrid multidimensional coded modulation that
advocates the use of all available degrees of freedom for conveyance of the information over few-mode fibers (FMFs), few-core fibers (FCFs) or few-core-fewmode fibers (FCFMFs) in order to solve the problems listed above simultaneously.

MacKay Codes

вАҐ

Following MacKay (1999) below are listed several ways to generate the sparse

matrices in order of increasing algorithm complexity (not necessarily improved

performance)

1. H-matrix is generated by starting from an all-zero matrix and randomly inverting
wc (not necessarily distinct bits) in each column. The resulting LDPC codes is an irregular code.

2. H-matrix is generated by randomly creating weight-wc columns.
3. H-matrix is generated with weight-wc columns and uniform row weight (as near as possible).

4. H-matrix is generated with weight-wc columns, weight-wr rows, and no two columns having overlap larger than one.

5. H-matrix is generated as in (4), and short cycles are avoided.

6. H-matrix is generated as in (5), and can be represented H=[H1|H2], where H2 is invertible or at least has a full rank.

for i = 0 to p-1 do for each b = 0 to c-1 do - Read r data values from MEM B located in the range [b * p * r + i * r, b * p * r + i * r + r - 1] - Sum them up and store the sum - Update MEM E at location (b * p + i) for each k = 0 to r-1 do - Subtract from the computed sum the value located at (b * p * r + i * r + k - 1) in MEM B - Use this value to update MEM C at location (k * p + ((p - k * b) mod p)) * c + b) end end
end
вАҐ Assuming that BPEs and CPEs can access their data simultaneously,
performing an iteration of the decoding algorithm takes 2p clock cycles.
вАҐCompared to a fully-serial implementation that performs (r + c) p clock
cycles per iteration, the improvement in latency is significant.

Multidimensional Signal Constellation Design
The D-dimensional optimum signal constellation design (DD-OSCD) algorithm:
Step 0 (Initialization). Choose arbitrary auxiliary input distribution. Choose DD-PAM for initial points and set the size of this constellation to M.
Step 1. Apply the Arimoto-Blahut algorithm to determine optimum source distribution.
Step 2. Generate sufficiently long D-dimensional training sequences from optimum source distribution. Group the samples from this sequence into M D-dimensional clusters. The membership to the cluster is decided based on Euclidean distance squared of sample point and signal constellation points from previous iteration. Each sample point is assigned to the cluster with smallest Euclidean distance squared.
Step 3. Determine the new constellation points as the center of mass for each Ddimensional cluster.
Repeat the steps 1-3 until convergence.

Concluding Remarks

вАҐ QC-LDPC codes and their related families including convolutional, spatiallycoupled, staircase and irregular LDPC codes are described.

вАҐ LDPC code design and iterative decoding algorithms are provided.
вАҐ The code rate adaptation is performed by partial reconfiguration of decoder by varying the number of employed block-rows. Another alternative to change code rate is to use the same regular parity-check matrix as template but employ the PBDs corresponding to different code rates.

вАҐ FPGA implementation of QC-LDPC codes is discussed.

вАҐ NB irregular QC-LDPC code design from PBDs provides the record coding gains.

вАҐ For instance, the NCG for 27% overhead irregular NB-LDPC code (of length 27286) is 12.4пА†dB (at BER of 10-15 ).

вАҐ We describe both 2D and multidimensional signal constellation design approaches and corresponding mapping rules.

вАҐ We describe rate-adaptive LDPC-coded modulation and nonbinary LDPC-coded modulation.
вАҐ We discussed hybrid multidimensional coded-modulation. пВІ This hybrid multidimensional CM employs both electrical and optical degrees of freedom to simultaneously address the key constraints in next generation optical transmission systems and networks (limited bandwidth of information infrastructure, high energy consumption, and heterogeneity of optical networks). пВІ In this scheme, the orthogonal prolate spheroidal wave functions have been used as the electrical basis functions, while polarization and spatial modes have been used as the optical basis functions. пВІ The hybrid CM scheme represents an enabler for ultra-high speed bit rates exceeding 1пА†Pb/s. пВІ The hybrid CM scheme is very flexible, as it can be used in various strategies ranging from pure multiplexing to full 4MN-dimensional signaling.
вАҐ We discussed LDPC-coded turbo equalization.
вАҐ We describe an rate-adaptive 4D coded-modulation.
