Influences of Inaccurate Estimation of Noise Variance in SumProduct Algorithm for DVB-T2 Receiver
Shingchern D. You and Shun-Jie Huang National Taipei University of Technology, Taipei Taiwan

Abstract—The low-density parity check decoder in the digital video broadcasting-second generation terrestrial (DVB-T2) receiver usually uses the sum-product algorithm for better bit error performance. However, the algorithm requires estimating noise variance, which is computationally expensive. In this paper, influences of inaccurate estimation of variance are investigated. Based on the simulation results, it is confirmed that a constant SNR may be used in the algorithm with acceptable performance.
I. INTRODUCTION
After the first generation of digital video broadcastingterrestrial (DVB-T) [1], the second generation of DVB-T, known as DVB-T2 [2] has also been standardized. The DVBT2 system uses several advanced techniques to allow for a higher transmission rate within the bandwidth of an analog TV channel. Owning to its higher transmission rate, the DVB-T2 broadcasting is preferable to DVB-T. Currently, the DVB-T2 services are available in several European countries. In Taiwan, the government plans to release new licenses for DVB-T2 broadcasting. Therefore, it is expected that DVB-T2 services will become more and more popular soon.
In terms of error control codes, the DVB-T2 system includes low-density parity check (LDPC) and BCH (Bose, Ray-Chaudhuri, and Hocquenghem) codes. For better performance, the LDPC code is usually decoded using an iterative algorithm, such as the sum-product algorithm (SPA) [3]. The first step of the algorithm is to find the log-likelihood ratio (LLR) of each incoming bit. However, the value of LLR is a function of the received noise variance. Therefore, the receiver has to estimate this parameter before conducting decoding. Unfortunately, performing good estimation for noise variance usually involves a complicated procedure, including computing a covariance matrix [4]. Furthermore, the estimation step has to be periodically performed as the environment may change. In terms of implementation, this step is computationally “expensive.” Therefore, it is highly beneficial to remove this step from decoding.
To do so, it is necessary to firstly investigate the impact of applying inaccurate estimation of noise variance on the error correction capability. With the results, the following question can be answered: whether it is possible to use a constant (noise) variance for different SNR conditions, whereas the error correction capability of the SPA is reasonably close to the exact noise variance.

According to Fig. 2 and Fig. 3, if SNRR > SNRT, then poor BER performance may result. On the other hand, if choosing

SNRT > SNRR, the BER degradation is minor. Therefore, it is feasible to use the smallest possible SNRR for all SNR cases. As SNRT = 5.4 dB is the smallest practical value in the experiments, it is reasonable to set SNRR = 5.4 dB. Note that a value smaller than 5.4 dB, say 5.0 dB, can also be used, but
with slightly lower BER performance, as also shown in Fig. 3. Furthermore, if the LLR value is to be obtained by a look-up
table in a hardware decoder, using a fixed SNRR reduces the number of tables to one. Thus, the hardware cost is reduced.

V. CONCLUSIONS
The experimental results show that it is better to underestimate the actual SNR than to over-estimate it. With this observation, a hardware receiver may use a constant value for all SNR cases to save the cost of SNR estimation circuit, and the cost of maintaining multiple SNR to LLR tables. The BER degradation due to such an arrangement is acceptable.
