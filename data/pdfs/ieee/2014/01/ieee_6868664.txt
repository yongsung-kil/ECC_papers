Flexible Multistandard FEC Processor Design With ASIP Methodology

Zhenzhi Wu, Dake Liu
Department of Electrical Engineering Linköping University
Linköping, SE-58 183, Sweden E-mail: zhenzhi.wu@liu.se, dake@isy.liu.se

Abstract—Designing decoder for forward error correction (FEC) is more and more challenging because of the requirements on simultaneous supporting of various wireless standards within one IC module. The flexibility, silicon cost and throughput efficiency are all necessary to be traded off. In this paper, by using ASIP methodology, software-hardware co-design is introduced to offer sufficient flexibility of FEC decoding. The decoding procedure can be programmable for decoding QCLDPC, Turbo and Convolutional Codes. Firstly, the common features from all mentioned algorithms and their corresponding datapaths are analyzed and a unified multi-standard datapath is introduced. Based on it, an application specific instruction-set is proposed and an ASIP (Application Specific Instruction-set Processor) for the FEC algorithms is designed. The firmware FEC codes are developed to adapt to standards. Synthesis results show that the proposed FEC processor is 1.54mm2 under 65nm CMOS process. It offers QC-LDPC decoding for WiMAX, Turbo decoding for 3GPP-LTE, and 64 states Convolutional code (CC) decoding at the throughput of 193 Mbps, 62 Mbps and 60 Mbps respectively under clock frequency of 200 MHz. The proposed ASIP provides programmable high throughput compared to other tri-mode hardware modules.
Index Terms—ASIP, Programmability, FEC, SDR.
I. INTRODUCTION
As multi-standard support has been an essential criterion for mobile devices nowadays, multi-standard FEC is in demand. The simple integration of multiple independent FEC modules without hardware reuse would cost too much silicon area and ends up to high chip price. In addition, ASIC (Application Specific Integrated Circuit) designs lack of flexibility, and a small change in the decoding parameters or routine may leads to the silicon redesign with high NRE (Nonrecurring engineering) costs. SDR (Software Defined Radio) is a promising technology for solving the problems. However, computation intensive FEC decoding can hardly meet the throughput requirement on the general purpose processor architectures. ASIP, on the other hand, is a promising solution to meet the requirements of high-performance, sufficient flexibility and low power consumption at the same time in a pre-defined scope of applications [1]. Different from general purpose processors, the instruction set and hardware architecture are specially optimized for the application.

There are several multi-standard proposals available in the literature review. Sun [2] proposed a flexible LDPC/Turbo duo-mode Soft-In-Soft-Output (SISO) decoder architecture with re-usable datapath. A multiple Flex-SISO parallel architecture is introduced for high-throughput decoding. It attains a high throughput and highly resource reuse. IMEC [3] proposed a multiple SIMD architecture with hierarchical multiple memory banks, which reaches high throughput and relatively low area cost. Carlo Condo [4, 5] introduced a multistandard decoder under network-on-chip architecture which is proven to be flexible enough to support most of standards related. Milestone researches listed above all dedicates for LDPC/Turbo duo-mode case. However both WiMax and WiFi require high-speed convolutional code (CC) decoding as well.

There are also two Tri-mode decoders available. FlexiChaP [6] obtains a high LDPC decoding rate and reaches limited throughput for Turbo decoding. RECFEC [7] also mentioned a 64 processor elements array architecture whereas no Synthesis result such as area cost is given in their research. Partly inspired by referenced above, we will introduce a tri-mode ASIP for FEC decoding.

The paper is organized as following. An ASIP designing flow for FEC application is proposed in section II. The supporting scope is discussed in section III. The decoding algorithms are selected and fine-tuned to our ASIP, which is discussed in section IV. In Section V, the re-usable SISO (Softin-soft-output) datapath for three algorithms is introduced. The top-level architecture is proposed in section VI. In section VII and section VIII, the ASIP instruction-set is introduced and the kernel codes for FEC decoding is shown. In section IX, the synthesis results are provided and the area cost as well as throughput are compared with all other proposals.

II. ASIP DESIGNING FLOW FOR PROGRAMMABLE FEC
We propose a methodology for designing FEC-ASIP. The scope of FEC (including QC-LDPC, CC and Turbo), throughput, acceptable silicon area cost are fixed firstly. Then general-purpose algorithms which can be fine-tuned into a multiplexed datapath are selected. After that, common parts among algorithms are abstracted and implemented by shared functional blocks. The control signals and address information for the decoding routines are represented by only a group of controlling indications, and from that the instruction-set can be defined. The instruction-set is benchmarked and fine-tuned by running algorithm kernel codes on the behavior simulator. Based on the specified function blocks and the instruction-set, the HW RTL description is developed. The correctness and performance are finally verified for both the functional design and silicon layout. On the other hand, the firmware code offers the supporting of all current wireless standards covered by the scope defined previously. The software and hardware are integrated finally, and an FEC-ASIP is designed. Our design flow is depicted in Fig 1.
The ASIP-FEC differs from a general purpose processor platform with FEC decoding program running on it. In that case the instruction-set is not optimized, and accordingly the throughput and the efficiency are limited. Besides, FEC-ASIP offers a programmable control path for configuring and scheduling the decoding procedure, and both the parameters as well as decoding flow can be redesigned by software. Therefore the life time of the FEC silicon design can be extended.
III. SPECIFICATION OF SUPPORTED SCOPE
For an application specific (referred to FEC here) processor, the supporting scope should be specified first and several key parameters including maximum throughput, maximum code size are considered. More efficient architecture is achieved through the setting of the scope. In this work, Quasi-Cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) is considered, because it is widely applied in wireless standards such as 802.16e and 802.11n. The 8-state PCCC (Parallel Concatenated Convolutional Code) Turbo is included because it is applied in several 3G/4G wireless standards including Universal Mobile Telecommunications System (UMTS) and 3GPP Long Term Evolution (LTE). Convolutional codes with constraint length from 5 to 9 is included due to its widely applications in many standards. Based on this specified scope, a set of algorithms which have high throughput and flexibility are considered.
When the Quasi-Cyclic attribute is introduced, at least a minimum parallel degree of Z can be obtained when the submatries size is Z×Z, and Z is designed to be 24-96 for commercial wireless standards, including 802.11ac, 802.11n and 802.16e.
The Parallel Layered Decoding Belief Passing (PLDBP) [10] has many implementation alternatives which varies on the methods of updating the check node, efficient candidates includes Bahl-Cocke-Jelinek-Raviv ( BCJR) (or TDMP) [11], min-sum, offset-min-sum [12] and min-submin [13].

For Turbo decoding, BCJR based log-MAP [14] algorithm is adopted by most of the Turbo decoding implementations [15-17], where Max-log-MAP is considered to be more efficient where the correction factor is given up, and thus suffering about 0.5dB performance loss at 10-5 BER [18]. Although Soft-Output Viterbi-Algorithm (SOVA) is acceptable also for Turbo decoding, it can only attain the same or slightly worse performance comparing to max-log-MAP [18, 19]. Better performance can also be reached by using compensate factor Look-Up Table (LUT). In this work, Max-log-MAP is adopted after trading off between the performance and complexity. In another aspect, code segmentation and sliding window is adopted to increase the throughput and reduce the memory size. When segmentation is applied, several solutions are presented in order to compensate the performance loss caused by the lack of the border value for forward-backward recursion. The NII (Next Iteration Initialization) algorithm [20] is more efficiency in hardware implementation. However, it suffers slightly performance loss which can be mitigated by more iterations.
For CC decoding, MAP structure discussed above for Turbo decoding can be re-used [21]. However, soft-input-hardoutput Viterbi algorithm is more efficient than MAP and suffers no performance loss when only hard decoded bits are demanded. In such case, only the traceback bit is recorded and the traceback procedure is more efficient than that of LLR calculation under large trellis structure. Thus we adopt hard output Viterbi algorithm. The received bits are split into several segments and decoded in the similar way to Turbo decoding.
To sum up, recursion decoding is adopted both for Viterbi and Turbo BCJR algorithm. For LDPC decoding, parallel TDMP (BCJR) is more preferred for its better reuse of Turbo decoding hardware. However, offset-min-sum based Check Node Update (CNU) method [21] can be modified to adapt the

D. Unified datapath Based on the data flows discussed above, a unified SISO
module with re-usable datapath is constructed. In forward recursion, the Branch Metric (a-priori message) is calculated inside BM1 sub-module first and then αi is calculated in Func1 sub-module. In backward recursion, βi (trace-back bits) is calculated based on the buffered input messages stored in Input Buffer and buffered αi stored in Alpha Buffer. Due to the similarities of algorithms, module BM1 and BM2 are shared by Viterbi and Turbo BCJR algorithm. Module Func1 and Func2 are shared by three decoding algorithms. There are also possibilities for memory sharing. The Input Buffer for LDPC and Turbo can be integrated into one. Traceback buffer for Viterbi algorithm can be merged with extrinsic message buffer for LDPC mode, named as Inner Buffer. The Alpha Buffer for Turbo and LDPC mode and the Path Metric Buffer in Viterbi mode can be merged as well. Fig 5 depicts a unified datapath for multi-mode FEC decoding.
V. TOP-LEVEL ARCHITECTURE OF FEC-ASIP
The top-level of FEC-ASIP shown in Fig 6 can be divided into three sub-systems: Control Unit, Datapath and Memory Subsystems. Datapath consists of N SISOs discussed in last section, where the number of SISO (N) is decided by the required throughput. For LDPC mode, Z parallel degree is processed in N SISOs, and each SISO processes 8 sub-layers. While performing Turbo decoding algorithm, received code is

split into N SISOs and is processed in parallel. While performing Viterbi decoding, the received code is also split into N segments with an overlapped area equaling to the traceback length, which guarantees the traceback for each SISO obtaining accurate steady state value to decode the bits close to tail. The SISOs are connected with each other by register buffers which only serve border NII value for Turbo.
The memory subsystems contain memory banks for storing channel messages, a-posteriori messages as well as output decoded LLR or decoded bits. In order to supply enough memory bandwidth, at least 8N bandwidth should be guaranteed following the LDPC decoding bandwidth requirement. Because outputs of memory banks need to be reordered for parallel Turbo decoding or cyclic-shifted for QCLDPC decoding, a permutation network is applied. There are also local buffers within each SISO mentioned in the last section.
The control unit is specifically designed to fit the FEC decoding needs, which consists of a PC counter, a program memory, an instruction fetch unit, a decoding unit, a memory address generator and interleaving look-up tables. The ASIP is initialized by the DMA. The decoded control signals are pipelined and dispatched. Address generator generates linear forward and backward addresses as well as control signals needed for the multiplexers in datapath. The interleaver is a group of LUTs supplying interleaving addresses and permutation routing addresses based on linear address.
The processor is designed to process signed 8-bit quantized data which are enough for processing these three algorithms. Part of the datapath has a data width more than 8-bits including ADD5, ADD6 and ‘maxe’ for Turbo extrinsic message calculation.
VI. ASIP INSTRUCTION-SET DESIGN
It has been observed that Turbo BCJR decoding, QC-LDPC layered decoding and Viterbi decoding algorithm share a common Forward-Backward recursion (FB-recursion) procedure, which can be controlled through several signals: alpha index, beta index, alpha enable and beta enable. When employing sliding window, the window index for alpha and beta are also needed. The FEC instruction is specified with index update signals and enable signals, as shown in TABLE I.
Compared to general purpose processors, where the address for each operand should be specified in each instruction, addressing for FB-recursion in our processor can be more simplified. Only the starting address and increasing/decreasing indication for the recursion is needed. Therefore, in the FEC decoding instructions, only two bits are used for updating addresses, and the code size can thus be minimized.
To support sliding window algorithm, a two-bit flag ‘br’ is introduced to represent the reaching of a sliding window border or a parallel degree border during recursions. In order to distinguish special trellis steps, a single bit flag ‘le’ denotes the last iteration of Turbo where a-posteriori L instead of extrinsic value Le is the output; it is also used for distinguishing the first iteration of LDPC from others where extrinsic value should be set to zero.

The instruction is executed once in a clock cycle. In forward recursion, ‘as’ is set to 1 to indicate the incremental addressing. When the address pointer reaches the border of the sliding window, ‘aw’ is set to 1 and ‘as’ is set to 0. ‘bw’ follows similar rule.
In most cases, an FB-recursion can be described as one FEC instruction iteratively executed for many times. Instructions executed in a repeating manner could minimize both the code size and the execution time. Therefore a repeating domain is introduced and coded into the instruction to support the iterative operations. When ‘repcnt’ is not zero, the instruction will iterate ‘repcnt’ times. For some cases such as LDPC decoding or turbo decoding, sliding window as well as decoding iterations form a nested loop which need future hardware acceleration. In such cases a hardware loop controller is needed to handle branching operation jumping to the preconfigured addresses. The branch flags are thus integrated into the instructions denoted as ‘c0’,’c1’ and ‘c2’ and the control of three loops in nested form can be supported.

VII. ASSEMBLY CODES AND RE-DESIGNING CAPABILITY
While executing of the ASIP instructions, the decoding procedure is conducted by software.
For Turbo BCJR decoding, the FB-recursion within a sliding window is performed by repeat instructions. The loop for a sliding window and its iterations are executed under the control of two hardware loop controllers. In each loop, the FEC instructions with FB-recursion signals are executed cycle by cycle. The code for the first trellis step of MAP (Maximum A Posteriori) decoding named as MAP1 and second step named as MAP2 are similar and therefore only MAP1 part is shown in Code I. The interleaving addresses are stored in 8-LUTs (four for forward and four for backward) and parallel accessed.
The implementation of QC-LDPC layered decoding algorithm has the similar nested loop structures. The outer loop is introduced to perform several decoding iterations, and the inner loop in traversal executes each layer (row) in the QCLDPC base matrix. Within each layer, non-zero elements are connected as a chain and therefore TDMP algorithm is continually performed. The implementation is shown in Code II. The Base Matrix is reordered to reduce the memory conflicts and stored in an LUT.
For Viterbi algorithm, ACS is the kernel sub routine performed by VITACS instruction with repeat factor, an iterate counter. The states for a trellis time step are processed by multiple clock cycles controlled by the instruction repeat domain. The trellis steps are sequentially computed in the inner loop. After it reaches the end of a sliding window, the traceback procedure is started by running VITTB instruction. The recursion is in backward direction with one step one cycle,

In our solution, the decoding routine is programmable and can be rewritten further to possibly meet the demands of new scenarios. Such as extra pipeline conflicts exist in decoding of a new standard which have not been considered before. The software can be modified with adding extra NOPs into code with easy, whereas traditional ASIC with fixed decoding flow is hard to change and therefore failed in supporting it.
We also developed an assembler dedicated for the FECASIP, translating the assembly code into machine code. The

machine code is stored in an 8kb instruction memory, which supports up to 256 lines of code. A Matlab algorithm model is implemented for verification golden vector generation. For checking the conflict of pipeline and instruction correctness, a cycle accurate simulator was developed using C++. It guarantees the instruction to be well executed without datadependency induced errors. The BER performance is obtained from the simulator as well. Null operation instructions are inserted when conflicts are detected through simulations. It has been tested that for most cases the pipeline has high or even full efficiency.

We compared throughput and silicon cost results with other multi-mode FEC implementations. Note that our proposal cost more resource to support Viterbi algorithm which is not covered in most of other proposals. The memory banks are not highly optimized for dedicated standards in order to obtain more compatibility for future standards. A multi-bank LUT for Turbo interleaver is employed for supporting arbitrary interleaving pattern. Considering all of them, the area cost as well as normalized throughput is competitive among all multi-standard FEC decoder proposals. It is shown that when considering supporting three decoding modes, our solution provides the highest Turbo decoding throughput and acceptable LDPC/CC decoding throughput.

IX. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we introduced a high throughput flexible FEC processor. Following the ASIP designing flow, we implemented a unified FB-recursion decoding procedure for QC-LDPC, Turbo and Convolutional codes. All the decoding procedure can be programmed by software. Through trading off the cost and flexibility, the ASIP decoding efficiency and area efficiency are guaranteed by our re-usable datapath and our parallel architecture. Case study reveals that the processor provides ASIC-like performance as well as programmable flexibility for FEC applications.
