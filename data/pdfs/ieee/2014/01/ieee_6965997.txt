Abstract—In this paper we investigate the highly bandwidthefficient Faster-than-Nyquist (FTN) signaling scheme under high order modulations. The FTN system is an emerging technology which has drawn attention in the contemporary spectrum-saving communication environment. Since FTN traditionally achieves high bandwidth efficiency through increased baud-rate, binary modulation is assumed in most research of FTN. The contribution of this paper lies in the extension into high order modulations and its assessment. This enables the communication systems to achieve higher data rates for the same bandwidth and receiver complexity than binary Nyquist signaling systems. Moreover, it is shown that an additional efficiency gain can be achieved by replacing the LDPC codes from the DVB-S2 standard by new optimized quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes whose parameters are matched with FTN signaling.
Index Terms—FTN, Faster-than-Nyquist, high order modulations

I. INTRODUCTION

Recently the 5G network, ultra high definition content delivery, and other technologies emerging on the horizon, have aroused intense interest in more sophisticated and efficient data transmission technologies than what is used today.
In this paper, we deal with a highly bandwidth-efficient communication method which is called FTN (Faster than Nyquist) signaling. By bandwidth efficiency, we mean an increase of the data transfer rate while simultaneously preserving the frequency bandwidth occupied by the transmitted signal. FTN was pioneered in the mid-70s by Mazo with his milestone publication [1], but interest for FTN grew only recently. A recent survey of FTN is available in [2] but we provide a short summary of it next.

The equalizing problem for higher order modulation in FTN involves the intricate equalizing and decoding principles and has been rarely exposed. However, with the restriction that the inputs are binary, one must reduce TΔ significantly below the Nyquist limit in order to obtain very high spectral efficiency. This grossly increases the computational complexity, and as we will see later, the heavy ISI so created is not well matched with standard LDPC codes which results in poor overall performance. Therefore, QPSK prohibits FTN to operate at very high spectral efficiencies. Going beyond binary inputs opens up a possibility to still use FTN at high spectral efficiencies with practical receiver complexity. Consider for example 16QAM inputs. In this case, the symbol time TΔ can be twice as large as the symbol time for a system with QPSK inputs. From the perspective of the receiver, the situation is now changed. With 16QAM, the ISI duration is in principle much less than for QPSK, but the amplified cardinality of the modulation generates a challenge. Whether or not the system based on 16QAM, equipped with a complexity constrained receiver, can reach spectral efficiencies beyond those reachable for QPSK, with the same complexity budget at the receiver, is an open question that has not been adequately addressed in the FTN literature.
We approach this problem from a practical point of view, and this paper summarizes our efforts to establish a suitable answer to this question by means of receiver tests of LDPC encoded transceiver systems. The outcome is that with the same LDPC code, FTN systems based on 16QAM can operate at spectral efficiencies that are out of reach for QPSK based systems with similar receiver complexity. Thus, for practical receivers and codes, although FTN achieves spectral efficiencies by reducing TΔ, there is limits to what can be achieved by QPSK, and at some point, the system should switch over to 16QAM. This outcome differs from purely theoretical results, as it is known that with binary inputs, one can reach the Shannon capacity of

the channel [7], rendering higher order modulation unnecessary. It is not surprising that the assumption of ISI existence leads
to new requirements to error-correcting codes used in the corresponding communication system. Matching code parameters with FTN parameters as well as with the order of modulation is an important issue which is not deeply developed yet. In this paper we demonstrate by examples a high potential of optimized [8] LDPC codes combined with FTN signaling. In particular, the bit error rate (BER) performance of belief propagation (BP) decoding of the optimized rate R = 1/2 QC LDPC codes with QPSK and FTN signaling are compared with the same performance of the QPSK-FTN-based system employing the rate R = 1/2 LDPC code from the DVB-S2 standard [9].
II. HIGH ORDER FTN EQUALIZATION/DECODING
A. Factor Graph for Equalizing and Decoding
The transmitter model for high order modulation in FTN systems is shown in Fig. 1. A data source generates binary data symbols which are encoded by a channel encoder. The encoded data are bit-interleaved before mapped to the constellation which generates {am} in (1), and a high order QAM constellation is considered in this paper. The QAM mapper outputs pass through a pulse shaping filter h(t) and the transmitter finally emits the FTN signal (1) for some particular symbol period T .
In the receiving part shown in Fig. 2, the received signal passes through a filter matched to the pulse shape in the transmitter and enters the equalizer block which produces equalized channel values. These are fed to a soft QAM demapper, get bit-interleaved, and finally LDPC decoded. These blocks constitute the iterative demodulation-decoding loop, including a bit-interleaver and a soft QAM mapper.
The iterative decoding process can be effectively visualized by the framework of factor graphs, see, e.g., [10]. The FTN equalizing/decoding process is depicted in the factor graph in Fig.3.

III. NUMERICAL RESULTS
In this section, the performance of the FTN scheme is investigated by computer simulations. The BER performance is presented as a function of Eb/N0 = Es/(N0η), where Es is the received signal energy per received signal, N0 is two-sided noise power spectral density and
η = R log2 M/τ
denotes the spectral efficiency in bits/s/Hz, R is the errorcorrecting code rate and τ = TΔ/T is the relative time period packing. In the simulations for QPSK modulation we compare the BER performance of Nyquist signaling (τ = 1) combined with the rate 3/5, 2/3, 3/4, 4/5, and 5/6 LDPC codes of length 64800 bits from the DVB-S2 standard and FTN signaling combined with the rate R = 1/2 LDPC code from the DVBS2 standard and rate R = 1/2 new LDPC codes obtained by optimization [8]. The time period packing is controlled by 0 < τ < 1, where τ = R log2 M/η is chosen to provide the same η as for Nyquist signaling. For higher order modulation only the standard LDPC codes were used in simulations.
A raised-cosine pulse with roll-off factor 0.4 is used for pulse shaping. The number of iterations for internal LDPC decoding is limited to 30 and the number of global iterations is limited to 5 for QPSK and 3 for 16QAM. The value of L, which directly specifies the complexity of the receiver was chosen to be 4 for both constellation orders. Interesting to note that performance with QPSK is not limited by the choice L = 4, but rather by the constellation size itself. By this we mean the following: for a given code rate and as τ decreases, one obtains a trajectory in the SNR-throughput plane. For different code-rates, one obtains different trajectories, and the designer should then choose the combination of τ and code rate that maximizes the throughput at a given SNR. If the receiver parameter L is chosen too small, certain points are severely damaged; in particular the ones that corresponds to small τ so that the FTN-induced ISI is severe. For QPSK, L = 4 is however sufficient for a wide range of τ that extends beyond the range that we are investigating in this paper. Hence, for QPSK, we gain very little by increasing L beyond 4. This implies that if we can conclude that a certain

A. Simulation Results for QPSK
The simulated BER performance for QPSK is given in Fig. 4 (spectral efficiencies η ∈ [1, 2]). The dashed curves are obtained when using Nyquist signaling with the DVB-S2 LDPC codes of different rates depending on the required η. In other words, τ is set to 1 and the BER curves coincide with the performance of BP decoding for the DVB-S2 LDPC codes. The solid curves are the BER performance for FTN signaling with τ < 1 used with the rate 1/2 DVB-S2 LDPC code, where τ is chosen to provide the same spectral efficiency as that for Nyquist signaling. The dotted curves in Fig.4 show the BER performance for FTN signaling used with the new rate 1/2 QC LDPC codes.
The newly constructed irregular QC LDPC codes are determined by their base matrix of size 16×32 and the corresponding degree matrices [8]. We found that the column degree distribution of the base matrix should depend of the required spectral efficiency, that is, on τ . The rate R = 1/2 QC LDPC codes were optimized for τ = 0.75 and τ = 0.67. The BER performance of the FTN-based system employing these codes was compared with the BER performance of Nyquist signaling combined with the standard LDPC codes of rates R = 2/3 and R = 3/4, respectively. It was compared with the BER performance of FTN-based system with the same values of τ employing the standard rate R = 1/2 LDPC code as well.
FTN signaling combined with the standard rate 1/2 LDPC code gains about 0.5 dB compared to Nyquist signaling with the DVB-S2 standard LDPC codes of the corresponding rates. By combining FTN signaling with the optimized LDPC codes we can win additionally about 0.3 dB. The FTN system performance starts to degrade roughly from τ < 0.52 where it has difficulties in falling down sharply. This is of clear operational meaning, as it implies that in order to reach high spectral efficiency, one must abandon binary inputs and move over to larger modulation orders.
B. Simulation Results for 16QAM
The simulation results for 16QAM are given in Fig. 4 (spectral efficiencies η ∈ [2, 3.33]). The FTN-based system’s performance with 16QAM degrades earlier than that for the system with QPSK. No matter what base code rate is used, the FTN-combined system begins to degrade roughly at τ < 0.8. Intuitively, multi-level equalization is less robust to the channel perturbation which explains the earlier performance saturation compared to the case of QPSK. Although the degradation occurs earlier for 16QAM compared to QPSK, the achieved spectral efficiency is higher for 16QAM. A degradation starting at τ ≈ 0.8 would correspond to a degradation starting at τ ≈ 0.4 for QPSK. However, in Section III-A, we saw that the degradation for QPSK starts already at τ ≈ 0.52. In other words, we may conclude that FTN signaling combined with 16QAM provides higher spectral efficiencies than when combining it

IV. CONCLUSION
In this paper, Faster-than-Nyquist signaling with high order modulation is investigated. The FTN scheme is attracting more and more attention as a potential technology to meet the future communication system requirements. The contribution of this paper lies in the extension into high order modulation in FTN based communication system and its assessment. This opens a pathway for increasing the data rate of present communication systems. Our main finding is that although FTN with binary inputs can in principle operate at any given spectral efficiency, this is not possible in practice due to the ensuing impractical receiver complexity in doing so and since standard codes are not well matched with FTN. A remedy is to switch over to 16QAM, as, for the same receiver complexity and the very same code, the FTN system can operate at spectral efficiencies beyond those reachable by QPSK. The obtained, due to using FTN signaling, gain is about 0.7 dB and the additional gain due to LDPC code optimization is about 0.3 dB.
