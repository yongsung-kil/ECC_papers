2014 8th International Symposium on Turbo Codes and Iterative Information Processing (ISTC)

Variational Message Passing Without Initialization Using A Free Energy Constraint
Murthy V. R. S. Devarakonda Email: murthyds@ieee.org

Abstract‚ÄĒIn iterative algorithms with graphical models, it is common to initialize messages. But this approach is not suitable in certain applications. In this paper, it is conjectured that if some messages are confined to a ball around their initialized values, then initialization can be avoided. Towards this goal, for networks specified with a bipartite graph, a variant of the sum-product algorithm is derived by adding an inequality constraint on the variable free energy in the underlying Bethe optimization problem. In the binary case, this leads to an upper bound that restricts the ‚Ąď1 norm of the extrinsic message (vector) to a ball around its (otherwise) initial value. Results are reported for binary LDPC decoders through simulations which confirm that initialization between decoding attempts can be eliminated and show that the algorithm tends to outperform the standard sum-product. A few more observations and comments on this approach, relation with other variational methods, and applications to analog processing and inference over distributed networks are outlined in the end.
I. INTRODUCTION
In any iterative algorithm, messages or unknown parameters must be initialized; the initial values are either fixed, or obtained from other sources. For a digital implementation this is trivial and does not pose a problem. However, inference on graphical models, with localized computations, has strong connections with electrical networks and analog implementations. The sum-product algorithm arises from the Bethe free energy minimization problem [1] and its relation to analog decoding has been well established [2]; but the need to initialize messages makes the analog designs impractical. One also finds other situations where initialization is infeasible. For example, the exact sampling time of data depends on the output of the inference engine or the arrival times of data are not synchronized with the inference process.
In this paper, we are particularly interested in the computation of a posteriori probabilities of discrete variables whose joint density is induced by a graph. We are interested in an approach that does not require initialization. Moreover, we seek to find an algorithm that does not deviate much from the sum-product algorithm. This approach is motivated both by analog decoders as well as by the observation that the sum-product algorithm arising from the (non-convex) Bethe free energy approximation generally gives better results [3] in many cases. In particular, we focus attention on a factor graph with binary variables and a single variable parametrization of messages using the log-likelihood ratios (LLR). In this case, the initialized message is an all-zero vector. In this paper, we make the reasonable assumption that the dynamics of the algorithm are continuous near the initialization point. That

is, we assume that if the LLR vector of the message (that needs initialization) stays within a ball of bounded radius throughout the iterative process, then message passing without reinitialization should be feasible. However, this may raise a fundamental question whether computation with arbitrary initial messages would give any meaningful result. We approach this problem indirectly by modifying the underlying optimization problem. We derive an algorithm with the required features by constraining some of the (data independent) entropy terms and (data dependent) average energy terms in the Bethe free energy. In other words, we impose an additional ‚Äúinequality‚ÄĚ constraint on some of the free energy terms in the cost function. This only restricts the feasible region to the interior of the set of locally consistent marginals [4] and leaves the cost function unchanged. Using this setup, a new message passing algorithm is proposed that makes use of a Lagrange multiplier to ‚Äúcontrol‚ÄĚ the growth of the extrinsic messages arriving at a variable node. The behavior of the algorithm is studied via simulations for LDPC codes of varying row and column weights of the parity check matrix and different code-rates. Simulation results confirm the conjecture and show that this method performs without reinitialization between decoding attempts and also converges in all the cases studied so far. However, a proof of convergence is not available. It is also observed that the bound on the extrinsic messages admits a sufficiently large range and is tolerant to variations. Finally, the algorithm performs comparably with or slightly better than the standard sum-product message passing algorithm at medium to high SNRs.
The paper is organized as follows. The algorithm is introduced and message passing rules are derived in Sec. II. Sec. III discusses decoder implementation details and simulation results. In Sec. IV the proposed approach is compared with a few variational approaches from the literature and some applications are outlined. The paper is concluded in Sec. V.

IV. DISCUSSION
There are some subtle differences with other approaches that are worth pointing out. The algorithm tends to perform comparably or better than standard sum-product, which uses message initialization. Note that in case of a digital implementation, nothing prevents us from initializing the messages and using a trade-off between performance gain and the number of iterations. It has been noticed that with initialization enabled, the algorithm converges faster and also outperforms standard sum-product by about 0.2dB at as low as 7 iterations (with a corresponding number of iterations used in SPM for fair comparison), and by about 0.5dB at 10 iterations, for the (204, 0.5, 3, 6) LDPC code. We also notice that, in Fig. 4, the algorithm performs better near the error-floor region of two codes (block lengths 204 and 1057) compared to SPM. The examples in this paper are concerned mainly with binary codes. For non-binary variables, it is expected that the ideas in Sec. II(A) can be adapted using a suitable parametrization of messages from an exponential family of distributions. Similarly, for problems other than LDPC codes, the only change comes from (11). How the algorithm performs in these cases is yet to be determined.

Message passing rules with similar features have appeared in literature. In (4) the entropies of the code variables are weighted by ŌČňú. Such weighting of entropies appeared in connection with convex approximations and leads to message passing rules that are quite similar to those in this paper. Examples are tree-reweighted approximations [8], uniformly re-weighted belief propagation [9], and various convex approximations [3]. In all these cases, the message scaling is typically fixed. The present algorithm differs in two aspects. First, it does not explicitly rely on convexification of the cost function. Second, the message scaling is time-varying wherein the parameter ŌČ is dynamically computed and applied. Some of the aforementioned work is concerned with approximations to beliefs or to the partition function. The applicability of the proposed method to these problems needs further work.
Some applications that can benefit from the proposed algorithm are now outlined. (a) Analog Decoding: In earlier work, message passing algorithms with localized computations were shown to be ideally suited for analog decoder implementations [2]. The analog implementations however required elaborate control circuitry to initialize the hidden messages between decoding attempts. The interconnect complexity scales as the number of edges (in the factor graph) in this case. In contrast, the proposed algorithm requires rescaling all the extrinsic messages characterized by only a single parameter (i.e., ŌČ) and the complexity scales only as the number of variables. As the algorithm is noted to be less sensitive to parameter choice, it is expected that the computation in (15) can be be replaced with suitable analog approximations. (b) Joint Timing Synchronization and Decoding: Another advantage is that the sample-and-hold at the decoder inputs can also be eliminated, as long as the input changes relatively slowly. This may be useful in systems that operate entirely in the baseband. In [10], a simple communication receiver is proposed where decoding takes place without knowing the exact sampling time in advance. The receiver demonstrates an aspect of code-aided

synchronization, that pushes the task of timing synchronization to the end of the processing chain. (c) Message Passing for Sensor Networks: In distributed hypothesis testing problems such as fault localization and event detection [11], sensors send local measurements corresponding to a global problem to a data center. It is assumed that the data from each sensor arrive asynchronously and are usually sparse in time. In an ad-hoc network, it may not be feasible for a data center to restart its processing algorithm every time it receives a new measurement. It would be advantageous to perform communication, inference, and sensing simultaneously.
V. CONCLUSIONS
Motivated by the need to avoid message initialization in analog processing, a new class of algorithms is introduced using an inequality constraint in the Bethe variational problem. For binary variables, a variant of sum-product algorithm derived from this leads to an upper bound on the ‚Ąď1 norm of the extrinsic message vector and is conjectured to enable message passing without initialization. Experiments on a few binary LDPC decoders demonstrated that the algorithm functions without initialization and gives good results. However, a proof of convergence, or conditions and parameter choices for which the method always succeeds are not available. The viability of analog decoders using this method is yet to be established. As the treatment of the inequality constraint only assumes strictly positive node beliefs, it is expected that this method can be adapted to non-binary variables and applications other than LDPC decoding.
