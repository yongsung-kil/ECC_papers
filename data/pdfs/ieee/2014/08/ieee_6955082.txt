AbstractŌĆöThe structure of certain subgraphs of the Tanner graph of an LDPC code, the trapping sets, has been identified as important for the error floor performance of iterative decoding algorithms. To investigate such sets requires the parity check matrix of the code to be generated with sufficient structure that allows useful information to be obtained while giving good codes. Structures that have been considered include combinatorial designs and classical finite geometries. More recently other finite geometric notions such as partial geometries and generalized d-gons have been considered with some success. This work considers aspects of this approach.
I. INTRODUCTION
The design of LDPC codes has been a topic of intense investigation over the past two decades and many codes have been designed that come close to achieving the Shannon bound on capacity. The codes used are often very long (several thousand bits or longer) and the iterative decoder requires many iterations to achieve good performance. Some codes exhibit the phenomenon of an an error floor, a region of the probability of error vs SNR (Eb/N0) curve where an increase of the SNR results in a relatively small improvement in the probability of error. This is a major concern for many applications such as coding for memories and high-speed optical communications and has been investigated widely [37]. A consensus has been developed from the work of many authors that suggests certain subgraphs of the Tanner graph of the code are of importance in producing error floors. These are referred to as trapping sets. In particular for good error floor performance there should be no small trapping sets [9], [11], [18], [23]ŌĆō[26], [31], [33], [34], [38], [49].
To investigate trapping sets requires considerable knowledge of the structure of the parity check matrix of the code. To this end a variety of combinatorial structures such as Steiner systems, balanced incomplete block designs, Latin squares and finite Euclidean and projective geometries have been used ( [10], [20], [22], [23], [26], [42], [46], [49], [52], [53]). This work continues in this direction by using partial geometries and generalized d-gons, in particular generalized quadrangles. While these are, in a sense, weaker notions than the combinatorial structures mentioned, they have sufficient properties and a broader range of parameters that allow interesting and useful results to be obtained.
It should be mentioned that many authors have investigated the use of Tanner graphs with large girth [27], [44] with

interesting results. There are examples of codes with very large girth yet have relatively poor error floor performance. Conversely there are codes with modest girth (say 6) and performance within a dB or so of the Shannon capacity. The authors believes that for a code to have good error floor performance it should have good structure, good minimum distance for the given rate and no small trapping sets.
The next section discusses trapping sets and attempts to justify their importance although the authors know of no tight analytic correspondence between error floors and the sizes of trapping sets. The associated notion of absorbing set [12] is also of importance but for reasons of space, not discussed. Section 3 discusses the use of partial geometries for designing parity check matrices and their trapping sets and discusses recent work of the authors on this problem. Section 4 looks at generalized quadrangles in a similar manner. Interest in generalized quadrangles arises from the fact they can be shown to have girth 8 and desirable trapping set sizes.
The debt the coding community owes to Robert Gallager for his remarkable insight that led to LDPC codes [14], [15] and to Michael Tanner [43]ŌĆō[45] whose work contributed so much to the subject is difficult to fully appreciate.
II. TRAPPING SETS OF LDPC CODES
Let G be the Tanner graph of a binary LDPC code, defined as the null space of an m ├Ś n binary matrix. It is assumed that the n vertices on the left of G are the variable nodes (VNŌĆÖs) of the code and the m vertices on the right are the check nodes (CNŌĆÖs). The code is to be decoded via a belief propagation (BP) decoder, such as the sum-product algorithm (SPA) or min-sum algorithm (MSA). The behavior of these algorithms depends on the cycle structure of the code as the iterations of the algorithm attempt to converge to a codeword.

III. PARTIAL GEOMETRIES

The term finite geometries is a rather broad one, encompassing the classical finite projective and Euclidean geometries as well as numerous other notions such as the partial geometries and generalized polygons or d-gons of interest here. All are discussed in terms of geometrical notions of incidence structures of points and lines as well as substructures of interest. An excellent introduction is the reference [4]. The classical text on such objects is that of Dembowski [8]. Many authors have considered such an approach (e.g. [17], [19], [28], [46], [47]).
To introduce the notion of a partial geometry, consider a system composed of a set N of n points and a set M of m lines where each line is a set of points. If a line L contains a point v, we say that v is on L and that L passes through (is incident with) v. If two points are on a line, then we say that the two points are adjacent or collinear and if the two lines pass through the same point, we say that the two lines intersect, otherwise they are parallel. IV. GENERALIZED QUADRANGLES
A generalized quadrangle (GQ) is a special case of a partial geometry with ╬┤ = 1 i.e. they enjoy the particular property in the geometry that if, for a point P that is not incident with the line L, there is a unique point P on L such that P and P are collinear i.e. there is a unique line through P that intersects L at the unique point P . Excellent for references for GQs include [16], [35], [48].
This is quite a strong property and, as a subset of partial geometry, the possible set of parameters for a GQ is somewhat restricted. In turn, they form a subset of the more general generalized polygons. There can be no triangles in such a finite geometric structure, and hence the associated Tanner graph of the point-line incidence matrix will have girth 8. Our aim in considering such an object, apart from being an interesting combinatorial configuration, is to determine if improving the girth to 8 (over the girth 6 partial geometries), will result in codes with better performance. Although these are only a small subset of girth 8 graphs, it is hoped they will shed some light on the matter.
We take the opportunity to give some background to a surprising amount of previous work on codes derived from such objects. Excellent references on finte GQs and d-gons are the books by Payne and Thas [35] and van Maldeghem [48]. We start by defining generalized d-gons (or generalized polygons) of which GQs are a special case (corresponding to d = 4).
Consider a point set N and a line set M which are viewed as the left and the right sets of vertices V of the bipartite graph expressing the incidence relations. A vertex of the graph is called thick if it has degree at least 3 and the generalized dgon is thick if all its vertices are thick. Informally, the relation is called a generalized d-gon if the girth g is twice the diameter d. If all point vertices have degree s + 1 and all line vertices have degree t + 1 the generalized d-gon is said to have order (s, t). The paper of Liu et al [30] contained not only a useful summary of the minimum distance bounds on the various constructions of the generalized quadrangles but established the following two important results:
1) A code whose parity check matrix is the incidence matrix of a generalized d-gon, for n even, has only codewords of even weight.
2) A code whose parity check matrix is the incidence matrix of a generalized quadrangle is quasi-cyclic.
This last point is important for code implementation. Its proof considers the transitivity of the automorphism group of the quadrangle which produces different sized circulant blocks for the different constructions. Expressions for these block sizes are given. It is hoped further work on this aspect will make the constructions easier to implement.
It is noted that the comments on trapping sets given for partial geometries discussed in the previous section are applicable here and two small comments are given to demonstrate the impact of the properties of generalized quadrangles.
Case i): An ovoid of the generalized quadrangle is a set of points such that every line in the quadrangle is incident with a unique point of S. Such ovoids, if they exist for a particular construction, must have 1 + st points. Suppose a set of ╬║ Ōēż 1 + st VNŌĆÖs has the property that each line is incident with at most one point of S. The CNŌĆÖs corresponding to these VNŌĆÖs then is just the set of ╬║(1 + t) lines that intersect the ╬║ VNŌĆÖs. Each such CN has degree 1. Since it is assumed t will be quite large and, in particular t > 3, the ratio ╬║/Žä = 1 + t > 4 and such a set of VNŌĆÖs cannot be a small trapping set.
Case ii) A spread S is a set of lines of the GQ such that each point of the quadrangle is incident with a unique line in S i.e. the lines of S are parallel. By duality if a spread exists it must have 1 + st lines. Suppose the ╬║ VNŌĆÖs are collinear. The corresponding CNŌĆÖs in the subgraph generated by such a configuration will have Žä = t╬║ CNŌĆÖs and for large t does not correspond to a small trapping set. Again each CN has degree 1.
The cases above are extreme but serve as encouragement that the bounds on the sizes of the trapping sets given for partial geometries might be sharpened for the case of GQŌĆÖs. Such an effort is ongoing.

V. CONCLUSION
The work has given an overview of the use of partial
geometries and generalized quadrangles in defining LDPC
codes and shown how their combinatorial structure can be used
to determine information on trapping sets. The work is only
of value if codes with good error probability performance and,
in particular, good error floors result. Current work is being
pursued on code structure for both partial geometries and GQs
as well as simulations to better understand these codes.
