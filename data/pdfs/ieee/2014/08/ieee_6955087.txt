On the Equivalence of the ACE and the EMD of a Cycle for the ACE Spectrum Constrained LDPC Codes
Kuntal Deka, A. Rajesh and P. K. Bora Department of Electronics and Electrical Engineering, Indian Institute of Technology Guwahati, Guwahati – 781039, India.
E-mail: {kuntal, rajesha, prabin}@iitg.ernet.in

Abstract—The Extrinsic Message Degree (EMD) of a cycle in the Tanner graph of a low density parity check (LDPC) code measures the connectivity of the cycle. As it is difficult to calculate the EMD, the Approximate EMD (ACE) is generally used. However, the ACE of a cycle is not always equal to its EMD. This paper presents some sufficient conditions for the equivalence of the ACE and the EMD of a cycle for the ACE spectrum constrained LDPC codes.
Index Terms—Low density parity check (LDPC) codes, cycles, EMD, ACE.
I. INTRODUCTION
For low density parity check (LDPC) codes of medium to long block lengths, the maximum likelihood (ML) decoding is not feasible because of the enormous number of codewords involved. Instead, some sub-optimal iterative decoding algorithms like the sum product algorithm (SPA) [1, 2] are devised. One significant assumption for which the SPA converges to the ML decoding is that the states of all the check nodes (CN) connected to any specified variable node (VN) are independent given the value of the bit corresponding to the VN [3]. Although this assumption holds asymptotically, it becomes invalid for finite-length codes after a certain number of iterations [3]. The reason for this is the presence of cycles in the Tanner graph. In a Tanner graph of girth g, the independence assumption becomes invalid after m number of iterations where m < g/4 < m + 1 [3]. So, the girth of the Tanner graph should be increased in order to delay the violation of the independence assumption.
A large number of construction methods have been proposed to eliminate the harmful small cycles from the Tanner graph. Hu et al proposed the progressive edge-growth (PEG) construction method which progressively assigns the edges to the VNs ensuring the maximization of the local girth at the VNs [4]. Tian et. al [5] have shown that all the cycles of the same length are not equally harmful. It is emphasized that the connectivity of the cycles is also crucial in addition to the length. A new measure of the connectivity of the cycles, the Extrinsic Message Degree (EMD) is proposed. The EMD of a cycle is defined as the number of CNs singly connected to the VNs involved in the cycle. To calculate the EMD of a cycle, one has to examine the subgraph induced by the VNs involved

in the cycle. In order to facilitate an easy calculation of the EMD, the Approximate Cycle EMD (ACE) [5] is devised. The ACE of a cycle C2i of length 2i is given by

II. SUFFICIENT CONDITIONS FOR THE EQUIVALENCE OF THE ACE AND THE EMD OF A CYCLE FOR THE ACE SPECTRUM CONSTRAINED CODES
For a cycle with its ACE not equal to EMD, some of the VNs share one or more CNs outside the cycle. In such a case, the cycle contains some sub-cycles. These sub-cycles have the following two constraints:
1) Girth constraint: The length of each sub-cycle must be greater than or equal to the girth.
2) ACE constraint: The ACE values of the sub-cycles must be greater than or equal to their corresponding ACE constraints.
First, using the girth constraint, a simple sufficient condition for the equivalence of the ACE and EMD of a cycle is proposed as follows.
Theorem 1: For an LDPC code with girth g, any cycle C2i of length 2i has equal ACE and EMD if i < g − 2.
Proof: Since the girth is g, the minimum number of VNs required to be present in any cycle containing some sub-cycles is imin = g/2 + g/2 − 2 = g − 2. Hence, any cycle C2i of length 2i, cannot contain any sub-cycles if i < imin = g − 2. In other words, the ACE and EMD of C2i will be equal if i < g − 2.
It should be noted that the sufficient condition in Theorem 1 is valid for any general LDPC code.
For the ACE constrained codes, the parity check matrices are constructed in such a way that any cycle must contain at least one VN with degree more than two [5]. So, the minimum possible ACE of any cycle for the ACE constrained codes is 1. The following simple theorem says that a cycle with the minimum possible ACE has same ACE and EMD.
Theorem 2: For any cycle C2i of length 2i, ACE (C2i) is equal to the EMD of C2i if ACE (C2i) = 1.
1A cycle is said to contain a sub-cycle if the set of VNs involved in the latter is a subset of those of the former.

Example 1: For an LDPC code of girth 10, all the cycles of length 10, 12 and 14 have equal respective ACE and EMD according to Theorem 1.
Example 2: Consider an ACE spectrum constrained code with the ACE spectrum of the form (∞, ∞, η6, η8, η10). According to Theorem 2, for a cycle C20 of length 20, if ACE (C20) = 1, then the ACE (C20) will be equal to its EMD.
Example 3: For an (∞, ∞, η6, η8, η10) code, all the 6-cycles have equal respective ACE and EMDs according to Theorem 1. According to Theorem 3, for a cycle C8 of length 8, the ACE (C8) will be equal to its EMD if ACE (C8) < η6 and for a cycle C10 of length 10, the ACE (C10) will be equal to its EMD if ACE (C10) < (η6 + η8) /2. Two particular cases are illustrated below.
1) For an (∞, ∞, 13, 9, 4) code, all the cycles of length 8 with the ACE value less than 13, will have equal ACE and EMD. For all the cycles of length 10 having ACE value less than 11 ((13 + 9) /2), the ACE will be equal to the EMD.
2) For an (∞, ∞, 5, 5, 3) code, no cycle of length 8 is guaranteed to have its ACE equal to the EMD. However, for all the cycles of length 10 having the ACE value less than 5, the ACE will be equal to the EMD.

A. Some Possible Applications
Some possible applications of the sufficient condition for the equivalence of the ACE and the EMD of a cycle for ACE spectrum constrained LDPC codes are discussed below.
1) For any cycle with equal ACE and EMD, the CNs connected to the VNs in the cycle but not involved in the cycle will be singly connected to the cycle. The reliability of the messages coming from these CNs can be increased relative to messages coming from the CNs involved in the cycle in order to enhance the benefit of the messages coming from the rest of the Tanner graph. In this way, better connectivity can be ensured for the isolated small cycles and the performance of the iterative decoders can be improved.
2) The high signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) performance of the LDPC codes over an additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel is influenced by a kind of combinatorial substructure present in the parity check matrix known as trapping set (TS) [10, 11]. A TS is designated by a 2parameter notation: (a, b), where a is the number of VNs in the TS and b is the number of odd-degree CNs in the subgraph induced by the VNs. A cycle with equal ACE and EMD can be considered as an equivalent TS as explained in the following. A cycle of length l contains l/2 number of VNs. If the ACE of the cycle, η is equal to the EMD, then the subgraph induced by the l/2 number of VNs contains exactly η number of odd-degree CNs. Hence, the cycle can be treated as an (l/2, η) TS. For example, the 8-cycle with ACE=4 in Figure 1(a) has equal ACE and EMD and thus it can be correctly considered as a (4, 4) TS. However, the 8 cycle in Figure 1(b) cannot be considered as a (4, 4) TS as in this case the ACE is not equal to the EMD. It can be observed from Theorem 2 and Theorem 3 that cycles with low ACE satisfy the sufficient conditions for equal ACE and EMD. It is also obvious that the cycles with low ACE are more detrimental. Moreover, the TSs formed by the cycles satisfying the sufficient conditions for ACE=EMD, are of elementary type [12] as the CNs in the induced subgraph have degree either one or two. As the elementary types of TSs are more detrimental [12], by finding out the cycles with ACE=EMD, a significant number of dominant TSs can be found out.

IV. CONCLUSION
We have derived three sufficient conditions for the equivalence of the ACE and the EMD of a cycle for the ACE spectrum constrained LDPC codes. The first sufficient condition is satisfied when the length of the particular cycle is less than a threshold determined by the girth. The second sufficient condition is a simple one and it says that when the ACE of the cycle is equal to the minimum possible value, i.e. 1, the ACE has to be equal to the EMD. According to the third condition which is the main result of this paper, the ACE of a cycle is guaranteed to be equal to its EMD if it is less than a threshold determined by the ACE constraints of the sub-cycles.
