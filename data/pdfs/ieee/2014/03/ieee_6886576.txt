Staircase Rate-Adaptive LDPC-Coded Modulation for
High-Speed Intelligent Optical Transmission
Yequn Zhang, Ivan B. Djordjevic
University of Arizona, ECE Department, 1230 E. Speedway Blvd. Tucson, AZ 85721, USA yequnz@email.arizona.edu
Abstract: We propose staircase rate-adaptive LDPC-coded modulation that is suitable for highspeed intelligent optical transmission. Compared with shortening of LDPC codes, larger coding gain can be obtained and error floor can also be effectively mitigated.
OCIS codes: (060.0060) Fiber optics and optical communications; (060.4080) Modulation; (999.9999) Low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes
1. Introduction
Forward-error correction (FEC) has been well recognized as the key technology to enable 100 Gb/s and beyond optical transmission [1,2]. Suggested by ITU, the soft-decision (SD) FEC as the potential candidate for the nextgeneration high-speed optical transmission has attracted significant interest in both academia and industry. Various FEC schemes with promising performance have been proposed [3-5]. The feasibility of hardware implementation of SD FEC in a 100 Gb/s coherent ASIC were also discussed [6,7]. As the standard optical fiber has been pushed to the physical limits and the Shannon limit has been approached, the concept of elastic optical networking [8,9] was proposed recently to accommodate the ever-increasing traffic demand by making better use of optical network resources, while rate adaptation is one of the essential features.
Rate adaptation can be achieved in both dimensions of modulation and coding. Thanks to the concept of software-defined optical communication, programmable modulation was proposed to dynamically adjust the data rate [10]. Most recently, time-domain hybrid-QAM was demonstrated to achieve finer rate adaptation and transponder cost reduction [11]. In contrast, data rate can be adjusted by changing the overhead of the employed FEC codes, namely rate-adaptive coding. One can either use a set of FEC codes of different code rates [12] to seek for higher spectral efficiency or do shortening or puncturing on a mother code [13] to avoid hardware complexity increase but incurring in performance degradation, or even a hybrid of the two [14]. Considering the precious onchip area within the 100 Gb/s and beyond, the optical transceiver cannot afford to implement multiple FEC codes, and it is highly desirable to have a rate-compatible FEC scheme that is both hardware-friendly and energy-efficient. Inspired by [15], we propose a rate-adaptive low-density parity-check (LDPC) code which is based on a mother code like shortening but more energy-efficient. Different with that in [1], we investigate the rate-adaptive LDPCcoded modulation of staircase structure while a binary LDPC code is adopted as the component code.
The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 introduces both the conventional and proposed rate-adaptive coding techniques, shortening of LDPC and staircase LDPC. Section 3 describes the staircase LDPC-coded modulation aiming for applications for 100 Gb/s and beyond optical transmission. The simulation results are presented as well. Section 4 concludes the paper.
2. Rate-Adaptive LDPC
An LDPC code is described by its parity-check matrix H, with encoding being performed by employing shift registers and mod 2 adders, and decoding based on message-passing algorithm on the Tanner graph derived from H. In this section, before introducing the proposed rate-compatible staircase LDPC code, we first present the conventional rate-adaptation approach, namely, LDPC shortening.
A. LDPC shortening
Shortening of an LDPC code pads zeros in place of information bits as shown in Fig. 1(a). During transmission, the padded zeros are not transmitted. The length of information bits is thus shortened while the codeword length is maintained unchanged. The receiver then sets the initial bit reliabilities, log-likelihood ratios (LLRs), of the padded bits as infinity. The code rate of the shortened LDPC code is then determined by the amount of zeros padded Kp as R=(K-Kp)/(N-Kp). By increasing the amount of zeros padded, the code rate can be adjusted from a high code rate of the mother code to any smaller value. However, shortening becomes less efficient as the code rate is lowered down.
B. Staircase LDPC
Staircase LDPC code reuses the coded bits instead of padding zeros. As shown in Fig. 2(b), the encoder always encapsulates the last Kr bits from a preceding codeword and (K-Kr) incoming information bits into a K-bit vector and

then performs encoding to generate another codeword. The exception for encoding is the 1st codeword in which zeros are padded as no preceding codeword exists. Similar as LDPC shortening, the re-encoded bits are not transmitted, the amount of re-encoded bits, denoted as Kr, determines the actual code rate as R=(K-Kr)/(N-Kr). For decoding, initial LLRs of the Kr re-encoded bits are obtained from the decoding of the preceding codeword as the bits are reused. Therefore, decoding of each codeword will use both the corresponding Kr bit LLRs produced by the decoding of its preceding codeword and the initial bit LLRs of the K-Kr bits calculated from the channel output. In order to leverage re-encoding, the mother code needs to be systematic. As a matter of fact, two features make the proposed staircase LDPC code a success. One is that as the Kr re-encoded bits are overlapped by two neighboring codewords, in the decoding of the second codeword the reliabilities corresponding to Kr overlapped bits in previous decoded codeword have been used, whose reliabilities are higher than channel reliabilities. The other is that decisions of the Kr overlapped bits are made after the decoding of the second codeword.
For both LDPC shortening and staircase LDPC, each decoding of a codeword actually outputs less information bits and thus the throughput of the decoder is decreased. The staircase LDPC code builds dependency over two neighboring codewords which is not preferable. However, the limitation of such inter-codeword dependency can in fact be resolved by simple codeword-wise interleaving as the hardware usually implements a set of parallel decoders.

3. Application to 100 Gb/s and beyond Optical Transmission

High-order modulation formats due to their high spectral efficiency are preferred for ultra-high-speed optical transmission. Dual-polarization QPSK (DP-QPSK) has already been standardized for 100 Gb/s optical transmission while dual-polarization 16QAM (DP-16QAM) is now being considered as the potential candidate for 400 Gb/s. Aiming for being applied to the future high-speed optical transmission, we propose staircase LDPC-coded DPQPSK/16QAM as shown in Fig. 2.
To be more efficient, we propose the staircase LDPC code as visualized in Fig. 2(a) for 100 G/s and beyond applications. Instead of building dependency simply between two continuous codewords as described in Section 2, we process a group of h codewords each time with h denoting the “stair height” and build dependency between the two neighboring groups of h codewords through re-encoding. Still, Kr bits at the end of each codeword will be reencoded once the actual code rate R (hence Kr) is determined. Since we proceed with a group of h codewords in one step, there are in total h·Kr bits to be re-encoded in order to generate the next group of h codewords. The h·Kr bits are block interleaved as indicated by the dashed area before assigning to the head of each codeword in the next group. With the h·Kr reused bits and the h·(K-Kr) incoming information bits, parity bits of each codeword are produced. To be consistent with the transmitter, bit LLRs of re-encoded bits should be de-interleaved before forwarding to the staircase LDPC decoders for decoding.
The staircase LDPC-coded modulation system is given in Fig. 2(b). The incoming h parallel information bit streams after being encoded according to the aforementioned rules are grouped into h/m subgroups, each of which is then bit-interleaved as [16] before mapping. By mapping each tuple of m bits onto a constellation point, in-phase (I) and quadrature (Q) signals are generated and further modulated on the optical wavelength for transmission. After passing through the amplified spontaneous emission (ASE) noise dominated channel, the received I and Q signals are estimated by the coherent detector and forwarded to the demapper. Bit LLRs are then calculated with the derived symbol LLRs from the demapper. After the parallel-to-serial converter (P/S), bit LLRs are de-interleaved and forwarded to each staircase LDPC decoder correctly. Bit errors are counted at the decoder output. In this work, we simulate 31.25-Gbaud staircase LDPC-coded DP-QPSK/16QAM optical transmission through ASE noise dominated channel. We apply identical coded modulation scheme to both polarizations. LDPC-shortening-based rate-adaptive BICM is also simulated for comparison with the same mother code, quasi-cyclic LDPC (13680, 12312) of rate 0.9 and girth 8 [12]. We set the “stair height” h as 72, the maximum number of decoding iterations as 20 and the number of bits per symbol m as 2 for QPSK and 4 for 16QAM. 4. Conclusion

In this paper, we have proposed staircase rate-adaptive LDPC-coded modulation for the next-generation intelligent high-speed optical transmission. By simulation, we have shown that the propose scheme is more energyefficient than the conventional rate-adaptation approach based on shortening of LDPC code. Error floor caused by LDPC shortening has been effectively mitigated by proposed adaptive coded-modulation.
