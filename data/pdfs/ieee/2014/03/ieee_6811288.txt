Abstract—Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) codes have
capacity-approaching performance over several channels of in-
terest. In practice, for good block-error rate performance, the
girth of the Tanner graph of an LDPC code needs to be as high
as possible. In theory, to show that block-error rate approaches
zero for increasing block-lengths, the girth of the Tanner graph
sequence needs to tend to infinity with block-length. To meet these
requirements, we construct sequences of large-girth irregular
LDPC codes for a given degree-distribution pair (DDP) by
applying a general node splitting algorithm on large girth graphs.
The obtained Tanner graph meets the required DDP up to a
suitable approximation. By optimizing the node-splitting method
and using suitable large-girth graphs, we show code constructions
with smaller block length for the same girth, when compared to
previous constructions. Similar gains in block length are observed
in the construction of sequences of large-girth protograph LDPC
codes. Simulations, over a binary erasure channel, confirm the
gains in block-error rate obtained by the large girth construction.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) codes have performance close to capacity over a variety of channels. These popular codes have made it to several standards today and have been studied by numerous researchers in the past two decades. LDPC codes are designed, constructed and decoded using Tanner graphs, which are graphical representations of their parity-check matrices. The girth of the Tanner graph, which is the length of the shortest cycle in the graph, is an important indicator and figure of merit in the design and construction of LDPC codes. In particular, iterations up to half the girth are accurate in message-passing decoding, and the minimum distance of the code increases exponentially with the girth [1]. Hence, in practice, girth is used as an important parameter for constructing LDPC codes.
Also, in theoretical studies, large girth is of importance in the analysis of block-error probability of LDPC codes [2] [3]. A sequence of graphs is said to have large girth if the girth grows as log n with the number of vertices n. By constructing sequences of LDPC codes with large girth, it is possible to show near-exponential fall for block-error rate with block-length. Hence, for constructing capacity-approaching sequences of LDPC codes with block-error rate tending to zero, large-girth constructions are important. While practical methods such as Progressive Edge Growth (PEG) [4] exist for generating high girth codes at small block-lengths, they typi-

III. CONSTRUCTION FOR IRREGULAR CODES

The idea of node-splitting constructions for large-girth LDPC codes was studied in [3]. Given degree distribution polynomials with the fractions λi and ⇢j assumed to be rational, the construction of large-girth irregular codes proceeds as follows:

1) Large-girth regular graph: The first step is to construct a k-regular large-girth bipartite graph for a suitably-chosen k. The value k is chosen to be the LCM of the left degrees, right degrees and the denominators of the fractions λi and ⇢j.
2) Node splitting: The left and right vertices of the large-girth graph above are split to obtain a Tanner graph with the required degree distribution. The edges incident on the original vertex are assigned to the split vertices, and this operation preserves the girth.

In [3], the graphs p,q were used for constructing largeX
girth graphs. In the node-splitting step, each node was split into nodes of the same final degree. As a result, the blocklength required for most DDPs was not in the practical range (typically, 20 or larger). In this work, we use the graphs

IV. LARGE-GIRTH PROTOGRAPH CODES

A protograph G = (V, C, E) is a bipartite graph with V and C being the sets of variable and check nodes, respectively, and E being the set of undirected edges that connect a vertex in V to a vertex in C. Multiple edges are allowed between a pair of nodes (v, c) 2 V ⇥ C. A protograph can be represented by a base matrix B, where B(i, j) is the number of edges between the i-th check node (denoted ci) and the j-th variable node (denoted vj). Therefore, the total number of edges in a protograph, |E|, is the sum of all entries in the base matrix. Optimized protographs can be obtained by differential evolution method as shown in [7]. Also, large-girth protograph LDPC codes can be obtained starting from |E|regular large-girth bipartite graphs by edge coloring followed by node splitting [7].
