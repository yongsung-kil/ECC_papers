Low-Power Dual Quantization-Domain Decoding for LDPC Codes

Shadi Abu-Surra, Eran Pisek, Thomas Henige, and Sridhar Rajagopal
Samsung Research America - Dallas Samsung Electronics
Richardson, Texas, USA Email: {shadi.as, e.pisek, t.henige, sridhar.r}@samsung.com

Abstract—In this paper we propose a new dual quantizationdomain LDPC decoder, which requires only 3-bit messages between the check nodes and the variable nodes. To reduce complexity and save power, check nodes processing is entirely in the 3-bit domain. However, to avoid loss in performance, the variable nodes processing is in a higher bit precision domain. A non-linear mapping is used to map the messages from one quantization domain to the other. Simulations and hardware evaluation of the proposed decoder showed greater than 50% reduction in power consumption with only 0.1 dB (0.2 dB) loss in bit-error-performance when compared to a reference 5-bits scaled Min-Sum decoder over the AWGN (fading) channel.
Keywords—LDPC; iterative decoder; layered scheduling, decoder architecture, quantization; non-linear mapping, lowpower.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) codes [1] have received a great deal of attention in recent years. This is due to their ability to achieve performance close to the Shannon limit [2], the ability to design codes which achieve high parallelization in hardware, and their support of high data rates. However, data rates in modern communication systems are ever-increasing, and the rise of battery-powered wireless communication and computing devices necessitates low-power hardware design wherever possible. As a result, there is continuing work in the area of reducing the complexity of LDPC decoding algorithm and improving the power-efficiency and throughput of LDPC decoding hardware.
LDPC codes typically decoded using an iterative message passing algorithm, where the messages iterate between the CNs and the VNs until a valid codeword is found or the maximum number of iterations is reached. A codeword c is valid if c∙HT = 0. Belief propagation (BP) algorithm [3] and Min-Sum algorithm [4] are traditional examples of LDPC decoding algorithms.
In this paper, we propose a dual quantization-domain (DQD) LDPC decoder, where the channel log-likelihood-ratio (LLR) values and the LDPC variable nodes (VNs) operate in one quantization domain, and the LDPC check nodes (CNs) operate in another quantization domain. A non-linear mapping is defined to transform the messages from one quantization domain to the other. Similar LDPC decoders were proposed in [5]-[7]. In [5], the authors described a quantized decoder with 3-bit messages, and showed that a decoder with small message alphabet can have iterative decoding threshold comparable to

that of the continuous-message-alphabets belief propagation decoder. In [5], 3-bit quantization was used for both the channel LLR values and the decoder messages. In [6] and [7], the authors presented a finite alphabet iterative decoder (FAID) for the binary symmetric channel (BSC). Interestingly, their decoder allows the use of a simple check node update rule similar to that in the min-sum decoder, but with lower bitprecision. Also focusing on BSC and codes with only degree-3 VNs, they were able to greatly simplify the variable node (VN) implementation.
Focusing on LDPC decoding solutions for battery-powered wireless communication devices, the main goal of this work is to propose an LDPC decoding algorithm with lower implementation complexity than the scaled Min-Sum [4], and without significant loss (<0.1 dB) in performance around bit error rate (BER) of 10-6. In contrast to the work in [6] and [7], the DQD decoding algorithm proposed in this paper is designed to operate over the AWGN and fading channels. Also, our proposed DQD decoder can support irregular LDPC codes. The DQD decoder evaluates a simple check node (CN) update rule, similar to that in the min-sum and FAID decoders. In our DQD decoder, two quantization domains are used: (a) a 5-bit uniform quantization is used for the channel LLR values, and (b) 3-bit messages with non-uniform quantization are used in the CNs. The proposed DQD decoder can be applied in both flooding and layered scheduling [8]. From a hardware perspective, LDPC decoders with layered scheduling (or, layered LDPC decoders) converge faster than flooding LDPC decoders, and they have smaller implementation area [9]. Consequently, this work focuses on the layered DQD decoder. Moreover, we study the effect of using different mappings between the decoder’s two quantized domains.
In this paper, a layered DQD decoder architecture for decoding the IEEE 802.11ad LDPC codes [10] is presented. The proposed DQD decoder architecture is implemented in FPGA and compared to that of the scaled Min-Sum decoder.
The paper is organized as follows. In Section II, we introduce the layered DQD decoder. In section III, we study various mappings between the decoder’s quantization domains. In Section IV, we present the simulation results to demonstrate the performance of the proposed DQD LDPC decoding algorithm. In section V, we present the layered DQD LDPC decoder architecture and discuss its hardware performance. In Section VI, we conclude the paper.

II. LAYERED DQD LDPC DECODING ALGORITHM
An LDPC code is defined by a sparse M×N parity check matrix H, where M represents the number of parity checks and N represents the number of bits in the codeword. Graphically, the H matrix of an LDPC code is represented by a Tanner graph which consists of M CNs and N VNs. A quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC code is a structured LDPC code in which H consists of Mp×Np sub-matrices, where each sub-matrix is either a Z×Z cyclic permutation matrix (i.e., cyclically shifted identity matrix) or a Z×Z all-zeros matrix. Note, the majority, if not all, of the LDPC codes used in standards (e.g., IEEE 802.16m, IEEE 802.11n, and IEEE 802.11ad) are QC-LDPC codes. In a layered scheduling, the QC-LDPC H matrix is divided into Mp layers, where each layer consists of the Z CNs in a row of the sub-matrices of H. Accordingly, each iteration is divided into Mp sub-iterations. In a sub-iteration only the CNs in the corresponding layer update the posterior-loglikelihood-ratios of the VNs in their neighborhood.

III. DQD LDPC DECODER’S MAPPING
In this section, we investigate which DQD mapping, DQD{ t1, t2, t3; d1, d2, d3}, results in the best performance. Note that a joint design of the LDPC code and the DQD mapping can results in a better performance. However, in this paper we will assume that the LDPC codes are known, and we only select the DQD mapping which results in the best performance around BER = 10-6. Throughout this paper, we are using the LDPC codes in [10] as examples.
There are several methods, which can be used for optimizing the DQD mapping. For example, similar to the method in [5], it is relatively simple to develop a density evolution tools to find the decoding threshold for different DQD mappings given the degree distribution of the codes. Although, this method typically returns the mapping with the best decoding threshold, we noticed that such a mapping has high error floor. Another way is to use a strategy similar to that in [12], which is based on the knowledge of the harmful topologies that could be present in the code. Though this method is very interesting, finding the harmful topologies for codes with irregular degree distribution over the AWGN is not a trivial task. Hence, this method would be more appropriate for applications that require very low error-floor.
In this work, the error-floor requirements are moderate (no error-floor down to ~10-6 BER), the codes are specific, and the maximum values for t3 and d3 are limited to 12 (for hardware

implementation reasons). For these reasons, we used a differential evaluation (DE) tool [13] to search for the best DQD mapping. In particular, we used the DE tool to find the optimal mapping, which results in the lowest BER, for the rate-1/2 and the rate-13/16 LDPC codes over the AWGN channel. In each case, we selected two signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) points (around BER 10-4, and 10-6, respectively) 2.4 dB and 3.0 dB for the rate-1/2 code, and 4.0 dB and 5.0 dB for the rate-13/16 code. The obtained mappings are shown in Table 1. After simulating these DQD mappings for all four LDPC codes in [8], we selected DQD{1, 3, 6; 1, 3, 6} for the lowSNR region and DQD{2, 4, 8; 2, 4, 9} for the high-SNR region. Alternately, one can run few iterations using DQD{1, 3, 6; 1, 3, 6}, then switch to DQD{2, 4, 8, 2, 4, 9}. Simulation results for the various DQD mappings are shown in the next section.

IV. DQD LDPC DECODER RESULTS
For the simulation results in this section, we evaluate the performance of our proposed DQD LDPC decoder using the IEEE 802.11ad [10] LDPC codes and BPSK modulation over the AWGN channel (Fig. 1-5) and the NLOS conference room (CONF) model [14] (Fig. 6). In simulations over the CONF channel, orthogonal frequency division multiplexing (OFDM) signaling and MMSE equalization in the frequency domain are used (each LDPC codeword is spread over the full bandwidth). In all simulations, the maximum number of iterations is 15, and each transmitted packet consists of 18 LDPC codewords.
Fig. 1 shows the BER and the packet-error-rate (PER) performance of the rate-1/2 LDPC code using three layered LDPC decoders: The floating-point scaled Min-Sum (SMS), the 5-bit SMS, and the DQD{2, 4, 8; 2, 4, 9}. In the case of the 5-bit SMS and the DQD decoder the channel LLR values are quantized using a 5-bit uniform quantization. Note that the DQD{2, 4, 8; 2, 4, 9} is less than 0.1 dB behind the 5-bit SMS decoder in the water-fall region. Interestingly, the 5-bit SMS showed an error floor starting at PER 10-2 (BER 10-3), while the DQD decoder reached PER 10-3 (BER 10-6) without showing any error-floor. Relative to the floating-point SMS the loss in performance is about 0.25dB. We also investigated the convergence speed for the DQD decoder. The DQD{2, 4, 8; 2, 4, 9} needed on average 0.31 more iterations than the 5bit SMS at SNR=3.2 dB (BER=10-6) as shown in Fig. 3.
In Fig. 2 we present the performance of some of the DQD decoders mentioned in Section III. B. Hardware Implementation Results
We implemented the DQD method in Verilog and replaced the data-path in the reference SMS LDPC decoder with the DQD data-path to obtain the DQD decoder. We then synthesized both the reference SMS and the DQD decoders in two hardware platforms: 1) Xilinx Virtex-7vx690tffg1930-2 FPGA (V7 FPGA) using Vivado synthesis and place-and– route tools, 2) ASIC platform using Cadence RTL Compiler tool with TSMC 65nmLP (HVT 125C worst case), and Global Foundries (GF) 65nmLP (sc9 1.08v HVT 125C worst case) processes. After that, we compared the hardware synthesis results between the reference SMS decoder logic and the newly created DQD decoder logic (memory blocks were excluded from the synthesis run in both DQD and SMS cases). Both SMS and DQD decoders implemented the WiGig [10] 672-bit LDPC codes and H-Matrices.

VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper we proposed a new dual quantization-domain LDPC decoder, which has the VNs operate in a high-precision domain, and the CNs operate in a low-precision domain. We were able to design a mapping between the high resolution domain and the low resolution domain, which resulted in less than 0.1 dB loss in performance when compared to the 5-bit scaled Min-Sum decoder. We also showed that the lowprecision of the CN in the proposed decoder does not result in significant increase in the average number of iterations needed for the algorithm to converge, when compared to the scaled Min-Sum algorithm.
Finally, we implemented both our proposed DQD decoder and the reference scaled Min-Sum using similar architectures on FPGA and ASIC platforms. On the FPGA platform, the DQD showed 45% improvement in power consumption and 64% reduction in logic cost. On the ASIC platform, the DQD showed 62% improvement in power consumption and 77% improvement in logic cost.
