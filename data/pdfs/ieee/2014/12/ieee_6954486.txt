Variable LLR Scaling in Min-Sum Decoding for Irregular LDPC Codes
Yin Xu, Student Member, IEEE, Leszek Szczecinski, Senior Member, IEEE, Bo Rong, Member, IEEE, Fabrice Labeau, Senior Member, IEEE, Dazhi He, Yiyan Wu, Fellow, IEEE, and Wenjun Zhang, Fellow, IEEE

Abstract—Min-sum decoding is a low-complexity alternative to the so-called belief propagation and consists in simplification of the nonlinear operation on the log likelihood ratios (LLRs) in the check nodes. The resulting suboptimality may be tempered via appropriate scaling of the LLRs, e.g., the fixed optimal scaling in the normalized min-sum algorithm, and variable scaling algorithms gradually appearing in the literature. However, up to now, none of the papers studied variable scaling both as per iteration and as per different check node degree, due to the prohibitive complexity of multioptimization over space of too many parameters. In this paper, we propose a generalized mutual information (GMI) of LLRs as the criterion to search for the scaling factors for different check node degrees in every iteration in a 1-D thus low-complexity manner. This approach is first analyzed via density evolution, and in addition can be extended to practical LLRs based formulas via Monte Carlo tools to cope with the mismatch issue. Bit error rate simulation results on two low-density parity-check codes show that our proposed GMI metrics have a noticeable gain over the variable scaling schemes that appeared in the literature.
Index Terms—Low density parity check codes, approximate/generalized mutual information, decoding, variable LLRs scaling.
I. INTRODUCTION
L OW-DENSITY parity-check (LDPC) codes were proposed initially by Gallager [1] in 1962, and were rediscovered by Mackay [2] in late 1990s. Due to the reason that LDPC codes can achieve performance near to Shannon capacity [3] under the mechanism of iterative message passing, they are now widely used in many currently deployed and future broadcasting systems [4], such as Chinese DTMB system [5], DVB related standards [6], ATSC 3.0 and Cloud Transmission systems in [7]–[9]. As is known, BP [10] is one of the most prominent decoding algorithms based on message passing, however for the high computational complexity at its check node, it is not commonly adopted in hardware implementation. MS algorithm was then invented by Fossorier et al. [11], which greatly reduces BP’s high complexity via introducing a simplified approximation in check nodes calculation, but degrades the

In order to improve the performance of MS algorithm, the NMS [12] algorithm and offset min-sum (OMS) algorithm [13], [14] were proposed, both of which are based on the idea of linear post processing. Basically, the NMS/OMS algorithm modifies the MS algorithm by means of a scalar function with a predetermined parameter, which aims to correct the overestimation problem of MS at the check node.
Up until now, many works appearing in the literature tried to further improve the decoding algorithm. For example, Wu [15] applies two fixed scaling factor after MS check node output, one is the same with that in NMS/OMS and the other is conditionally multiplied based on whether the corresponding check equation is satisfied. And in another paper [16] the authors modify the OMS algorithm by applying one of two scaling factors depending on the value of check node output. Basically, the two schemes are in spirit very similar with the NMS/OMS algorithm, and scaling factors of both two algorithms are all searched empirically, though is hardware friendly, is not proved to be universally applicable.
In the other two papers [17], [18], the authors indeed provide a heuristic method, that they first explain the value of the scaling factor is actually highly related to the reliability of incoming LLR values. And since the LLR values change every iteration, the scaling factor should be changed adaptively as well. Upon the idea, it was then proposed to use an optimal post processing function (nonlinear and computationally prohibited) for MS output and approximate it via a scaled linear function, in which variable scaling factor is obtained in every iteration by minimizing the mean square error between the two functions. However there exists two problems in this method. First of all, for simplicity, it is assumed all LDPC codes are with uniform check node degree, i.e., scaling factor only varies with iteration. While actually, check node irregular codes do exist in current communication standards and theoretically the reliability of MS output is also related with the check node degree. Therefore, the scaling factor may also vary with different degrees to possibly gain any further improvement. Secondly, due to complex expression of the post processing function, the method is unable to be analyzed with real LLRs and can only be analyzed based on theoretical tools (EXIT chart), which means the scaling factors obtained via this method (under the theoretical Eb/N0 threshold) are not well matched to real LDPC decoder with a relatively worse but more practical threshold.
Throughout the literature, check-node irregular codes are studied by Fossorier and in his paper [19] scaling factor does vary with different check node degrees. However the scaling factor does not vary with iteration due to the prohibitive complexity of the multidimensional optimization over two many parameters.
In this work, we study the variable scaling schemes systematically for irregular LDPC codes, including both check-node regular and check-node irregular codes, and then propose to use the generalized mutual information (GMI) based metric for the search of scaling factors. It is further pointed out that for regular LDPC codes [17], α(i) selected as per iteration only improves slightly the decoding convergence speed, thus a fixed optimal scaling factor α is good enough for regular LDPC codes. This point of view can also be explained from the EXIT chart perspective that the EXIT chart under the threshold of a regular LDPC code has one particular extrinsic value IEop,Vt that leads to the single narrowest gap between the two EXIT curves (one is for variable node decoder , the other is for check node decoder). And by applying this IEop,Vt value instead of IE(i,)V to (13), the optimal value αopt is then obtained.
On the other hand, for irregular LDPC codes [18], especially those well optimized codes, the EXIT curves are so closely near each other under the threshold that there is hardly any apparent single narrowest gap existing, thus no IEop,Vt observed any more. In this sense, any mismatched expression of LLRs (Extrinsic information) in whichever iteration could possibly result in premature convergence to undesirable value. Thus in the case of irregular LDPC codes, it is natural for us to expect that carefully optimized variable scaling sequence is very likely to perform better than the fixed scaling scheme in terms of BER. Under the methodology in (13), the authors first predict the ideal decoding trajectory in the EXIT chart under the threshold, and then pinpoint {IE(1,V) , IE(2,V) , . . . , IE(I,mVax)} values crossed by the EXIT curve and the zigzag decoding curve (in here Imax is the maximum number of iteration adopted), finally apply the {IE(1,V) , IE(2,V) , . . . , IE(I,mVax)} into (13) to obtain the optimized α(i) in every iteration i in a very careful manner to avoid stuck. In here, we denote this method from literature as iteration dependent EXIT-chart based postprocessing metric, and use the abbreviation I-EXIT-P for the rest of the paper.

Though this I-EXIT-P method [17], [18] of searching variable scaling factors for MS decoding is heuristic, it actually has two weak points as follows.
One disadvantage is that the I-EXIT-P metric is only studied and applied in a theoretical point of view, i.e., the optimal scaling factor (for regular code) or scaling sequence (for irregular code) is analyzed under EXIT chart threshold. However, as is known, threshold observed under EXIT chart is generally much smaller than the practical threshold of the real LDPC code due to the ideal assumptions within EXIT chart tool itself, e.g., infinite code length, free of cycles, i.i.d. etc. Under this circumstance, the variable scaling factors obtained via I-EXIT-P will cause mismatch when they are applied to the real MS decoder. In order to get matched scaling sequence for practical LDPC decoder, α(i) might better be obtained via metrics that are analyzed with real LLRs under practical threshold if possible. However, due to the complexity of detailed expression of the post processing function (seen in (10) (11) (12)), it is intractable to extend the theoretical I-EXIT-P metric (13) to the formation that mainly consists of variables like practical LLRs.
Another disadvantage is that, it is assumed that all irregular codes are of variable node irregular but with check node regular, i.e., the check node degree is identical. In this sense, the post processing function f (ζ, dc, IE(i,)V) and thus the corresponding α(i) in (13) are simplified to only vary with the Extrinsic information IE(i,)V; in other words only vary with iteration i. While actually check node irregular codes could be designed for special purpose, and there are currently many standards (such as DVB) that adopt some codes with not only variable node irregular but check node irregular as well. From theoretical point of view, the reliability of MS output is also related to the degree of check nodes, normally the larger the degree, the less reliable the LLR is. In this sense, scaling factors should depend on different check node degrees as well.
Regarding this, there does exist certain paper [19] in the literature studying the mechanism of choice of different scalings αj with respect to different check node degree dj. However the problem with this method is that the scaling does not change with iteration i (thus degrading the performance) due to the prohibitive complexity of multi-dimensional optimization.

III. SIMULATION RESULTS
In this section, we perform bit-error rate (BER) simulation for different decoding algorithms on two irregular LDPC codes: one code is check node regular, to show that our ID-GMI metric (for check regular codes ID-GMI is equal to I-GMI) is better in terms of BER than both NMS algorithm with optimal fixed scaling factor and I-EXIT-P metric from literature; the other code is of check node irregular to present the performance improvement of ID-GMI over the conventional NMS with only one fixed scaling factor, NMS with optimal degree dependent scaling set (we use the abbreviation D-NMS for the rest of the paper) and the I-EXIT-P metrics. For the check node irregular code, we also apply I-GMI metric, to see how it degrades the performance compared with ID-GMI.
When doing simulation, similar to the common condition used in the literature, we set the maximum decoding iteration number as 50 and channel as AWGN. And since most of the decoding algorithms are evaluated in BPSK mode in the literature, therefore we first compare the BER performance under BPSK. Besides, considering BPSK is not the common user case in practice, we also extend the BER simulation in high order constellation (in this paper, we select DVB-T2 standard’s 256 QAM as a representative high constellation case) to see if the performance gain in BPSK is aligned with that in high order constellation. Finally, to ensure the reliability of the results, at least 25 erroneous blocks has to be attained for each SNR point, and for BER below 10−6, at least 105 blocks has to be attained.
The check node regular LDPC code is from DVB-T2 standard with rate of 1/2 and length 64800, and the corresponding bit node and check node degree distributions are λ(x) = 0.2857x + 0.2571x2 + 0.4572x7 and ρ(x) = x5 respectively. And its BER curves for different decoding algorithms are shown in Fig. 1.
Before comparing the BER performances for different decoding algorithms of this 64800 code, details on how to obtain the scaling factor/sequence for some of these algorithms should be clarified in advance. First of all, the optimal scaling factor for NMS algorithm is α = 0.85, and the value is searched based on practical BER simulations under BPSK AWGN channel, i.e., to run BER simulations for scaling candidates from 0.5 to 1.0, using 0.1, 0.05, 0.02 or 0.01 as a step, gradually narrowing them down to one scaling factor with the best BER performance at 10−6 or below meanwhile having no obvious error floor at least down to 10−7. And, I-EXIT-P is the variable scaling metric from literature [17], [18], the corresponding scaling sequence varying with iteration is obtained using (13), in which Ie(i,)v is obtained according to EXIT chart theory. IV. SUMMARY
In this paper we examine variable scaling metrics for MS decoding algorithm systematically, and point out that in the literature the variable scaling schemes only considered scaling factors varying with iteration or with check node degree, yet none considered varying scaling factors at both ways (iteration and degree) due to the prohibitive complexity of multi-dimensional optimization over too many parameters. To get around this problem, we propose the generalized mutual information (GMI) of LLRs based on density evolution, which can be used to search for the scaling factor αj(i) individually thus one-dimensionally for check node degree dj in iteration i. Furthermore, to better match the scaling factors with practical LDPC decoder, we extend the pdf based equation of the metric to real LLRs based formulas via Monte Carlo approximation.
Basically, the scaling sequence set {α1(i), α2(i), . . . , αd(icN) }iI=ma1x searched in one dimensional way via our proposed GMI metric is not ideally globally optimized, but based on the idea of “greedy” optimization. Still, simulation results on two irregular LDPC codes have shown that our GMI algorithm can perform better than the fixed/variable scaling MS algorithms appearing in the literature. In particular, the BER simulation results under BPSK AWGN on the check node regular code with length 64800 and rate 1/2, show that our iteration dependent GMI metric is better performed than the iteration dependent I-EXIT-P metric (appeared in literature) and the NMS algorithm by 0.15dB, while only 0.1dB away from BP algorithm. In addition, the BER simulation results under BPSK AWGN on check node irregular with length 10032 and rate 1/2, show that our Iteration and Degree dependent GMI is able to approach the BP algorithm with distance less than 0.05dB away, meanwhile better than the degree dependent NMS algorithm and iteration dependent I-EXIT-P metric by more than 0.1dB. Moreover, simulation results in high order constellation (256 QAM) for different decoding algorithm on the two LDPC codes are also presented in the paper to show our proposed ID-GMI algorithm is also effective in high order constellation. To be specific, for 64800 code under 256 QAM, our ID-GMI algorithm is better than NMS and I-EXIT-P by 0.2dB and 0.3dB (enlarged gain compared with that under BPSK) respectively though is 0.2dB away from BP algorithm; for 10032 code under 256 QAM, our ID-GMI is now more than 0.1dB better than our simplified version I-GMI and is almost 0.2dB (enlarged gain) better than D-NMS and I-EXIT-P algorithm though is 0.2dB away from BP algorithm all due to the impact of high order constellation.
Finally, GMI metric can also be extended to find the optimal offset factors for OMS algorithm, which we do not cover in here.
