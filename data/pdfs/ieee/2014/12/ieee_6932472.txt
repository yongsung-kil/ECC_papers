On the Construction of Low Error Floor LDPC Codes on Rectangular Lattices
Xiongfei Tao, Pan Liu, Zhuoming Feng, and Zuoqi Hu

Abstract—In previous literature, arithmetically constrained slope sequences have been adopted for the construction of girth eight (3, k) LDPC codes on rectangular lattices. This method, however, creates a certain number of small trapping sets, which degrade overall performances. In this letter, an improved construction of a class of (3, k) LDPC codes on rectangular lattices is presented. Relevant constraints are derived to eliminate small trapping sets to improve performance in the error floor region. The check matrices of the proposed codes are introduced into quasi-cyclic structures, where experimental investigation shows favorable error rate performance over AWGN channels.
Index Terms—Trapping sets, rectangular lattices, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, low error floor.
I. INTRODUCTION
L OW-DENSITY PARITY-CHECK (LDPC) codes have a hot topic in coding theory over the last few decades. They demonstrate remarkable performance close to Shannon limit. Among existing constructions of structured LDPC codes, the design of quasi-cyclic (QC-) LDPC codes has drawn considerable interest from researchers, primarily due to the fact that QC-LDPC codes possess significant advantages in encoding and decoding implementation, and enable the storage of coding matrices with only a few memory units. To this effect, QCLDPC codes are featured in a number of communication system standards.
LDPC codes have been extensively researched, yet relevant error floor phenomena have remained an open problem. Trapping sets are commonly known to be responsible for the error floor region; among these trapping sets, the main culprits are the so-called elementary trapping sets (ETSs). Several efforts have been made to lower the error floor of LDPC codes. Asvadi et al. [1], for example, employed cyclic liftings to eliminate the dominant trapping sets of the code. By eliminating their constituent short cycles, the designed codes performed significantly better in the error floor region. Khazraie et al. [2] improved the Progressive-edge-growth (PEG) algorithm by equipping an efficient algorithm to search the ETSs. We analyze the resulting relationships between small ETSs and slopes on the lattices, where the derived relationships can be used to facilitate the selection of slope to avoid the presence of small ETSs.

II. CONSTRUCTION LDPC CODES ON 2-D LATTICES

Vasic et al. constructed Tanner graphs without 4-cycle or 6-cycle based on 2-D lattices [7], which is summarized as follows.
Consider a 2-D rectangular integer lattice L = {(x, y) : 0 ≤ x ≤ j − 1, 0 ≤ y ≤ q − 1}, a lattice is defined as a set of points. Starting at a point (0, a), a line with slope s passes through j points on the lattice, i.e., a line is an assemble of j points {(x, a + x · s mod q) : 0 ≤ x ≤ j − 1}. Starting at every points with x = 0, k sets of lines corresponding to k different slopes are constructed, and the line-point incidence matrix is equivalent to the check matrix of a (j, k) LDPC code. For the construction of (3, k) regular LDPC codes we let j = 3 and we denote the slope-set comprising k slopes by S. In the construction of [7]q is a prime or a prime power, though not in actuality a necessary condition. Even for a non-prime numbers of q, it is possible to obtain the girth eight LDPC codes by carefully selecting the slope set.
As long as q satisfies mod((s1 − s2) · b, q) = 0, for ∀ s1, s2 ∈ S, ∀ b ∈ [0, k − 1], any two lines constructed on the lattice have at most one common point. Cycles of length four are removed, guaranteeing that the generated code has a girth no less than 6. Lines constructed with a similar slope set may produce many triangles on the lattice. Fig. 1(a) shows a triangle consisting of three lines labeled l0, l1, and l2 with slopes s0, s1, and s2 respectively, where the three intersection points are labeled as p0, p1, and p2. We assume that the coordinate of point p0 is (0, a), and it can be seen from the figure that there are two paths from p0 to p2. According to the two different paths, the y coordinate of p2 has two expressions, (a + 2s1 mod q) and (a + s0 + s2 mod q). Therefore, we obtain the equation shown below:

III. SMALL TRAPPING SETS OF 2-D LATTICES LDPC CODES
In this section, we analyze the relationship between small ETSs and slopes and derive a set of constraints. If slopes in a slope-set satisfy these constraints, then the LDPC codes constructed by this slope-set will lack certain small ETSs, which leads to lower error floors of the codes. The aforementioned triangle condition can be easily avoided so that the codes are free of cycles of length six. In the following analysis, we discuss codes with girths of at least eight.

A. (4, 4) Trapping Sets
In (3, k) regular LDPC codes with girths of eight, a (4, 4) trapping set is exactly a cycle of length eight. Fig. 1(b)–(g) illustrate all possible (4, 4) trapping sets on rectangular lattices. By elaborative selection of slopes, most forms of (4, 4) ETS (i.e., 8-cycle) in Fig. 1 are eliminated, and the only form remaining on the lattices is (d), where l0, l2 have the same slope and l1, l3 have the same slope. Under this conditions, Fig. 1(d) is an inevitable case. Therefore the codes constructed on such lattices have girths at most eight.

B. (8,0) Trapping Sets
Considering a (4, 4) ETS as according to Fig. 1(d), and assuming that the slopes of the four lines are different, we rotate this ETS by 180 degrees to obtain a new ETS. In Fig. 1(h), there are two (4, 4) ETSs as corresponding to Fig. 1(d), where one is comprised of four solid lines in the figure and the other one is comprised of four dash lines. It also can be seen from the figure that the dash line (4, 4) ETS is a rotative version of the solid line (4, 4) ETS. By selecting the proper position of these two ETSs, the four white-shaded points in the dash line ETS are merged with the four white-shaded points in the solid line ETS which forms a (8,0) trapping set. To construct LDPC codes with dmin no less than 8 the constraint below must be satisfied.

IV. NUMERICAL RESULTS
In this section, we conduct experiments to test the performance of the codes proposed in this letter. We also construct some counterparts with the same length and rate by progressive edge growth (PEG) algorithm [8] for comparison. All the codes are decoded by BP decoding algorithm over AWGN channels and the maximum iteration is set to be 100.
The experiment deals with the performance of codes obtained from the slope sets listed in Table I, these four codes are all girth eight codes and free of (5, 3), (7, 3), (6, 2), and (8, 2) ETSs.
C1 and C2 have minimum distances dmin = 16. Their frameerror-rate (FER) performances are included in Fig. 3. For comparison, Fig. 3 also shows the FER performances of a (185, 3, 5) LDPC code and a (234, 3, 6) LDPC code constructed using the PEG algorithm. The (185, 3, 5) PEG code has a girth g = 8 and a minimum distance dmin = 16; however, the code has a number of (5, 3), (7, 3), (8, 2), and (9, 1) ETSs, the (234, 3, 6) PEG code has a girth g = 8, minimum distance dmin = 12, and the code shows a number of (5, 3), (7, 3), (6, 2), (8, 2), and (9, 1) ETSs. From Fig. 3 demonstrates that C1 and C2 have lower error floors than their PEG counterparts.
C3 and C4 have minimum distances dmin = 12. We further construct a (1771, 3, 11) LDPC code and a (2796, 3, 12) LDPC code for comparison. The girths of these two PEG codes are both eight, and they both have a minimum distance dmin = 12. The codes, however, have a number of (5, 3), (7, 3), (6, 2), (8, 2), and (9, 1) ETSs. The FER performances of code C3, C4, and their two PEG counterparts are included in Fig. 4, which shows that our codes outperform the PEG codes.

V. CONCLUSION
In this letter, we proposed an improved method to construct LDPC codes free of small trapping sets on rectangular lattices. For the construction of regular LDPC codes with column weight j = 3 on lattices, we derive certain slope constraints which effectively avoid the presence of small trapping sets, based on the condition of girth eight as used in an algorithm to search proper slope sets. The construction is simple and efficient, and provides a set of codes with lower error floor.
