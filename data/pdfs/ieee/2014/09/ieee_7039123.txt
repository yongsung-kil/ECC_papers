Sparse Generator Matrices for Some Families of Quasi-Cyclic Low-Density Parity-Check Codes

Marco Baldi, Giovanni Cancellieri, Franco Chiaraluce
DII ‚Äď Universit√† Politecnica delle Marche Ancona, Italy
{m.baldi, g.cancellieri, f.chiaraluce}@univpm.it

Abstract‚ÄĒIn this paper, we provide a general form for sparse generator matrices of several families of Quasi-Cyclic LowDensity Parity-Check codes. Codes of this kind have a prominent role in literature and applications due to their ability to achieve excellent performance with limited complexity. While some properties of these codes (like the girth length in their associated Tanner graphs) are well investigated, estimating their minimum distance is still an open problem. By obtaining sparse generator matrices for several families of these codes, we prove that they are also Quasi-Cyclic Low-Density Generator Matrix codes, which is an important feature to reduce the encoding complexity, and provides a useful tool for the investigation of their minimum distance.
Keywords‚ÄĒGenerator matrix, minimum distance, QC-LDGM codes, QC-LDPC codes, sparse matrices.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes are state-of-theart forward error correcting codes able to approach the channel capacity under belief propagation Soft-Input Soft-Output (SISO) decoders [1]. A widespread family of LDPC codes is that of Quasi-Cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes, which have attracted a great interest in literature and applications. QCLDPC codes are defined by structured parity-check matrices, formed by circulant blocks, which facilitate both the analysis of these codes and their hardware/software implementation. Several design techniques exist to design QC-LDPC codes with good properties and performance (see [2]-[7] and the references therein). Despite the great interest on these codes, and mostly due to the fact that they are defined through their parity-check matrices, their generator matrices have scarcely been investigated in previous literature, at the authors‚Äô best knowledge. However, finding sparse generator matrices (if they exist) is useful (at least) for the following two reasons:
i) A sparse generator matrix G allows to exploit an encoding circuit with less connections (i.e., gates), as well as an encoding software performing less operations, with respect to the general case of a dense G.
ii) A sparse generator matrix G allows to derive a tight upper bound on the code minimum distance, which coincides with the minimum row weight of G.
Codes having a sparse generator matrix are also called Low-Density Generator Matrix (LDGM) codes. Some authors have proposed to design LDPC and QC-LDPC codes which are also LDGM by construction [8], [9], but the existence of sparse generator matrices for general QC-LDPC codes has never been

investigated. In the previous work [10], we have found that sparse generator matrices exist for array LDPC codes, which are a special class of QC-LDPC codes. In this work, we extend the analysis to other families of QC-LDPC codes, and show that they are all characterized by the existence of a sparse generator matrix having a distinctive form.
The paper is organized as follows: in Section II we remind the notation and definitions concerning several families of QCLDPC codes, in Section III we provide a general form for the generator matrices of such codes, and in Section IV we report some examples.

III. GENERATOR MATRICES OF QC-LDPC CODES
A general form for the generator matrix of the QC-LDPC codes listed in the previous section is illustrated in Fig. 1: it is formed by a k √ó n matrix which has null elements everywhere, except for a main diagonal formed by k0 blocks, named G0, G1,
G2,‚Ä¶, Gk0 ‚ąí1 . Each of these blocks is formed by 1√ó (r0 +1)
circulant matrices, having a structure which changes on the basis of the considered class of QC-LDPC codes. Then, the generator matrix may include some additional rows, marked in grey in the figure, which compensate for the increase in the code dimension which follows from the possible presence of linear dependent rows in the parity-check matrix. The presence of these rows and their structure will also be discussed for each specific family of codes.
The sparse nature of this matrix follows from the presence of a great number of null entries in the bottom-left and topright parts. Furthermore, the blocks Gi are also usually sparse, as we will see next. In the following, we describe how to apply this general form to each specific class of QC-LDPC codes we consider. We provide general expressions for the computation of the blocks Gi, which allow to build the parity-check matrix.
A first important result which arises from the expressions we obtain for the blocks Gi is the possibility to identify those choices of the parity-check matrix yielding some reduction in the maximum value of minimum distance achievable. In fact, in some cases, the blocks Gi may have a row weight smaller than the maximum achievable, due to the choice of the

elements of H. It is important to note that, in some cases, the blocks Gi may have not full rank. In such cases, the matrix G actually defines a subcode of the code defined by H, rather than exactly the same code.

In order to generalize the analysis to whatever value of n0, we can proceed as follows. We start by considering the first four columns of HI, as in (8), and starting from them we obtain G0, as we have seen. Then we discard the first column and include the fifth. This way, we obtain another matrix with the same form as (8), but with different indexes. By considering this new matrix, and using the same approach we used to compute G0, we obtain the second block, G1. We continue by discarding the second column and including the sixth, such that we obtain G2, and so on. This way, we can proceed until we have included the last column, which is at position n0. Finally, we must add the two extra rows, in the same way as we have done in the case with n0 = 4.
From the form of the generator matrix we have obtained, it results that the minimum distance of CPM-LDPC codes with r0 = 3 is dmin ‚Č§ min{24, 2q}. Also in this case, choosing q < 12 is not of interest, therefore the upper bound becomes equal to 24. There are some cases in which the value of this general upper bound on the minimum distance may become smaller. In fact, some reduction in the minimum row weight of G occurs when one or more of the sets I0,j contain some duplicate elements. In this case, each value appearing twice does not produce any contribution in the corresponding rows of G (because we work over the binary field) and their weight

decreases. An example confirming this possibility is provided in Section IV.

Actually, the generator matrices we obtain are not very sparse, but this is simply due to the need to consider toy values of q in order to provide reasonably simple examples. Since the weight of the generator matrix does not depend on q, it becomes sparser and sparser for increasing values of q, which are also those of practical interest.
