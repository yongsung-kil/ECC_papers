Modeling BP Decoding Error Events with the LDPC Codes of the DVB-S2 Standard
J.-C. Sibel, M. Crussie`re and J.-F. He´lard UEB, IETR
INSA de Rennes, CNRS UMR 6164 Rennes, France
jean-christophe.sibel@insa-rennes.fr

Abstract—In this paper, we focus on the behavior of the Belief Propagation (BP) algorithm when decoding the Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) code of rate 3/4 in the DVB-S2 standard. By studying the topological structure of its Tanner graph, we raise properties inherent to the degree distribution that turns out to be strongly correlated with the decoding failures. The irregularity of the degrees seriously damages the performance, visible by an abrupt error floor. We accordingly propose a novel model of the error events based on the degree distribution which helps simulate typical error events and observing the flaws of the DVB-S2 code. This work could be used as a good basis to design future codes with a better decoding behavior, especially in the error floor region.
Index Terms—DVB-S2, LDPC codes, iterative decoder, decoding failures.
I. INTRODUCTION
The Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes, introduced by Gallager in 1963 [1], are widely used in practical standards these days, e.g. WiMAX (IEEE 802.16e), LTE (4G) or Digital Video Broadcast - Satellite of second generation (DVB-S2). In the latter one [2], several properties are used to construct codes that ensure flexibility, robustness and reasonable complexity. First of all, various rates, from 1/4 to 9/10, are proposed to face different atmospheric conditions between satellites and earth antennas. After that, to near the Shannon’s limit, the block size is recommended to be large, either N = 16200 or N = 64800 bits. The stair-case part in all codes enables a weakly complex encoding process. Codes accordingly need to be irregular codes [3] to offer competitive performance.
The decoding process of DVB-S2 codes is achieved by the Belief Propagation (BP) algorithm, created by Pearl in 1988 [4] as an inference solver for graphically represented problems. The computation complexity of the BP decoder linearly scales with the code length N , which is relevant for a practical use. Yet, BP is often suboptimal, contrary to the the Maximum Likelihood Decoder (MLD), as the most of the practical codes contain loopy topological structures in their graph [5].
A strong effort has been made in the literature to analyze the sub-optimality of BP. The complexity of this problem is first that codes are too large to enumerate all local structures they contain. Afterwards, not all loopy structures are equivalent according either to their size, or to their inherent topology or to their influence on the decoding results. Among these undesirable structures, famous ones are known as trapping

sets, as introduced by Richardson [6]. It has been shown that small trapping sets were responsible for the error floor, an abrupt degradation of the error rate slope for high Signal-toNoise Ratio (SNR) values. Analyzing the error floor is a hard task as extracting analytical equations is near impossible. Very few papers tackle this approach [7], [8]. Three main strategies are followed to fight against the error floor. The first strategy is to design LDPC codes either by constraining the degree distribution [9] or by forbidding small trapping sets [10]. This method performs well on small codes but is not well suited for large N values. The second strategy consists in modifying decoding rules of BP [11]. Results are very impressive but the new decoders are hard to adapt on strongly irregular codes. Another decoder, the generalized BP described in [12], is well fitted to treat trapping sets but only for codes of moderate size and degrees. The third strategy proposes decoders with a post-processing task. This consists in correcting residual decoding errors [13],[14], e.g. in the DVB-S2 standard, an outer BCH code is embedded to correct at most twelve errors in the information word of length K = 48600 for the LDPC code of rate 3/4 and length N = 64800. This enables a quasi-error free transmission (one error per hour), i.e. a frame error rate below 10−7. However, LDPC codes of DVB-S2 are not perfectly designed as it has been explained in [15], their minimum distance is quite poor. By focusing on these LDPC codes, it should then be necessary to extract their flaws in order to open a research on new designs before increasing the complexity of the transmission chain with a BCH code.
In this paper, we propose a novel approach to the suboptimality problem of BP, focusing on the DVB-S2 code of rate 3/4 and length N = 64800 with a 8-PSK modulation. Our idea is to correlate the code structure and the decoding behavior to model error events. In this way, we have designed a simulator of typical error events encountered at different SNR values. Such a simulator yields results that allows an easy and fair interpretation of the error floor. We include an analysis obtained with this simulator to propose a re-design of LDPC codes in the DVB-S2 standard. We organize the article as follows. Section II introduces basics and concepts of LDPC codes and BP decoding. In section III, we present an analysis of the structure of the DVB-S2 code and clues about the loopy structures it contains. This orients the study of section IV dedicated to the error events model.

Contrary to the assumption previously made in III-A by only looking at local densities, 3-degree bit nodes (in gray) are much more connected than 12-degree bit nodes (in white). BP messages outgoing any bit node of the code are then mainly passed toward bit nodes of degree 3. We then assume that BP messages adjacent to 12-degree bit nodes are less affected by any BP event. Thereafter, we will see that unlike Fig. 3, 2-degree bit nodes strongly contributes to BP failures.

Thus, the first step of the loop extractor is to construct Gi(d) for a given bit node Xi, for a given depth d and a given length l using a breadth-first-search algorithm. In the second step, we compute all possible paths of length equal or less than l from Xi inside Gi(d) using a depth-first-search algorithm. Any of these paths that eventually comes to Xi, without repeating other nodes, is then a loop that involves Xi of a maximum length l.
The accuracy of this loop extractor depends on the depth d. A weak condition to obtain an efficient algorithm is that l must be less than 2d as a loop of length l impacts iterative decoders only after l/2 iterations. This justifies that small loops are
much more serious than large loops. Loops of large lengths (10, 12, . . . ) can surely be found in DVB-S2 codes and they
are certainly more numerous than short loops but they exert
less influence on iterative decoding. As a consequence, we only consider loops of length l ≤ 10. Fig. 4 gives the average percentages of L-loops against the bit node index obtained with the proposed loop extractor with d = 5 and l ≤ 10.

The shortest loops are found to be of length six. 8-loops are clearly more numerous than 6-loops for all bit nodes, but the main point is that 2-degree bit nodes are involved in twice as more 6-loops than other bit nodes. Combined with the fact that 2-degree bit nodes cannot escape from any loop as they only have two neighbors, we can predict that the BP decoder is to be quite inefficient to decode them.
By considering these results altogether with the connectivity, we state that the decoding properties will also be specific for each bit node degree. In the next section, we look further at this idea as we investigate on the decoding behavior of BP with experiments for each of the three degrees.
IV. ERROR EVENTS OF THE DVB-S2 CODE
The previous analysis offered a relevant guideline to investigate on the decoding failures of the DVB-S2 codes, using bit nodes degree as the main parameter. In this section, we present the evolution of the decoding behavior of these codes, depending on this parameter. We finally introduce a new method to fairly model error events of DVB-S2 codes.
A. Preliminaries
To observe reactions of the BP decoder to the noise channel, we first submit BP to LLRs of very high magnitude. The protocol consists in forcing only one subgraph of the whole code to be in error when entering the BP. More precisely, for a given bit node Xi, we force any node Xj ∈ Gi(2) to be strongly in error by affecting a very negative LLR: LLRj = −L, L 0. We then observe the number of decoding errors compared with the number of channel errors.

These preliminary results confirm that associating decoding performance of BP on DVB-S2 codes with the bit nodes degrees is a relevant method to analyze and model the decoding behavior.

These results are closely correlated with the topological structure of the DVB-S2 code. We previously explained that this code was made of numerous loops in such a way that 2-degree bit nodes were more involved in 6-loops that bit nodes of other degree. These short loops surely prevent the BP decoder from converging to the channel input codeword. To understand this phenomenon, we distinguish two natures of errors:

• persistent errors: any bit node Xi in error before and after BP,
• created errors: any bit node Xi correct before BP and erroneous after BP.

Figure 9. Origins of decoding errors of BP on AWGNC when decoding the DVB-S2 code of rate R = 3/4 with a 8-PSK modulation
By comparing with Fig. 7, we see that persistent errors have the same behavior as 3-degree errors while created errors and 2-degree errors similarly behave. In other words, the most of the created errors emerge from bit nodes of degree two. The direct interpretation is that short loops entail weakly connected bit nodes to change values along the decoding process, which results in a non-null Hamming distance between the input codeword and the decoding output word, that is to say errors.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we studied the structure of the DVB-S2 code of rate 3/4. We raised the topological property stating that bit nodes of smallest degree were involved in more short loops than the other bits. We then considered the degree distribution as a parameter to work on the decoding failures. We observed that, indeed, errors at the BP output were clearly dependent on the bit nodes degree. We also saw that the error floor was due to errors created by BP on 2-degree bit nodes. We constructed a novel model of typical error events against the SNR. This highlighted that the BCH code in the DVB-S2 standard could be of less correction capability. We finally analyzed that 2degree bit nodes used to accelerate the encoding process could be re-designed to decrease the error floor.
The methodology followed in this work is flexible enough to be used on other LDPC codes. We then conclude that this constitutes a good basis for the design of future LDPC codes.

The interesting point is in the error floor region. In the DVB-S2 standard, a BCH code is included as an outer code to correct at most 12 residual errors in the information word provided by the LDPC decoder, after deleting the redundancy part. As already mentioned in III, 2-degree bit nodes are only found in this redundancy, therefore, the BCH code only treats residual errors from bit nodes of degree three and twelve. According to I3 in Fig. 11, errors on 3-degree bit nodes only represent less than 10% of all BP decoding errors in such a way that less than ten errors on the information word may be counted. The BCH could then have a weaker correction power to ensure a good reliability in this SNR interval.
When now dealing with the other intervals I1 and I2, the number of 3-degree and 12-degree bit errors is on the increase. The total number of bit errors then quickly exceeds the correction capacity of the BCH code. As an example, at 8.0 dB, n3 = 11 whereas at 7.97 dB, n3 = 20. Considering this observation altogether with the previous one about I3, the BCH code only perfectly performs at 8.0 dB, that is quite poor.
From another point of view, the error events simulator may be useful to accurately design DVB-S2 codes. On one hand, 2-bit nodes are very useful to accelerate the encoding process. In addition, they balance the decoding complexity as the first part of H, index i ≤ 5400, is very dense. On the other hand, they degrade the decoding performance for high SNR values. Therefore, designing the LDPC codes considering a re-design of 2-degree bit nodes is a criterion to take into account.
