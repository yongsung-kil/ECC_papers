More on the Minimum Distance of Array LDPC Codes
Haiyang Liu, Shipei Yang, Gang Deng, and Jie Chen

Abstract—Array low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are a special class of quasi-cyclic LDPC codes constructed from a pair of integers (m, q), where q is an odd prime, and m ≤ q. In this paper, an improved upper bound on the minimum distance of this class of codes is given.
Index Terms—Array codes, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, minimum distance.
I. INTRODUCTION
L OW-DENSITY parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] have been the subject of a considerable amount of research in the recent years due to their remarkable error-control performances under iterative decoding algorithms. However, these algorithms always exhibit an error floor phenomenon in high signal-tonoise ratio (SNR) region, which is related to small trapping sets [2], [3], the structural weaknesses in the graphical representation of the code. As a result, the knowledge of trapping sets, especially small trapping sets, is crucial in analyzing the error floor performance of an LDPC code.
Apart from trapping sets, another property that limits the performance of an LDPC code in high SNR region is its minimum distance [4]. For a given linear code, the minimum distance determines the possibility of undetected errors, which also contribute considerably to the error floor of the code. Besides, since the support of any codeword is a trapping set, the value of minimum distance provides an upper bound on the sizes of trapping sets of any graphical representation of the code.1 Unfortunately, it is very difficult to compute or approximate the minimum distance of a linear code in general [5], [6]. However, if we restrict ourselves to linear codes with certain algebraic structures, then the determination of minimum distance or the derivation of bounds on it is possible.
In this paper, we focus on a class of algebraic LDPC codes, namely, array LDPC codes. An array LDPC code C(m, q) [7] is a quasi-cyclic code designed according to a pair of integers (m, q), where q is an odd prime and m ≤ q. The quasi-cyclic property makes this class of codes suitable for practical implementations. For example, the encoding of a quasi-cyclic LDPC

Since the code C(m, q) has nice algebraic structure, it is an interesting problem to investigate d(C(m, q)), the minimum distance of C(m, q), especially for any fixed m and sufficiently large q. The problem was first studied by Mittelholzer [9], where an upper bound on d(C(m, q)) is given for 3 ≤ m ≤ 6. In [10], the authors proved that d(C(2, q)) = 4 and d(C(3, q)) = 6 for any prime q, respectively. It was shown independently in [11], [12] that d(C(4, q)) = 10 for q ≥ 11. Through investigations on C(m, q) for several small m, an interesting property has been found [12]–[14]: for a fixed m and sufficiently large q, the support matrices2 of small-weight codewords of C(m, q) have a normal form that is independent of q. This property can be used to derive an upper bound on d(C(m, q)). First, we collect some support matrices of the same size for a fixed m through computer searches of small-weight codewords of C(m, q) for some small q. Subsequently, we try to show that these support matrices can be brought into a normal form that is independent of q, which means this form corresponds to a codeword for any sufficiently large q. As a result, the column number of the normal support matrix gives an upper bound on d(C(m, q)). Using these methods, the upper bound d(C(5, q)) ≤ 12 was established in [12]. Recently, Rosnes et al. proposed an algorithm to search support matrices for smallweight codewords of C(m, q) in [14] and provided the upper bounds d(C(6, q)) ≤ 20 and d(C(7, q)) ≤ 24.
Since C(m, q) is a regular quasi-cyclic LDPC code with column weight m, it follows that d(C(m, q)) ≤ (m + 1)! [15]. Recently, Baldi et al. [16] presented a general form for the generator matrix of an array LDPC code and showed d(C(m, q)) ≤ 2m holds for sufficiently large q. However, tighter upper bounds on d(C(m, q)) have only been obtained for the abovementioned specific values of m. In this paper, we prove an improved general upper bound on d(C(m, q)), and provide some related numerical results.

Although the best known results are tighter than ours as m grows, the above-mentioned computer-search-based approach (both probabilistic and exhaustive) has some drawbacks. First, it is unclear whether the support matrices of small-weight codewords of C(m, q) have a normal form for any m. As m grows, it is difficult to find regularities in the small-weight codewords of C(m, q) through computer search results on small q. To illustrate this point, we list our computer search results for m ∈ [5, 8] and q up to 23 in Table III.6 (Note that computer searches for m ≤ 7 were also performed in [12], [14].) Although d(C(5, q)) are same when q ≥ 13, for m ≥ 6 no regularity is apparent.7 Second, as m and q grow, finding small-weight codewords of C(m, q) by direct computer search becomes intractable. For instance, the probabilistic algorithm in [19] failed to find any codeword of weight less than 2q = 58 when applied to C(8, 29).8 As another example, the exhaustive search using the algorithm in [20] doesn’t complete when applied to C(7, 13) with the threshold τ set to 18, due to excessive running time.9 For larger m and q, the computational complexity of exhaustive search is more daunting. Taken together, these considerations suggest that our improved general upper bound on d(C(m, q)) is meaningful despite its relative looseness.
