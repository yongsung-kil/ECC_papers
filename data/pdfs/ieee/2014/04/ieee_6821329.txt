Improved Energy Efficiency for Optical Transport Networks by Elastic Forward Error Correction
Anders Rasmussen, Metodi P. Yankov, Michael S. Berger, Knud J. Larsen, and Sarah Ruepp

Abstract—In this paper we propose a scheme for reducing the energy consumption of optical links by means of adaptive forward error correction (FEC). The scheme works by performing on the fly adjustments to the code rate of the FEC, adding extra parity bits to the data stream whenever extra capacity is available. We show that this additional parity information decreases the number of necessary decoding iterations and thus reduces the power consumption in iterative decoders during periods of low load. The code rate adjustments can be done on a frameby-frame basis and thus make it possible to manipulate the balance between effective data rate and FEC coding gain without any disruption to the live traffic. As a consequence, these automatic adjustments can be performed very often based on the current traffic demand and bit error rate performance of the links through the network. The FEC scheme itself is designed to work as a transparent add-on to transceivers running the optical transport network (OTN) protocol, adding an extra layer of elastic soft-decision FEC to the built-in hard-decision FEC implemented in OTN, while retaining interoperability with existing OTN equipment. In order to facilitate dynamic code rate adaptation, we propose a programmable encoder and decoder design approach, which can implement various codes depending on the desired code rate using the same basic circuitry. This design ensures optimal coding gain performance with a modest overhead for supporting multiple codes with minimal impact on the area and power requirements of the decoder.
Index Terms—Elastic optical networks; Optical transport networks; Optically switched networks; Rate adaptive forward error correction.
I. INTRODUCTION
T he concept of dynamic data rate adaptation is widely used in modern wireless transmission technologies, where spectral efficiency is traded off for higher reliability on low-quality links by changing parameters such as the modulation format or the code rate of the forward error correction (FEC) [1]. On optical links, on the other hand, the trend has commonly been to use fixed data rates, due to the more static conditions of the underlying transmission

medium. However, in recent years the concept of “elastic optical networks” has received increasing attention as a means of reducing the overall power consumption of optical networks. The main idea is to dynamically adjust the links depending on the current capacity demand to the most power-efficient configuration, which will still provide the required throughput. Of the parameters that can be modified to reduce power consumption at the cost of throughput, we have described a few below:
• Reducing the number of active channels: This is one of the more efficient ways of reducing power consumption, as all the dedicated optical and electrical equipment for the deactivated channels can be put in low-power mode or turned off completely. However, opening and closing a channel takes time and can cause disruption in the traffic running on the link.
• Reducing the per-channel optical symbol rate: This method results in less load on the electronics that need to process the data such as the digital signal processors (DSPs) and the FEC. The downside is a possible loss of synchronization in the receiver as the change is taking place.
• Using less spectrum-efficient modulation schemes: As is known from general transmission theory, less spectrumefficient modulation schemes can function at a lower signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) for a given bit error rate (BER) [2]. Hence, less regeneration, amplification, and signal processing will be needed. As with the symbol rate, the digital electronics such as the FEC can also run at a lower rate, thus saving power. Again, changing the modulation will usually cause a temporary disruption to the link until the DSP algorithms converge to the new modulation format [3,4]. However, recent work has shown that it is indeed possible to change modulation formats between optical packets without disruption, at the cost of a small amount of extra overhead before each packet [3,4]. It would also be possible to retain the same channel throughput with a less efficient modulation format if the spectrum was allowed to overlap adjacent (free) channels in the fiber. Thus, shutting down a channel would save the energy not only for that channel but for neighboring channels as well. However, since the current International Telecommunication Union standard requires each channel to stay within a fixed 50 GHz

• Adding more FEC overhead: Inserting extra parity bits at the cost of lower effective throughput will increase the FEC’s ability to correct errors and thus provide a so-called coding gain corresponding to increasing the SNR. As a consequence, less regeneration, amplification, and signal processing is needed to achieve the same BER. The FEC overhead can be changed without disrupting the traffic if the decoder knows exactly where in the bit stream this change is taking place—given that the FEC is designed with this functionality in mind.
While all of these parameters are good candidates for saving power, there is a clear difference in how often the different parameters can be changed in practice as well as the potential power reduction from changing the parameters.
In this paper, we focus on the FEC overhead as a means of dynamically trading off bandwidth for power reduction and aim to use the extra overhead to reduce the power consumption of the FEC circuitry itself. The main idea is that modern iterative decoders will need fewer decoding iterations, and thus less power, to restore the data as the parity-to-information bit ratio increases. As mentioned, an advantage of manipulating the FEC is that this can be done with no ill effect to the live traffic on the link, since the physical part of the transmissions system is independent on the FEC format. Also, with the right implementation, the rate resolution can be made very high with limited impact on the hardware complexity of the FEC. For these reasons, the code rate is an excellent parameter for utilizing small and brief traffic variations, i.e., the ripples in the capacity demand, to conserve energy. Even though the immediate power reduction will be relatively small compared to, e.g., turning off an entire WDM channel, the effect will accumulate, as it can run continuously on top of other power reduction measures. Hence, it is possible to save a small amount of power all the time.
In order to facilitate seamless integration into existing systems, the proposed scheme is designed as a transparent add-on to transceivers running the optical transmission network (OTN) protocol [6] and is fully backward compatible. Hence, this proposed extension can easily coexist with current OTN implementations in a network.
The rest of the paper is structured as follows: Section II gives an overview of related work in the field of adaptive FEC for optical networks. Section III gives a basic introduction to the type of FEC codes used in this paper and the challenges related to dynamic rate adaptation. Section IV describes how the OTN standard is modified to allow frame-by-frame rate adaptation, and Section V describes how manipulating the rate can reduce the power consumption of the FEC decoder. Section VI presents simulation results, which indicate how this scheme will affect the power consumption of an optical transmission system for a specific subset of FEC codes with different code rates. Section VII concludes our paper and provides an outlook for future work.

II. RELATED WORK
In recent years, there has been increasing interest in the idea of employing the familiar concepts from adaptive radio communication to optical links. In [7] an adaptive FEC for optical communication systems was proposed that was later expanded in [8] to include adaptive modulation. The FEC adaptation is done by using a separate FEC algorithm for each rate, which gives the optimal FEC performance, although it is more costly in terms of hardware complexity. The main goal of the scheme is to automatically trade off transmission bandwidth for optical reach by using the measured BER at the receiver to select the appropriate FEC/modulation combination. The authors assume that a feedback path exists to relay this information back to the transmitter. This scheme works well for finding the most efficient static parameters for an optical link but lacks the capacity to perform fast adaptation without packet loss, since it is not designed for dynamic power reduction. Reference [9] describes the benefit of using soft-decision (SD) FEC codes, specifically low-density parity check (LDPC) codes [10], to improve the reach of long haul optical networks, but also highlights the heavy complexity and high power consumption of the iterative SD decoding. To reduce the overall power consumption of the network, the authors suggest adapting the number of decoding iterations based on the BER performance, which comes from the quality and length of the individual links. Using fewer iterations results in lower coding gain but reduces the processing load on the FEC decoder. Using dynamic frequency and voltage scaling (DFVS), this translates to a more than linear reduction in the power consumption of the decoder with respect to the number of iterations. In a simulated U.S. network, the authors show a power reduction of up to 82% in the FEC decoders compared to using the worst case number of iterations for all links. As with the rate adaptive FEC described in [7] and [8], this scheme is also focused on optimizing the FEC of a static link. However, the main idea of reducing the number of iterations based on the post-FEC BER is well suited for elastic transmission systems, where the BER performance can be improved at the cost of decreased throughput. This is explained further in Section V.

The standard FEC code used in OTN is a hard-decision Reed–Solomon (RS) (239,255) code [6,11]. As depicted in Fig. 1, this FEC makes it possible to transmit data reliably at much lower SNRs than without FEC coding. The cost is a 7% parity overhead on top of the information bits, corresponding to a code rate of approximately 0.9. To further improve the noise resilience of optical transmission systems, the more advanced LDPC FEC codes have been suggested [9]. An LDPC code of rate R  k∕n, where k is the length of the information sequence and n is the length of the transmitted coded sequence, is usually defined by its parity check matrix H. The number of columns of H is n, and the number of rows shows how many checks the code performs on the received code word. A bit sequence c is a valid code word if cHT  0, where 0 is an all-zero vector and HT is called the syndrome former of the code. LDPC codes are usually decoded using an iterative message passing algorithm, e.g., sum–product or min-sum, which work with soft bits. Working with a soft bit means that instead of using the received bit value (“1” or “0”), which may be in error due to channel noise, the decoder also includes information about the reliability of the bit. Using SD decoding is shown to give gains of up to 3 dB compared to hard-decision decoding, depending on the channel, the code, the decoding algorithm, etc. [11]. The reliability of the bit is usually represented by its log-likelihood ratio (LLR). The LLR Lb of a certain bit b is given by the log of the ratio of the probabilities of that bit being a “1” and a “0”: Lb  logpb  1∕pb  0. The hard decision is obtained by taking the sign of the LLR: “1” if Lb > 0 and “0” if Lb < 0. By including soft information in the FEC calculations, an SD LDPC is able to achieve a coding gain much higher than standard hard-decision codes like RS(255,239) and can reconstruct information at much lower SNR values. Each iteration of the LDPC code increases the certainty of the LLR values. After a number of iterations, a hard decision is made based on the sign of the LLR, and the bits are passed on to the next processing step in the receiver.

B. Soft-Decision Decoding of Convolutional Low-Density Parity Check Codes
In [13] the authors proposed a new class of LDPC codes, called LDPC convolutional codes (LDPC-CCs). LDPC-CCs rely on the periodic structure of the parity check matrix to accommodate practical encoding and message-passing decoding of arbitrary block lengths. Here we describe the properties of the LDPC-CCs regarding practical implementation and possibility for rate adaptation. For a more comprehensive description and analysis of the error-correction capabilities of the codes, the reader is referred to [13,14]. Convolutional codes, and thus LDPC-CCs can be described by their memory M. The memory of a conventional convolutional code shows how many previous bits the current encoded bit is dependent on. In the present work, we restrict ourselves to codes with rates R  b∕b  1, where b is an integer, and memories M  T  1, where T is the paritycheck matrix period, because of the simple matrix construction. In the context of LDPC-CC, the memory will show how many previous groups of b  1 bits the current group of b bits depends on. We first construct a bT × T low-density matrix randomly [11], and then use the unwrapping procedure described in [13] to obtain the syndrome former HT. It is shown in [13] that the encoding can be performed by a shift register with variable connections to an output xor. These connections are given by the syndrome former, and are thus periodic. Decoding is performed by a number of processors, each performing one iteration of a message-passing algorithm (here we use the min-sum algorithm).
The choice of the code memory is crucial to performance, as well as to hardware implementation complexity and scalability of the system. In [13] it is proven, that the minimum distance of the code, and therefore its error-correction capability, grows with M. However, increasing M requires increasing T, which leads to increased encoding and decoding complexity. Long period means that the encoding and decoding circuits need to be larger, and also increases the hardware memory requirements due to the necessity to store at least one period of the syndrome former.
1) Rate Adaptive Coding: The most straightforward approach to changing the code rate is to use different codes for each required code rate as proposed in [8]. The benefit of this approach is that each code can be tailored to provide

Rasmussen et al.
the optimal BER performance at the given rate. The drawback is that the hardware will need to support multiple different codes, potentially requiring a separate encoding/ decoding circuit for each code, which increases the hardware complexity of the FEC. A different approach to support a large number of code rates with minimum hardware complexity is to implement just one mother code of medium rate and then use puncturing and shortening to respectively increase and decrease the code rate at the output. Puncturing works by selectively removing parity bits from the encoded frame before transmission and inserting erasures, i.e., bits with zero LLR values, before decoding at the receiver. Shortening works in a similar fashion, but reduces the number of information bits instead. Since the min-sum is very sensitive to puncturing/ shortening, this method would require very careful design of the puncturing/shortening pattern.
To get the best possible variable code performance while keeping the hardware overhead to a minimum, we propose using a system with a static random-access memory (SRAM)-defined encoder/decoder circuit. Such a system would use the same basic logic blocks to dynamically change the syndrome former to the one for the desired code rate. This is done simply by manipulating the connections from the shift register, based on the content of the SRAM. In this way, the codes for each rate can be highly optimized, since they do not have to rely on the same base matrix. Both the encoder and the decoder are designed to support the lowest possible rate, which will require the most hardware logic due to the increased length of the code for a fixed information word length. The higher rates can then be obtained from the base circuit by manipulating the upper address bits of the SRAM. The exact design and implementation of the dynamic encoder/decoder is out of the scope of this paper.
2) Terminating the LDPC-CCs: Since the OTN is a frame-based communication standard, each code block must be at most as long as the frame. This requires that the encoding process is stopped once the desired length is reached, while the encoded bit sequence c must still be a part of the code space, or cHT  0. The process of ensuring code integrity is known as termination.
Termination of a convolutional code usually means feeding the encoder a certain bit stream, which sets the shift register to the all-zero state. However, since the connections here are flexible, and since the encoder has feedback, this is not as straightforward as for conventional convolutional code. In [14], the authors derive a termination circuit for the LDPC-CCs. However, it is rather complex for hardware implementation. In this work, we choose to terminate the encoder by zero-padding, i.e., we add a number of zeros to the information, which are known to the receiver, and the resulting parity bits are sent on the channel along with the original code word. This does not bring the shift register to an all-zero state, but if the zero sequence is long enough, the performance degradation due to the improper termination is negligible. Here we use a zero sequence of length bM  1, which we found to result in less than 0.1 dB loss compared to the code with proper termination

IV. FRAME-BY-FRAME RATE ADAPTATION

In order to perform truly gapless code rate adaptation, the receiver needs to know exactly where in the bit stream the new code rate starts. For this purpose out-of-band signaling as described in [7] is insufficient. Instead we propose transmitting this information along with each optical frame. Not only will this approach enable gapless code rate transitions over a single link, but it also makes it possible to use this scheme in packet switched optical networks, where the incoming packets may originate from different sources and have traveled along different light paths, thus requiring different code rates to reliably reach the receiver. In the following subsections we describe how this extra information is encoded into the standard OTN frame format and how the system as a whole can be modified for rate adaptive SD codes while retaining backward compatibility with existing OTN equipment.

A. Frame Format

Figure 3 shows the basic frame format used by the OTN standard. The first 6 bytes of the frame contain the frame alignment sequence (FAS), a fixed pattern that is used to detect frame boundaries in the serial bit stream. We propose adding an additional code identifier (CI) field immediately after the FAS that conveys the code rate of the frame to the receiver. The new frame format is depicted in Fig. 4. It has the same overall format as the standard OTN frame, aside from the CI field and a variable amount of SD FEC bits, which can be placed at the end of the frame or distributed over the entire frame depending on the type of SD code used. This is of special concern, since the CI field is not covered by the FEC. This issue will be addressed in the following section.

C. Frame Alignment
The purpose of the OTN FAS is to allow the receiver to align itself to the boundaries of the incoming frame. Once aligned, the following boundaries are implicitly given by the fixed frame length, and hence for subsequent frames, the FAS only serves to validate the correct alignment. In our proposed design, the length of a frame is no longer fixed but rather a function of the CI value. Furthermore, the system is designed to function at BERs above 10−2, which is much higher than a standard OTN, so the OTN practice of scanning for error-free FASs to achieve and maintain frame lock is not suitable for our revised system. In order to locate the frame boundaries in this proposal, the following changes are made to the standard OTN frame aligner, as depicted in Fig. 6. The same modifications apply for detecting loss of frame alignment:
1. The FAS field is allowed to contain a small amount of errors e to facilitate alignment at higher BERs than standard OTN systems.
2. To reduce the risk of aligning with a false FAS (e.g., in the payload), the system scans for the whole 6 byte FAS, as opposed to a standard OTN, which only scans for the middle 4 bytes.
3. The correctness of the CI field immediately following serves to further verify the frame alignment.

The number of allowed errors e in the FAS has a significant impact on how fast the system regains frame alignment after a service disruption has occurred. As depicted in Fig. 7, two consecutive acceptable FASs (errors < e) are required to obtain a lock. If e is too low compared to the BER, the probability of receiving two such FASs is correspondingly small. Hence, it will likely take several frames to obtain frame alignment. On the other hand, if e is too large, there will be a higher risk of false frame alignment, which is equally problematic. Due to the large FAS used, and the fact that two sequences need to be detected to obtain frame alignment, the issue of false frame alignment is only relevant for large values of e. To ascertain how the choice of e affects the frame alignment time of the system, the average number of frames to obtain alignment has been calculated as a function of the SNR for different values of e. The results are depicted in Fig. 7. As is shown, it is very important not to set e too low, as this may cause extremely long alignment times as well as a high risk of losing alignment during normal operation. For the very low SNR values, which are supported by the codes presented in this paper, an e value of 5–8 is recommended for optimal performance.

As discussed in [9], it is possible to reduce the power consumption of an iterative FEC circuit by reducing the number of iterations performed on the data. This can be done if the SNR margin of the link is high enough to absorb the resulting loss in coding gain. However, the SNR margin can also be artificially increased by selecting an FEC code with a lower rate, i.e., by sacrificing information throughput for additional parity bits. With more parity information to work with, the FEC decoder is able to achieve the same BER with less decoding iterations, just as if the actual signal quality was increased. Hence, instead of operating a code with a high rate and many iterations, we can choose to lower the rate and only use a couple of iterations to achieve the same BER. Since the receiver can see the code rate directly from the header of the incoming frames, the number of iterations performed by the iterative decoder could be configured accordingly based on predetermined BER estimates. However, to achieve maximum flexibility and performance, we propose letting the FEC decoder itself determine the required number of iterations based on its own output, as discussed in Section III.
In order to save power in the decoder by reducing the number of iterations, it is important that the actual decoder circuitry is designed with this feature in mind. In the following section, we investigate how two different implementation strategies, using a serial or a parallel

In a serial iterative decoder, all iterations are performed by the same physical circuit. Hence, the required clock speed of the circuit is a function of the number of iterations and the desired decoder throughput. In order to convert this into reduced power consumption, we propose using the well-known concept of DFVS as suggested in [9] and described in [15]. The basic concept is to dynamically decrease the clock frequency driving the decoder to match the current load. Hence, if the maximum capacity of the iterative decoder is eight iterations but only two iterations are required, the circuit can be driven at 1∕4 its maximum frequency. Since the power consumption of modern microchips increases approximately linearly with the clock frequency, this translates to a factor 4 reduction in power consumption. In addition, when the clock frequency is reduced, the voltage to the circuit can be decreased as well, reducing the power consumption even further. The approximate power consumption can be described by the equation P  k · F · V2 [15,16], where k is a constant of the circuit, F is the clock frequency, and V is the core voltage. A simulation of the normalized power consumption as a function of the number of iterations is depicted in Fig. 9. Instead, the redundant iteration circuits can be bypassed once the estimated BER of a frame is low enough. The basic principle of such a system is depicted in Fig. 10. Based on an estimation of the signal quality, the input from the link will run through X iteration elements to reduce the BER. Once the desired number of iterations has been reached, the processed data is redirected through simple bypass elements instead of proceeding through the iteration chain. The bypass elements consist of a small multiplexer and a delay line and thus have minimal power consumption compared to the much more complex LDPC logic. By means of clock gating [19], the remaining iteration circuits can be deactivated, thus eliminating the dynamic power dissipation and leaving only the much smaller static power dissipation (i.e., leakage current) [16]. In this fashion, a reduction in the number of decoding iterations for a frame can provide an approximately linear reduction in the power dissipation of the LDPC decoder.
When calculating the power consumption of the FEC decoder at different code rates, the complexity in terms of number of computations per information bit (or in this case, per frame) of the LDPC decoder for each iteration also needs to be taken into account. The complexity of a decoding iteration is linearly dependent on the number of “1”s in H [11]. As discussed in Section III, H is designed from a fairly small matrix of size bT × T, where regular structure is not hard to obtain. The unwrapping procedure ensures that this regularity is kept for the resulting complete matrix. The degree of the variable nodes and check nodes can therefore be kept constant for the different rates, and the complexity will scale linearly with the length of the code when the information, i.e., the size of the inner OTN frame, is kept constant. Hence, even though the lower code rates allow for fewer decoding iterations, each iteration will consume more power. The codes we analyze here are of rate R  b∕b  1, for b  1; 2; …; 6. If we reduce the rate from Rhigh to Rlow, the complexity will be increased by Rhigh∕Rlow. In the worst case, the rate will be reduced from Rhigh  6∕7 to Rlow  1∕2, with the complexity increasing by 12∕7 ≈ 2. In order to reduce the complexity overall, the number of iterations will have to be decreased sufficiently enough to compensate for the complexity increase due to the lower code rate. This term will be taken into account when estimating the overall power consumption reduction on a per-frame basis.

VII. CONCLUSION
In this paper we have demonstrated a new scheme for energy conservation on optical links through adaptive FEC. The paper demonstrates how a rate adaptive OTN-based transmission system can be built with minor adjustment compared to a standard OTN transceiver, while retaining backward compatibility with existing

equipment. The proposed system can perform rate adjustments on a frame-by-frame basis with no disruption to the traffic on the link. While adaptive FEC has several applications, this paper has focused on using FEC adaptation of concatenated RS and SD LDPC-CC FECs as a means of reducing power consumption in the FEC decoder itself during periods of low load. The presented studies show that the number of necessary decoding iterations to reach errorfree transmission can be significantly reduced by decreasing the code rate R, at the cost of decreased throughput. By reducing the code rate, and thus the number of decoding iterations, during periods of low-capacity demand, the power consumption of the investigated FEC can be reduced by up to 75% compared to its nominal value. Since the scheme presented in this paper can perform FEC adjustments on a frame-by-frame basis, it is able to provide a constant power reduction, adapting to the static conditions of the connection as well as to the dynamics of the network on a microsecond timescale. As such, excess SNR margin and unused data bandwidth are readily converted into fewer joules per information bit with no ill effect on the transmission system.
