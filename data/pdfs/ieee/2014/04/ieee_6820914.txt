2014 IEEE International Symposium on Bioelectronics and Bioinformatics (IEEE ISBB 2014)

Thermal-Aware Task Mapping for Reconfigurable Channel Decoding

Shu-Yen Lin, Cheng-Hung Lin, and Ho-Yun Su
Department of Electrical Engineering Yuan Ze University
Jungli, Taiwan, 32003, R.O.C. sylin@saturn.yzu.edu.tw

Abstract—Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) and Turbo codes are the most popular channel coding for wire-line and wireless communication systems. The reason is that their errorcorrecting abilities are much closer to the Shannon limit. In real design, many channel decoders are designed with the reconfigurable architectures to support multiple communication systems. In the reconfigurable architectures, only parts of the function units works in runtime, and the task mapping become very important. The unfair task mapping may result in the unbalanced thermal distribution and increase the hotspots in the channel decoder chip. In this work, we propose three thermalaware task mapping schemes for a reconfigurable radix-4 softinput soft-output (SISO) kernel of channel decoding. By the proposed thermal-aware task mapping, the peak temperature can reduce up to 4.8°C in our experiments.
Keywords—LDPC code, Turbo code, Thermal mapping, Hotspot
I. INTRODUCTION
Recently, mobile devices have become the norm in wireless markets. In the prevalent wireless market, two major standards are LTE and WiMAX. However, high-end mobile devices need high-quality wireless transmission service requirements. The turbo code [1] and low-density parity-check (LDPC) code [2] are adopted in LTE and WiMAX standards because of their excellent error correction performance. In addition, LDPC code has been adopted in an e-Health application [3]. Recently, a reconfigurable soft-input soft-output (SISO) decoding kernel [4] displayed the combination of turbo and LDPC decoding algorithm by extending the dual-mode turbo decoding algorithm [5] to the LDPC decoding algorithm [6]. This reconfigurable SISO kernel has good flexibility and scalability to support turbo and LDPC decoding for LTE and WiMAX standards. Moreover, several reconfigurable SISO kernels can be used in multi-core system-on-a-chip (SoC) or network-on-achip (NoC) systems for a flexible multi-standard platform.
For high-throughput communication platform, not only power but also thermal issue must be considered. In [9], the static task mapping scheme for network-on-chip based LDPC decoder was proposed. The proposed scheme made the thermal profile more uniform. It reveals that the task mapping can solve the thermal issue. For the multi-mode channel decoding design, the task mapping scheme between different modes is an important issue. Bad mapping methods may result in thermal hotspot in the chip. In this work, new results of timing, floorplan, and power of the reconfigurable radix-4 SISO kernel

[4] are given first. Then, we analyze this SISO kernel and propose three different mapping schemes. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
¾ We analyze the timing, power, and thermal behaviors of the reconfigurable SISO architecture [4].
¾ We propose three thermal-aware task mapping schemes to reduce the peak temperature and solve the mismatch thermal profiles between turbo and LDPC mode. The experimental results show that the temperature can reduce up to 4.8°C.
II. RECONFIGURABLE RADIX-4 SISO KERNEL [3]
To support the alternative single-binary/double-binary (SB/DB) turbo decoding for LTE/WiMAX and LDPC decoding for WiMAX, an area-efficient reconfigurable radix-4 SISO kernel was proposed in [4]. This SISO kernel employs the radix-4 SB/DB enhanced max-log-maximum a posterior probability algorithm (EML-MAP) in [5] for radix-4 turbo decoding. In order to have the decoding for LDPC codes, this SISO kernel also employs the radix-4 forward and backward algorithm (FBA) that is extended from radix-2 FBA in [6].
Fig. 1 shows the block diagram of proposed radix-4 SISO kernel. The key computational components include the branch metrics calculator unit (BMU), the radix-4 forward recursive processing unit (RPU-α), radix-4 backward RPU (RPU-β) and radix-4 dummy RPU (RPU-βd), the state metrics cache (SMC), the a-posteriori calculator unit (ACU), and the extrinsic calculator unit (ECU). L/T signal of this SISO kernel is the control signal to select the LDPC/turbo mode. The SISO kernel excludes the check node memory (CNM), bit node memory (BNM), and channel value memory (CVM) from the kernel design because they are highly standard dependent. The details of the reconfigurable radix-4 SISO kernel are discussed in [4].
III. ANALYSIS OF RECONFIGURABLE CHANNEL DECODING
In this section, we analyze the timing, power, and thermal behaviors of the reconfigurable channel decoding. Based on the analysis, we propose three thermal-aware task mapping schemes, which are introduced in Section IV.

A. Timing Analysis
Fig. 3(a) shows the timing diagram of reconfigurable radix-4 SISO kernel in turbo mode based on the sliding window decoding where W = 24 is the basic window size. The radix-4 SISO kernel activates all modules in turbo mode. The input data are channel value and a-priori LLR. The output data are a-posteriori LLR and extrinsic LLR. The decoding time of 7L completes a basic window decoding.
Fig. 3(b) shows the timing diagram for reconfigurable radix-4 SISO kernel in LDPC mode based on the two-phase message-passing where z = 96 is the expending factor of QCLDPC matrix. In LDPC mode, the radix-4 SISO kernel activates the Radix-4 RPU-α, Radix-4 RPU-β, Radix-4 RPUβd and ACU. In the first phase, the input data are extrinsic LLR and the output data are new extrinsic LLR. In the second phase, the input data are new extrinsic LLR and channel value. The output data are a-posteriori LLR. The decoding time of 8z completes an iteration decoding.
The decoding time for the turbo decoding is much higher than that for the LDPC decoding. If we make the overall decoding time for turbo decoding close to the one for the LDPC decoding, the number of decoded LDPC information blocks is 6x higher than the number of decoded turbo information blocks. Hence, the ratio of LDPC and turbo information blocks is set to 6:1 in the following simulations.

B. Power and Thermal Analysis
TABLE I shows the power evaluation of reconfigurable radix-4 SISO kernel for the alternating turbo and LDPC decoding. The post-layout power result is evaluated in TSMC 90-nm CMOS process. We assume that this kernel is operated at 250-MHz frequency and 1.1-V core voltage. The result shows that the power in LDPC mode is lower than the one in turbo mode. Besides, RPU-α, RPU-β, RPU-βd, and ACU are the major power contributors. In turbo mode, all modules work and the total power is 382.6 mW. In LDPC mode, the working modules are changed at different time intervals and the total power is 103.78 ~ 197.24mW.
To analyze the thermal behaviors of the reconfigurable channel decoding, we must have the floorplan information. Fig. 2 shows a rough floorplan for the multi-mode radix-4 SISO kernel in a core area of 0.36 mm2. The location and the area ratio for each component are also revealed in Fig. 2.

Accurate area evaluations are obtained by using Verilog HDL codes synthesized in TSMC 90-nm CMOS process. Note that Ctrl+CS and ACU are divided into many parts in the floorplan.
In Fig. 4, we analyze the thermal behavior of reconfigurable radix-4 SISO kernel for the alternating turbo/LDPC decoding. RPU-α, RPU-β, RPU-βd, and ACU are the major thermal contributors. The result shows that the increase of the temperature in turbo mode is higher than the one in LDPC mode. Because all modules work at each time interval, the temperature increases continuously in turbo mode. In LDPC mode, the temperature increases slower than the temperature in turbo mode. Based on the analysis of the thermal behaviors, we propose three thermal mapping algorithms to overcome the unbalanced increase of the temperature in turbo and LDPC modes, which are introduced in Section IV.

IV. THERMAL-AWARE TASK MAPPING
According to the thermal analysis in Section III, three thermal-aware task mapping algorithms are proposed in this section. The key ideas are shown as follows:
¾ Balance the unbalanced increase of the temperature in turbo and LDPC modes.
¾ Apply idle cycles to avoid the accumulated power in turbo mode.
¾ Reduce the duration time of the turbo decoding by using multiple SISO kernels.
The detailed mapping schemes are introduced in part A~C.

SISO kernels can be merged into a group (Group 0 and Group 1). In each group, two SISO kernels can work simultaneously with double throughput rate. Besides, one working SISO kernel and one idle SISO kernel are also supported, but the throughput is the same as single SISO kernel. For one working kernel and one idle kernel within a group, it is possible to further reduce the temperature by using the scheme described in part A. Turbo sub-block decoding with time Ts/2 can be migrated from the working SISO kernel to the idle one within a group, as shown in Fig. 7(b). The middle part of turbo subblock decoding is chosen to avoid the accumulated power in turbo decoding.

A. Interchange the sub-block decoding between turbo and LDPC mode According to the unbalanced increase of the temperature
between turbo and LDPC mode, we must remap the tasks to reduce the peak temperature. Because the peak temperature is due to the accumulated power in turbo mode, we avoid the consecutively execute the turbo block decoding in our mapping method. If the decoding time for each Turbo and LDPC block are T and L, T and L can be divided into many sub-blocks with decoding time Ts and Ls. Besides, the sub-blocks in turbo and LDPC mode are interchanged in time to reduce the accumulated temperature in turbo mode. For instance, Fig. 5 shows one turbo and one LDPC blocks are divided into four turbo and four LDPC sub-blocks.
Fig. 5 The mapping scheme to interchange the sub-block decoding between turbo and LDPC mode.
B. Add idle cycles within turbo sub-block decoding In this scheme, we apply the scheme in part A and add the
idle cycles to reduce the accumulated temperature in turbo mode, as shown in Fig. 6. We add the idle cycles with duration time I in the middle of the turbo sub-block, and each turbo sub-block decoding are divided into two sub-blocks decoding with duration time Ts/2. This method can avoid the continuous accumulation of the power in the turbo sub-block decoding. However, the overall turbo decoding time also increases.

V. EXPERIMENTS
In this section, we evaluate the performance and thermal results for the proposed task mapping methods in Section IV. For thermal simulation, HotSpot 5.02 [7] is applied for thermal simulation. The chip and package parameters are referred to in [8]. The ambient temperature is set to 45°C. In our experiments, we analyze the cases to decode 16 turbo blocks and 96 LDPC blocks, which satisfy the aforementioned ratio.
A. Interchange sub-processes with different Ts and Ls
In this experiment, we analyze the interchange sub-blocks with different Ts and Ls. Three cases are considered: Ts = T/48, T/24, and T/12. Ls are equal to L/48, L/24, and L/12, which represents 8z, 16z, and 32z cycles. Fig. 8(a) shows the temperature for the simulation time between 1 to 2 ms. The result shows that Ts = T/48 can achieve the lowest temperature and the least temperature variation. In the simulation to decode 16 turbo and 96 LDPC blocks, the maximal temperature for these cases are 65.82, 65.84, and 66.17°C. Ts = T/48 also achieves the lowest temperature.
In Fig. 8(b), two possible task mapping schemes are discussed. Turbo-LDPC means the SISO kernel continuously decodes 16 turbo blocks and 96 LDPC blocks. LDPC-Turbo represents the SISO kernel continuously decodes 96 LDPC blocks and 16 turbo blocks. Turbo-LDPC and LDPC-Turbo are the baseline in our experiments. These mapping schemes result in the peak temperatures, 68.23 and 68.25 °C. Comparing to these methods, the SISO kernel applying Ts = T/48 can reduce the peak temperatures from 2.41 to 2.43°C.

B. Different idle cycle with different decoding time
In this experiment, we add different idle cycles within the turbo sub-block decoding. Four idle cycles (I) are considered: 0, Ts/8, Ts/4, and Ts/2. Fig. 9(a) shows the temperature for the simulation time between 1 to 2 ms. The result shows that I = Ts/2 can achieve the lowest temperature. To decode 16 turbo and 96 LDPC blocks, the maximal temperatures for I = 0, I = Ts/8, I = Ts/4, and I = Ts/2 are 65.82, 65.78, 65.5, and 65.02°C. Although adding idle cycles can reduce the maximal temperature, it also increases the decoding time. Fig. 9(b) shows the decoding time for these cases. The decoding time increases 6.5%, 12.94%, and 25.89% for I = Ts/8, I = Ts/4, and I = Ts/2.
C. Task Migration by Multiple SISO Kernels
In this experiment, we analyze the task migration for multiple SISO kernels. First, we compare the single SISO kernel with the 2 SISO kernels. Both of them have the same decoding throughput. The result for the simulation time between 1 to 2 ms is shown as Ts = T/48 and T/48 + SISO2 in Fig. 10(a). The 2 SISO kernels can achieve lower temperature than the 1 SISO kernel. In the simulation of decoding 16 turbo and 96 LDPC blocks, the maximal temperature for these cases are 66.1 and 63.94°C. the 2 SISO kernels can reduce 2.16°C than the 1 SISO kernel. Besides, the 2 SISO kernels adding the idle cycles are also considered. The cases with I = Ts/8, Ts/4, and Ts/2 are shown in Fig. 10(a). Ts = T/48 + SISO2 + I=Ts/2 can achieve the lowest temperature. However, the decoding time also increases, which is discussed in Section VB. In Fig. 10(b), we summarize the improvement of the proposed task mapping methods. Comparing to the LDPCTurbo, Ts = T/48 can reduce 2.2°C. If Ts = T/48 adding the idle cycles, I = Ts/2, the temperature can reduce 3.2°C, but the decoding time increases. If we also consider 2 SISO kernels to further reduce the temperature, the temperature can reduce up to 4.8°C.
