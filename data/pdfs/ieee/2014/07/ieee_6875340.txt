I. INTRODUCTION

Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have been widely investigated due to their strong error correcting capability and their reasonable encoding and decoding complexity. In practical applications, protograph-based LDPC codes [1]– or, more succinctly, protograph codes – have received much attention because of their structure design and efficient hardware implementation.

Many approaches to the construction of protograph codes have been proposed. In particular, Divsalar et al. [2] invented the family of accumulate-repeat-4-jagged-accumulate (AR4JA) codes, which have typical minimum distance that grows linearly with block size, as well as iterative decoding thresholds approaching the Shannon limit on binary-input additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels. Recently, Nguyen et al. constructed another class of protograph codes, which we call NND codes, that also satisfy the linear minimum distance growth property with thresholds on the AWGN channel that are even closer to the Shannon limit than those of AR4JA codes [3]. It has also been shown that protograph codes work well over intersymbol interference channels [4]. Ratecompatible protograph code constructions were proposed in [3], [5], [6].

Most protograph constructions that yield capacity-

approaching thresholds, such as the AR4JA codes and NND

codes, make use of punctured variable nodes, sometimes called

precode nodes. However punctured variable nodes tend to slow

decoder convergence, so many iterations are required to decode

This suggests that NND codes may not be suitable for latencysensitive applications, such as storage devices and real time communications, where a large number of decoder iterations cannot be tolerated.
In this paper, we present a new family of protograph codes with no punctured variable nodes. Our protograph codes have the linear minimum distance growth property and the lowest iterative decoding thresholds among protograph codes without punctured variable nodes that have been reported in the literature. Simulation results show that these new protograph codes perform better than NND codes when the number of decoder iterations is small.
To construct the new protograph codes efficiently, we exploit the lengthening method which generates protographs of higher rates by expanding sequentially from a mother protograph [3]. The mother protograph is constructed by using both differential evolution and exhaustive search techniques.

III. PROTOGRAPH DESIGN
Protograph design involves the search for a base matrix which has the best possible threshold while preserving the linear minimum distance growth property. The size of the search space is at most (1 + ep)mpnp − 1, where ep denotes the maximum allowable integer entry in the base matrix. Due to the exponential growth of the search space as a function of the size of the base matrix, it is difficult to find an optimal base matrix of large size in a brute force manner. More precisely, we cannot carry out such an exhaustive search on a standard desk-top computer when ep > 2 and mpnp > 16. In this paper, we therefore concentrate only on the design of base matrices with number of rows mp = 4.
With mp = 4, we would need to limit the number of columns np to 4. However, to generate codes of rates 1/2 to 9/10, np has to range from 8 to 40. Therefore, a brute-force exhaustive search is not feasible. To overcome this difficulty, we exploit the lengthening method proposed in [3], whereby base matrices of higher rates are generated by expanding sequentially from a mother base matrix. To find the mother

base matrix of size 4×8, we exploit the optimization technique known as differential evolution [8].
In the remainder of this section, we describe the search methods – differential evolution and partial brute-force search – used in our code optimization. We then present the design procedure and apply it to the construction of our new family of protograph base matrices.

A. Differential Evolution

Differential evolution is a parameter optimization algorithm that iteratively tries to improve candidate solutions with regard to a given cost function.

The cost function in our problem is the ensemble threshold computed by the PEXIT algorithm [9] with 500 iterations, which is an approximate method of density evolution [10] for protograph codes. Since the PEXIT algorithm is not only sufficiently accurate3 but also has lower complexity than actual density evolution, we can run the differential evolution algorithm over many iterations. (We use the decoding threshold as our cost function rather than, say, the bit-error probability computed by the PEXIT algorithm with a small number of iterations. This is because our goal is to construct protograph codes that can perform well not only with one specific number of iterations, but also over a range.)

IV. FINITE LENGTH PERFORMANCE

Protograph-based LDPC codes are generated from our base matrices in two lifting steps. First, we lifted the base matrix by a factor of 4 to make all entries 0 or 1, using the progressive edge growth (PEG) algorithm [12] to avoid short cycles. Then, based upon a target information block length, we lifted the resulting 0-1 matrix, using the PEG algorithm to determine a suitable circulant permutation matrix for each nonzero entry.

Simulation results showed that, when the number of decoder iterations is limited (e.g., to a maximum of 20), the new protograph codes perform better than state-of-the-art protograph codes [3] and comparably to R4JA codes [2]. In future work, we plan to design protographs with no punctured variable nodes for rate-compatible protograph-based LDPC codes [3], [5]. We also plan to optimize our protograph design using a figure-of-merit based upon scaling methods [13], rather than by using thresholds computed by the PEXIT algorithm, to more precisely predict finite-length protograph code performance.
