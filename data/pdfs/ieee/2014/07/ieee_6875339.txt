LDPC Convolutional Codes versus QC LDPC Block Codes in Communication Standard Scenarios

Irina E. Bocharova and Boris D. Kudryashov

Rolf Johannesson

Dept. of Information Systems

Dept. of Electrical and Information Technology

Lund University, Lund, Sweden

St.-Petersburg, Russia, Email: {irina,boris}@eit.lth.se

Abstract—Outstanding asymptotical performance demonstrated by low-density parity-check (LDPC) convolutional codes (CC) makes them strong competitors with respect to quasicyclic (QC) LDPC block codes (BC) currently used in a variety of communication standards. However, typically communication standards, for example, DVB-S2 or WIMax standards impose rather serious restrictions on the structure of the employed codes. These restrictions are related to different implementation issues such as existence of low-complexity encoding and decoding, short decoding delay etc. Two scenarios are considered. In one scenario, short-delay and low-complexity constraints are taken into account. In the other scenario, the complexity requirement is relaxed. Both LDPC CCs and QC LDPC BCs with optimized degree distribution and girth profile, which enable lowcomplexity encoding, are constructed for these scenarios. Having both delay and complexity constraints yields QC LDPC BCs that outperform the LDPC CCs. In this scenario LDPC CCs play an important role mostly for constructing tailbiting QC LDPC BCs. On the other hand, assuming only a decoding delay constraint the LDPC CCs can be superior compared to the QC LDPC BCs at the relatively low signal-to-noise ratio region. Moreover, under practically acceptable decoding delays also the LDPC CCs with low-complexity encoding structure beat records in approaching the Shannon limit. A new LDPC CC is presented achieving the BER 10−7 with decoding delay 96000 bits at 0.62 dB, that is, performing only about 0.43 dB from the Shannon limit.
I. INTRODUCTION
The potential of LDPC CCs was demonstrated by random coding arguments long time ago (see, for example, [1], [2], [3]). It is commonly accepted that LDPC CCs significantly outperform their block counterparts. Their excellent nearcapacity behavior motivates attempts to include LDPC CCs into modern communication standards such as WIMax [4], DVB-S2 [5], DVB-T2 [6]. Then the reasonable question arises: why despite of more than 15-year history of existence of LDPC CCs no practical schemes based on them are used in communication standards? One reason could be that unlike LDPC BCs, the thorough application-oriented optimization of LDPC CCs has not been performed yet.
So far, not many publications compare LDPC CCs and LDPC BCs under decoding delay and complexity constraints. Relatively recently, such a comparison was performed in [7]. The authors considered regular protograph-based LDPC CCs and LDPC BCs with relatively small number of ones in each column of their parity-check matrices. Their main conclusion is that LDPC CCs have better BER performance than the

corresponding LDPC BCs, especially for rather short delays (up to 550 information bits). Irregular LDPC BCs and CCs were not included in this comparison.
Typically irregular LDPC BCs are superior compared to regular ones. For this reason they are used in different communication standards. Moreover, in order to facilitate linearcomplexity encoding, the parity-check matrices of the QC LDPC BCs selected for WIMax [4], DVB-S2 [5], and DVB-T2 [6] standards have so-called bi-diagonal structure.
In this paper, we focus on irregular LDPC CCs and QC LDPC BCs both with fast low-complexity encoding. The QC LDPC BCs are obtained by tailbiting their parent convolutional codes. We apply our optimization procedures described in [8] and [9] to codes from these two classes in order to obtain both LDPC CCs and LDPC BCs with good base matrices, degree distributions, and girth profiles. To satisfy delay requirements we restrict the maximal possible degree of the monomials in the parity-check matrix of the LDPC CCs and the tailbiting length of the QC LDPC BCs.
The paper is organized as follows. In Section II, we briefly review notions and optimization techniques which are used for code design in this paper. Complexity and delay restrictions are explained in Section III. Then in Section IV, the BER performances of sliding window decoding (SWD) [10], [7] and belief propagation (BP) decoding are simulated for the new LDPC CCs and LDPC BCs, respectively. We analyze rate R = 1/2 codes with three different delays: 576, 2304, and 64800 bits and compare them with codes in the WIMax and DVB-S2 standards with the same parameters. We show that if the decoding complexity is not limited then the constructed LDPC CCs can have slightly better BER performance in low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) regions than the LDPC BCs from the standards and QC LDPC BCs obtained by applying the same optimization technique. If both decoding delay and complexity are taken into account then the obtained LDPC CCs lose compared to the LDPC BCs from standards. Conclusions are drawn in Section V.

In order to construct optimal, in terms of their BER performance, LDPC CCs or QC LDPC BCs we should solve an optimization problem which is computationally intractable. However, it is shown in [9] that a suboptimal solution can be found with an acceptable computational complexity and without significant loss of generality by restricting the sizes of search sets (the sets of possible base and degree matrices) using multi-criteria analysis. It is well-known (see, for example, [7] and references therein) that one problem related to SWD is the existence of low-weight columns in the parity-check matrix (7). When constructing new LDPC CCs we have done an attempt to avoid low-weight columns by optimizing the structure of the submatrix H0 in (4). However, the BER performance obtained when using such optimized convolutional codes turned out to be worse than for nonoptimized ones. A possible explanation of this failure is the existence of a large number weight-2 columns in the bidiagonal parity-check matrix considered in the next section.

For our simulations we have chosen a compromise shown in Fig. 1: the buffer content is updated by a linear combination of channel output and newly obtained soft estimates. This updating is performed only for a sub-window of length Sc for which final hard decisions are made as well. In most simulations we used α = 1/2. For large block lengths and low SNRs α = 1/4 was preferred.
The arguments in favor of introducing α are the following. First, by experiments we found that complete updating (α = 1) leads to error propagation, and keeping non-updated channel data (α = 0) leads to worse BER performance. Second, using all W c estimates for updating is not efficient due to the existence of low-weight columns in the parity-check matrix (7) (see, for example, [7]).
The larger parameter S the fewer decoding trials in which the same subblock of c channel outputs participates. Thus, by choosing S an additional BER performance-decoding complexity trade-off can be obtained.
The decoder complexity can be measured by several parameters like delay, number of computations, memory requirements, feasibility for parallelization, etc. The role and significance of each parameter varies depending on a specific application. In our investigation we restricted ourselves to studying rate R = 1/2 codes for the following three delay values: relatively small (576 bits), average (2304 bits), and very large (64800 bits). The motivation for such a choice is the existence of strong block code-competitors used in the communication standards WIMax [4] and DVB-S2 [5].

It follows from the presented plots that if the complexity restriction is relaxed under fixed decoding delay then the optimized LDPC CCs with SWD show better BER performance behavior than the BP decoding of LDPC BCs from the WIMax and DVB-S2 standards as well as the optimized QC LDPC BCs from [8] in the low SNR region. The observed higher floors for convolutional codes can be explained by the small monomial degrees and the existence of low-weight columns in the parity-check matrix (7). We simulated LDPC CCs with base matrices of different sizes (8 × 16, 12 × 24, and 16 × 32), and typically the larger base matrices yield better BER performance. However, for short and moderate delays (576 and 2304), since permissible syndrome former memories are rather small, the code C1 with a base matrix of size 12×24 was selected (Figs. 2, 3), whereas for large delay (64800) the code C2 with larger base matrix, (16 × 32) shows better BER performance (Fig. 4).
If we require the decoding complexity for LDPC CCs to be comparable with that of BCs then typically the syndrome former memory m is around L/2 blocks of length c, which is smaller than monomial degrees for BCs. As a consequence, for short and moderate delays LDPC CCs C1 lose compared to the optimized block counterparts. For very short delays (576) C1 has even worse BER performance than the LDPC BC from the WIMax standard. However, for moderate delays (2304) code C1 outperforms the WIMax LDPC BC of length 2304, in the relatively low SNR region.
If long decoding delay is acceptable then even under constrained decoding complexity rather large m is allowed. However, in this case the LDPC CC C2 still has worse BER performance compared to the block code from the DVBS2 standard and loses significantly compared to the new optimized LDPC BC B4. Moreover, its BER performace has clearly visible floor region at high SNRs.
Notice that the new optimized LDPC BCs with m = 500 have significantly better BER performance than the LDPC BC from the DVB-S2 standard. Moreover, one of the new codes B4 has length 32384, that is, two times shorter decoding delay than the code from the DVB-S2 standard. The difference between parent LDPC CCs of the new QC LDPC BCs and the newly constructed LDPC CCs is the parameters of optimization; for example, permissible syndrome former memory. In other words, constructing optimized QC LDPC BCs we, in fact, construct LDPC CCs and then the difference between the two classes of codes reduces to the difference in the length of decoded blocks and the difference in termination procedures.
If the complexity restriction is relaxed then the optimized LDPC CC wins compared to the optimized LDPC BC with approximately the same delay at the low SNR region. V. CONCLUSION
The obtained results show that if irregular LDPC CCs suitable for low-complexity encoding are optimized under delay-complexity constraints then they are still inferior to their optimized block counterparts and LDPC BCs from the standards. However, they play important role as parent codes for QC LDPC BCs and are potentially good candidates to be channel capacity approaching codes when large coding delays are acceptable.
