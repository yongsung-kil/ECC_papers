Quasi-Cyclic LDPC Codes on Two Arbitrary Sets of a Finite Field

Juane Li, Keke Liu, Shu Lin and Khaled Abdel-Ghaffar Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, University of California, Davis, CA 95616, USA
Email: {jueli, kkeliu, shulin, ghaffar}@ucdavis.edu

Abstract—This paper presents a simple and flexible method for constructing QC-LDPC codes based on two arbitrary sets of a finite field. Based on this method, a high-rate, high-performance and very low error-floor QC-LDPC code is first constructed and then a class of rate-1/2 QC-LDPC codes whose Tanner graphs have girth 8 or larger is presented. Also presented is a reducedcomplexity iterative decoding algorithm for QC-LDPC codes.

I. INTRODUCTION
Among various types of LDPC codes, quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes are commonly preferred in practical applications due to their advantages in encoding and decoding implementations. There are two major approaches in construction of QC-LDPC codes, graph-theoretic and algebraic approaches. QC-LDPC codes constructed algebraically using mathematical tools such as finite fields, finite geometries and combinatorial designs, in general, give better overall performances in terms of coding gain, rate of decoding convergence and error-floors.
This paper is concerned with construction of QC-LDPC codes of very high rate and rate-1/2 and their decoding with reduced decoding complexity.

II. A GENERAL CONSTRUCTION OF QC-LDPC CODES

(1)

From (1), we easily prove that the matrix B has the following structural properties: (1) all the entries in a row (or a column) of B are distinct elements in GF(q); (2) each row (or each column) of B contains at most one zero element; and (3) no two rows (or two columns) in B have identical entries at any position. The following theorem gives another important property of B which can be easily proved (see also [1], p. 4039).

Theorem 1. Every 2 × 2 submatrix in B is non-singular.

The property given in the above theorem is referred to as the 2 × 2 submatrix (SM) constraint on B.
Let A be a (q − 1) × (q − 1) circulant permutation matrix (CPM) over GF(2). We label both rows and columns of A

from 0 to q − 2. A is uniquely specified by the location of the unique 1-component of its top row (or the 0-th row). There are exactly q − 1 distinct CPMs of size (q − 1) × (q − 1). For 0 ≤ j < q − 1, we represent the nonzero element αj of GF(q) by the (q − 1) × (q − 1) CPM, denoted by A(αj ), for which the single 1-component of its top row is at the location j. This matrix representation is referred to as the (q − 1)-fold CPM-dispersion [2] of the field element αj. Since there are q − 1 nonzero elements in GF(q) and there are exactly q − 1 different CPMs over GF(2) of size (q − 1) × (q − 1), there is a one-to-one correspondence between a nonzero element of GF(q) and a CPM of size (q − 1) × (q − 1). Therefore, each nonzero element of GF(q) is uniquely represented by a CPM of size (q − 1) × (q − 1). For the 0-element of GF(q), its binary matrix dispersion is defined as the (q −1)×(q −1) zero matrix (ZM), denoted by A(α−∞).
Let αls,t = αis +αjt with 0 ≤ s < m and 0 ≤ t < n. Since αls,t is an element in GF(q), ls,t is in L. Then, we can express B in the form: B = [αls,t ]0≤s<m,0≤t<n. If we replace each entry αls,t in B by its corresponding CPM A(αls,t ), we obtain an m×n array H of CPMs and/or ZMs of size (q−1)×(q−1) which is an m(q − 1) × n(q − 1) matrix over GF(2). The null space of H gives a QC-LDPC code C. The 2×2 SM-constraint on B ensures that the Tanner graph G of C has girth at least 6 [1]. The matrix B is called the base matrix for constructing the QC-LDPC code C. The parity-check array H is called the (q − 1)-fold CPM-dispersion of B.
Since B satisfies the 2 × 2 SM-constraint, it is clear that any submatrix B′ of B with size larger than or equal to 2 × 2 also satisfies the 2 × 2 SM-constraint. Then, the null space of the (q − 1)-fold CPM-dispersion H′ of B′ gives a QC-LDPC code C′ whose Tanner graph G′ also has girth at least 6. Using the Fourier transform technique given in [1], we can prove the following theorem (proof omitted).
Theorem 2. A necessary and sufficient condition for the Tanner graph G of the QC-LDPC code C with base matrix B given by (1) to have girth at least 8 is that no 3 × 3 submatrix in the base matrix B has two identical non-zero terms in its determinant expansion.
The condition given in Theorem 2 is referred to as 3 × 3 SM-constraint on B. The necessary and sufficient condition for the Tanner graph of a QC-LDPC code to have girth at least 8 was also proved in [3] but given in a different form. However, the necessary and sufficient condition given in Theorem 2 is much easier to apply. The 2 × 2 and 3 × 3 SM-constraints of a base matrix will be used for determining the girth (or a lower bound) of the Tanner graph of a QC-LDPC code constructed

Example 1. Let α be a primitive element of the prime field GF(131). Let S1 = 1, α1, α2, α3, α4, α5 and S2 =
α6, α7, . . . , α128, α129 be two subsets of nonzero elements of GF(131), one with 6 elements and the other with 124 elements. Form a 6 × 124 base matrix B over GF(131) in the form given by (1). All the entries in B are nonzero elements in GF(131). The 130-fold CPM-dispersion of B gives a 6 × 124 array H of CPMs of size 130 × 130. The array H is a 780 × 16120 matrix over GF(2) with constant column weight 6 and constant row weight 124. The rank of H is 775. The null space of H gives a (6, 124)-regular (16120, 15345) QC-LDPC code C of length 16120 with rate 0.952 (a very high-rate code). The girth of the Tanner graph G of C is exactly 6. The bit and block error performances of the code decoded with 5, 10 and 50 iterations of a scaled min-sum algorithm (MSA) [4], [5], with a scaling factor 0.75 (optimized) are shown in Fig. 1. The performances are computed by an FPGA decoder. From Fig. 1, we see that the decoding of the code converges fast. The performance gap between 5 and 10 iterations is less than 0.2 dB and the performance gap between 10 and 50 iterations is less than 0.1 dB. With 50 iterations of the scaled MSA, the code achieves a BER (bit error rate) of 10−12 without a visible error-floor. It has a beautiful waterfall performance. At the BER of 10−12, the code performs 1.33 dB away from the Shannon limit and achieves a 8.45 dB coding gain over the uncoded BPSK system.
The above construction of base matrices gives a very flexible construction of QC-LDPC codes with girth at least 6. A special case of this construction is to set S1 = S2 = GF(q) = 0, 1, α2, . . . , αq−2 . In this case, the base matrix B defined by (1) is a q × q Latin square over GF(q) [6]. Construction of QC-LDPC codes using Latin squares as base matrices was proposed in [7].
III. CONSTRUCTION OF RATE-1/2 QC-LDPC CODES WITH GIRTH EIGHT OR LARGER
Suppose that we replace a nonzero entry in B = [αls,t ]0≤s<m,0≤t<n by the zero element of GF(q). In the (q − 1)-fold CPM-dispersion H of B, this replacement results in replacing a (q −1)×(q −1) CPM in H by a (q −1)×(q −1) ZM which is referred to as masking [5]. Let k be a nonnegative integer less than the number of nonzero entries in B. Then, replacing k nonzero entries in B by k zeros amounts to masking k CPMs at the locations in H corresponding to the locations of the k nonzero entries in B which are being replaced by zeros. Therefore, masking the array H can be done by masking its base matrix B. Masking k CPMs in H amounts to removing k(q − 1) edges from G. Removing these edges from G may break many cycles in G. As a result, the resultant Tanner graph G⋆ may have a much smaller number of short cycles or a larger girth, or both, and the code given by the null space of the incidence matrix of G⋆ may perform better than the code given by the null space of H.
The operation of masking the base matrix B = αls,t 0≤s<m,0≤t<n can be modeled mathematically as a

Hadamard matrix product. Let Z = [zs,t]0≤s<m,0≤t<n be an m × n matrix with the zero element and unit element of GF(q) as entries. Define the following product of Z and B: Bmask = Z ⊗ B = zs,tαls,t 0≤s<m,0≤t<n where zs,tαls,t = αls,t if zs,t = 1 and zs,tαls,t = 0 if zs,t = 0. In this matrix product operation, some nonzero entries in B are replaced by 0’s. If all the entries in B are nonzero, the number of nonzero entries being replaced by 0’s is equal to the number of zero entries in Z. The (q − 1)-fold CPM-dispersion of Bmask gives an m × n masked array Hmask of CPMs and ZMs. We call Z and Bmask the masking matrix and the masked base matrix, respectively.
In the following, we present a class of rate-1/2 (3, 6)regular QC-LDPC codes whose Tanner graphs have girth 8 or larger. Each such a code is constructed by masking a 4×8 base matrix B(4, 8) over GF(q). In doing so, we choose a matrix B in the form given by (1) with m ≥ 4 and n ≥ 8. Then, we search B to find all possible 4 × 8 submatrices in B from which, with proper masking, masked matrices with column weight 3 and row weight 6 can be obtained and they satisfy the 3 × 3 SM-constraint. The null space of the (q − 1)-fold CPMdispersion Hmask(4, 8) of each of these 4×8 masked matrices gives a (3, 6)-regular QC-LDPC code of length 8(q − 1) with girth at least 8. Using the cycle enumeration algorithm given in [8], we find the number of the shortest cycles of the Tanner graph of each of these codes and determine its girth.
To mask a 4 × 8 submatrix B(4, 8) of B, we need a 4 × 8 masking matrix Z(4, 8) such that the masked 4 × 8 matrix Bmask(4, 8) = Z(4, 8) ⊗ B(4, 8) has column and row weights, 3 and 6, respectively. There are many such 4 × 8 possible masking matrices. To simplify the search and to avoid constructing various masking matrices, we only find those 4×8 submatrices in B which contain no zero entry and mask them with the following fixed masking matrix:

IV. A REDUCED-COMPLEXITY ITERATIVE DECODING SCHEME
Let H be an m × n array of CPMs and/or ZMs of size (q − 1) × (q − 1). Let H0, H1, . . ., Hm−1 denote the m row-blocks of H, each consisting of n CPMs and/or ZMs of size (q − 1) × (q − 1). For 0 ≤ k < m and 0 ≤ s < q − 1, let hk,s = (hk,s,0, hk,s,1, . . . , hk,s,n−1) be the s-th row in the k-th row-block Hk which consists of n sections, hk,s,0, hk,s,1, . . . , hk,s,n−1, each containing q −1 components. Each section is the s-th row of either a CPM or a ZM in the k-th row-block Hk. If we cyclically shift all the n sections of hk,s simultaneously one place to the right within the sections, we obtain the (s + 1)-th row hk,s+1 of Hk which also consists of n sections and each section is the (s + 1)-th row of a CPM or a ZM. The above cyclic-shift within each section of hk,s is referred to as section-wise cyclic-shift of the row hk,s. For s = q − 2, the section-wise cyclic-shift of hk,q−2 results in the 0-th row hk,0 of Hk. Consequently, all the rows of the k-th row-block Hk can be obtained by section-wise cyclically shifting the 0-th row hk,0 q−2 times. This section-wise cyclicshift of the rows of H maintains the CPM-structure of each row-block of H.
Let H⋆0 be an m × n(q − 1) matrix which consists of the first rows h0,0, h1,0, . . ., hm−1,0 of the m row-blocks H0, H1, . . ., Hm−1 of the parity-check array H. Then, it follows from the section-wise cyclic structure of H, that the entire array H can be obtained by section-wise cyclically shifting H⋆0 q − 2 times. This section-wise cyclic structure allows us to decode the QC-LDPC code given by the parity-check array H based on the submatrix H⋆0 alone.
Each decoding iteration based on H⋆0 is called a decoding sub-iteration. At the end of each decoding sub-iteration, the reliabilities of the received symbols are updated with a chosen reliability updating algorithm. Then, the reliability vector (n(q −1) components in n sections) and the received sequence (n(q − 1) symbols in n sections) are section-wise cyclically shifted to the left by one position and used as the input information (maybe together with the channel information) to carry out the next decoding sub-iteration based on H⋆0. It is clear that any q − 1 decoding sub-iterations performed based on H0⋆ are equivalent to one decoding iteration based on the entire parity-check array H. At the end of each decoding subiteration, the syndrome s of the hard-decision of the received sequence (after section-wise cyclically shifted one position to the left) is computed based on the entire parity-check array H. If s = 0, we stop the decoding process; otherwise, we continue the decoding process until a preset maximum number of decoding sub-iterations is reached.
The above decoding process of a QC-LDPC code simply revolves around H0⋆ iteratively. Since the CPM-structure of the parity-check array H is preserved, the wire routing advantage due to the CPM-structure of the parity-check array [12] is maintained. This is a major advantage of the proposed CPMbased iterative decoding scheme over the revolving iterative

decoding (RID) scheme proposed in [11]. Wire routing is a critical issue in hardware implementation of a LDPC code. Another major advantage of this newly proposed decoding scheme over the RID scheme is that it does not require column and row permutations of the parity-check matrix H as the RID does. We call this CPM-based revolving iterative decoding scheme the CPM-RID scheme.

V. CONCLUSION AND REMARK
In this paper, we first presented a simple and very flexible method for constructing QC-LDPC code with CPM structure based on two arbitrary sets of a finite field. Next, we constructed a high-rate QC-LDPC code that achieves highperformance with very low error-floor. Then, we presented a masking and search algorithm for constructing a class of rate1/2 QC-LDPC codes whose Tanner graphs have girth 8 or larger with small number of short cycles. Finally, we presented a reduced-complexity iterative decoding scheme.
