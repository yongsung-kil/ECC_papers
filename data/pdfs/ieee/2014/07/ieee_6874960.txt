Absorbing Set Characterization of Array-Based Spatially Coupled LDPC Codes
David G. M. Mitchell∗, Lara Dolecek†, and Daniel J. Costello, Jr.∗ ∗Dept. of Electrical Engineering, University of Notre Dame, Notre Dame, Indiana, USA,
{david.mitchell, costello.2}@nd.edu †Dept. of Electrical Engineering, University of California, Los Angeles, Los Angeles, California, USA

Abstract—Absorbing sets are combinatorially defined objects existing in the Tanner graph of a low-density parity-check (LDPC) code that have been shown to cause failures in the iterative message-passing decoder when transmission occurs over the additive white Gaussian noise channel. In this paper, we study the absorbing set properties of a class of high-rate array-based spatially coupled LDPC (SC-LDPC) codes that are constructed by coupling together L array-based LDPC block codes. We prove that the smallest absorbing sets existing in the Tanner graph of the SC-LDPC code have the same size as those in the corresponding uncoupled LDPC codes, and the number of such sets grow linearly with L. We show that spatial coupling greatly reduces the average number (per symbol) of minimal sets compared to the uncoupled codes, and we explain that this reduction is due to many absorbing sets and small cycles being ‘broken’ by the coupling process. The large reduction in the number of minimal absorbing sets suggests that arraybased SC-LDPC codes will have significantly improved decoding performance in the high signal-to-noise ratio regime compared to the corresponding uncoupled LDPC codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] form a particularly powerful class of capacity approaching codes with practically realizable decoder implementations based on iterative belief propagation (BP) decoding. However, small graphical sub-structures existing in the Tanner graph [2] of LDPC codes can cause the iterative decoding algorithm to get trapped in error patterns. These weaknesses contribute significantly to the performance of the code in the error floor, or high signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), region of the BER curve. Richardson developed this concept in [3] for the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel and defined these structures as trapping sets. In that paper, trapping sets were shown to have a strong influence on the position and slope of the error floor. In [4], the authors proposed an important subclass of trapping sets called absorbing sets that were shown to account for nearly all decoding errors in the error floor of certain LDPC codes. An absorbing set is defined as a decoder-independent combinatorial object that can be studied analytically. So-called fully absorbing sets have further been shown to be stable under bit-flipping operations [5].
Array-based LDPC codes [6] are a particular class of high rate (γ, p)-regular quasi-cyclic LDPC codes defined by a parameter pair (γ, p), where p ≥ γ and p is prime. The paritycheck matrix of an array-based LDPC code is composed of a γ × p array of p × p circulant permutation matrices. Fan [6] first showed that array-based LDPC codes have excellent performance, in particular for moderate block lengths. A study of the absorbing set spectrum of array-based LDPC codes was

performed in [4], where the minimal (dominant) absorbing sets were classified and combinatorial expressions were given for the exact number of such sets.
Spatially coupled LDPC (SC-LDPC) codes are constructed by coupling together a series of L identical and disjoint, or uncoupled, Tanner graphs into a single coupled chain, and they can be viewed as a type of LDPC convolutional code (LDPCCC) [7], since spatial coupling is equivalent to introducing memory into the encoding process. SC-LDPC codes have been shown to have excellent iterative decoding performance [8], [9] and good asymptotic minimum distance and trapping set properties [10]. Recently, a construction of array-based LDPCCCs with good iterative decoding performance was presented in [11].
In this paper, we study the absorbing set properties of a class of high-rate array-based SC-LDPC codes that are constructed by coupling together L array-based LDPC block codes. We prove that the smallest absorbing sets existing in the Tanner graph of the SC-LDPC code have the same size as those in the corresponding uncoupled LDPC codes and that the number of such sets grow linearly with L. We also show that the average number (per symbol) of minimal sets is greatly reduced compared to the uncoupled codes, and we explain that this reduction is due to many absorbing sets and small cycles being ‘broken’ in the coupling process. While we restrict our attention to a certain class of array-based SC-LDPC codes in this paper, we conjecture that a similar improvement of the absorbing set spectrum can be obtained by coupling other LDPC block code structures following the procedure presented here.

IV. ARRAY-BASED SPATIALLY COUPLED LDPC CODES
In this paper, we construct array-based SC-LDPC codes by using a particular type of edge spreading or unwrapping procedure [7], [12] defined by a “cutting” vector ξ = [ξ0, ξ1, . . . , ξγ−1], which consists of γ step parameters where 0 ≤ ξ0 < ξ1 < · · · < ξγ−1 ≤ p. The cutting vector is used to generate two component parity-check matrices H0 and H1, of size γp × p2, as follows:
• for each ξt, t = 0, 1, . . . , γ − 1, the p × p permutation matrices in row group t and column group j, j < ξt, of H(γ, p) are copied into the equivalent positions in H0;
• permutation matrices in row group t and column group j, j ≥ ξt, of H(γ, p) are copied, if they exist, into the equivalent positions in H1;
• the remaining positions in H0 and H1 are set to zero.

We conclude this section by comparing the average number of minimal absorbing sets A(3,3) and A(4,2) in uncoupled array-based LDPC block codes and SC-LDPC codes for a variety of primes p. This comparison is shown in Fig. 4. Recall that, for array-based LDPC block codes C(3, p), A(3,3) = p−1 and A(4,2) = 3(p − 1)/2. For array-based SC-LDPC codes C(3, p, L, ξ), the values of A(3,3) and A(4,2) depend on the cut ξ as well as p. Minimal cuts ξ(∗a,b) were obtained by computer search for p ≤ 23 and p ≤ 11 for absorbing sets of sizes (3, 3) and (4, 2), respectively. For larger primes p, particular cuts ξ were hand-selected. Given ξ and p, A(3,3) and A(4,2) were calculated as L → ∞ (which is an upper bound on A(3,3) and A(4,2) for any finite L). We observe that the average number (per symbol) of (3, 3) and (4, 2) absorbing sets is significantly reduced for array-based SC-LDPC codes compared to uncoupled array-based LDPC block codes for all p. The SC-LDPC code curves are approximately straight lines, with smaller gradient than the corresponding block code curves. Consequently, the reduction in the number of minimal absorbing sets obtained by spatial coupling array-based LDPC block codes increases with p. Moreover, we observe that, unlike the uncoupled LDPC block codes, there are fewer (4, 2) sets than (3, 3) sets and, as a consequence, spatial coupling provides a very large decrease in the average number of (4, 2) fully absorbing sets.
VI. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we studied the absorbing set properties of a class of high-rate array-based spatially coupled LDPC codes that are constructed by coupling together L array-based LDPC block codes. We proved that, like the corresponding uncoupled codes, the smallest absorbing sets existing in the Tanner graph have sizes (3, 3) and (4, 2), and the number of such sets grows linearly with L. We also showed that the average number (per symbol) of minimal sets is greatly reduced compared to the uncoupled codes, and we explained that this reduction is due to many absorbing sets and small cycles being ‘broken’ by the coupling process. The large reduction in the number of minimal absorbing sets suggests that array-based SC-LDPC codes will have significantly improved decoding performance in the high SNR regime compared to corresponding uncoupled LDPC codes. Preliminary simulation results support this claim.
