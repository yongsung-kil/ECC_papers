Optimized Geometric LDPC Codes with Quasi-Cyclic Structure
Xueqin Jiang, Moon Ho Lee, Shangce Gao, and Yun Wu

Abstract: This paper presents methods to the construction of regular and irregular low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes based on Euclidean geometries over the Galois field. Codes constructed by these methods have quasi-cyclic (QC) structure and large girth. By decomposing hyperplanes in Euclidean geometry, the proposed irregular LDPC codes have flexible column/row weights. Therefore, the degree distributions of proposed irregular LDPC codes can be optimized by technologies like the curve fitting in the extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) charts. Simulation results show that the proposed codes perform very well with an iterative decoding over the AWGN channel.
Index Terms: Column decomposing, Euclidean geometry, EXIT chart, Galois fields, irregular, low-density parity-check (LDPC), parallel bundles, regular, pi µ-flats.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, first proposed in the early 1960’s by Gallager [1] and re-discovered in 1996 [2], have recently attracted much attention due to their capacityapproaching performance and low decoding complexity. As an attractive class of LDPC codes, quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes are well suitable for certain practical applications since they can easily be encoded and decoded with linear complexity [3].
LDPC codes can be classified with regard to the number of ‘1’s in each column and each row of the parity-check matrix as regular or irregular. Irregular LDPC codes are characterized by having a non-constant number of ‘1’s in each column and each row and are defined by the so-called variable/check-nodedegree distribution. Regular LDPC codes are characterized by having a constant number of ‘1’s in each column and each row. In Gallager’s original work [1], only regular LDPC codes were considered. Howerver, Richardson et al. [4] showed that irregular LDPC codes with carefully chosen degree distributions perform very close to the Shannon limit on the erasure channel. Empirically, the same is true for other channels. To find good irregular LDPC codes, a powerful tool called extrinsic informa-

tion transfer (EXIT) chart was introduced in [5] for optimizing the degree distributions in the Tanner graphs or equivalently the parity-check matrix. It was stated in [5] that, to optimize the variable/check-node-degree distributions in the Tanner graph of irregular LDPC codes for a given code rate, the parity-check matrix should have at least three different column/row weights. As the column/row weight defines the variable/check-node degree and the variable/check-node degree corresponds to the column/row weight, we use the terms variable/check-node degree and column/row weight interchangeably.
Methods for the construction of regular QC LDPC codes based on Euclidean geometries have been proposed in [3], [6]– [11]. The girth of these geometric regular LDPC codes is only 6, which limits the error correcting performance. By column/row splitting [3], [6], these regular QC LDPC codes can be extended into irregular LDPC codes. However, the resulting irregular codes have two drawbacks: 1) They are not quasi-cyclic, and thus the encoding and decoding complexity is high; 2) there are only two different column/row weights and thus regular variable/check-node-degree distribution (not adjustable) in the parity-check matrix. Therefore, their degree distributions can not be optimized by tools like EXIT charts [5]. In this paper we first present two approaches to the construction of geometric regular QC LDPC codes with girth larger than 6. By a hyperplane decomposing method, irregular QC LDPC codes can be obtained. Compared to the geometric irregular codes proposed in [3], [6], our proposed irregular LDPC codes have two advantages: 1) They are quasi-cyclic; 2) they have flexible column/row weight distributions by decomposing hyperplanes in Euclidean geometries. We employ the EXIT chart technique to find an optimal variable/check-node-degree distributions for our proposed irregular QC LDPC codes. We also demonstrate further that our proposed codes perform very well over the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel with an iterative decoding algorithm.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section II gives a brief review of Euclidean geometries and introduces three steps to design the proposed Euclidean geometric QC LDPC codes. In Section III, we construct regular QC LDPC codes with large girth. Irregular QC LDPC codes are constructed in Section IV. Section V introduces an approach to find optimized variable/check-node-degree distributions for the proposed irregular QC LDPC code. Examples and simulation results of the optimized geometric irregular LDPC codes are given in section VI. Section VII concludes the paper.

B. Euclidean Geometric QC LDPC Codes Design
In this paper, the design of the geometric regular and irregular
QC LDPC codes, consists of the following three steps: Step 1: Construct a base matrix B based on µ-flats in Euclidean geometries E(d, ps) over the Galois field GF (ps). Step 2: Design a shift matrix S by replacing each ‘0’ of the base matrix B with a ‘∞’ and replacing each ‘1’ in the mth row and nth column of the base matrix B with an integer a(m,n) in the range [0, q − 1]. Step 3: Construct a quasi-cyclic parity-check matrix H by replacing each a(m,n) with a q × q circulant permutation matrix P a(m,n) which cyclically shifts the identity matrix to the right by a(m,n) positions and replacing each ‘∞’ with a q × q zero matrix.

B. The Second Class of Regular QC LDPC Codes
The second special case is δ = N (d, µ2, s, p)/p(d−µ2)s. When δ = N (d, µ2, s, p)/p(d−µ2)s, all the parallel bundles of µ2-flats are used to construct the base matrix, then each µ1-flat is contained in A(d, µ2, µ1, s, p) µ2-flats. Therefore, the row weight is a constant number, A(d, µ2, µ1, s, p).

Obviously, H also has the same column/row weight and code rate as its corresponding base matrix BR. VI. CODE DESIGN EXAMPLES AND SIMULATION RESULTS
In the following, we provide an example to illustrate the construction process of the proposed irregular QC LDPC codes. For simplicity, we only consider the column decomposing in the optimization of the degree distribution.
Example 4: Consider the Euclidean geometry EG(6, 2). Let µ1 = 0, µ2 = 3, σ = 15 (15 parallel bundles) and q = 66. We construct a 64 × 120 regular base matrix BR whose rows correspond to all the points in EG(6, 2) and whose columns correspond to all the 3-flats in EG(6, 2). The row weight and column weight of BR are 15 and 8, respectively. Replace each ‘0’ of BR with a ‘∞’ and replace each ‘1’ of B with an integer in the range [0, 65], then we obtain a 64 × 120 shift matrix, and finally obtain

a 4288×8040 parity-matrix H with girth 8. The null space of H gives a QC LDPC code with code rate 0.4667 and code length 8040. The EXIT chart for this QC LDPC code for Eb/No = 3.2 dB is shown in Fig. 3. Notice that the performance of this code
is not good, but we can use it as an example to demonstrate our
column decomposing method. Note that a 3-flat can be decomposed into 2-flats and 1-flats. Therefore, by using the column decomposing, columns of weight 8 (corresponding to 3-flats) can be replaced by columns of weight 4 (corresponding to 2flats) and weight 2 (corresponding to 1-flats). Via the curve fitting approach on the EXIT chart, we find an optimized degree
distribution pair. We denote the corresponding (16616,12328) irreguar LDPC code as C3. Fig. 4 shows the EXIT chart for irregular QC LDPC code C3 for Eb/No = 1.9 dB. The variablenode degree distribution of C3 is given in Table I. The fraction λ2 of C3 is 0.5161, which implies that this code has a bad error floor performance at high SNR. We reduce λ2 to trade threshold SNR for better BER performance at high SNR. The resulted irregular QC LDPC code is denoted by C4. For performance comparison, we construct a geometric LDPC code proposed in
[3], [6]–[8] which has the same code length and code rate as C3 and C4. First, we construct a 4096 × 4160 matrix from 65 parallel bundles in EG(2, 6). Then, with the column splitting method proposed in [3] and [6], we split each column into three
columns with two different column weights 21 and 22. The null space of the obtained 4096 × 12480 matrix gives an irregular LDPC code C5.
Fig. 5 shows the BER performance of codes C3, C4, and C5 over the AWGN channel and for a BPSK modulation. As can be seen that C3 has a good performance at the low SNR region and C4 has the good performances at both of the low SNR region and the high SNR region. C4 has no error-floor of BER down to 10−7. Therefore, C4 has good tradeoffs between the BER performance at low SNR and he BER performance at high SNR. We can see that the coding gain of about 2.9 dB is achieved by C4
