Variable Min-Sum Decoding Based on Generalized Mutual Information Metric
Yin Xu, Student Member, IEEE, Dazhi He, Yunfeng Guan, Yijun Shi, Wenjun Zhang, Fellow, IEEE

Abstract— Min Sum algorithm simplifies the non-linear check node operation of Belief Propagation algorithm via linear approximation, which greatly reduces the complexity of realization of decoder but degrades the performance as well. The resulting sub-optimality could be tempered via scaling of LLRs, e.g. fixed optimal scaling applied to Min Sum output resulting in the Normalized Min Sum algorithm, and variable scaling schemes gradually appear in literature. In this paper, we study the variable scaling decoding algorithm, and propose to generate variable scaling sequences via generalized mutual information (GMI) metric. Simulation results on real LDPC codes for different decoding algorithms have shown that our GMI metric performs better than the variable scaling scheme appearing in literature, and meanwhile improves substantially in terms of BER over the conventional Normalized Min Sum algorithm.
Index Terms— Decoding, Generalized mutual Informatin, LDPC, Variable min sum.
I. INTRODUCTION
L OW density parity-check (LDPC) [1] codes with BP [2] decoding can achieve performance near to Shannon limits [3]. However, for its high computational complexity at check nodes processor, BP is not commonly used in hardware implementation. Min-Sum (MS) [4] algorithm was then invented by Fossorier, which greatly reduces BP’s high complexity via introducing a simplified approximation in the calculation at check nodes, but degrades the performance as well. In order to improve the performance of MS algorithm, many works [5] [6] were published, and the most effective methods include Normalized Min-Sum (NMS) [7] and Offset Min-Sum (OMS) [8] algorithms both of which are based on the idea of linear post processing. Intuitively, the value of the optimal scaling factor is highly related to the processor’s incoming LLR values. And since those LLR values change as per decoding iteration, thus, to further improve the NMS algorithm, the scaling factor should be changed adaptively as well.
Recently, many papers appeared in Literature discussing the variable scaling methods. Zhao [9] applies two fixed scaling

And In another paper [10] the author modifies the OMS algorithm by choosing one of two different scaling factors conditional on the value of min-sum output. Basically, the scheme in [9] and [10] should be classified into the fixed scaling methods, since both of them have only two fixed choices, not exactly adapted with changing LLR values. Moreover, scaling factors of both two algorithms are empirical values, based on simulation trials, thus are not proven to be universally optimal.
In paper [11] [12], Lechner has introduced the variable scaling decoding algorithm based on a non-linear optimal post processing function, which changes per iteration. To overcome the high complexity brought by non-linearity, he further approximates the optimal post processing function via a linear function characterized by a Normalized scaling factor, according to Minimum Mean Square error metrics. Since the post processing function changes per iteration, and the change of function follows tightly up to the change of Extrinsic information per iteration, thus the variable scaling series are obtained according to the decoding trajectory in EXIT CHART. In particular, Lechner’s metric of search of scaling is only analyzed theoretically (i.e., via EXIT chart tools or Density Evolution). As is known, due to the ideal assumption of theoretical tools, the scaling series obtained under the theoretical thresholds are not well matched to LLRs under real threshold in practical decoder. In this sense the scaling sequences obtained via EXIT chart may degrades the performance of real decoder. However, due to the complex expression of optimal post processing function, the metric of searching scaling can hardly be used in real LLR based analysis.
In our work, a Generalized Mutual Information (GMI) [13] based metric is proposed as a criterion to select the scaling factor adaptively per iteration. For theoretical analysis, the computation of GMI formula requires the knowledge of probability distribution function (PDF) of LLRs which can be studied via density evolution theory. In addition, due to the simplicity of the expression of GMI formula, the metric can also be extended to real code and real LLR based practical scenarios by applying Monte Carlo simulations.
Finally the BER simulation results on a real LDPC code (from DVB-T2 standard) show that the proposed GMI metric based variable scaling decoding algorithm performs better than

Before attempting to discuss the BER performance, the following clarifications must be made. The optimal scaling factor for NMS decoding is α = 0.85 , which is searched based on real BER simulations, i.e. to run BER simulation for scaling factor from 0 to 1.0 using 0.2, 0.1, 0.05, 0.02 or 0.01 as steps, and gradually narrow it down to the scaling factor 0.85 with best BER performance down to 1e-6 or even below meanwhile having no error floor at least down to 1e-7. In addition, the scaling sequence of I-EXIT-P is searched and obtained based on EXIT chart tools via equation (10). As for our proposed GMI metric, the scaling sequence is searched based on equation (19) around BP threshold.
Now to see from Fig. 1, it is very obvious that our GMI-based variable scaling method is better than the optimal NMS (with α = 0.85 ) algorithm and the I-EXIT-P metric (appeared in literature [11] [12]) by 0.15dB, and is very close to BP’s performance with less than 0.1 dB distance away. And, due to the mismatch between the theoretical scaling sequence (obtained in theoretical SNR threshold) and real threshold of practical ldpc code, the I-EXIT-P metric is not seen to surpass the optimal NMS algorithm.

IV. CONCLUSION
In this paper, a variable scaling decoding algorithm (called I-EXIT-P metric) has been studied and the shortcoming of scaling mismatch between theoretical threshold and practical threshold has been pointed out. On top of this, we propose the search of variable scaling via generalized mutual information metric, and easily extend this metric in the application of practical LLRs of real ldpc code, solving the mismatch issue. Simulation results on 64800 rate 1/2 code have shown that our proposed GMI metric is better performed by 0.15 dB than the I-EXIT-P metric appearing in literature and the NMS algorithm,
