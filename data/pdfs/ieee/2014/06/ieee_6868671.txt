Gbit/s Era

Abstract—Power and flexibility are important constraints in the design of new chips. The efficiency extracted from a design is increasingly becoming a dominant question, and several techniques and technological advances can be used to optimize efficiency in its energy and functionality domains. These two characteristics are critical in digital communication systems that must work accordingly with multiple communication standards at different power, throughput and latency requirements. In this work, we focus on the physical layer Forward Error Correcting (FEC) system, due to the tight throughput and latency constraints they are required to meet, and develop specialized processing engines for Low–Density Parity–Check (LDPC) codes decoding, a class of widely standardized codes. The engines were developed for execution on Field–Programmable Gate Array (FPGA) devices by exploring dataflow and widepipeline design approaches, and have the design flexibility to target different LDPC codes, since they were implemented using recent High–Level Synthesis (HLS) tools. The generated engines and architectures allow achieving highly efficient decoders with decoding throughputs ranging from 16 Mbit/s to 1.2 Gbit/s at energy efficiencies of 42 to 908 Mbit/Joule/iteration, while the achieved clock frequencies of operation vary from 80 to 300 MHz. Furthermore, our bandwidth analysis shows that workload boundaries do not impose limitations on a system bus.
I. INTRODUCTION
The massification of mobile device usage and internet connected systems has intensified the demand for increasingly effective channel bandwidths, offering higher data rates at low latencies, adding further constraints to Quality of Service (QoS) and Quality of Experience (QoE) requirements of digital communication systems. Among the modules composing a digital communication system, the Forward Error Correcting (FEC) sub-system plays a crucial role by implementing powerful Error–Correcting Codes (ECCs) that allow the system to operate near channel-capacity [1]. Accordingly, the closer to capacity, the higher the numerical complexity of the methods implemented by the FEC system and the higher is the developer effort to find suitable designs that match the system requirements. While traditional High–Performance Computing (HPC) platforms offer plenty of processing power they are deemed unsuitable due to excessive power consumption; on the other hand, embedded platforms meet power constraints

While Application–Specific Integrated Circuit (ASIC) designs are a standard approach to overcome this challenge, and in the recent past several Application–Specific Integrated Processor (ASIP) strategies also proved to surpass power and throughput challenges, we turn our attention to Field–Programmable Gate Array (FPGA) devices that offer design flexibility and also incorporate High– Level Synthesis (HLS) that allow to generate dataflow- and wide-pipeline-based accelerators [2], [3].
In this paper, we exploit dataflow and wide-pipeline approaches to generate Low–Density Parity–Check (LDPC) decoders, using the Maxeler MaxCompiler and the Altera OpenCL compiler [2], [3]. We show how design flexibility, energy efficiency and processing power constraints can be met on reconfigurable devices through the development of specialized LDPC cores and their organization into an LDPC engine. We are able to deliver data throughputs scaling up to 1.2 Gbit/s at 908 Mbit/Joule/iteration energy efficiency, while keeping the development effort, cost and delay lower than those associated with Register Transfer Level (RTL)-based development. The main contributions of this work are:
• development of specialized LDPC decoding engines using JAVA-based HLS dataflow descriptions, and widepipeline architectures, based on OpenCL, which retain throughput and flexibility through the global control of the architecture design, by identifying the most intensive kernels, exploiting dataflow patterns, and locality.
• analysis of the memory bandwidth limitations for the decoding engines proposed, and of the obtained throughput for a number of executed iterations per codeword.

B. LDPC Engine Architecture
The designed LDPC core architecture is meant for integration in an array of m cores, in an m-parallel kernel engine architecture [7]. While fully parallel decoders would involve a very high number of LDPC cores, at a maximum of one core per node, it has been shown in [6] that structures embedded in the LDPC Tanner graph can be leveraged to reduce m.
Naturally, the LDPC core introduces a dispatching overhead, a result of its pipelined CN and BN datapaths, that can be masked by pipelining the decoding modes. Thus, the trailing CNs and BNs can be concurrently executed in the LDPC core, provided no memory hazards occur. For the case of QC–LDPC codes, if the selected m is a multiple or sub-multiple of the expansion factor zf [8], this challenge can be successfully overcome, while for code structures such as LDPC–IRA, it can be addressed by cyclically shifting the BN and the CN node that initiates decoding in each phase [7].
IV. SPECIALIZED DATAFLOW AND WIDE-PIPELINE LDPC ENGINES
A. LDPC Dataflow Engine
Using the Maxeler MaxCompiler [2], [9] based on a dataflow model, we can describe the LDPC core (Figure 2), as a hardware block and instantiate it an arbitrary m number of times when composing the actual LDPC engine [7]. The engine is associated with a dedicated input stream for γ messages and with one output stream for αˆ messages (2) or the hard-decoded bit states (4).
The structured properties LDPC codes can be exploited to simplify the memory organization of the dataflow engine so that it never stalls on fetching data from memory. In particular,

we have observed that data can be laid out in BRAM banks, each associated with a single LDPC core [6], [10]. Thus, data is fed to the accelerator through the input stream and used to populate the corresponding BRAM banks in an initial stage, at the same time that the output stream is delivering the decoded data. The main advantage of this design is the ability to decouple the LDPC core from the remaining LDPC engine architecture, enabling the MaxCompiler to aggressively search for hardware regions with different clocks. This is feasible also due to the double-buffering strategy employed at the input where communication and computation are overlapped in order to avoid bandwidth limitations.
The BN and CN operation modes are seamlessly interchangeable in the LDPC cores. The input and output streams are connected with the BRAMs with a feedback loop while respecting the Tanner graph structure. Switching between modes occurs according to the number of BN nodes and CN nodes in the Tanner graph. It is important noticing that, due to the latency associated with the LDPC cores and interconnection network, the input and output streams of the memory are shifted in time. This means that, to avoid introducing stalls in the pipeline, and to maintain maximum streaming performance, we need to guarantee the absence of memory hazards not only within each m consecutive nodes processed but also between processing modes whenever overlap occurs. This can be achieved by adjusting at compile time the order how the Tanner graph is traversed. Moreover, because the number of connections to BN nodes and CN nodes in the Tanner graph can vary independently with different code rates, the latency of the LDPC cores for each of the operation modes can also vary. This unbalance creates another synchronization issue when switching between operation modes, and can be solved by introducing FIFO buffers at the output of the BN and CN dataflow nodes to equalize the latency of both streams.
Whereas ASIC constraints may drive the designer to find a compromise on the parallelism level of the LDPC engine (m), and optimize it according to the LDPC characteristics [11], in reconfigurable platforms, such as FPGAs, the ultimate constraint in the design size lies in the total amount of logic resources provided by the FPGA. Moreover, while previous works on reconfigurable hardware also focused on optimizing the number of LDPC cores in order to have the design fit into the FPGA [10], nowadays FPGA are large enough to accommodate several LDPC engines. Thus, we propose a more flexible design that combines the number of cores per engine (m) and the number of engines per device (p). Using MaxCompiler we were able to explore different configurations in the design space and incorporate up to p=8 LDPC cores.

V. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
The Altera OpenCL wide-pipeline engine was synthesized for the rate 1/2 WiFi code with length N =1944 bits, in order to increase m beyond a single CU. The Maxeler-generated dataflow LDPC engines was synthesized for the N =64800 bits rate 1/2 Digital Video Broadcasting–Satellite2 (DVB–S2) code and up to p×m=720 cores were driven. We compare with LDPC GPU decoders [7], [14].
A. Apparatus
The dataflow LDPC decoder was run on a Xilinx Virtex 5 LX330T MAX2336B board, connected to the host system via PCIe x8 2nd Generation with a theoretical peak bandwidth of 4GB/s, while the wide-pipeline decoder was run on a Altera Stratix V D5 Nallatech PCIe 385 N board, connected to the host system via PCIe x8 3rd Generation with a peak bandwidth of 7.88 GB/s. We also include synthesis results for a MAX3 board based on a Xilinx Virtex 6 SX475T. We obtain the dynamic power by measuring the system when running the LDPC engines and subtracting it from the measurements obtained while the host system is idle using a power data acquisition tool based on an Allegro ACS712 Hall effect current sensor and a PIC18F4550.
B. LDPC Engine Throughput
Dataflow LDPC Engine: For DVB–S2 codes, we could incorporate LDPC engines with m={45, 90, 180, 360}. Furthermore, the number of engines included in the dataflow accelerator could be replicated in the range of p={1, 2, 4, 8}. Previous works that dealt with a reduced number of LDPC cores [10], [11] were motivated, in the FPGA case, to fit the engine in a device where resources were scarce for an engine with a higher number of cores, and in the ASIC case, to allow a better optimization of chip power and area. However, as lithography processes scale down the logic resources provided in nowadays FPGA devices support higher number of LDPC cores (m) and multiple LDPC engines in the same device.
The number of clock cycles that an LDPC engine with m cores needs to run is roughly twice of that of an engine with

2m cores. Thus, for the same clock frequency the throughput expected from the former is roughly half of the latter. A closer inspection to the throughput results shown in Table I shows that due to the different clock frequencies of operation of the different solutions, this relation does not hold. In particular, LDPC engines with less cores support higher frequency of operations due to the lower overhead introduced by the control logic. Nevertheless, the relation between frequency and the number of cores does not scale linearly. Hence, for the number of cores tested, the decoders with the highest throughput are the ones with the larger number of cores.
Regarding the effects of adding extra engines, we can observe that there is only a slight overhead increase. This is clear for example when comparing an accelerator with m=90 and another with two engines of m=45, i.e., accelerators with the same absolute number of cores. The overall effect, across both the MAX2 and the MAX3 boards, is that for any combination of p≥1 and m, the best performance is always achieved for p= 1 and m=360.
With the methodology proposed, the LDPC accelerators allow decoding throughputs from 270 to 1296 Mbit/s for 10 decoding iterations. Although for some applications there is a fixed throughput that must be meet, and therefore achieving higher throughput is not the main design goal, for simulation purposes this allows considerable speedup gains when compared with programmable simulators based on Central Processing Units (CPUs) or GPUs [12], [14].
Wide-pipeline LDPC Engine: As shown in Table I the wide-pipeline LDPC decoder yields a decoding throughput of 16 to 21 Mbit/s, respectively for m=1 and m=2 (m the number of CUs). The OpenCL wide-pipeline accelerator has the advantage of rapid prototyping across both CPU and GPU devices [13]. However, it has some scalability issues: 1) the logic utilization of the LDPC wide-pipeline engine saturates for only 2 CUs; 2) although the utilized FPGA board supports a clock frequency of up to 800MHz, the Altera OpenCL compiler sets a maximum pace of 240MHz for a single CU; and 3) in our experiments, the introduction of the second CUs imposed routing constraints that translated into lower clock frequencies, namely the 240MHz that was observed for 1 CU reduced to 157MHz when doubling the number of CUs, thus limiting the potential maximum performance of the decoder. A possible way to overcome this challenge is by tuning the data granularity at the work-item level so that more data can be processed by less work-items, with the objective of reducing the number of scheduled work-items at a higher rate than the probable drop in the clock frequency of operation.

In order to assess the effects due to bandwidth limitations, we computed the number of decoding iterations executed per second for each LDPC decoding architecture in relation to the number of decoding iterations executed per codeword. The results are shown in Figures 4(a) and 4(b) for the dataflow and the wide-pipeline based LDPC decoders.
As seen in Figure 4(a), the throughput performance can be divided into two regions: i) on the left-hand side, the PCIe bandwidth dominates the decoding process and the architectures are not able to perform at their maximum capacity; and ii) on the right-hand side, when the PCIe connection bandwidth no longer poses a constraint, the number of executed decoded iterations per second saturates. Since the number of iterations executed by the LDPC decoder can be related to the Signal to Noise Ratio (SNR) conditions of the channel, we can evaluate the proposed dataflow decoder architecture efficiency for different qualitative SNR ranges, shown in Figure 4(a) as High SNR if falling below four decoding iterations per codeword and lower SNR if more than that occurs [5]. For Lower SNR values, the PCIe bandwidth does not pose a constraint to the performance of our architecture, since the PCIe bus is able to feed data at a higher rate than it is consumed. However, for high SNR values—when the number of iterations executed per codeword is low—the execution is communication-bounded. Moreover, in the scenario where all decoders issue only one decoding iteration, as in Figure 4(a), the dataflow decoders would be limited to ∼300 Mbit/s—with

the exception of the Max2 and Max3 (1, 45) configurations. The wide-pipeline LDPC decoders convey a different situ-
ation as seen in Figure 4(b). In this case, neither the SDRAM nor the PCIe bandwidth pose a real constrain to the achieved decoding throughput. Hence, there is no tradeoff regarding either the SNR or the amount of logic resources utilized. While not being limited by the bandwidth of the connecting interfaces may seem an advantage of the design, in reality it is due to the poorer throughput of the decoder.
D. LDPC Engine Energy Efficiency
Energy efficiency is defined as the number of Mbits (106) decoded per Joule per decoding iteration (Mbit/J/iter). We profiled the {(p=1, m=45), (p=2, m=45), (p=4, m=45), (p=2, m=90)} dataflow LDPC decoders for their energy consumption and efficiency (see Figure 5 and Table II). We included energy efficiency results for equivalent GPU solutions [7], [14]. VI. ADVANTAGES AND DISADVANTAGES OF THE PROPOSED APPROACH
The results obtained with the proposed LDPC decoding engines vary widely, especially in terms of the obtained decoding throughput, not only as a function of the number of cores included in the dataflow design, but also when comparing with the wide-pipeline approach. As previously said, the higher logic utilization of the FPGA resources by the wide-pipeline approach led to its benchmark for the smaller length WiFi LDPC code. Also, the levels of parallelism of both approaches are somewhat non-comparable. Whereas the dataflow approach was able to incorporate a number of LDPC cores as high as p×m=720, the wide-pipeline approach could only incorporate 2 CUs. Comparing the wide-pipeline decoder and the dataflow decoder with (p, m)=(1, 45) on the MAX2 board, we find that the decoding throughput levels are one order of magnitude apart, which is about the same difference between the number of CUs in the wide-pipeline and the number of LDPC cores.
Consequently we could speculate that a larger FPGA could bring the wide-pipeline decoding throughput inline with the dataflow decoder. In order to obtain a relative estimate, the decoding throughput was divided by the number of LDPC cores included in the design (p, m), and the results obtained are shown in Table I as the rate of decoded bits per core. In the strict sense, in the wide-pipeline approach a core stands for CU in the pipeline. As shown, the wide-pipeline approach shows higher computational power per CU than the dataflow LDPC

engines (one order of magnitude above). Notwithstanding, the composition of the dataflow cores lead to more efficient solutions that increase performance for the available logic resources, namely they achieve decoding throughputs in the order of the Gbit/s at relatively low frequencies of operation.
VII. CONCLUSIONS
The methodology proposed allows to save significant time in all these phases and has illustrated how a bottom-up approach can be pursued using HLS descriptions to arrive at an LDPC decoding engine architecture that not only achieves energy efficiencies one order of magnitude higher than conventional programming architectures, but also achieves maximum throughputs in the Gbit/s range, in the dataflow LDPC engine case, by exploring the maximum level of parallelism in the proposed parallel LDPC decoding engines, which competes well in terms of throughput against ASIC LDPC decoders.
