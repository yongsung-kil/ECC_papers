An Analysis into the Loopy Belief Propagation Algorithm over Short Cycles
Nithin Raveendran, Shayan Garani Srinivasa Department of Electronic Systems Engineering Indian Institute of Science, Bangalore, India, 560012.
Email: {nithin,shayan.gs}@dese.iisc.ernet.in

Abstract—We investigate into the loopy belief propagation algorithm for binary low density parity check (LDPC) codes having cycles of small girth. Independence assumption among messages passed, assumed reasonable in all configurations of graphs, fails the most in graphical structures with short cycles. We investigate into this limitation and propose a modified algorithm, by considering dependency in the probability domain. This improves the performance of decoding over such graphs when compared to the original message passing algorithm at higher signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), thereby, yielding lower error floors.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low density parity check codes [1] have been investigated extensively in coding theory over the last two decades owing to superior performance close to Shannon’s capacity [2] over a variety of channels. The pioneering work done by Gallager in the early 1960s remains fundamental to the decoding algorithm [1]. In order to get towards capacity, we need to go with ultra-large block lengths, choice of random codes along with an optimal decoding algorithm i.e., guaranteed maximum-likelihood (ML) performance. These constraints are practically challenging for system realization with today’s technology. However, theoretical techniques that can take us an extra step ahead towards these goals are important from SNR perspective. Most of the prior research works focussed on the application of the original decoding algorithm over different channels through better construction of graphs i.e., avoiding short cycles and large block lengths [3], [4]. There has not been substantial amount of investigation looking at the assumptions and approximations in the original decoding algorithm. The independence assumption used during the computation of variable and check node messages [1] is an approximation in case of Tanner graphs having cycles. It is well known that loopy belief propagation is suboptimal in such graphs [2], [5], [6]. Analysis of loopy belief propagation algorithm by Weiss [7] shows that there are certain classes of loopy networks wherein the belief update gives the correct maximum a-posteriori probability (MAP) estimate. Guyader and Fabre have investigated the decoding over short cycles by conditioning on a node [8]. In their approach, by breaking the cycle, one can obtain Markov chain structures amenable for exact estimation through belief propagation. Their method has been demonstrated over simple examples such as the wheel code. We proceed with this motivation to break the dependency

induced in short cycles by looking back at the iterative time steps.
The phenomenon of error floors occurring due to imperfect code designs has also been widely researched in recent past. Richardson [9] defined certain combinatorial objects called trapping sets (or near codewords) causing the error floor phenomenon. Vasic´ et al. developed a trapping set ontology [10], [11] for identifying small graphical structures and their extensions that lead to error floors. Amiri et al. [12] have looked into certain special trapping sets called ‘absorbing sets’. Using a search and elimination algorithm, they have demonstrated better code designs [13]. In all these approaches, the decoding algorithm follows the original Gallager’s approach, however, the code design is controlled via elimination of pathological structures which lead to decode failures in the high SNR region. The general theme of code construction is to avoid short cycles and work with higher girth ensembles. This compensates for the sub-optimality of the algorithm quite effectively. But, to utilize the full potency of a sparse parity check matrix, such restrictions must be avoided. Also, there are many cases where such short cycles are unavoidable. For instance, the overall factor graph representing both code structure and channel model for an LDPC coded two-dimensional inter-symbol interference (ISI) channel has many short cycles. These degrade the performance of message passing algorithm. So, a more fundamental but different approach is to look into the decoding algorithm itself. We look at graphical structures leading to error floor in a different perspective. This makes the work relevant to data storage systems also. We would like to analyze girth g = 4 graphs and eventually towards generalized update equations for higher girth conditions. Also, the investigation into girth four structures is of interest particularly in the decoding of quantum LDPC codes [14] without using entangled states. Therefore, this analysis is important from the perspective of classical and quantum LDPC decoding methods.
The paper is organized as follows. In section II, we start our analysis by carefully looking at the limitations of the loopy belief propagation when messages are passed within the Tanner graph. We introduce the notion of time and link it with girth of the graph for analyzing the dependency of messages over time. In section III, we modify the update equations in the sum-product algorithm (SPA) in probability domain to incorporate statistical dependency between messages that are linked via short cycles. II. LOOPY BELIEF PROPAGATION
Belief propagation (BP) algorithm [5] is the underlying method used in iterative decoding of LDPC codes [1]. The BP algorithm is exact in the case of tree structured graphs and achieves ML performance.
The introduction of cycles into a Tanner graph creates two significant limitations in the message passing algorithm. 1) Messages passed through a cycle becomes dependent over time. Ignoring this dependency contradicts the extrinsic message passing in the belief propagation algorithm. 2) Overwhelming of information due to ‘double counting’ saturates the beliefs at nodes that are part of short cycles resulting in gain saturation. The notion of time in the evolution of messages is related to the iterative steps of the algorithm. Each iteration at time t in the message passing algorithm consists of extrinsic messages passed from check nodes cj to variable nodes vi and back to check node as shown in Fig. 1. Intuitively, using the entire history of messages passed along the cycle of girth g = 4, along with the appropriate cycle dependencies, statistical dependency on messages can be removed.

B. Dependency due to double counting
In case of a graph with cycles, there is an effect of overwhelming of information due to ‘double counting’ [7] which corrupts the beliefs at the node. This can occur in the first iteration itself when the graph has cycles of girth g = 4. As an

Fig. 4. Girth 4 graph segment illustrating double counting at t = 1. Instead
of considering the channel reliability p2 once, decision making step uses the same value twice due to the cycle.

intuitive example, consider Fig. 4. Here c1(11)(0) and c2(11)(0) is just the channel probability p2 in the first iteration. They both convey the same information and need to be ‘counted’ only once when making a decision on v1. Decision making step in the message passing algorithm considers these check node messages as statistically independent which is ‘clearly’ not the case. If considered so, the belief at variable node v2 dominates the decision on the variable node v1 and vice versa, leading to gain saturation. To enforce statistical dependency, the joint probability term is correctly evaluated by going backwards in iteration from time t by looking into correlations from previous updates.
Example: In the original update for making a soft decision on a variable node, the messages are considered independent. From Fig. 4, this is evaluated as follows:
P r(x(11) = 0) = P r(x(10) = 0 | y1)P r(c(111) = 0)P r(c(211) = 0). (2)

Since v2 is the only extrinsic variable node connected to check node c1 and c2, we can write equation (2) as

P r(x1(1) = 0) = p1p2p2.

(3)

Here the channel probability at node v2 dominates the decision.

Using modified update, wherein, the messages within the cycle are considered dependent, double counting is prevented. A counterpart for equation (2) in the modified update is computed as follows:

P r(x(11) = 0) = P r(x(10) = 0, c(111) = 0, c(211) = 0 | y1). (4)

Using conditional independence and Bayes’ theorem, we have

P r(x(11) = 0) = p1P r(c(111) = 0)P r(c2(11) = 0 | c(111) = 0). (5)
Conditional probability term P r(c2(11) = 0 | c(111) = 0) evaluates to unity. This results in

P r(x(11) = 0) = p1p2.

(6)

In the modified update, using the dependency, we have successfully avoided double counting of message from v2. This example is a precursor to the generalized modified update discussed in section III.

IV. SIMULATION RESULTS In this section, we present bit error rate (BER) vs. Eb/N0(dB) curves to study the efficacy of our modified message passing update. For this, we consider an LDPC code with parity check matrix of size (n − k) × n = (600 × 1200). Block length n of the code is 1200 and code rate is 0.5. We constrain the H matrix to be regular with 200 isolated cycles of girth g = 4, with a column weight of 3 and a row weight of 6. We consider a simple memoryless channel with additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) of variance N0/2. We have ensured that the noise conditions are ‘exactly’ the same for both the decoding algorithms.

V. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
We developed a modified algorithm with update equations specifically for nodes involved in short cycles of girth g = 4 with an eye towards understanding the limitation of the sum product decoding algorithm leading to error floors. The simulation results show better performance than the original algorithm that is limited by independence assumption. The improvement is significant at high SNR region, suggesting, a possible cause to the error floor effects on such graphs. This motivates us to look into graphs of moderate girth of size six and eight based on practical constraints. Ultimately, removing all the dependency will guarantee an ML decoding performance on general graphs. This should lead towards the Shannon limit, albeit, with a complexity overhead.
