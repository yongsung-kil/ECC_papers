Spatially-Coupled Counter Braids
Eirik Rosnes Department of Informatics, University of Bergen, N-5020 Bergen, Norway, and the Simula Research Lab.
Email: eirik@ii.uib.no

Abstract—A counter braid (CB) is a novel counter architecture for per-flow measurements on high-speed links. CBs were introduced by Lu et al. in 2007 and they have an asymptotic compression rate (under optimal decoding) matching the entropy lower bound of the flow size distribution. A CB has a layered structure and compresses the flow sizes “on-the-fly” as new packets arrive. In this work, we apply spatial coupling to CBs and show numerically that spatially-coupled CBs (SC-CBs) exhibit improved iterative decoding thresholds. Furthermore, we show that single-layer SC-CBs, which are in fact compressed sensing schemes for nonnegative signals, have a superior undersamplingsparsity phase transition trajectory (under iterative decoding) in the sparse region compared to random Gaussian measurement matrices with ℓ1-norm minimization reconstruction.
I. INTRODUCTION
Per-flow measurements on high-speed links is a challenging problem. Typically, over a period of a few minutes, millions of flow sizes need to be measured. Flows are uniquely identified by packet headers. Thus, a measurement scheme must update counters as new packets arrive on a link. Inter-arrival time between packets can be as short as 40 nanoseconds for a 10Gbps link. Therefore, such a measurement scheme requires large arrays of high-speed counters. However, such memories are expensive and they can even be technological challenging to build. Thus, significant research efforts have been made to develop accurate per-flow measurement techniques that require less high-speed memory.
Counter braid (CB) is a counter architecture inspired by sparse graph codes. It was introduced by Lu et al. in [1– 3] and has a layered structure. CBs use less space as the flow sizes are compressed “on-the-fly”. A theoretical analysis of maximum-likelihood (ML) decoding was performed in [3] showing that the entropy lower bound can be achieved for any input flow size distribution that has at most power-law tails and decreasing digit entropy. The approach is a “typicalset” decoding approach and the number of layers grows as (log(n))3/2, where n is the number of flows to be counted.
In this work, we propose spatially-coupled CBs (SC-CBs) and analyze their behavior. Spatial coupling was first introduced in the coding theory literature by Felstro¨m and Zigangirov in [4], where they proposed convolutional or SC low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. These codes have very good belief propagation (BP) decoding thresholds due to the phenomenon of threshold saturation. In [5], it was shown that for the binary erasure channel (BEC), the BP decoding threshold saturates to the maximum a posteriori (MAP) decoding threshold of the underlying ensemble. This result was recently extended to binary memoryless channels

Compressed sensing (CS) [9, 10] provides a mathematical framework which shows that sparse signals can be recovered from far less measurements than required by the NyquistShannon sampling theorem. Actually, CS can be seen as reducing the dimensionality of a signal known to be sparse. On the other hand, CBs compress a signal with decreasing digit entropy [3]. II. COUNTER BRAIDS
A CB is a counter architecture of flows composed of several layers. In particular, CBs have a layered structure with ml counters of depth dl bits in the lth layer, where l = 1, . . . , L (L is the total number of layers in the architecture). The number of distinct flows to be counted is denoted by m0.
The lth layer of a CB can be represented by a bipartite graph Gl = Gl(Fl ∪ Cl, El), l = 1, . . . , L, where Fl (of size ml−1) denotes the set of flow nodes (on the left side of the bipartition), and Cl (of size ml) denotes the set of counter nodes (on the right side of the bipartition). The set of edges of the graph is denoted by El. The lth layer is connected to the (l − 1)th layer by a bijective mapping Ξl(f ) = c on the set of flow nodes of the lth layer, where f ∈ Fl and c ∈ Cl−1, l = 2, . . . , L. IV. SPATIALLY-COUPLED COUNTER BRAIDS
Spatial coupling is a technique to improve the performance
of iterative algorithms operating on sparse graphs by imposing
a band structure and termination (as for terminated convolu-
tional codes) on the underlying sparse graph. Below, we will
formalize this methodology to CBs. We will first describe SC-
CBs and then look into the corresponding density evolution
recursion. The ensemble of SC graphs Gc(λ, ρ, N, w) with m0 = κN
flow nodes (on the left), where κ, N , and w are positive integers and w ≤ N + 1, is defined as follows. We assume that κ is chosen such that κRjL′(1)/R′(1) = κRjk/γ and κL′(1)/w = κk/w are integers. A collection of N flow-node groups are placed at positions {1, 2, . . . , N } and a collection of M = N +w−1 counter-node groups are placed at positions {1, 2, . . . , M }. Each flow-node group contains κ nodes of degree k, and each counter-node group contains κRjk/γ nodes of degree j. Now, the κL′(1) = κk edge sockets in each group of flow and counter nodes are partitioned into w groups using a uniform random interleaver. Let the set of flow (resp. counter) node sockets in the nth group at position i be denoted by Pi(,fn) (resp. Pi(,cn)) for n = 0, 1, . . . , w − 1. The SC ensemble is constructed by adding edges that connect the sockets in Pi(,fn) to the sockets in Pi(+c)n,n. Different graphs are obtained by different socket associations. Note that this
construction leaves some sockets of the counter-node groups
at the boundary unconnected and these are removed. In the
following, for notational convenience, we will denote the coupled ensemble Gc(λ, ρ, N, w) by the short-hand notation Gc(k, γ, N, w), since λ(z) and ρ(z) are uniquely identified by k and γ, respectively.

VI. CONCLUSION
In this work, we have studied CBs and proposed SC-CBs.
In particular, we have shown numerically that SC-CBs exhibit
improved iterative decoding thresholds compared to uncoupled
CBs and that their thresholds converge to a fixed value as the number of positions N and w grow. CBs were originally introduced as an efficient counter architecture for per-flow
measurements on high-speed links, but can also be seen as a
CS scheme for nonnegative signals. Moreover, we have shown
that SC-CBs do in fact give a superior undersampling-sparsity
phase transition trajectory (under iterative decoding) in the sparse region compared to random Gaussian measurement matrices with ℓ1-norm minimization reconstruction.
