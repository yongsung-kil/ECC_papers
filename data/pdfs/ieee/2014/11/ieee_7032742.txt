Multi-Gb/s Multi-Mode LDPC Decoder Architecture for IEEE 802.11ad Standard

Sabooh Ajaz and Hanho Lee Department of Information and Communication Engineering, Inha University, Republic of Korea
Email: hhlee@inha.ac.kr

Abstract—This paper presents an area-efficient multi-Gbps multi-mode LDPC decoder architecture for 60GHz wireless gigabit communications. A novel, low-complexity local switch is proposed to implement the multi-mode dynamic column-shifting scheme. Furthermore, the usage of a one’s-complement instead of a two’s-complement number system is explored. Moreover, an efficient quantization method is also presented to reduce the memory and area requirement of the decoder. The synthesis and layout is performed using TSMC 65-nm CMOS technology. Post layout results show that the proposed decoder requires only 0.575 mm2 of area to achieve a throughput of 9.25 Gb/s for all code rates defined under IEEE 802.11ad. The proposed architecture shows much better throughput, as well as better area- and energyefficiency, compared to other multi-mode LDPC architectures.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity check (LDPC) codes, first proposed by Gallager in 1962 have attracted much attention because of their excellent error-correction performance, inherent parallelism and high throughput potential. Therefore, they are widely used in communications standards such as IEEE 802.15.3c and IEEE 802.11ad [1]. The development of multi-gigabit data transmission techniques for 60GHz-band wireless communications systems have necessitated the implementation of highthroughput low-power LDPC decoder architectures to meet the continuing demand for ever-higher data rates. Various decoder architectures are presented for IEEE 802.11ad LDPC codes [2]–[5]. Weiner et al. [2] developed a decoder using check node granularity by placing pre- and post-routers, but no architecture was presented for these routers. This decoder achieves only 3 Gb/s with 1.3 mm2 of area. Park et al. [3] used an architecture similar to [2] but improved performance by using a special embedded dynamic random access memory (EDRAM). It achieves the required throughput of 9 Gb/s with 1.6 mm2 of area. Li et al. [4] implemented a half-row pipelined decoder, which achieves an impressive area of 0.16 mm2 but suffers from the problem of very low throughput. In fact, it shows only 3.45 Gb/s using 65-nm CMOS technology. Our previous work [5] showed an efficient multi-mode LDPC decoder for IEEE 802.11ad. The decoder provided a throughput of 6 Gb/s with 1.1 mm2 of area. However, the major problem with this decoder is limited throughput, since IEEE 802.11ad standard calls for a throughput of nearly 9 Gb/s. Hence, a more efficient architecture is required to achieve the required throughput. Furthermore, a new low complexity local switch is presented in this work to implement a dynamic column shifting scheme. This paper proposes an area-efficient, high-throughput multimode QC-LDPC decoder architecture based on a novel one’s complement scheme.

II. ONE’S COMPLEMENT TECHNIQUE
The min-sum based [6], [7] LDPC decoder architecture generally consists of variable node units (VNUs) and check node units (CNUs). The check node computation for minsum based algorithms requires the inputs in signed magnitude (SM) format. But variable node additions and subtractions are performed in two’s-complement (2’s) format. Thus, almost all decoder architectures includes 2’s-to-SM and SM-to-2’s converter blocks in the critical path. A 2’s-to-SM conversion operation is shown in Fig. 1(a). Basically, it requires an addition operation and an inversion operation depending upon sign. The sign-dependent inversion operation is performed using a low-complexity exclusive-or (XOR) gate. The addition operation is the major source of critical path and hardware complexity. Conversion between one’s-complement (1’s) and sign-magnitude format does not require an addition operation, as shown in Fig. 1(b). So, it only adds an XOR gate in the critical path.
The main difference between 2’s-complement and 1’scomplement addition is end carry addition. Carry out is neglected in 2’s-complement addition, whereas final carry out is added in the result for 1’s-complement addition. So, the implementation of a 2’s-complement adder is simple compared to the 1’s-complement adder. This explains the core reason why 2’s complement is a preferred number system for most LDPC decoders. But the above discussion about the addition is missing the crucial factor of ”Cin” for 2’scomplement adders. In fact, it is assumed that the ”Cin” is zero. The proposed 1’s-complement scheme actually exploits the ”Cin” in a 2’s-complement adder. That is, the proposed scheme employs a 2’s-complement addition operation for 1’scomplement numbers. Hence, it exploits the convenient sign magnitude conversion of a 1’s-complement scheme and the simpler addition of a 2’s-complement scheme.

As described in [5], the local switch is required only for groups with 2block rows (G1 to G4 of rate-1/2 and G3, G4 for rate-5/8). A 2×2 switch is required if the entries deviate from the default position and take any of three switched positions shown in Fig. 3(b). The quantization value of the decoder directly affects the area and a critical path. Generally a 5-bit LLR quantization is a preferred choice for various IEEE 802.11ad LDPC decoders [2]–[4]. A quantization reduction incurs some BER loss. A through analysis showed that the BER loss at 4-bit quantization is due to excessive saturation. More values get saturated as the number of iteration increases. In case of convergence failure we have observed that majority of the errors are corrected within first few iterations (typically within 3 or 4 iterations), but higher number of iterations actually impose a negative effect on convergence and many saturated values actually increases number of errors. Saturation inside LDPC decoders occurs because of additions and subtractions of LLRs and cto-v messages. We have verified that the numbers of saturated values are reduced by restricting c-to-v message values. Restricting or limiting c-to-v values to lower quantization levels give an additional advantage of reduced memory because c-tov messages are also stored in memory. Our simulations show a considerable BER improvement when magnitude of c-to-v values is reduced to 2-bits quantization, while LLR’s and vto-c messages are quantized to 4-bits.

IV. IMPLEMENTATION AND PERFORMANCE COMPARISON
The proposed LDPC decoder architecture was modelled in the Verilog HDL. The synthesis and layout was carried out using synopsys design tools and TSMC 65-nm CMOS technology. Table II shows the implementation and comparison results. Although the single-mode LDPC decoder [4] shows a low area for a single-mode application (rate-13/16), it achieves much lower throughout (3.45 Gb/s at five layered iterations), resulting in much worse area efficiency. The half-row layered decoder [4] shows much less power consumption, but it needs much more time ((3 × layers × iterations) + 1) compared to the proposed decoder (layers × iterations + 1). Hence, it consumes more pJ/bit, resulting in worse power efficiency. Multi-mode LDPC decoder [3] addressed the problem of power consumption by introducing embedded DRAM. The power computation showed impressive improvement, but the decoder requires a much greater area because of dual frame processing and five-stage pipelining. V. CONCLUSION
A multi-mode pipelined layered LDPC decoder for IEEE 802.11ad standard is presented. Moreover, a novel 1’scomplement scheme and a reduced quantization scheme is presented to reduce area and improve critical path of the proposed decoder. The proposed decoder occupies only 0.575 mm2 of core area. In addition, it achieves a throughput of 9.25 Gb/s at 400 M hz for all modes. In terms of throughput, complexity, energy efficiency and area efficiency, the proposed multi-mode LDPC decoder is superior to the previous works.
