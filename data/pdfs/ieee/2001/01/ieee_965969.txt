Structured Iteratively Decodable Codes Based on Steiner Systems and Their Application in Magnetic Recording
Bane Vasic Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering
University of Arizona, Tucson 85721

Abstract-This paper introduces a combinatorial construction of a class of iteratively decodable codes, an approach diametrically opposed to the prevalent practice of using large, random-like codes. Our codes are wellstructured and, unlike random codes, can lend themselves to a very low complexity implementation. A systematic way of constructing codes based on Steiner systems and the Zv, group is presented, and a hardware efficient encoding algorithm is proposed. A substantial performance improvement of high-rate Steiner codes over the existing schemes used in magnetic recording systems is demonstrated..
I. INTRODUCTION
After the discovery that discovery that in additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels long Gallager codes [9] can achieve near-optimum performance [17], a race has begun to come as close as possible to the Shannon limit [27]. In the past few years several lowdensity parity check codes (LDPCs) have been designed with performances very close to the theoretical limit [26] [18]. Also, a significant insight into iterative decoding has been gained due to Kshischang et al. [14] and McEleice et al. [20] (among several others) interpretations of a message passing in terms of belief propagation in graphical models [24] [8]. The graphical model that provides a natural setting in which to describe message passing was introduced by Tanner [30] and reintroduced by Wiberg et al. [33]. Despite this tremendous progress, in the heat of the race code complexity issues have been neglected by many authors. Although the considerations related to higher capacity, speed and error performance are important, the complexity factors tend to dominate system architecture and design considerations, especially in extremely high speed applications such as magnetic recording. Iterative decoders proposed so far have very high hardware complexity and are incapable of operating in the > 1Gbps regime, a speed of the next generation of magnetic recording read channels. The high complexity of the proposed schemes is a direct consequence of the fact that in random codes a large amount of information is necessary to specify positions of the nonzero elements in a parity check matrix. structured low-density parity check codes, a concept diametrically opposed to the prevalent practice of random code construction. (Fossorier, Lin and Kou’s geometric approach [13] is the only exception the author is aware of). The parity check matrix of these codes is completely determined by a small set of parameters, and can lend itself to a very low complexity implementation.
Although bipartite graphs are a quite useful tool for visualizing the message passing algorithm, they are not that convenient in code design. The construction presented in this paper is purely combinatorial and based on balanced incomplete block designs [2], a concept more than a century old and extensively studied. The concept of combinatorial designs is well known, and their relation to coding theory is profound. Many classes of codes including extended Golay codes and quadratic residue QR codes can be interpreted as designs (see [3]). Using combinatorial designs for establishing bounds on minimum distance of LDPC codes has been recently proposed by MacKay and Davey [37].
Our codes are based on Steiner triple systems [16], [4] and the Zv, group, where v is the number of parity check equations describing the code. We construct the balanced incomplete block design using Netto’s [22] difference families with v ≡ 1 mod 6 , v prime power, and show that the complexity
of these codes is extremely low and is basically determined by the size of a difference family upon which a block design is based. An efficient encoding algorithm that exploits the cyclic structure of balanced incomplete block designs is also proposed.
The application of Steiner-type codes in magnetic recording system is illustrated in the second half of the paper. The application of LDPC in magnetic recording has been an active research area in past several years. The results of several studies have been recently reported [7], [31], and several schemes based on random codes have been proposed [6], [5], [23], but no scheme has offered sufficient coding gain to justify a huge increase in encoder/decoder complexity. The first limiting factor in designing a code for magnetic recording is the fact that the code rate must be high (8/9 and higher) because it is practically difficult to compensate for a rate loss by any improved detection technique of reasonable complexity, especially at high recording densities. The second limiting factor is thermal asperities. A thermal asperity occurs when, for example, a read head hits a dust particle on a disk, and may produce such a long burst of errors that cannot be corrected even by a very powerful code, in which case a so called sector read retry must be performed. In order to assess the performance of the proposed codes, we consider a media noise dominated system in which soft information is extracted from a partial response channel using the soft Viterbi algorithm (SOVA) [10] operating on a channel trellis, and then passed back and forth from SOVA to message passing algorithm (MPA). The motivation for using this setting is to understand the potential of Steiner-type codes in a decoding scheme that has a sufficiently low complexity to be implemented in read channel chip operating at speeds above 1Gbps.
II. STEINER SYSTEMS AND THEIR EQUIVALENCE WITH BIPARTITE GRAPHS
A balanced incomplete block design (BIBD) is a pair (V,B), where V is a v-set and B is a collection of b ksubsets of V, called blocks, such that each element of V is contained in exactly r blocks and any 2-subset of V is contained in exactly λ blocks. Since bk=vr, and λ(v1)=r(k-1), the notation BIBD(v,k,λ) is used for a BIBD on v points, block size k, and index λ. The BIBD with a block size k=3 is called a Steiner triple system. A resolvable Steiner triple system with index λ=1 is called a Kirkman system. These combinatorial objects originate from Kirkman’s famous schoolgirl problem posted in 1850 [11] (see also [29]), and have been intensively studied in the combinatorial literature. The Ray-Chaudhuri and Wilson solution of the schoolgirl problem [25] leads to the systematic construction of Kirkman systems that will be used in this paper. For example the collection B={B1,B2, …,B7} of blocks B1={0,1,3}, B2={1,2,4}, B3={2,3,5}, B4={3,4,6}, B5={0,4,5}, B6={1,5,6} and B7={0,2,6} is a BIBD(7,3,1) system or a Kirkman system with v=7, and b=7. We define the block-point incidence matrix of a (V,B) as a v×b matrix A=(aij), in which aij=1 if the i-th element of V occurs in the j-th block of B, and aij=0 otherwise. Each block is incident with the same number of points k, and every point is incident with the same number r of blocks. If b=v, and hence r=k, the BIBD is called symmetric. The concepts of a symmetric BIBD(v,k,λ) with k≥3 and of a finite projective plane are equivalent [19 Chpt 19]. If think of points as parity check equations and of blocks as bits in a linear block code, then the AT defines a parity check matrix H of a Gallager code [9]. The row weight is k, column weight is r, and the code rate is R=(brank(H))/b. MacKay [18] showed that in AWGN channels long Gallager codes with random sparse parity check matrices (referred also as low-denisty parity check codes) can achieve near-optimum bit error rate performance when decoded using iterative algorithms based on belief propagation in graphs [14]. To visualize the decoding algorithm, the parity check matrix is represented as a bipartite graph with two kinds of vertices [20], [33], [30]. The first subset is comprised of bits, and the second is a set of parity check equations. An edge between a bit and an equation exists if the bit is involved in the check. Translated to the block design terminology, the two sets of vertices are V and B, and an edge between v and Bj exists iff v is in Bj. In other words, H is the block-point incidence matrix AT. Therefore, the concepts of a BIBD(v,k,λ) and of a regular bipartite graph are equivalent. We will try to underline the equivalence between the bipartite graph and BIBD terminology in every step of the code design. Notice that it is desirable to have each bit “checked” in as many equations as possible, but because of the iterative nature of the decoding algorithm the bipartite graph must not contain short cycles. In other words the graph girth (the length of the shortest cycle) must be large. These two requirements are contradictory, and the tradeoff is especially difficult when we want to construct a code that is both short and has a high-rate. The girth constraint is equivalent to the constraint that every telement subset of V is contained in as few blocks as possible. If each t-element subset is contained in exactly λ blocks, the underlying design is known as t-design. An example of a 5-design is the extended ternary Golay code (see [3]). However, the codes based on t-designs (t>2) have short cycles, and therefore we will restrict ourselves to the 2-designs, i.e. BIBD, or more specifically to the designs with the index λ=1. The λ=1 constraint means that no more than one block contains the same pair of points, or equivalently that there are no cycles of length four in a bipartite graph. It is also equivalent to the constraint that no pair of columns of a parity check matrix contains two ones at the same position.

III. CODE DESIGN
We have shown that the concept of Steiner systems offers a tool for designing codes without short cycles. Since codes constructed in this way are structured, they lend themselves to a low complexity implementation. In this section we define a simple construction of Steiner systems using difference families of Abelian groups.
Let V be an additive Abelian group of order v. Then t kelement subsets of G, Bi={bi,1,…,bi,k} 1≤i≤t form a (v,k,λ) difference family (DF) if every nonzero element of G can be represented exactly λ ways as a difference of two elements lying in a same member of a family, i.e. occurs λ times among the differences bi,m-bjn, 1≤i,j≤k, 1≤m,n≤k. The sets Bi are called base blocks. If V is isomorphic with Zv ,a group of integers modulo v, then a (v,k,λ)DF is called a cyclic difference family (CDF). In general, the parity check matrix is of the form H=[H1 H2…Ht], where each sub-matrix is a circulant matrix of dimension v×v. The CDF codes have a quasicyclic structure similar to Townsend and Weldon selforthogonal quasi-cyclic codes [38]. Each orbit in the design corresponds to one of sub-matrix in the parity check matrix of quasi-cyclic codes. We can conclude that the shortened self-orthogonal codes (with removed identity matrix) are LDPC codes.

It is straightforward to construct a BIBD design once the
CDF is known. However, finding the CDF is much more
complicated problem and solved only for some values of v, k and
λ. In this section we describe how to construct a CDF. In order to keep our exposition simple, we will use the Kirkman system
difference families constructed by Netto [22] more than a century
ago. Netto’s construction is applicable if v is a prime power v≡1 (mod 6). When v is a power of prime, then Zv is a Galois field (GF(v)), and we can define a multiplicative group Ψ of the field. Let ω be a generator of the multiplicative group (a primitive element in GF(v)) [21]. Write v as v=6t+1, t≥1, and for d, a divisor of v-1, denote by Ψd the group of dth powers in GF(6t+1), and by ωiΨd the coset of d-th powers of ωi. Then the set { ω iΨ2t |1 ≤ i ≤ t } defines a Kirkman (6t+1,3,1) difference
family [22] (see also [34]). For GF(2⋅6+1), the group can be generated by a primitive element ω1=2. Since the order of ω is v1=12, ω12=1, we need the groups of 2t=4 powers of ω1 and ω2 to create base blocks. The powers of ω are {2i mod 13: 1≤i≤12}= {1,2,4,8,3,6,12,11,9,5,10,7}, and the sets B1={2,5,6} and B1={4,10,12} are base blocks of a resulting Kirkman (13,3,1) system. Other triplets can be obtained by adding g∈{1,..,12}.
An alternative combinatorial way of constructing a CDF is

V. APPLICATION IN PARTIAL RESPONSE SYSTEMS
The aim of this section is to estimate the performance of BIBD codes under the most restrictive complexity constraints. We consider a scheme in which a BIBD code is combined with the SOVA and followed by the RS code. This scheme is very likely to be implemented in next several generations of read channels because it offers a good balance among complexity, performance and the architectural modifications in present chips required for iterative decoding.
We consider a media noise dominated system in which the soft information is extracted from a partial response channel using SOVA [10] operating on a channel trellis, and then used in a MPA, passed back to SOVA and so on, using bit likelihoods obtained in MPA as a priori probabilities for SOVA. SOVA rather than the Bahl et al. algorithm (BCJR) [1] is employed because the BCJR is prohibitively complex to implement. We consider an architecture in which SOVA and MPA are concatenated, and a final decision can be taken either after SOVA or after the MPA. The notation (S-M)i is used for a scheme in which SOVA and MPA are concatenated i times, while (SM)i-S is used for a scheme in which after i concatenated pairs of SOVA and MPA, the SOVA is applied once more, and the hard value is produced as an output. The MPA uses the “min-sign” approximation for updating bit likelihoods [9], [31], and the likelihoods have a precision of five bits. The precision of soft information in SOVA and Viterbi is also five bits.
The maximal number of rounds is i=3. Furthermore, in order to reduce misscorrections due to a small number of iterations and convergence problems and in order to reduce the effect of overwhelming the RS decoder, the “power” of iterative decoder is limited in two ways. First, before combined with the intrinsic information, the extrinsic information is scaled by a constant (in our simulations the scaling factor is 0.5, and it is not optimized). Secondly, if after the i rounds of MPA and SOVA the codeword being processed is still not a valid codeword (syndrome is nonzero), not a single correction is made.
We illustrate the application of BIBD codes using the Kirkman (81,3,1) system, with the difference family: {{0,1,39},{0,2,58},{0,3,34}, 0,4,21}, {0,5,67}, {0,6,67}, {0,6,15},{0,7,36},{0,7,36},{0,8,59},{0,10,63},{0,11,37}, {0,12,61},{0,13,48},{0,16,40},{0,27,44}}. It is an illustrative example, and the families are not optimized to deal properly with the propagation of low likelihoods in MPA. Low likelihoods originate from error events that have not been corrected by SOVA, and may cause convergence problems if the affected bits belong to the same cycle. It can be verified that the last two blocks are short, and do not produce 81, but only 68, nonoverlapping

blocks. In our code, only blocks that do not overlap are used, resulting in a codeword length of 1040, and code rate R= 0.922. The effect of short blocks, is not addressed in this paper, but the “trick” similar to the one we used can be exploited to increase code rate in non prime power family cases.

Notice that the assumption that the syndrome at the end of decoding is known may not be a realistic assumption. The reason is that at very high speeds recomputing the syndrome and verifying whether the codeword is valid introduces extra latency in the amount of whole codeword block (1040 bits ). This extra latency in addition to already large latency of at least 2i blocks, necessary to perform i rounds of SOVA and MPA (2i+1 blocks for (S-M)i-S schemes) may be unacceptable. If the syndrome is not known, the propagation of errors in iterative decoding is higher than in the “laboratory conditions” because the probability of miss-correction increases. V. CONCLUSIONS
We have introduced a combinatorial construction of a class of high rate iteratively decodable codes using Steiner (v,3,1) systems and the Zv, group. This construction generates codes with column weight 3 and minimum girth 6. We have constructed a balanced incomplete block design using Netto’s [22] cyclic difference families with v ≡ 1 mod 6 , v prime power, and showed that the encoding
and decoding complexity is extremely low and is basically determined by the size of the difference family upon which the block design is based. We have analyzed the performance of these codes in high density magnetic recording systems. The focal point of our analysis is to asses the performance of Steiner-type codes in a realistic environment, and under tight constraints imposed on the hardware complexity of iterative decoding typical for extremely high speeds. We have shown that significant coding gain over Viterbi detection is achievable.

An interesting problem left for future research is whether some group other than Zv, can also lead to a low complexity implementation. Different choice of a group may reduce the effect of propagation of error events in MPA. The systematical solution of this problem involves searching over all nonisomorphic groups, removing groups with fixed points, since such groups support localized message passing (the equivalent effect is produced by a bad interleaver). On the other hand the groups with small Pasch factor, i.e. small number of 4 set blocks (bits) sharing 6 points (equations), should be favored since they prevent a local message passing .
