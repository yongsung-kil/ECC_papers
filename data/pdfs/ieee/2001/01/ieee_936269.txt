1. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1]have attracted great attention due to their capacityachieving performance and low iterative decoding complexity. At large block lengths, irregular LDPC codes can even outperform the best-known turbo codes [2]. The construction of LDPC codes is
usually random [3], [4], [5], [a]. Gsing a concen-
tration theorem, the authors of [6] show that for an ensemble of sufficiently long LDPC codes with given degree sequences of the underlying Tanner graph (TG), the performance, under belief propagation decoding, tends to be concentrated at the average performance of the ensemble. This average is close to the asymptotic performance predicted for the ensemble based on the degree sequences and the assumption that the TG is cycle-free [6]. How-

ever, at short block lengths (10000 bits or shorter), the T G will most probably contain quite a few cycles which are short with respect to the average number of iterations required for decoding. In fact, it is observed that short codes significantly deviate from the predicted performance 121. It is also known that for short LDPC codes, the performance often varies significantly over the ensemble, especially at high signal-to-noise ratios (SNFb) [5]. Motivated by this, we propose an efficient method to search for good LDPC codes in a given ensemble.
The notion that message-passing algorithms work well if the graph does not contain too many short cycles is widely accepted. In this work, we formalize this “folk knowledge” by introducing a performance criterion, which we call girth distribution. It reflects the distribution of cycles in a TG and is easy to compute. In the following, we first define girth distribution, discuss its importance and study its behavior as a function of block length. \Ye then present simulation results which show that codes selected by this criterion most probably outperform randomly selected codes in the ensemble. Intuitively, girth distribution is related to the

sub-optimality of the iterative decoder. It is well-

known that for a cycle-free TC:, belief propagation

results in optimal decoding. The girth of a symbol

node indicates the length of the shortest path, or

equivalently the smallest number of iterations, for

a message sent by that node to propagate back to

the node itself. Before this number of iterations is

reached, the “belief” associated with the node is

“optimally” propagated to the rest of the graph.

To have a performance closer to the optimal, it is therefore favorable to make symbol nodes’ girths

as large as possible, or in other words, to have more

symbol nodes with larger girths.

The computation of girth at a given node U is

easy: a tree is “grown)’ step by step starting from

the (!root’)U . At step IC, all the nodes at distance

IC from U are included into the tree. This proce-

dure is repeated until, at step IC, a node connected to at least two nodes included at step IC - 1 is in-

cluded. This identifies the formation of the first

cycle. Integer 2k is then the girth at node U . The

complexity of this algorithm is low and quite man-

ageable for short block lengths. In fact, it is easy

to show that the complexity of computing the girth

distribution is O ( n 2 ) w, here n is the block length.

It is interesting t o investigate the behavior of the

girth distribution as a function of n. To perform

this, we construct four ensembles of rate-1/3 LD-

P C codes using construction 2A [3] at block length-

s 960, 1920, 5100 and 11100 bits. Each ensemble contains 2000 codes, all with the same TG degree sequences. For each ensemble, we compute the mean girth distribution (averaged over the ensem-
ble), the standard deviation of girth distributions
(with respect to the ensemble average) and the histogram of the girth averages (Figure 1). It can be seen that for given degree sequences, as the block length increases, TGs on average have more nodes with larger girths, and girth distributions have less variation. The latter phenomenon, which is consistent with the concentration results of [6], indicates that the graph structure, as reflected through the girth distribution, tends to be homogeneous across the ensemble for large block lengths. Short codes, however, vary widely in girth distribution. In the following, based on the heuristic relationship between the girth distribution and the performance of LDPC codes decoded by belief propagation, we propose an efficient method of finding LDPC codes that perform very well, most probably better than a randomly selected code from a given ensemble.
111. SEARCHFOR GOODLDPC CODES
Using a random construction, such as construction 2.4, one can obtain an ensemble of LDPC codes all with the same block length and the same T G degree sequences. While in many cases the performance of codes in the ensemble varies considerably, it is computationally very expensive to find a good code merely based on trial and error. Here, to overcome this, we propose selecting the code with the “best” girth distribution. As the selection criterion for girth distribution, we choose the first order description, i.e., the average, and select the code with the highest girth average.
This method is applied to the design of LDPC codes for the transport of MPEG-2 and ATM packets. The packet lengths are considered to be 1.504 and 456 bits, respectively. For the two cases, using construction 2 4 we construct ensembles of I,DPC codes with block lengths 3480 and 1268, respectively. The histograms of the girth averages for the two ensembles are given in Figure 2 (upper panel). For each ensemble, we then select the code with the highest girth average. Simulations are performed over AWGN channel with BPSK modulation. Bit-error rates (BE&) and message-error rates (MERs) are obtained for both codes and plotted with full lines in Figure 2 (lower panel). For the sake of comparison, we also select one code from each ensemble at random. The BER and MER curves for these codes are also given in Figure 2 (lower panel) with dashed lines.
For both randomly selected codes, an early "flooring" effect is observed in the error-rate curves. This severely limits the performance of the codes at high SNRs. For the selected codes, however, such effect has been eliminated, significantly improving the performance at high SNRs.

It is worth noting that for each ensemble, a few more codes have also been chosen at random and tested. They all perform worse than the selected ones. Also, for the selected codes, the improvement in performance comes at no cost in complexity, i.e., the average number of decoding iterations is almost the same as that for the randomly selected codes.
In another paper [7], we have shown that short LDPC codes designed by the method reported in this article can outperform their turbo product code counterparts. The latter are proposed by some researchers as a prime choice for delay and complexity sensitive applications, see, e.g., [8], and the references therein.
