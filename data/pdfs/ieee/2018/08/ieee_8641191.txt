Sequential Scheduling for Belief-Propagation Demodulation of Rate Compatible Modulation
Huilian Zhang and Shaoping Chen Hubei Key Laboratory of Intelligent Wireless Communications, South-central University for Nationalities, Wuhan 430074, China
Email: spchen@scuec.edu.cn

Abstract—In this paper, two sequential scheduling (SS) algorithms, group-shuffled BP (GS-BP) and bit-to-symbol residual BP (BS-RBP), are proposed to accelerate the convergence speed of the demodulation of rate compatible modulation (RCM). Unlike the conventional BP demodulation that works in a parallel or flooding manner, the proposed schemes work in serial manner. Since the proposed schemes may make full use of the latest messages available in current iteration, a faster convergence rate and a lower bit error rtae (BER) may be achieved. Simulations show that the proposed schemes can effectively accelerate the convergence speed of RCM demodulation and improve the bit error rate (BER) performance.
I. INTRODUCTION
Due to its capability of achieving smooth rate adaptation in highly dynamic wireless channels, rate compatible modulation (RCM) has received a wide attention in recent years [1], [2].
In RCM, the modulated symbols are generated from weighted summation of selected source bits. Since the mapping matrix of RCM modulation is sparse, where only a small number of entries are nonzero in each row or column, the classical belief-propagation (BP) algorithm, which has been successfully used for decoding low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, was adopted for RCM demodulation with some modifications [1]. Although the mapping matrix of RCM and the parity-check matrix of LDPC code share the same “sparsity” characteristics, BP based RCM demodulation has a much higher computational complexity than BP based LDPC decoding. The reason lies in the fact that the messages of symbol nodes in RCM demodulation are calculated by complex convolution operations while those at check nodes in LDPC decoding are obtained by simple addition operations. To tackle the problem of high complexity, Rao et al. presented in [3] a variable weight RCM scheme, which may effectively reduce the computational complexity by employing weight sets with small elements or small cardinality at low SNR regions. Wang et al. presented in [4] a method of using lookup tables and approximate computing to lower the complexity of convolution operations. In addition, BP based decoding and its fast computation are proposed for the data recovery by reducing the size of the graphical model in [5].
The above approaches work well at reducing the computational complexity of BP demodulation for RCM. But they have some limitations for they work in a parallel (flooding) manner, making them unable to use the newest message in the current iteration. Thus, they cannot achieve a fast convergence

rate. To our best knowledge, there are no works reported in the literature that investigate sequential scheduling (SS) schemes for accelerating the convergence speed of RCM demodulation, which is the focus of our work in this paper. Inspired by those works of SS strategies that have been successfully used in LDPC decoding [6]–[13], we propose two SS schemes for RCM demodulation, which are shown to be capable of accelerating its convergence of demodulation and lowering its error floor.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section II briefly reviews RCM and its conventional BP demodulation. Section III presents our proposed SS schemes, i.e., GS-BP and BS-RBP algorithms. Computational complexity analysis and simulation results are provided in Section IV. Finally, conclusion is drawn in Section V.
II. BACKGROUND
RCM is fundamentally a kind of superposition coded modulations [14], [15]. The modulated symbols y = [y1, y2, · · · , yi, · · · , yM ]T are generated from weighted summation of selected source bits x = [x1, x2, · · · , xj, · · · , xN ]T via mapping operation y = Q · x, where Q is an M × N sparse RCM mapping matrix, where each row has only L nonzero entries (L is usually no more than 10), which are elements of the predetermined weight set. Similar to the Tanner graph widely used in LDPC codes description, the bits-to-symbols mapping of RCM can also be illustrated with a bipartite graph that is composed of bit nodes (BNs), symbol nodes (SNs) and connecting edges. Each edge in the bipartite graph corresponds to one nonzero weighting factor in the mapping matrix. The RCM demodulation can be executed by using BP algorithm, which is based on message passing in the bipartite graph. Denote B(i) as the set of bit nodes connected to symbol node i and B(i) \ j the set B(i) excluding bit node j, and denote S(j) as the set of symbol nodes connected to bit node j, and the like.

Since the mapping process of RCM is similar in form to LDPC encoding, it is intuitive to consider whether those SS strategies [6]–[13] that have been successfully used in LDPC decoding are applicable to RCM demodulation. Our research in this paper proves that they can be applied into RCM demodulation with some modifications that match the features of RCM, accordingly, two SS algorithms are presented.

A. The GS-BP demodulation algorithm

In order to make use of the latest messages available as sufficiently as possible such that a faster convergence can be achieved, we propose a group-shuffled BP (GS-BP) demodulation algorithm for RCM. Specifically, we vertically partition the mapping matrix Q into KG groups, with each group containing LG columns, namely, LG = N/KG. During each iteration, the messages are updated group by group such that those latest available B2S messages, which have already been updated in the previous groups, can be used to update the S2B messages of subsequent groups during the current iteration. This strategy of message update is the key difference that distinguish GS-BP demodulation algorithm from the conventioal BP demodulation algorithm, in which the B2S messages can only be used in the next iteration. The reason why we choose vertical partitioning, rather than horizontal partitioning as that in the LBP [7] for LDPC decoding, lies in that the number of RCM symbols used for successful demodulation varies for different channel conditions, resulting in the number of rows of the mapping matrix varying. Thus, the horizontal partitioning will become more complex and unmanageable.

B. The BS-RBP demodulation algorithm

Informed dynamic schedules (IDSs) [9]–[13] are a category

of asynchoronous BP based decoding algorithms for LDPC

codes, among those IDSs, residual belief propagation (RBP)

algorithm [8] is the most representative and pioneering one.

RBP algorithm introduces the so called residual of the message

to evaluate the importance and the contribution to the whole

iteration of this message, the residual of an message is defined

as the difference between the values of this message before

and after an update. The residual of a certain message mk is

computed as:

r(mk) = ||fk(m) − mk||

(8)

where mk and fk(m) denote the values of this message before and after update, respectively. The underlying motivation of IDSs is that a message with the largest residual means that it is very likely to be located in a part of the graph that hasn’t converged yet, the update of this message may make the largest contribution to the upcoming status of convergence, therefore, if this message is updated preferencially, the convergence speed is very likely to be accelerated.
Although IDSs perform well in accelerating the convergence speed of LDPC decoding, we find that they cannot

be directly borrowed for RCM demodulation due to some intrinsic difference between LDPC codes and RCM. For most of the existing IDSs used for LDPC decoding, C2V residuals precomputation is required for the selection of preferential update. This results in a heavy computational complexity that is the cost we have to pay for a better performance. In LDPC decoding, this burden can be significantly alleviated by adoption of approximate calculation of C2V messages [9], where C2V residuals are approximately calculated by many additions and a few multiplications, which is acceptable in practice. However, due to the heavy computational burden of convolution calculation in equation (3), the computation of S2B messages in RCM is much more complex than the computation of C2V messages in LDPC codes. Obviously, it is infeasible to choose the symbol-to-bit residuals as the metrics for selecting the messages to be preferentially updated. For the sake of a lower computational complexity, we choose bitto-symbol residual as the metric for selecting the messages to be preferentially updated. A BS-RBP algorithm for RCM demodulation is proposed according to this metric, which is described in algorithm 2.
To facilitate the following description, we define “one round iteration” as the case that the number of S2B messages that have already been updated is equal to the number of nonzero entries in the mapping matrix of RCM.

B. Simulations
In this section, we provide a large number of simulations to investigate the performance of the proposed GSBP and BS-RBP algorithms. To demonstrate the advantages of the proposed algorithms, the BER performance and the convergence performance of conventional BP, GS-BP and BSRBP algorithms are illustrated for comparison. In simulations, AWGN channel is assumed, the length of the source bits is fixed to be N = 400, and the weight set {±1, ±1, ±2, ±4} is used for RCM symbol generation. Fig. 2 (a) shows the comparison of bit error rate (BER) performance with 200 RCM symbols used for demodulation (M = 200) after 3 round iterations. It is observed that GS-BP and BS-RBP obviously outperform conventional BP. Specifically, at BER = 10−2, the GS-BP and BS-RBP can achieve about 7dB and 5.5dB gain over conventional BP, respectively. At Es/No = 25dB, conventional BP reachs a BER of about 10−2, but GS-BP and BS-RBP have much lower BER, about 10−5 and 10−4, respectively. Fig. 2 (b) shows BER comparison under the same conditions except that 400 RCM symbols are used for demodulation (M = 400). The BER performance advantages of our proposed schemes are reconfirmed.
It is also observed from Fig. 2 that GS-BP will achieve a better BER performance if the mapping matrix is partitioned into more groups. The intuitive explanation is that the more

groups the mapping matrix is partitioned, the more sufficiently the latest messages available are used, resulting in a better performance. But when the number of groups becomes large enough (≥ 50), the BER improvement will be unobvious as the number of groups increases. Hence, we do not need to set the number of groups to be too large.
Fig. 3 compares frame error rate (FER) vs. the number of iterations for different algorithms, where 200 RCM symbols are used for demodulation. It is observed that our proposed algorithms obviously outperform the conventional BP in terms of convergence speed. In Fig. 3 (a), when the number of iterations is large enough (larger than 40), all algorithms have nearly the same FER performance, showing that the information contained in the noisy RCM symbols is not sufficient enough for successful demodulation at Es/No = 15dB. In addition, it is observed in Fig. 3 (b) that at Es/No = 20dB, only about 6 iterations are required for GS-BP and BS-RBP to achieve convergence, while 9 iterations are required for conventional BP. Besides, both GS-BP and BS-RBP outperform the conventional BP in terms of FER performance after convergence. It is also observed that GS-BP can achieve a lower convergence FER than BS-RBP. The explanation is the inherit greediness of RBP based algorithms, where a small group of the edge messages in the Tanner graph are updated with a large number of times while other edge messages are ignored. Since RBP cannot make full use of messages from all received symbols, the BER performance after convergence is degraded. We will investigate less greedy algorithms for RCM demodulation in our future work.
In conclusion, simulation results validate the advantages of our proposed algorithms over conventional BP in both convergence speed and BER performance.
