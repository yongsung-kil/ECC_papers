Information-Propagation-Based Scheduling for the Fast Convergence of Shuffled Decoding

Taehyun Kim, Student Member, IEEE, and Jun Heo , Member, IEEE

Abstract— This letter presents a scheduling scheme for the fast convergence of shuffled decoding. The messages from the variable node group that is expected to propagate the most reliable information are updated first. Mutual information is used to measure the information reliability. Simulation results show that the average number of iterations is reduced by adopting the proposed schedule, compared with previous scheduling schemes. In addition, information-theoretic analysis results show that mutual information in the proposed scheme increases faster than in the previous scheme.
Index Terms— Low-density parity-check codes, shuffled decoding, scheduling, mutual information.
I. INTRODUCTION
L OW-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes were proposed by Gallager in 1962 [1]. Ever since Mackay rediscovered LDPC codes [2], several key studies have investigated their practical usage. In general, the decoding complexity of LDPC codes is represented as O(NeImax), where Ne is the number of edges, and Imax is the maximum number of iterations, respectively. As the number of edges depends on the design of the code, the only way to minimize this complexity bound is to reduce the number of iterations.
The scheduling strategy affects the required number of iterations. In parallel scheduling, check-to-variable (C2V) messages are updated simultaneously, and then variableto-check (V2C) messages are updated simultaneously [1]. On the other hand, in serial scheduling, messages are updated sequentially according to a specific order. The check-nodewise scheduling method is called layered decoding [3], and the variable-node-wise scheduling method is called shuffled decoding [4]. Although the number of iterations required for serial scheduling is half that of parallel scheduling, the decoding delay increases because of the serial process. To decrease the decoding delay, partial-parallel serial scheduling is used. The nodes are divided into a number of groups; the nodes in a group update their messages in parallel, while the groups are processed serially.
In serial scheduling, the updating order is an important factor, since it affects the decoding convergence speed. High-

to-low column-weight-based scheduling for irregular LDPC codes was proposed in [5]. The authors showed that the convergence speed can be faster when messages from highdegree variable nodes are updated first, and low-degree variable nodes are updated later. When the variable nodes have equal degrees, the order is based on the reliability of the related C2V messages [6].
The convergence speed of the serial scheduling increases when the received signal’s randomness due to channel noise is considered. Informed dynamic scheduling [7] predicts the increment of the message reliability, called the residual information, and then updates the node with the largest increment. Thereafter, various dynamic scheduling schemes [8]–[11] were proposed for fast convergence. The drawback of dynamic scheduling is the additional complexity caused by computing the residual information.
The edge-wise fixed scheduling scheme [12] proposed by Lee et al. converges as fast as dynamic scheduling. To determine the order, they focused on the mutual-information increment. The edge with the largest predicted mutual-information increment is updated first. Although edge-wise scheduling has the fastest convergence speed among the fixed scheduling schemes, it requires much more time than the other scheduling schemes. Because its operation time per iteration is ‘the number of edges’ times that of parallel scheduling.
In this letter, we introduce a scheduling scheme for the fast convergence of shuffled decoding. The variable node that is expected to propagate the most reliable information is updated first. Mutual information is used to measure the information reliability.
The remainder of this letter is organized as follows. In Section II, we explain the mutual-information calculation and the details of the proposed algorithm. The simulation results of the proposed scheme are discussed in Section III. Finally, Section IV concludes this letter.

B. Scheduling Based on Information Propagation
To reduce the decoding delay, we grouped the variable nodes according to the base matrix. For instance, the j-th group consists of type-j variable nodes. Therefore, the N variable nodes are divided into Nb groups, and each group contains Z nodes.
The basic principle of the proposed scheduling scheme is that the variable-node type that is expected to propagate the most reliable information to numerous check node types updates first. The reliability of information is measured using the mutual information, and the number of connected check node types is equal to the degree of variable node.

III. SIMULATION RESULTS
Simulations were performed over an additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel with binary phase shift keying (BPSK) modulation. For comparison with the proposed scheme, we simulated several scheduling schemes for shuffled decoding: Original [4], column-weight-based scheduling (CW) [5], and informed fixed scheduling (IFS) [6]. As the bound of convergence speed, edge-wise scheduling (EW) [12] was also simulated. We simulated several practical LDPC codes with different lengths and rates. Three LDPC codes, specified in the IEEE 802.11ad standard [15] with codeword length N = 672 and code rates R = 1/2, 5/8, and 3/4, were used to evaluate the performance. In addition, we show the simulation results for three LDPC codes specified in the IEEE 802.11n standard [16]. Two of the codes have length N = 1944, and rates R = 1/2 and 3/4. The other code has length N = 1296 and R = 2/3. The decoding algorithm is a normalized min-sum algorithm with scaling factor 0.75, and the maximum number of iterations is limited to 10.
Figures 1 and 2 show similar error correction performances for each schedule. Then, the convergence-speed comparisons are given in Fig. 3 and 4, in terms of the normalized average number of iterations, which is obtained by dividing the average number of iterations of each schedule by the average number of iterations of the original schedule. From the simulation results, we confirmed that the proposed scheme converges faster than the previous schemes, and the gap between the proposed scheme and the previous schemes increases as the SNR increases. In the case of the 802.11ad code with rate 3/4, the schedules of CW, IFS, and the proposed scheme are equal. The scheduling effect increases as the number of nodes in a group decreases.
