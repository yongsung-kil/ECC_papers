Block-error Threshold Analysis of Protographs in 5G-Standard
Asit Kumar Pradhan and Andrew Thangaraj Department of Electrical Engineering
Indian Institute of Technology Madras, Chennai 600036, India Email: asit.pradhan,andrew@ee.iitm.ac.in

Abstract— Block-error threshold analysis of protographs in 5G standard are considered over binary erasure channel (BEC) and binary additive white Gaussian noise (BIAWGN) channel. For protographs with degree-one variable nodes, conditions are derived to ensure equality of bit-error threshold and blockerror threshold. Using this condition, it is shown that blockerror threshold and bit-error threshold for protographs in in 5G standard are the same. Protographs with block-error threshold close to capacity are designed and shown to have better error rate performance than the protographs in 5G standard.
I. INTRODUCTION
Protograph low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] are popular in practice and used in several communication standards. 3GPP have proposed protograph based LDPC codes to be used in data channel of 5G communication [2], [3]. Several applications of 5G require high data speed at very low frameerror rate. Bit-error threshold is defined as the maximum value of channel parameter below which bit-error rate goes to zero as blocklength becomes large. Similarly, Block-error threshold is defined as the maximum value of channel parameter below which block-error rate goes to zero as blocklength becomes large. To ensure low block-error rate at high data speed, codes should be designed to have block-error threshold close to channel capacity. However, block-error threshold of many popular codes, such as AR4A [4], protograph-based raptor-like codes (PBRL) [5], with bit-error threshold close to capacity are unknown. Designing protographs to have good bit-error threshold does not guarantee good block-error threshold. For example, simulation result shows that AR4A protographs [4] have bit-error threshold close to capacity and block-error threshold away from capacity. In [6], authors have shown that block-error and bit-error thresholds are the same under message-passing decoder for codes having minimum variable node degree greater than two. In our earlier work [7], we have extended the block-error condition in [6] allowing degreetwo nodes in protograph LDPC ensembles under the condition that the degree-two subgraph of the protograph is a tree and minimum variable node degree is greater than one. However, computer search shows that low-rate codes (rate ≤ 1/3), such as AR4A , PBRL codes, protographs in 5G standard [2], with bit-error threshold close to capacity have significant fraction of degree-one variable nodes.
In this work, we extend the block-error threshold condition in [7] to allow degree-one bit nodes, which play an important

role in designing low-rate codes with block-error threshold close to capacity. Protographs in 5G standard [2] and PBRL codes [5] satisfy the block-error threshold condition derived in this paper, while AR4A protographs [4] do not satisfy the block-error threshold condition. Using the new blockerror threshold condition, we have designed low-rate codes with block-error threshold close to capacity. For the binary additive white Gaussian noise (BIAWGN) channel, we have designed a code of rate-1/3 and blocklength-64800 which has better BER/FER performance than the rate-1/3 PBRL code and protographs in 5G standard.
Rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section II introduces definition and notation for protograph LDPC codes and describes density evolution over binary erasure channel (BEC). Section III derives conditions on protograph and component codes under which block-error threshold equals bit-error threshold for large-girth ensembles. Optimization of protograph DGLDPC code is described in Section IV.
II. DEFINITION AND PRELIMINARIES
A. Protograph LDPC codes
Following the notation in [1], a protograph G = (V ∪ C, E) is a bipartite graph with the bipartition V and C called the set of variable or bit and check nodes, respectively, and E being the set of undirected edges that connect a variable node in V to a check node in C. Multiple parallel edges are allowed between a variable node and a check node. The nodes and edges in the protograph are ordered, and the i-th variable node, check node and edge in the protograph are denoted, respectively, vi, ci and ei. The variable and check nodes connected by an edge ei are denoted vei and cei , respectively.
B. Structure of Protographs in 5G Standard
As shown in the Fig 1, each protograph in 5G standard has five submatrices: A, D, O, E, I. The submatrix A corresponds to the information bits. First column of the submatrix D has weight three and rest of its columns form a bidigonal structure. Parity bits corresponding to the submatrix D are generated from only information bits. O is a zero matrix. I is an identity matrix. Parity bit corresponding to a column of the submatrix I is generated by using information bits and a parity bit corresponding to a column of D.

Parity check matrix corresponding to optimized protographs, protographs in 5G standard, and AR4A protographs are obtained by cyclic progressive edge growth described in [8]. Optimized protographs in this work have better block-error threshold than protographs of same rate in 5G standard by 0.1dB. For example, the 28 × 41, rate -1/3 protograph in this work which allows multiple edges between nodes has block-error threshold of −0.405dB, whereas the 46 × 68, rate-1/3 protograph in 5G standard which does not have multiple edges between nodes has a block-error threshold of −0.225dB. Although block-error threshold condition is derived assuming infinite blocklength, FER performance of optimized protographs are better than their corresponding codes in 5G, PBRL, and AR4A protographs when blocklength is finite as shown in Fig 5. For example, at FER=10−3 and blocklength 64000, the rate-1/5 protograph proposed in this paper has a gap of 0.463dB to capacity, whereas the rate-1/5 protograph in 5G standard has a gap of 0.513dB to capacity.
In summary, we designed low-rate codes with block-error threshold close to capacity. From simulation, we observe that optimized codes have better FER performance than comparable protographs of same rate. This work provides a theoretical basis for LDPC codes in 5G standard.
