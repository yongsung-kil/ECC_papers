Dual-mode Channel Decoding Kernel Design  
Using FBA-based Layered LDPC Decoding  
Cheng-Hung Lin and Jin-Kun Shen  
Department of Electrical Engineering 
Yuan Ze University 
Jungli, Taiwan 32003, R.O.C. 
chlin@saturn.yzu.edu.tw 
 
Abstract—This paper presents a dual-mode low-density 
parity-check (LDPC) and turbo decoding kernel for powerline 
communication systems. Because the layered decoding schedule 
achieves a fast convergence speed, the proposed dual-mode 
decoding kernel implements the forward-backward algorithm 
with the layered decoding schedule. A multifunction a posteriori 
calculator, one key module of the dual-mode decoding kernel, is 
proposed to achieve high hardware sharing in this paper. Thus, 
the proposed dual-mode decoding kernel can achieve efficient 
hardware usage. 
Keywords—low-density parity-check,, HomePlug, G.hn. 
I. 
 INTRODUCTION 
Recently, the connected home and internet of things (IoT) 
applications let the powerline communication systems become 
the most popular research issues. HomePlug standard adopting 
double binary (DB) turbo code [1] is usually exploited in the 
powerline communications for connecting power source and 
power cord. G.hn standard adopting low-density parity- check 
(LDPC) code [2] was mentioned to support all types of in-
home wiring which is employed in IoT applications. Since 
HomePlug and G.hn standards adopted the distinct channel 
codes, it is feasible to design a multi-mode turbo/LDPC 
decoding 
kernel 
to 
support 
the 
different 
powerline 
communication applications. 
For wireless applications, the multimode soft-in soft-out 
(SISO) kernel design [3] has been proposed to support the 
alternative turbo and LDPC decoding. It employs the DB 
enhanced max-log-MAP algorithm (EML-MAPA) with warm-
up hybrid window (HW) decoding schedule in turbo mode and 
forward-backward algorithm (FBA) with the two-phase 
message passing (TPMP) decoding schedule in LDPC mode.  
However, the work in [3] requires a large number of iteration 
to maintain correcting performance and leads to a low 
throughput in LDPC mode because of employing the TPMP 
decoding schedule. To solve this problem, this paper initially 
proposes FBA-based layered LDPC decoding. Using the 
layered decoding schedule provides a fast convergence speed 
in correcting performance compared with the TPMP schedule 
[4]. Besides, this paper also introduces the modified dual-mode 
(turbo/LDPC) SISO kernel that employs EML-MAPA with the 
next initial iteration (NII) HW decoding [5] in turbo mode and 
the layered FBA decoding in LDPC mode.

III. 
DUAL-MODE CHANNEL DECODING KERNAL 
The architecture of the proposed dual-mode decoding 
kernel can be referred to Fig. 4 in [3]. However, the proposed 
decoding kernel employs the NII HW schedule in turbo mode. 
Thus, the dummy backward multifunction recursion processing 
element (MFRP-βd) in [3] must be removed in the proposed 
dual-mode decoding kernel because the NII HW schedule 
computes no initial state vectors for the MFRP-β. Without the 
MFRP-βd, a modified multifunction a posteriori calculator unit 
(MMFACU) is proposed to implement the function that 
calculates the new extrinsic LLR values by MFRP-βd in [3]. 
Thus, the MMFACU achieves more hardware sharing and 
hardware usage than the work in [3].

key unified module of the radix-4 layered FBA. The 
MMFACU is composed of 4 ADDER units, 4 MIN&MAX units, 
1 MAX2 unit, 1 extrinsic calculator unit (ECU), and 1 LDPC-
SIGN unit. To smoothly operate the radix-4 layered FBA in 
hardware, we separate the a posteriori LLR operation into 
several additions and comparisons which are represented 
ADDER units and MIN&MAX units, respectively. In turbo 
mode, the data flow is from the ADDER units through the

EML-MAPA. In LDPC mode, the data flow is from 
MIN&MAX units which generates the total minimum values (3) 
from the MFRP-α and MFRP-β to the LDPC-SIGN unit to

Fig. 2(a) shows the detailed architecture of MIN&MAX 
units for the turbo and LDPC decoding. In turbo mode, each 
MIN&MAX unit has 2 4-input and 1-output comparators which 
have totally 6 comparators in the MMFACU. To support the  
LDPC decoding for G.hn, there are 48 values which need to 
calculate using 24 2-input and 1-output comparators in the 4 
MIN&MAX units. As the results, the 2 4-input and 1-output 
comparators are divided into 6 2-inputs and 1-output 
comparators in each MIN&MAX unit. The MIN&MAX unit 
completely unifies the turbo and LDPC decoding without 
redundant hardware overhead. Fig. 2(b) shows the detailed 
architecture of ADDER unit for the turbo and LDPC decoding. 
In turbo mode, each ADDER unit calculates α, β and γ together 
and has totally 16 adders to calculate a posteriori Radix-4 LLR 
of DB EML-MAPA. In LDPC mode, the ADDER unit 
generates the V2C messages (4) of the radix-4 layered FBA. 
To support the LDPC decoding for G.hn, there is a summation 
of 10 values in each row, where are 5 extrinsic values and 5 
C2V messages. However, the ADDER unit calculates 8 rows at 
the same time, so there are totally 80 values and need 40 adders. 
Each ADDER unit only activates 10 adders and there are 6 
adders are idle in LDPC mode.

CONCLUSION 
In this paper, the dual-mode decoding kernel was proposed 
to employ the layered FBA for LDPC decoding. The 
MMFACU was also proposed to achieve high hardware 
sharing and hardware usage. The proposed dual-mode kernel 
design achieves a large area reduction to perform the 
alternative LDPC/Turbo decoding.
