Efficient Encoding of Quasi-Cyclic Low-Density Parity-Check Codes

Tingting Liang, Peng Zhang, Changyin Liu
School of Information Engineering Communication University of China
Beijing, China 1103391943@qq.com

Jin Liu
Engineering Research Center of Digital Audio and Video Communication University of China Beijing, China

AbstractвҖ”A compact encoding process with three phases is proposed for quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes. A back substitution circuit is shared at the first and third phases. A high-speed vectorвҖ“dense-matrix multiplier is well designed for the second phase, which offers tradeoffs between speed and memory. The proposed encoding method just removes some nonzero circulants of a sparse parity-check matrix H, while the classical RichardsonвҖ“Urbanke (RU) approach splits H into multiple submatrices and destroys its integrity. It is shown that the proposed encoder runs fast, has low complexity, and is well compatible with multiple code rates.
KeywordsвҖ”QC-LDPC codes; encoding method; encoder; CDR
I. INTRODUCTION
Because of their better performance, low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes were adopted as the channel coding scheme for 5G in 2016. As an important subclass of LDPC codes, quasi-cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes with low implementation complexity have been previously adopted by many international standards, such as IEEE 802.15.3c, IEEE 802.11n, IEEE 802.16e, Digital Television Terrestrial Multimedia Broadcasting (DTMB), and Convergent Digital Radio (CDR) [1-4].
To satisfy the increasingly demanding data rates, fast encoders with low complexity are required. A QC-LDPC encoder based on a sparse parity-check matrix H is often preferable to the one based on a dense generator matrix G. The most widely used encoding method based on H is the RichardsonвҖ“Urbanke (RU) approach [5]. Zhong et al. first applied it to QC-LDPC codes. The RU encoding approach is broken into 6 phases, uses a small dense matrix and five sparse submatrices, and involves sparse-matrixвҖ“vector multiplication, dense-matrixвҖ“vector multiplication, and back substitution [6]. To reduce the complexity, each H in IEEE 802.15.3c is well designed and does not involve dense-matrixвҖ“vector multiplication [1]. To simplify back substitution, QC-LDPC codes in several existing standards have multiple-diagonal parity-check structure. For example, each H in IEEE 802.11n and IEEE 802.16e has dual diagonals [2, 7], and each H in DTMB has three diagonals [3]. In CDR system, there are four code rates, О· = 1/4, 1/3, 1/2, and 3/4. When О· = 1/2 and 3/4, H has dual diagonals; otherwise, it has general structure.

In this paper, we simplify the RU encoding process into 3 phases by using a big sparse matrix instead of five small sparse submatrices. Also, we design an encoder which only requires two circuits, back substitution circuit and high-speed vectorвҖ“ dense-matrix multiplier. The proposed method and encoder are suitable for nonsingular H of arbitrary structure, and hence we take CDR standard as an example to compare performance with the encoder in [6].

B. Circuit Structure
Computing viвҖІ by (13) in ascending order of iвҖІ is a back substitution process, which can be implemented by the circuit shown in Fig. 3. The block column numbers and shift numbers of nonzero circulants in HвҖІ, H1, k1, H1, k2, вҖҰ, Hi, kN, Hi+1, k1, вҖҰ, Hc, kN, are stored in a lookup table (LUT) block row by block row. The LUT is implemented by the read-only memory (ROM), and the accumulator Acc2 is its read address generator. The ROM outputs si,k and the source address k. v is stored in the random-access memory (RAM), which outputs the source operand vk. The barrel shifter rotates vk (or 0) si,k times to the left, and the resulting vector is added to the accumulator Acc1. Usually, the subscript kN of Hi, kN is bigger than k1 of Hi+1, k1. Therefore, if k at the output port of the ROM becomes smaller, this means that viвҖІ is evaluated by (13), and the circuit is ready to calculate viвҖІ+1. At the moment, the destination operand viвҖІ is written into the RAM, Acc1 is cleared to zeros, and the destination address iвҖІ is incremented by 1.
C. Effects of Path Latency
In Fig. 3, the barrel shifter with a dichotomy structure has П„ pipeline stages, where П„ = вҢҲlog2bвҢү is the smallest integer not less than log2b. To keep iвҖІ in sync with viвҖІ, the path latency of П„ clock cycles in the destination address generator is used to compensate for the inherent delay of the barrel shifter.
In (13), k < iвҖІ. However, the path latency in the destination address generator can cause k вүҘ iвҖІ. When this happens, the outputs invalid vk because viвҖІ (let alone vk) is not yet evaluated.

If k < iвҖІ, read address is incremented by 1, ROM and RAM output new data, and vk is fed into the barrel shifter; otherwise, the outputs of the read address generator, ROM, and RAM remain unchanged, and 0 is fed into the barrel shifter.
D. Difference between the Two Phases
Recall that x doesnвҖҷt need to be computed at the third phase. If the first L block rows in B are zeros, the first L sections of py also donвҖҷt need to be computed because these sections are exactly equal to the results at the first phase. In short, the first L and last u block rows of HвҖІ are not used at the third phase. Therefore, the initial and final values are different at the two phases, whether for Acc2 or for Acc3.
E. Speed
Suppose that there are Оұ and ОІ nonzero circulants in HвҖІ and its middle (c вҖ“ L вҖ“ u) block rows, respectively. Because of the path latency, clock cycles spent on calculating x and py at the first and third phases is lower bounded by (Оұ + П„) and (ОІ + П„), respectively. The total time is lower bounded by (Оұ + ОІ + 2П„) clock cycles.
F. Complexity
Since b is often big, we only consider the resource consumptions of ROM, RAM, the barrel shifter, and the accumulators Acc1 and Acc2. In such a situation, the proposed circuit needs (вҢҲlog2tвҢү + вҢҲlog2bвҢү)Оұ bits of ROM, (t + u)b bits of RAM, П„b flip-flops, b two-input XOR gates, and one read address generator.
V. VECTOR-DENSE-MATRIX MULTIPLIER
The type IV RLA (RLA-IV) module is especially suitable for the implementation of (4) [8]. px = (vtвҖ“c+1, vtвҖ“c+2, вҖҰ, vtвҖ“c+u) can be evaluated in b clock cycles by a multiplier with u RLAIV modules. To speed up, a high-speed vectorвҖ“dense-matrix multiplier based on u modified RLA-IV modules is proposed, as shown in Fig. 4. Let ОҰj represents the jth block column of ОҰT for 1 вүӨ j вүӨ u. Unlike the LUT of a RLA-IV module, the modified LUT is formed by the first R rows of each circulant of ОҰj, instead of only the first row (i.e. the circulant generator). The result is that the degree of parallelism is improved R times.

VII. CONCLUSION
By using a big matrix instead of five trial ones derived from the parity-check matrix, we give a compact QC-LDPC encoder, consisting of two well-designed circuits. The high-speed vectorвҖ“dense-matrix multiplier has optional parallelism, offering tradeoffs between speed and memory. The proposed encoder has good compatibility with multiple code rates by only increasing ROM. It is shown that, compared with the the encoder in [6], the proposed one can greatly improve speed and reduce logical resources at the cost of more memory. Besides CDR system, the proposed method is generally suitable for other QC-LDPC codes. It provides a good alternative for application designers.
