New Min-Sum Decoders Based on Deep Learning
for Polar Codes
Bin Dai∗, Rongke Liu∗, and Zhiyuan Yan† ∗School of Electronic and Information Engineering, Beihang University, Beijing, China †Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, Lehigh University, Bethlehem, PA 18015, USA
Email: {daibinok, rongke liu}@buaa.edu.cn, zhy6@lehigh.edu

Abstract—In this paper, we propose two novel min-sum (MS) decoding algorithms based on deep learning for polar codes, an offset min-sum (OMS) and a scaling offset min-sum (SOMS) algorithm. The parameters of both algorithms are different from iteration to iteration, and are obtained by training over a deep neural network. Our simulation results show that the OMS algorithm has roughly the same error performance as a previously proposed multiple scaling min-sum (MSMS) algorithm, and that the SOMS algorithm performs better than all existing BP-based algorithms. Since the OMS algorithm requires only an addition as opposed to a multiplication in the MSMS algorithm, the OMS algorithm is more suitable for hardware implementation. The two proposed decoding algorithms provide a tradeoff between complexity and error performance.
Index Terms—Polar codes, deep learning, belief propagation
I. INTRODUCTION
P OLAR codes [1] have been regarded as one major breakthrough of channel codes due to their capacity-achieving property. Compared with Turbo and low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, polar codes [2] have lower encoding and decoding complexity when decoded by successive cancellation (SC) [1]. Successive cancellation list (SCL) decoding [3] of polar codes improves the performance with higher complexity. Since polar codes have excellent bit error rate (BER) performance for short block lengths based on list decoding, they have been adopted as the channel coding scheme for control information for 3GPP eMBB scenario [4]. To meet the stringent throughput and latency requirements of 3GPP eMBB scenario, the iterative belief propagation (BP) decoder [5] for polar codes is attractive because it is inherently parallel. However, BP decoding suffers from a higher computational complexity. Hence, the min-sum (MS) algorithm [6] for polar codes was proposed to reduce the decoding complexity.
In recent years, deep learning technologies have provided dramatic performance improvements compared with conventional hand-crafted schemes in many tasks. For example, stateof-the-art results are achieved in the speech recognition [7], game playing [8] and machine translation [9]. Recently, deep learning technologies show potential for applications in the field of channel coding. One of more recent applications for which deep learning has shown promise is the design of deep learning-aided decoders for error-correcting codes [10]– [15]. The deep learning-aided decoder [10] can achieve near optimal performance by learning a dataset of 2k codewords (k is the number of information bits), leading to exponential

complexity during the training period. Even with the powerful machine learning libraries such as Theano [16] and Tensorflow [17], this exponential complexity still hinders straight-forward learning for practical code lengths as shown in [11]. It was observed in [11] that the decoder works for only very short lengths and cannot scale to arbitrary code length. To scale for larger lengths, the idea of using deep learning techniques to train the weights of BP deocder for high density parity check (HDPC) codes has been proposed in [12]. Cammerer et al. [13] use the neural network based components in [11] to replace some sub-blocks of BP decoder for polar codes. This method works better for larger sub-blocks, but still has high decoding complexity. To achieve low complexity and latency, a multiple scaling MS decoder, referred to as the MSMS decoder henceforth, is proposed in [14]. This method achieves better performance than the original BP decoder and can be used for practical code lengths. Using machine learning techniques, the neural offset MS [15] achieves a better performance compared with the traditional BP decoder for BCH codes. As we know, scaling MS decoder and offset MS decoder have similar performance for LDPC codes [18].
Hence, in this paper, we consider MS decoders for polar codes which take advantage of both scaling and offset. We firstly apply offset MS (OMS) decoder to polar codes and also propose a scaling offset MS (SOMS) decoder for polar codes. Similar to the MSMS algorithm in [14], the offsets and scaling factors are different from iteration to iteration, and they are obtained by deep neural network. Although scaling offset MS decoder has been studied for LDPC codes, our learningaided SOMS decoder is novel for polar codes to the best of our knowledge. Simulation results show that the OMS decoder has similar performance to the MSMS decoder in [14] and that our SOMS decoder performs better than all existing BPbased algorithms. In addition, the proposed OMS algorithm is more suitable for hardware implementation, since it requires only an addition as opposed to a multiplication in the MSMS algorithm. The two proposed algorithms provide a tradeoff between complexity and error performance.
This paper is organized as follows. In Section II, a short background on polar codes is presented and the BP decoding algorithm is briefly described. Section III presents the proposed OMS and SOMS decoding algorithms. Then, the configuration of proposed algorithms based on deep learning is introduced in Section IV. Experiment results are provided in Section V and Section VI concludes this paper.

II. PRELIMINARIES
A. Polar Codes
Channel polarization [1] is a method to construct capacityachieving codes for symmetric binary-input discrete memoryless channels (B-DMCs). To generate an (N, K) polar code, the encoding process is divided into two steps. First, K information bits are assigned to the more reliable K positions of N -bit messages u = (u1, u2, . . . , uN ). The remaining (N −K) unreliable positions are set to a value known by both encoder and decoder. They are usually set to 0 and are called frozen bits. B. Belief-Propagation Decoder
Belief-propagation (BP) decoder is an iterative decoding method for channel codes over factor graph [19]. The BP algorithm can also be used to decode polar codes, because they can be represented by factor graphs. Different from the successive cancellation (SC) decoding and its list extension called successive cancellation list (SCL) decoding, BP decoder is an iterative message passing process, whose structure is a better match to neural networks and enables parallel processing. In general, an (N, K) polar code can be represented as an n-stage factor graph, where n = log2 N . The factor graph contains (n + 1) × N nodes. Node (i, j) has two types of log likelihood ratio (LLR) messages: left-to-right message Ri,j and right-toleft message Li,j for 1 i N and 1 j n + 1. All the soft messages are transmitted and updated iteratively among adjacent nodes over the factor graph.

To approach the performance of the BP decoder, the SMS decoder with a unified scaling factor α = 0.9375 is presented in [6]. In addition, multiple scaling factors for different nodes are optimized to obtain better results in [14]. V. EXPERIMENTS
In this section, we first introduce the DNN training details. Then, we show the BER results for different algorithms for decoding polar codes. We also present and discuss the scaling factor and offset histograms for the SOMS algorithm. Python 3.6 and MATLAB are used in the simulation analysis. To make a fair comparison, the results are tested on the (64, 32) and (512, 256) polar codes used in [14].

A. Neural Network Training
Our DNN is performed on the TensorFlow framework [17]. Due to geometric uniformity of linear codes, the training sets are generated by the all-zeros codewords through an additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel. The SNRs range from 1 dB to 4 dB. Training is implemented using mini-batch SGD

method as mentioned before. We apply the adaptive method Adam [21] with learning rate η = 0.001. The mini-batch size M = 120 (30 codewords for each SNR). Note that all offsets are initialized to 0 and the initial scaling factors are set to 1.
B. Testing Results
We compare the BER performance of the proposed OMS and SOMS algorithms with some existing algorithms for the (64, 32) and (512, 256) polar codes used in [14] based on Monte-Carlo simulation. For a fair comparison, our comparison considers only BP-based decoding algorithms due to potential throughput and latency issues of SC-based decoding algorithms. The results of the proposed algorithms, BP algorithm and the MSMS algorithm from [14] are produced on our simulation platform. For the testing data, we generated random binary messages (100000 frames for each SNR). The frame error rates (FERs) at these SNRs are at least 10−3, and hence for each SNR we have at least 100 frames in error. The number of iterations for all algorithms is set to 5. Fig. 5 shows the BER results for different algorithms for the two polar codes. The OMS algorithm performs roughly the same as the MSMS algorithm, which achieves better BER performance than the conventional BP decoder. Moreover, the SOMS algorithm outperforms the other three algorithms, and has an obvious advantage in the high SNR region.
C. Parameter Histograms for OMS or SOMS algorithms
The histograms of offsets and scaling factors for the SOMS algorithm of the (64, 32) polar code with respect training epochs are given in Figs. 6 and 7, respectively. Each training epoch consists of 200 mini-batches. At the beginning, most parameters are close to the standard normal distribution centered at their initial values. From epochs 2 to 5, it can be observed that the decoders are learning the multiple parameters. The training process can converge by the steady histogram after 4 or 5 epoches. Figs. 6 and 7 show that the parameters converge quickly because only a few epochs are needed to train the DNN. Hence, the proposed algorithms can be used for practical code lengths based on the DNN.
D. Hardware Implementations
The proposed OMS and SOMS algorithms based on deep learning are also hardware-friendly since the training and testing are both based on the same factor graph shown in Fig. 1. The hardware architecture of the proposed algorithms are similar to that of the MSMS algorithm. Thus, we will briefly represent the difference in the hardware implementation between proposed algorithms and the MSMS algorithm. Since the OMS algorithm uses no multiplications which only takes additions, the OMS algorithm can only use the adder instead of the multiplier for the MSMS algorithm. Thus, the OMS algorithm will lead to more efficient hardware implementations than the MSMS algorithm. However, the SOMS algorithm uses the adder, and the multiplier, which has a higher implementation complexity. Thus, the proposed OMS and SOMS algorithms provide a tradeoff between performance and complexity. All learning-aided BP based algorithms would need more memories to store extra coefficients.

VI. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
In this paper, we proposed two novel MS (OMS and SOMS) algorithms using parameters obtained by a deep neural network. At the expense of additional complexity, the SOMS algorithm outperforms all existing BP-based algorithms. Furthermore, the parameter histograms for the proposed algorithms show that they can be trained through the DNN in a few epochs for practical code lengths.
