Abstract—We present a loop-removing algorithm for lowdensity-parity-check-code (LDPC) and a model for spatially multiplexed multiple-input-multiple-output (MIMO) ultraviolet communication (UVC). The performance of the LDPC in 2×2 UVC system is demonstrated with simulation and experimental results.
Keywords—4-loops-removal, LDPC, MIMO, UVC
I. INTRODUCTION
Compared with other wireless communication, UVC has many advantages such as non-line-of-sight (NLOS) links, all-directional and anti-jamming [1,2]. However, UVC has two disadvantages: high bit error rate and low bandwidth. Large attenuation of the atmosphere channel results in low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) at the receiver. Thus, we need to find a solution to reduce BER at such a low SNR. LDPC has been proved an effective way to reduce the BER [3,4]. In our previous work[5], we designed a LDPC heuristic check matrix construction method suitable for UVC, but that construction method may cause loops in the check matrix and aggravate the error correction. In this paper, we will present an algorithm to remove loops in the LDPC check matrix and apply this loop-removed LDPC to the UVC

system. Our simulation results show that the loop removal can achieve about 5dB extra coding gain for different code lengths. However, LDPC coding will occupy the limited UVC bandwidth. MIMO is an effective means to improve spectral efficiency, which has been demonstrated in UVC [6]. In this paper, we will propose a model for spatially multiplexed MIMO UVC system to improve effective bandwidth. Our simulation and experimental results show for low SNR and low-power interference light, the MIMO system can obtain 22.6dB coding gain and BER of 1.87×10-4.
II. MIMO UVC SYSTEM WITH LDPC
Basic structure of a MIMO UVC system with LDPC is shown in Fig.1. The transmitter includes the modulator, LDPC encoder and light emitting diodes (LED) arrays; the receiver involves the photo multiplier tubes (PMTs), demodulator and LDPC decoder. The LED arrays can be modulated by intensity-modulated signals encoded by LDPC encoder. The PMTs detect the UV light and converts the light signals to electrical signals. Then, the electrical signals are processed by the MMSE signal detection method. Finally, the demodulation and LDPC decoding will recover the emitted signal.

III. LOOP REMOVAL ALGORITHM AND SIMULATION RESUILTS
A. Loop Removal Algorithm
As proved in [7], the small loops in the Tanner graph representation of LDPC are detrimental to BER performance, especially for those loops with length 4 (i.e. 4-loops). For a 4-loop, the bit likelihood information will circulate infinitely between four nodes. Not only does LLR transferring fail to complete error correction, but also a correct node may be turned into a wrong one. Therefore, we design a 4-loop removing algorithm to improve the error correction efficiency.

Fig. 2 shows the basic principle of the 4-loop removing algorithm. The process can be divided into two steps, i.e. 4-loop positioning and removing. During the 4loop positioning, the vector posi = [posi1 posi2 … posin] represents the position of all n ones in the i-th row, where posin is the entry for the column of the n-th one in the i-th row. By comparing any two vectors posi and posj, we can check whether there is a 4-loop between the i-th and j-th rows if the sums of two corresponding entries in both rows are equal to 2, and these entries (i.e. posik, posil, posjk and posjl) represent the loop’s position. Then, we try to find a row m, where neither posmk nor posml is equal to one. We interchange posmk or posml with the corresponding entry in the i-th or j-th row. Since such entry interchanging may generate a new 4-loops, the algorithm repeats the above checking process until the entire check matrix does not involve 4-loops. After this algorithm, a new check matrix without 4-loops is produced. The pseudo-code of the algorithm is shown in Fig. 3.

end for until loops removed

The impact of 4-loops on the performance of LDPC can be clearly observed in Fig.4. With the changing of SNR, the BERs of LDPC-coded system and un-coded system are both changed. Thus, we use the coding gain as the LDPC code performance criteria, i.e. the ratio of uncoded system BER to the coded one. As the SNR becomes lower, the impact of 4-loops is getting negligible and the coding gain difference between LPDC with and without 4loops is getting smaller. While the SNR becomes larger, the uncorrectable errors caused by the 4-loops becomes more, and the coding gain difference is increasing. Our simulation results show that the loop removal can achieve about 5dB extra coding gain for different code lengths. The 512-code-length LDPC without 4-loops can achieve 35dB coding gain and BER is lower than 10-5 if SNR is 6dB. Another conclusion worth attention is when the code length increases, the impact of 4-loops is getting weak. The reason of this phenomenon is for the same statistical density distribution, longer code length means lower error probability.
