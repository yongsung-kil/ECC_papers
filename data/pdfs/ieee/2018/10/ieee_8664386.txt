Further Results on the Error Correction Capability of Irregular LDPC Codes under the Gallager A Algorithm

Masanori Hirotomo Dept. of Information Science
Saga University Saga, 840–8502 Japan Email: hirotomo@cc.saga-u.ac.jp

Hiroto Tamiya Security Research Laboratories
NEC Corporation Kanagawa, 211–8666 Japan Email: h-tamiya@ay.jp.nec.com

Masakatu Morii Dept. of Electrical and Electronic Engineering
Kobe University Kobe, 657–8501 Japan Email: mmorii@kobe-u.ac.jp

Abstract—In this paper, we investigate the error correction capability of irregular LDPC codes. We have shown relationships between the decoding failure and the structures of the Tanner graph of irregular LDPC codes under the Gallager A algorithm. In this analysis, we focused on the structures of connecting degree-two variable nodes, called 2-cycle. In this paper, we present new structures of the subgraph which induces the decoding failure of irregular LDPC codes under the Gallager A algorithm. Furthermore, we analyze relationships between the newly found structures of subgraph and the decoding failure of irregular LDPC codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are linear codes defined by sparse parity-check matrix [1]. LDPC codes are often represented by bipartite graphs which are called as Tanner graphs. Iterative decoding algorithms for LDPC codes perform message passing on the Tanner graph. The error correction capability depends on the structural properties of the Tanner graph. The decoding failures are characterized by subgraphs of the Tanner graph, termed stopping set [2], trapping set [3]. However, since trapping sets inducing the decoding failure depend on decoding algorithms, it is difficult to show theoretically the relationship between trapping sets and the decoding failure of belief propagation (BP)-based iterative decoders.
To analyze the decoding failure of iterative decoders, the error correction capability of column-weight-three LDPC codes has been reported in [4] and [5]. In the analysis, it focuses on the relation between correctable error patterns and fixed sets of variable nodes under the Gallager A algorithm. Also, the trapping set ontology is shown in [6]. It is a database of trapping sets that summarizes the topological relations among trapping sets. Furthermore, as extensions from regular LDPC codes to irregular LDPC codes, characterizations of trapping sets of variable-regular LDPC codes are shown in [7], [8] and [9]. Also, these characterizations are generalized to irregular LDPC codes [10].
Irregular LDPC codes show better error performance than regular LDPC, but show error floor at the high SN region. So, it is important to analyze the error correction capability of irregular LDPC codes. Although they focused on trapping set

in the analysis of [7], [8], [9], [10], variable nodes of degree 2 in irregular LDPC codes are important factors for inducing the decoding failures of iterative decoding algorithms. In [11], we have shown the error correction capability of irregular LDPC codes under the Gallager A algorithm. We focused on the message passing on the degree-2 variable nodes, called 2cycle. Then, we showed relationships between the decoding failure of the Gallager A algorithm and 2-cycle of irregular LDPC codes.
In this paper, we show further results on relationships between 2-cycle and decoding failure of irregular LDPC codes under the Gallager A algorithm. Although we considered that errors occur in 2-cycles in [11], we theoretically analyze behaviors of message passing when errors occur in variable nodes connecting to 2-cycles. Then, we obtain several configurations consisting of 2-cycle and some variable and check nodes which induce the decoding failure of the Gallager A algorithm. Using these subgraphs, we can estimate the frame error rate of irregular LDPC codes which is much closer the simulation results than the estimates by [11].
II. GALLAGER A ALGORITHM
Gallager A algorithm is a binary message passing algorithm for decoding LDPC codes over the binary symmetric channel (BSC). In the Gallager A algorithm, the value of messages passed between variable nodes and check nodes is 0 or 1. Without loss of generality, we assume that the all zero codeword is transmitted. Let rv be the value of received word corresponding to the variable node v. Let wl(v, c) denote the message passed by a variable node v to its neighboring check node c at lth iteration. Let wl(v, c) denote the message passed by a check node c to its neighboring variable node v at lth iteration. The detailed procedure of the Gallager A algorithm is referred in [4] and [5].

V. FRAME ERROR RATE ESTIMATED BY CONFIGURATIONS BASED ON 2-CYCLE
In this section, we compare the number of sets of variable nodes found by Theorem 1 with that found by Figs. 2–4.
Method 1 implies a searching algorithm for 2-cycles of Theorem 1 in a given irregular LDPC code. Method 2 implies a searching algorithm for subgraphs of Figs. 2–4 in a given irregular LDPC code.
In the method 2, we search the sets of variable nodes inducing the decoding failure by focusing on Figs. 2–4. Although we derived Theorems 2–4 under the assumption that the messages 1 do not come from the outside of subgraphs of Figs. 2–4, the messages 1 may come from the outside of subgraphs via the connection of whole Tanner graph. Hence,

we remove the sets of variable nodes inducing the decoding success from the sets of variable nodes found by the method 2 by analyzing the message passing around the subgraphs of Figs. 2–4.
Method 3 determines that the sets of variable nodes causes the decoding success if the messages 1 comes from the outside of subgraphs of Figs. 2–4. In the method 3, we consider the message passing in a structure like a tree. In this structure, when we found the same node at a certain depth, we deal with these nodes as one node. The path is expanded from the root of a bad variable node based on the Gallager A algorithm, and the child is add when the message 1 passes. A bad variable node has check nodes as its children if it has at least one parent. Also, a good node has check nodes as its children if it has its parents more than deg v − 1. Then, we determine whether a check node has variable nodes as its children that the number of its parents to be odd or even. We omit the description of detailed procedure of methods 1, 2 and 3 because of space limitations of this paper.
We apply the methods 1, 2 and 3 to three irregular LDPC codes. Two codes are defined by PEGirReg252x504 and PEGirReg504x1008 [14]. The other is defined by the Tanner graph of 1002 variable nodes and 501 check nodes whose degree distribution is optimized by λ(x) = 0.23802x+0.20997x2 +0.03492x3 +0.12015x4 +0.01587x6 + 0.00480x13 + 0.37627x14, ρ(x) = 0.98013x7 + 0.01987x8 [13]. VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we have shown new relationships between decoding failures and subgraphs including 2-cycles of irregular LDPC codes under the Gallager A algorithm. Using these subgraphs, we can estimate the frame error rate of irregular LDPC codes which is much closer the simulation results than the estimates by [11]. To future work, we will generalize these results to other iterative decoding algorithms.
