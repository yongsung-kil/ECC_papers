Convergence Behavior of LDPC Decoding and Application to Early Termination
Janik Frenzel∗, Stefan Mu¨ller-Weinfurtner∗, Johannes B. Huber†, Ralf R. Mu¨ller† ∗Intel Deutschland GmbH, Nuremberg, Germany
†Friedrich-Alexander-Universita¨t Erlangen-Nu¨rnberg, Germany

Abstract—The iterative algorithms employed to decode Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes are not guaranteed to converge to a valid code word for every receive word. The goal of Early Termination (ET) is to prevent the waste of resources in such cases. In this paper, we conduct an analysis of the suitability of ET criteria by tracking the evolution of parameters available from decoder-internal states. We find that the commonly selected parameters, e.g., the mean Log-Likelihood Ratio (LLR) magnitude or the fraction of satisfied parity checks, result in similar probabilities of detecting non-converging receive words. We propose a novel criterion for Early Give-Up (EGU) and evaluate its performance using a cost metric based on the retransmission of individual code blocks initiated by higher-layer protocols.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes are usually decoded in an iterative manner [1]. Such algorithms are suitable for practical implementations as they offer low complexity and often converge quickly [2]. There is, however, no guarantee that an iterative decoder finds a valid code word (regardless whether correct or not), e.g., because the decoder gets stuck in a local minimum or ends up oscillating and never converges [3].
Early Termination (ET) comprises two strategies: Early Success (ES) and Early Give-Up (EGU). Mitigating the effects of “bad” receive words as described above is the purpose of EGU. By contrast, ES stops the decoder if it has converged to a valid code word. We consider ES solved for 5G New Radio (NR) since the code’s inherent parity checks and the code-block Cyclic Redundancy Check (CRC) are sufficient ES indicators [4]. Whereas EGU is most promising for achieving complexity reduction in the region of low Signal-toNoise Ratio (SNR), ES is more useful in the region of high SNR. Nonetheless, good ET criteria should be so reliable that they can operate over a wide SNR range.
One of the earliest proposals for an EGU criterion is presented in [5]. The authors observe that the sum of Log-Likelihood Ratio (LLR) magnitudes exceeds an SNR-dependent threshold after some iterations if the decoder converges. Other criteria leveraging the same parameter are presented in [6] and [7] among others. Criteria based on the number of satisfied (or unsatisfied) parity checks are presented in [8]–[10]. Finally, the number of sign flips in the hard decision from one

iteration to the next is another option for formulating an EGU criterion [11]. Still, most criteria use inelegant parameters and often require storing information from previous iterations, e.g., the last vector of parity checks.
An unanswered question is whether the choice of the EGU parameter has an impact on the decision performance and what the limits on detecting non-promising receive words are. In this paper, we extend the convergence analysis used in [5]–[8], which is an application of Density Evolution [12]. In essence, the statistics for a parameter are tracked for converging and non-converging receive words individually. Whereas the authors of [5]– [8] use this type of analysis mostly to visualize the convergence behavior or to justify their EGU criterion, we apply means from the field of detection theory to judge the general suitability of criteria for EGU.
Assessing the performance of EGU rules needs to be more sophisticated than simply comparing the resulting Block-Error Rate (BLER) and Average Number of Iterations (ANI). In lieu thereof, we introduce a cost metric which incorporates potential retransmissions initiated by higher-layer protocols. Additionally, we propose an easyto-use EGU criterion with good error-floor performance.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section II gives an introduction into iterative decoding and the fundamentals of decision theory as well as the notation used therein. The convergence analysis of the decoding process is presented in Section III. Consequently, our novel EGU criterion and the cost metric for evaluation are presented in Section IV. The paper concludes in Section V.

The convergence behavior of iterative decoding may be predicted by a parameter V . In the following, we denote one realization of V by v and analyze several variants. For compact notation, we use the L1-norm as well as the L0-“norm” which counts the number of nonzero elements in a vector. We define for i = 0 . . . n − 1: Mean LLR Magnitude v0 = L 1/n Fraction of Satisfied CNs v1 = 1 − s 0/(n − k) Fraction of Large LLRs v2 = |{Li : |Li| > θ}|/n We want to assert a probability of P ≥ 0.99 for a correct hard decision of one symbol and hence select θ = 5, corresponding to P ≈ 0.9933 when the natural logarithm is chosen in (2).
EGU works as follows: From one of the parameters above, the parity-check result without EGU, Σ, is estimated by Σˆ . The estimate is determined by comparing v against a threshold. Σˆ controls the decoding, i.e., Σˆ = 1 signals a stop. Estimation errors, i.e., Σˆ = Σ, either lead to undesired retransmissions or unnecessary iterations.
We evaluate the decision performance by analyzing the pdfs p0(v) and p1(v), cf. (11) and (12). More specifically, we estimate p0(v) and p1(v) by histograms in every iteration to trace the decoding convergence.
The conditional means E{V | Σ} provide a simple way of evaluating the histograms. The larger their difference, the better the decision performance. However, more meaningful conclusions can be drawn when the approximations of p0(v) and p1(v) are considered in their entirety, as is the case with AROC, cf. (15).

IV. EVALUATION OF EARLY-TERMINATION PERFORMANCE
We use our findings from Section III to present a new EGU criterion and compare it against existing ones.
A. Criteria for Early Give-Up For comparison, we select two criteria from literature.
In the following, let (·)(l) denote the realization of a variable in the l-th iteration. On the one hand, we use the criterion based on V0 from [17], labeled C0: Definition. C0: Stop iterating if v0(l) < v0(l−1) for more than T (not necessarily consecutive) iterations.
On the other hand, we use the criterion from [9] (which is based on V1) and call it C1: Definition. C1: Stop iterating if v1(l) < v1,min at l = lmin or if v1(l) ≤ v1(l−1) for T consecutive iterations.
These two criteria are unsatisfying because our convergence analysis suggests that the realization of V in each iteration by itself is sufficient. Furthermore, we choose to build our new criterion on V2 to verify the results from the histogram analysis and show that V2 is a suitable parameter for EGU, too. Nonetheless, we would like to stress that our criterion also works well with other parameters. We propose criterion C2 where v2(l) is implicitly defined by a third parameter θ: Definition. C2: Stop iterating if v2(l) < v2,min for any l ≥ lmin.

The major advantage of C2 is it being memoryless. With the cost metric proposed in the next section, we find the parameters listed in Table I to be optimal for our simulation setup described in Section III-B.
The minimal number of iterations used in C1 and C2 can be derived from the histogram analysis. If lmin is too small, the decision is unreliable and produces many false positives. Conversely, resources are wasted on nonconverging receive words if lmin is too large. For best results, lmin is selected such that AROC shows a local maximum (cf. Fig. 5). Although the exact location of the maximum depends on the SNR, the criteria perform well with a fixed value.
B. Means of Evaluation
Traditionally, LDPC-decoding performance is measured in terms of the resulting BLER. In addition, we consider the Average Number of Iterations (ANI). The two metrics are inversely related: Permitting more iterations usually lowers the BLER [7]. In other words, every achievable point in the BLER-ANI-space is Paretooptimal. Hence, we need a metric which results in a unique best solution to fully evaluate EGU.
In literature, one such quantity is the Bit-Error Rate (BER) Performance Loss Ratio (BPLR) which relates the increase in BER against the decrease in ANI and averages the result over a range of SNR values [11]. We find two things deficient about this criterion: Firstly, the BLER is a better performance indicator than the BER since a vanishing BER does not necessarily imply a vanishing BLER [18]. Secondly, the BPLR ignores the fact that, in practice, a failed decoding attempt is followed by a retransmission of the same code block using a Hybrid Automated Repeat reQuest (HARQ) Type-II process, leading to another attempt with more information. On the contrary, a full HARQ environment is deployed in [9]. Simulating a full HARQ process, however, is impractical for an efficient evaluation.

V. CONCLUSION EGU checks are essential components of practical iterative decoders. Although the convergence of iterative decoding can be measured by very different parameters like the mean LLR magnitude or the number of satisfied parity checks, the parameters’ capabilities of detecting non-promising receive words are almost identical. Thus, comparing EGU criteria in fact means comparing decision rules. Our proposed rule offers a low error floor, making it suitable for an always-on operating mode. The rule’s good performance is affirmed by the RAC metric. What is more, the RAC metric helps to find the optimal parameter set and might be useful for optimizing other components of an iterative decoder which face a similar trade-off between ANI and error rate. Regardless, all EGU criteria compared in this paper are static and require careful optimization for each code. An ideal criterion yet to be found would dynamically adjust its decision rule without preceding parameter optimization.
