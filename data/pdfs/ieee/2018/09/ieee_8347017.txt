Efficient Low-Complexity Phase Noise Resistant Iterative Joint Phase Estimation and Decoding Algorithm
Andrey Kreimer and Dan Raphaeli , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract— In this paper, we present a new low-complexity iterative joint phase estimation and decoding algorithm which can be applied to low-density-parity-check (or turbo) codes for channels affected by strong phase noise. The algorithm exhibits a very good performance and a very low complexity even in strong phase noise, high code rate, and high-order constellations. The proposed algorithm works by first obtaining a preliminary pilots-aided phase estimation. Next, the expressions for the belief propagation messages are approximated around that reference and reduced to a Gaussian canonical form. This results in a simple closed form of a recursive update. Simulations results for phase-shift-keying modulation of size 8 and quadrature-amplitude modulation of size 64 are presented and compared with other algorithms in the literature.
Index Terms— Approximation methods, iterative methods, message passing, phase noise.
I. INTRODUCTION
T HE subject of iterative decoding algorithms for additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels affected by carrier phase noise has attracted extensive study in recent years due to its importance in many applications using high carrier frequency, mainly satellite communications. Recent works have proposed factor graphs (FG) based algorithms, which are naturally integrated into a low-density-parity-check (LDPC) code decoder (or turbo decoder), leading to a very high performance. In [1], a general framework of factor graphs and the sum-product algorithm (SPA) is presented. The exact SPA equations for iterative decoding of a sequence of coded symbols transmitted over an AWGN channel and affected by carrier phase noise are shown in [2]. These equations are a result of SPA algorithm applied to the FG representing the joint a posteriori probability density function (PDF) of the information bits and the random channel phase noise process, given the received signal.

The DP-BCJR is used as the benchmark algorithm in this and the other mentioned papers [2], [6], [7]. Due to the high complexity of the DP-BCJR, several works propose low complexity approximations to the SPA equations. These approximations are obtained by following the canonical distribution approach suggested in [8], i.e., the messages propagated on the FG by the SPA take values belonging to a certain parametric family of distribution functions, such that the approximated SPA reduces to updating and propagating the function parameters.
Colavolpe et al. [2] have proposed a practical, computationally efficient algorithm using Tikhonov canonical distribution, achieved by applying suitable approximations to the SPA equations. This method, though usually shows good performance/complexity tradeoff, tends to show an error floor with high rate codes. The method was extended to a case of uncompensated frequency noise presence in [9]. Shayovitz and Raphaeli [6] have developed another algorithm by approximating the SPA messages as Tikhonov mixtures. By doing so, they achieved a very close performance to the DP-BCJR. However, good results for high order constellations require much higher complexity and were not demonstrated. In [10], the Tikhonov algorithm described in [2] is modified for a parallel implementation, by treating pilots-separated blocks of received symbols independently, making it more suitable for 2nd-generation satellite digital video broadcasting (DVB-S2) receivers. In [7], a different approach for the factor graph construction is proposed, without explicit representation of the channel parameters, since they are a priori averaged out. The resulting algorithms are suited for parallel implementation of the detector/decoder. The performance of the best algorithm proposed in that paper, for a case of time-varying phase noise, was slightly worse than the Tikhonov [2] algorithm under the tested conditions. A phase synchronization method based on expectation maximization (EM) algorithm, which is embedded into the LDPC iterative decoding process, is proposed in [11] for constant phase noise model. Another FG-based algorithm is proposed in [12] for iterative decoding of turbo-like codes in the presence of unknown channel parameters, achieving a polynomial complexity. By doing so they achieved similar performance to the Tikhonov method, with a reduced computational complexity. In [14], variational message passing is employed for the factor nodes in the graph that represent the nonlinear model due to the presence of phase noise. This method does not require the knowledge of the AWGN variance and achieves performance which is close to that of Tikhonov algorithm, with a reduced complexity.
To increase the transmission rates, decoding algorithms which perform well for high-rate codes and/or high-order constellations are essential. In [15], a separate MAP synchronization and iterative decoding algorithm is proposed, suitable for high-order modulations. The explicit initial coarse phase estimate is refined in each iteration of the decoding process using the a posteriori probabilities of the information symbols from the decoder. The phase noise estimates are used to compensate the received samples which are served as an input to the decoder. It was shown that it achieves slightly worse performance than the joint decoding algorithm in [9] under the tested scenario.
In this paper, we propose a new low complexity iterative joint phase estimation and decoding algorithm, also derived from approximating the SPA framework laid out in [2], which shows very good performance in all code rates and constellation sizes. First, we obtain an explicit phase reference using pilots interleaved in the data or using non-data aided methods. Next, we create a linear approximation of the phase exponent around the reference phase. Using this approximation, we derive a Gaussian canonical form (GCF) model and its recursive equations for joint phase estimation and decoding. As opposed to other algorithms that use an external phase estimation like [15], the phase estimation is not used for phase compensation and there is no refinement over the iterations.
The derivation of the proposed algorithm uses a similar approximation to the one used in the phase-locked-loop (PLL) theory. However, there is a profound difference. In the PLL case, the phase reference around which the linear approximation is made is the phase estimate of the previous symbol, which makes the algorithm recursive and prone to cycle slips or loss of lock. By separating the phase reference process from the PDF calculation process, the algorithm is much more resilient. In fact, when the mean of the phase estimate of the previous symbol is used as a phase reference in our proposed algorithm, and in high SNR conditions, we show that the proposed algorithm is reduced to a decision directed loop (DLL). This reduction is shown just for better understanding the algorithm since this choice is not recommended and is shown to give very poor results compared to the results obtained by using an external phase reference obtained from pilots or non-data aided methods.
The computational complexity of our suggested algorithm is evaluated and compared to other known algorithms from literature. Bit-error rate (BER) performance results are presented by computer simulation, taking into account the widely used Wiener phase noise model [2], [4]–[7], [9], [13]–[18].

The rest of this paper is arranged as follows. Section II describes the system model and the framework for the problem. Section III contains the derivation of the proposed algorithm in detail. Several reference phase acquisition methods which are used along with the proposed algorithm are covered in Section IV. Section V describes the difference between the proposed algorithm and a decision-directed-loop. Section VI lists the complexity evaluation for several versions of our algorithm and describes additional methods which can be used to achieve further simplification of it. Finally, numerical results obtained in terms of computer simulations are shown in Section VII, and Section VIII summarizes the conclusions.

IV. REFERENCE PHASE ACQUISITION METHODS The reference phase θˆk required for the algorithm described in this paper can be obtained by various ways. In this paper we have considered some of the best non-data aided and pilotsaided phase estimation methods from literature: DecisionDirected Loop, Viterbi and Viterbi [21] estimator and finite-lag Wiener filter [17]. Updating the phase reference based on the soft data obtained during the decoding process was analyzed. We have also analyzed the option of using the resulting mean μk−1 of the message pf (θk−1) from the previous symbol as θˆk. This is covered in Section V. In this section we will list the phase estimation methods that we have tested in our research.

VI. COMPLEXITY EVALUATION AND WAYS TO FURTHER REDUCE IT
The computational complexity of the algorithm described in this paper in terms of additions/multiplications of real numbers and lookup table (LUT) accesses for the nonlinear functions (e.g., evaluation of Gaussian PDF at specific points) is 42M + 8 real-number additions and multiplications and 4M LUT accesses per coded symbol per decoder iteration. For M -PSK modulations (where all symbols are of equal power), it is 30M + 19 additions and multiplications and 4M LUT accesses.
The complexity imposed by the preliminary phase estimation is not included in the calculations above. However, this overhead is negligible. If a finite-lag Wiener filter for pilots is used followed by linear interpolation (which is the preferable method, as it will be shown in Section VII), then the overhead is as follows. For each pilot, there are at most 2N/P − 1 additions and multiplications (multiplication by filter coefficients and summation) and a single LUT access for the argument extraction. For the linear interpolation, for each pilot symbol there are 2 operations to calculate the increment value; for every data symbol there is a single addition of this increment. Therefore, the complexity of the finite-lag Wiener filter phase estimation is 1 + 2N/P 2 + LU T /P operations per symbol. Since the phase reference remains constant over the iterations, this complexity should be also divided by the number of iterations.

According to [2], for M -PSK modulations, the computational complexity of the DP-BCJR algorithm (which is considered as the optimal algorithm for sufficient number of quantization levels L) is 13M L+10QL−9L−3M operations and 3M L + 2QL − 3L − M LUT accesses, where Q is an integer satisfying Q < L. The complexity of the “Tikhonov mixture” [6] algorithm is not provided as exact expressions in terms of operations and LUT accesses, but its complexity is between the “Tikhonov” algorithm and the DP-BCJR. The proposed algorithm in this paper (the Max-Alpha version) has even lower computational demands than the mentioned algorithms. VII. SIMULATION RESULTS
Several computer simulations were performed to test the performance of our algorithm in terms of BER as a function of Eb/N0 (Eb being the signal energy per information bit and N0 is the one sided noise power spectral density σ2). Two different LDPC codes were tested: code A with parity check matrix of size 400x800 (code rate R = 0.5) and code B with parity check matrix of size 737x4095 (R = 0.82 LDPC code 4095.737.3.101 from [26]). Modulations that were examined were 8-PSK and 64-QAM. 8PSK was simulated both with code A and B. For 64-QAM, only code B was tested. The simulations were carried out for the phase noise values of σΔ = 0.05 and 0.1 rad/symbol, and different values of pilot spacing P .
For 8PSK, DP-BCJR with L = 8M states, where M is the constellation size, was the reference algorithm that was used for comparing our algorithm performance to. For 64-QAM, the “known phase” case was used as a reference, since DP-BCJR was too complex to simulate. The number of iterations in the decoding process was limited to 30. For each value of Eb/N0, 1,000,000 transmitted blocks were simulated, unless the number of failed blocks has reached 100. “Tikhonov” algorithm [2] performance was also included in the simulations. For 8PSK, “Tikhonov mixture” [6] algorithm results were also provided - denoted by “KLCluster”. Table I lists the different notations used in this section and their meaning.

B. Comparison of Reference Phase Acquisition Methods
Here, we will present a comparison of the different preliminary phase estimation methods mentioned in Section IV. The comparison is in terms of estimation MSE, achieved using simulations, and in terms of BER performance when used by our algorithm. The MSE results are shown in Fig. 5 for 8PSK, σΔ = 0.1rad and P = 20. The BER performance of our algorithm with reference phase achieved by different methods is shown in Fig. 6. For the Wiener filter phase estimator based on data symbols, we used the result of pilots-only Wiener filter for ambiguity resolution. We found that this way works better than taking the nearest pilots or the pilots that fall into a predefined region around the estimated point and using their averaged phase for disambiguation. For the case of finite-lag Wiener filter for data symbols, the parameter m (the power the absolute value is raised to) was set to 2. The same value of m = 2 was also used for V&V algorithm.
We found that a very good performance is achieved if a finite-lag Wiener filter is applied to the pilot symbols only, following an interpolation (zero padding and low-pass filtering) and it outperforms all other methods of phase reference acquisition that we have tested. The interpolation is required because the phase reference θˆk is needed for all the symbols: pilots and data. In fact, we found that a simple linear interpolation can be used without performance loss. It can be seen that using μk−1 as the phase reference for the symbol at index k (which is one of the conditions for the algorithm to become a DDL) compromises the performance. Another conclusion from Fig. 6 is that employing the method described in Section IV-C, i.e., estimating the phase from pilots only on first iteration, and afterwards updating the phase estimation on each iteration of the decoding process using soft decisions for all symbols, does not improve the results for higher values of Eb/N0. C. BER Performance Compared to Other Algorithms
The results of BER performance of our algorithm compared to existing algorithms from the literature are shown in Fig. 8a-8c for 8PSK and 64-QAM. The GCF algorithm outperforms Tikh for both constellations. The gap between the two increases with the code rate R. Note that the Tikh algorithm suffers from an error floor in both cases: 64-QAM and 8PSK with R = 0.82, whereas the GCF algorithm is immune from such a problem. The gap between the full GCF algorithm and the approximated ones is less significant for the bigger constellation. We see negligible improvement when using a priori vector rather than a priori constant variances σk2 and νk2, but we see a small ∼ 0.2 − 0.4dB improvement if we do calculate them each symbol.
Pilots spacing influence on the performance of the preliminary phase estimation was studied. As expected, the performance of the algorithm degrades as the pilots spacing is increased, since the quality of the reference phase deteriorates. This is because there are fewer pilots to extract the phase from in case the pilots solely are used in the estimation, or the ambiguity resolution by pilots is harmed in case of estimation techniques based on data symbols. However, we learned that the superiority of the Wiener-filter phase estimation, based on pilots only, over other methods that we have tested is retained as the pilots spacing is increased. BER-performance simulation results supporting this are shown in Fig. 8d. Results for Tikh algorithm are also shown in this figure, to illustrate its dependence on pilots spacing.
