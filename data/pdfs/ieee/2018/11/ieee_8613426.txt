Abstract—In this work, we consider type-II quasi-cyclic LDPC codes with girth eight from a Sidon sequence. We first derive the necessary and sufficient conditions guaranteeing girth-eight type-II QC-LDPC codes. By combining these conditions and the concept of a Sidon sequence, two classes of type-II codes are subsequently proposed with girth up to eight. We discuss the distance upper bounds of the two classes of codes and show that the second class provides a larger distance upper bound. In particular, to the best of our knowledge, the second class we proposed yields the first algebraic construction for girtheight type-II codes with rates larger than a half and distance upper bounds exceeding twelve. Via simulations, we show that the girth-eight type-II codes from the second class significantly outperform the existing CDF-based girth-eight type-II codes, and that they perform better than or almost identically to the randomly generated girth-eight quadr. congr. codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
Quasi-cyclic (QC) low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have recently attracted increasing attention as they facilitate encoder/decoder implementations [1][2][3][4][5]. The paritycheck matrix (PCM) of a QC-LDPC code is an N × M array of P × P circulants. A circulant may be a zero matrix (ZM), a circulant permutation matrix (CPM), or a summation of w (2 ≤ w ≤ P ) distinct CPMs. The weights of the three types of circulants are 0, 1 and w, respectively. A PCM composed of CPMs is associated with a classical QCLDPC code, that composed of CPMs and ZMs a type-I code, and that with weight-2 circulants but without weightw′ (w′ ≥ 3) ones a type-II code. Compared with classical QC-LDPC codes, type-I/II codes commonly permit of larger upper bounds on minimum distance [5], which hopefully result in larger minimum distances for well-designed codes. On the other hand, as girth is an effective indicator to ensure a lower bound on minimum distance, it is generally desired to be as large as possible for QC-LDPC codes [1][5]. For this reason, circulants with weight three or more are not considered for the construction of QC-LDPC codes with girth above six, as it inevitably leads to cycles of length six (6-cycles) [6].
Type-II QC-LDPC codes with girth six have been proposed in [7][8][9][10]. For the direction of type-II codes with girth eight, three proposals [11][12][13] have been presented; however, none of them guarantees a girth eight due to the omission of some patterns of 6-cycles (see Section II.B for details). Besides, the method in [13] is limited to half rate, the one in

[11] only yields codes with small distances (upper-bounded by 12 for rates larger than a half), and that in [12] only provides half-rate codes with distances upper-bounded by 24.
From a special integer sequence which is referred to as a Sidon sequence [14], some families of classical/type-I QCLDPC codes with girth at least eight have been proposed in our earlier works [3][4]. In this paper, a Sidon sequence is employed to construct type-II QC-LDPC codes with girth eight. The rates of the novel type-II codes are not limited to a half, and the distances of them not upper-bounded by either 12 (for rates larger than a half) or 24 (for the half-rate case).
II. NECESSARY AND SUFFICIENT CONDITION
Let a, b, c, d, f and g be integers over {1, 2}, and let x, y, and z be integers over {0, 1} satisfying x + y + z ≤ 1. Let i, j and k be distinct integers such that 0 ≤ i, j, k ≤ N − 1, and r, s and t be distinct integers such that 0 ≤ r, s, t ≤ M − 1. The exponent matrix of a type-II QC-LDPC code can be expressed as E = [{e1(i, r), e2(i, r)}]N×M , where e1(i, r), e2(i, r) ∈ {0, 1, · · · , P − 1, ∞}. The entries {e1(i, r), ∞} and {∞, e2(i, r)} are abbreviated to {e1(i, r)} and {e2(i, r)}, respectively. The PCM of a type-II code can be simply obtained by E and P , via an exponent-coupling procedure [10]. Note that all cycle-governing equations in this section are restricted to circulants with non-zero weights.
A. Cycle of length four
The necessary and sufficient conditions for a 4-cycle to exist in one or more circulants with weight at most 2 have been presented in [8]. There are in total three cases as follows. (A1) a 4-cycle (Fig.1a) exists in a single weight-2 circulant if and only if (iff) 2[e1(i, r) − e2(i, r)] = 0 (mod P ). (A2) there is a 4-cycle (Fig.1b) in two weight-2 circulants iff e1(i, r) − e2(i, r) = ±[e1(i, s) − e2(i, s)] (mod P ). The equality for the transpose case of (A2) is similar to this equality, and hence omitted. (A3) a 4-cycle (Fig.1c) exists in four circulants (with weight 1 or 2) iff ea(i, r) − eb(j, r) + ec(j, s) − ed(i, s) = 0 (mod P ).
It should be pointed out that the above equations governing 4-cycles have also been equivalently proposed in [5]. In contrast to 4-cycles, the necessary and sufficient conditions for 6-cycles have not been thoroughly investigated so far.

B. Cycle of length six
In this section, the necessary and sufficient conditions for a 6-cycle to exist in one or more circulants with weight at most 2 are derived on the basis of Equ. (4) in [1]. There are eight cases in total (excluding the three transpose cases shown later), as is clear from the classification below. (B1) a 6-cycle (Fig.1d) exists in a single weight-2 circulant iff 3[e1(i, r) − e2(i, r)] = 0 (mod P ). (B2) there is a 6-cycle (Fig.1e) in two weight-2 circulants in a row iff 2[e1(i, r) − e2(i, r)] = ±[e1(i, s) − e2(i, s)] (mod P ).
There are three cases of 6-cycles in three circulants (all weight-2, or two weight-2 plus one weight-1). (B3a) there exists a 6-cycle (Fig.1f) iff (−1)x[e1(i, r) − e2(i, r)]+(−1)y[e1(i, s)−e2(i, s)]+(−1)z[e1(i, t)−e2(i, t)] = 0 (mod P ).
Note that there are two patterns of 6-cycles in Fig.1g. (B3b) for the first case, the two ’1’s (black dots) in the right upper square are within one CPM, and hence the two associated exponents cancel out each other. Therefore, such a 6-cycle exists iff e1(i, r) − e2(i, r) = ±[e1(j, s) − e2(j, s)] (mod P ). (B3c) for the second case, the two ’1’s in the right upper square are within two CPMs. As a result, a 6-cycle exists iff (−1)x[e1(i, r) − e2(i, r)] + (−1)y[e1(i, s) − e2(i, s)] + (−1)z[e1(j, s) − e2(j, s)] = 0 (mod P ).
For 6-cycles in four, five or six circulants, there is only one pattern for each case. (B4) there is a 6-cycle (Fig.1h) in four circulants (with at least one weight-2 circulant) iff ea(i, r) − eb(j, r) + ec(j, s) − 3ed(i, s) + e1(i, s) + e2(i, s) = 0 (mod P ). (B5) a 6-cycle (Fig. 1i) exists in five circulants (the unpaired square being a weight-2 circulant) iff ea(i, r) − eb(j, r) + ec(j, s) − ed(i, s) = ±[e1(i, t) − e2(i, t)] (mod P ). (B6) there exists a 6-cycle (Fig.1j) in six circulants iff ea(i, r) − eb(k, r) + ec(k, t) − ed(j, t) + ef (j, s) − eg(i, s) = 0 (mod P ).
The equalities for the transpose cases of (B2), (B3a) and (B5) are similar to their respective equalities, and hence not listed here for simplicity.
Theorem 1: The girth of a type-II QC-LDPC code is at least eight, iff all the conditions of type A and type B (including four transpose cases) are satisfied.
Remark 1: In [11], 6-cycles in Fig.1d/e/f/h/i are overlooked. In [12], 6-cycles in Fig.1d is noticed, and two conditions which are equivalent to the equation in case B1 are proposed to avoid

such cycles; however, 6-cycles in Fig.1e/h/i are neglected, despite the nonexistence of 6-cycles in Fig.1f/g/j owing to the structure (Fig.1, [12]). In [13], although 6-cycles in Fig.1f/g/j cannot exist due to the structure (Fig.1, [13]), 6-cycles in Fig. 1d/e/h/i are neglected. Therefore, none of the three methods suffices for a type-II QC-LDPC code with girth eight.

Definition 1 [3][14]: A Sidon sequence over ZP , is a sequence of C integers {s0, s1, · · · , sC−1} with the property that the sums si + sj(i ≤ j) (mod P ) are distinct.
Throughout this paper, let S be a Sidon sequence over ZP (P even), and ai(k), b(ik) ∈ S for 0 ≤ k ≤ L−1, 0 ≤ i ≤ N −1.
Theorem 2: The girth of PCM from Method I is eight if the five conditions are all satisfied: (i) N ≥ 4; (ii) a(ik) − bi(k) is odd; (iii) a(ik1), b(ik1), a(ik2) and b(ik2) are distinct, 0 ≤ k1 < k2 ≤ L − 1; (iv) |{a(ik), b(ik)} ∩ {am (ko)d(i+1,N), b(mko)d(i+1,N)}| ≤ 1; (v) ai(k1), bi(k1), a(mko2d)(i+1,N) and bm (ko2d)(i+1,N) are distinct, 0 ≤ k1 < k2 ≤ L − 1.
Proof : First, consider 4-cycles. Condition (A1): It is impossible, as a(ik) and b(ik) are two different elements in S. Condition (A2): It is impossible, since ai(k1), bi(k1), a(ik2) and bi(k2) are elements in S satisfying (iii). Besides, there exists no transpose of (A2) due to the structure of the exponent matrix in Method I. Condition (A3): As N > 2, there is no such 4-cycles within a single E(k); such cycles cannot exist within two blocks, E(k1) and E(k2), owing to (iii).
Next, consider 6-cycles. Condition (B1): As 3[a(ik)−bi(k)] ≠ 0 (mod P ) (LHS is odd but P even), it is impossible. Condition (B2): Similarly, as 2[ai(k1) − bi(k1)] ± [a(ik2) − bi(k2)] is odd but P even, Condition (B2) cannot hold. Condition (B3a): It is impossible as the summation of any three odd differences cannot be a multiple of an even P . Condition (B3b) cannot hold owing to (iv) and (v). Condition (B3c) is impossible due to the structure of Method I. Condition (B4) is impossible, as N > 2 and ai(k1), bi(k1), ai(k2) and bi(k2) are elements in S. Condition (B5): Such 6-cycles may only appear within two or three blocks. There are two subcases. (Subcase1): three weight-2 circulants are in the same row of three blocks; however, subcase1 is impossible due to (iii). (Subcase2): two weight-2 circulants are in the same row and the other one in another row, and the three weight-2 circulants are located in either two or three blocks; however, neither of them exist, owing to (iv) and (v). Condition (B6) is impossible as N ≥ 4.
Finally, the four transpose patterns (one for 4-cycles and three for 6-cycles) cannot exist due to the structure. Consequently, the girth is at least eight. On the other hand, as there always exist 8-cycles in any two weight-2 circulants (located in a row), the girth is upper-bounded by eight. Q.E.D.

Definition 2: If {1 ≤ α0 < · · · < αL−1 ≤ N − 1} satisfies that mod(αi + αj, N ) ̸= 0 for any pair (i, j) and mod(αi + αj + αk, N ) ̸= 0 for any triple (i, j, k), then it is called a TTSF sequence.

VI. CONCLUSION
In this work, the necessary and sufficient conditions to ensure a girth at least eight have been derived for type-II QC-

LDPC codes. From a Sidon sequence, two methods have been proposed for constructing type-II QC-LDPC codes with girth up to eight. Compared with the existing approaches for girtheight type-II codes, the novel codes from the second method have flexible rates and larger distance upper bounds.
