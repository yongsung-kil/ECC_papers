Efficient Coding Architectures for Reed–Solomon and Low-Density Parity-Check Decoders for Magnetic and Other Data Storage Systems

Arijit Mondal , Satyannarayana Thatimattala, Vamshi Krishna Yalamaddi, and Shayan Srinivasa Garani
Department of Electronic Systems Engineering, Indian Institute of Science, Bengaluru 560012, India
High-performance error correction codes are used in high-density data storage devices to overcome noise and channel impairments. In this paper, we develop novel and efficient decoding architectures for Reed–Solomon (RS) and low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes that are used in almost all data storage devices. First, we present a high-speed low-latency hard-decision-based pipelined RS decoder architecture that computes the error locator polynomial in exactly 2t clock cycles without parallelism. The RS decoder is a two-stage pipelined engine operating at the least latency possible, thereby, significantly reducing the size of the delay buffer. The RS decoder is implemented using Cadence tools and Kintex-7 field programmable gate array (FPGA). The technology-scaled normalized throughput of the pipelined RS decoder is almost two times compared with the existing decoders. The overall processing latency is reduced by almost 80% compared with the existing designs. Second, we design a high-throughput LDPC decoder using layered and non-layered min-sum algorithm based on non-uniform quantization (NUQ) on an FPGA kit. Unlike the standard state-of-the-art uniform quantization used in virtually all decoder circuits, our NUQ technique: 1) achieves a slight performance improvement of ∼0.1 dB in the signal-to-noise ratio using equal number of bits and 2) yields 20% area savings (using 1 bit less) for the block RAMs used for storing intermediate check node and variable node messages.
Index Terms— Berlekamp–Massey (BM) algorithm, error correction codes (ECC), field programmable gate array (FPGA), high speed, Kintex-7 FPGA, layered min-sum, low-density parity-check (LDPC), low complexity, low latency, non-uniform quantization (NUQ), Reed–Solomon (RS) decoder.

I. INTRODUCTION
P RACTICAL magnetic and flash storage technologies are pushing toward densities beyond 2 Tb/in2 [1]. To meet stringent reliability rates over such high-density storage media, efficient error correction codes are inevitably required. With the emerging TDMR technology [2], [3], high-speed, lowlatency [4], and low-power [5] detectors are needed for processing multiple tracks. These detectors are still based on 1-D designs applicable toward multi-track detection prior to error correction decoding. Though 2-D codes would be the eventual state of the art in TDMR systems, current array-based magnetic recording systems [6] still employ 1-D codes that work within a shingled recording framework [7]. Two main classes of error correction codes popularly exist; namely, algebraic and iterative codes. Algebraic-based Reed–Solomon (RS) codes and iterative-based low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are the most commonly used error correction techniques in data storage systems. While RS decoders provide guaranteed error correction capability, carefully constructed LDPC codes guarantee low error floors with an acceptable rate of miscorrection performance [8], [9].
RS codes are popularly used because of their guaranteed burst error correcting capability in combating thermal asperities. They are also widely used in a variety of traditional applications such as optical, satellite, wireless, and networking

Implementing a high-speed and area-efficient RS decoder architecture is always a key challenge. The state-of-the art RS decoders implemented on FGPA barely reach a maximum throughput of 2 Gbps [10]. Recently, Xilinx released an RS decoder intellectual property (IP) [11] supporting a maximum throughput of 3.2 Gbps for the RS (255, 239) code. Furthermore, the latency of our decoder is very low compared with the existing decoders [11], [13]–[15].
Soft decision decoding of LDPC codes is the state-of-the-art technique in almost all commercially available storage devices. LDPC decoding admits parallelization and thus suitable for hardware implementations. The standard belief propagation algorithm or the sum–product algorithm (SPA) gives the best decoding performance [16]. However, the SPA requires a lot of complex logarithmic and multiplicative components at the check node unit (CNU). The min-sum algorithm (MSA), which is a simplification of the SPA, is well suited for hardware design owing to its simplicity [17], [18]. The traditional MSA processes the block rows in parallel, leading to a large Silicon area. The layered MSA [19], [20] is the state-of-the-art decoder architecture used in all LDPC-based decoder engines. B. VLSI Architectures of the Decoder
The hard decision RS decoder can be implemented with two popular algorithms, namely the Berlekamp–Massey (BM) algorithm and the modified Euclidean (ME) algorithm, to solve the key equation [23] toward computing the error locator polynomial. The ME algorithm is easier to understand and implement, whereas the BM algorithm is efficient in terms of computational complexity and implementation. We proceed with the BM algorithm to implement a decoder circuit on FPGA. The error values are computed using the error evaluator polynomial [23]. Pipelining is employed to the RS decoder to achieve higher throughput. The popular threestage pipelined RS decoder [25] is shown in Fig. 1. In a pipelined architecture, the overall throughput is decided by the slowest pipelined stage and its computation time. Hence, to increase the throughput with efficient area utilization, each

The key equation solver (KES) stage that computes the error locator polynomial using the BM algorithm requires 2t iterations. Given the syndromes and the error locator polynomial, Chien search and error magnitude computation (CSEMC) stage computes the error evaluator polynomial. The error locator and error evaluator polynomials are used to find the error locations and the corresponding error magnitudes. Parallelism can be employed to adjust the number of clock cycles required for the syndrome computation (SC) and the CSEMC stages. In addition, we use a delay buffer to buffer the received symbols. The size of the delay buffer depends on the latency of the decoder.
In this paper, we present a novel low-complexity inversionless BM (iBM)-based KES architecture that takes exactly 2t cycles. We also present a novel architecture to find the error evaluator polynomial that takes t cycles. In total, our proposed architecture has the same critical path delay and hardware requirements as that of the existing iBM architectures [26]. Our RS decoder architecture has the least latency even though our KES has more critical path delay than the reformulated iBM (RiBM)-based architecture [26]. We use parallelism to ensure that the SC unit takes exactly 2t cycles as the KES stage in order to maximize the throughput. With this parallelism, we combine SC and KES stages into a single stage without affecting the throughput. Our overall RS decoder is a two-stage pipelined engine with significantly less pipeline registers and delay buffers. To maximize the throughput and minimize the latency, we make use of the efficient architecture for error locator and magnitude computation [25] coupled with the J parallel Chien search architecture [27] to find the error locations and the corresponding error magnitudes simultaneously.
With all these available blocks, our major contribution is the following. We propose and develop a novel twostage pipelined RS decoder architecture. With this approach, the KES stage needs to wait for only two extra cycles with respect to the SC stage to initialize the BM iterative procedure. Hence, the throughput achieved by our design is almost the same as the three-stage pipelined decoder with significant improvement in latency.

III. DESIGN OF THE LDPC DECODER USING NON-UNIFORM QUANTIZATION
We will now discuss the design of LDPC decoder architectures using NUQ and compare the performance of various architectural realizations. For practical reasons, we use the MSA for our hardware design. The MSA does not require complex logarithmic and trigonometrical calculations, thus avoiding the use of bulky LUTs. There is a tradeoff between speed and area in any hardware design. Non-layered MSA, which does parallel processing of block rows, can be used in scenarios demanding high throughput. On the other hand, if the lower area is required, layered MSA can be used. Layered MSA processes the block rows serially, thus achieving a low area. Also, the layered MSA provides better SNR performance than its non-layered counterpart attuned to the non-linear dynamics of the algorithm.
We have designed LDPC decoders using both layered and non-layered MSA using NUQ. The design can easily be scaled and worked out for all code parameters of interest. This section

B. Quantization
In hardware, LLRs need to be represented by a finite number of bits, leading to a tradeoff between area, performance, and complexity with a choice of bit precision [29]. In order to devise a quantization scheme, we consider a (384, 4096) code with a parallelism of 128. It was found that a 5 bit quantization

Fig. 6. Block diagram of LMS engine which finds optimum quantization levels.

provided reasonably small SNR performance degradation of ∼0.5 dB with respect to floating point precision. If we go for higher number of bits, the SNR gap can be reduced significantly. However, there is a tradeoff; increased SNR gain comes at the cost of higher area and power. If the design demands are close to floating point performance, we can sacrifice in terms of area and power. Using 6 bit quantization instead of 5 bits would incur ∼20% increase in area. It was also found that choosing variable quantization levels boosted the performance of the decoder. NUQ has a lower bit budget for the same SNR performance and provides better performance at the same bit budget due to inherent non-linear dynamics of the algorithm.
For storage systems, throughput, area, and power are the factors that lead to difficulty in design as one would not prefer to re-read, re-encode, and re-write back on the medium. The primary challenge in NUQ is to obtain the optimum quantization levels that would minimize the quantization error. The histogram of the LLRs evolves with each iteration due to density evolution. As the algorithm is non-linear, it is difficult to model the exact behavior of the LLRs with iterations and fitting a probability model for the same. So, we use a least mean square (LMS)-based approach for finding the optimum quantization levels during the simulations in software. Once the quantization numbers are received from the LMS engine designed on software, they are used as LUTs for hardware design of the decoder. It should be mentioned here that the non-uniformly quantized version of the decoder will not have the sign conversion units as the additions and subtractions use LUTs. We have considered a general parity check matrix with L block rows, c block columns, and a parallelism p. The LLRs for one frame are stored in an RAM having a depth c and width p × b, where b represents the number of bits used for quantization.
In the layered MSA [30], the block columns and block rows are processed serially. Fig. 10 shows the block diagram of the decoder. The architecture consists of CNUs, barrel shifters, adders, subtractors, and BRAM for storing the intermediate P, Q, and R messages. The CNU array is composed of p parallel CNU units, which compute the partial state for each row to produce R messages in block serial form. The R messages for each layer are stored in BRAMs of depth c and width p × b (b being the number of bits) as they would be used in subsequent iterations. We require the previous Q messages for the adder which are stored in a BRAM of depth c and width p × b. The MUX is required to supply new LLRs to the decoder when the decoder has corrected the previous frame or the maximum iteration limit is reached. Signed to 2’s complement and 2’s complement to signed converters are required before and after the CNU in the case of uniform quantization.
The MUX at the input of the cyclic shifter takes care of the initialization for new frames. In the beginning, the output of the R select unit is set to a zero vector. The output of the subtractor is stored in a BRAM, which would be required later for the adder. The output of the subtractor is also fed to a set of barrel shifters. The CNU array receives the output of the cyclic shifter serially, and the partial stage operation starts. After c clock cycles, the partial state processing is complete, and the final state stage produces the final state for each check node in one clock cycle. After this, the R select unit within each CNU starts producing c values of check node messages serially. Thereafter, the check node messages are fed to the second set of barrel shifters to reverse the shifts applied by the first. The output of these cyclic shifters thus

represent the R messages. The P messages are then computed by adding the delayed version of the Q messages (which are stored in BRAM until serial CNU produces the output) to the R messages. The R messages are then stored in a R message BRAM, which would be used in subsequent iterations. The next block row is now ready to be processed as the P messages are directed by the MUX to the subtractor. The next block row is operated in a similar way as explained before. For the next iteration i (if the syndrome has not been satisfied for iteration i − 1), the R select unit directs R messages from the previous iteration to the subtractor. The process continues till the frame has been corrected or a maximum iteration limit has been reached. The primary blocks of the decoder are explained in detail as follows.
1) Check Node Unit: The CNU [31] in Fig. 11 emulates the operations at the check node on a Tanner graph. It sends back the minimum of the values received from a certain variable node, discounting the variable node. The CNU consists of a minimum value N1 and a second minimum value N2 finder, a partial state which stores N1 and N2 temporarily and updates them on each clock cycle, a final state which stores the final N1 and N2 value, and a sign processing unit which takes care of the sign of the LLR to be sent. Incoming variable messages are compared with two up-to-date least minimum numbers, to generate new partial state. In this state, we have N1 (first minimum value), N2 (second minimum value), and the index of N1. The final state is achieved after all the messages have been received. The R selector then assigns one of these two values (N1and N2) based on the index of N1 and sign of R messages generated by the XOR logic.
2) Barrel Shifter: The barrel shifter contains s stages which produce shifts of 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, . . . , 2s−1, respectively. Each stage contains p numbers of MUXes in parallel. The shift value is determined by a signal shi f ter _b, which is an s bit signal. For any stage n, if the shi f ter _b bit connected to it is 1, a shift of 2n−1 is produced, otherwise no shift is produced. Thus the barrel shifter can produce any amount of shift required from 1 to 2s − 1 in one clock cycle. The architecture of the barrel shifter is shown in Fig. 12.

Hard decision is also computed from the sign of the sum.

IV. HARDWARE EMULATION OF THE AWGN CHANNEL
To evaluate the performance of algorithms in digital storage systems, we need efficient simulation tools. Using datadependent noise prediction, the overall read channel can be approximated with an ISI channel and AWGN. Thus, rapid generation of high-quality Gaussian random numbers is useful for many emulator applications. Software delays are inherent in long-run simulations. To avoid such delays and to complete the simulation in real time, we have investigated into a hardware emulation. The set up involves a VC707 FPGA board to emulate the system. A synthesized model of the channel is used to perform the simulations at a very high speed, but we have to look for a compromise between the performance and hardware complexity. We will discuss the details of the implementation of our emulator in this section.
A. Box–Muller Transform Method
The Gaussian noise sample is generated in two steps as there is no direct way of generating Gaussian random numbers. Box–Muller [36] has proposed an indirect way of

generating Gaussian random numbers using a transformation approach. As a first step, we generate uniform random numbers using a linear feedback shift register-based hardware using Box–Muller transformation and then convert uniform random numbers to Gaussian.
The Box–Muller transformation approach is widely used in software simulations. It generates two Gaussian random numbers using two uniform random numbers. The uniform random numbers should be statistically independent to get two independent random numbers. The Box–Muller algorithm can be understood as representing the output random numbers as coordinates in a 2-D plane. This means that there are 210 possible uniform numbers. We need three LUTs each of length 210. For cosine and sine function LUTs, the magnitude varies from +1 to −1. So, these LUTs have 12 bits in each location, out of which least 10 bits are for fractional values, 1 bit is for magnitude, and 1 bit is for sign. (−2 ln u1)1/2 representation always gives a non-negative value, implying no sign bit is required. The minimum value of u1 is 2−10 (10 bit uniform number) that gives the maximum value of +3.72 for (−2 ln u1)1/2. So, the magnitude varies from 0 to 3.72, requiring 10 bits for fractional value and 2 bits for magnitude representation. Likewise, three LUTs each of length 1024 and width of 12 bits were implemented using BRAM memories using core generator IP.
C. Results
A Gaussian random number generator is implemented on a VC707 FPGA board and simulated for 105 samples. The hardware is designed in such a way that it gives two random numbers in each clock cycle with the same magnitude, but variation in phase. Figs. 17 and 18 show the Gaussian random variable density function with cosine and sine phases, respectively. This signal generator was useful for our hardware circuit.

V. CONCLUSION
RS and LDPC are highly popular coding schemes of significant practical importance in storage systems. Efficient implementation of these codes is vital for realizing performance gains in hardware.
In the first part, we proposed a high-speed low-complexity architecture for RS decoders with the lowest latency using a two-stage pipelined architecture. We addressed the tradeoff between performance and area and compared it with existing RS decoders. Our RS decoder hardware has the lowest latency and highest throughput to date on FPGA with a maximum throughput of 24 Gbps for the RS (255, 239) code. Our RS decoder synthesized using UMC 65 and 130 nm CMOS technologies has higher TSNT and lower latency than existing designs with potential applications in high-speed ECC circuits.
In the second part of this paper, we designed a nonuniformly quantized, Area-efficient LDPC decoder based on layered and non-layered MSA for an FPGA circuit prototype. We also used Cadence tools to compare gate count numbers. Our FPGA-based LDPC design is useful for architectural evaluation of LDPC-based coded systems. The proposed layered design achieves a throughput of 2.128 Gbps at 150.015 MHz, while the non-layered design achieves a throughput of 6.64 Gbps at 150 MHz. But the resources on the board utilized by the non-layered decoder are almost three times the layered decoder. By comparing the gate count numbers with other state-of-the-art works, the throughput of the proposed design was found to be considerably better within the code specifications.
