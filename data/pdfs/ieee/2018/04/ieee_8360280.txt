Construction and Decoding of OSMLD Codes
Derived from Unital and Oval Designs
Otmane EL MOUAATAMID‡, Mohamed LAHMER§, Mostafa BELKASMI‡ ‡ ENSIAS, Mohammed V University in Rabat, Morocco § EST, My Ismail University in Meknes, Morocco otmane_elmouaatamid@um5.ac.ma mohammed.lahmer@gmail.com m.belkasmi@um5s.net.ma

Abstract—A construction of One-Step Majority-Logic Decodable (OSMLD) codes based on the incidence matrices of Balanced Incomplete Block Designs (BIBD) is given. In this paper, we focus on unital and oval designs which are constructed from a Projective Geometry (PG). Thus, we derive from the unital and oval designs new systematic and non-systematic OSMLD codes with rates and lengths not available with existing OSMLD codes. Simulation results show that the derived codes converge well under Iterative Threshold Decoding with an efficient trade-off between complexity and performances.
Index Terms—One-Step Majority Logic Decodable (OSMLD) Codes, Iterative Threshold Decoding (ITD), Block Designs, Balanced Incomplete Block Designs (BIBD), Unital Designs, Oval Designs.
I. INTRODUCTION
One-Step Majority-Logic Decodable (OSMLD) codes were first found in 1954 by Reed [1] for a class of multiple errors correcting codes discovered by Muller. Mitchell et al in 1961 showed that the triple error correcting code (15,5), a quadruple error correcting (75, 45) cyclic code, and a double error correcting (21, 11) cyclic code which is studied by Green and San Soucie in 1958 [2], and Prange [3] in 1959 respectively, may be decoded by a simple majority rule [5]. Most majority logic decodable codes found so far are cyclic codes, an algebraic approach can also be taken to the decoding of convolutional codes. The first majority logic decoding algorithms for convolutional codes that have been termed selforthogonal by Massey, were formulated by him in 1963 [4]. There have been several works dealing with the association between codes and designs. Firstly, the characteristics of the design are used to define the parameters of the code. Secondly, the combinatorial designs [9], [11], [12] can be used to construct and define the most known algebraic codes such as the Reed-Muller codes[13].
OSMLD codes are very suitable for computer applications. OSMLD codes are known as codes with minimum redundancy that also characterized by the following features [5]:
- Uniform logic circuit for easy implementation. - Partial self-correcting and diagnosing decoder. - Parallel processing. - Ultra High-speed encoding and decoding. The main contributions of this work are the construction and decoding of new OSMLD codes derived from combinatorial

designs. The construction of these OSMLD codes has based on the incidence matrices of unital and oval designs which are derived from Projective Geometry (PG). We have classified These OSMLD codes into two classes, systematic and non-systematic OSMLD codes, where the systematic codes classified into two sub-classes, class 1 and class 2. To decode these OSMLD codes, we have used the Iterative Threshold Decoding (ITD) algorithm which is based on SISO extension of Massey threshold decoding algorithm [4] was developed mainly for decoding Parallel Concatenated Block Codes [18] and product codes constructed from OSMLD Codes [19], later it was enhanced for decoding not only Generalized Parallel Concatenated OSMLD Codes [20] but also OSMLD block codes [21]. ITD algorithm has an improvement in error correcting related to standard Majority-Logic Decoding (MLD). This codes derived from unital and oval designs as Balanced Incomplete Block Designs (BIBD) and Partially Balanced Incomplete Block Designs (PBIBD). Previous works on this subject were done by Rudolph [6], Smith [7] and Hsiao [8]. Other papers using BIBD for constructing Low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes are shown in [16], [17], their results consist of using the incidence matrix of a BIBD as the parity check matrix of the LDPC code. In this paper, we have focused on unital and oval designs as Block Designs (BD) derived from the projective geometry to construct new systematic and nonsystematic OSMLD codes. We classified the systematic codes into two sub-classes, class 1 In which these systematic codes are derived from unital and oval designs as a BIBD, and class 2 where we derive these codes from unital or oval designs as PBIBD, such that codes of class 2 represent the dual codes of class 1. The material of the description of the unital and oval design is given by Assmus and Key [13], [14]. According to coding theory and design theory, the blocks (or lines) of a design can represent the dual structure of a linear code C. The obtained dual code contains a set of orthogonal parity check equations, on each symbol j where 0 < j < n. As a consequence, the minimum distance can be easily determined.

II. COMBINATORIAL DESIGNS
Combinatorial design theory is interested in arranging elements of a finite set of subsets to satisfy certain properties. The theory of combinatorial has strong connections with other fields in discrete mathematics. Besides linear and abstract algebra and number theory, we mention especially its links to the theory of graph, incidence and finite geometry and permutation group theory [9]. In this section, we give a short overview of combinatorial design theory. We begin with examining Block Designs, which are the central object in our study to construct OSMLD codes.

A. Block Design
A Block Design or just a t-design is a pair D = (P, B), where P is a set of v elements, called points, and B is a family of k∗ − subsets of P, called blocks. These blocks are subject to the condition that every t − subset of P must be contained in exactly λ blocks. The value λ is sometimes called the index of a design. We make the natural assumption that 0 < k < v and t ≥ 2 to avoid trivial designs. Thus, a regular design has six parameters: t, v, b, r∗, k∗ and λ is sometimes denoted as t − (v, k∗, λ) or by t − (v, b, r∗, k, λ). However, the six parameters are not independent but must satisfy the two relations:

Traditionally, k and r are used as parameters of a design. We shall use k∗ and r∗ to avoid confusion with the notation used in Coding Theory.
There are special names given to certain classes of designs such as a Balanced Incomplete Block Designs (BIBD) and Partially Balanced Incomplete Block Designs (PBIBD).

B. Balanced Incomplete Block Designs
A design with t = 2 is called a balanced incomplete block design, also BIBD or BIB design. A design with index λ = 1 is called a Steiner Design. A Steiner design with v points and k = 3 is a Steiner triple system or ST S(v), a design with k = 4 is a Steiner quadruple system or SQS(v). There are other designs such as a finite affine geometry EG(2, q) designs, and finite projective geometry P G(2, q) designs as given by Carmichael [24] and Bose [9] from the projective geometry P G(2, q) we can derive others designs such as oval designs, and unital designs. In this work, we focus only on unital designs.

C. Partially Balanced Incomplete Block Designs
Let P be a v-set with a symmetric association scheme defined on it. A partially balanced incomplete block design with s associate classes (PBIBD(s)) is a design based on the v-set P with b blocks, each of size k, and with each treatment appearing in r blocks. Any two treatments that are ith associates appear together in i blocks of the PBIBD(s). The numbers v, b, r, k, λi (0 < i < s) are the parameters of the PBIBD(s). One writes PBIBD(v, k, λi) and lets N be the v × b(0, 1) incidence matrix of the PBIBD(s)[10].
