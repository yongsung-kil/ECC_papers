Noisy Gradient Descent Bit-Flipping Decoder Based on Adjustment Factor for LDPC Codes

Bin Dai , Rongke Liu, Member, IEEE, Chenyu Gao, and Zhen Mei

Abstract— As a class of bit-flipping (BF) decoder, the noisy gradient descent bit flipping (NGDBF) algorithm outperforms the original GDBF and other BF algorithms. To increase the reliability of the inversion function, we propose a modified NGDBF algorithm by introducing an adjustment factor on the syndrome. Moreover, for the multi-bit flipping, we present an adaptive inversion threshold which depends on the numbers of negative bipolar syndromes. The simulation results show that our proposed algorithm outperforms the NGDBF algorithm for both single-bit and multi-bit flipping schemes.
Index Terms— LDPC codes, gradient descent bit flipping, multi-bit flipping.
I. INTRODUCTION
C HANNEL coding plays an important role in the transmission of data on communication systems. Particularly, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1], [2] have recently received a lot of attention, due to their excellent performance. The advances of LDPC codes have mainly been achieved by using the message-passing (MP) algorithms. In terms of error performance, the sum-product (SP) algorithm [3], one of the MP algorithms, can obtain the highest coding gain. However, the BP decoder introduces very high computational complexity, which is of limited interest for the application of LDPC codes. To reduce the decoding complexity, the min-sum (MS) algorithm [4] simplifies the check node (CN) updating procedure. To reduce error floor, a noise perturbation based method is proposed in [5]. However, both of them still require a large number of arithmetic operations during each iteration.
Different from MP algorithms, bit-flipping (BF) algorithms present lower complexity and are much simpler for hardware implementation. Generally speaking, BF algorithms decode by flipping one bit or a group of bits based on the inversion function. The inversion function corresponding to a variable node (VN) is a reliability metric to decide the location of the flipping bit. Although BF algorithms are much simpler than the MP algorithms, their bit-error rate (BER) performance is far from optimal. To balance the BER performance and decoding complexity, many variants of BF algorithms [6]–[9] were proposed. Among various BF algorithms, the recently

The NGDBF algorithm introduces a weighting parameter ω, but it does not distinguish whether the influence of different partial syndrome values (si, i ∈ M (k)) on the k-th VN is positive or not, where M (k) will be defined in next section. When si has a negative effect on the k-th VN, it will reduce the reliability of the inversion function.

A. Single-Bit Flipping Based on Adjustment Factor
As observed in (2), the inversion function is determined by the information from the channel and adjacent partial bipolar syndrome values. As stated in [9], the local maximum likelihood bit-flipping decisions are mainly influenced by the partial syndrome values in later iterations. A weighting parameter ω is multiplied by the sum of adjacent partial syndromes in [9]. C. Complexity Analysis
In this section, we mainly analyze the complexity for computing the inversion function and determining which bit or bits are flipped. Consider the LDPC codes with average column weight dv and average row weight dc. For the S-A-NGDBF algorithm, at the initialization, n real comparisons are required to determine the bipolar hard-decision symbol sequence u. To compute xkyk, since xk ∈ {−1, +1}, the operation of multiplying plus or minus one can be ignored. For later iterations (T > 0), n real comparisons are required to obtain aki. The calculation of si (i = 1, . . . , m), takes m(dc − 1) XOR operations. Since aki can only be equal to λ1 or λ2, the second part of (4) takes two multiplications after using factorization. Besides, dv+1 real additions are needed for each EkT . To determine which bit is flipped, n − 1 real comparisons are required for the S-A-NGDBF algorithm. To conclude, the computation complexity per iteration for the S-A-NGDBF algorithm contains ndv + n real additions, m(dc − 1) XOR operations, 2n real multiplications and 2n−1 real comparisons. Similarly, we can derive that ndv + n + m real additions, m(dc − 1) XOR operations, 2n + 3 real multiplications and 2n real comparisons are required for the M-A-NGDBF algorithm. B. Convergence Analysis
The main purpose of the class of GDBF algorithms is to find the global maximum of the objective function f (x). However, the objective function is a non-linear function and has many local maxima. All GDBF and its variants attempt to maximize the objective function by adjusting x iteratively. Hence, the convergence analysis can be done by observing the value of objective function for each iteration. For any ML-decodable case, the original transmitted codeword c˜ should be the ML solution. Then the global maximum for f (x) is obtained by introducing ˜c in (1).

V. CONCLUSION
In this letter, to judge whether the adjacent partial syndromes have a positive or negative effect on the VN, an improved noisy GDBF algorithm based on an adjustment factor is proposed. In addition, we have proposed an adaptive inversion threshold for multi-bit flipping which can take advantage of the syndrome and improve the decoding efficiency. The proposed algorithm has been compared with the NGDBF algorithm and MS algorithm and simulation results show that our algorithms (A-NGDBF) outperform the NGDBF algorithms and have faster convergence speed.

Fig. 3. BER convergence comparison on code 1 at Eb/N0 = 4 dB.
After 60 iterations, the rise of S-GDBF stops and the f (x) flattens out. For S-NGDBF, it reaches the global maximum value at the 100-th iteration. Particularly, our proposed S-A-NGDBF algorithm can reach the global maximum value after 80 iterations, indicating that the S-NGDBF and S-A-NGDBF algorithms can escape from the spurious local maximum. In addition, the convergence speed of S-A-NGDBF algorithm is faster than the S-NGDBF algorithm. Compared with the single-bit flipping algorithms, the multi-bits flipping shows a faster convergence speed. Similar to the S-GDBF algorithm, the M-GDBF algorithm stops the increase of f (x) value after 20 iterations. However, the M-A-NGDBF and M-NGDBF algorithms can reach the global maximum value after 15 and 40 iterations, respectively. It is observed that the M-NGDBF algorithm also stops at the spurious local maximum (10 to 40 iterations) and escapes from the local maximum after 40 iterations, while our proposed M-A-NGDBF algorithm can reach the global maximum directly after 15 iterations.
