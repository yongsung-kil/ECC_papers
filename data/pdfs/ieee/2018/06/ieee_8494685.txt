Reduced Message-Passing Bitwidth for Hardware Implementation of ADMM-LP Decoding
Omar Samhan, Stark Draper

Abstract—We consider the implementation in hardware of linear-programming (LP) decoding for low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. Previous work has shown that the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) can be applied to solve LP decoding in a message passing manner amenable to hardware. Wasson et al. demonstrated the feasibility of a hardware implementation in a field-programmable gate array (FPGA). However, the area footprint and message bit-width of the implemenation was not competitive with classic alternatives such as min-sum decoding. In this paper we leverage recent work by Jiao et al. on implementating the central computational primative of ADMMLP decoding (a certain Euclidean projection) via look-up tables (LUTs). While Jiao’s work indicated the possiblity of significant bit-width reduction, it left open a number of questions about how to translate those ideas into hardware and what algorithmic modifications would be required. In this paper we answer those questions and develop a modified version of Wasson’s ADMMLP decoding algorithm, realizing a bit-width reduction of about 50%, thereby yielding a hardware implementation of ADMM-LP decoding that is competitive with min-sum.
I. INTRODUCTION
L DPC codes have generated a lot of interest in the past couple of decades due to their near-Shannon capacity achieving performance and their relatively simple decoding [1], [2]. The two most notable algorithms used to decode LDPC codes are belief propagation (BP) and the min-sum algorithm, both of which rely on a message-passing framework. Implementing these algorithms on hardware is straightforward and intuitive due to their inherit distributed nature. However, BP and min-sum suffer from an error-floor phenomena at high SNR which renders them less attractive for certain applications where high reliability is critical [3].
In [4], Feldman et al. relaxed the maximum-likelihood (ML) problem into a linear program (LP). LPs are much easier to solve and have been thoroughly studied since the 1950’s [5]. LP decoding addresses a couple of key drawbacks of BP and min-sum. First, LP decoding has been observed to suffer from little-to-no error-floor at high SNR. In addition, LP decoding has a “ML certificate” property which guarantees the ML solution if and only if the result is an integer vertex [4]. Despite its benefits over BP, LP decoding proved to be unfeasible to implement in practice for LDPC codes with large blocklengths and codes with high check degrees (the number of constraints grows exponentially in the code’s check degrees). Nevertheless, a number of research groups saw the potential of LP decoding and strove to make it feasible. Taghavi and Siegel tackled the large number of constraints through an adaptive approach. They started off with minimal constraints, and then determine if a “cut” occurred in the unit

hypercube. Additional constraints were added to avoid the so-called cuts [6]. In this approach, one solves multiple LPs each with fewer number of constraints compared to basic LP decoding. Rosnes and Helming in [7], [8] and Liu and Draper in [9] took the adaptive LP decoding a step further by studying its structure and performance for non-binary and ternary fields.
To make LP decoding viable in practice, Barman et al. applied a classic decomposition technique in optimization called the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) [10]. The application of ADMM to LP decoding yields an algorithm that alternated between finding the optimal primal and dual variables. This novel approach resulted in an algorithm with a message-passing structure similar to BP and min-sum that makes it attractive for hardware. The bottleneck of the algorithm was the projection on the d-dimensional “parity-polytope”, PPd. Although the ADMM application to LP yielded a feasible solution, the error rate was observed to be inferior to BP due to non-integer vertices, candidate solutions which spawn from the LP relaxation. To divert the solution away from such non-integer vertices, Liu and Draper proposed penalizing the LP objective [11]. The penalty functions rendered the optimization problem non-convex but there was a substantial performance boost in decoding performance. The penalized ADMM-LP was also computationally competitive with BP and, thus, appealing for practice.
A hardware implementation of ADMM-LP on a FPGA was not explored until the work by Wasson et al. [12]. These authors were able to reduce the complexity of the projection onto the PPd in [13] by exploiting the geometric structure of the PPd and transforming the problem into a simplex projection via a relatively simple operation [14]. An ADMM-LP FPGA implementation was thus proved to be viable. However, two main concerns arose with the FPGA implementation. First, its area utilization was large and, second, its check-tovariable and variable-to-check messages bit-width was wide when compared to min-sum implementations.
More recently, Jiao et al. reduced the computation complexity of PPd projection by quantizing the vector to be projected and using a LUT to search for a pre-evaluated projection [15]. This approach came at a cost of high memory utilization for the LUT structure, one that was somewhat reduced using the rotation invariance of the PPd. From empirical results, the authors showed that the input to the PPd projection module can be based on a relatively small number of quantization levels while suffering minimal degradation in projection accuracy.
This result inspired us to further expand on the LUTbased ADMM-LP decoder by looking at the input and output alphabet of the parity-polytope projection as opposed to just

II. PROBLEM FORMULATION
Let C be a binary linear code of length n and dimension k over the field Fn2 . The code C can be fully defined by the parity check-matrix H of size m×n, where m = n−k, such that C = {x ∈ {0, 1}n : Hx = 0 (mod 2)}. The rate of the code R = k/n determines the amount of information sent per codeword symbol. The rows of the parity-check matrix H are the set of constraints which need to be satisfied in order to verify the membership of a codeword. Each row (check) of H is indexed by J = {1, 2, . . . , m}. Similarly, each column (variable) of H is indexed by I = {1, 2, . . . , n}. The neighborhood of check j ∈ J is the set of variable indices where the row j has non-zero entries. More formally, Nc(j) = {i ∈ I : Hj,i = 0}. Likewise, the neighborhood of variable i ∈ I is Nv(i) = {j ∈ J : Hj,i = 0}.
Consider a stochastic binary-input output-symmetric memoryless channel with a transition probability P(y|x) where y ∈ Yn and x ∈ C are the channel output and input respectively. Algorithm 2 is a modified version of Algorithm 1 with a finite alphabet cardinality for the input vector to, and output vector from, the PPd projection. To make Algorithm

2 compatible with the LUT-inspired alphabet, we make three key modifications.
First, the dual-variable updates have been moved from the check to the variable nodes. This allows us to limit the check node computation to PPd projection only. The result of the first change is that the input to the check node is the same as the input to the PPd projection. Since the output of the projection is element-wise in the alphabet Z, we can have Nc(j) LUTs in each check node which map a value in Z to a particular LUT index. The dual variable update exchange is possible because the updates are element-wise. This means that the dual variable updates are not strictly bound to the check node updates. We comment that these LUTs are distinctly different from the LUT proposed in [15]. While LUT mapped the input of the projection routine to the output (and therefore worked on d-dimensional vectors), the LUTs we employ work on scalars and so are much smaller. For the projection routine, we can use the LUT-based projection, or the hardware projection routine developed by Wasson [12], or any other approach.
The second change is the addition of a LUT(·) and LUT−1(·). At the check node, the LUT(·) operation maps the pre-evaluated finite alphabet of the (component-wise) PPd projection output to a LUT index. Note that check-to-variable messages are indexed into the LUT alphabet and are not the actual values of the projection. In other words, in contrast to [12], the check-to-variable messages are real valued numbers in fixed-point format, thus algebraic operation can be performed on those messages. Conversely, in our implementation, at the variable node, the LUT−1(·) operation is needed to convert the indices into their respective values in Z.
The third major addition is the uniform quantizer on line 15, defined in equation (9). Note that no LUTs are needed for variable-to-check messages since the quantization naturally maps a set of finite real-valued numbers into fixed-point representations. If the algorithm is centered around zero, as in [12], this will make the quantized values map seamlessly in fixed-point (Q format).
