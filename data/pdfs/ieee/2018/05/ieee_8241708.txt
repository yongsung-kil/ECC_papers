PEG-Like Design of Binary QC-LDPC Codes Based on Detecting and Avoiding Generating Small Cycles
Xuan He , Student Member, IEEE, Liang Zhou, and Junyi Du, Student Member, IEEE

Abstract— In this paper, we propose a new multi-edge metric-constrained quasi-cyclic progressive edge-growth algorithm (MM-QC-PEGA), which is suitable for constructing both single- and multi-weighted (binary) QC low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes with arbitrary lengths, rates, circulant sizes, and variable node (VN)-degree distributions. The MM-QC-PEGA is able to detect all cyclic-edge-set-minimumvirtual cycles (CMVCs), as it accurately computes the metric value of each CMVC with an a posteriori test. In addition, we propose a new greatest-common-divisor (GCD)-approximation for time efficiently approximating the metric value of a CMVC without a posteriori tests, and propose a new GCD-approximated MM-QC-PEGA (G-MM-QC-PEGA) by using the GCDapproximation to replace the a posteriori test involved in the MM-QC-PEGA. As a result, the G-MM-QC-PEGA is faster than the MM-QC-PEGA, and the CMVCs undetectable to the G-MM-QC-PEGA under multi- and single-weighted QC-LDPC code graphs have minimum lengths of eight and ten, respectively. Moreover, we propose a new masking technique that is efficient in masking the parity-check matrix consisting of an array of arbitrary circulants of the same size. Compared with the several existing works, our proposed algorithms could perform better or comparably in terms of avoiding generating small cycles and error performances. Our proposed algorithms somewhat perfect the works of QC-LDPC code construction.
Index Terms— Approximate cycle extrinsic message degree (ACE), cyclic-edge-set-minimum-virtual cycle (CMVC), design, detect, edge-trial, greatest-common-divisor (GCD), masking, multi-edge local girth, progressive edge-growth (PEG), quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) code.

Q UASI-CYCLIC low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes [2] could lead to simple hardware implementations, and their performances can approach the Shannon limit. Thus, they have been the primary choice for practical applications, such as being a standard in the 5G wireless communication. The construction via optimizing the girth and the extrinsic message degree (EMD) may be the most popular way to construct QC-LDPC codes [3]–[26]. In general,

the construction methods in [3]–[26] can be classified to three main categories: the algebraic construction [3]–[10], the protograph-based construction [11]–[22], and the progressive edge-growth (PEG)-like design [23]–[26].
The algebraic construction methods in [3]–[10] first construct a base parity-check matrix (PCM) based on certain algebraic structures (such as finite fields and finite geometries), and then optimize the base PCM’s error performance via masking [5]. Typically, the base PCM consists of an array of singleweighted circulants of the same size and is free of 4-cycles (cycles of length 4), but the circulant size is limited to be some specific numbers (such as a prime or the power of a prime). Masking technique is very effective in eliminating small cycles (cycles of short lengths and poor EMDs) and in optimizing error performance of the base PCM. However, to the best of our knowledge, no existing masking techniques, or at least not the masking techniques used (or mentioned) in [3]–[10], have both of the following two advantages: 1) to be effective in masking a PCM whether it consists of an array of only single-weighted circulants or not (i.e., high flexibility); 2) to be time-efficient for constructing masking matrices with relatively large sizes (i.e., low computational complexity).
The protograph-based construction methods in [11]–[22] first construct a small base matrix (protograph), and then cyclically lift the base matrix to a desired QC-LDPC code. Indeed, the PEG-like designs in [23]–[27] can be regarded as a special class of the protograph-based construction in that these designs construct and cyclically lift the base matrix at the same time. As a variant of the PEG algorithm [28], the QC-PEG algorithm (QC-PEGA) [23] is flexible in constructing QC-LDPC codes by constructing a cyclic edge set (CES) in each stage. However, the CES-minimum-virtual cycles (CMVCs) undetectable to the QC-PEGA under multiand single-weighted Tanner graphs (TGs) [29] (in this paper, a TG indeed refers to a QC-TG) have minimum lengths of 4 and 8, respectively. To detect the CMVCs related to a CES that has not been established, in fact, we could use an a posteriori test: add the CES, then test the minimum resulting cycles, and finally remove the CES, as mentioned in [25]. Moreover, Diouf et al. [26] figured out the characterizations of the PEG-undetectable 8- and 10-cycles under single-weighted TGs, and proposed an improved QC-PEGA to detect these cycles without a posteriori tests.

Inspired by the works in [30] and [31], the MM-QC-PEGA constructs a CES in each stage that, relative to the TG setting of this stage, could potentially maximize the minimum resulting cycles in a latter stage, after a certain number of CESs are established. The MM-QC-PEGA computes the metric value of each CMVC with the a posteriori test. As a result, it is able to detect all CMVCs, and is effective in avoiding generating small cycles.
We also propose a new greatest-common-divisor (GCD)approximation for time-efficiently approximating the metric value of a CMVC without a posteriori tests. The CMVCs undetectable to the GCD-approximation under multi- and single-weighted TGs have minimum lengths of 8 and 10, respectively. It can be regarded as a tradeoff between the QC-PEGA and MM-QC-PEGA, as it has both moderate ability to detect small CMVCs and moderate computational complexity.
Moreover, we propose a new masking technique, called PEG-masking, based on the MM-QC-PEGA. The PEG-masking has high flexibility and low computational complexity, and is thus considered better than the existing masking techniques in [3]–[10]. Compared to several existing works, our proposed algorithms could perform better or comparably in terms of avoiding generating small cycles and error performances. It is worth mentioning that our proposed algorithms somewhat perfect the works of QC-LDPC code construction.

• The MM-QC-PEGA is proposed for constructing QC-LDPC codes.
• The GCD-approximation is proposed for time-efficiently approximating the metric value of a CMVC, and the G-MM-QC-PEGA is proposed based on the GCDapproximation.
• The PEG-masking is proposed, which has high flexibility and low computational complexity.

II. PRELIMINARIES, NOTATIONS, AND BACKGROUND
A. Graph
A graph is denoted as G = (V, E), with V the node set and E the edge set. For two arbitrary nodes u0, u1 ∈ V , the node pair (u0, u1) is called an edge, and u0 and u1 are called endpoints of (u0, u1). More specifically, if (u0, u1) ∈ E, (u0, u1) is an (real) edge in G; otherwise, (u0, u1) is a virtual

edge in G. A path of length L connecting nodes u0 and u L is denoted as a sequence of L + 1 nodes: u0 u1 · · · u L , where (ui , ui+1) ∈ E for 0 ≤ i < L. This path is called simple if ui = u j and u j = ut for 0 ≤ i < j < t ≤ L. (In this paper,
only simple paths are considered.) Moreover, this simple path
forms a length-L cycle if u0 = u L. For two arbitrary nodes u0, u1 ∈ V , the distance between u0 and u1 is defined as the
length of any shortest path connecting them. If u0 and u1 are not connected by any path, their distance is defined as ∞.

C. Circulant and QC-LDPC Code

A (binary) circulant is a square matrix where each row-

vector is cyclically shifted one element to the right relative

to the preceding row-vector. (The first row-vector’s preceding

row-vector is the last row-vector.) The number of nonzero

entries in a row (or column) is regarded as the circulant’s

weight. If a circulant has weight w, the circulant is called

w-weighted. Moreover, if w = 0, the circulant is called a zero

circulant; if w = 1, the circulant is called single-weighted;

and if w > 1, the circulant is called multi-weighted.

E. ACE Spectrum and VNLGD
The approximate cycle extrinsic message degree (ACE) [35] value of a path is defined as j (dv j − 2), where dv j is the degree of the j -th VN of this path, and the summation is taken over all VNs of this path. The ACE spectrum [36] of depth dmax of a TG G is defined as a dmax -tuple η(G) = (η2, η4, . . . , η2dmax ), where η2i , 1 ≤ i ≤ dmax is the minimum ACE value of all 2i -cycles in G. In addition, η2i = ∞ if G has no 2i -cycles. The comparison rule between two ACE spectra of depth dmax , say η(1) and η(2) (TGs are omitted), in [32] is defined as
η(1) > η(2) ⇐⇒ ∃ j ≤ dmax η2(1j) > η2(2j) and η2(1i ) = η2(2i ), 1 ≤ i < j .

The VN-local-girth distribution (VNLGD) [31] of a TG is defined as a polynomial φ(x ) = i>0 pi x i , where pi refers to the fraction of VNs with local girths i . If no VNs’ local girths are i , pi is considered as 0, and pi xi is thus omitted from φ(x). The comparison rule between two VNLGDs, say φ(1)(x ) and φ(2)(x ), in [31] is defined as
φ(1)(x ) < φ(2)(x ) ⇐⇒ ∃ j > 0 p(j1) < p(j2) and pi(1) = pi(2), i < j .

V. PEG-MASKING
Consider the H in (1), and let Z = [zi, j ]0≤i<J,0≤ j<K be a J × K binary matrix. Masking H by using Z leads to M = [zi, j Hi, j ]0≤i<J,0≤ j <K , where zi, j Hi, j is Hi, j if zi, j = 1 and an N × N zero matrix if zi, j = 0. Obviously, M’s height, width, and circulant size are equal to H’s, and the “1” entries (edges) in M are a subset of those in H. Given H, the conventional masking techniques in [3]–[10] focus on constructing Z to improve M’s performance. In fact, we can use the MM-QC-PEGA to directly construct M, if the input parameters of Algorithm 1 are properly set and the edges that could be possibly constructed are restricted to those in H. This idea leads to the PEG-masking.
Specifically, the PEG-masking contains two steps: 1) Regarding the input parameters of Algorithm 1, input H, then initialize m, n, N as the corresponding ones of H, and initialize D as any desired and valid6 VN-degree sequence. 2) Restrict the resulting edges as a subset of H’s by changing the condition in line 3 of Algorithm 2 to “(ci , vc) ∈/ Et and (ci , vc) exists in the corresponding TG of H”. In this case, Algorithm 1 works as the PEG-masking, and the output is the corresponding TG of M.

The PEGmasking can be generalized, without too much effort, for masking a base PCM that consists of an array of permutation matrices of the same size, such as those PCMs derived from geometry decomposition [5].

VII. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we have proposed the MM-QC-PEGA, which is suitable for constructing both single- and multi-weighted QC-LDPC codes with arbitrary lengths, rates, circulant sizes, and VN-degree distributions. The MM-QC-PEGA is able

to detect all CMVCs, as it computes the metric value of each CMVC with the a posteriori test. In addition, we have proposed the GCD-approximation for time-efficiently approximating the metric value of a CMVC without a posteriori tests, and have proposed the G-MM-QC-PEGA by using the GCD-approximation to replace the a posteriori test involved in the MM-QC-PEGA. Regarding the comparison in Table I, the G-MM-QC-PEGA can be regarded as a tradeoff between the MM-QC-PEGA and QC-PEGA [23]. Moreover, our proposed PEG-masking has high flexibility, low computational complexity, and good performances in terms of both cycle-structure and error rate, and is thus considered better than the existing masking techniques in [3]–[10].
The simulations indicate three things: 1) The G-MM-QCPEGA is a very good approximation of the MM-QC-PEGA, and both of them can outperform the QC-PEGA in terms of both cycle-structure and error performance. 2) The SWR seems unnecessary for both MM-QC-PEGA and G-MM-QCPEGA. 3) Increasing the edge-trial of both MM-QC-PEGA and G-MM-QC-PEGA positively affects the cycle-structure and the error performance of resulting QC-LDPC code. It is worth mentioning that our proposed algorithms somewhat perfect the works of QC-LDPC code construction.
