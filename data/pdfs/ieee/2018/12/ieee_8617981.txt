Hardware design of Euclidean Projection modules for ADMM LDPC decoding
Hayfa BEN THAMEUR1, Bertrand LE GAL2, Nadia KHOUJA1, Fethi TLILI1, Christophe JEGO2 1 SUPCOM, GRESCOM Lab, University of Carthage, Tunisia.
2 Bordeaux INP, CNRS IMS, UMR 5218, University of Bordeaux, France.

Abstract—The ADMM decoding is a novel LP-based algorithm that can improve the decoding performances of the conventional BP decoding algorithms for LDPC codes. However, it was shown that the Euclidean projection (EP) computation involved in the ADMM algorithm limits the throughput performances of the ADMM hardware decoders and drastically increases their silicon cost. Recently, different techniques have been proposed in the literature at the algorithmic level to improve the hardware characteristics of the EP module without penalizing the error correction performances of the ADMM decoder. In this article, we present the implementation results of optimized hardware design of these theoretical approaches. Then, an evaluation of their real interest for high throughput and low complexity ADMM LDPC decoder design is provided.
I. INTRODUCTION
The LDPC decoding has gained a great attention in the error correcting code (ECC) area in the last two decades. In 2003, Feldman proposed a new decoding technique, based on linear programming (LP) [1], as an alternative to the beliefpropagation (BP) approach to extend the use of LDPC codes for high reliability applications, such as magnetic recording and fiber-optic transport networks [2]. However, the first formulation of the LP decoding problem has been shown to be neither computationally competitive with BP nor compatible for hardware implementation. A first attempt to overcome such issues is to apply the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) to the initial LP problem. In fact, the ADMM is a large scale decomposition technique known for its distributed nature, its scalability and its strong convergence guarantees [2]. Hence, an efficient iterative message passing LP decoding algorithm based on the ADMM has been stated in [2]. Later, several algorithmic improvements have been proposed in literature to enhance the error correction performances of the ADMM algorithm [3] [4].
From a commercial and industrial standpoint, an efficient hardware implementation of the ADMM decoder in terms of hardware complexity and throughput is a crucial step towards its practical application for communication systems adopting LDPC codes. Yet, the main technical bottleneck is the development of a hardware-compatible EP module.
The first hardware-friendly formulation of such an intricate operation was introduced in [5]. It takes advantage of the preceding algorithmic simplifications of the EPs propounded in [2], [6], [7]. However, a data sorting operation is still needed to perform the EP. In [8], software implementation showed that the EP consumes up to 60% of the check node execution time

and it is dominated by the sorting function. Such experimental results have motivated the EEC community, interested in ADMM decoding for LDPCs, to develop hardware oriented approximations of the EP algorithm of [5].
Recently, a first description of the hardware design of the ADMM decoder based on the flooding [9] and the horizontal layered schedules [10], [11], using the EP of [5] and targeting FPGA circuits, was provided. With the presented synthesis results in these works, authors have attested the decisive impact of the EP on lowering the throughput of the ADMM decoders while increasing its hardware resources’ consumption, compared to BP decoders.
Lately, the complexity of the EP was challenged by means of two reformulations of the exact EP of [5] (EEP): the iterative EP (IEP) [12] and the LUT-based EP (LEP) [4]. Both EPs aim at approximating the EEP by estimating the difference coefficient [12] or storing the output of projecting the quantized input vector to the EP in LUTs [4]. According to original works, the proposed EP algorithms seem to tradeoff the hardware cost and the error correction performances of the experimented ADMM decoders. Yet, only theoretical evaluation has been afforded. Therefore, this study is the first step in asserting whether or not the IEP and the LEP are competitive alternatives to the EEP [5] for real time and low complexity hardware ADMM decoder implementations.
In this paper, three main contributions are reported. First, the hardware design of both the IEP and the LEP is described in Section II while briefly reviewing the ADMM decoding problem and the EEP. Second, three implementation architectures of each of the three EPs are presented. They aim to improve the studied EP’s hardware features in terms of latency. Finally, synthesis results are detailed in Section IV where the hardware performances of the EPs are further discussed.
II. PROPOSED HARDWARE MODELS
A. ADMM decoding and the EEP
At the core of the LP decoding problem of LDPCs is the fundamental polytope which corresponds to the intersection of a group of small check polytopes PPd where d is the check node degree [1]. In fact, PPd is the convex hull of all the length-d binary vectors with any even number of ones. With the introduction of the ADMM technique, an euclidean projection onto the PPd is indispensable to maintain the theoretical guarantees of the LP and its viability for efficiently decoding large-scale LDPC codes [2]. A detailed description of the ADMM-LP problem is provided in [2].

The implementation schematic of the IEP is depicted in Figure 3. The starting task is to determine the facet of the PPd in which the projection point lies, using the cut search algorithm [6]. This step is accomplished by the same FID and ST modules of the EEP with minor changes. First, for the ST block, the component-wise subtraction of v from 1 is removed. Second, the indicator vector f lies in {−1, 1}d. The following task is to find the EP output once the hypercube projection of v is outside the PPd. This job is performed by the DCE module which implements the second part of Alg. 1 (Lines 8 : 12) in [12]. The algorithm has an iterative execution path whose steps can not be straightforwardly fully parallelized. It is composed of three processes: shifting the vector v, its projection onto the unit hypercube [0, 1] (UHP module) and the computation of the projection coefficient η. The summation of the UHP’s output is fulfilled using a minimum-depth adder tree [9].
Regarding the EEP, although no sorting operation is required, the iterative nature of the DCE processing unit would certainly penalize the latency of the IEP approach.

III. PROPOSED HARDWARE ARCHITECTURES
The structure of the ADMM LDPC decoders bear resemblance to the one of BP LDPC decoders. Indeed, it was shown in [9]–[11] that for the flooding or the layered-based schedule, the decoders are always composed of: memory units for storing variable nodes and intermediate messages, processing units (PU) and an interleaver. Only, the linking network and the PUs’ internal computations change. Consequently, the BP LDPC decoding parallelization schemes are still viable for ADMM decoders: (a) it is possible to process one message per clock cycle per PU as in [14] or (b) to process dc messages per clock cycle per PU to increase the throughput as in [15].
Especially, for the EP module, its hardware features are potentially depending on the selected implementation scheme of the LDPC decoder. To provide a fair evaluation of the studied EP approaches, in this work, we consider the design of: (a) a low-cost implementation that is adapted to semi-parallel decoders and (b) a high-throughput implementations that fulfill fully-parallel decoder needs.

IV. SYNTHESIS RESULTS
For hardware design of the studied EPs, implementation cost depends heavily on the value of d, messages bit-width and EPs’ parameters. Thus, for fair comparison, we consider the fixed-point representation of [9], d ∈ {6, 16}, frequency f ∈ {100, 200} MHz, L = 10 [4] for the LEP and Imax = 30 [12] for the IEP. In addition, the usage of FPGA DSP and SRL blocks has been disabled for an easier and fairer comparison.

In fact, for all EPs, their best performances in terms of latency and frequency are obtained by the A3 at the cost of high logic resources (LR) consumption. Such observation is mainly due to (a) the memory partitioning for the LEP to allow simultaneous d memory read/write (b) parallelizing EPs internal computations and (c) pipelining operations when data dependencies exist (especially inside the DCE module of the IEP). Meanwhile, the lowest LR cost is exhibited by A1 at the expense of slower EP modules. Indeed, up to 0.57% of available slice components are consumed by the EPs (A1) with respect to 5.7% when implementing the IEP with A3. Yet, with A1, for the LEP (EEP), the number of elapsed clock cycles is 4.4× (3.1×) greater than the one with A3. Moreover, the synthesis statistics for the A2 is relatively close to A3 except for the latency metric. Although, it is minimized by a factor of 2.1 for the EEP and LEP, the iterative behavior of the IEP still limits its latency improvement. Besides, regardless of the selected architecture, the employed LRs, for the EEP and LEP, do not exceed 1% of available resources on the FPGA device.

Clearly, the IEPs yield the worst hardware performances that are basically determined by the value of Imax which is a code-dependent parameter. Regarding EEPs, the lowest latency is 9 achieved at 157 MHz. Although, they consume up to 5 (15) times more LUTs (flip flops) than LEPs, the mean square error (MSE) [13] between LEP and EEP outputs is important. Indeed, we have performed the projection of 109 input vectors with the LEP and the EEP. An MSE equals to 92.8 was obtained. It can be reduced by significantly raising the value of L which entails the enlargement of the LEP memory and the increase of the the search algorithm complexity as well [4]. Meanwhile, EEPs consume up to 6 (8) times less LUTs (flip flops) than IEPs which afford an MSE of 0.13.
The above observations can be also concluded for f =200 MHz, where EPs latency performances are roughly doubled. This is due to the raise of pipeline stages. For LEPs and IEPs, LR consumption is almost 1.2× higher than the case of f =100 MHz. Yet, for EEPs, minor LR evolutions are detected.
From Table I, similar tendencies of implementation performances can be observed for d = 16. LR utilization is almost 3 times higher than the case of d = 6. Also, with A1 and A3 architectures, the gap of latency between the EEP and the LEP is further enhanced compared to the case of d = 6. This can be explained by the fact of adopting the degree-8 decomposition approach when implementing the LEP. Thus, the EP onto PP16 is performed by using twice the projection onto PP8 plus one EP onto PP4 which results in spending more clock cycles to handle the layered execution of the intermediate EPs while reducing the memory cost by 99%. However, it needs at least 24× BRAMs higher than the EEP and the IEP.
A key challenge in implementing ADMM hardware decoders is the scalability of the EP module. Unlike the EEP, the LEP and the IEP are highly constrained EPs. As stated in their related works, both the LEP and the IEP parameters are code and ADMM objective function dependent, which hinders their capability to support different LDPC codes. According to the implementation results, the EEP seems to dynamically trade latency for hardware resources consumption which promotes its convenience for designing ADMM LDPC decoders that exhibit competitive performances to the BP decoders. Furthermore, the proposed EEP is a hardware optimized version of the EEP designed in [9]. Indeed, synthesis results on the same target as in [9] show that the proposed EEP yields a latency of 17 at a working frequency of 235 MHz while using 3914 ALMs.

V. CONCLUSION
The euclidean projection is the principal bottleneck of hardware implementation of ADMM LDPC decoders. In this paper, the first implementations of the LEP [4] and the IEP [12] approaches and an optimized implementation of the EEP [5] were carried out. Three different setups were applied for the studied EPs for a fair comparison. Experimental results have demonstrated that approximate EP techniques, recently introduced at the algorithmic level, are not as interesting as the exact EP [5] in terms of latency, throughput and scalability. Thereby, they can be considered as nonviable for real-time hardware implementation. Finally, the developed optimized EP implementation of [5] yields lower latency than the one designed in [9]. It should be able to double the throughput of the ADMM LDPC decoder described in [9].
