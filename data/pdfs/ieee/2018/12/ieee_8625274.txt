High data rate and flexible hardware QC-LDPC decoder for satellite optical communications

Vincent Pignoly, Bertrand Le Gal, Christophe Jego CNRS IMS Laboratory, Bordeaux INP, University of Bordeaux, France

AbstractŌĆöSatellite optical communications are trendy for future satellite wireless communications and introduce new challenges concerning throughput and error correction for the channel decoder. This paper details a custom solution based on LDPC code and architecture co-design. This solution was designed to deliver an efficient trade-off in terms of decoder throughput and error correction performance. A flexible architecture that implements an Offset Min-Sum algorithm on FieldProgrammable Gate Array (FPGA) is proposed. A resulting system composed of 5 elementary LDPC decoders that fit in a single Zynq Ultrascale+ FPGA device reaches more than 10 Gbps with error correction performance similar or better than present current satellite communication standards.
I. INTRODUCTION
Introduced by R. Gallager in 1969 [1], Low-Density ParityCheck codes are a popular class of valuable linear block codes. This forward error correction can reach performance near the Shannon limit about channel capacity. It is the reason why LDPC codes have been chosen in many wireless communications standards like WiMAX, Wifi and also in satellite communication standards such as DVB-S2 [2], DVB-S2x and in CCSDS. The present popularity of optical wireless communications for satellites, with high throughput expectations (up to 10 Gbps), increases the need of efficient solutions providing LDPC codes with good performance and also high efficiency hardware decoder architectures.
In order to enable the design of high throughput LDPC decoders (Ōēź 1 Gbps), previous standards have focused on quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes. QC-LDPC codes [3] form a sub-class of structured codes that should achieve performance close to unstructured LDPC codes [4] but with a structure that facilitates the decoder implementation. QC-LDPC structure is convenient for parallel decoder architectures. Indeed, it provides at least a constant parallelism for computation and memory access. Furthermore, encoding QC-LDPC is easy and low complexity fast encoder have been designed [5]. However, obtaining QC-LDPC codes that provides at the same time fast convergence, low error floor and interesting features for hardware implementation (e.g. regularity and large expansion factor) is very challenging. For instance, in DVB-S2 standard, to achieve all these properties, a BCH decoder was added after the LDPC decoder to delay the error floor. For current satellite communications, Jet Propulsion Laboratory designed a long frame (64k bits) DVB-S2 decoder [6] that fills 5 FPGA,

one of which is dedicated to the BCH decoder. This design achieves a data rate of 1.25 Gbps. However, new space optical communications expect higher throughput and there comes the need of innovative methods to reach more than 10 Gbps. So the proposed contribution consists in a codesign of QCLDPC codes and the associated decoder architecture to face this challenge. Because of the application, LDPC codes from DVB-S2 standard is chosen as the main point of comparison.
Nowadays, construction of efficient LDPC codes and particularly QC-LDPC codes is still a subject of research [7], [8], mainly on the code structure. For space applications, some methods have been proven to be very effective. First, there is Irregular Repeat Accumulate (IRA) codes constructed by Progressive-Edge-Growth (PEG) algorithm [9] used with Approximated Cycle Extrinsic (ACE) constraint [9]. Then, Accumulate Repeat Jagged Accumulate (ARJA) [9] is another family of LDPC codes that have been chosen in the CCSDS standard. Finally Finite Geometry (FG) can be used for algebraic LDPC code construction [9]. These methods have been used to design the proposed codes.
LDPC codes are decoded by message-passing iterative algorithms. The Min-Sum algorithm [10] is one of them and achieves good performance close to optimal Belief Propagation (BP) algorithm but with a lower computational complexity tackling the check node processing complexity by replacing hyperbolic tangents with summation and minimum functions [10]. Offset Min-Sum (OMS) and Normalized MinSum (NMS) are alternatives that bring error correction closer to optimal against a tiny increase of computational complexity. It is the reason why they are adopted in many hardware decoder implementations [11], [12].
The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. In section 2, notations and OMS algorithm are introduced. Then, section 3 presents different QC-LDPC codes compared to DVB-S2 standard. In section 4, the hardware architecture dedicated to OMS algorithm for the selected codes is detailed. Finally, section 5 summarizes the experimental results and compares them with previous works.

A. Notations

LDPC codes can be illustrated by a Tanner graph [13] with N variable nodes (VN) and M check nodes (CN). N (j) denotes the set of VNs associated to CNj and M (i) denotes the set of CNs linked to VNi.The number of connections for a node is called the degree and is denoted dv(i) for VNi and dc(j) for CNj. If dv and dc are constant for all nodes, the LDPC code is regular. In the particular case of QC-LDPC, codes have a structure composed of (Z, Z) sub-matrix Sk,l. In most cases, each sub-matrix is either an identity matrix shifted by a number between 0 and Z ŌłÆ 1 or a matrix filled with zeros. However, identity matrices could replace other kinds of submatrices such as in DVB-S2 standard [2]. But, it results in more complex decoding due to data dependencies among CNs.
B. Decoding algorithm
There are different techniques to decode LDPC codes at receiver side. Sum-Product algorithm (SPA) [9] or LP-based one [14] are the most efficient approaches in terms of BER and FER performance. However, their computational complexity discards them from hardware decoder implementation. Indeed, computation complexity variants were proposed to this purpose e.g. Offset Min-Sum or Normalized Min-Sum. These variants drastically reduce the computational complexity at the cost of a slightly error correction performance loss, especially when LDPC codes are regular. To reach at the same time low computational decoding complexity and high throughput performance, hardware decoders have to exploit a layered based scheduling of the decoding process. Contrary to the common flooding based formulation of the decoding process, layered based decoding enables to twice reduce the number of decoding iterations at equivalent error correction performance and also reduce by about 30% the memory required by the decoder [15]. However, this layered approach limits the maximum computation parallelism during the decoding process and consequently the data rate.
The horizontal layered algorithm with offset Min-Sum approximation is given in [16]. Log-likelihood ratios (LLR) are the inputs and thus the initial values in variable nodes Ti(0). Ti(jt) and Ej(ti) are messages from VNi to CNj and CNj to VNi respectively. ╬Ę is the offset used in the OMS to improve the accuracy of the extrinsic messages. This decoding algorithm has been chosen for its low complexity in terms of operations comparing to BP or other min-sum variants (e.g. 3-min [17]). Indeed, as demonstrated in the experimentation section, the logical element usage is the main bottleneck of FPGA-based implementation.
III. EFFICIENT QC-LDPC CODES
A. Decoder design friendly QC-LDPC codes
On one hand, high throughput applications in space optical and wireless communications require a LDPC code with a powerful error correction capacity with a given rate R including very low error floor. Space optical communications deal with code rate greater than 0.7 so this study is focused on codes with rates between 0.72 and 0.82. On the other hand,

codes need to be decoder design friendly. QC-LDPC are often chosen to achieve this. First, thanks to the regular structure of QC-LDPC codes, the decoding process can be efficiently parallelized and a memory mapping without memory access conflict can always be found. The higher the expansion factor of the code Z is, the higher the throughput should be at the cost of more computational complexity. Considering Z = 256 for instance, 256 VN to CN messages can be sent to 256 CN processing units. In an optimal case, the decoding throughput of a layered based decoder can be approximate by :

An expansion factor of Z = 256 is often a higher bound in QC-LDPC code design. In fact, the hardware implementation cost (e.g. the barrel shifter cost) grows non linearly. Furthermore with higher values of Z, dependencies between the CNs increase so computation parallelism is reduced, introducing idle cycles. Other LDPC code parameters can also impact the hardware decoder efficiency e.g. the density of the matrix. This directly affects the throughput because the number of operations increases proportionally with the code density. From a hardware point of view, decreasing the number of iterations allows to increase the throughput at the drawback of performance cost. Actually, the impact of the iteration number is also a major bottleneck. Finally, the matrix regularity is an valuable characteristic for decoder design. From an architecture point of view, a constant degree dc enables an optimal processing units usage.
All this decoder design friendly attributes compel the LDPC code definition. In some cases, LDPC codes need to be adapted to reach these attributes at the expense of performance.
B. Selection of different LDPC codes
In order to define LDPC codes with fast convergence and low error floor, different families of LDPC codes where investigated : IRA, ARJA and FG codes. They are compared with LDPC codes from DVB-S2. Different parameters were considered to obtain QC-LDPC codes that present a useful trade-off between BER/FER performance and decoder design friendliness. A. ASIP architectural model
The ASIP architectural model of the decoder is detailed in Figure 3. It is composed of a controller, a VN memory, an interleaver, a CnToVn message memory, Z processing units (PU) and the IO interface like most of related work architectures [12]. As mentioned earlier, to optimize the hardware implementation, the architecture is dedicated to QC-LDPC codes. A number of PU equal to the expansion factor Z is fixed. Each PU includes dedicated processing logic and is connected to the message memory to store Eij messages. The interconnection network enables to route message between the variable nodes and the check nodes through a single interleaver. Indeed, such as in [12], the Z VN elements move through VN memory during the decoding, discarding the requirement of a de-interleaver. The interleaver is implemented thanks to a barrel shifter, whereas two Benes networks were used in [16]. This evolution represents the major complexity fall-down. Finally, an interface manages input and output data. This architectural model is flexible and can be applied to all QC-LDPC codes. It is fully efficient for LDPC codes when Z = 2z with z Ōłł N because of the interleaver structure.
Two successive blocks compose a PU as shown in [16]. The first one is dedicated to parity check processing and the second one updates LLR. This two steps can work in a pipeline mode if independent CN computations are available during the data processing.
The parity check processing with OMS algorithm is done in the first block. The computation of the parity check node Ej(t+1) needs dc(j) clock cycles. The second block updates the LLR values of variable nodes and also needs dc(j) clock cycles with one cycle for each LLR.

At this point, the regularity of the LDPC code is necessary to avoid the insertion of idle clock cycles during the decoding process to manage dc switching.
Furthermore, two main memories are necessary in the ASIP architecture with one for VN values and another one to store messages from CN to VN. During the decoding process, data are exchanged in packs of length Z. For this reason, the memories are assigned to RAMs of width nBits ŌłŚZ. It enables to access all necessary data in one single clock cycle.
The control signals for the overall decoder elements are generated thanks to an instruction set controller. At each clock cycle, the controller sends signals to control the decoder thanks to a set of 15 custom instructions. The instruction set and the datapath have been tailored around the addressed application. The different instructions are stored in a ROM and translated into signals that are sent to each block. Thus, each hardware resource can be activated independently. This approach provides flexibility and low-recurrent costs when a novel LDPC decoder has to be designed.
B. Flexibility and reuse possibilities thanks to the proposed automated design flow
A strength of the proposed decoder architecture is its flexibility. It can be used for all QC-LDPC codes. The design flow has been automated [16]. The synthesis process generates the instruction for decoder controller according to the H matrix. Moreover, this process reorders the CN computations to benefits from CN computation parallelism during the decoding process. However, CN dependency can be critical and idle processing cycles are necessary. The generation process can be configured in different ways e.g. the number of processing units, the data formats, or the number of frames to decode in parallel. Indeed, in many cases, when Z is high, a low number of CN set (M/Z) limits the computation parallelism at runtime due to data dependencies. To reach high usage rate of the decoding architecture, the processing of multiple frames in parallel becomes an efficient solution: (a) this technique reuses the processing resources without hardware over cost, and (b)

VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, an ultra high throughput QC-LDPC decoder is proposed. It was co-designed with an exploration of this kind of LDPC codes while keeping hardware efficiency in mind. The proposed architecture exploits as much as possible the structure of QC-LDPC codes. Main strengths of this architecture are its high throughput, its important flexibility and its error correction performance with the proposed designed LDPC codes. Its implementation can reach more than 10 Gbps with a latency of 30 ┬Ąs on a single FPGA device. One can note that the proposed architecture is very competitive considering the state of art. Finally, the exploration of QC-LDPC codes shows that selected codes present error correction performance similar and sometimes better than current space communications standards. The proposed trade-off is compliant with the needs of future space optical communications.
