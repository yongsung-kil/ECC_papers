Computing the Asymptotic Expected Multiplicity of Leafless Elementary Trapping Sets (LETSs) in Random Irregular LDPC Code Ensembles

Ali Dehghan and Amir H. Banihashemi,
Department of Systems and Computer Engineering, Carleton University, Ottawa, Ontario, Canada

Abstract—Leafless elementary trapping sets (LETSs) are known to be among the most harmful substructures of Tanner graphs of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes in the error floor region. It is well-known that in the asymptotic regime of infinite block length, the only LETS structures that have finite non-zero average multiplicity are those that contain a single (simple) cycle. The average multiplicity of all the other LETSs, whose structures contain more than one cycle, tends to zero asymptotically. The asymptotic average multiplicity of LETS structures with a single cycle for variable-regular LDPC codes has already been derived by the authors in a previous work. In this paper, we compute the asymptotic average multiplicity of such structures for irregular LDPC code ensembles. The derivation involves counting the average number of cycles of a specific length with a specific combination of variable node degrees. We demonstrate that the asymptotic results, derived in this paper, match well with the LETS multiplicities of finitelength LDPC codes chosen randomly from the ensemble, even for rather short block lengths.

I. INTRODUCTION

The performance of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes under iterative message-passing algorithms is dependent on the structure of the code’s Tanner graph, in general, and the distribution of short cycles, in particular, see, e.g., [1]. The cycles have a particularly important role in the error floor performance of LDPC codes, where they form the main substructure of the trapping sets [2]. Counting cycles of a given length in a Tanner graph is, however, known to be NP-hard [3]. It is thus of interest to obtain some knowledge of the distribution of short cycles in a Tanner graph, including the expected values. This can help in the analysis and in guiding the design of LDPC codes. The expected value can also be used as an approximation for the number of cycles of a given length in a given graph in the ensemble. Recently, the cycle structure of random regular and irregular Tanner graphs was studied in [4]. In particular, it was shown in [4] that the asymptotic expected value of the number of cycles of length c in irregular graphs with variable node degrees {di}ni=1 and check node degrees {di}ni=1, as the size of the graph tends to infinity, is approximated by

The other graphical structures which play a more direct role in the performance of LDPC codes in the error floor region are trapping sets. Trapping sets are often classified based on their number of variable nodes a and the number of odddegree check nodes b, in their subgraph. A trapping set with a variable nodes and b odd-degree check nodes is said to belong to the (a, b) class. It is well-known that among trapping sets, a particular category, called leafless elementary trapping sets (LETSs) is often among the most harmful, see, e.g., [2], [5], and the references therein. The knowledge of LETSs is thus helpful in the analysis of the error floor of LDPC codes and in the design of LDPC codes with low error floor. Finding the LETSs of an LDPC code is, in general, NP-hard [6], [7]. In [8], it was proved that, as the size of the Tanner graph tends to infinity, among LETSs of constant size, only those that contain a single (simple) cycle have a non-zero average multiplicity. For variable-regular Tanner graphs with variable degree dv, for a given a, the only class of LETSs that satisfies this condition is the (a, (dv −2)a) class. In [8], the result of [4] on the asymptotic average multiplicity of cycles of length 2a in variable-regular Tanner graphs was used to estimate the average multiplicity of LETSs in this class.
For irregular LDPC codes, however, for a given a, simple cycles of size 2a can correspond to (a, b) LETS classes with different b values, depending on the combination of the degrees of the a variable nodes that are in the cycle. It is easy to see that the range of b values, for a given a, is a(dvmin − 2) ≤ b ≤ a(dvmax − 2), where dvmin and dvmax are the minimum and the maximum variable node degrees, respectively. In this paper, we compute the asymptotic expected number of cycles of different (fixed) lengths with different combinations of variable node degrees in irregular Tanner graph (LDPC code) ensembles. We then use this result to derive the asymptotic expected number of LETSs in all the classes with non-zero multiplicity. Numerical results are also presented to demonstrate that the asymptotic theoretical results provide good estimates for the multiplicity of LETSs of finite-length codes, even at short block lengths.

Proof. First, we introduce the method that we use to construct a random Tanner graph. The method is the same as the one used in [4]. Suppose that the variable nodes are labeled as {u1, u2, . . . , un}, and check nodes as {w1, w2, . . . , wn }. For each node z with degree d(z), we assign a bin that contains d(z) cells, and consider a random perfect matching (bijection) to pair the cells on one side of the graph to the cells on the other side. The bipartite graphs constructed by connecting any two paired cells with an edge are called configurations. There are η! configurations, where η is the number of edges in the graph. In the rest of the proof, we assume that configurations are selected uniformly at random. From each configuration, we construct a Tanner graph such that if there is an edge between two cells, then we place an edge between the corresponding nodes (bins) in the Tanner graph. The resulted Tanner graphs can thus be considered as images of the configurations that are obtained from the random perfect matchings. Following the notation in [4], we denote the ensemble of bipartite graphs so constructed by G∗, and note that the graphs in G∗ can have parallel edges. We also note that a uniform distribution over the configurations induces a non-uniform distribution over G∗. The next step in the construction of G is to remove all the graphs with parallel edges from G∗. It is now easy to see that a uniform distribution over the configurations, with the condition that images with parallel edges are rejected, induces a uniform distribution over G. Based on the results of [8], we know that the only LETS structures that have finite non-zero multiplicity asymptotically are those with only a simple cycle. In Theorem 2, we derived an asymptotic approximation for the expected number of such cycles with different lengths and with different combination of variable degrees. (Note that the result of Theorem 2 is asymptotically valid for simple cycles since the asymptotic average multiplicity of any cycle which is not simple tends to zero.) The following proposition, whose proof is straight forward, along with the results of Theorem 2 can be used to estimate the asymptotic multiplicity of LETS structures in different classes for irregular codes.
Proposition 1. Consider an ensemble G of irregular LDPC codes, and suppose that constants a and b are such that the asymptotic expected multiplicity of (a, b) LETSs in G is a finite non-zero value. Then, the asymptotic average multiplicity of (a, b) LETSs in G is equal to the asymptotic expected number of simple cycles of length 2a in G whose variable node degrees add up to 2a + b.
The following example explains how the combination of Theorem 2 and Proposition 1 can be used to estimate the multiplicity of LETS structures in different classes.
Example 1. Consider the ensemble of irregular LDPC codes with degree distributions λ(x) = λixi−1 +λjxj−1 and ρ(x) = xk−1, where i ≥ 2, j > i and k > 2 are constant integers. For any fixed value of a ≥ 2, any class of (a, b) LETSs with the value of b in the range a × (i − 2) ≤ b ≤ a × (j − 2) has a non-zero finite expected value asymptotically. For each such class, with given a and b values, we can uniquely determine the number of variable nodes of each degree, i and j, that exist in simple cycles belonging to the (a, b) LETS class. For this, one needs to solve the following system of linear equations: iαi + jαj = 2a + b and αi + αj = a, to find the number of variable nodes of degrees i and j, αi and αj, in the cycle.
