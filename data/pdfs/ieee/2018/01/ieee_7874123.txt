On the Construction of LDPC Codes Free of Small Trapping Sets by Controlling Cycles
Xiongfei Tao, Yufei Li, Yonghe Liu, and Zuoqi Hu

Abstract— Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes exhibit excellent error correcting capability. However, small trapping sets in the Tanner graph are harmful to the iterative decoding algorithm. In this letter, we present a method of constructing (3, n) girth-eight quasi-cyclic LDPC codes with low error floor by removing the small trapping sets from the Tanner graph. To address this issue, we analyze the relationship between eightcycles and small trapping sets of Tanner graphs based on fully connected base graphs without parallel edges. We find that if some eight-cycles are not found in the Tanner graphs, any elementary trapping set in the range of a ≤ 8 and b ≤ 3 is removed naturally. We also derive a lower bound on the permutation size for the construction of such codes. The experimental simulation shows a favorable error rate performance with lower error floor over additive white Gaussian noise channels.

To help analyze and design LDPC codes, many algorithms are proposed to determine the small trapping sets of an LDPC code, such as those in [6], [7]. Nguyen et al. [8] established a database of elementary trapping sets (ETSs) called the trapping set ontology (TSO). They also presented a construction of LDPC codes without small ETSs based on the TSO.

We analyze the relationship between the 8-cycles and small ETSs and show that if certain 8-cycles are removed from the Tanner graph of LDPC codes, small (a, b) ETSs with a ≤ 8 and b ≤ 3 can be eliminated naturally.

I. INTRODUCTION
A MONG the designs of structured low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes, the most widely known design is the protograph LDPC code [1]. Protograph LDPC codes are constructed using a lifting method from a smaller base graph, and the consequent LDPC codes acquire quasi-cyclic (QC) structures, also called QC-LDPC codes.
Sudden deterioration of the error rate performance may be encountered in the high signal-to-noise-ratio (SNR) region while decoding LDPC codes by iterative algorithms, which is called the error floor. The error floor is mainly attributed to the presence of small trapping sets. Many researchers directed their efforts toward reducing the error floor of LDPC codes. Zheng et al. [2] focused on addressing the error floor problem of irregular LDPC codes. Other efforts were aimed at avoiding small trapping sets to reduce the error floor of regular LDPC codes. Diouf et al. [3] improved the progressive-edge-growth (PEG) [4] algorithm to construct LDPC codes free of (5, 3) trapping sets and with a minimization of (6, 4) trapping sets. Asvadi et al. [5] proposed a lifting method to eliminate the constituent short cycles of LDPC codes; consequently, the dominant trapping sets were removed. The LDPC codes

An (a, b) trapping set is defined as a set of a variable nodes, b check nodes with odd-degrees in the subgraph induced by these variable nodes. A trapping set is regarded as an elementary trapping set (ETS) if all of its check nodes have either degree one or two. Nguyen et al. [8] found all possible small ETSs to establish a database called TSO and used line-point representations to illustrate ETSs to conveniently establish their topological relations. In order to differentiate among (a, b) ETSs with nonisomorphic line-point representations, the authors employed (a, b){i } to index each representations.
III. CONTROLLING THE EIGHT-CYCLES
In this line-point representation of trapping sets, variable nodes correspond to lines, and check nodes correspond to points. Moreover, the degree-2 check-nodes are denoted by “◦” and the degree-1 check-nodes by “•”. The LDPC codes investigated in this letter are lifted from a 3×n fully connected base matrix without parallel edges; thus, three check nodes of any variable node must be located on the three block rows, (i.e., u, v, and w), as shown in Fig. 1(a).
A. (5, 3) Elementary Trapping Sets
An 8-cycle has four check nodes on the three block rows. An 8-cycle has six possible combinations of the block row number of the four check nodes: {u, w, u, w}, {u, v, u, v}, {w, v, w, v}, {u, v, u, w}, {w, v, w, u} and {u, v, w, v}.
The study by [8] indicated that larger ETSs can be obtained by adjoining variable nodes to smaller ETSs, this process is called evolution. A girth-eight (3, n) LDPC code has only one form (5, 3) ETS, i.e., (5, 3){2} ETS, which is formed by adding one variable node to a (4, 4) ETS.
Fig. 1(b) represents an 8-cycle on two block rows {u, w, u, w}. The 8-cycle is also a (4, 4) ETS, and the check nodes of four degree-1 are on the same block row v. Given that the base graph has no parallel edges, no variables are connected to any two check nodes of c1, c2, c3, and c4, which means that no (5, 3) ETS is formed by adding one variable to the 8-cycle. Likewise, no (5, 3) ETS can be evolved from 8-cycles of {u, v, u, v} and {v, w, v, w}.

B. (7, 3) Elementary Trapping Sets
From [8], we know that the (7, 3) ETSs are direct successors of (5, 3) ETSs and (6, 4) ETSs. On the assumption that the 8-cycles of {v, u, v, w} are removed from the Tanner graph, no (5, 3) ETSs exist; thus, (7, 3) ETSs can only evolve from (6, 4) ETSs. The line-point representations of (6, 4) ETSs are illustrated in Fig. 2.
If the block row numbers of the four check nodes c1, c2, c3, and c4 on the cycle L1 of a (6, 4){1} ETS in Fig. 2(a) are w, u, v, and u, respectively, the block row numbers of the remaining check nodes in this (6, 4) trapping set can be distinctly determined. No parallel edges are found in the base
graph; thus, no variable nodes are connected to c5 and c8 or c6 and c7. With the assumption of girth eight, no variable nodes are connected to c5 and c7 or c6 and c8. However, a (7, 3) ETS will be obtained by adding a variable node connected to c5 and c6 or c7 and c8. If a variable node that is connected to either c5 and c6 or c7 and c8, an 8-cycle will be formed, and both of them have four check nodes with the block row number of {w, v, w, v}. To avoid the existence of (7, 3) ETSs evolving from the (6, 4) ETS in Fig. 2(a), 8-cycles whose check nodes have the block row number of {w, v, w, v} should be removed. Similarly, if the block row numbers of the four check nodes c1, c2, c3, and c4 on the cycle L1 in Fig. 2(a) are u, w, v and w, respectively, the 8-cycles whose check nodes have the block row number of {u, v, u, v} should be removed to prevent (7, 3) ETSs evolving from such (6, 4) ETSs. Now, we only have 8-cycles whose check nodes exist on {u, w, u, w}, {u, v, u, w}, and {w, v, w, u}; in other words, any 8-cycles with two check nodes on block row v are removed. In the following, we will illustrate that the (7, 3) ETSs are absent under this condition.
• If the block row numbers of the four check nodes c1, c2, c3, and c4 on the cycle L2 in Fig. 2(b) are u, w, u, and v, respectively, both check nodes c5 and c6 will be on block row v, obviously, regardless of the block row of c7, it will contradict the assumption of no two check nodes with block row v on a cycle.
• If the block row numbers of the four check nodes c1, c2, c3, and c4 on the cycle L3 in Fig. 2(c) are u, v, u, and w, respectively, c5 must be on block row u. Consequently, the four check nodes of degree-1 are all on block row v. Given that no parallel edges on the base graph, no variable nodes are connected to those check

V. CONCLUSION
In this letter, we proposed a method to construct LDPC codes free of small ETSs by controlling the 8-cycles. The LDPC codes constructed in this letter are lifted from a 3 × n fully connected base matrix. We investigated how the 8-cycles participate in small ETSs and found that for a block row v, if 8-cycles with two check nodes in block row v are absent in the Tanner graph, small (a, b) ETS with a ≤ 8 and b ≤ 3 can be avoided spontaneously. The construction is simple and efficient, providing a set of codes with lower error floor.
