Exploiting Locality for Improved Decoding of Binary Cyclic Codes
M. Nikhil Krishnan , Bhagyashree Puranik , Student Members, IEEE, P. Vijay Kumar, Fellow, IEEE, Itzhak Tamo, Member, IEEE, and Alexander Barg, Fellow, IEEE

Abstract— In this paper, we show how the presence of locality within a binary cyclic code can be exploited to improve decoding performance and to reduce decoding complexity. We pursue two approaches. Under the first approach, we show how the ordered statistics decoding (OSD) method can be modified by inserting a simple single round belief-propagation step at the start that involves only the local codes. The resultant locality-aware OSD algorithm yields an appreciable signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) gain for a given level of reliability and essentially the same level of decoder complexity. Under the second, trellis decoding approach, we show that the careful introduction of locality results in the creation of a cyclic subcode that possesses lower maximum state complexity. In addition, we present a simple means of deriving an upper bound to the state complexity profile of any cyclic code that is based only on the zeros of the code. Furthermore, we show how the decoding speed of either locality-aware OSD or trellis decoding can be significantly increased in the presence of locality, in the moderate-to-high SNR regime, by making the use of a quick-look decoder that often returns the maximum likelihood code word.
Index Terms— Locality, ordered statistics decoding, belief propagation, maximum likelihood decoding, trellis decoding.

One of the most developed research directions aimed at reducing the complexity of ML decoding has been trellis decoding and associated trellis representations of codes [2], [3]. Several schemes that approximate ML decoding have been suggested in the literature; among them such reliability-based decoding methods as the Chase algorithm [4], decoding based on ordered statistics [5], and others [6, Ch. 10].
At the same time, significant progress has been achieved in decoding particular code families based on the constraints imposed either by the parity-check matrix or other structural properties of the codes. Arguably the most far-reaching results along these lines have been accomplished for low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes and related families based on their iterative decoding [7]. Iterative decoding schemes have also been applied to other codes and a variety of other problems in information and communication theory.
The focus of this paper is a class of codes introduced recently by Gopalan et al. [8] that find application in distributed storage. The main idea of [8] stems from the need to minimize network communication between storage nodes and is formalized in the concept of locality wherein an erased code symbol can be repaired using a small subset of other symbols of the codeword, usually called the recovery set of the symbol. For this reason, these codes are alternately referred to as codes with locality or as locally recoverable codes (LRCs). In the context of linear codes, locality translates into the requirement that every coordinate of the codeword appear in a low-weight parity-check equation of the code. Thus, while we do not require that all the parities are of low weight, a linear LRC has a set of low-weight parities whose union contains all the code coordinates. This feature bridges the classes of LRCs and LDPC codes, and has been used in previous research [9], [10] to prove bounds on the rate of LRCs with a given minimum distance.
The described connection between LRCs and LDPC codes is the starting point of this research which develops in the context of Ordered Statistics Decoding (OSD). Introduced by Fossorier and Lin [5], OSD represents a way to decode binary linear block codes based on reliability of received symbols and provides a flexible trade-off between complexity and performance. The main idea of our work is to decode LRCs in two stages, beginning with one step of belief propagation (BP) decoding using local parities, and continuing with running OSD decoding on the entire code block. We also pursue another, related line of thought, namely the impact of locality on trellis decoding (trellis complexity) of linear codes.
Locality constraints introduced in [8] were generalized by Kamath et al. [14] to include stronger local codes and vector alphabets. They were further extended by Sasidharan et al. [15] who defined a hierarchical structure of several levels of locality constraints. At the same time, Wang and Zhang [16] suggested another generalization of LRCs wherein every coordinate has several independent (disjoint) recovery sets, much like orthogonal parities employed much earlier in threshold decoding of linear codes [17]. When we wish to emphasize the fact that our codes possess several recovery sets, we call them LRCs with availability. In this paper we observe that the decoding algorithm we are having in mind is fitted well to hierarchical LRCs and LRCs with availability, and therefore use them in our examples to quantify the improvements in decoding performance.
The main class of codes studied in this paper is cyclic LRCs. These codes were initially studied by Tamo et al. [18] and are derived from a more general family of Reed-Solomon-like LRCs defined earlier in [19]. We note that decoding of cyclic codes has been of interest in coding theory due to the rich structure possessed by these codes. Of the large number of works devoted to ML decoding of cyclic codes, we mention the paper by Vardy and Be’ery [20] which targeted algebraic properties of cyclic codes similar to [18]. The main results in [20] pertain to reducing trellis complexity of cyclic codes by studying the direct sum and concurring-sum structures existing in BCH codes. The direct sum structure connects their results to LRCs with disjoint repair groups, and this is one of the settings that we pursue in this paper. We note that the constructions in [18] and [19] also form codes with disjoint recovery sets.
While LRCs were initially developed for efficient data protection in distributed storage systems [8], in this paper we depart from this application and use the locality feature to suggest new decoding procedures of general linear codes, relying on their locality properties. The applications that we have in mind pertain to classic uses of errorcorrecting codes (communication over noisy channels) and are not primarily intended for data coding in distributed storage.
Principal Contributions: This paper shows how locality can be exploited to improve the decoding performance of binary cyclic codes. If the binary cyclic code does not possess the desired locality to begin with, we show how locality can judiciously be introduced, keeping the above goal in mind. We study the impact of locality in decoding under two settings, namely, ordered statistics decoding (OSD) and trellis decoding. In either case, decoding begins with a quicklook ML decoding stage that relies on the presence of local

codes with disjoint supports to reduce decoding delays and the average decoding complexity.
Under the first setting, we experimentally evaluate the impact of having locality with respect to a locality-aware OSD scheme introduced in the paper. This algorithm can be applied to binary cyclic codes having disjoint repair groups, hierarchical locality or else availability, and provides appreciable performance gains in terms of the SNR via a single round of belief propagation based on local parity-checks alone. Furthermore, we present a Generalized Distributive Law (GDL)-based modification of this algorithm to tackle more general situations, where the assumption on disjointness of repair groups is not needed. The significant performance gains arising from the application of the locality-aware OSD can be seen in Figures 2, 3 and 5.
With regard to the trellis decoding of cyclic codes, our results assume the coordinate ordering introduced by Vardy and Be’ery [20] that aligns certain direct-sum structures present in the cyclic code. The results include:
• showing how to introduce locality in order to reduce trellis state complexity at certain specific coordinate levels,
• an upper bound on the state complexity profile of the code regardless of whether or not it has locality,
• showing how an earlier result of [20] that provides an upper bound on maximum state complexity of the trellis of cyclic codes has an interpretation in terms of locality, and extending this result to cyclic codes possessing a hierarchical structure of locality. The paper is organized as follows. In Section II, we discuss preliminaries and prior work, with a focus on explaining how certain patterns of zeros of the cyclic code imply locality properties. In Sections III and IV, we study the impact of locality under the locality-aware OSD and trellis decoding settings, respectively.
II. LOCALITY AND CYCLIC CODES
A. Codes With Locality
Codes discussed in this paper belong to the class of LRCs [8] and are linear of length n and dimension k over a field Fq unless specified otherwise. We write their parameters as [n, k]. Let [l] {1, 2, . . . , l} and [a, b] {a, a +1, . . . , b}.
Definition 1 [8]: Let C be a linear code and let 0 ≤ i ≤ n − 1. The i th coordinate of C is said to have locality r if, for every codeword c (c0, c1, . . . , cn−1) ∈ C, there exists a subset Ri ⊂ [0, n − 1] \ {i } with |Ri | ≤ r such that ci is a linear combination of the symbols {c j , j ∈ Ri }. i.e., ci =
j∈Ri λi, j c j , where λi, j ∈ Fq and λi, j = 0 . Here, both Ri and {λi, j } depend on i but not on c. A code C is said to have r all-symbol locality, or simply locality r , if all the coordinates {i : 0 ≤ i ≤ n −1} have locality r . A code having r -all-symbol locality is called an LRC with locality parameter r .
The subset Ri is called the recovery set of the coordinate i . It is clear that every coordinate j ∈ Ri ∪ {i } can be found from the remaining coordinates in the set Ri ∪ {i }. Hence we call the set Ri ∪ {i }, a repair group. Most constructions of

sess two-level hierarchical locality with locality parameters [(rmid, δmid), (rbase, δbase)], if
(i) for every i th symbol, there exists a ‘middle’ local code, Cimid with i ∈ supp(Cimid) such that dim(Cimid) ≤ rmid and dmin(Cimid) ≥ δmid; (ii) furthermore, for each j th symbol of the code Cimid, there exists a ‘base’ local code Cib,ajse with j ∈ supp(Cib,ajse) ⊆ supp(Cimid) such that dim(Cib,ajse) ≤ rbase and dmin(Cib,ajse) ≥ δbase.

This definition affords a straightforward extension to more than two levels of local code hierarchy.

A. Locality-Aware Modification of the OSD Algorithm

In this section, we begin with considering binary linear

codes that have either disjoint locality or a stronger property of

hierarchical locality. Note that the latter property can be seen

as a special case of the former one because the code contains

middle level codes with disjoint supports. For both cases,

we make the assumption that the smallest-level local parity-

checks are disjoint and their union includes all the coordinates

of the code. These assumptions ensure that the algorithm

takes a simple form. In our discussion on overlapping locality,

we explain how to extend the locality-aware OSD to include

other cases where these assumptions do not hold.

In the locality-aware OSD algorithm, we first consider a

supercode of C denoted by Cloc, whose dual code is precisely the space spanned by the local parity-checks on C. Cloc is a

concatenation (direct sum) of single parity-check codes (sim-

ilarly, middle codes) for the disjoint (similarly, hierarchical)

locality scenario. We carry out ML decoding (which we call

Quick-Look ML Decoding, QL-MLD) of the received vector

y with the underlying code assumed to be Cloc. The disjoint supports of the local codes permit the local codes to be ML-

decoded independently.

In the case of disjoint locality, ML decoding of the local

code can be carried out via a simple, 2-step procedure: (i) hard-

decision decoding of the individual symbols (ii) if this does not

yield a valid codeword in the single parity-check code, flipping

the least reliable bit (i.e., the bit corresponding to the received

symbol with the lowest absolute value) to obtain the ML local

codeword. A little thought will show that, if the n-length

output vector obtained by putting together individually ML-

decoded local codewords results in a codeword in C, the decod-

ing procedure can be halted and we will have obtained the

ML codeword in C. If the code under consideration is having

hierarchical locality as well, i.e., middle codes having disjoint

supports are present, one can in fact carry out the QL-MLD

based on these middle codes. This essentially involves ML-

codeword decoding (say, using the Viterbi algorithm) each

middle code independently.

Our simulations indicate that QL-MLD trick works well

most of the time even when the local codes are rather weak

single parity-check codes, as in the case of disjoint locality.
