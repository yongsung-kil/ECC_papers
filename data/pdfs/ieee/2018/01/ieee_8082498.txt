An Iterative Check Polytope Projection Algorithm for ADMM-Based LP Decoding of LDPC Codes

Haoyuan Wei and Amir H. Banihashemi , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract— Alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) is a popular technique for linear-programming decoding of low-density parity-check codes. The computational complexity of ADMM is dominated by the Euclidean projection of a real-valued vector onto a parity-check polytope. Existing algorithms for such a projection all require sorting operations, which happen to be the most complex part of the projection. In this letter, we propose an iterative algorithm, without sorting operation, for projection onto the parity-check polytope. The proposed algorithm has a worst case complexity linear in the input dimension compared with the super-linear complexity of existing algorithms.
Index Terms— Alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM), low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, linear-programming (LP) decoding of LDPC codes, Euclidean projection, check polytope projection.
I. INTRODUCTION
A LTERNATING direction method of multipliers (ADMM) is a distributed optimization technique suitable for solving large scale optimization problems [1]. This method was recently used by Barman et al. [2] to solve the linear programming (LP) decoding problem of binary low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes [3]. Since then, there has been a flurry of research activity in this area [4]–[13]. A main thrust has been to reduce the computational complexity of ADMM, which is dominated by the projection of a real-valued vector onto the parity-check (check) polytope [2], [5]–[10], [12], [13].
The original check polytope projection algorithm of [2] involves two sorting operations that constitute the majority of computational complexity. To reduce the complexity of check polytope projection, Zhang and Siegel [5] proposed a more efficient projection algorithm based on the cut-search algorithm of [4]. This algorithm requires only one sorting operation. Both projection algorithms of [2] and [5] have complexity O(d log d), where d is the degree of the parity check node. The projection algorithm was further simplified in [6], where the projection onto the check polytope was transformed to the projection onto a simplex. The algorithm of [6] still involves a partial sorting operation. It has an average complexity linear in d and a worst case complexity quadrature in d. More recently, Wasson and Draper [8] focussed on the hardware implementation of the projection algorithm. They combined the ideas of cut-search algorithm of [5],

and simplex projection of [6], and adapted them for implementation in FPGA. The algorithm, however, still requires sorting operations which dominate the complexity. The software implementation of ADMM on multicore processors was studied in [9], and it was shown that the check polytope projection and the constituent sorting operations are the bottleneck in such an implementation. Wei et al. [7] reduced the computational complexity of ADMM-based LP decoder by reducing the number of Euclidean projections rather than focussing on reducing the complexity of the projection itself. Most recently, Jiao et al. [13] used quantization and look-up tables to reduce the complexity of the Euclidean projection, at the expense of relatively large memory. Debbabi et al. [10] and Jiao et al. [12] improved the speed of convergence of ADMM, and consequently reduced the average number of Euclidean projections per decoding instance, by devising new messagepassing schedules.
In this letter, we propose a novel iterative check polytope projection algorithm which does not involve the complex operation of sorting. For a fixed number of iterations, the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm is linear in d. This is in comparison with the super-linear complexity of all existing projection algorithms.

II. PRELIMINARIES

A. ADMM Decoding and Check Polytope Projection

B. Comparison With Other Projection Algorithms
The most complex component of check polytope projection in all existing algorithms [2], [5], [6], [8], [9], is the sorting operation. In Algorithm 1, however, there is no sorting operation. Instead, the algorithm involves only simple arithmetic operations. In particular, the iterative algorithm has, in the worst case, linear complexity in d, if one limits the number of iterations to a constant. This is in comparison to the superlinear complexity of the other algorithms. We note that the proposed projection algorithm can be particularly attractive for hardware implementation as it requires a relatively small amount of hardware resources. We, however, acknowledge that the iterative nature of the algorithm limits the degree of parallelization, and can consequently increase the latency.
IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
In our simulations, we consider the transmission of coded bits over an additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel with binary phase shift keying (BPSK) modulation. For the ADMM decoding, we use the l2 penalized version [11] due to its superior performance. In this algorithm, the parameter μ is set to 2.8, and the penalty parameter α and over-relaxation coefficient are set to 1.7 and 1.8, respectively. The maximum iteration number for ADMM is selected to be 60.

V. CONCLUSION
In this letter, we proposed an iterative check polytope pro-
jection algorithm to reduce the computational complexity of
ADMM in the context of LP decoding of LDPC codes. Unlike
existing projection algorithms, the proposed algorithm avoids
sorting operation and only uses simple arithmetic operations.
The worst-case computational complexity of the proposed
algorithm is linear in the input dimension. This is versus
the super-linear complexity of existing algorithms. While the
proposed projection algorithm requires less resources to be
implemented in hardware, the iterative nature of the algorithm
would increase the latency. We hope that this work would be
a step in the direction of making ADMM decoding of LDPC
codes more attractive in practice. In particular, a detailed study
of hardware implementations of the proposed algorithm would
be interesting.
