Abstract—In this paper, we present a new low-complexity algorithm for computing the projection onto the parity polytope in the context of ADMM-based LP decoding. This projection is the heart of the ADMM algorithm, that usually involves a sorting operation, which is the main effort of the projection. Our proposed algorithm relies on new findings in the recursive structure of the parity polytope and works by fixing selected components in an iterative manner. The absence of the sorting operation makes it preferable for efficient hard- and software implementations. In addition, we can show that this new projection algorithm requires up to 37% less arithmetic operations compared to state-of-the-art projections.
Index Terms—Alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM), linear programming (LP) decoding, parity polytope projection
I. INTRODUCTION
Linear Programming (LP) decoding is a rather new decoding approach, that was first established in 2003 by Feldman et al [5]. The LP decoding problem is the relaxation of the Maximum-Likelihood (ML) decoding problem onto a special polytope. Especially by introducing redundant parity-checks [13], the error-correcting performance of LP decoding is getting very close to the ML decoding performance. Therefore, LP decoding has become an interesting topic of research for nearly all relevant code classes.
Recently, the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) [2], an iterative method from convex optimization, was proposed for solving the LP decoding problem. In the context of LP decoding, the main effort of ADMM is the projection onto the so-called parity polytope (see Section III). The projection complexity grows with the number of ones in each row of the code’s underlying parity-check matrix, thus ADMM-based LP decoding is mainly performed for LowDensity Parity-Check (LDPC) codes. This projection is the key of the ADMM algorithm and requires a sorting operation in all exact implementations from literature. However, sorting can become a major problem for efficient hardware implementation.
In this paper, we evolve an extended theory of parity polytopes that allows us to present a new efficient projection algorithm that does not require sorting operations. The proposed algorithm iteratively fixes selected components of the projection. Since at least one component is fixed in each iteration, the number of iterations is limited and the problem size is strictly decreasing. Results on the number of needed

arithmetic operations show that our approach not only circumvents the sorting operation, but additionally requires up to 37% less arithmetic operations than state-of-the-art projection algorithms.
The paper is structured as follows: Section II summarizes related work. In Section III, we define the considered problem, i. e. the projection onto the (even) parity polytope, recap some properties of this polytope and derive analogous results for the odd parity polytope. Section IV shows the main concept of this projection, i. e. fixing of components of the projection. In Section V, this projection problem is regarded as an iterative structure which is utilized in the projection algorithm. The whole projection algorithm is finally summarized in Section VI. Numerical results are presented in Section VII. The paper is concluded in Section VIII. In the following, the proofs are omitted for brevity. For the main theorem, the idea of the proof is described.
II. RELATED WORK
The ADMM was used for solving the LP decoding problem for the first time in [1]. For their projection algorithm onto the parity polytope, the authors characterize the resulting point as a convex combination of two slices of the polytope and use two sorting operations. A faster projection method is presented in [14]. Here, the projection is reduced to the projection onto a special face of the polytope, which is found by the cutsearch algorithm of the authors [13]. Their main effort is the sorting of a vector, whose size can be up to the check degree d. Sorting is also the main effort in the projection of [10]. The authors reduce the problem to the projection onto a simplex, which is solved by an algorithm from [4]. Another projection from [4], that projects onto a simplex, is used in the algorithm of [12]. The main effort is a partial sorting and finding a median [3]. These mentioned projection algorithms are all exact. An approximative algorithm, that does not require sorting operations, is presented in [11]. This method is based on the projection onto a face from [14] and approaches the wanted point from outside the polytope. A projection method with a look-up table is proposed in [8]. The authors use the symmetry of the parity polytope for reducing the size of the table. In [7], the size of the table is further reduced by optimizing the choice of the points that are used in the table.

Before entering the loop, we initialize l = d, which tracks the number of components of z that are not yet computed. The variable f tracks the index of the first uncomputed component of z, whereas f old stores the value of f from the beginning of the current while loop iteration. VIII. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we presented a new projection algorithm for ADMM-based LP decoding. It works by iteratively fixing

some components of the input, thereby reducing the problem
size in each iteration. In contrast to other exact state-of-the-art
projections, our proposed algorithm does not require a sorting
operation and additionally requires up to 37% less arithmetical
operations. These properties make it a very good choice for
future hardware implementations.
