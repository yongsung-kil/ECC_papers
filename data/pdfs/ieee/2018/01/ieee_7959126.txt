Decision-Directed Retention-Failure Recovery With Channel Update for MLC NAND Flash Memory
Chaudhry Adnan Aslam, Student Member, IEEE, Yong Liang Guan, Member, IEEE, and Kui Cai, Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract— To recover from the retention noise induced errors in NAND flash memory, a retention-aware beliefpropagation (RABP) decoding scheme for low-density paritycheck codes is introduced. The RABP is a two-stage decoding scheme in which the memory cell’s charge-loss effect is systematically compensated. In RABP decoding, instead of read retries for data recovery, the probable victim cells are first determined with the help of read-back voltage signal and the decoded bit decisions. Then, for such suspected victim cells, their log-likelihood-ratio regions are modified in such a way as to absorb the effect of cell voltage downshift caused by retention noise, and then a second round of belief-propagation (BP) decoding is performed afresh, often with decoding failure recovery. Furthermore, leveraging on the RABP decoded bit-error pattern, an RABP assisted channel update (RABP-CU) algorithm is proposed which re-estimates the latest cell voltage distribution parameters without incurring new memory sensing operations. This is achieved by minimizing the mean squared error between the measured and predicted bit error/erasure values. Through simulations, it is shown that the RABP decoder increases the retention time limit by up to 70% compared with single round of BP decoding. The proposed RABP-CU algorithm further extends the data retention time.
Index Terms— NAND flash memory, data retention noise, retention-failure recovery, flash channel estimation, voltage distribution, belief-propagation decoding, error-rate performance.
I. INTRODUCTION
N AND flash memory has revolutionized the non-volatile data storage industry by offering large storage capacity, high data throughput, fast read-response time and low power consumption. It has been ubiquitously used in a wide range of applications, ranging from portable digital electronic products to high-performance enterprise data centers. However, its further proliferation has been hampered due to the emergence of data retention noise which degrades the flash data reliability [1]. As the memory cell size shrinks employing sub-20 nm process technology, only fewer electrons can be stored over

This problem exacerbates with the multi-level-cell (MLC) technology, where intervals between adjacent voltage levels are increasing small. As a result, the voltage distributions tend to overlap, which leads to high raw bit-error-rate (BER).
In current design practice, error correction codes (ECCs) are being used to tolerate the increasing raw memory errors. However, the conventional hard-decision decoding codes, such as the Bose-Chaudhri-Hocquenghem (BCH) and Reed-Solomon (RS) codes, are no longer feasible for the application to high-density NAND flash technology. Instead, soft-decision decoding low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have emerged as a promising candidate [2]–[9]. The beliefpropagation (BP) decoding of an LDPC code requires finegrained memory sensing precision for accurate computation of log-likelihood-ratio (LLR) information of each code-word bit, as the decoding performance heavily depends upon the quality of LLR values.
In NAND flash memory, however, the default memory sensing (read threshold) levels, and the stored voltage distribution parameters (mean and standard deviation) that are needed to generate the LLR information, tend to become outdated and sub-optimal with increasing data retention time and memory program/erase (PE) cycles. This may lead to ECC decoding failure and thus demand flash channel estimation afresh. Typically, many retention-failure recovery and flash channel estimation algorithms require additional memory sensing operations. Note that the total read latency of NAND flash memory based devices is composed of firmware processing, memory sensing, and DMA (data transfer from controller to NAND flash memory) times, and the most time consuming process is the memory sensing operation. Thus, additional re-reads may compromise the benefits brought by the flash technology. Hence, for the effective implementation of softdecision ECC, we need new system-level solutions which can perform the retention-failure recovery and flash channel estimation without incurring additional read operations.

Furthermore, the cell re-programming operation itself may induce more cell-to-cell interference (CCI) which is again a major source of errors in today’s flash memory chips. When the number of retention errors are large and exceed the error-correction capability of ECC decoder, the FCR do not work anymore. In this case, some recent data recovery techniques attending to retention failures, like the error-prediction error-recovery (EPER) [12] and flash-defibrillator (FD) [13], where the charge-reduced cells are recharged by applying recovery pulses, and the retention-optimized-reading (ROR) [14] and read-channeloptimization (RCO) [15], where the failed stored data is recovered by adaptively applying read threshold levels, can be invoked. However, all these methods require extra memory sensing operations which may lead to increase in the read latency and power consumption.
With regards to flash channel estimation, which involves finding the voltage distribution parameters correspond to each cell state, Cai et al. [16] and Luo et al. [17] present the off-line and on-line voltage modeling schemes (Off-VM) and (On-VM), respectively, which characterize the flash channel using empirical measurements and develop statistical models for threshold voltage distribution. However, both these schemes estimate the flash channel for a given PE count, while ignoring the effect of charge leakage due to increasing data retention time. The adaptive read threshold (ART) [18] is another method for characterizing the voltage distribution while using a limited number of re-reads. Apart from that, a detector parameter estimation (DPE) algorithm, which determines the best-fit voltage distribution parameters by matching the actual and modeled distributions functions, is reported in [19]. When ECC of a memory page fails with initial distribution parameters, these estimation algorithms can be invoked by employing extra memory sensing operations. This is followed by more memory sensing operations for the re-trial of error correction. This whole process results in significant read latency due to excessive memory sensing operations and large amount of memory-to-controller data transfer.
To preclude the excessive re-reads, a decision-directed estimation (DDE) strategy is presented in [20], in which the decoded bit-error patterns are used to formulate an optimization problem for the adjustment of voltage distribution parameters. However, for the DDE scheme to work, the availability of correctly decoded data is a pre-requisite. Furthermore, the DDE scheme only finds the distribution mean values, whereas the distribution standard deviation values are assumed to be an scaled factor of distribution mean values, which may not be true. Finally, the DDE scheme is also susceptible to large estimation errors and thus may sometimes become unstable.
Researchers have also proposed some innovative code construction methods which facilitate the memory controller in the selection of read threshold levels, particularly when no prior information of voltage distribution is available. One such example is the balanced modulation which ensures that the code-word contains an equal number of zeros and

ones, hence facilitating the adjustment of dynamic read thresholds [21], [22]. The rank modulation [23], which stores the data contents in the relative voltage order over a set of memory cells, can also tolerate the charge leakage. Since the charge leakage process results in a unidirectional drift of cell voltages, it is likely that the relative order of cell voltages is still preserved after the retention period. However, rank modulation faces some technological issues which need to be addressed before its practical implementation. For instance, rank modulation requires a large number of read operations to learn the voltage level ordering, which may significantly increase the read latency.
B. Contributions
In this paper, we perform the retention-failure recovery and flash channel estimation by using the initial read threshold levels, thus avoiding the time and power consuming excessive read operations. In the first half of the paper, we present a novel retention-aware belief-propagation (RABP) decoding scheme for retention-failure recovery, specifically when the memory cells are severely influenced under the retention noise effect. In this scheme, we employ the BP algorithm to decode the stored flash data. In case of decoding failure, we modify the input LLR computation by re-mapping the LLRs of voltage quantization regions, based on the measured read-back voltage signal and the decoded code-word bits, and initialize a second round of BP decoding by using the modified LLR values. The proposed RABP decoding improves the flash channel tolerance against the data retention errors and enables the BP algorithm to successfully decode the flash data for longer period of retention time.
In the second half of the paper, we propose an RABP assisted channel update (RABP-CU) algorithm for estimating the mean and standard deviation parameters of threshold voltage distribution by using the measured bit error/erasure counts which are obtained after the execution of RABP decoder. Specifically, we iteratively minimize the mean squared error between the measured and predicted bit error/erasure counts, employing the gradient descent method, and determine the best-fit mean and standard deviation values. In case of RABP decoding failure, wherein the measured bit error/erasure counts are unavailable, we compute the approximated values of bit error/erasure counts with the help of detected bit counts, and then invoke the proposed RABP-CU algorithm.
The overall scheme works as shown in Fig. 1. The flash memory controller first attempts to read the memory page with initial read threshold levels and then executes the conventional BP (CBP) decoding. If the retention noise is weak and the decoding outcome is successful, no further processing is required. Otherwise, the controller attempts to recover the retention-failure data by using the proposed RABP decoding and then proceeds to the RABP-CU algorithm. A prior version of this paper was published in [24], but the work presented here has been significantly extended.

A. Data Retention Effect on Voltage Distribution
The unremitting charge leakage over increasing data retention time reduces the cell’s threshold voltage level and eventually shifts the memory cell down, into an adjacent (lower) cell state, leading to an incorrect detection of stored data symbol, as shown in Fig. 3. This figure illustrates four voltage distribution functions for 2-bit per cell flash memory. Note that the data retention noise not only shifts the voltage distribution functions towards the erased state s11, but also increases their variance. While the retention noise affects the programmed state voltage distribution functions, it has inconsequential effect on the erased state memory cells [14]. Thus, in the proposed RABP-CU algorithm, we only attempt to find the mean and standard deviation parameters for the programmed state memory cells.
In Fig. 4, we simulate our 2-bit per cell flash channel model and plot the amount of change in mean and standard deviation values, denoted by μs10∼s01 and σs10∼s01 , respectively, over a range of data retention time, keeping the PE cycles fixed at 5K and 10K. It should be noted that the voltage distribution parameters correspond to the erased state s11 changes very slightly. Thus, we assume the values of μs11 and σs11 to be negligible. We observe that the highest programmed state s01 shows the largest amount of shift, where the mean and standard deviation values change up to 0.4 V and 0.055 V, respectively, by the retention time of 105 hours. This is because the retention shift is proportional to the amount of charged electrons stored over the cell’s floating-gate, thus a higher programmed state shifts faster than a lower programmed state.

III. RETENTION-AWARE BELIEF-PROPAGATION (RABP) DECODING ALGORITHM
As memory cell’s threshold voltage level continuously shifts over the data retention time, the initial voltage distribution parameters yield incorrect LLR information. This may lead to BP decoding failure. In the following section, we will first describe the method for the selection of read threshold levels and then present a retention-failure recovery technique which precludes the new memory sensing operations.

B. Retention-Failure Recovery via RABP Decoding
When BP decoding fails due to a large number of retention errors, new memory sensing operations may be performed in order to update the voltage distribution parameters and to subsequently compute a new set of read threshold levels [16]–[20]. This is followed by the re-trial of error correction decoding using these updated parameters to recover the incorrectly decoded code-word. This whole process, however, significantly affects the read-response time of the flash channel as it requires additional read operations not only for the channel estimation but also for the second round of BP decoding.
To preclude the excessive re-reads necessitated when BP decoding fails, and to counter the escalating effect of data retention errors, we propose the retention-aware beliefpropagation (RABP) decoding which constitutes of 2 rounds of BP decoding. The first round provides a hard-decision estimate of the code-word bits, denoted by {cˆmsb, cˆlsb}, corresponding to the MSB and LSB positions. In case of decoding failure, we will update the LLR information, denoted by Lˆ msb and Lˆ lsb, by reversely exploiting the charge leakage effect, and execute a new (second) round of BP decoding.
In Fig. 6, we illustrate an example of 6-level non-uniform quantization scheme where the threshold voltage range is split into 4 data regions, denoted by D1, D2, D3 and D4, and 3 erasure regions, denoted by E1, E2 and E3. C. Error-Rate Performance of RABP Decoder
To analyze the error-rate improvement under the proposed RABP decoding scheme, we simulate a binary column-weight 5 regular LDPC code, constructed using the progressiveedge-growth (PEG) algorithm [37]. This code has input (K ) and output (N) block length of 7, 200 and 8, 000 bits, respectively, and code-rate of 0.90. This LDPC code is decoded using informed fixed scheduling based shuffled beliefpropagation (BP) algorithm [38], where maximum iteration count is set to 25 per BP decoding round. Note that, besides shuffled BP decoder, the LDPC code can also be decoded via improved layered BP decoder [39], or by employing dynamic BP scheduling schemes [40], [41]. In this work, we set the the LLR weighting coefficients to be equal to one, that is ζn = 1 and ηn = 1 for n = {1, 2, . . . , 6}. We leave the optimization of ζn and ηn as a possible future work.

It can be seen that the number of errors are noticeably decreased after the first round of decoding, specifically at smaller values of retention time. This helps the proposed RABP decoding to perform the retention-error correction. In Fig. 9, we plot the

Similarly, we can notice that the RABP decoding improves the FER performance by up to 3 order-of-magnitude at retention time of 1, 500 hours.

parameters in order to select the optimal read threshold levels,

and hence facilitates more accurate computation of LLR infor-

This reduces the future possibility of BP decoding failure.

VI. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
An innovative retention-aware belief-propagation (RABP) decoding strategy is proposed to mitigate data loss associated with retention noise in NAND flash memory. The proposed RABP decoding exploits the knowledge that the memory cell’s threshold voltage tends to drop gradually with data retention time. Under this effect, the probable charge-loss memory cells are detected by jointly analyzing their readback voltage signal and the corresponding decoded code-word bits. In case of decoding failure, a new set of log-likelihoodratio (LLR) values that enables retention-erasure correction or retention-error correction is computed. Then a fresh round of BP decoding is performed. Simulating a model-based 2-bit per cell NAND flash memory, it is shown that the retention time endurance of the proposed RABP decoding scheme is increased by up to 70% and the error-rate performance is improved by up to 3 order-of-magnitude, when compared to conventional BP decoding. In conjunction with RABP decoder, a flash channel update algorithm (RABP-CU) is proposed to re-estimate the voltage distribution mean and standard deviation parameters, leveraging on the decoded error/erasure patterns. The proposed RABP-CU algorithm finds the bestfit voltage distribution parameters by employing the gradient descent (GD) method. The estimated mean and standard deviation values are shown to improve the channel estimation accuracy over increasing data retention time, with estimation errors below 0.02 V. Meanwhile, the complexity of the proposed estimation algorithm also reduces with increasing data retention time. By virtue of the proposed RABP-CU algorithm, the retention time tolerance is increased by up to 41× (times) at 6K PE cycles. More importantly, this performance gain is achieved without performing additional memory sensing operations which are time and power consuming.
As a possible future work, the LLR weighting coefficients ζn and ηn can be optimized for more effective retentionerasure correction and retention-error correction. For instance, considering Fig. 7, these values can be designed such that ζ6 > ζ4 > ζ2 > ζ5 > ζ3 > ζ1 and η6 > η4 > η2 > η5 > η3 > η1.
