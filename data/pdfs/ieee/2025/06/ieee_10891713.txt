Dual Diagonal LDPC: A Resource-Efficient Channel Coding Scheme for CubeSat Applications
AMR ZEEDAN TAMER KHATTAB , Senior Member, IEEE RIDHA HAMILA , Senior Member, IEEE NIZAR ZORBA , Senior Member, IEEE Qatar University, Doha, Qatar
The establishment of high-speed connectivity through CubeSats requires innovative solutions to overcome the challenges associated with the development of low-power, high-speed transceivers within the size and mass limitations inherent to CubeSats. Among the most computationally demanding tasks of any transceiver are demodulation and channel decoding, typically requiring a high utilization of hardware resources. This article proposes a channel coding scheme based on low density parity check (LDPC) coding, offering a markedly efficient allocation of resources to address these computational demands. In contrast to traditional LDPC encoding approaches, we develop a simplified algorithm based on an irregular repeat-accumulate (IRA) dual diagonal parity check matrix construction, resulting in an extremely resource-efficient code. The enhanced algorithm efficiently conserves hardware resources through the shared utilization of the parity check bit generator. Moreover, a corresponding low-complexity decoder, based on efficiently combining the Min-Sum (MS) algorithm and the Bit-Flipping (BF) algorithm, which employs the Log-Likelihood Ratio (LLR) is proposed.. I. INTRODUCTION
Small-size communication satellites have increased markedly within the global wireless communication sector, particularly in the context of evolving technologies such as smart homes, smart cities, industry 4.0, and the Internet of Things (IoT) platforms [1]. Conventionally, communication satellites have been characterized by large sizes and geosynchronous orbits [1]. However, there is a shift in the industry toward the development of constellations composed of small satellites operating in low-Earth orbits (LEOs) to offer communication services, exemplified by initiatives such as Starlink and Amazon Project Kuiper [2]. LEO orbits provide the advantages of a shorter propagation delay and a smaller path loss. A CubeSat, also known as a nanosatellite, is characterized by compact dimensions, measuring 10 cm × 10 cm × 10 cm, and has a mass of 1.33 kg for a single unit (1 U), where CubeSats can be up to 12 U in size [4]. CubeSats are used for a variety of applications, including communication services, Earth observation, remote sensing, commercial applications, technology demonstration, space exploration, and military applications [3]. The number of CubeSats launched has grown rapidly in recent years and is expected to continue to grow significantly in the coming years [5]. As a result, the development of compact energy-efficient high-speed communication systems suitable for CubeSats is becoming increasingly more crucial to meet the increasing demands for high-speed connectivity.
In contrast to conventional satellites, CubeSats require significantly reduced development costs, shorter development cycles, enhanced service flexibility, improved risk distribution, lower communication latency, decreased human power requirements, lower energy consumption, and a more compact size, as highlighted by [3] and [6]. However, most existing CubeSat communication systems operate at low data rates, typically below 10 Mbps, with relatively high power demands exceeding 5 W [3]. Therefore, there is a pressing need for innovative solutions, particularly on the baseband side, to develop high-speed and energy-efficient CubeSat modems [4].
A. Motivation
In recent years, interest has been increasing in studying the potential of improved channel coding techniques to advance space communication systems [7], [8], [9], [10]. Low density parity check (LDPC) codes, renowned for their capacity-approaching properties [11], [12], enable significant reduction in transmission power for a given bit error rate (BER) [13], [14]. LDPC codes are currently integral to 5G networks and constitute a mandatory component of

B. Literature
1) Channel Coding for CubeSats: CubeSat communication faces unique challenges due to their small size and limited resources [1]. Reliable data transmission is critical for mission success, and channel coding plays a vital role in achieving this [4]. Although traditional Bose–Chaudhuri– Hocquenghem (BCH) codes have historically been used in satellite communications due to their ease of implementation and effectiveness, their limitations become evident in CubeSats [18]. The exponential scaling of the decoding complexity with the length of the code in the BCH codes poses a significant barrier for resource-constrained CubeSat processors. Furthermore, the inflexibility of BCH codes, often designed for specific data rates and error correction capabilities, limits their adaptability to diverse CubeSat mission profiles [19].
Turbo codes and convolutional codes offer strong error correction performance. However, their decoding complexity can be higher compared to LDPC codes, making them less attractive for power-constrained CubeSat hardware [20]. Recent advances aim to bridge this gap [21], [22]. The work in [21] proposed low-complexity iterative decoding algorithms for turbo codes, demonstrating their potential application in CubeSat communication systems. Similarly, studies on tailbiting convolutional codes with efficient decoding schemes present a viable alternative for specific scenarios, as explored by [23].
Polar codes have garnered significant interest due to their capacity-achieving properties and efficient decoding [24]. However, their implementation on resourceconstrained platforms such as CubeSats requires further exploration. Jali et al. [24] investigated hardware architectures for polar code decoders, paving the way for their potential application in CubeSat communication systems.
LDPC codes have emerged as a powerful error correction technique for CubeSat and small satellite communication due to their flexibility, error correction capability, and ability to efficiently decode on resource-constrained hardware [8]. In fact, LDPC is the most widely used channel coding scheme for state-of-the-art CubeSats [4]. For example, the authors in [25], [26], and [27] all employed LDPC codes for their CubeSat communication systems. Moreover, the Consultative Committee for Space Data Systems (CCSDS) has adopted a family of LDPC codes specifically tailored for CubeSat applications [10]. These codes offer a range of code rates, allowing CubeSat

developers to achieve a balance between data rate and errorcorrection performance. These CCSDS LDPC codes offer several advantages. First, standardization ensures seamless communication between CubeSats and ground stations equipped with compatible decoders, eliminating compatibility issues and simplifying deployment [28]. Second, the range of code rates caters to diverse mission needs. Missions that prioritize high data throughput can leverage higher code rates, while those requiring low BER performance can employ lower code rates for improved error correction [29]. This flexibility allows CubeSat developers to tailor their communication systems to specific mission requirements. Third, the CCSDS standards prioritize code design and decoding algorithms optimized for resource-constrained CubeSat hardware. This ensures reliable communication without exceeding the CubeSat’s limited processing power and energy budget [30]. However, implementing CCSDS LDPC codes requires careful consideration. Decoding still requires a considerably high level of processing power [31]. The selection of appropriate hardware with sufficient capabilities is crucial for real-time communication [32]. For advanced applications, developers often need to design custom decoders for greater control of the balance between throughput, BER performance, and complexity [30], [31], [32]. Therefore, many active research efforts are focused on developing low-power decoding algorithms for LDPC codes. In addition, hardware acceleration techniques utilizing FPGAs have the potential to significantly improve decoding speed while maintaining low power consumption. FPGAs offer a balance between flexibility and performance, making them suitable for implementing decoders that can handle the varying communication demands of CubeSats while maintaining low power consumption [32]. These advancements will enable real-time communication for even more demanding CubeSat missions.
A key challenge in implementing LDPC decoders on CubeSats is their power consumption. In [30], [31], and [32], the authors investigate hardware implementations of CCSDS LDPC coders for CubeSats. Their work demonstrate the feasibility of LDPC for CubeSat systems but highlights the need for further optimization. Low-power decoding algorithms and specialized hardware architectures are active areas of research, especially exploiting the inherent parallelism of LDPC decoding for power reduction. The majority of existing CubeSat communication systems employ already existing LDPC algorithms without consideration to customizing or improving them to better suit the specific requirements of CubeSats especially in terms of high data rates at low power consumption. This work fills this gap by developing and implementing improved LDPC coding algorithms that support a high data rate at low power consumption and competitive BER performance suitable for CubeSat communication systems.

parity check matrices, each having different characteristics [34]. Some of the most common LDPC codes and parity check matrix constructions are summarized below.
a) Gallager’s construction: Proposed by Robert G. Gallager, this method generates regular LDPC codes with a fixed degree of variable and check nodes. The parity check matrix has a simple structure with constant row and column weights [35].
b) Progressive edge growth (PEG) algorithm: PEG is an iterative algorithm for constructing irregular LDPC codes, where variable and check node degrees vary. It starts with a small matrix and incrementally adds rows and columns to achieve the desired code properties. PEG can generate LDPC codes with a wide range of degree distributions [36].
c) Quasicyclic LDPC codes (QC-LDPC): QC-LDPC codes are a special class of LDPC codes where the parity check matrix exhibits a cyclic structure. QCLDPC uses an algebraic algorithm for constructing structured matrices [37]. In QC-LDPC, a pseudorandom sparse matrix is generated where the nonzero elements are replaced by cyclically shifted identity matrices [38]. These codes are often used in practical applications due to their efficient encoding and decoding algorithms [39].
d) Irregular repeat-accumulate (IRA) codes: IRA codes are a type of irregular LDPC codes that can be constructed by repeating and accumulating a smaller base matrix. They offer a good tradeoff between performance and complexity. IRA codes are suitable for hardware implementations and have been used in digital video broadcasting systems [40].
e) Random construction: Random construction methods involve generating LDPC codes by randomly filling the entries of the parity check matrix. These codes can have good performance but may require iterative optimization to achieve the desired properties. The Tanner graph of random LDPC codes can be highly irregular [33].
f) Extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) chart-based design: The EXIT chart is a graphical tool used to design LDPC codes. It helps visualize the tradeoff between variable node and check node degree distributions to achieve the desired performance [41].
The choice of LDPC matrix construction depends on the application requirements, desired code performance, and implementation constraints. Regular LDPC codes are often used when simplicity and mathematical analysis are essential. However, it is known that irregular LDPC codes perform better than regular codes [33]. In fact, irregular LDPC codes have gained popularity due to their capacity-approaching performance and design flexibility. IRA-LDPC codes have simple structure and linear encoding complexity [33]. Their variable degrees for variable and check nodes enable tailored code designs for diverse channel conditions, ensuring efficient error correction in various applications [40]. Despite their irregular structure, they have

reasonable encoding and decoding complexity, adaptable to both software and hardware implementations [40]. In addition, their recognition in industry standards such as Wi-Fi and 5G underscores their practical relevance [40]. Consequently, our proposed scheme is based on an IRA-LDPC code with a structured parity check matrix that results in good performance with a simplified hardware architecture.
Considering LDPC decoding algorithms, there are many existing algorithms each having its own characteristics in terms of complexity and BER performance. Several algorithms with varying complexity have been proposed for realizing a well-adjusted tradeoff between BER performance and implementation complexity [42], [43], [44]. Nevertheless, designing an algorithm that can support high throughput while having good BER performance and low implementation complexity remains a challenging problem [25], [33]. There are several works on the development of efficient, in terms of performance and complexity, LDPC decoding algorithms such as bit-flipping (BF), sum-product (SP), min-sum (MS), and gradient descent bit flipping (GDBF) algorithms. These algorithms considerably vary in their complexity and BER performance.
The BF algorithm [45], based on hard-decision decoding, has the lowest complexity, however, it typically has lower BER performance compared with other algorithms. Some variations have been proposed in the literature to improve its BER performance [46], [47]. For example, the BF algorithm proposed in [46] involves dynamic computation of probabilities for BF at variable nodes. Likewise, weighted BF algorithms [47] necessitate updating of reliability values throughout the decoding process. Consequently, these modified algorithms require more complex operations compared to the original BF algorithm. At the same time, they are only capable of achieving modest improvement in the BER performance.
On the other hand, the SP algorithm, based on softdecision decoding, has very good decoding performance but at the expense of very high complexity [48]. Modified SP algorithms simplify the check node operations by converting the nonlinear functions used in the original algorithm to approximate quantization tables [49], [50].
The MS algorithm has significantly simplified check node operations compared to the SP algorithm. The relatively simple logical and arithmetic operations used in the MS algorithm make it suitable for implementations with complexity constraints [51]. Consequently, the MS algorithm has lower BER performance compared to the SP algorithm [51]. Several variations of the MS algorithm have been proposed, such as normalized MS [52], offset MS [53], and adaptive quantization MS [54]. Although these variations result in an improved BER performance, their practical implementation remains a challenge due to their increased complexity [53], [54].
The GDBF algorithm is a message-passing algorithm that belongs to the family of belief propagation algorithms, which iteratively update the probabilities of transmitted bits based on the noisy signals received [55]. GDBF uses gradient descent techniques to refine the bit estimates

C. Contributions
In [4], we presented a critical review of CubeSat onboard transceiver architectures and identified their limitations and future directions, including the use of improved channel coding algorithms. In [3], we presented our overall proposed baseband system architecture incorporating a differential binary phase shift keying (DBPSK) modulatordemodulator (modem). The focus was on the implementation and complexity of the pulse shaping filter. DBPSK is selected because it meets the power source constraints in CubeSat. Since DBPSK does not require carrier phase estimation at the receiver, the need for a phase-locked loop is eliminated, resulting in a simplified implementation of the demodulator. In this article, a highly resource-efficient baseband architecture is proposed based on DBPSK modulation and LDPC channel coding. The proposed scheme is particularly designed to support CubeSat applications which impose strict power and hardware resource constraints while requiring reliable and high-speed communications. To this end, an IRA dual diagonal LDPC (DD-LDPC) code is constructed, and the corresponding low-complexity encoding and decoding algorithms are developed. Unlike existing DD-LDPC codes [58], [59], [60], [61] that employ quasicyclic constructions, our code is based on IRA construction resulting in distinct features in the structure of the parity check matrix, the encoding/decoding algorithms, the complexity, and the performance. The resulting code is highly efficient in utilizing hardware resources and the decoder is developed such that it can be implemented based on a fully parallel hardware architecture allowing it to support high data rates. Detailed theoretical and simulation results are presented to demonstrate the performance of the system. The main contributions of this article can be summarized as follows:
1) construction of a high code rate, 3/4, (512, 384) IRA DD-LDPC code (see Section III);
2) development of a low-complexity recursive encoding algorithm that requires 8192 less computations compared to existing DD-LDPC codes [27], [58], [59], [60], [61] (see Section III);
3) development of a resource-efficient MS-BF decoding algorithm with lower complexity than MS, namely, O(n2(n − k)), and 1.5 dB lower SNR than BF at 10−6 (see Section IV);

4) considerations of the practical implementation aspects of DBPSK and pulse shaping filter of the baseband transceiver (see Section II);
5) theoretical and numerical computational complexity analysis of the proposed scheme confirming the reduction in the number of operations required by our algorithm compared to existing algorithms (see Section IV);
6) derivation of the theoretical bit error rate (BER) expression for the developed scheme (see Section IV);
7) numerical results demonstrating that our proposed system has good BER performance, supports the highest information rate, around 22.5 Mbps, and has the lowest number of decoding iterations, 10, among similar systems (see Section V).

II. SYSTEM MODEL
A. Channel Model
CubeSats, being small and typically deployed in LEOs, often operate in line-of-sight (LOS) conditions, where direct communication paths between the satellite and the ground station prevail [62], [63], [64], [65]. Depending on whether the LEO satellite is communicating with fixed or mobile ground station and whether the ground station is in urban or rural area as well as the elevation angle of the LEO satellite, the channel model varies and may include multiple states, as well as small-scale fading [62], [63]. The dominant LOS free space pathloss (FSPL) characteristic is confirmed by [62] for higher elevation angles in rural areas and assuming a fixed (nonmobile) ground station. The simplified LOS FSPL model enables the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel model to be used as an acceptable approximation for CubeSat to ground station communication channel1 [62], [63], [64], [65]. It is also worth mentioning that the LOS FSPL model is suitable for intersatellite communications, which is a key enabling technology for LEO satellite constellations [25], [66], [67], [68], [69], [70].

B. Baseband Architecture
The architecture of our baseband system is illustrated in Fig. 1. At the transmitter, LDPC channel encoding is performed on the input bit stream, which consists of k information bits to produce the channel coded signal consisting of n bits. Second, the generated channel symbols are DBPSK modulated to produce the modulated signal. Third, pulse shaping is applied on the modulated signal using a raised-cosine filter (RCF) with 0.5 roll-off factor (α = 0.5). Finally, the generated baseband signal goes through the AWGN channel. At the receiver, the received noisy signal goes through the detector which outputs the detected modulated symbols. After that, the modulation symbols are DBPSK demodulated to obtain the channel symbols which are then decoded using the LDPC decoder to output the final bit stream. Each of these blocks are explained in detail in the following paragraphs.
As it is well-established, LDPC codes are under the class of linear block codes where the encoding is performed in blocks of data. The parity check matrix (H), the matrix describing the relations that the components of a codeword must satisfy, of an LDPC code consists of a small number of nonzero elements, where the sparsity of H determines the minimum distance of the code and the decoding complexity [71]. It is the requirement of LDPC H matrix to be sparse that distinguishes LDPC code from other block codes. The parity check matrix has dimensions (n − k) × n, where each block of k information bits is encoded to a codeword of n bits [72].
The majority of CubeSat transceivers commonly adopt low-order modulation schemes such as BPSK, QPSK, and GMSK, primarily due to the complexity of higher order modulation schemes and the stringent power consumption limitations inherent in CubeSats [3]. While GMSK is favored for its efficient spectral characteristics, it exhibits inferior BER performance compared to alternatives such as BPSK [3]. In fact, BPSK is one of the most extensively

utilized modulation schemes in CubeSats due to its straightforward implementation and minimal power requirements for a given throughput [4]. Consequently, DBPSK, a variant of BPSK, is chosen to align with application requirements, specifically the power source constraints in the satellite. DBPSK demodulation eliminates the need to estimate the phase of the carrier, operating as a noncoherent receiver. This eliminates ambiguity in the phase that may arise if the constellation undergoes rotation during transmission through the channel. Generally, differential phase shift keying requires a less complex receiver compared to phase shift keying and has better bandwidth requirement [4]. Since DBPSK does not require carrier phase estimation at the receiver, the need for a phase-locked loop is eliminated and the implementation of the demodulator is simplified. Therefore, we employ DBPSK in our CubeSat transceiver due to its lower complexity receiver and the relatively small difference in the BER performance.
To perform DBPSK mapping, we have developed a straightforward procedure employing the “XOR” gate. At the nth time slot, we want to map the data bit bn to a mapped bit mn. Since mn should only change from its current state in the case that bn = 1, the “XOR” operation between the previous mapped bit mn−1 and the current data bit bn achieves this outcome. Hence, DBPSK mapping can be implemented by applying the following:

mn = mn−1 ⊕ bn.

III. LOW-COMPLEXITY RECURSIVE DD-LDPC ENCODING
A. DD-LDPC Code
In the encoding process, the generator matrix is a necessary component, as highlighted by [74]. Obtaining the generator matrix typically involves transformations from the parity check matrix, often accomplished through techniques such as Gaussian elimination. However, while the parity check matrix is sparse, this characteristic is not guaranteed for the generator matrix. This lack of sparsity contributes to increased storage requirements and encoding complexity, as mentioned by [74]. A strategy to address this challenge involves constraining a submatrix of the parity check matrix (H) to be lower triangular. This approach eliminates the necessity of deriving a generator matrix, resulting in linear encoding complexity. Further, this submatrix is chosen to have a dual diagonal substructure combined with sparse circulant permutation matrices resulting in a category of LDPC encoders called DD-LDPC codes [59], [60], [61]. Notably, this technique is employed in the LDPC encoding scheme of the DVB-S2 standard [74], [75]. In our work, we adapt and refine this method to create a custom DD-LDPC code. The modifications aim to minimize both encoding and decoding complexity while preserving satisfactory BER performance and achieving high throughput.

1) CubeSat systems are in general very limited in terms of availability of energy as well as allowable sizes and weights. Such limitations are reflected on our design by seeking methodologies to encode and decode LDPC codes with higher efficiency and lower complexity.
2) The trend in modern CubeSat applications is move from typical low rate communication systems used only for command and telemetry to high throughput communications with higher data rates and higher reliability for communications applications utilizing CubeSat constellations. Such trend is reflected on our objective to use LDPC codes with faster encoding and decoding operations mandating the use of shorter code lengths and higher code rates.

The utilization of the dual diagonal form for the submatrix results in a noteworthy reduction in the hardware resources required for the encoding process. This efficiency stems from the fact that the computation of each parity check bit is solely dependent on the preceding parity check bit and the associated data bits, as dictated by A(n−k)×k. Importantly, this matrix is prestored at the transceiver, further streamlining the encoding calculations. In addition, in comparison with LDPC parity check matrices with a lower triangular form, our dual diagonal structure requires fewer “XOR” operations for encoding, resulting in a reduced requirement for “XOR” gates in the hardware implementation. Moreover, the dual diagonal format entails a lower overall number of computations during encoding. Specifically, encoding based on the defined construction involves k × (n − k) computations, whereas traditional encoding methods require (n2 − k2)/2 computations.

IV. RESOURCE-EFFICIENT DD-LDPC DECODING
A. Log-Likelihood Ratio (LLR)-Based Decoding Algorithm
A customized hybrid iterative decoding algorithm for LDPC codes, which is based on modifications of the MS and BF algorithms is developed. Combining and customizing the MS and BF algorithms leads to an enhanced error correction performance compared to that of the BF algorithm and lower computational and implementation complexity compared to that of the MS algorithm. This hybrid approach capitalizes on the strengths of both algorithms. Moreover, the sparsity of A and dual diagonal structure of B implicitly reduce the number of computations performed by the decoder.2

Fig. 3. Architecture of proposed hybrid BF-MS decoding algorithm.

Decoding starts by initializing the variable node messages using the received codeword, given by (25), and the channel’s likelihood information whose probability density functions (PDFs) are given in (26)–(28), represented as LLRs, which will be explained in detail in subsequent paragraphs. These LLRs reflect the reliability of each bit in the received signal.3 The heart of our proposed hybrid MS-BF algorithm lies in its message passing approach and the decision on choosing one of the two algorithms to decode a certain group of bits while using the other for the remaining bits. The optimal matching between algorithmic choices and particular codeword segments is the key enabler for the reduction of the overall complexity and the improvement of the overall performance. The main concept of our hybrid decoding approach is that more important bits (those that have high variable node degree) are decoded using an MS algorithm while less important bits (those that have low variable node degree) are decoded using a BF algorithm. The high level flow of the proposed hybrid BF-MS decoding algorithm is shown in Fig. 3. During each iteration, the decoder performs the following steps.
i) MS update (variable nodes to check nodes): In the MS step, for each variable node, the decoder calculates messages to be sent to connected check nodes. These messages represent the accumulated reliability information and are computed by finding the minimum of the accumulated LLRs from other variable nodes linked to the same check node. The MS update benefits from its ability to handle high SNRs effectively.
ii) BF update (check nodes to variable nodes): In the BF step, the decoder evaluates the messages sent from check nodes to variable nodes. If a check node indicates an inconsistency (i.e., the parity check equation is not satisfied), the decoder flips the value of the variable node connected to the problematic check node. It is known that a valid decoded codeword r is verified if it satisfies the following:

Then, vector v is extracted from M by employing the nonzero elements of H ensuring that v contains the relevant LLRs for the variable nodes involved in the current check node we are interested in. Based on that, the number of zeros in v is counted. This information is used to determine the reliability of the soft information and influence the decision of flipping bits. If the number of zero elements in v is greater than or equal to 2, the algorithm sets the reliability of the check node, represented by qi, to a low value. This low value indicates that the information from this check node should not be given much weight in the subsequent decisionmaking process. If the number of zero elements in v is equal to 1, qi is calculated as the product of all nonzero elements of v divided by the norm of v. This calculation provides a measure of the consistency between the nonzero elements and their expected values. If there are no zero elements in v, qi is calculated as the product of all elements of v. This

Then, the decoder flips a data bit participating in the unsatisfied equations and checks if they have been satisfied. The condition ze( j) = 0 ensures that the flipping process focuses on error locations. In case all equations have been satisfied, the value of the decoded bit is updated. In case they have not been satisfied, this process is repeated until all the parity check equations are satisfied or the maximum number of decoding iterations is reached. Finally, the decoder outputs both the decoded codeword and the number of decoding iterations.

This can be attributed to the fact that for the 0.5 code rate, there are 256 parity check bits, as compared to only half of that (128 bits) for the 0.75 code rate. Moreover, it is evident that for both code rates there is a very good agreement between the theoretical and simulated BER curves with a slight mismatch. Such slight mismatch is typical in BER calculations of LDPC codes based on the GA as can be seen, for instance, in [92] and [96]. This mismatch is largely due to the several assumptions and approximations employed in the calculation of the BER formula including the GA, the all-zero codeword assumption, and the infinite block length assumption. Obtaining a theoretical upper/lower bound on the simulated BER is generally not possible since the calculation of the LDPC theoretical BER formula is only approximate [77], [78], [93]. Results obtained in similar works fail to achieve such strict upper/lower bound because the curve is subject to many approximations. Overall, the match between our simulated and theoretical BER curves compares very well with other results in the literature [77], [78], [92], [93], [96]. B. BER Performance
Fig. 6 illustrates the BER performance of the decoder using BPSK and DBPSK, which is the modulation scheme used for our CubeSat system. As expected, the coded BPSK system has better BER performance compared to the coded DBPSK system. Nevertheless, DBPSK scheme is selected for our transceiver due to its lower implantation complexity. The coded BPSK BER curve is plotted because it will be referred to later in the BER comparison between different algorithms. It is seen that at low Eb/N0, the BER of the coded DBPSK scheme is marginally lower than that of the uncoded DBPSK scheme. However, this is typical with other LDPC decoding algorithms as demonstrated in Fig. 9. On the other hand, for moderate and high Eb/N0, the coded scheme has significantly better BER performance compared to the uncoded scheme, as expected. For instance, at a BER of 10−6, the uncoded DBPSK Eb/N0 requirement is 11.2 dB, while the coded DBPSK Eb/N0 requirement is only 7.8 dB.
In Fig. 7, the theoretical and simulated BER curves, using DBPSK, are plotted for different numbers of maximum decoding iterations. It can be noticed that the theoretical BER more closely matches the simulated BER for higher iterations. For higher number of iterations, the decoder’s state gets closer to the true codeword. Therefore, the effects of error propagation become less dominant, and the decoding process starts to resemble the simplifying assumptions made in the theoretical model. As the decoder converges toward the true codeword, the impact of simplifying

Furthermore, it is observed that the match between the theoretical and simulated BER is improved for the longer codeword. For longer codewords, the improved error correction capability and longer block length bring the real decoder behavior closer to the assumptions of the theoretical model, specifically the infinite block length assumption. Consequently, we observe a better match between the theoretical and simulated BER curves for the 1024 codeword compared to the 512 codeword. It is worth noting that while the theoretical BER expression does not directly depend on the code rate or code length, it depends on the degree distributions which change for different code parameters.

VI. CONCLUSION
A resource-efficient high data rate high-performance LDPC channel coding scheme has been proposed for CubeSat applications. We developed improved IRA DD-LDPC recursive encoding and MS-BF LLR-based decoding algorithms based on a customized irregular structured dual diagonal parity check matrix. Furthermore, we considered the practical implementation aspects of modulator, demodulator, and pulse shaping filter of the transceiver. A thorough theoretical and numerical analysis of the computational complexity and BER performance of the proposed channel coding and modulation scheme was presented. The conducted simulation has not only validated the functionality but also demonstrated the enhanced performance of the proposed DD-LDPC algorithms. A comprehensive estimation of the type and number of operations required by each block was provided. This step is crucial for assessing the hardware resources necessary for implementing the system on FPGA, enabling the development of an efficient hardware architecture that maximizes the data rate while minimizing the power consumption, a significant challenge in CubeSat transceiver designs [98]. Notably, our system boasts the highest code rate of 3/4 among comparable works, supports the highest information rate, approximately 22.5 Mbps,

Moreover, our proposed DD-LDPC scheme has better BER performance than both BF and GDBF algorithms and is only outperformed by more complex algorithms such as MS. Preliminary implementation on the Cyclone V FPGA shows that the system is capable of achieving up to 1 Gbps at less than 3 W.
