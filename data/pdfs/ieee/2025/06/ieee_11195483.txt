AbstractвҖ”The problem of determining the existence of a protograph-based QC-LDPC code with girth g and shortest blocklength for a prescribed protograph has been investigated in the literature. In some of these works, the goal was to obtain an algebraic expression for the blocklength in terms of the dimensions of the biadjacency matrix of the protograph and a lifting factor N . An algebraic expression has been found for N when g > 4 is desired in the case of the fully connected (allone) protograph but only lower bounds for the cases with larger girth. Computer search has been used in most cases to determine the exact value for N that gives the shortest blocklength for a particular protograph. In this paper, we present a promising connection between the lifting factor N (and therefore the blocklength of a QC-LDPC code) and the product of the pivots of the Hermite normal form of a matrix constructed from certain substructures in the protograph called tailless backtrackless closed (TBC) walks.
I. INTRODUCTION
Quasi-cyclic (QC) low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are an attractive subclass of the well-known family of LDPC codes invented by Gallager [1] in the early 1960s. The capacity-approaching performance of members of the family of LDPC codes has made them suitable for applications such as deep space communication and satellite communications. QC-LDPC codes are attractive, in particular, since they can be encoded in linear time with shift registers [2] and their simple structure leads to efficiencies in decoder design [3]. Moreover, it has been shown that QC-LDPC codes perform well compared to random LDPC codes for short to moderate blocklengths [4], [5]. These are some of the reasons why QCLDPC codes appear in many industry standards, including those developed by the Consultative Committee for Space Data Systems (CCSDS) [6].
It is well-known that the structure of QC-LDPC codes, and the graph representation of an LDPC code in general, plays a fundamental role in determining the performance of the code under iterative decoding algorithms. The girth [7], together with the number of short cycles [8], and other graphical structures composed of short cycles, such as trapping sets [9], are important parameters to measure the iterative decoding performance of the code. As a consequence, once a target application is considered and a protograph is prescribed, a question that arises is whether it is possible to reduce, or eliminate, these undesirable substructures.
The problem of determining the existence of a protographbased QC-LDPC code with girth g and shortest blocklength for a prescribed protograph, which would be helpful to answer

the question in the previous paragraph, has been investigated in the literature [10]вҖ“[16]. In some of these works, the goal was to obtain an algebraic expression for the blocklength in terms of the dimensions of the biadjacency matrix of the protograph and a lifting factor N . An algebraic expression has been found for N when g > 4 is desired in the case of the fully connected (all-one) protograph but only lower bounds for the cases with larger girth [10], [14], [16]. Computer search has been used in most cases to determine the exact value for N that gives the shortest blocklength for a particular protograph.
In this paper, we present a promising connection between the lifting factor N (and therefore the blocklength of a QCLDPC code) and the product of the pivots of the Hermite normal form of a matrix constructed from certain substructures in the protograph called tailless backtrackless closed (TBC) walks. This approach is also used to give algebraic constructions of QC-LDPC codes.

V. HERMITE NORMAL FORM
An m Г— n matrix A is unimodular if it has rank m, integer entries, and det(B) = 0, Вұ1, for every m Г— m submatrix B

of A. In particular, a square matrix U is unimodular if it has integer entries and det(U ) = Вұ1.
Theorem 10 ([25]). Let M be an m Г— n matrix with integer entries. There exists an m Г— m unimodular matrix U such that H = U M , and where the matrix H = (hij) satisfies the following conditions:
1) H is upper triangular (that is, hij = 0 for i > j), and any rows of zeros are located below any other row.
2) The leading coefficient, or pivot, of a nonzero row is always strictly to the right of the leading coefficient of the row above it; moreover, it is positive.
3) The elements below pivots are zero and the elements above pivots are nonnegative and strictly smaller than the pivot.
Definition 11. The matrix H in Theorem 10 is called the Hermite normal form of the matrix M .

Remark 17. We computed the matrix of coefficients of all the TBC walks of length 6 and obtained rank 8, which means that it is not possible to get rank 9 (the number of exponents to choose in the protograph) no matter what combination of TBC walks is chosen. In consequence, every rank-8 system of equations will have one free variable.

VIII. CONJECTURE ON Nmin
Let us suppose that we want to construct a QC-LDPC code from a specific protograph having biadjacency matrix B = (bij)ncГ—nv . Once a specific lifting factor N is chosen, it is natural to ask whether we can avoid short cycles for that selection of N or, in other words, whether we can get a target girth g for that particular value of N . One way to determine if this is possible is by knowing what is the smallest lifting factor, denoted by Nmin, for which this is possible.
Definition 18. Let a and b be two integers such that a вүӨ b. Then a is said to be sufficiently close to b if a is in the range [b, b + c] for a very small positive integer c.
Conjecture 19. Let H be a polynomial parity-check matrix of a QC-LDPC code constructed from a protograph G and assume that H has girth g for lifting factor N . If N is sufficiently close to the smallest lifting factor Nmin for which there exists a polynomial parity-check matrix constructed from G having girth g, then the product of the pivots of the Hermite normal form of the matrix of coefficients of the TBC walks in W (g, 1) is equal to N .

IX. CONCLUDING REMARKS
In this paper, we presented a connection between the lifting factor N (and therefore the blocklength of a QC-LDPC code) and the product of the pivots of the Hermite normal form of the matrix of coefficients of certain TBC walks in the protograph. This approach is promising to solve the problem of determining the existence of a protograph-based QC-LDPC code with girth g and shortest blocklength for a prescribed protograph. This strategy was also used to give algebraic constructions of QC-LDPC codes.
