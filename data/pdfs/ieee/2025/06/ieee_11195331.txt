Abstract—Spatially Coupled Low-Density Parity-Check (SCLDPC) codes are characterized by very long codeword lengths. For this reason, they are usually decoded with sliding window algorithms, which allow piecewise processing and decoding of the codeword symbols. In order to mitigate error propagation, it is possible to adapt strategies, such as non-uniform window sizes and node doping. In this paper, we propose a novel adaptive decoding schedule, which can be integrated with the aforementioned strategies. Numerical results confirm that the proposed approach can successfully detect error propagation events with more accuracy than conventional log-likelihood ratio-based approaches. Simulation results show that the error rate performance of time-invariant SC-LDPC codes significantly improves when the proposed strategies are adopted.
Index Terms—SC-LDPC codes, sliding window decoding.
I. INTRODUCTION
Spatially coupled low-density parity-check (SC-LDPC) codes, first introduced as LDPC convolutional codes [1], are attracting significant attention due to their optimal asymptotic performance [2], [3]. A convenient way to decode SC-LDPC codes is through sliding window decoders, which perform belief propagation (BP) only on a portion of the code Tanner graph [4], resulting in significant savings in decoding latency, complexity, and storage. This is particularly relevant for streaming applications, where the frame length is extremely large, but also important for terminated transmissions. The known issue of error propagation in sliding window decoding under certain conditions [5] provides the main motivation for this work. Unlike the pipelined decoding originally proposed in [1], this paper considers iterative decoding within the window. This design choice enables the use of adaptive mechanisms such as the stopping criterion we propose.
Several strategies for error propagation mitigation in SCLDPC codes have been proposed in the literature. In [6], the authors propose adaptive solutions to mitigate the problem: adaptively increasing the number of iterations of decoding

These techniques require modification of the decoding schedule. Another technique was proposed in [7], where the authors suggest strategically including some low-degree check nodes in the code coupled graph (check node doping). In this case, the decoding schedule needs to be altered (in doped positions) and it also causes a rate loss, the value of which depends on the number of doped positions. Variable node doping was proposed in [8]. This procedure consists in periodically setting some bits to 0 at the encoder. In this case, the decoding process does not change, but it still results in a rate loss. The idea of adaptive doping was introduced in [9], [10], where the authors propose sending doping requests to the encoder only when the average of the total log-likelihood ratios (LLRs) within certain decoding windows does not exceed a predetermined threshold. In this case, also, a rate loss is unavoidable, and it is necessary to have access to a noiseless binary feedback channel to transmit the doping requests. A scaling law to predict performance of SC-LDPC codes with doping was presented in [11].
In this paper, we propose a novel method for detecting the start of an error propagation event, enabling the mitigation procedure to be triggered at the correct moment, regardless of the chosen approach. Specifically, we investigate an iteration threshold trigger that is dependent on a partial syndrome state of the SC-LDPC code window decoder. This is a sort of side-channel analysis (see [12], for example). The proposed technique has a significantly lower implementation complexity compared to the LLR-based strategy proposed in [6], and employed in [7]–[10], where a threshold for the average decoded LLR magnitude should first be determined and then the value must be computed over successive iterations in the decoder and compared to this threshold, resulting in significant complexity and latency costs. If the threshold is not chosen properly, the error rate performance is poor. While, in principle, our approach also requires the determination of an iteration threshold, we show that setting it to the (known) maximum number of iterations run by the decoder yields very good results without further tuning; moreover, our

B. Sliding window decoder

A sliding window decoder applies iterative BP decoding on a portion of the code’s Tanner graph (equivalently, paritycheck matrix). In terms of the matrix, a sliding window of size W ≥ ms + 1 operates on a W c × W a portion of the parity-check matrix with knowledge of any previously decoded symbols. At the end of the graph the window size might effectively increase or decrease, according to the specific decoding schedule employed [15]. Within each window, the first a symbols (target symbols) are decoded, and then the windows slides c rows down and a columns to the right in H. To consider complete parity-check equations during the decoding process, it is necessary to take into account all the symbols participating to the parity-check equations involving the target symbols. In other words, the generic j-th decoding window needs to take into account the min{j, ms}·a columns of H preceding the window (set as read-only symbols). If an error occurs when decoding the symbols operated on by a certain sliding window, the error may propagate to future code blocks due to the memory of the code. When operating near capacity, this can be up to a potentially unlimited number of future symbols (when L → ∞) [9].

In [9], [10], these are set as k0 = 0 and kend = a − 1, i.e., detection is based on the target symbols. It is mentioned in [6] that taking a decision only based on the first a symbols within the decoding window might be sub-optimal. For example, an optimal value of the threshold might depend on ms and on the operating signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). Algorithm 1 does not incorporate any mechanisms to address error propagation. To tackle this, we propose to raise a flag whenever some iterations threshold ι is reached and the parity-check equations under evaluation remain unsatisfied. If the flag is triggered, a mitigation procedure is activated. We apply a specific variant of variable node doping as our mitigation technique. This technique consists in setting the value of the LLRs of read-only symbols to the “clipping” values, when the flag is true, i.e., setting the symbols to be essentially certain and known, which corresponds to a reset in the transmission at the cost of a rate penalty. Other mitigation techniques could also be applied, such as check node doping, adjusting the window size, modifying the maximum number of iterations, etc. The corresponding modified algorithm is shown in Algorithm 2, where φ is the index of the last parity-check equation to be checked within the τ -th decoding window.

V. CONCLUSIONS

The results, in terms of BER performance under sliding window decoding with W = 10 for CA and W = 65 for CB are shown in Figs. 3 and 4, respectively. They show that the partial syndrome and iteration trigger adaptive decoding schedule proposed in Algorithm 2 is very effective, and yields the best error rates for both the considered codes, even though it might lead to a heavy rate loss when Eb/N0 is relatively low. When, instead, Eb/N0 is moderate to high, the rate penalty is negligible. The newly proposed method is particularly fruitful on CB because of its large memory: an undetected error within a certain decoding window would influence (at least) the 12 subsequent decoding windows. Our method performs well in detecting errors, and this has a huge impact on performance. Even though not relevant for the goals of our paper, it is worth noting that, in some cases, systematic doping performs worse than the absence of doping. This occurs because of the rate penalty when no error propagation occurred and “good” LLRs get discarded due to unnecessary doping requests.

We presented a technique for detecting errors during the sliding window decoding of SC-LDPC codes that uses the partial syndrome and the decoder’s iteration count as a trigger for adaptive doping. Notably, our method introduces no significant computational overhead beyond traditional sliding window decoding. Each decoding iteration includes a few additional checks to determine whether the mitigation process should be triggered, but their cost is negligible compared to the overall decoding effort (see [16] for a nice description of the energy-error correction trade-off for windowed decoders of SC-LDPC codes). Our numerical results highlight the remarkable accuracy of this detection method, enabling notable error rate performance improvement in TI SC-LDPC codes for both small and large syndrome former memories, at the cost of a coding rate penalty. Although the analysis in this paper focused on TI codes, due to their convenient representation and ease of reproducibility, the proposed method also performs very well when applied to time-varying SC-LDPC codes.
