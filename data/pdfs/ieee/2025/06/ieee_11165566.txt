Universal Block-Parallel Decoder
Xia An1, Chao Zhang2, Jian Song3
Department of Electronic Engineering,

Abstract—As wireless communication systems evolve, new
frameworks place increasing demands on terminal performance,
including throughput, latency, power consumption, and size.
Software-Defined Radio (SDR) systems have the potential to sup-
port multiple communication standards and enabling on-demand
upgrades due to their flexibility. However, purely software-based
implementations for physical layer protocols reduce efficiency,
necessitating the use of external accelerators such as FPGA
for specialized computation. Specifically, channel decoding con-
tributes a lot to the physical layer’s complexity, and thus is highly
demand for FPGA specialized implementation while retaining
the universality of the decoding architecture in some level. This
paper presents an adaptive quantized and normalized QC-LDPC
universal decoder based on a block-parallel architecture with
pipeline and instruction-driven approach. The proposed decoder
improves throughput and reduces computational complexity
while maintaining flexibility for various QC-LDPC codes. Exper-
imental results demonstrate that the adaptive quantization and
normalization scheme significantly outperforms existing methods
in terms of decoding performance and maximum frequency,
offering a more efficient solution for SDR systems.
Index Terms—low-density parity-check (LDPC) code, adap-
tive quantized and normalized min-sum algorithm (AQNMSA),
block-parallel decoding, universal decoder, 5th-Generation New
Radio (5G-NR)
I. INTRODUCTION
As wireless communication systems continue to evolve,
new communication frameworks are driving higher demands
on performance metrics such as throughput, latency, power
consumption, and size for terminal devices. [1] Software-
Defined Radio (SDR) systems [2], which achieve partial soft-
ware implementation on terminals, offer significant potential
to support a wide range of existing communication standards
while enabling on-demand upgrades to accommodate future
communication technologies.
For higher-layer communication protocols, a fully software-
based implementation is appropriate for flexible implementa-
tion. [3] However, for physical layer communication protocols,
a purely software-based approach significantly reduces the
efficiency of the communication system. Therefore, external
accelerators, coprocessors, and other methods are typically
employed to accelerate computation. [4]
The corresponding author of this work is Chao Zhang.
The channel decoding module contributes more than 40%
of the complexity to the physical layer of a wireless com-
munication system. [5] Thus, its decoding complexity and
performance are crucial to the overall performance of the
system. In SDR communication systems, the channel decoding
module is typically implemented using Field-Programmable
Gate Array (FPGA) [6], Graphics Processing Unit (GPU) [7],
or multi-core Central Processing Unit (CPU) [8]. Compared
to Application-Specific Integrated Circuit (ASIC) implemen-
tations, GPUs and CPUs offer better reconfigurability but
suffer from lower throughput, higher latency, and higher power
consumption. FPGA implementations, on the other hand, are
closer to ASIC in terms of performance, meanwhile less
flexible and requires more development effort.
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [9] have been
widely applied in many communication and broadcasting
standards such as the 5th-Generation New Radio (5G-NR)
[10] [11], Advanced Television System Committee (ATSC) 3.0
[12], and Digital Terrestrial Multimedia Broadcast Advanced
(DTMB-A) [13] [14]. All of the existing LDPC codes in
specifications adopt the quasi-cyclic (QC-) structure. [15]
In the context of LDPC decoding, iterative message passing
(MP) algorithms are usually applied, where the optimal belief
propagation (BP) algorithm, i.e., the sum-product algorithm
(SPA) [16], could provide near optimal decoding threshold
performance but requires relatively high computational re-
sources. The min-sum algorithm (MSA) [17] and its improved
algorithms, e.g., normalized MSA (NMSA) [18], adaptive
NMSA (ANMSA) [19], adaptive quantized and normalized
MSA (AQNMSA) [20], have low computational and storage
overhead, but degraded threshold performance.
To support the universal decoding of QC-LDPC codes,
an FPGA-based LDPC decoder employs a block-parallel
and instruction-driven pipeline architecture for high through-
put and flexible reconfigurability, and an optimal instruction
scheduling strategy is applied to the universal architecture.
[21] However, the universal architecture leads to a degra-
dation in decoding performance. For instance, the block-
parallel architecture, which can effectively support decoding
of different base graphs, is limited by the block size in terms
of throughput. Increasing parallelism improves throughput but
increases resource consumption while reducing area efficiency.

Here we consider an alternative approach to improve the
performance of the universal decoder, i.e., to introduce an
adaptive quantization and normalization strategy to the uni-
versal decoder. By reducing the quantization bit-width of the
internal signals in the decoder, we can simultaneously increase
the throughput and reduce the computational complexity of the
decoder. Furthermore, thanks to the adaptive quantization and
normalization strategy, the degradation of decoding threshold
performance caused by the reduced quantization bit-width is
almost negligible.
The main contributions of this paper are summarized as
follows:
• We demonstrate an adaptive quantized and normalized
QC-LDPC universal block-parallel decoder, which ap-
plies a block-parallel decoding architecture and incor-
porates a pipeline and instruction-driven approach. The
decoder is capable of decoding various QC-LDPC codes
with different base graphs and code rates, and achieves
high throughput and low latency.
• We develope a QC-LDPC universal codec test system
based on FPGA. This test system was used to evaluate the
decoding performance and resource utilization of several
typical modes of the 5G-NR codes.
• We compare the proposed adaptive quantization and
normalization scheme with other schemes. Experimental
results show the advantages of our scheme over other
schems in terms of decoding complexity and throughput.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section
II introduces the adaptive quantization and normalization
strategy as well as the QC-LDPC universal block-parallel
decoder. Section III elaborates on the FPGA implementation
of the adaptive quantization and normalization strategy, and
illustrates the structure of QC-LDPC universal codec test
system. Section IV provides the experiential results of the
universal decoder applying 5G-NR LDPC codes. Section V
concludes the paper.
II. ADAPTIVE QUANTIZATION AND NORMALIZATION FOR
UNIVERSAL DECODER
In this section, we introduce the adaptive quantization
and normalization strategy for LDPC iterative decoding algo-
rithms, and depict the structure of QC-LDPC universal block-
parallel decoder with pipeline structure and instruction-driven
approach.
A. Adaptive Quantization and Normalization Strategy
The adaptive quantization and normalization strategy is de-
signed to reduce the computational complexity of the decoder
while maintaining a good trade-off between decoding perfor-
mance and complexity. The quantization and normalization
process is adaptive in the sense that the decoder adjusts the
quantization intervals and normalization factors of the internal
signals according to the look-up table (LUT) and current
searching indices, i.e., check-ratio and edge-type.
The edge-type is the current node’s type of edge, which
is determined manually by the base graph structure. The
check-ratio is the unsatisfied check nodes rate of the
nodes of current edge-type. In practical implementation,
the LUT for the quantization and normalization parameters
((check-ratio, edge-type), (T, α)) is preconfigured in the de-
coder, and the LUT is determined by the base graph of the
current LDPC code with multi-edge-type density evolution
(MET-DE) tool.
The quantization and normalization process is performed in
the following steps through each iteration:
1) The decoder uses the current decoding result bits
to calculate the current unsatisfied check nodes rate
check-ratiocur of the nodes of current edge-type.
2) The decoder uses the edge-type and check-ratiocur of
the current node to get the current quantization interval
Tcur and normalization factor αcur from the LUT.
3) The decoder uses qL, T0, and Tcur to convert the high
bit-width output of the variable node unit (VNU) into
the low bit-width input of the check node unit (CNU).
4) After the CNU process is done, the decoder uses qH,
T0, and Tcur to convert the low bit-width output of the
CNU into a high bit-width intermediate variable, and
multiplies it by αcur to obtain the high bit-width input
of the VNU.
Note that the above description is regardless of the schedul-
ing strategy of the decoder, and for layered-scheduling, the
VNU process is broken into two steps before and after the
CNU process. In this case, they both needs quantization
conversion and normalization, but use different quantization
intervals and normalization factors.
B. QC-LDPC Universal Block-Parallel Decoder
The decoding process of a single node the i-th layer in
the QC-LDPC universal decoder is shown in Algorithm 1.
The decoder employs a block-parallel architecture and layered-
scheduling, where the decoding process of the nodes in the
same lifting block are performed in parallel, and the decoding
process of each layer is executed in sequence. A QC-LDPC
shift network (QSN) is applied to support the data shifting of
the nodes in the same lifting block.
We apply a 6-stage pipeline structure to the QC-LDPC uni-
versal block-parallel decoder, which consists of the following
stages: Instruction Processing (IP), Barrel Shifting (BS), Q
value Calculation (QCa), Q value Comparison (QCo), A value
Update (AU), A value Write back (AW). The function of each
stage is as follows:
1) IP: The IP stage is responsible for fetching the in-
struction from the instruction memory and decoding the
instruction to determine the operation to be performed
in the current cycle.
2) BS: The BS stage is responsible for shifting the input
data to the correct position according to the instruction.
3) QCa: The QCa stage is responsible for the VNU process
before CNU, i.e., calculating the Q value of the current
node based on the input data.

B. Structure of QC-LDPC Universal Codec Test System
In this section, we provide a detailed description of the
structure of the QC-LDPC universal codec test system. The
test system consists of the following modules: Binary Signal
Generator, LDPC Encoder, Channel, LDPC Decoder, and
Result Examination. The structure of the test system is shown
in Fig. 3. For the channel module, we employ an open-
source HDL code for generating additive white Gaussian noise
(AWGN) [22]. The original information bits output by the
Binary Signal Generator are cached into the Info Bit RAM.
When the decoder completes the decoding operation and
outputs the hard-decision bits, these information bits are read
from the RAM and compared to perform the verification
process. The results are recorded in the form of frame errors.
Specifically, if any bit error occurs in the information bit
positions of a single frame (or in other words, a codeword),
it is counted as one frame error. Simultaneously, the total

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we have presented an adaptive quantized and
normalized QC-LDPC universal block-parallel decoder based
on a pipeline and instruction-driven approach. The proposed
decoder improves decoding performance and throughput while
maintaining flexibility for various QC-LDPC codes. Exper-
imental results demonstrate that the adaptive quantization
and normalization scheme outperforms existing methods in
terms of decoding performance and maximum frequency, and
meanwhile maintain a moderate FPGA resource utilization,
offering a more efficient solution for SDR systems.
