Belief Propagation Decoding for Short Codes
on Structured Sparse Parity-Check Matrices

Abstract—As successfully adopted in standard long code scenarios, belief propagation (BP) decoding has been considered a promising universal decoding candidate for next-generation wireless communications. However, when applied to short codes, BP decoding suffers from poor error correction performance due to harmful cycle structures in the Tanner graph. In this paper, we address this issue by designing a structured, sparse parity-check matrix (ssPCM) framework, composed of multiple cycle-free parity-check row blocks (PCRBs). The resulting ssPCMs feature regular row weights and perform better than the state-of-theart 4-cycle-free row redundant PCMs across Bose-ChaudhuriHocquenghem (BCH) codes of length 63.
I. INTRODUCTION
Driven by emerging ultra-reliable and low-latency communications (uRLLC), the demand for robust short-length channel codes has grown substantially [1], [2]. Meanwhile, the concept of unified decoding has been proposed toward next-generation wireless communications, aimed at efficiently handling various channel codes across standards and scenarios [3]–[6]. Therefore, research on universal decoding for short codes has drawn increasing attention, which gave rise to the revisit to ordered statistics decoding (OSD) [7], the transformation to polar decoding [8], the enhancement to belief propagation (BP) decoding [9], [10], and new algorithms such as guessing random additive noise decoding (GRAND) [11]. Each approach exhibits distinct trade-offs in terms of decoding complexity, error correction capability, and scalability across diverse code lengths and rates. Given that practical universal decoders should ideally support both short and long codes, and the current reliance on low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes and BP decoding for long code standards [12], universal BP decoding stands out as one of the most promising solutions.
By factorizing the maximum likelihood (ML) objective, BP decoding transforms a parity-check matrix (PCM) into a bipartite Tanner graph composed of check nodes (CNs) and variable nodes (VNs) [13]. When the PCM is sufficiently sparse, BP decoding can closely approach ML performance, which explains the success of LDPC codes in long code scenarios. However, for short codes, especially non-LDPC short codes, the PCM is frequently dense with numerous short cycles, typically length-4 cycles (4-cycles). Such short cycles increase bit-to-bit correlation and degrade error correction performance. One classical strategy to mitigate this issue

is to increase the number of BP decoding attempts, each using a different PCM variation [14]–[16]. Another more recent approach leverages neural networks to adjust messagepassing parameters of BP decoding, known as neural BP [17]. Although both approaches are capable of improving performance, they remain constrained by the high density of short cycles within the limited dimension of performed PCMs.
A promising avenue for further enhancement is to expand the PCM dimension, thereby enabling more sparsity while preserving the original parity constraints. In particular, by introducing auxiliary VNs (AVNs), it becomes possible to remove dense 4-cycles and mitigate short-cycle effects [18]– [20]. The main challenge is to avoid excessive AVNs, as their unknown messages can undermine the performance benefits gained from sparsity. In our previous work [10], [21], we tackled this challenge by removing 4-cycles from a redundantrow PCM with low row weights and then carefully designing the row selection rule to minimize the number of AVNs and maximize the connectivity among 6-cycles. Numerical simulations on RM(32, 16), BCH(63, 45), and eBCH(64, 24) codes exhibited near-ML performance. Nevertheless, for many other short codes, removing all 4-cycles sometimes requires adding hundreds of AVNs, thus weakening the cycle connectivity among low-degree AVNs and limiting performance gains.
In this paper, we propose a new framework for extracting a structured, sparse PCM (ssPCM) for short codes, combined from multiple parity-check row blocks (PCRBs). Each PCRB consists of all coded bit columns and one AVN column, with no cycles within a single block. We further prove that in the proposed ssPCM, there are no cycles exclusively among AVNs. For BCH codes of length 63, the proposed ssPCM shows more favorable cycle characteristics, more regular row weights, and enhanced error correction performance than the extracted 4-cycle-free row-redundant PCM in [10], achieving near-ML decoding performance.

B. Strategies to Optimize Parity-Check Matrices
Short cycle (e.g., 4-cycles) and small trapping sets degrades the performance of BP decoding [22], [23]. Without altering the inherent parity constraints, one can improve performance by transforming or augmenting the PCM to mitigate these harmful cycle structures. In this section, we summarize several key methods using the Hamming(7, 4) code as a guiding example. Following [24], a trapping set is denoted as T (a, b), which contains a VNs and connects to b odd-degree CNs within the subgraph induced by this set. As shown in Fig. 1, the original PCM of Hamming(7, 4) features three 4-cycles, and each of them forms a smallest trapping sets T (2, 1).

1) Cycle-Reduced PCM: A straightforward approach is to

apply linear row operations to the original PCM H to reduce

the overall number of 4-cycles [9], [15], [25], [26]. While these

operations can make the matrix more sparse, they typically

cannot remove all 4-cycles within the limited (N − K) × N

matrix size. For the Hamming(7, 4) code, the original 3 × 7

PCM already has the minimum achievable number of 4-cycles.

2) 4-Cycle-Free PCM: When s rows and t columns inter-

3) Row-Redundant PCM: Another approach is to extend

the row dimension of PCMs beyond (N − K) by append-

weights [27], [28]. This redundancy increases the connectivity

redundant PCM remains dense and thus still susceptible to

4-cycles, while also increasing computational complexity.

4) 4-Cycle-Free Row-Redundant PCM: In [10], the two

approaches above are combined by progressively selecting

rows from a low-weight parity-check search space, removing

4-cycles while minimizing the number of AVNs. The row size

(≥ N −K) is then determined by maximizing the connectivity

among 6-cycles to mitigate the impact of small trapping sets

further. For Hamming(7, 4), this approach selects 6 rows and

removes all 4-cycles. Shown in Fig. 1, it achieves the lowest

density and the highest minimum trapping set size T (3, 3).

III. PROPOSED STRUCTURED SPARSE PARITY-CHECK MATRICES FOR BP DECODING
Although appending redundant rows disrupts small trapping sets and introducing AVNs increases matrix sparsity, excessively adding AVNs may be counterproductive. Since

AVNs behave similarly to punctured VNs in rate-compatible LDPC codes, which do not receive channel messages and must instead obtain messages from coded bits during iterations, short cycles completely within low-degree AVNs are especially prone to trapping states. To address this issue, we propose a structured PCM design framework that limits the negative impact of AVNs on sparsifying PCMs of short codes. In the resulting ssPCM, every AVN is connected with coded bits, which guarantees its message recovery in one iteration. We prove that there is no cycle that lies entirely between AVNs and that no AVN participates in a 4-cycle. As a result, our design fulfills the three criteria outlined in [29], which target both sparsity and robust error correction performance.

A. Cycle-Free Parity-Check Row Block

We first focus on highly symmetric codes whose minimumweight PCMs generally satisfy the rank requirement N − K. Let w0 denote the minimum weight of all parity-check rows. We state the following lemma:

Lemma 1. Let hi1 and hi2 be two distinct minimum-weight parity-check rows, each of weight w0. Then, the number of overlapped columns between hi1 and hi2 is at most w0/2.
Proof. Let t be the number of overlapping 1s between hi1 and hi2 . Suppose, for contradiction, that t > w0/2. Consider the row hi3 = hi1 ⊕ hi2 . The weight of hi3 is wi3 = (w0 − t) + (w0 − t) = 2w0 − 2t. Since t > w0/2, it follows that wi3 < w0, which contradicts the minimality of w0. Therefore, t cannot exceed w0/2.

Next, we select s minimum-weight rows {ci1 , ci2 , · · · , cis }
that intersect in exactly t = w0/2 columns {vj1 , vj2 , · · · , vjt }. Introduce an AVN v′ and form an extra parity-check row:

c′ : v1 ⊕ v2 ⊕ · · · ⊕ vt ⊕ v′ = 0.

(3)

Add this new CN c′ to the selected s rows. The resulting s + 1 rows, with s×t 1s removed, constitute a PCRB with a uniform row weight of w0/2 + 1. We establish the following lemma:

Lemma 2. Within a PCRB, the weight of each coded bit column is at most 1, and there are no cycles of any length.

Proof. Suppose there exists a coded bit column h:,j with weight greater than 1. Let hi1,j = 1 and hi2,j = 1. Since t = w0/2 positions of 1s in hi1 and hi2 are excluded to the newly added row c′, the overlap between these two rows must
exceed w0/2, which contradicts Lemma 1. Thus, each coded bit column has the weight of at most 1 within a PCRB.
The only column that may appear in multiple rows is the AVN column v′, but exactly one AVN is introduced per PCRB.

weight greater than 1, the PCRB is cycle-free.

Lemma 3. No AVN is involved in any 4-cycles, and there are no cycles of any length exclusively among AVNs.

Proof. Each AVN is connected only to CNs within a single PCRB. According to Lemma 2, no cycles exists within any single PCRB, thus no AVN can participate in a 4-cycle. Moreover, since different AVNs are located in different PCRBs, they do not share the same CNs. Therefore, no cycles of any length can be formed entirely among AVNs.

The approximate cycle extrinsic message degree (ACE) is a widely used metric to assess cycle connectivity in finite-length LDPC design [22]. The ACE of a cycle is defined as the total number of edges connected to CNs not involved in this cycle. A large minimum ACE for short cycles generally indicates a larger minimum trapping set size. Let v = {v˜1, v˜2, · · · , v˜N } represent the sorted set of coded bit columns, where their degrees d(v) satisfy d(v˜1) ≤ d(v˜2) ≤ · · · ≤ d(v˜N ). Then, we have the following theorem.
Theorem 1. Within a PCRB, any cycle that includes one or more AVNs must contain at least two coded bit VNs. Furthermore, for any 6-cycle involving AVNs, the minimum ACE value is bounded below by smin + d(v˜1) + d(v˜2) − 5.
Proof. By Lemma 3, no AVN is involved in 4-cycles. Consider a cycle of length g ≥ 6 that includes at least one AVN. Suppose, for contradiction, that such a g-cycle contains fewer than two coded bit VNs. Then, the number of AVNs in this cycle will be at least g/2 − 1. Since each AVN connects with two CNs to form a cycle and no two AVNs share a same CN, the total number of CNs in this cycle is at least 2 × (g/2 − 1) = g − 2. For g ≥ 6, we have g − 2 > g/2, which contradicts the bipartite structure of a cycle. Hence, any cycle containing AVNs must include at least two coded bit VNs.
Moreover, by Lemma 3, a 6-cycle cannot form solely among AVNs. Consequently, any 6-cycles involving AVNs contain

C. Progressive Parity-Check Row Block Construction
We start by defining a parity-check row search space. If the minimum-weight parity-check rows alone suffice to meet the rank of N − K, then all such rows form the search space. Otherwise, we construct a low-weight search space based on Algorithm 2 in [10], with row weights in {w0, w1, · · · , wL}. For each row weight wl, 0 ≤ l ≤ L, we progressively create PRCBs until the rank limit provided by all weight-wl rows is reached. Given a maximum number of intersection columns t, which is not necessarily equal to wl/2, introducing an AVN and an ACN can still generate a PCRB satisfying the properties of Lemma 2. In particular, if any two rows overlap in at most one column, it is possible to assemble a special weight-wl PCRB free of the AVN, which avoids 4-cycles (though larger cycles may remain). Since such a PCRB has no AVN, it still adheres to Lemma 3 and Theorem 1 when combined with other PCRBs. Fig. 2 illustrates the structure of combined B PCRBs. The block size s + 1 and the eliminated-overlap column size t may vary with blocks due to the progressive construction procedure and possible different row weights.
For each PCRB selection, every pair of check rows in the current search space is traversed to find the PCRB that maximizes the block row size s + 1. Among all such candidates, the following three rules guide the final selection:
1) increasing the rank, 2) minimizing the number of 4-cycles in the ssPCM, 3) minimizing the variance of coded bit column weights.
Since 4-cycles may occur across two different PCRBs, Rule 2 aims to reduce their impact on BP decoding. Moreover,

Rule 3 prevents the formation of coded bit columns with too small weights, which promotes strong connectivity of 6-cycles involving AVNs according to Theorem 1.
The progressive PCRB combination procedure can terminate when the rank is satisfied, which generates a matrix labeled ssPCM-I with rank N − K + B. Alternatively, we can continue appending more PCRBs to further increase the cycle connectivity, in line with Theorem 1. The number of PCRBs can be determined by Monte-Carlo simulation for performance or constrained by acceptable computational complexity (e.g., the maximum number of allowable edges). We refer to the extracted matrix in this mode as ssPCM-II.
IV. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
In this section, we evaluate the performance of BP decoding on the proposed ssPCM and compare it against alternative PCMs. The BP decoder runs for up to 20 iterations with a flooding schedule. For ssPCMs and other girth-4 PCMs, which have regular or nearly regular row weights, we set α = 0.5 in accordance with the guidelines in [31]. By contrast, for the 4-cycle-free PCMs, which have largely irregular row weights, we follow [10] to use α = 0.7 for a good trade-off. All frame error rate (FER) curves are plotted down to 10−6 to meet the stringent requirements of uRLLC. We also report the edge count of PCMs to provide insight into decoding complexity.

B. Length-63 BCH Codes
Fig. 4 presents the FER performance of ssPCM-I and ssPCM-II for primitive BCH codes of length 63, where ssPCM-II is chosen to maximize FER performance under an edge limit of 2, 000. Apart from BCH(63, 57), BP decoding on the dense original PCMs ( ) consistently underperforms Berlekamp-Massey (BM) decoding ( ), which indicates the harmness of dense 4-cycles. Sparsifying the matrix into ssPCM-I substantially enhances performance with only a few hundred edges ( ). Since the number of PCRBs precisely meets the rank condition, the coded bit column weights in ssPCM-I are moderate. However, once the PCRB has a large size (for K = 18, 24, and 45), the improvement is more pronounced due to a high ACE value of 6-cycles. As more PCRBs are combined, the weight of coded bit columns continuously grows, which creates ssPCM-II with further enhanced performance ( ). We also compare our proposed ssPCM with the 4-cycle-free row-redundant PCM in [10] ( ). For K = 45, both methods produce a 63 × 72 PCM with the same matrix structure. However, for other information lengths, the

strategy of [10] prioritizes removing all 4-cycles, which often necessitates many AVNs. In such cases, short cycles may form entirely in the AVN columns, which reduces their connectivity to coded bits and lowers the ACE value. For K = 16, 18, and 30, ssPCM-II outperforms the PCM from [10] by nearly 1 dB with only 1.2× ∼ 1.4× complexity.
V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we propose a new method to extract a structured, sparse parity-check matrix (ssPCM) for short codes. By ensuring robust connectivity of cycles involving auxiliary bits, the proposed ssPCM, with improved sparsity, achieves near-ML performance for most of length-63 BCH codes. The structure of PCRBs, characterized by regular row weights, enables unified message-passing parameters and efficient implementations. The enhanced BP on a single ssPCM can be further combined with multiple BP attempts and neural BP for systematic optimization, and also extended to longer codes.
