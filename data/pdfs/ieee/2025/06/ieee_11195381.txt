Protograph-Based LDPC Codes with Local Irregularity
Vincent Wu¨st, Erdem Eray Cil, and Laurent Schmalen Communications Engineering Lab, Karlsruhe Institute of Technology (KIT), 76131 Karlsruhe, Germany
vincent.wuest@kit.edu

Abstract—Forward error correcting (FEC) codes are used in many communication standards with a wide range of requirements. FEC codes should work close to capacity, achieve low error floors, and have low decoding complexity. In this paper, we propose a novel category of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, based on protograph codes with local irregularity. This new code family generalizes conventional protograph-based LDPC codes and is capable of reducing the iterative decoding threshold of the conventional counterpart. We introduce an adapted version of the protograph extrinsic information transfer (PEXIT) algorithm to estimate decoding thresholds on the binaryinput additive white Gaussian noise channel, perform optimizations on the local irregularity, and simulate the performance of some constructed codes.
Index Terms—LDPC codes, Multi-Edge-Type LDPC codes, Protograph-based LDPC codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
O NE of the most heavily used families of forward error correcting (FEC) codes over the last decades is the family of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. LDPC codes gained interest because they can perform at rates close to the capacity [1] and have lower error floors than, e.g., Turbo codes [2]. Being an extensively studied subject, different types of LDPC codes were developed, enforcing some structure on LDPC codes that provide enough degrees of freedom to reach favorable iterative decoding thresholds while maintaining a feasible encoding and decoding complexity, low error floors, and linear minimum Hamming distance growth.
One promising and widely used code class are protograph LDPC codes [3]. Multiple heuristic measures to optimize their decoding thresholds, such as introducing a precoding structure, are known to simplify the code construction [2]. A useful tool to derive the iterative decoding threshold and further facilitate the code design is the protograph extrinsic information transfer (PEXIT) analysis [4]. One protograph code ensemble using a precoding structure with a low decoding threshold is the accumulate-repeat-by-4-jagged-accumulate (AR4JA) code [2]. Besides a low decoding threshold, it also has low encoding and decoding complexity and fulfills the necessary condition for protographs to have linear minimum Hamming distance growth. However, the rate-1/2 AR4JA code still has a 0.4 dB

A code family generalizing protograph-based LDPC codes are multi-edge-type LDPC (MET-LDPC) codes [6]. METLDPC codes allow various edge types that can have different number of nodes involved, and other than for protographs, they do not have to inherently consist of regular subgraphs. Being a more general code family, they are expected to achieve better performance than protograph-based codes. However, the optimization of MET-LDPC codes must consider a large number of parameters [6], [7], which might complicate the optimization in practice. Protographs can be seen as a subcategory of MET-LDPC codes, and hence we seek a generalization of protograph-based LDPC codes that enables a simpler optimization than MET-LDPC codes.
The generalization of protographs is based on the idea that introducing local irregularities in subgraphs might improve its decoding threshold. Local irregularities are also present in spatially coupled LDPC (SC-LDPC) codes and contribute to their outstanding performance [8]. Besides the local irregularities at the boundaries, irregular SC-LDPC codes can also be designed [9]. Another approach to introduce irregularities is to mask an existing base graph [10]. The mask determines whether the corresponding connections of a subgraph will be used in the final code. Both SC-LDPC codes and the masking technique provide local irregularities before lifting, which can be modeled by a conventional protograph.
In this paper, we introduce a new generalization of a protograph-based LDPC code called prograph-based code with local irregularity (PLI). PLIs generalize conventional protographs by introducing a local irregularity within each edge type. Note that they are still a subclass of MET-LDPC codes. Their iterative decoding threshold can be calculated by modifying and extending the conventional PEXIT algorithm. We call the adapted version of the PEXIT algorithm PLIEXIT. Using the PLI-EXIT algorithm, we optimize PLIs with respect to the iterative decoding threshold. Since conventional protographs are special cases of PLIs, we use the rate-1/2 AR4JA protograph as an initial point for optimization. By comparing simulation results of both conventional protographs and PLIs over the BI-AWGN channel, we investigate the performance improvements achieved.

II. PROTOGRAPH LDPC CODES
We denote the protographs known in the literature as conventional protographs. We first revisit conventional protographs before introducing PLIs.

A. Conventional Protographs
A protograph can be characterized by a graph with potentially parallel edges, with an example depicted in Fig. 1. The protograph consists of variable nodes (VNs), indicated by circles, and check nodes (CNs), indicated by square boxes, that form a bipartite graph, i.e., VNs can only be connected to CNs and vice versa. We denote the total number of CNs by m (m = 2 in Fig. 1), and the total number of VNs by n (n = 3 in Fig. 1). An edge of the protograph can be characterized by its endpoints and multiplicity (the number of parallel edges). For example, in Fig. 1, the edge between VN v3 and CN c2 has endpoints (2, 3) and multiplicity 2.
Equivalently, another way of characterizing a protograph is to use a protomatrix (also known as a base matrix) B, with B ∈ N0m×n. To obtain a binary code from a protograph, the protograph is lifted. The lifting procedure (also known as the copypermute procedure) yields a parity-check matrix (PCM) H from the base matrix B. Graphically, the lifting procedure can be thought of as copying the base graph S times and then permuting edges that originate from the same VN to CN

connection (i, j) in the base graph such that parallel edges are eliminated. In matrix form, we can lift (1) by replacing each entry Bi,j (the value of B at position (i, j)) with the sum of Bi,j randomly chosen, non-overlapping permutation matrices of size S × S. The final code H is of dimension Sm × Sn and consists of mn block matrices of dimension S × S. We denote the S × S subgraph of H, derived from the entry (i, j) in B, as the local rate-0 code Hi,j.

B. Iterative Decoding Threshold

One of the most important performance indicators for protographs is the iterative decoding threshold [2]. A common tool to determine the iterative decoding threshold is the PEXIT algorithm [4]. The BI-AWGN channel is completely characterized by the fraction Eb/N0, i.e., the energy needed per information bit Eb divided by the channel noise power density N0. The PEXIT algorithm determines whether an iterative decoder converges for a given value of Eb/N0. The update equations to determine the decoding threshold are given in (3)-(6). For a given protograph, PEXIT is said to converge if IApp converges to 1 for all variable nodes vj within some predefined number of iterations.

D. PLI-EXIT
A few adaptions are necessary to generalize the PEXIT algorithm to the PLI-EXIT algorithm. The PLI-EXIT equations are given in (13)-(15). The initialization step and (14) are identical to the conventional case. For the VN to CN update, the MI of the messages that must be considered depends on the various possible combinations of local VN degrees, which must be weighted according to the joint local VN degree

distribution. The MI of messages from VN vj to CN ci can be calculated by averaging over the local VN degree distributions of all the MI of messages from the incoming edges from CNs different from ci (i.e., LM̸=i,j (K̸=i,j)), and by averaging over the local edge perspective degree distributions of the MI of the outgoing message from VN vj to CN ci (i.e., λi,j(ki,j)). For the latter, we consider the edge perspective, because we want to determine the MI of a message on a single edge (despite that the multiplicity might be larger). For the CN to VN update, no averaging mechanism is required because the check nodes are regular, i.e., the amount of incoming edges and outgoing edges are fixed as they are for conventional protographs. The a-posteriori MI needs to be updated in a fashion similar to the VN to CN update. However, as there is only MI of incoming messages to be considered, we average over all the local VN degree distributions (i.e., LM·,j (K·,j)).

B. Multi-Edge Degree Distribution Optimization
When optimizing multiple local degree distributions at the same time, the dimensionality of the optimization problem increases. To simplify the optimization, we perform elementwise optimization. In an iterative process, we optimize one edge after the other and only stick to the optimized local degree distribution if it outperforms the corresponding regular edge. We repeat this process until we do not see any further improvement. In each element-wise optimization we use the same optimizer and the same Ω as in the single-edge case. The edges that improved the decoding threshold when applying a non-trivial local VN degree distribution are (2, 2), (3, 3) and (3, 4). This procedure leads to the code C2. IV. SIMULATION RESULTS

The error rates of both codes C1 and C2 start to drop at their iterative decoding thresholds. However, it should be noted that the error rates of code C2 decay less quickly than those of the conventional protograph-based code CConv constructed with (16). The code C2 also has a higher error floor than the other codes, for which no frame errors were observed at larger values of Eb/N0 during our simulation. When investigating the dominant error patterns within the error floor, we observe that in most cases they involve VNs with degree 2. It is known in the literature [2], [5], [14], that a code with too many degree-2 VNs is prone to have an error floor and lacks linear minimum Hamming distance growth. The conventional rate-1/2 AR4JA code already contains as many degree-2 VNs as possible to fulfill this condition. Both C1 and C2 violate this condition, as the local VN degree distributions introduce additional degree-2 VNs.

After optimizing the local degree distribution for the rate1/2 AR4JA code, we generate a PCM by lifting the protograph. In this paper, we use random permutation matrices for lifting with a lifting factor of S = 100 000 which leads to a block length of 400 000. Such block lengths are used in high-througput (e.g., optical) communications and continuousvariable quantum key distribution [12]. After lifting, all 4cycles were removed, as in [13]. We use sum-product decoding with 1000 decoding iterations to decode after transmission over a BI-AWGN channel.
In Fig. 2, we show the bit error and the frame error rates. The iterative decoding thresholds are shown as vertical dotted lines. The threshold of code C2 was roughly 0.4 dB better than for the conventional protograph and only 0.04 dB below capacity. In case of optimizing a single edge (code C1), the decoding threshold improves by roughly 0.3 dB and is only 0.16 dB below capacity.

V. CONCLUSION
In this work, we introduced protographs with local irregularity, which generalize the concept of conventional protographs by introducing local degree distributions on the edges of the base graph. This leads to a higher degree of freedom when optimizing the code while maintaining a structure that is suitable for optimization. To do so, we use the PLI-EXIT algorithm that extends the PEXIT algorithm by incorporating the generalized model structure. The results obtained by optimizing the local degree distributions significantly outperform the conventional protograph-based code. The reference rate-1/2 AR4JA code was outperformed in terms of the decoding threshold by 0.3 dB and 0.4 dB for different codes we obtained. The error floor observed for the code with an improvement of 0.4 dB in the decoding threshold can most likely be explained by the large number of degree-2 VNs.
