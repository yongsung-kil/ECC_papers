Abstract—This paper focuses on the analysis and design of improved 5G low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes for fasterthan-Nyquist (FTN) signaling. We first propose the protographbased extrinsic information transfer (PEXIT) chart analysis for the LDPC-coded FTN system using the sum-product algorithm (SPA) based on the Ungerboeck observation model, where the distribution of the output mutual information from the detector is approximately derived using least squares fitting. With the proposed PEXIT chart analysis, we then design the improved LDPC codes for the coded FTN signaling aiming to achieve a lower decoding threshold and thereby better error performance. The proposed codes are optimized based on the raptor-like structure of the 5G LDPC codes and also support rate compatibility. The proposed codes reveals two distinct LDPC code design criteria for FTN signaling, i.e., 1) no information bits should be punctured; 2) columns with high column weights should be removed in the base graph. The advantages of the proposed codes are explicitly verified by our numerical results, where noticeable coding gains compared to existing codes and coded Nyquist systems can be observed.
I. INTRODUCTION
With the full commercialization of the fifth-generation (5G) mobile communication, research on the sixth generation (6G) mobile communication is accelerating. Compared to 5G, 6G is expected to impose more stringent requirements on spectral efficiency. To meet these stringent requirements, the adoption of innovative technologies is essential. The traditional Nyquist signaling targets to achieve no inter-symbol interference (ISI) by sacrificing the spectral efficiency. To improve the spectral efficiency, the faster-than-Nyquist (FTN) signaling has become a promising technique that has drawn considerable attention for its potential to boost spectral efficiency compared to the traditional Nyquist signaling.
FTN signaling was firstly applied by Shannon in 1948 [1] and formally introduced by Mazo in 1975 [2]. The basic idea of the FTN signaling is to transmit symbols at rates faster than the Nyquist rate, thereby enhancing the spectral efficiency without increasing the required time frequency resources. Different from traditional Nyquist signaling that avoids ISI by maintaining orthogonality, FTN signaling embraces controlled

ISI and relies on strong equalizers for reliable transmission. The Shannon capacity of FTN signaling can approach the unconstrained capacity of the shaping pulse and has been proven to be higher than that of the Nyquist signaling with a non-sinc shaping pulses [3]. Therefore, FTN signaling has been investigated in various communication applications, such as satellite communications [4], optical communications [5] and wireless communications [6]–[8].
Due to the inherent ISI at the FTN transmitter, desigining tailored channel codes is of significant importance for FTN systems. In [9], rate-1/2 optimal convolutional codes for FTN signaling have been found through convergence analysis and exhaustive search. Furthermore, a special type of convolutional codes named output-retainable convolutional codes has been proposed in [10]. In particular, the code trellis is designed to have overlap with the ISI trellis. This allows joint detection and decoding schemes with reduced complexity. In [11], the block Markov superposition transmission (BMST) codes are applied to the FTN system, leading to a new type of FTN signaling with spatially-coupled structure. In this manner, the lowcomplexity window decoding can be adopted with improved error performance. Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have also been investigated for the FTN signaling in [12], where a unified searching approach is proposed to optimize LDPC codes with a large girth.
Even though some good codes have been reported in the FTN literature, the code design for FTN signaling has not been fully investigated, particularly for supporting rate compatibility. A good example of rate-compatible codes is the 5G LDPC codes that exhibit excellent performance in Nyquist signaling. However, the direct application of such codes to FTN signaling often results in limited performance [13]. Moreover, the performance analysis of 5G LDPC codes for FTN signaling and designing their improved variants in general ISI channels are still an area of interest. Motivated by this, this paper focuses on the analysis and design of improved 5G LDPC codes using the protograph-based extrinsic information transfer (PEXT) chart. The main contributions are listed as follows:
• Analysis for the Coded FTN System: We apply the least-squares fitting to approximate the input-output mutual information (MI) of the FTN detector. According to this result, the PEXIT analysis for the LDPC-coded FTN system is developed.

II. PRELIMINARIES
A. 5G LDPC Codes
The 5G LDPC codes are a type of rate-compatible quasicyclic (QC) LDPC codes with raptor-like structure. There are two base matrices corresponding to two base graphs, i.e., referred to as base graph 1 (BG1) and base graph 2 (BG2), which are specifically designed for different information lengths and code rates [14]. The scatter diagram of BG2 is shown in Fig. 1, where each dot represents a permutation matrix of size Z × Z. In the diagram, the parts A and E consist of circulant permutation matrices and zero matrices, the part I is an identity matrix, O is a zero matrix, and D is a dualdiagonal matrix. Given the base matrices, the parity-check matrices H of size mbZ × nbZ can be constructed by the lifting operator with a lifting factor Z. Typically, to enhance the error performance for Nyquist signaling, the first 2Z bits (corresponding to the two columns of the base graph) of the 5G LDPC codes are punctured.

B. LDPC Code Design for FTN System
In this subsection, we utilize the proposed PEXIT chart analysis to assist the design of protograph-based LDPC codes by evaluating the decoding thresholds of various code structures. Specifically, for practical consideration, we consider three cases for comparison, as outlined below:
• C1: The standard 5G LDPC code, where the first 2Z (corresponding to the first two columns of the base graph) information bits are punctured.
• C2: The same base graph structure as the standard 5G LDPC code but without puncturing of the first 2Z information bits.
• C3: The first two columns of the base graph are removed since they have the maximum column weights compared to others. Note that we also delete some rows in the base graph to ensure that all rows and columns in the base graph have a weight greater than 1. Comparing C1 and C2, it is evident that due to the presence of ISI, puncturing of information bits as commonly adopted for Nyquist systems is not suitable for FTN systems. This is not surprising since the FTN detector generally produces less reliable outputs compared to the Nyquist counterpart and consequently, the punctured information bits are difficult to be recovered. In addition, comparing C3 with C2, removing the two columns with the highest weights can greatly mitigate the error propagation, which often appears in the channel-coded FTN systems due to the insufficient reliable detection outputs. V. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we have presented a PEXIT chart analysis tailored for the LDPC-coded FTN signaling. Based on this analysis, we proposed improved 5G LDPC codes that achieve a lower decoding threshold compared to the standard 5G LDPC codes, while maintaining the raptor-like structural features and also supporting rate compatibility in practice. Our numerical results demonstrate notable performance gains over the standard 5G LDPC codes in the FTN system. Moreover, the proposed LDPC-coded scheme also shows better error performance than the well-designed coded Nyquist counterpart. This validates the effectiveness and potential of the proposed LDPC codes for energy-efficient FTN transmissions.
