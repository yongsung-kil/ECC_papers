Abstract—This work presents FPGA implementation of LowDensity Parity-Check (LDPC) encoder. Low-density coding is an effective method for ensuring reliable communication over noisy channels while minimizing the probability of error. We use Verilog hardware description language (HDL) to simulate this encoder for various code rates. The encoder is implemented on the Xilinx Artix-7 FPGA(XC7A35T-ICPG236C) kit using FPGA 28 nm CMOS technology and DE10-Lite FPGA Kit using FPGA 55 nm CMOS technology.
Index Terms—FPGA Implementation, Linear encoding, Generator matrix complexity, LDPC codes, Speed, Area and Power.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes are strong errorcorrecting method that helps send data reliably over noisy channels. These codes are introduced by Robert Gallager in 1962, however, not widely used for many years [1]. In the late 1990s, advancements in computing and decoding techniques brought them back into focus. LDPC codes are among the most effective coding schemes available because they can provide error correction performance that is nearly equal to the theoretical Shannon limit [2]. These codes can correct a large number of errors, making them highly reliable for noisy communication channels. Today, they are commonly used in wireless networks like 5G and Wi-Fi. The bock diagram of standard communication model is shown in Figure 1. In this model, the source block generates information using sequences, represented as row vectors x of length k. The encoder processes this data and produces a codeword c of length n. The coding rate of any LDPC code is defined as the ratio R = k / n. The codeword c is transmitted over the channel and arrives at the receiver as the vector y. But noise and interference cause y to be a distorted representation of c. Given the sent codeword, the probability density function, which characterizes the possibility of receiving various values, determines the channel’s output y.LDPC codes can be represent in two ways like matrix representation and graphical representation. A low-complexity encoder design and an effective encoding technique were presented by the authors in [9] for the quasicyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes that are utilized in the 5G NR standard. Their technology, as compared to earlier approaches, drastically decreased the amount of memory and hardware area. The 5G NR standard requirements were satisfied by the achieved throughput. However, the study primarily examined the architecture and encoding technique, ignoring aspects such as system integration and error correction performance.
For QC-LDPC code encoding in 5G NR, the authors of [10] suggested a novel partially parallel architecture. This solution was adaptable to all 5G NR codes and increased hardware utilization efficiency (HUE). In order to improve the processing schedule and cut down on encoding time, they employed a genetic algorithm. In comparison to earlier designs, the redesigned encoder produced more throughput, lower latency, and better HUE. Its design, testing, and implementation may be impacted by the LDPC encoder’s increased complexity due to its partially parallel architecture.
A 5G NR parallel QC-LDPC encoding technique was presented by the authors in [11]. With its adaptable architecture, they created a very area-efficient parallel encoder that enables pipelined operations and parallel encoding. Significant area reductions and outstanding performance were demonstrated by the encoder. However, the study was primarily concerned with making sure that 5G LDPC codes were compatible with other base graphs, which made it less appropriate for usage in applications or coding schemes other than 5G.
In order to satisfy the low-latency and high-throughput requirements of 5G NR data transmission, researchers concentrated on lowering hardware latency and power consumption in base stations in [12]. Because of their adaptable design, they can accommodate the various code lengths and rates needed for 5G NR. Excellent data throughput was demonstrated by the experimental results. However, the study focused mostly on speeding up and decreasing latency, perhaps ignoring factors like system integration, design complexity, or error correction performance.
For IEEE 802.11n/ac/ax QC-LDPC codes, the authors in [3] suggested a two-step encoding approach to lower complexity and enable completely parallel designs. Using XOR-gate trees, they presented a VLSI encoding architecture that produced excellent throughput with little hardware consumption. To guarantee dependability and standard compliance, This encoder must be thoroughly tested and verified before being integrated into real-world systems. Any problems with implementation or design could have an impact on the overall dependability and performance of the LDPC encoding process.
A versatile and effective encoder for the IEEE 1901 standard, which is utilized in power line communication (PLC), was presented in a study [13]. The objective was to use less space and electricity while still processing data at the necessary speed. The implementation’s outcomes validated the

encoder’s efficacy. Nevertheless, despite its power and space efficiency design, encoding speed, delay, or error correction capability may be compromised. These elements may have an impact on data transmission’s overall dependability and performance.
The authors of the study [14] proposed a pipelined design as a compact and effective technique for LDPC encoding. They made parity-check matrices simpler by using Gauss Elimination. Both conventional and wave pipelining techniques were employed to overcome the space and delay issues. The suggested designs demonstrated reduced power usage and space requirements when tested and implemented on an FPGA. The LDPC encoders used less memory because to the Maximal Rate Pipelining (MRP) structure. However, the study didn’t address other performance factors; it just looked at power, area, and memory utilization.

E. Comparison of Power at Various Technology Node
In this study, we utilized both the Xilinx Artix-7 FPGA (XC7A35T-ICPG236C) Kit and the DE10-Lite FPGA Kit for circuit implementation. The Xilinx Artix-7 FPGA is manufactured using a 28nm CMOS technology node, whereas the DE10-Lite FPGA is based on 55nm CMOS technology. It is well known that as the technology node decreases, the power consumption of a circuit also decreases due to reduced leakage current and lower switching power. Additionally, a smaller technology node leads to a reduction in chip area, allowing for more compact and efficient circuit designs. To analyze the impact of these differences, we compared the power consumption for a circuit design with a 3-bit input and a 6-bit output, operating at a 1/2 code rate. The results of this comparison are presented in Table 2. Based on the observed data, it is evident that the 28nm CMOS technology used in the Xilinx Artix-7 FPGA consumes significantly less power compared to the 55nm CMOS technology in DE10-Lite FPGA.

CONCLUSION
Using the Xilinx Tool, we have developed the linear time encoder in simulation and synthesis. For hardware implementation, the Xilinx Artix-7 FPGA(XC7A35T-ICPG236C) Kit is utilized. The algorithm acknowledges the inputs as such message, size of the H-matrix, and produces the output of the encoded codeword. This paper highlights the critical role of LDPC in communication systems and offers a comprehensive method for efficient power management. The suggested system drastically lowers power consumption, increases frequency control flexibility, and boosts overall system efficiency, representing a major advancement in the field and offering a viable way to improve power management in communication systems.
