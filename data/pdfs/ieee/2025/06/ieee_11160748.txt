A Practical Method for Power Saving in 4G, 5G, and Beyond 5G Channel Decoders Using RBIR

Anusha Gunturu, and Ashok Kumar Reddy Chavva, Senior Member, IEEE Beyond 5G Team, Samsung R&D Institute Bangalore, India Email: {anusha.g, ashok.chavva}@samsung.com

Abstract—With sustainability as one of the key requirements and the design principles of the sixth generation (6G) communications, implementation based power saving solutions that benefit both base station (BS) and user equipment (UE) sides have gained significant interest in the cellular industry research. Channel decoding is one of the receiver modules that can help reduce the power consumption of the receiver significantly, and is applicable to both BS and UE. In this paper, we propose a universal list size and iteration number predictor (ULIP), for reducing the power consumption, applicable for 4G, 5G and beyond-5G channel decoders. We propose to use a link abstraction abstraction metric called received bit information rate (RBIR), that captures the time-varying channel conditions to identify and choose the iteration and list sizes for these decoders, to reduce the power consumption. We evaluate the proposed ULIP for regulating the iteration number in turbo and low-density parity-check (LDPC) decoders, used in 4G and 5G data channels, respectively, and the list size in polar decoder, used in 5G control channel. We also verify the proposed ULIP for regulating the list size in the recently introduced polarization-adjusted convolutional (PAC) decoder, a prospective scheme for 6G. We show that the proposed solution has upto 54.7% and 67.4% reduction of iteration numbers in LDPC and turbo decoders, respectively, for a target block error rate (BLER) of 10%, and upto ∼ 92% reduction of list sizes in both polar and PAC decoders, for a target BLER of 0.1%, compared to conventional decoding methods.
Keywords—4G, 5G new radio (NR), 6G, iterative decoder, ldpc codes, list decoder, polar codes, polarization-adjusted convolutional (PAC) codes, received bit information rate (RBIR), turbo codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
Sustainability is identified as one of the international mobile
telecommunications (IMT)-2030 requirements by international
telecommunication union - radiocommunication sector (ITU-
R), for the sixth generation (6G) [1]. With this, both standard
and implementation based solutions are being extensively in-
vestigated for energy saving at both base station (BS) and user
equipment (UE) sides in the cellular industry. The standard
based solutions are being explored since Release 18 in the third
generation partnership project (3GPP), and are being continued
in Release 19 as part of the fifth generation (5G)-Advanced,
which will further be extended to 6G study in Release 20 [1].
In parallel, implementation based solutions are being looked
into, that can have significant impact on energy saving, while
maintaining the compatibility with the existing systems and
deployments. Channel coding is one of the important blocks in the cellular
transmitter-receiver (Tx-Rx) chain that determines the latency
and reliability of the link. The decoders used, specifically in
the cellular receiver chain often operate iteratively or some-
times based on a bit-by-bit list decoding process, leading to

higher latency and power consumption [2]–[6]. For example, the decoders of turbo and low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes operate iteratively, which are used as channel coding schemes in the 3GPP defined fourth generation (4G) long term evolution (LTE) and 5G new radio (NR) cellular technologies, respectively, for data channels in both uplink (UL) and downlink (DL) directions. Successive-cancellation list (SCL) decoder is a list size based decoder, which is used for decoding polar codes in 5G NR UL and DL control channels. The SCL decoder with list size L, decodes bit-by-bit, considering L simultaneous paths in the process till the decoding ends, and can be viewed as L decoder cores running in parallel. Similarly, the recently discovered polarization-adjusted convolutional (PAC) codes, which are being considered for beyond 5G and 6G technologies, also use a list-based decoder, similar to that of SCL, with an additional convolutional operation [2], [3]. Multiple methods are proposed in the literature, independently, for reducing the average number of iteration for turbo and LDPC decoders, and the average list size of polar and PAC codes, that help in reducing the power consumption and the latency of the decoding process [3]–[6]. However, there is no common method introduced in the literature till now, that is universally applicable to regulate the iteration number, I in the iterative decoders, and list size, L in the list-based decoders, utilizing the time-varying and frequencyselective channel conditions opportunistically, to the best of our knowledge.
In commercial deployments, meeting a target block error rate (BLER) is of utmost importance [7]. We will show in this paper that, for a target BLER, the required iteration number in the iterative decoders, and the list size in the listbased decoders vary with signal to noise ratio (SNR). At very high SNR, smaller iteration number/list size is sufficient, whereas, at lower SNR, higher iteration number/list size is required to achieve the same target BLER. Received bit information rate (RBIR) is a link abstraction metric that can map the received SNR, calculated using the time-varying and frequency-selective channel estimates on the reference signals, to the system BLER [8].
In this paper, we propose a practical universal list size and iteration number predictor (ULIP), that is commonly applicable to regulate the iteration numbers and list sizes in the channel decoders of 4G, 5G, and beyond 5G systems such as turbo, LDPC, polar, and PAC. B. Motivation
In practical cellular system implementations, all the receiver algorithms at both BS and UE are designed and optimized to meet the set target BLER, denoted as BLERTarget. We simulated and observed that the required I and L of the decoders vary as a function of SNR, to meet the target BLER, as shown in Fig.2. Fig. 2a shows the BLER performance of 5G NR LDPC codes, with code rate of 1/3, as a function of SNR, and Fig. 2b shows that of 5G NR polar codes of encoded length N = 128, with code rate of 1/4, for additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel. It is important to note that choosing the right iteration number/list size helps in reducing the power consumption of the decoding process significantly. In this section, we briefly discuss the calculation of RBIR metric, and the implementation details of the proposed ULIP using RBIR.

[8]. For a given LTE/NR DL/UL transmission with a total of

The identified adaptive iteration number/list size, KAdap in the above procedure is fed to the considered iterative/list-based decoder along with the channel LLRs, before the channel decoding procedure starts. The list-based polar/PAC decoder then uses only KAdap decoder cores for decoding the data, whereas, the iterative turbo/LDPC decoders run for only KAdap number of iterations, which results in power saving. The proposed ULIP is easy to implement at the receiver given its LUT based implementation adding near-zero complexity.
IV. PRACTICAL IMPLEMENTATION ASPECTS OF THE PROPOSED ULIP
The method of link abstraction which involves the calculation of RBIR metric is already implemented in some of the LTE and 5G NR UEs in the existing systems, for the purpose of link adaptation. This proposal however, can be applied to both BS and UE, given the decoders are common to both of them. Thus, the RBIR calculation needs to be additionally implemented at the BS, similar to that of UE, to aid in reducing the power consumption of the UL decoding operations.
For the BS side implementation, based on the recent trends, open-RAN (O-RAN) architecture is one of the important architectures for virtualization of RAN [15]. It proposes the receiver operations split between radio unit (O-RU) and the distributed unit (O-DU). This is done with the purpose of keeping the implementations of O-RU simple, for commercially viable deployments. In the latest ORAN 7.3 split architecture, channel estimation is performed at O-RU, and the decoding is done at O-DU. With the proposed ULIP, the RBIR can be calculated at O-RU using the channel and noise estimates as given in Section III-A, and further iteration number/list size can be estimated using RBIR as given in Section III-B. The

identified iteration number/list size can then be transmitted from O-RU to O-DU using fronthaul link, where the decoder can use it for the decoding process.
V. PERFORMANCE EVALUATION
In this section, we present the simulation results in terms of BLER and average iteration number for turbo and LDPC decoders, and average list size for polar and PAC decoders, evaluated with the proposed ULIP, and compare them with that of fixed iteration number/list size decoders. We also compare the effectiveness of the proposed ULIP with genie method. The genie method is non casual, that assumes the knowledge of the transmitted codeword at the receiver, and knows the exact iteration number and list size to proceed with, at the receiver. It is shown in the literature as the limit of any possible adaptive iteration/list decoding.We evaluate the proposed ULIP with the 3GPP defined fading channel models: extended pedestrian A (EPA)-5Hz of LTE [13], and tapped delay line (TDL)A [14] of NR 5G, with delay spread (DS) of 30 ns, and a maximum Doppler shift (fD) of 100 Hz. We used the values of γcod, ranging from 1 to 4, on trial and error basis, to compensate for false alarm rates in RBIR model prediction as mentioned in Section III-A [8]. Simulations are performed using LTE and 5G NR link level simulators, implemented in MATLAB, with the simulation parameters as listed in Table I. The transmission bandwidth for control and data transmissions is specified in terms of resource blocks (RB) in Table I [12]. We evaluated multiple modulation and coding scheme (MCS) indices covering quadrature phase-shift keying (QPSK), 16quadrature amplitude modulation (16-QAM), and 64-QAM modulation schemes, for both turbo and LDPC decoders. Each MCS index corresponds to a code rate of a modulation scheme, and an MCS index combined with the number of RBs gives the codeword length [17], [18]. Further, we evaluated multiple code rates such as {1/2, 1/3, 1/4} with QPSK modulation scheme, for both polar and PAC codes, with an encoded length of 128 [10], [12].

VI. CONCLUSION

In this paper, we proposed an RBIR-based practical ULIP
for adaptively identifying and regulating the iteration number
for iterative decoders such as turbo and LPDC, and list size
for list-based decoders of polar and PAC codes, to reduce the
power consumption. The proposed ULIP can be used in any
iterative or list-based decoder, and is applicable to both BS
