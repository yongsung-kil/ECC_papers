FPGA Accelerated Adaptive LDPC-based Quantum Error Correction by Bitwise Pipeline Parallelism

Bingze Ye, Jiyuan Liu, He Li* School of Electronic Science and Engineering, Southeast University, China
Corresponding author: helix@seu.edu.cn

Abstract—Quantum networks are at the forefront of nextgeneration communication technologies, providing unparalleled security by leveraging the fundamental properties of quantum mechanics. However, noise and interference adversely affect information sharing within quantum networks, causing quantum bit errors and diminishing the quality of quantum keys. To address this issue, high-speed quantum information error correction is necessitated and this work proposes an FPGA-accelerated error correction system based on low-density parity check (LDPC), incorporating algorithm-hardware co-optimizations. The proposed hardware architecture is applicable to generic LDPC soft decision algorithms and can adaptively select LDPC matrices with different code rates based on the quantum bit error rates. Experiment results demonstrate that the system exhibits high performance in error correction acceleration, achieving a throughput of 728 Mbps and up-to 9.4× speedup compared to existing FPGA-based LDPC error correction implementations.
Index Terms—LDPC, FPGA, Quantum Error Correction
I. INTRODUCTION
Quantum networks, leveraging the principles of quantum mechanics, are poised to revolutionize secure communication through quantum key distribution (QKD) [1]. However, the presence of noise and interference in quantum key sharing can lead to errors in the received quantum bits (qubits), thereby disrupting the stability and reliability of the quantum keys. To address this challenge, a real-time error correction system is essential to promptly detect and accurately rectify the errors in the received qubits.
As one important application scenario in quantum networks, satellites can cover a wide area and achieve secure communication between different geographical locations [2]. Considering the constant changes in reception angle, climate conditions and terrain, the channel conditions of satellite signals are constantly fluctuating, leading to the instability of quantum bit error rates (QBERs) [2]. Therefore, the adaptability to different QBERs in the quantum error correction system is required for reliable quantum network communication.
Among various error correcting codes (ECCs), the LDPC code has emerged as a popular choice, due to its low algorithm complexity, high transmission efficiency and high error correction success rates [3] [4]. Moreover, FPGA offers high reconfigurability and pipeline parallelism capability, making it more suitable for accelerating LDPC algorithms via the investigation of architectural optimizations, compared to generalpurpose processors (e.g. GPUs and CPUs) [5].
In this paper, we implement a high-throughout LDPC-based quantum error correction accelerator on FPGAs, which is able to tune the LDPC matrices in the runtime, adaptive to different QBERs ranging from 0.01 to 0.11. The proposed

hardware architecture is generic and applicable to various LDPC soft decision algorithms. Technical novel contributions of the proposed method are summarized as follows:
• A real-time and QBER-adaptive quantum key error correction accelerator that selects appropriate LDPC matrices based on different QBERs, reducing quantum error correction latency.
• A novel bitwise parallel and pipeline architecture based on algortihm-hardware co-optimizations, combining sparsity analysis, efficient dataflow and memory partition.
• Quantitative and qualitative comparisons against the stateof-the-art LDPC-based quantum key error correction implementations, demonstrating a throughput of 728Mbps and up-to 9.4× speedup.
II. BACKGROUND
2.1 Quantum Key Sharing in Quantum Networks
Future-proofing quantum networks with quantum key distribution has been an effective strategy to combat the escalating threats of data theft, applicable in secure communication scenarios such as satellite and air-ground communication [2] [6]. QKD ensures that any eavesdropping attempts can be detected, providing a secure method for exchanging cryptographic keys. However, quantum networks face challenges, particularly in maintaining quantum coherence and minimizing qubit errors over long distances [1]. Quantum states are sensitive to their environments, leading to decoherence, which poses a barrier to effective key sharing.
To tackle these issues, interest in high-performance quantum error correction techniques is growing. By using errorcorrecting codes, quantum networks can detect and correct errors without directly measuring the quantum states, preserving qubit coherence. Techniques such as LDPC codes enhance the reliability of QKD systems, ensuring secure key distribution despite noise [7].
2.2 LDPC Based Quantum Key Error Correction
1) LDPC Algorithms LDPC codes, as a representative of linear codes, are defined by particularly sparse parity check matrices. The decoding algorithms of LDPC codes can be divided into two categories: hard decision and soft decision [8]. Hard decision decoding directly flips bits based on verification equations, while soft decision decoding iteratively calculates posterior probabilities, allowing LDPC codes to approach the Shannon limit [8]. And there are several LPDC soft decision algorithms, including

sum-product algorithm (SPA), min-sum algorithm (MSA), normalized min-sum algorithm (NMSA) and offset min-sum algorithm (OMSA) [9] [10].
Essentially, these soft decision algorithms share the same decoding process, which can be divided into the following three steps:
• Initialization step: receive external messages and initialize internal messages according to check matrix.
• Iteration step: update the internal messages in each iteration in the order of horizontal step and vertical step.
• Decision step: bit decision based on the posterior probability after iteration.
Based on the same decoding process framework, different LDPC soft decision algorithms perform different arithmetic operations in probability domain or logarithmic domain. SPA and NMSA are representative algorithms in the probability and logarithmic domains, respectively. SPA involves a large number of multiplication operations, resulting in high computational complexity and resource consumption in hardware implementation. On the contrary, NMSA represents probability information as likelihood ratios, which converts multiplication operations into addition operations, providing significantly low computational complexity.
2) FPGA Implementation
FPGA has a high degree of parallel processing capability, which enables hardware level optimization for LDPC decoding algorithms, achieving higher throughput and lower latency. Moreover, it brings convenience to achieve integration control and to further implement adaptive selection of an appropriate parity-check matrix based on QBERs [5].
With FPGAs, various design methods have been proposed, including fully parallel decoding, partially parallel decoding, hybrid decoding and layered decoding [11] [12]. These methods are essentially different levels of parallelism in the error correction process. Higher parallelism increases throughput and processing speed while consuming a significant amount of resources and reducing system scalability.
III. METHOD
3.1 Overview of The Proposed Hardware Architecture
We propose a hardware architecture applicable to various LDPC soft decision algorithms, which only requires changing the calculation operation according to different algorithms. As shown in Fig.1, the proposed LDPC decoding hardware architecture consists of five processing units.
• Check matrix selection unit: select a parity check matrix for the subsequent LDPC decoding.
• Variable nodes process unit: perform initialization operation during the first iteration and horizontal step in subsequent iterations, updating the internal messages from variable nodes to check nodes.
• Check nodes process unit: perform vertical step during iterations, updating the internal messages from check nodes to variable nodes.

• Address control unit: ensure the correctness of timing for reading and writing BRAMs.
• Decision unit: complete the final judgment and obtain the corrected data.

3.4 Performance and Memory Footprint Optimizations Based on Matrix Sparsity
The zero element in LDPC parity check matrix represents that there is no relationship between the check node and the variable node in this bit’s position. Therefore, we search the positions of nonzero element to calculate the valid data separately, which can significantly avoid the resource waste. In order to further conserve resources, we directly store the position messages of one elements in each matrix instead of the complete matrix as a preprocessing step. After the selection of the parity check matrix, we can initialize the data structures of internal messages with the aforementioned nonzero positions.

3.5 Generic and Adaptive Error Correction
Through LDPC encoding, the codeword adds redundant bits to the information bits. More redundant bits bring higher error correction performance, however requiring more error correction time and hardware resources. In order to balance error correction performance with computation latency, we have implemented a runtime QBER analysis module to adaptively select a parity check matrix based on a specific QBER.

IV. RESULTS

We deployed two error correction accelerators on FPGA based on SPA and NMSA in register transfer level (RTL) programming in Vivado 2024.1. The quantum error correction system is implemented on the FPGA (xczu48dr-fsvg1517-2-e).

4.1 Performance Evaluation

Throughput is indeed an important performance parameter in LDPC error correction decoding, which measures the amount of information successfully processed by the system per unit time, as shown in the following equation,

T = N × Fmax

(7)

CP I × NOI

while N represents the length of codeword, Fmax represents the maximum clock frequency. CP I and N OI represent the number of clock cycles per iteration and the number of iteration, respectively.
In our error correction system, the basis length of codeword to be corrected is 100, which serves as a unit and can be flexibly extended to an arbitrary size for real quantum keys error correction. As for CP I, the proposed hardware architecture enables us to complete one error correction iteration in less than 20 clock cycles. The actual clock cycles consumed is related to the code rate of the chosen matrix, characterised in CP I = N × wr + N × wc × CR + 4. wr and wc represent the sparsity of row and column respectively in LDPC parity check matrix, and both are initialized when generating LDPC matrices. CR is the code rate ranging from 0.2 to 0.9. Thanks to the proposed bitwise pipeline parallellism architecture, the number of clock cycles consumed per iteration is less than 20, therefore, we demonstrate that our error correction accelerators reach a throughput of up-to 180 Mbps for SPA and 728 Mbps for NMSA, respectively.

4.2 Comparisons and Discussions

As shown in Table II, the throughput in our experimental results has been significantly improved compared to previous implementations, resulting in up-to 12.4× and 9.4× speedup in SPA and NMSA, respectively. In addition, SPA-based error correction accelerator requires 379121 LUTs, 39743 FFs, 306 BRAMs and 988 DSPs, and NMSA-based error correction

accelerator requires 49090 LUTs, 21733 FFs, 231 BRAMs and 1136 DSPs. We notice that many previous works do not mention the use of DSP resources. By adopting DSPs, we have reduced the consumption of LUTs and FFs, leading to a balance design using available hardware resources on FPGAs.
Moreover, most existing implementations utilize a single code rate for error correction, while some supporting multiple code rates without the adaptability to select among them. We have innovatively implemented the adaptability to choose different code rates based on QBER in LDPC error correction, further reducing quantum error correction latency.
The modular design of our proposed architecture allows for seamless expansion beyond the existing LDPC error correction system, supporting longer codeword correction by parallel processing of multiple 100-bit error correction systems. This flexibility ensures that the architecture can be adapted to meet the demands of higher data rates and more complex applications, maintaining its performance and reliability for large scale systems.
V. CONCLUSION
A novel real-time adaptive LDPC-based error correction accelerator for FPGA devices is proposed in this paper, which features high throughput and applicability to generic LDPC soft decision algorithms. The proposed new hardware architecture is based on the parallel pipeline capability of FPGA and the sparsity of LDPC matrices, extremely reducing the number of clock cycles in error correction to improve the throughput. The implementation results show that the proposed error correction accelerator outperforms state-of-theart implementations in terms of throughput and adaptability.
