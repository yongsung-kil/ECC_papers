Burst Correcting Capability of Block-Permutation LDPC Codes

Alina M. Veresova, Andrei A. Ovchinnikov
Department of Infocommunication Systems and Technologies Saint-Petersburg State University of Aerospace Instrumentation
Saint-Petersburg, Russia amveresova@gmail.com

Abstract—The article examines the impact of the blockpermutation structure of low-density parity-check codes on their ability to correct single error bursts. The length of the corrected burst is estimated, depending on the size of the blocks used in block-circulant and block-permutation codes with different parameter settings. The article also estimates the probability of finding codes with maximum achievable correcting capabilities when using different construction methods. It is found that using a block-permutation construction allows for a high probability of achieving the maximum possible correcting capability, regardless of the parameters used. However, when using block-circulant codes, it can be difficult or impossible to find codes with the maximum achievable length of burst to be corrected for nonprime block sizes. Thus, the block-permutation construction turns out to be more effective in terms of its correcting capabilities, and can be used in situations where storing the code in a compact form of a base matrix is not a significant criterion.
Keywords—error burst; correcting capability; low-density parity-check code; block-permutation construction; block-circulant construction
I. INTRODUCTION
Linear codes are used to transmit information over telecommunication channels. At the same time, noise-resistant encoding and decoding algorithms based on the assumption of independence of errors in the channel are used to ensure the reliability of transmission. At the same time, it is known that in the described transmission media, interference is correlated over time, and errors are grouped into error bursts. Although classical methods combined with modern low-density paritycheck constructions (LDPC) [1] make it possible to achieve sufficiently low error probabilities when using decorrelation procedures, these procedures reduce achievable transmission rates [2].
Thus, it is necessary to consider code constructions aimed at correcting error packets. An important characteristic of such codes is their correcting capability in channels with memorythe maximum length bmax of the error burst that can be corrected.
The paper [3] describes a procedure for determining the correcting capability of linear codes when correcting single error packets. This procedure, unlike determining the minimum code distance, has polynomial complexity and is based on calculating the rank of certain submatrices of the parity-check matrix.

The analysis of the burst correcting capability of cyclic codes is carried out in [4], [5], [6]. Approaches to evaluating the effectiveness of burst error correction codes are discussed in [7]. In [8], the correcting capability of polar codes is investigated, and it is shown that the length of the corrected packet for such codes is quite small. The issues of decoding convolutional codes in channels with memory have been discussed in [9]. The article [10] focuses on the estimation of the burst correcting capability of some ensembles of blockcirculant low-density parity-check codes. For these constructions, the correcting capability is limited by the block size from above and reaches this value for certain sets of code parameters. Additionally, in article [11], PEG (progressive edge-growth) constructions and random low-density paritycheck codes, along with block-circulants, are considered. The burst correcting capability of these codes is close to that of block-circulant constructions with similar characteristics.
An algorithm for decoding single-error packets with linear codes was proposed in [12], demonstrating high efficiency when using block permutation code structures. Article [13] describes a method for determining the burst position in block permutation codes, which significantly speeds up the decoding process.
Thus, block-circulant low-density parity-check codes exhibit high correcting capability with high computational efficiency. However, there are numerous extensions of this class, and the study of these extensions is an important task. This paper will consider the applicability of various constructions of block-permutation codes for burst error correction.

Their correcting capability was investigated in [3], and the consideration of random two-dimensional constructions does not make practical sense. III. ESTIMATION OF BURST-CORRECTING CAPABILBITY
For random code constructions, the burst error-correcting capability is also a random variable, as it depends on the presence of linear dependencies between columns in the paritycheck matrix. Specifically, if there is a cycle of length 4 in the matrix, the code's correcting capability is zero, so these codes were not used in further experiments. An example of the distribution of corrected packet lengths for 100 randomly generated codes with parameters γ = 3, ρ = 6, and m = 14 is shown in Fig. 3. It can be seen that the proportion of codes with near-maximum correcting capability is quite large. Therefore, we will use the maximum corrected burst length as the metric for our ensemble in the future.
Let us consider ensembles of random block-circulant and block-permutation codes with parameters γ = 3 and ρ = 6. The rate of these codes is approximately 0.5. For clarity, we will not consider the maximum length of the corrected burst, but rather its deviation from the maximum achievable value, which is Δbmax = (m – 1) – bmax. Fig. 4 shows the dependence of the described value on the block size for a sample of 1000 for each value of m considered. It can be seen that for blockpermutation codes, the sample always includes a code with the maximum possible length of the corrected packet, that is, bmax = m − 1, while for block-circulant codes with even block size, bmax = m − 2, resulting in Δbmax = 1. IV. CONCLUSION
The article evaluates the error correction capability of block-circulant and block-permutation codes with a low density of parity checks when correcting single error bursts. Codes with a small number of blocks are considered, ensuring the largest ratio of the length of the corrected packet length to the code length.
It has been shown that block-circulant codes with γ = 3 and ρ = 6 have a high probability of providing bmax = m – 1 for odd block sizes and bmax = m – 2 for even block sizes. At the same time, block-permutation codes with similar parameters have the maximum possible correcting capability, regardless of block size m. Increasing the code rate by using ρ = 12 leads to a decrease in the probability of detecting a block-circulant code with the maximum correction capability. However, even when considering 100 codes, the difference between bmax and m − 1 is not more than Δbmax = 4.
Increasing the number of stripes in the parity-check matrix of a block-circulant code to γ = 4 allows us to achieve bmax = m – 1 for any given m. However, the probability of finding such a code for even block sizes is between 10−4 and 10−2, and the search process may take a long time.
