Construction-D′ Lattices Based on Raptor Codes for Unconstrained AWGN Channels

Pegah Sharifi and Khadijeh Bagheri Department of Mathematics and Computer Science Amirkabir University of Technology (Tehran Polytechnic)
Tehran, Iran {p.sharifi96, kbagheri}@aut.ac.ir

Hassan Khodaiemehr and Chen Feng Faculty of Applied Science, School of Engineering
The University of British Columbia (UBC) Okanagan Campus, Kelowna, BC, Canada {hassan.khodaiemehr, chen.feng}@ubc.ca

AbstractÐRaptor codes provide low encoding and decoding complexity, making them effective in unreliable networks. This paper introduces a novel framework for constructing multilevel lattices using Raptor codes, integrating Construction-D′ to enhance joint coding and modulation. We employ a multilevel decoder with Belief Propagation (BP) and concatenate Luby Transform (LT) codes with quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) pre-codes, streamlining the construction process. Simulation results demonstrate that the proposed Raptor lattices significantly outperform traditional counterparts in Frame Error Rate (FER) over additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels.
Index TermsÐFountain codes, Raptor codes, Multilevel Lattice, Multistage Decoding, AWGN channel.
I. INTRODUCTION
The evolution of communication technologies increases the need for robust coding, with lattice-based methods as a strong solution. Lattices are defined mathematically as discrete additive subgroups of Rn. Levels in this framework refer to a hierarchy where layers encode information with different error protection levels. A three-level lattice may have two coded layers for error correction and one uncoded layer for direct transmission, balancing reliability and throughput to adapt to channel changes.
In [1], Poltyrev introduced the study of unconstrained AWGN channels using lattices, which are infinite arrays based on real-valued algebra. These lattices are more power-efficient at high SNRs than traditional constellations. Lattices can be constructed using various methods through linear codes, referenced as component codes [2]. These lattices utilize linear code decoders for efficient decoding. When parity-check matrices are available, Construction-D′ is suitable, using nested linear codes. Construction-A is for one-level lattices and relies on the generator or parity-check matrix of the component code. Construction-D′ generalizes Construction-A by requiring multiple component codes. The performance of these lattices is closely linked to their component codes. For example, QCLDPC lattices [3], derived from QC-LDPC codes, support linear-time encoding using shift registers. Other studies include generalized Construction-D′ LDPC lattices [4] and low-density lattice codes (LDLC) [5].
This study uses Raptor codes to create a 3-level lattice based on a generalization of Construction-D′ [4], with encoding

and decoding methods from [6] and outer codes as per [7]. The pre-codes use QC-LDPC codes, offering lower encoding complexity than traditional LDPC codes due to structural benefits. Despite appearing straightforward, integrating Raptor codes with Construction-D′ posed significant implementation challenges. Creating a suitable parity-check matrix from QCLDPC and LT codes was complex and led to a Tanner graph with a girth of 4, impairing error performance due to short cycles. We introduced a novel modification to the parity-check matrix to remove these cycles, improving error performance beyond that of LDPC-based Construction-D′ [4]. This highlights the need to carefully design parity-check matrices to optimize Raptor codes in lattice constructions.
This paper is structured as follows: Section II introduces the preliminaries on Raptor codes and lattices, along with the criteria for selecting our component codes. Section III presents the Raptor code design and details the encoding process for Construction-D′ lattices. Sequential decoding of the proposed lattices is addressed in Section IV. In Section V, we present the results of our simulations. Lastly, Section VI offers the concluding observations.
II. PRELIMINARY CONCEPTS AND DEFINITIONS
A. Raptor Codes
Raptor codes are part of fountain codes, which are rateless and not limited by block size. They produce unlimited output symbols from a source message, letting devices reconstruct data with any set of encoding symbols meeting or exceeding source symbols. This adaptability allows transmission as needed for data recovery. Rateless codes enable decoding with consistent quality across various channel conditions.
To retrieve the original message, the receiver must collect slightly more than k output symbols. A fountain code decoding algorithm recovers k input symbols from any m output symbols with high probability. In well-designed fountain codes for an erasure channel, m is nearly equal to k, and decoding time is linear in k. LT codes are the first of such codes. In LT codes, the degree of each output symbol d is determined by the output degree distribution, a probability distribution over the set {1, . . . , k}. Then, d input symbols are selected, and their values are appended to form the output symbol. The encoder

creates a bipartite graph linking source and encoded symbols. When the average degree d¯ is much smaller than k, the graph is sparse, enabling the use of an irregular low-density generator matrix. In these sparse graphs, the output symbols’ average degree approaches loge k, where e is the natural logarithm’s base, classifying LT codes as random linear fountain codes. Decoding complexity rises with increasing code length. The logarithmic growth of the average output degree with input symbols makes a linear-time encoder and decoder difficult. A capacity-achieving LT code’s average degree also grows logarithmically with message size, further complicating lineartime design [8].
Raptor codes were developed as a practical improvement over LT codes, achieving linear-time encoding and decoding by combining an LT code with an outer code to address gaps [9]. They are a sophisticated type of random linear fountain codes. They are constructed by combining an LT code with a high-rate pre-code, often an LDPC code, and perform better than LT codes. Some Raptor codes may be constructed using two-layer pre-codes. The pre-code, with linear-time encoder and decoder, recovers original and redundant input symbols.
This paper uses the method from [7] for constructing LT codes, focusing on heuristic parameters to find optimal parity bit degrees D1, D2, . . . , Di, . . . and their lengths L1, L2, . . . , Li, . . ., avoiding linear programming (LP) problems typical in traditional Raptor or LT code construction. QC-LDPC codes, a subset of LDPC codes with parity-check matrices of circulant permutation or zero matrices, serve as pre-codes. These offer advantages such as low-complexity encoding/decoding, good error performance. QC-LDPC codes are constructed using algebraic and graph-based methods. We use employed QC-LDPC codes for QC-LDPC lattices [3], [10]. In our code design, key parameters include girth g, row weight wr, column weight wc, circulant size ϑ, and a base matrix B which is considered as a wc × wr all-one matrix. Initially, parameters were found through trial and error, but we later developed rules for suitable pre-code selection. We set g = 8 and optimize parameters to maximize wr and minimize ϑ for fixed code length N = ϑ · wr. The column degree is typically 2 or 3, potentially higher for longer codes, like 4. Our QCLDPC construction algorithm searches shift values for each group to maintain girth conditions. If desired codes are not found for certain N , we adjust N . For a dimension 1000 lattice, we initially needed N around 700 but constructed with N = 696, wr = 12, wc = 3, and ϑ = 58 for dimension 995. Similarly, using N = 708, wr = 12, wc = 2, and ϑ = 59, we got dimension 1025, with both lattices having similar error performance. Careful parameter attention is crucial for Raptor code performance and reliability. Using QC-LDPC as a pre-code reduces Raptor code complexity, leading to faster encoding/decoding and lower power and memory usage.
This study uses systematic Raptor codes for lattice building, which are favored due to their wide application range. They are built on systematic LT codes, created by adding an identity matrix to a non-systematic LT code generator.

IV. DECODING
In this section, we focus on the decoding process of the Raptor codes, which involves both the QC-LDPC and LT components. The decoding can be effectively represented and handled using a modified Tanner graph structure [11]. This modification enables the utilization of parity check equations as termination conditions for the decoding algorithm, thereby enhancing the overall performance.

B. Multistage Decoding of Construction-D′ Lattices
The multistage decoding process for Construction-D′ lattices was initially introduced in [13], which necessitated re-encoding by the generator matrix during decoding. As shown in [4], it is possible to compute the coset directly instead of performing reencodings within the hypercubical lattice constellations derived from Construction-D′. In this study, the decoding method employed is based on the approach detailed in [6], which serves as an augmentation of the framework established in [14]. It utilizes a sequential cancellation decoding algorithm specifically tailored for Construction-D′. The process of re-encoding is executed utilizing the check matrix, thereby allowing for the implementation of non-hypercubical constellations.
Consider a scenario where a lattice point denoted as x is transmitted and y = x + w represents the vector received from an AWGN channel. In this case, the Gaussian noise vector w is made up of independent, identically distributed (i.i.d.) Gaussian random variables with variance σ2 and a mean of zero.
