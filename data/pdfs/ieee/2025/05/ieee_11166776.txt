Abstract—This paper presents an encoder for 5G New Radio (NR)’s physical channel encoding which uses Quasi-Cyclic, Lowdensity parity check codes (QC-LDPC). The encoder utilizes a forward substitution algorithm within a configurable circuit structure, allowing it to adapt dynamically to various base graphs associated with 5G-LDPC codes. A key innovation is the storage of only quantized permutation values for each submatrix of the parity check matrix, rather than the entire parity check matrix, significantly reducing memory requirements. The encoder in this paper gives a throughput of up to 32.36 Mbps for a scaling factor of Z=192 at the frequency of 50Mhz.
Index Terms—5G NR, Low-Density Parity Check, QC-LDPC, Encoding algorithm.
I. INTRODUCTION
One of the key technologies in wireless communication systems is “Channel Encoding”, which improves communication reliability [1]. While many channel coding techniques are available, only one works closest to Shannon’s limit: “Low Density Parity Check” (LDPC) [1] [2]. Hence, this technique has the advantage of high encoding gain. Developed by Gallager in 1962, LDPC codes were later revisited and refined by Mackay and Neal in 1996. Over the years, they have gained considerable attention because of their remarkable error correction performance and low decoding complexity. These advantages led to LDPC codes being adopted as the standard channel coding scheme for 5G New Radio (NR) data channels, as determined during the 3rd Generation Partnership Project (3GPP) conference in 2016 [3]. In comparison to Turbo codes (used up to 4G in mobile communication), LDPC codes provide superior error correction and enable highly parallelizable decoding processes, making them more efficient for modern communication standards [3]. As LDPC codes approach the Shannon limit in terms of performance, employing a direct encoding method can lead to a quadratic increase in data storage and computational complexity with regard to code length that can go up to 8448 bits of input [2] [3], makes the encoding with low complexity challenging.The definition of LDPC codes comes with their parity check matrix, while the generator matrix is usually unknown. Several approaches have been proposed to improve the hardware complexity of LDPC encoders [4] [6]. One common method is Gaussian elimination, in which systematic encoding is done by deriving

a generator matrix from the parity check matrix. However, this method has a significant drawback for large block sizes, as storage requirements increase dramatically with large blocks, limiting its practical use. To address this issue, extensive research has been conducted over the years on a particular type of LDPC code called Quasi Cyclic Low Density Parity Check (QC-LDPC) code. These codes provide several benefits compared to other LDPC codes, especially regarding hardware implementation for encoding and decoding. Using straightforward shift registers and logic circuits, QC-LDPC codes facilitate the creation of low-complexity encoders [7]. Proposed by Richardson and Urbanke, the Richardson–Urbanke (RU) algorithm rearranges the parity-check matrix (H) into an approximate lower triangular (ALT) form by performing targeted permutations of its rows and columns. This process effectively maintains the sparsity of the matrix, but often results in a longer critical path, thereby reducing the performance of LDPC encoders for high-speed operations [8].
A low-complexity, high-throughput QC-LDPC encoder architecture specifically designed for the 5G standard is introduced in this work. The 5G NR encoding scheme incorporates two base graphs: BG1 and BG2. BG1 is optimized for users with higher traffic demands near the tower, while BG2 addresses the needs of users situated farther away. The proposed design achieves high throughput for BG1 in 5G NR while significantly reducing area and memory storage requirements.

uniform dimensions defines a binary QC-LDPC code. In constructing the parity-check matrix (H) for such codes, the shift coefficients P[r,c]in the base graph are critical. In this graph, 1s and 0s correspond to circulant permutation matrices (CPMs) and zero matrices, both of size Z×Z. Positive integers P[r,c] are replaced by CPMs of the same size, shifted by P[r,c] % Z. B. Developments in LDPC Encoding
Numerous researchers, both domestically and internationally, have studied low-complexity LDPC code encoders. The paper titled “An efficient LDPC encoder architecture for space applications” presents a parallel encoder that employs recursive convolution in accordance with the Consultative Committee for Space Data Systems (CCSDS) standard [11]. This design achieves high throughput while minimizing resource consumption [11]. An LDPC encoder based on a blockrow cycle under the CMMB (Chinese Mobile Multimedia Broadcasting) standard, leveraging the cyclic properties of QCLDPC parity check matrices is given [12]. A three hardware encoding structure for QC-LDPC: (I) a serial SRAA (shiftregister adder-accumulator) structure unsuitable for high-speed encoding;(II) a parallel version with higher frame delay; and (III) a TWO-STAGE structure that requires a full-rank parity check matrix is discussed by Z. Li, et al. [13]. T. T. B. Nguyen et al. discuss a forward substitution encoding algorithm, which is several times more efficient than direct and RU methods but only works with the BG1 [14]. This paper builds an encoder based on the forward substitution encoding algorithm, which is also discussed by Ke Jia, et al. [3] which gives higher throughput, utilize less hardware by reusing the modules, and is suitable for BG1 of 5G NR.

IV. DESIGN ARCHITECTURE
The encoding algorithm described in section III can be implemented in various ways and in different levels of parallelism if the fully parallel way is decided it will take a lot of hardware resources [15]. The LDPC encoder has input in the form of (kb x Z) where kb is 22 for BG1 and Z is the lifting factor which ranges from 2 to 384 which means the input to the LDPC encoder ranges from a minimum of 44 bits and a maximum of 8448 so managing these many bits in parallel will require a lot of hardware so to utilize minimal hardware resources the encoding is done in pipelined manner using modules illustrated Fig.5.
The encoder design proposed in this paper presents a framework that accommodates a variety of base matrices for the set index (ils=1), specifically within the context of the base graph employed in 5G NR for lifting factor Z, which ranges from 3 to 384. This illustrates the encoder’s versatility in meeting diverse communication requirements, as referenced in [15]. In terms of architecture, the input buffer is designed to receive information bits in a serial format. This design choice is essential to ensure efficient processing and preparation of the information bits for subsequent operations and hardware constraints. Once these bits are acquired serially, they are stored in the memory and are transitioned to the next processing block, which executes the computation of the lambda value. Central to this process is a cyclic shifter, which acts as a dynamic linking component that derives input from the predefined base graph. For each entry that exists within this base graph, the cyclic shifter effectively populates several parity check submatrices denoted as A, B, C, and D. The significance of these submatrices lies in their capacity to facilitate the computations necessary for generating both core and extra parity bits. The accuracy of these parity calculations is crucial for maintaining the reliability and integrity of the encoded data, particularly in error-prone communication environments. The lambda value is computed through a systematic application of the equation (1) referenced in Section 3. This computation employs a modulo adder, a mathematical construct that efficiently performs the necessary arithmetic operations to derive the lambda value. The same computational block utilized for calculating lambda is also tasked with the generation of extra parity bits once core parity bits are calculated. This dual functionality not only optimizes resource utilization but also streamlines the encoding process. Throughout these calculations, all addition operations are conducted via XOR (exclusive OR) logic. This characteristic

is particularly advantageous, as XOR operations are known for their computational efficiency, contributing to the overall performance of the encoder. The use of XOR in place of conventional addition elevates the system’s reliability and speed, which are critical parameters in high-speed communications. Once all core parity bits and extra parity bits are calculated they are then moved to the output buffer module along with the information bits to form the final codeword by concatenating information bits, core and other parity bits as mentioned in section III. The final codeword is then shifted out serially and given to the next block in the physical layer for further processing.

VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, a high bit rate QC-LDPC encoder leverages systematic encoding principles and parallel real-time processing. Constructed with a forward substitution algorithm, our encoder’s accuracy is confirmed through simulation. This integrated encoder significantly enhances 5G NR systems by delivering faster encoding and better memory efficiency compared to general-purpose processors. Results show that for scaling factors Z less than 96, encoding time is reduced. So, system throughput can be further increased by reusing these blocks to compute codewords in parallel. Overall, this efficient encoding process contributes to minimizing latency in critical 5G NR applications, such as enhanced mobile broadband (eMBB) and fixed wireless access, where any delays can negatively affect user experience.
