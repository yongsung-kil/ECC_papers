Abstract—Physical layer signal processing presents significant challenges in military communication systems, where robust systems must retrieve data efficiently and rapidly. Therefore, this research focuses on the decoding of powerful low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. Conventional LDPC decoders often become bottlenecks because they use cumbersome high-precision real number arithmetic. Recent approaches from the literature use compressive information bottleneck methods to reduce this complexity. They rely on integer messages and simple look-up operations, easing software implementations.
Implemented on a chip, however, information bottleneck decoders, still need many logical gates to synthesize the decoding operations. As countermeasure, we directly utilize the bit-level representations of the exchanged messages. We propose to learn mutual-information-maximizing decoding circuits directly using genetic algorithms, instead of designing look-up tables and synthesizing them with elementary logical gates afterwards. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed LDPC decoders outperform traditional suboptimal decoders, such as the min-sum decoder, in terms of bit error rate.
This paper was originally presented at the NATO Science and Technology Organization Symposium (ICMCIS) organized by the Information Systems Technology (IST) Scientific and Technical Committee, IST-209-RSY – the ICMCIS, held in Oeiras, Portugal, 13-14 May 2025.”
I. INTRODUCTION
Signal processing poses significant challenges for electrical engineers, often characterized by high computational demands and processing times. Therefore, reducing computational load, processing time, and power consumption in communication systems is a high in interest topic, particularly in military applications where reliable and timely communication can be the difference between mission success and failure. For instance, reduced computational load directly leads to longer battery life for handheld devices on the battlefield, ensuring that soldiers remain connected without compromising operational effectiveness. Moreover, one critical element in the communication chain often presents itself as a bottleneck due to their intricate operations. This element is the channel decoder [1].
Channel decoders play a vital role in ensuring data integrity during transmission, as they enable the correction of errors introduced by the physical communication channel or hostile jamming. Although various channel coding techniques have

emerged over the years, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have gained prominence as well as Polar codes. This research focuses on LDPC codes due to their wide implementation in contemporary standards like IEEE802.16e, WiMax, and 5G-New Radio (5G-NR). It is worth noting that it is likely that future military systems will also utilize this powerful coding technique in the context of 5G-NR [2].
Traditionally, LDPC decoders employ a message-passing algorithm (i.e., the belief propagation algorithm) that demands high-precision real number arithmetic. The nature of the messages and arithmetic lead to complex and costly node operations to decode the received messages. While suboptimal decoders like the min-sum decoder reduce computational load, they introduce a performance degradation in terms of bit error rate (BER) and need high-precision representation of messages.
To alleviate these issues, the literature [3]–[5] reports lowcomplexity solutions through the use of look-up-tables (LUTs) achieved by the information bottleneck (IB) method, which allows for efficient decoding by compressing observed variables into discrete representations. When it comes to the hardware implementation of IB decoders in silicon, however, the LUTs have to be synthesized using elementary logical gates. Another efficient approach to implement IB-like LDPC decoding operations are reconstruction-computation-quantization (RCQ) decoders [6]–[8]. Their general idea is to design non-uniform quantizers and reconstruction mappings at the nodes. Node operations are reduced to simple mappings and fixed-point additions that can be implemented with low efforts.
In this paper, we explore a novel idea to design LDPC code decoding operations with the IB principle. We apply genetic algorithms to directly learn logical circuits that maximize the relevant mutual information for the decoding. These circuits perform elementary bit-level operations on the messages processed in the decoder. Therefore, we propose replacing LUTs with simpler circuits that utilize the bit-level representations of decoding messages during message-passing decoding operations. This approach implies simple binary operations that can be easily translated to matrix multiplication in Galois field(2) (GF(2)) to perform check node operations. II. PRELIMINARIES
In this section, we describe briefly binary LDPC decoding, the IB problem, GAs as an optimization tool and how stateof-the-art IB decoders work. Each concept is introduced to afterwards be employed in the direct design of simple logical circuits that maximize mutual information for LDPC decoding.

A. LDPC Decoding

LDPC codes introduced by Gallager in [9] can be characterized by a generator matrix G or a parity-check matrix H, which also detects if a codeword belongs to the code. The
special characteristic of LDPC codes is the sparsity of the non-zero elements of the parity-check matrix H. To generate codewords c of an LDPC code, one multiplies G with the uncoded data u, like

c = G · u.

(1)

On the other hand, the orthogonal parity-check matrix H with Nc rows and K columns fulfills,

H · y = 0Nc×1 ⇔ y ∈ C,

(2)

by delivering an all-zero vector, if the received codeword y belongs to the code.
In an LDPC decoder, two kinds of nodes are distinguished: variable nodes and check nodes. The connections between

and its corresponding equivalent Tanner graph in Figure 1. The parity-check matrix H dictates the connections of the
vertices of the bipartite graph. There are as many check nodes as rows in H and the number of variable nodes matches the number of columns. Each entry in the matrix not only represents the parity-checks of the block code but also describes the connections between the check nodes and variable nodes corresponding to that specific row and column. The number of connections of a node is referred to as the degree of the node, denoted as dc for check nodes and dv for variable nodes.
Moreover, LDPC codes, as a special type of block codes, can be efficiently decoded using message-passing algorithms. Messages are sent repeatedly along the Tanner graph’s edges. In this procedure, many parity-checks that involve few bits, fix by small contributions the whole long codeword using iterations. The type of data passed along the connections and the node operations designate the algorithm employed. For example, belief propagation decoders work with real numbers and log-likelihood ratios (LLRs) while bit-flipping algorithms operate with binary messages. In the iterative decoding process, both variable and check nodes compute operations with the incoming messages from their attached node. However, the delivered messages of a node edge are computed without including in the calculation the messages provided by that same node edge. In this way, extrinsic information is generated in each iteration. More information about the decoding process of real-arithmetic LDPC decoders can be found in [5], [10] and about the IB LDPC decoders in Section II-D.

B. Information Bottleneck Method
The IB method [11] is a compression technique introduced by Tishby et al. in [12] that preserves mutual information. While traditionally employed in machine learning, the IB method’s application in communications is rather new. Prior studies [13]–[16] have provided initial implementations, and this work links the IB framework to rate-distortion theory [17], [18], noting both similarities and key differences [3], [19].

In the research literature, the IB method’s adaptability extends to the design of other communication system components. For instance, it has been used for parameter acquisition in quantization [20] and in developing demodulators that adhere to the mutual information preservation principle [21].
In a nutshell, the IB method differentiates between a relevant variable X ∈ X , an observed variable Y ∈ Y, and a compressed random variable T ∈ T , which are interconnected via a Markov chain (X → Y → T). A schematic that summarizes the problem is shown in Figure 2. The objective is to find a mapping from random variable Y onto compressed variable T in the form of a conditional distribution pT|Y(t|y).
At the same time, this mapping maximizes the Shannon’s mutual information I(X; T) between the random variables X and T, as expressed in the optimization problem:

max I(X; T) for a fixed |T | < |Y|,

(4)

pT|Y (t|y)

A strong point of the IB method, unlike other compression methods such as rate-distortion theory [17], [18], is that it only requires the joint distribution pX,Y(x, y) as input to perform the mapping from Y to T. The solution mapping pT|Y(t|y) dictates how much information from X is preserved in T. The IB approach effectively assigns values of Y to T while maximizing I(X; T). Solutions of Equation (4) may be classified into deterministic mappings and soft mappings. While deterministic solutions consist of zeros and ones (i.e., each y maps to a specific t), the probabilistic solution describes transition probabilities. As in [3], [21], we will restrict ourselves to deterministic mappings p(t|y) ∈ {0, 1} ∀(y, t).
To find the solution, in [12] an iterative procedure with three consistent equations is derived by using Lagrangian multipliers. However, in this project GAs serve as a robust optimization tool for the the design of compression mappings that can be implemented using elementary bit-operations in GF(2). GAs operate on a set of candidate solutions termed population.

C. Genetic Algorithms
In this research, GAs substitute the iterative method to implement the IB method to design the check node operations. This approach is similar to the one exposed in [21], in which the best parameters θ that characterize the system are found iteratively.
The magic of GA optimization resides in that a first random generated population of size L of parameter values θ(l,0) = [θ0, θ1, . . . , θM−1], through selection and crossovers,

converges to an optimum θ∗. Generation by generation, the principle of evolution is mimicked in the GA optimization by selecting the best or fittest individuals θ(l,n) to contribute to the creation of the next generation n + 1. Throughout this paper, l ∈ {0, 1, . . . , L − 1} refers to the index in the population, while n ∈ {0, 1, . . . , N − 1} refers to the generation of the GA.
Natural selection is substituted by a fitness function, ffit θ(l,n) that evaluates the goodness of each individual as a solution to the optimization problem and allows us to rank them. Similarly, DNA chains and chromosomes are simulated using binary representations of the population members. While the real world representation is called phenotype, the binary representation of a tentative solution is called genotype g = [z0, z1, . . . , zM−1]. Each zm is a binary vector in {0, 1}qm with qm being the bit resolution.
After getting the genotype of the population members, they are assessed by the fitness function and the best solutions of iteration n are selected. Then, a mating operation called crossover is performed. As described in Figure 3, the operator combines the genotypes of two parent individuals, g(l0,n) and g(l1,n), to produce offspring, mimicking the biological process of DNA recombination. For instance, in Figure 3 one can observe that the genotype of the parents is split over the same crossover point and then concatenated together to deliver two offsprings. The crossover point does not have to necessarily be in the middle of the genotype and can cut through the bits of one of the parameters zm.
Following crossover, a mutation operation (i.e., random bit flipping in purple color in Figure 3) may be applied to introduce random alterations to the offspring’s genotype, thereby preserving genetic diversity and preventing premature convergence to suboptimal solutions. Another technique known as elitism is often employed, too. It ensures that the best-performing individuals are retained in the next generation, thereby preserving high-quality solutions.
With the crossover results or new population obtained, the process starts again. The new population is evaluated with the fitness function, ranked and mated. The optimization algorithm is repeated until a stop criterion is met. Usually, it is a maximum number of generations. More details can be found in [22]–[24].
Overall, GAs leverage these elements—population representation, fitness evaluation, selection, crossover, mutation, and elitism — to efficiently navigate vast search spaces and optimize multi-parameter problems where brute-force approaches are impractical. Additionally, our problem and the possibility to represent a decoding logical circuit with a binary matrix fits perfect with the binary nature of GA optimization.

Although the final implementation appears simple, designing LUTs that maximize mutual information is a complex task. A key technique for addressing this is density evolution, extensively detailed in [28]. This method enables the analysis of the asymptotic performance of the decoder’s output mutual information over iterations as a function of signal quality, by determining the probability distributions of messages exchanged in an LDPC decoder. When combined with IB, density evolution facilitates the design of LUTs comprised of hard mappings pT|Y(t|y) that yield t labels from {0, 1, · · · , |T | − 1}. This new operation now delivers t ∈ T from the compressed random variable event space instead of a real number.
Nevertheless, one primary problem with LUT decoding might be the number of entries when dealing with high cardinalities. To tackle the dimensionality problem Kurkoski et. al in [29] proposed to rely on the concatenation of LUTs with a reduced number of entries to deliver intermediate t labels before the final update message. Each LUT gets two inputs that come directly from the variable nodes, from updated messages or a mixture of both. This yields to the reduction of the number of total entries and M intermediate look-ups to complete the

iteration. For iteration zero to there are |Y|2 + |T ||Y| · (dc − 2) look-ups and, |T |2 · (dc − 1) for the following ones. In the context of this work, m ∈ {0, 1, . . . , |M| − 1} denotes the concatenation index within a decoding iteration i. This method is applied in [3], too.
For completeness and reference, the results presented in [3] are replicated using LDPC code [25]. First, a density evolution analysis is conducted to assess the asymptotic performance of the decoder. It is important to note that although the decoder is optimized for a specific channel quality, it remains valid for all. In other words, it is designed offline but used unmatched.
III. DESIGN OF BIT-LEVEL LDPC DECODING OPERATIONS
In this section, we develop the innovative idea of directly learning logical circuits that maximize mutual information for LDPC decoding, rather than designing LUTs and attempting to synthesize them in hardware. Firstly, the complexity of the circuitry for decoding LUTs is highlighted in terms of the number of gates. Secondly, we explain how to learn simple logical circuits to substitute check node operations using the powerful tools of density evolution and GA optimization.
A. Complexity Considerations on LUT Implementations
In the previous section, we successfully designed the stateof-the-art decoders in [3] that compute decoding operations with LUT concatenations. In this example, LUTs of 256 entries are designed to handle 4-bit precision integer messages for each two-input operation used in the decoder. V. CONCLUSION
In conclusion, this work successfully demonstrates that GAs can effectively optimize and directly learn decoding circuits for check node calculations in LDPC decoders. The research reveals that learning decoding operations at a bit-level using IB method and GAs is competitive with conventional minsum decoders. The findings support the notion that GAs can adaptively explore a wide variety of circuit configurations, leading to powerful decoding operations. Importantly, the reduced computational load achieved through our approach has significant military relevance, as it directly contributes to longer battery life for handheld devices used on the battlefield. Additionally, as we discuss advancements in 5G-NR technology, it is likely that future military systems will also adopt this powerful coding technique.
Future work should focus on replacing LUTs in variable nodes to further reduce computational complexity. Another very interesting topic to explore are new structures with few binary parameters that can be optimized for maximum preservation of mutual information with GAs for decoding. Overall, this work lays the groundwork for further advancements in efficient LDPC decoder designs.
