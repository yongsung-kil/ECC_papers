TRHyper: Low-Complexity Hypernetwork for Channel Neural Decoding With Learning Weights in Tensor Ring Format
Yuanhui Liang , Member, IEEE, Chan-Tong Lam , Senior Member, IEEE, Qingle Wu , Benjamin K. Ng , Senior Member, IEEE, and Sio-Kei Im , Member, IEEE

Abstract— In this letter, we propose a low-complexity hypernetwork for channel neural decoding with learning weights in tensor ring (TR) format, called TRHyper. The internal parameters and the number of layers of the TRHyper based channel neural decoding algorithm can be updated without retraining. We design the size of each TRHyper layer according to the size of the factor tensor in tensor ring format. During the training phase, we reuse the storage space for the learning weights of the main decoding network, so the proposed TRHyper no longer require additional storage space for its learning weights. Numerical results show that for low-density parity check (LDPC) codes, the performance of the TRHyper based channel neural decoder is similar to that of the original decoder, while for BoseChaudhuri-Hocquenghem (BCH) codes, the performance slightly exceeds the original decoder.
Index Terms— Hypernetwork, channel neural decoding, tensor ring, LDPC, BCH.
I. INTRODUCTION
THE hypernetworks can be used to improve the adaptability of channel neural decoders and avoid retraining due to changes of the internal parameters and the number of layers of the main network [1]. However, the deployment of additional hypernetwork increases the computational complexity of the neural decoding algorithm, making the already limited communication hardware resources even more challenging. Tensor decomposition, including tensor-ring (TR) decomposition have been widely used in model compression in recent years because they can be used to overcome the curse of dimensionality [2], [3], [4], [5]. In recent years, tensor decomposition has also been used to reduce the complexity of intelligent communication algorithms, achieving good compression effects due to its solid mathematical foundation and regular data structure [6], [7], [8]. The authors in [9], [10], and [11] introduced TR decomposition into channel neural decoding, which greatly reduced the required storage space and computational complexity while maintaining decoding

The neural weights are first tensorized into a high-order tensor, and then the high-order tensor is decomposed into several low-order factor tensors, whose parameters are much smaller than the original high-order tensor [11]. The factor tensors obtained by the decomposition are used to approximate the original tensors. The approximation will bring about certain precision errors, which will be partially compensated in the learning process of the neural network. The high complexity of the hypernetwork-assisted channel neural decoding algorithm hinders its deployment on mobile hardware. In addition, the hypernetwork in [1] is not suitable for channel neural decoding with learning weights in TR format, which also limits its application. We propose to jointly design the TR decomposition and hypernetwork to address the above challenges, which is also the main motivation of this study.
In recent years, the relationship between tensor decomposition and neural networks has been actively explored and applied to different fields, such as interpretability exploration of deep learning and compression of neural networks. The authors of [12] and [13] used Hierarchical Tucker (HT) tensor decomposition and tensor-train (TT) decomposition to explain the expressive power of convolution neural network (CNN) and recurrent neural network (RNN), respectively. The equivalence between neural networks and hierarchical tensor decomposition is established. They demonstrated that the shallow networks correspond to canonical polyadic (CP) decomposition, while deep networks correspond to HT decomposition [12]. Directly mapping the efficient tensor structure to the neural structure by adding nonlinear activation functions is called the nonlinear TT format in [14]. The authors used nonlinear TT decomposition to compress the neural network and achieved a compression effect that exceeds TT decomposition. Our work is to further explore the relationship between TR decomposition and neural networks for reducing the complexity of the hypernetwork-assisted channel neural decoding algorithm. Our main contributions are as follows:
• We propose a low-complexity hypernetwork for channel neural decoding, called TRHyper, which enables the internal parameters and the number of layers of the main decoding network with learning weights in TR format to be updated without retraining.
• For our proposed TRHyper, depending on the training stage, the space storing the learning weights of the main decoding network is reused to store the learning weights of the hypernetwork, which results in the saving of storage resources.

III. TRHYPER: LOW-COMPLEXITY HYPERNETWORK SOLUTION FOR LEARNING WEIGHTS IN TR FORMAT
To address the high complexity and performance degradation issue above [1], we propose a low-complexity hypernetwork solution for channel neural decoding with learning weights in TR format, called TRHyper, which makes full use of the relationship between neural networks and TR decomposition, reusing the storage space of the factor tensors in the TR format to store the learning weights of the hypernetwork, saving a lot of storage resources.

A. Hypernetwork Based on TR Decomposition
Suppose a tensor W is decomposed by TR to obtain m factor tensors of W1, W2, . . . , Wm. Assume that the input is X, and the m factor tensors decomposed above are used as learning weights of the neural network. Adding an activation function f between every two tensors can form the structure of the neural network. We can build an m-layer neural network with Y as the output as follows:
Y = f (f (· · · f (f (X ∗ W1) ∗ W2) ∗ · · · ) ∗ Wm). (4)

The above process builds a bridge between m tensors and an m-layer neural network. An m-layer hypernetwork can be established from the m factor tensors obtained after the TR decomposition of the learning weights.
B. Static TRHyper (S-TRHyper)
Our proposed S-TRHyper is a hypernetwork composed of m-layer neural networks, which is used to assist in training the channel neural decoding network. When the learning weights of the channel neural decoding network are factor tensors in TR format, the size of each layer of the S-TRHyper is specifically designed to be the same size as the corresponding factor tensor in TR format [9], [11]. When training the S-TRHyper network to obtain the optimal weights of the main channel neural decoding network, the storage space for storing the m factor tensors can be reused to store the learning weights of the m layers in the S-TRHyper. In this way, the storage resources of the learning weights of the S-TRHyper are saved. During the training phase, the optimization of the main network can be completed by training S-TRHyper, which saves the training cost required for the optimization of the main network. The schematic diagram of S-TRHyper when m is equal to 4 is shown in Fig. 1. The difference from the schematic diagram of TRHyper in [1] is that the TRHyper here is designed together with TR decomposition, and the remaining parameters can be found in [1]. The S-TRHyper solution can be divided into 5 steps as follows.
Step 1: For the NNMS+ algorithm, a TR decomposition scheme is designed for its learning weights to obtain m factor tensors {G1v′,c, . . . , Gmv′,c} with size {D1, . . . , Dm} after decomposition.
Step 2: Design storage space O according to m and {D1, . . . , Dm}. The number of layers of the designed hypernetwork is the same as the number of factor tensors, that is, both are m. In addition, the size of the learning weight tensor Wi, i = 1, . . . , m of each layer is the same as the size of the corresponding factor tensor Di, that is size(Wi) = size(Giv′,c), i = 1, . . . , m. Therefore, the storage space O designed above

can be used to store the learning weights of the designed hypernetwork.
Step 3: During training, the storage space O is used by the hypernetwork to store the learning weights Wi, i = 1, . . . , m. m tanh activation functions are added between the learning weight tensors to form an m-layer hypernetwork. The designed hypernetwork does not have its own loss function. It uses the cross-entropy loss function together with the main network. The input of the hypernetwork corresponds to the input of the main network, that is, the LLR tensor received by the channel or the information tensor svt output from the previous layer of the main network.
Step 4: As shown in Fig. 1, the established hypernetwork is trained together with the main network, and the learning weights are updated under the guidance of the loss function and the backward propagation mechanism.
Step 5: After the training, we will get the optimized TRHyper based TR-NNMS+ algorithm. The learning weights of the m layers of hypernetwork use the size of the m factor tensors respectively. The forward calculation process of hypernetwork can be seen as the inverse process of TR decomposition. Therefore, the output of the hypernetwork is the optimal learning weights of the TR-NNMS+ algorithm.
Step 6: If the channel environment changes, repeat steps 4 ∼ 5.
C. Dynamic TRHyper (D-TRHyper)
Different from the S-TRHyper above, our proposed D-TRHyper will interact with the main network at each layer. Each hypernetwork unit G consists of a neural network [1], which is similar to the S-TRHyper. Each layer of the main network accepts the learning weights generated by the hypernetwork to complete the calculation of the current layer. Then the output of each layer is not only used as the input of the next layer, but also provided to the hypernetwork as the calculation of the next iteration. The remaining operations are consistent with the S-TRHyper.

IV. COMPLEXITY ANALYSIS
In this section, we analyze the complexity of channel neural decoding algorithms based on hypernetwork proposed in this letter. We assume that the number of learning weights of the hypernetwork used in this letter is W, and the required memory storage is O(W). As shown in Table I, we list the memory storage required for different types of hypernetworks.
If the static hypernetwork proposed in [1] is adopted, d neural networks are required to generate learning weights for the main TR-NNMS+ decoding network, where d is the number of factor tensors obtained by TR decomposition. So the memory storage d ∗ O(W) is required to store the learning weights of the hypernetwork for TR-NNMS+ decoding network. If the dynamic hypernetwork proposed in [1] is adopted, the learning weights and the memory storage occupied by the learning weights are the same as those required by the static hypernetwork. For both the static and dynamic TRHyper proposed in this letter, since the memory storage of the learning weights is reused, no additional storage space is required to store the learning weights of the TRHyper. Therefore, for the channel neural decoding algorithm with learning weights in TR format, our proposed TRHyper saves d times of memory storage resources.
In the above, we only calculated the memory storage required for the learning weights of the hypernetwork. It should be pointed out that the addition of hypernetwork, in addition to introducing additional learning weights, also includes activation functions that need to be calculated and stored. We assume that there are L activation functions in this neural network. We also assume that the computational complexity required by the L activation functions during training is O (L). If the static hypernetwork proposed in [1] is adopted, d neural networks are required. Therefore, dL activation functions are required, which require a computational complexity of d∗O (L). If the dynamic hypernetwork proposed in [1] is adopted, dT ∗ L activation functions are required, which requires a computational complexity of dT ∗ O (L). Since the static TRHyper we proposed only requires one neural network, it only requires L activation functions and a computational complexity of O (L). Similarly, the dynamic TRHyepr we proposed requires T ∗ L activation functions and a computational complexity of T ∗ O (L). Therefore, for the channel neural decoding with learning weights in TR format, our proposed TRHyper saves d times of computing resources as shown in Table II.

VI. CONCLUSION
We have proposed a low-complexity hypernetwork for channel neural decoding, called TRHyper, with learning weights in TR format. The space storing the learning weights of the main decoding network can be reused to store the learning weights of the hypernetwork, resulting in the saving of storage resources. Numerical results show that the proposed TRHyper based channel decoding slightly outperforms the original decoding for BCH codes, while having comparable performance for LDPC codes, with significantly reduced complexity. For future works, the complexity of the activation functions can be further reduced by using quantization. Moreover, the extension of low-complexity hypernetworks for graph neural networks can also be considered.
