Abstract—In this paper, we study the use of the alternating method of multipliers (ADMM) to decode LDPC codes at moderate blocklengths (100 to 1000 bits). Placing ADMM decoding in a mixed-integer optimization framework, this allows us to leverage an exploration-exploitation paradigm to realize further errorcorrection performance for the same budget of total number of iterations. We demonstrate our ideas with numerical simulations for the (155,64) rate-0.4 Tanner Code and the (480,360) rate-3/4 code from the IEEE 802.22 WRAN standard.
I. INTRODUCTION
The emergence of new applications in wireless communications, such as 6G and machine-type communication [1] has spurred considerable investigation of short-to-medium blocklength error-correcting codes (<1000 bits) [2]. In the past decades, low-density parity check (LDPC) codes have been the focus of significant research, owing to their superior error-correction performance at long blocklengths when paired with iterative message-passing belief propagation (BP) decoders [3]. The requirements of the shorter blocklength regime motivate the development of new LDPC decoders that can flexibly trade off complexity for error-correction performance.
Ideally, one would have a single family of code-decoder pairs that could adapt to a wide range of blocklengths, code rates, signal-to-noise ratios, and target error rates. In the context of LDPC codes, one can increase the total number of iterations in an attempt to reduce the error rate. However, increasing the number of iterations for BP decoding results only in a moderate decrease in error rate. This is in contrast, for example, to polar codes with CRC-aided successive cancellation list (CA-SCL) decoding [4]. For polar codes under CA-SCL decoding, by increasing the size of the list and consequently increasing the decoder’s complexity, we can considerably improve the error-correction performance. It would be valuable to develop a decoder for LDPC codes that can also flexibly trade off complexity for error-correction performance, similar to how CA-SCL decoding accomplishes this for polar codes.
In an effort to improve the complexity-performance tradeoff, we introduce Mixed-Integer ADMM (MI-ADMM) decoding. In MI-ADMM decoding, we successively tighten the relaxation used in Linear Programming (LP) decoding by adding integer constraints. The idea of using integer constraints in LP decoding was first explored by Draper et al. [5] in the context of Adaptive LP decoding [6], where they used general-

purpose LP solvers and a naive search. In contrast, MI-ADMM decoding is amenable to efficient hardware implementation [7] and incorporates smarter strategies to search for codeword solutions. The possibility of adding integer constraints to ADMM decoding was noted by Kraft and Wehn [8], where they sketched the general approach and listed some challenges of using such a scheme to perform maximum likelihood (ML) decoding. In this paper we realize a practical decoding scheme that places ADMM decoding in a mixed-integer framework, provide results for some codes, and describe some of the design tradeoffs involved.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. In Section II, we provide a summary of ADMM decoding and of its parity polytope projection. In Section III, we introduce MI-ADMM decoding. In Section IV, we provide numerical results.

III. MI-ADMM DECODING
In this section, we present the mixed-integer ADMM (MIADMM) decoding algorithm, where we introduce integer constraints to tighten the LP relaxation. This places ADMM decoding into a branch-and-bound framework akin to that

sketched in [8]. In Section III-A, we first introduce the integerconstrained ADMM solver, which is a subroutine of the complete MI-ADMM decoding algorithm, defined in Section III-B. The idea of “pinning” particular variables to {0, 1} arises in various places in the LP literature: for example in [5], [8] in the context of mixed-integer LP decoding. It is also used in [11] where a low-complexity recursive approach to projection is developed by projecting onto a sequence of even- or odd-parity polytopes of smaller dimension. Lastly, in Section III-C we discuss how MI-ADMM decoding introduces an explorationexploitation trade-off.

B. Mixed-Integer ADMM Decoding
We now present the MI-ADMM decoding algorithm, which places ADMM decoding into a branch-and-bound (B&B) framework. The B&B procedure builds a tree by sequentially partitioning the solution space and storing the sub-problems as nodes in a tree structure. MI-ADMM decoding essentially constructs a B&B tree of sub-problems that partitions the solution space using integer constraints. B&B starts from the root node, which is the LP relaxation. The decoding procedure then successively “branches” on each subsequent node by adding new integer constraints, creating new nodes (and new sub-problems). MI-ADMM decoding solves these sub-problems until a valid codeword is found, or the maximum number of iterations is met. The MI-ADMM decoder has three main steps: (i) node selection, (ii) (integer-constrained) ADMM solving (as discussed in Section III-A), and (iii) variable selection.

First, the node selection step involves using the search strategy to pick the next node to explore. The particular search strategy used has a considerable impact on the complexity and performance of the decoder. Choosing a poor search strategy that traverses much of the B&B tree without finding good potential solutions would lead to increased decoding times, increased memory requirements, and decreased errorcorrection performance. We found that best-first-search consistently outperforms other search strategies. Best-first-search uses a heuristic cost function to choose the next node, along with a priority queue for the node ordering. The heuristic cost function, or cost estimation method, highlighted in the results in Section IV is dubbed strong estimation. Strong estimation performs one extra ADMM iteration with the new integer constraints and calculates the cost as the Lagrangian in equation (4). The priority queue is then ordered based on the lowest cost.
After the node is selected, this sub-problem is passed to the integer-constrained ADMM solver. The solver runs for Iinner ADMM iterations. If the output of the ADMM solving step is a codeword, then we have our solution. If not, then the algorithm continues on to the variable selection step. We note that we assume ℓ2-penalized ADMM decoding as the underlying ADMM solver [16]. Under penalized ADMM decoding, the objective function is no longer linear, making it difficult to bound the objective value. For this reason, the “bounding” portion of branch-and-bound (e.g., as discussed in [8]) is not considered.
Finally, the variable selection step involves selecting the next branching variable to which to apply an integer constraint. This step creates two new sub-problems, both of which are added to the B&B tree as new branches, introducing the integer constraints 0 and 1 for the newly selected variable. In this paper, we employ the most fractional rule as our variable selection method. The most fractional rule branches on the variable closest to 0.5, stated simply as arg mini∈K |xi − 0.5|. The intuition for this heuristic is that it constrains the most “problematic” variables, i.e., those furthest from being integer. Different heuristics can be employed for node selection, cost estimation, and variable selection to improve the decoding performance, e.g. see [17]. C. Branch Exploration vs Node Exploitation
The maximum number of iterations for the ADMM solver controls the computational complexity/error-correction performance trade-off. While there is overhead in the variable selection and cost estimation steps, the majority of the computational time remains associated with the ADMM update steps. Within the B&B framework of MI-ADMM decoding, we have a choice of how we want to distribute the total number of ADMM iterations throughout the nodes of the B&B tree. Standard ADMM decoding can be interpreted as being at one (trivial) end of a spectrum of MI-ADMM decoders, equivalent to allocating all of the iterations to solving only the root node of the B&B tree. At the heart of MI-ADMM decoding is this branchexploration/node-exploitation trade-off. We quantify this as the trade-off between the number of inner iterations (Iinner) and the maximum total iterations (Imax). Here, Iinner is the maximum number of ADMM iterations performed at each individual node of the B&B tree, while Imax is a max on the sum total number of inner iterations for the whole algorithm. By fixing Iinner as a fraction of Imax, we typically improve the errorcorrection performance at the cost of extra complexity. The extra complexity comes from the variable selection and cost estimation steps. Keeping track of the B&B tree itself requires additional computation and memory not needed by standard ADMM decoding. The total number of nodes of the B&B tree that can be explored is Imax/Iinner.
IV. NUMERICAL RESULTS
In this section, we present results for MI-ADMM decoding, where we demonstrate further improvement is possible over the standard ADMM decoder. For the ADMM decoder (both with and without integer constraints), ℓ2-penalization is used along with early termination. The augmented Lagrangian parameter µ and the penalty parameter α were found using a grid-search. The over-relaxation parameter ρ was set to 1.9

and the early termination parameter ϵ was 10−5. The integerconstrained ADMM solvers and the standard ADMM decoder use a horizontal layered update schedule [18]. Binary phaseshift keying (BPSK) modulation was used over the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel. We present results for two short-to-medium blocklength codes: the (155,64) rate-0.4 Tanner Code (C1) [19], and the (480,360) rate-3/4 code from the IEEE 802.22 WRAN Standard (C2) [20]. The simulations were performed on the Niagara supercomputer at the SciNet HPC Consortium [21]. The results for the ML simulations are courtesy of [22].
The FER performance of MI-ADMM as a function of both the Eb/N0 and Imax for the (155,64) rate-0.4 Tanner code (C1) is plotted in Fig. 1. MI-ADMM shows significant improvement over both ADMM and BP decoding. At Imax = 20000 and at an operating FER of 10−3, MI-ADMM decoding demonstrates a gain of around 0.4 dB over ADMM decoding.
In Fig. 3 we see that for the WRAN code (C2) at low SNR (≤ 3.0 dB), ADMM decoding at Imax = 20000 performs similarly to MI-ADMM decoding at the same Imax. At higher SNRs, MI-ADMM at Imax = 20000 improves on ADMM decoding by 0.25 dB. At 4.0 dB, MI-ADMM with Imax = 1000 achieves the same FER as ADMM at Imax = 20000.
The FER performance as a function of Imax for code C1 is more apparent in Fig. 2. Figure 2 plots the Imax required to achieve an operating FER of 10−3 at a given SNR. This is the Pareto frontier for the optimal iteration-SNR trade-off. MI-ADMM decoding achieves superior performance as Imax is increased compared to BP or ADMM for any Imax. At Imax = 10000, the gap between MI-ADMM and ADMM reaches approximately 0.35 dB, and the gap to BP decoding at the same number of iterations is just under 1 dB. Although the complexity of a BP iteration is not equal to that of an ADMM iteration, it is important to notice that the performance of BP does not scale as well compared to MI-ADMM decoding under increasing total iterations, which can be seen from the increasing gap in Fig. 2 as total iterations is increased. It is interesting to note that (standard) ADMM outperforms BP at these short blocklengths, an observation also made in [23].

While increasing Imax improves the frame error rate, it also increases the worst-case decoding time. However, the decoder often finds a codeword earlier in the tree search, meaning the average decoding is much lower. In our singlecore CPU simulations, the (155,64) Tanner Code at an Eb/N0 of 3 dB using MI-ADMM decoding with Imax = 20000 had a worst-case time of 3.22 seconds, but an average-case time of 9.5 × 10−4 seconds. For the (480,360) WRAN code (C2) at Eb/N0 = 3 dB, the worst case time was 12.5 seconds, while the average case time was 9.43 × 10−2 seconds.
