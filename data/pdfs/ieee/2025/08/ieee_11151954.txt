Abstract—Bit-flipping (BF) algorithms for decoding low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have attracted considerable attention due to their low complexity and high parallelism. While recent improvements have enabled certain BF decoders to approach the performance of belief-propagation (BP) decoding, many of these enhancements rely on complex mechanisms such as loop detection, decoder switching, or costly online computations. In this paper, we propose enhanced BF decoding algorithms that integrate refined checksum weighting and flipping function designs. The proposed weighting scheme jointly exploits soft-valued channel information and hard-decision consistency to better reflect the reliability of each checksum, while maintaining negligible online complexity. Additionally, we propose a flipping function design that implicitly discourages repeated flipping by incorporating the accumulated flip count into its computation, thus suppressing decoding loops without requiring explicit loop detection or decoder switching. Simulation results demonstrate that the proposed decoding strategies yield notable performance gains over existing BF decoders, with only a minor complexity increase.
Index Terms—LDPC codes, bit-flipping decoding, checksum weighting, flipping function, low-complexity decoding
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, first introduced by Gallager [1], have been adopted in several modern communication systems, including fifth-generation (5G) mobile communication. When decoded using the conventional belief-propagation (BP) algorithm (also known as the sum-product algorithm (SPA) [1]), LDPC codes can achieve near-capacity error-correcting performance, especially for long codeword lengths [2], [3]. However, due to the high complexity and iterative nature of the SPA, many simplified and enhanced decoding variants have been proposed [4], [5] to offer trade-offs among performance, complexity, and latency.
Apart from the variants of SPA, another widely discussed class of low-complexity alternatives is the bit-flipping (BF) decoding algorithms. The original BF algorithm proposed by Gallager [1] is a hard-decision decoder that is simple and hardwarefriendly. Despite its simplicity, its error-correction performance is notably inferior to BP-based methods. As a result, various enhancements to the conventional BF algorithm have been developed to close this performance gap. Most enhancements incorporate soft-decision information into the flipping functions

(FFs), which are used to estimate the reliability of bit decisions to improve both accuracy and performance. For instance, soft channel values are used in weighted BF (WBF) algorithms [6]– [9] to scale the binary checksums in the FFs, and are also employed in the gradient descent BF (GDBF) algorithm [15] to capture the correlation between bit decisions and received signals. These modified BF algorithms demonstrate varying levels of improvement over the original BF approach, hence forming the basis for many advanced BF decoders proposed in the literature [10]– [14]. In recent years, many enhanced versions of BF algorithms have been developed based on the GDBF algorithm, such as the noisy GDBF [16], the probabilistic GDBF [18], and the unified penalty GDBF (UP-GDBF) [20], in which random perturbations and/or penalty terms are introduced into the FFs to further improve decoding performance.
While the introduction of stochastic perturbations and penalty terms has proven effective in enhancing the performance of BF decoders, the generation of such random elements inevitably increases decoding complexity. Prior studies have shown that decoding failures in BF algorithms are frequently caused by the emergence of decoding loops [6], [7], [14], in which invalid decoded sequences reappear periodically after a fixed number of iterations. Consequently, rather than relying solely on randomness, an alternative strategy is to design FFs that inherently reduce the risk of decoding loop formation during the iterative decoding process. In our experiments, we have observed that loopy flipping behavior often stems from the weight generation mechanisms within the FFs. In WBF algorithms, checksums are scaled by soft-valued weights representing checksum reliability. In contrast, BF algorithms such as the conventional BF, GDBF, and the approach in [10] use either unit weights or fixed constants, which we refer to as hardvalued weights or non-weighted components in this paper. In most of the BF algorithms discussed, whether soft- or hardvalued weights are used, the checksum weights are initialized at the beginning of decoding and remain unchanged thereafter. Although this static weighting approach simplifies implementation, such fixed weights—serving as indicators of checksum reliability—gradually lose their effectiveness in representing re-

In addition, the use of static weights restricts the FF values to a small set of discrete real numbers. As a result, some FF values may remain persistently small, while others oscillate between large magnitudes. This imbalance can lead to loopy decoding behavior, in which certain bits are flipped repeatedly while others are never selected. Some dynamic weighting schemes, such as those proposed in [13], may offer a solution to this issue. The substantial computational burden and implementation cost associated with dynamic weight updating, however, may preclude its practical deployment.
To address these issues, this work proposes two refined strategies to improve BF decoding. First, we develop advanced checksum weighting schemes that enhance the estimation of parity-check reliability. By integrating soft channel observations with hard-decision consistency, the proposed methods refine the initial weight definitions used in conventional WBF decoders such as IMWBF and RRWBF. This results in a more robust representation of checksum reliability throughout the decoding process, thereby improving error correction without introducing significant complexity. Second, we propose a novel GDBFbased flipping function that adapts to the quality of the current decoding state: when a bit flip leads to a better objective value, the next flipping decision is granted more influence; otherwise, less. As a result, repeatedly-flipped bits become less likely to be selected, mitigating decoding loops. It is worth mentioning that the (1944,972) LDPC code used in our simulations is an irregular code adopted in IEEE 802.11n, which indicates that the proposed strategies remain effective even for practical irregular LDPC codes.

Section III presents the proposed refined checksum weighting schemes and the novel FF design that incorporates flip history. Simulation results and performance comparisons are discussed in Section IV. Finally, Section V concludes the paper.
II. FLIPPING FUNCTIONS AND CHECKSUM WEIGHTS
A binary (N, K) low-density parity-check (LDPC) code C of rate R = K/N is defined by an M × N parity-check matrix H = [hmn], hmn ∈ {0, 1}, where each column and row correspond to a coded (variable) bit and a parity-check equation, respectively. The Tanner graph associated with C consists of N variable nodes (VNs) and M check nodes (CNs), representing the N coded bits and M parity checks, respectively. The nth VN is denoted by vn, and the mth CN by cm. An edge exists between vn and cm if and only if hmn = 1. We define N (m) as the index set of VNs involved in the mth check equation, and M(n) as the index set of CNs connected to vn. The degrees of vn and cm are denoted by dv(n) = |M(n)| and dc(m) = |N (m)|, respectively, where |·| denotes set cardinality.

(x0, x1, . . . , xN−1) with xn = 1 − 2un, and transmitted through an additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel with zero mean and variance σ2. III. NEW CHECKSUM WEIGHTING STRATEGIES AND ENHANCED FF DESIGNS

A. Refining Weights for WBF Decoders
Although incorporating soft-valued checksum weights into the FFs can improve the accuracy of bit reliability estimation, these weights are typically computed only once at the beginning of decoding and remain fixed throughout. Such static weights may take on excessively small or large received channel values, leading to FFs that are either suppressed or overly amplified. This can result in misestimation of bit unreliability. Beyond estimation errors, our experiments reveal that extreme checksum weights are closely associated with the emergence of decoding loops. In IMWBF decoding, for instance, the weight wm(IMn) in (3) can become nearly zero when one of the |yn′ | values is very small, effectively suppressing the contribution of the corresponding checksum in the FF computation. Conversely, in RRWBF decoding, the weight wm(RnR) in (5) may become excessively large when |yn| is small, causing the FF to be dominated by a single inflated term. In either case, the resulting FF may remain nearly constant or oscillate between extreme values, increasing the risk of repeatedly flipping the same group of bits and forming persistent decoding loops.
In [14], it was shown that when a decoding loop is detected, switching from an FF based on soft-valued checksum weights to one using hard-decision weights can substantially improve error-rate performance compared to using the former alone. This observation inspires us to explore the design of a unified FF that incorporates both soft- and hard-valued weighting components, with the aim of outperforming pure soft-weighted approaches without relying on explicit loop detection or decoder switching.

B. Enhanced FF Designs for GDBF Decoding
Although the FF in GDBF decoding does not employ softvalued checksum weights—thus avoiding excessive amplification or suppression of individual checksums—its output values remain highly discrete. Once the received channel value yn is given, the corresponding FF yields only a limited set of possible values. This lack of fine-grained reliability resolution increases the risk of repeatedly selecting the same bits for flipping, thereby contributing to the formation of decoding loops. To mitigate this issue, we propose incorporating the cumulative bitflip count directly into the FF computation. This modification not only enriches the dynamic range of the FF values, but also

At the start of decoding, each fn is initialized to zero. Thereafter, whenever the nth bit is flipped, its count is updated as fn ← fn + 1. Although γl may be dynamically adjusted across iterations, we assume a constant value γl = γ in this work, with the optimal setting determined empirically through simulation.
The above modification serves two purposes: it increases the granularity of FF values, and it discourages repeated flipping of the same bit by reducing its FF values based on the associated flip history. Implicitly, this design treats each flip as a corrective action—suggesting that a frequently flipped bit is more likely to have been corrected and thus should be considered more reliable. However, such an assumption is not entirely valid. In GDBF decoding, where bit flips are guided by local gradient approximations rather than soft reliability, frequent flipping may simply indicate unstable or oscillatory behavior rather than successful correction.

After flipping a bit, we observe the resulting change in g(x). If g(x) decreases, we regard the updated state as a more reliable basis for making the next decision. This asymmetric rule adjusts the level of encouragement for upcoming flipping decisions based on the quality of the current decoding state, rather than evaluating the flipped bit itself.

B. Complexity Considerations
The simulation results demonstrate that the proposed checksum weighting strategies and flipping function modifications are both effective and computationally efficient. Compared with the IMWBF and RRWBF/mRRWBF decoders, the proposed RFWBF-I and RFWBF-R algorithms introduce only minor additional complexity —specifically, some extra additions and multiplications during the weight initialization phase. No additional operations are required during the actual decoding iterations.
For the RFGDBF decoder, the additional complexity stems from maintaining a flipping count fn for each decision bit and computing one extra term in the FF evaluation. Nevertheless, unlike the s-NGDBF and UP-GDBF decoders, the proposed RFGDBF does not require the generation of random penalties, thereby avoiding the need for pseudorandom number generators and related overhead. Consequently, the overall complexity increase remains modest while enabling substantial performance gains.
V. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we proposed two refined strategies to enhance BF decoding: advanced checksum weighting schemes and a novel FF that incorporates bit-flipping history. The proposed weights combine soft and hard information to improve checksum reliability without significant overhead. Simulation results show clear performance gains over conventional weighted BF decoders. Additionally, the flip-history–aware FF reduces repeated bit flips and decoding loops by using an asymmetric update rule that reflects both local reliability and global decoding progress. The (1944,972) LDPC code adopted in IEEE 802.11n,

used in our simulations, is an irregular code widely deployed
in practice—demonstrating that the proposed strategies remain
effective in real-world coding scenarios. Overall, the proposed
methods enhance error-rate performance with only minor com-
plexity increases, making them suitable for low-complexity,
hardware-friendly decoding applications.
