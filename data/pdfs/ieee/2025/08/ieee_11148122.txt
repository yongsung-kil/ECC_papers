A Fast Messages-Passing Algorithm for Counting Short Cycles in Bipartite Graphs
Hong Chen and Xuan He
Information Coding and Transmission Key Lab of Sichuan Province, Southwest Jiaotong University, China Email: ch3233546@163.com, xhe@swjtu.edu.cn

Abstract—As short cycles have significant impact on the lowdensity parity-check (LDPC) decoding performance, counting short cycles for LDPC code graphs (more generally, for bipartite graphs) are important. In this paper, we first develop a new efficient implementation for the improved message-passing algorithm (IMPA) for counting short cycles of lengths between g and 2lmax in bipartite graphs for any given lmax ∈ [g/2, g − 1], where g is the girth. Building upon this, we further propose a fast message-passing algorithm (FMPA) by employing a level-wise activation strategy. The FMPA has storage complexity O(δ|E|) and computational complexity O((lmax − g/2 + 1)|E|2), where δ is the minimum of the maximum node degree of each partition and |E| is the number of edges. Extensive numerical results demonstrate that, the FMPA can always run significantly faster than the IMPA for any lmax, and generally run faster than the modified breadth-first search algorithm for lmax = g/2+1, g/2+2.
I. INTRODUCTION
Originally proposed by Gallager in the early 1960s, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] are capacityapproaching codes that enable low-complexity iterative decoding. As a result, they have been extensively adopted in modern communication systems [2], [3]. Tanner graphs [4], which are a type of bipartite graphs (bigraphs for short), can equivalently represent LDPC codes. It is well known that short cycles in Tanner graphs have significant impact on the LDPC decoding performance. For example, short cycles are the primary constituent of trapping sets, which dominate the error floor performance of LDPC codes [5]. As a result, it is necessary to develop efficient algorithms for counting short cycles for Tanner graphs (more generally, for bigraphs). Note that non-bipartite graphs can be converted to bigraphs by adding a new node to each edge which splits the edge into two edges.
However, counting cycles of a given length in a general graph is generally an NP-hard problem [6], even in a bigraph [7]. Fortunately, counting only short cycles of lengths shorter than 2g for a bigraph does have polynomial-efficient algorithms, where g is the girth which denotes the length of the shortest cycles. Specifically, Karimi and Banihashemi [8] proposed a message-passing algorithm (MPA) for counting these cycles for

bigraphs with storage complexity O(δ|E|) and computational complexity O(g|E|2), where δ is the minimum of the maximum node degree of each partition and |E| is the number of edges. Building upon the MPA, Li et al. [9] developed an improved message-passing algorithm (IMPA) which maintains the same asymptotic complexities while reducing the actual running time of the MPA by a factor of two. More recently, Dehghan and Banihashemi [10] introduced a modified breadth-first search algorithm (MBFSA) to count cycles of lengths between g and g + 4 for bigraphs. While it always has storage complexity O(|E|), its computational complexities are O(|V ||E|), O(|V ||E|), and O(|E|2) for counting cycles of lengths g, g+2, and g + 4, respectively, where |V | is the number of nodes. Note that the MBFSA is realized via enumerating the types of cycles of a given length. When the cycle length exceeds g + 4, elaborating all cycle types becomes increasingly cumbersome, rendering the MBFSA less practical.
In this paper, we first develop a new efficient implementation for the IMPA for counting short cycles of lengths between g and 2lmax for any given lmax ∈ [g/2, g − 1] (as the IMPA has been proven to be faster than the MPA). Building upon this, by further employing a level-wise activation strategy, we propose a fast message-passing algorithm (FMPA). The FMPA has storage complexity O(δ|E|), which is the same as that of the IMPA. Meanwhile, the FMPA has computational complexity O((lmax − g/2 + 1)|E|2), which can be significantly lower than that of the IMPA (i.e., O(g|E|2) for any given lmax ∈ [g/2, g − 1]), particularly when g is large and lmax − g/2 + 1 is a constant. We evaluate the running time of different algorithms for counting all cycles of lengths between g and 2lmax for five LDPC codes. It is shown that, the FMPA can always run significantly faster than the IMPA. Compared to the MBFSA, the FMPA generally runs several times slower for lmax = g/2, slightly faster for lmax = g/2 + 1, and several times faster for lmax = g/2 + 2.
This paper is organized as follows. Section II provides preliminaries of graphs. Section III develops a new implementation for the IMPA. By further incorporating a level-wise activation strategy, Section IV proposes the FMPA. Section V evaluates the running time of different algorithms. Finally, Section VI concludes this paper.

II. PRELIMINARIES REGARDING GRAPHS
In this section, we introduce preliminaries regarding graphs mainly based on [11, Chapter VI]. For a given graph G = (V, E), we denote the node set and edge set by V (G) and E(G), or V and E in brief, respectively. Each node in V has a unique label, e.g., v1, v2, . . .. An (undirected) edge e ∈ E is denoted by (u, w) or equivalently (w, u), with endpoints u, w ∈ V . In this paper, we only consider simple undirected graphs, which have no loops (an edge with identical endpoints) or parallel edges (two or more edges connecting the same pair of endpoints).
For a graph G = (V, E), the node sequence P = (v1, v2, . . . , vk+1) is a walk of length k in G if (vi, vi+1) ∈ E, ∀ i ∈ [k], where v1 and vk are called the starting node and ending node, respectively. Equivalently, such a walk may be characterized by its corresponding sequence of k edges, i.e. (e1, e2, . . . , ek) such that ei = (vi, vi+1), ∀ i ∈ [k], in which e1 and ek are called the starting edge and ending edge, respectively. The walk P = (v1, v2, . . . , vk+1) is further called a path of length k if any two nodes and two edges in P are respectively distinct except v1 = vk+1 may hold. We further call the path P = (v1, v2, . . . , vk+1) a cycle, if k ≥ 2 and v1 = vk+1. We denote ηk as the number of k-cycles (cycles of length k) in graph G, where equivalent cycles (i.e., cycles differing only by cyclic permutations of their starting node or reversal of traversal direction) are counted once. The girth of a graph G, denoted by g, is defined as the length of the shortest cycle in G. For example, the graph in Fig. 1(a) has g = 4, η4 = 5, η5 = 0, and η6 = 6.
For a graph G = (V, E) and any node v ∈ V , Nv(v) ≜ {u : (u, v) ∈ E} and Ne(v) ≜ {(u, v) : (u, v) ∈ E} denote the sets of neighbor nodes and neighbor edges of v in G, respectively. Denote dv ≜ |Nv(v)| as the degree of v in G. Given root node u, for any i ∈ Z+, the i-th level is defined as Li ≜ {v : there exists a path of length i between u and v}. An example for illustrating the levels of the graph in Fig. 1(a) is shown in Fig. 1(b).
A graph G = (V, E) is called a bipartite graph (or bigraph for short), if the node set V can be partitioned into two disjoint subsets U and W (i.e., V = U ∪ W with U ∩ W = ∅) such that every edge e ∈ E connects a node in U to a node in W (i.e., E ⊆ U × W ). IV. FAST MESSAGE-PASSING ALGORITHM

When only counting for the smallest cycles, say ηg, . . . , η2lmax with lmax − g/2 > 0 being a constant, the IMPA in Algorithm 1 has computational complexity O(g|E|2). If g grows with |E|, this complexity is relatively very high (e.g., compared to the computational complexity O(|E|2) of the MBFSA [10] for counting (g + 4)-cycles). Thus, it is necessary to reduce the computational complexity of the IMPA particularly for lmax − g/2 being a constant. This motivates us to propose a new MPA, called FMPA.
A key observation on the IMPA is that, the active node set At is generally overestimated by (3), particularly for t ≤ g/2 + 1 (explained later). That is, some nodes in At may not need to perform any message update (e.g., say (4)) as there are no length-(l = t−1) paths starting at the root while ending at them. For example, v8 ∈ A2 in Fig. 2(b) and v1 ∈ A3 in Fig. 2(c) belong to this type of nodes. Thus, an efficient optimization for the IMPA is to reduce At to include only the true active nodes, each of which is necessary to perform the message update of (4).
Recall that for any l ≥ 0, the l-th level Ll includes any node that there exists a length-l path between it and the root. Thus, it is valid to set At for t ≥ 2 as follows:

V. NUMERICAL RESULTS
In this section, we evaluate the running time of our FMPA, the MBFSA [10], and the IMPA [9] for counting short cycles for LDPC codes C1–C5. We do not implement the MPA [8] since it has been shown in [9] that, the IMPA generally runs twice as fast as the MPA. C1 is the famous Tanner code [12]. C2 (PEGReg504x1008), C3 (PEGirReg504x1008) and C5 (10000.10000.3.631) are taken from [13], which were also used in [8]–[10]. C4 is a standard 802.3an code [3]. According to Table III, the FMPA always runs significantly faster than the IMPA, particularly for the sparsest code C5, verifying the advantage of the level-wise activation strategy in (10) compared to the activation strategy in (3). When comparing the MBFSA and FMPA: i) For lmax = g/2,

the MBFSA generally runs several times faster, owing to its lower asymptotic complexity with a smaller constant factor (counting g-cycles is the simplest). ii) For lmax = g/2 + 1, the MBFSA runs slightly slower except on C4, as its lower asymptotic complexity is offset by a larger constant factor. iii) For lmax = g/2 + 2, the MBFSA generally runs several times slower, as it exhibits the same asymptotic complexity but with an even larger constant factor. Among codes C1–C5, the FMPA exhibits the poorest relative performance on C4 yet the best on C5. This is because C4 is relatively dense with a large δ = min{dU , dW } = 6, whereas C5 is the sparsest with the smallest δ = 3.
VI. CONCLUSION
This paper first developed a new efficient implementation for the IMPA [9] for counting short cycles of lengths between g and 2lmax in bipartite graphs for any given lmax ∈ [g/2, g − 1], where g is the girth. Building upon this, the FMPA was proposed by further employing a level-wise activation strategy. The FMPA has storage complexity O(δ|E|), and its computational complexity O((lmax − g/2 + 1)|E|2) is the lowest in the literature for lmax ≥ g/2 + 2. Extensive numerical results show that, the FMPA can always run significantly faster than the IMPA, and thanks to its small constant factor, it is generally faster than the MBFSA [10] for lmax ≥ g/2 + 1.
