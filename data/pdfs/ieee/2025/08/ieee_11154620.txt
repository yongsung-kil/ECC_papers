Abstract—Adding redundancy to the parity-check matrix representation has been shown to improve iterative decoder performance in the classical setting due to the consequent removal of small stopping sets or other structures that adversely affect the performance. In this paper, we examine the effect of redundancy on iterative syndrome decoder performance, with the goal of improving decoding for quantum LDPC codes. In particular, we provide a sufficient condition for the graph representation of a code to guarantee that the syndrome decoder converges to the input syndrome for any given input error.
I. INTRODUCTION
Iterative decoder failure of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes is often attributed to the presence of certain combinatorial structures in the code’s graph. The graph depends on the choice of parity-check matrix, and several works have shown that parity-check matrices with added redundancy can have significantly better decoder performance [1]–[4]. In particular, by adding redundancy, many of the small harmful structures are removed. To that end, some prior work has focused on how much redundancy is needed to guarantee certain predictors of decoder performance (such as minimum stopping set size) are as large as possible (e.g. [1]). Analogously, for a code with minimum distance d, the smallest amount of redundancy needed for the bit-flipping decoder to decode up to (d − 1)/2 errors (called the pseudoredundancy) is studied in [5].
In this work, we explore the effect of redundancy on the performance of the syndrome-based decoders, particularly as that is the decoder of choice for quantum LDPC (QLDPC) codes. Our goal is to determine whether enough redundancy can guarantee that the decoder converges, regardless of the weight of the error vector. In syndrome-based decoding, the syndrome is the input and the goal is for the decoder to output an estimated error eˆ whose syndrome σeˆ matches the input syndrome σe. Once the syndromes are matched, the estimated error is applied to correct the error introduced by the channel. Error correction fails if the decoder fails to match the syndrome (i.e. it never outputs) or if there is a mis-correction, that is, the decoder produces a logical error. For QLDPC codes, a logical error occurs if e + eˆ is not a stabilizer.

Low density parity-check (LDPC) codes are codes that have sparse parity-check matrix representations. For a paritycheck matrix H of an LDPC code, its bipartite Tanner graph representation is the graph G = (V, W ; E) where the vertex set V corresponds to the codeword coordinates and the set W corresponds to the parity check equations. The vertices are called variable and check nodes, respectively. For vi ∈ V and cj ∈ W , the edge (vi, cj) ∈ E if and only if hj,i = 1 in H. The sparsity of H ensures that the graph is sparse, making it amenable to low complexity iterative decoders [9].
For quantum CSS codes, since the parity-check equations can be partitioned into those that have nonzero entries in HX and those that have nonzero entries in HZ, the corresponding Tanner graph has variable nodes that have two edge types, those determined by X errors and those determined by Z errors. Each check node is incident to only one edge type.
There is typically a correlation between X and Z errors, but for CSS codes we can ignore such correlation [10] and treat them over two independent binary symmetric channels. Given e = (eX, eZ), the binary representation of a Pauli error acting on n qubits, the corresponding error syndrome is

Observe that HX is used to decode Z errors and HZ is used to decode X errors. An all-zero syndrome indicates that the error e commutes with all the stabilizers and thus it is an undetectable error. If f is itself a stabilizer, then by definition H ⊙ f T = 0T , implying σf = 0T . This means σe+f = σe for any Pauli error e, so the addition of a stabilizer results in an undetectable change to the syndrome. This implies that syndrome-based decoding can only be performed up to stabilizer errors. Two Pauli errors e, f are called degenerate errors if they yield the same syndrome σe = σf , or equivalently, if e + f is a stabilizer. Such degeneracy has no classical analog.
Given an error e, a syndrome-based decoder receives the corresponding syndrome σe and, after initializing an estimated error eˆ, updates the estimated error eˆ by seeking to match σe = σeˆ, at which point it returns eˆ as the estimated error.
There are several ways the syndrome-based decoder can succeed or fail for QLDPC codes. First, the decoder successfully recovers the exact error e when σe = σeˆ and eˆ = e, and it successfully recovers a degenerate error when σe = σeˆ and e + eˆ ∈ rs(H). Logical operators are the elements of the Pauli group that commute with S. Elements of S commute

with each other so they are naturally (trivial) logical operators. Decoding failure occurs if there is a logical error, meaning σeˆ matches σe but e + eˆ is not a stabilizer. Finally, decoding failure occurs if σeˆ never matches σe. This can happen when σeˆ oscillates between values, and never converges, or when σeˆ does converge, just not to σe.
We now present the syndrome-based Gallager B decoder. Using the binary representations of stabilizers and error patterns, let e = (e1, e2, . . . , en) ∈ Fn2 denote an error pattern, where ei = 1 if variable node vi is in error, and ei = 0 otherwise. Thus, e is an incidence vector of the error locations. The neighboring checks of the variable nodes are either satisfied or unsatisfied, where an unsatisfied check means the incoming error messages sum to 1 (mod 2) and a satisfied check means the incoming error messages sum to 0 (mod 2). These check node values correspond to the input syndrome σe = (σ1, σ2, . . . , σk)T .
The decoder initializes the estimated error as the all-zero error pattern. That is, all variable nodes send message symbols of 0. The outgoing check node message to a variable node neighbor is the XOR of extrinsic variable node messages and syndrome input value. The outgoing variable node message is the majority value among incoming extrinsic check node messages. In a tie, the value 0 is sent, since a low weight error pattern is assumed.
The error pattern at the ℓth iteration, denoted eˆℓ, is the majority among all incoming check node values at each variable node. In a tie, the decoder assumes there is no error at this variable node. The estimated syndrome value for the ith check node ci in the ℓth iteration is

[11] studied the effects of trapping sets on this syndrome decoder. Following their terminology, sets of variable nodes that, when initially in error, result in a trapping set are called failure-inducing sets. Absorbing sets are a related structure that characterizes iterative decoder failure for classical LDPC codes [12]. An (a, b)-absorbing set A in a Tanner graph G is a subset of variable nodes such that |A| = a, there are b odd degree vertices in WA, and every variable node v ∈ A has more even degree than odd degree neighbors in GA. We showed in [13], [14] that many absorbing sets are failure-inducing sets for QLDPC codes under syndrome-based Gallager B decoding.

This approach led to several works using redundancy to remove related structures harmful to various decoders (e.g. [2], [3], [5]).
Similarly, we have observed that with syndrome-based iterative decoders, by adding redundancy either to the matrix or by modifying the graph in some way, we can remove certain failure-inducing sets. Cases such as the (4, 0)-absorbing set of Figure 1(a) were extensively considered in [11] where the authors coined the term symmetric stabilizer. Figure 1(a) is indeed a symmetric stabilizer. Note that the red and blue errors split the absorbing set into two copies of isomorphic graphs. They claimed that this symmetry forces the decoder to oscillate from one degenerate error to another and never converge. One way to handle such errors is to break the symmetry, which in our case is done by adding two additional check nodes as in Figure 1(c).
However, the issue goes beyond the symmetry. It is worth pointing out that a symmetric stabilizer is an (a, 0)-absorbing set. As we show in [14], unions of absorbing sets that overlap in certain ways are also detrimental. Specifically, [14, Thms. 11 & 13], tell us that even non-isomorphic copies can lead to failure-inducing sets. This is the case precisely when the two constituents both are absorbing sets. Therefore, while the strategy of breaking the symmetry would work in some cases, other cases would need to be considered separately, depending on the nature of the failure-inducing set. We show here that beneficial redundancy can be added systematically for failure-inducing sets stemming from absorbing sets.

VI. CONCLUSIONS
We demonstrated how additional redundancy reduced the
number of absorbing sets and ultimately decoding errors for
Gallager B and BP+OSD syndrome decoders. We presented
a necessary and sufficient graph condition ensuring that the
syndrome decoder will converge within two decoder iterations.
Desiring such fast convergence requires a strong condition on
the code’s Tanner graph; in future research, we will explore re-
laxed conditions that guarantee convergence within a practical
number of iterations. Additionally, Table I shows that maximal
redundancy is not needed for guaranteed convergence. In
future research, we will explore thresholds on the amount of
necessary redundancy needed to guarantee convergence.
