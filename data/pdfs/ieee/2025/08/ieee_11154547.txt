Deep Unfolded Optical Decoder for LDPC Codes with System Noise Mitigation
Lantian Wei†, Tadashi Wadayama†, Kazunori Hayashi‡ † Department of Computer Science, Nagoya Institute of Technology, Nagoya, Japan
‡ Graduate School of Informatics, Kyoto University, Kyoto, Japan wei.lantian@nitech.ac.jp, wadayama@nitech.ac.jp, hayashi.kazunori.4w@kyoto-u.ac.jp

Abstract—We propose an all-optical low-density parity-check (LDPC) decoder using deep unfolding with learnable damping parameters to mitigate system noise. Our approach enhances the performance of tensor-computable belief propagation (TensorBP) algorithm in noisy optical implementation while preserving its matrix-based parallel processing capabilities. Simulation results show that our method significantly outperforms conventional BP decoder under various system noise conditions, approaching ideal noiseless BP decoder performance in high signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) region. This work advances practical optical implementations of LDPC decoders for next-generation high-speed communication systems.
Index Terms—LDPC codes, belief propagation, optical computation, deep unfolding
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] have been widely adopted in various communication systems owing to their excellent error-correction performance and practical decoding algorithms. With the advancement of communication technology, new application scenarios pose higher requirements for LDPC decoding. Terahertz communication, which explores ultra-high frequency bands to achieve Tbps-level transmission rates, demands matching ultra-high-speed decoding capabilities [2], [3]. Meanwhile, all-photonics network (APN) seeks end-to-end all-optical domain signal processing to reduce optical-electrical conversion overhead [4], [5]. These developments present significant challenges to traditional electronic LDPC decoders.
Facing these challenges, optical domain signal processing has emerged as a promising approach with distinctive advantages. Optical processing inherently offers parallelism and ultra-high bandwidth, enabling real-time data processing with exceptional throughput. Recent advances in programmable photonic circuits, nanophotonic devices, and optical non-linear elements have paved the way for implementing complex algorithms optically [6], [7]. These technological breakthroughs have significantly enhanced the flexibility and precision of optical computing while substantially reducing energy consumption, addressing the bottlenecks faced by traditional electronic processing systems in ultra-high-speed scenarios.
Given the significant advantages of optical signal processing, it is natural to consider its application to LDPC decoding.

Optical LDPC decoding offers several key benefits that make it particularly attractive for next-generation communication systems. The fast processing speed of optical implementations is capable of supporting Tbps-level transmission rate, meeting the demands of ultra-high-speed applications. Additionally, optical decoders provide low energy consumption, which aligns well with green communication trends. Furthermore, the suitability for all-optical implementation eliminates the overhead associated with optical-electrical conversion, leading to more efficient end-to-end processing in optical networks.
Various optical error correction implementations have been explored in previous research [8]–[10]. Complete error correction systems have been developed using Hamming codes with optical logical gates [8], while all-optical Hamming decoding achieved microsecond-level latency [9]. Probability-domain BP decoders using phase-encoded signals have successfully balanced performance with implementation complexity [10]. Despite these advances, practical optical LDPC decoders still face significant challenges in maintaining error-correction performance comparable to their electronic counterparts, particularly when considering the inherent noise in optical systems.
Many researchers have extensively investigated BP algorithms under noisy conditions and their applications to LDPC codes [11], [12]. The performance limits of LDPC decoders in noisy environments have been studied [11], where concentration results were provided and density evolution equations for the noisy Gallager A decoder [1] were derived. Two robust algorithms for noisy BP (censoring BP and averaging BP) were proposed [12], proving that under contraction mapping conditions, these algorithms can effectively mitigate the impact of computational noise. However, these methods are difficult to implement with optical components. Censoring BP requires frequent recalculation while averaging BP needs to store all historical messages.
We propose an all-optical LDPC decoder based on Tensorcomputable BP (Tensor-BP) algorithm [13] with learnable damping parameters to mitigate system noise. Specifically, our damping approach incorporates information from only the previous iteration, thereby getting similar smoothing effects to averaging BP but being more feasible for optical implementation. Unlike traditional BP methods, Tensor-BP reformulates the decoding process into matrix-vector product (MVP), making it naturally suited for optical processing. The key advantage lies in its representation of the Tanner graph

using specially constructed matrices that enable the entire decoding process to be executed through matrix operations. This structure maps well to optical computing, where MVP can be performed in parallel using photonic components like Mach-Zehnder Interferometers (MZI) and phase shifters (PS) [14]. Furthermore, for some specific matrices, like permutation matrices, circuits based on optical switches can directly implement MVP operation without requiring MZIs. In optical systems, these operations can be executed at high speeds by encoding values in light’s properties and leveraging the inherent parallelism of wave propagation. This approach eliminates the need for explicit node-by-node message passing, significantly improving throughput while minimizing latency and critical advantages for ultra-high-speed communication applications.
The main contributions of this paper are as follows:
• Reformulating the Tensor-BP algorithm to operate in the complex domain, which eliminates separate sign and magnitude processing while enhancing decoding stability and simplifying optical implementation.
• Proposing a novel deep unfolded damping Tensor-BP decoder based on complex-domain formulation, with learnable damping parameters, enabling robust errorcorrection performance in noisy optical environments where conventional methods significantly degrade.
• Proposing a photonic circuit architecture design that leverages the matrix-based formulation of damping Tensor-BP, establishing the theoretical foundation for implementing ultra-high-speed parallel processing in nextgeneration communication systems.
Through computer simulations, we demonstrate that our proposed framework consistently outperforms conventional approaches under various system noise levels. Our trainable damping parameters effectively regulate message flow, enabling robust decoding even in noisy optical environments.

II. PRELIMINARIES

A. LDPC Codes

LDPC codes, first introduced by Gallager [1], are a class of linear block codes defined by a sparse parity-check matrix. Due to their excellent error-correction performance and hardware-friendly decoding, LDPC codes have been widely adopted in modern communication standards. B. Tensor-computable BP (Tensor-BP) Decoding

BP decoding, as one of the most widely used decoding algorithms for LDPC codes, presents implementation challenges in optical computing environments. Conventional node-to-node message passing is difficult to implement efficiently in optical hardware due to complex routing and scheduling requirements. For ultra-high-speed communication applications, decoding

algorithms need to be reformulated to leverage the inherent parallelism of optical systems.
Tensor-BP [13] provides a promising direction for optical implementation by reformulating the decoding process into matrix operations. This matrix-based approach is naturally compatible with optical computing architectures, where matrix-vector operations can be efficiently performed through the physical properties of light using components such as MZI and PS. The key idea is to represent the Tanner graph structure using specifically constructed matrices that capture the connectivity patterns of variable nodes and check nodes. C. Photonic Components
In this subsection, we briefly review the key photonic components for optical LDPC decoder implementation.
1) Addition Components: A photonic addition component can be composed of a beam splitter (BS) and a PS [15]. When using a photonic addition circuit, the output signal power is halved, resulting in signal attenuation, which necessitates the use of optical amplifiers for cascaded operations and constitutes one of the main noise sources in the system.
2) Scalar Multiplication Components: Photonic multipliers leverage nonlinear optical effects through Mach-Zehnder modulators (MZMs) or semiconductor optical amplifiers (SOAs). MZMs convert electrical signals to optical intensity variations, while SOAs achieve multiplication by controlling bias current.
3) MVP Components: Programmable photonic MVP components can be constructed from MZIs [14], combining BS, PS, and mirrors in specific arrangements.
4) Nonlinear Function Components: Photonic nonlinear functions utilize material properties or specialized structures including microring resonators, saturation-based devices [6], [7], and photonic crystal structures to realize logarithmic, exponential functions, and hyperbolic tangent [16], [17].

D. Deep unfolding (DU)
DU is a model-driven deep learning approach [18]. DU methods obtain a deep network by unfolding each iteration of the basis iterative algorithm in the time direction. For iterative decoding of linear codes, weighted BP using DU has demonstrated significant improvements by learning optimal message scaling factors [19], [20]. Each processing step in the original iterative algorithm is regarded as a layer in the network, and damping factors or message weights are optimized by minimizing a loss function. Our work extends this framework to learn optimal damping parameters for noise mitigation in optical LDPC decoders.

III. PROPOSED METHOD

A. Deep Unfolded Optical Decoder with System Noise

1) System Noise Model: In practical optical implementations of the Tensor-BP decoder, system noise is inevitable and primarily originates from optical components, especially the SOAs used to compensate signal attenuation after addition operations. We model this amplifier noise as additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN), which is well-established in [15] where SOAs generally exhibit Gaussian noise characteristics under standard operating conditions. This noise affects both message passing stages as

The learnable damping parameters {ai, bi} can be physically implemented through programmable phase shifters in the optical domain. These phase shifters are configured according to the optimized parameter values obtained from offline training and can be precisely adjusted through electronic control signals. In the physical implementation, the damping parameters for each iteration correspond to specific phase shifters in the optical path, directly influencing the mixing ratio of messages. This approach enables fine-tuning of the message passing process without increasing system complexity, effectively mitigating the impact of system noise.
It is worth noting that for quasi-cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes, the MVP of U TU and V TV with message vectors can be implemented based on optical switches, eliminating the need for complete MZI arrays, thus reducing system complexity and power consumption.

As the system noise increases, the performance of the conventional Tensor-BP algorithm degrades significantly. At σs = 1.5, n = 204, the performance loss is about 1 dB at BER 10−5 compared to the noise-free case. The loss reaches almost 2 dB when n = 1008. In contrast, our proposed method effectively mitigates the noise impact, and at σs = 1.5, for the n = 204 code, the performance is improved by nearly 0.9 dB compared to the conventional method. For the n = 1008 code, the performance is improved by nearly 1.5 dB.
The improvement is most obvious in the waterfall region, indicating that the learned damping factor successfully prevents the error propagation caused by system noise. In high SNR region, our method gets closer and closer to the performance of an ideal noise-free decoder, indicating that deep unwrapping can effectively compensate for the imperfections of optical components.

C. Message Distribution Evaluation

To visualize the impact of system noise on decoding performance and demonstrate the effectiveness of our damping approach, we examine the message distributions during the decoding process. Figure 3 shows the kernel density estimates of variable-to-check messages (β) at different decoding iterations for our experiments with code length n = 204 at SNR = 3 dB. We also observed similar patterns for checkto-variable messages (α), though these are not displayed in the figure. For the conventional Tensor-BP decoder operating in an ideal environment (σs = 0, top row), the message distributions exhibit clear bimodal characteristics that become more pronounced as iterations progress. When introducing system noise (σs = 1, middle row), these distributions become significantly degraded, showing less separation between the positive and negative clusters. This degradation occurs because system noise interferes with effective message passing, causing the decoder to stall in an unresolved state.

V. CONCLUSIONS
This paper presented an all-optical LDPC decoding framework using DU to address system noise challenges in optical implementations. By leveraging the capability of optical circuits to process complex-valued information directly, our approach reformulates the Tensor-BP algorithm to operate in the complex domain, eliminating separate sign and magnitude processing while maintaining decoding effectiveness. We introduced trainable damping factors that effectively regulate message flow, significantly improving performance in noisy optical environments. Simulations demonstrate our approach maintains robust error-correction performance where conventional methods degrade, with notable improvements in the waterfall region and near-ideal performance at high SNR.

Future work will focus on theoretical analysis using density
evolution with the damping method, and hardware implemen-
tation to fully realize the potential of all-optical LDPC de-
coders in next-generation high-speed communication systems
