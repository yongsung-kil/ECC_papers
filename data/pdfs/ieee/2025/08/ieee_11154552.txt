High Girth Spatially-Coupled LDPC Codes with Hierarchical Structure
Haizheng Li, Sisi Miao, and Laurent Schmalen Karlsruhe Institute of Technology (KIT), Communications Engineering Lab (CEL), 76187 Karlsruhe, Germany
Email: {haizheng.li@kit.edu}

Abstract—Quasi-cyclic (QC) low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are a class of LDPC codes with a simple construction facilitating hardware implementation while achieving excellent performance. In this paper, we introduce an algorithm that constructs QC spatially-coupled (SC) LDPC codes with large girth while keeping the constraint length small. The algorithm offers a “protograph to basegraph” construction, focusing on finding small lifting sizes of QC codes while avoiding short cycles. This work extends the hierarchical quasi-cyclic (HQC) construction for block LDPC codes proposed by Wang et al. to the spatially coupled case. The construction is based on the cycle relevant matrix (CRM) derived from the periodic structure of time-invariant SC-LDPC codes. Numerical results show that the proposed algorithm effectively achieves the target girth with a small lifting factor, enabling low-complexity SC code construction.
Index Terms—channel coding, LDPC codes, spatially-coupled codes, high-girth construction, protograph-based code

In [10], a construction that eliminates short trapping sets was proposed. It was shown that codes with smaller girth may even outperform those with larger girth when the small trapping sets are eliminated.
In this work, we propose to construct high-girth QC SCLDPC codes based on their cycle relevant matrix (CRM) derived from the time-invariant SC-LDPC code structure, and then adopt the concept of hierarchical quasi-cyclic (HQC) LDPC codes [8]. The construction complexity is minimized by systematically exploiting the constraints imposed by the hierarchical circulant and convolutional structures, leading to girth-optimized low-complexity SC-LDPC codes with small coupling widths and small code lengths. The construction is efficient even for high-rate codes, which typically have large and dense protographs.

I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] are an important family of channel codes with outstanding error correction capabilities. Spatially-coupled (SC) LDPC codes, or LDPC convolutional codes [2], yield superior performance due to the threshold saturation phenomenon [3]. They are considered promising in future high-throughput communication systems [4], which typically require low error floors. Short cycles are widely regarded as detrimental to the decoding performance, often contributing to the error floor under iterative decoding [5]. To mitigate this, extensive research has focused on constructing LDPC codes with large girth, both for block codes [6]–[8] and SC codes [9], [10]. The progressive edge growth (PEG) algorithm constructs LDPC block codes by greedily optimizing the local girth caused by every edge [7]. The PEG algorithm was extended to quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes in [11]. Girth-6 SC protographs were constructed in [9] by introducing a stringent structural constraints to the protograph. This construction leads to SC-LDPC codes with girth up to 10; however, the resulting coupling widths are rather large. SC-LDPC codes were also considered in [8], where the whole SC protograph was treated as a block and undergoes block code construction. This rather trivial extension leads to time-variant SC-LDPC and the construction complexity increases exponentially in the number of spatial positions; both properties are not desired in practical SC-LDPC code designs.

II. PRELIMINARIES
An [n, k] block binary LDPC code is defined by the kernel of a sparse parity-check matrix (PCM) H ∈ Fm 2 ×n, where k = n − rank(H) is the code dimension and n is the block length. The PCM of a (dv, dc) regular LDPC code has dc ones in each row and dv ones in each column. The code has the rate r = k/n and the design rate rd = 1 − dv/dc. The code can also be represented by its Tanner graph G = {N , E} [12], which is a bipartite graph with a set of check nodes (CNs) C and a set of variable nodes (VNs) V with N = V ∪ C. An edge ei,j ∈ E exists between the i-th CN and the j-th VN if Hi,j = 1, where Hi,j is the entry of row i and column j of H.
A protograph GP = {NP , EP } is a small bipartite graph similar to a Tanner graph but allowing repeated edges. The multiplicity of the edges is then denoted as Pi,j, which forms the protomatrix P ∈ NM×N . A Tanner graph can be constructed by QC lifting with a lifting factor S, where we replace each entry Pi,j in the protomatrix with a circulant matrix of size S × S, which has the row weight Pi,j, resulting in a QC LDPC code [6]. In addition, all the circulant matrices of size S × S form a ring that is isomorphic to the ring of polynomials F2[x]/⟨xS − 1⟩. Therefore, we can equivalently represent a QC LDPC code by a polynomial matrix H(x), with the circulant matrices in H replaced by the correspond-
ing polynomials under the isomorphism. A special case are type-I QC LDPC codes where H(x) contains only zeros or monomials xs ∈ F2[x]/⟨xS − 1⟩, where xs corresponds to the cyclic permutation matrix (CPM) that has ones on its s-left
shifted diagonal under the isomorphism.

III. CYCLES IN LDPC CODES WITH STRUCTURE

In a Tanner graph G = {N , E}, a cycle of length ℓ is a sequence of distinct nodes n1, n2, . . . , nℓ with ℓ ≥ 4, such that the edges (ni, ni+1) ∈ E for i = 1, 2, . . . , ℓ − 1 and (nℓ, n1) ∈ E. Or, we can directly identify a cycle as the sequence of edges in G. A graph has girth g when it does not contain any cycles of length shorter than g. In what follows, we highlight how to simplify the cycle identification by exploiting the structure induced by QC lifting and edge spreading in SC-LDPC codes.

V. HQC FOR SC-LDPC CONSTRUCTION
In this section, we introduce two algorithms for constructing HQC SC-LDPC codes from given SC protographs.

A. Block Construction and Spread
The first algorithm exploits the fact that spreading the edges of a block code does not introduce additional cycles [13].

Hence, we can “despread” the SC protograph PSC back to a block protograph P , apply Alg. 1 to it, and spread the resulting block code back to an SC code, which has automatically at least the same girth. This method is straightforward. However, when adding the submatrices back to a block protograph, we lose the information about the spreading process, which may itself remove some cycles that originally existed in the block code. Nevertheless, we can make use of this property by constructing H(x, y) with lower girth g′ = g − 2 and checking the girth of HSC(x, y) after spreading. The success of the construction is probabilistic and we need to repeat the process until the target girth is achieved or some other stopping criterion is met, indicating a failure in construction. The construction algorithm is described in Alg. 2. In fact, none of the algorithms in this paper guarantees a successful construction for an arbitrary protograph, target girth and lifting factors. We need to “try and check” for all of the construction algorithms. For simplicity, we leave out this “try and check” process in the algorithm descriptions.

VI. NUMERICAL RESULTS
We present numerical results demonstrating the effectiveness of the construction algorithms under different setups. We measure the effectiveness by the minimum lifting size Smin required to achieve a specified girth for a given protograph since it is generally easier to meet the girth requirements with a larger lifting factor.
First, we consider QC SC-LDPC codes with a regular (dv = 3, dc) underlying block code. The protograph of this underlying block code has an all-ones matrix of size dv × dv. We set dc to 6, 7, 8, respectively, and sweep over different coupling widths w ∈ {2, 3, . . . , 12}. We then try to construct girth-g = 10 QC SC-LDPC codes with Alg. 2 and Alg. 3. We set Sy = 1 and the minimum lifting size Smin = Sx reduces with increasing w. The results are shown in Fig 2. Alg. 3 can achieve lifting factors approximately half as large as those of Alg. 2. We conclude that Alg. 3 is more effective than Alg. 2 for constructing high-girth SC-LDPC codes with various lifting factors.
In many applications, e.g., high-throughput optical communication systems, high-rate codes are required. However, highrate codes often result in large, dense protographs, complicating code construction. In Fig. 3, we apply both algorithms to construct (3, 15) and (4, 20) regular QC SC-LDPC codes with design rate rd = 4/5, y-lifting size Sy = 4, target girth g ∈ {8, 10} and protographs consisting of a dv × dc all-one matrix. Alg. 3 still largely outperforms Alg. 2, and for the g = 10 case, no meaningful results can be obtained by Alg. 2. We highlight that our proposed algorithm yields an SC-LDPC code of Smin = 461, yielding a batch size of Smin·Sy ·dc = 36880 and w = 4, which is considered practical.
In Fig. 4, we compare multiple realizations of (3, 6) regular QC SC-LDPC codes of coupling width w = 2 with girth 6 and 10, respectively, constructed with lifting factors Sx = 30 and Sy = 1 from the same protograph (all-one matrix). The codes of girth 10 perform far better on average than the codes of girth 6, which validates our motivation for constructing codes with higher girth.
