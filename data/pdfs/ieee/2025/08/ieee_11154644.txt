Abstract—In this paper, we explore a construction of binary parity-check codes from nonbinary codes, with a specific focus on Reed-Solomon codes. The parity-check matrices of these codes come from the 1964 construction of superimposed codes by Kautz and Singleton. While some basic bounds on parameters of these parity-check codes are known, the specific parameters are highly dependent on the nonbinary code that one starts with. In this paper, we delve more deeply into parameters of this new class of girth six codes, showing that they are quasi-cyclic, providing bounds on the dimensions of the codes, and bounds on minimum distances in some specific cases. We present a table of small code parameters and note that some of these codes meet the best known minimum distance for binary codes. We draw connections to similar constructions in the literature, but importantly, while existing literature on related codes is largely simulation-based, we present a novel algebraic approach to determining new bounds on parameters of these codes.
Index Terms—Reed-Solomon codes, parity-check codes, LDPC codes, MDPC codes
I. INTRODUCTION
Parity-check codes are codes defined via a parity-check matrix, which is useful in determining code parameters and often forms the basis for a decoding strategy. Notable families include low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, moderatedensity parity-check (MDPC) codes, and codes based on Tanner graphs. LDPC codes and their iterative decoding algorithm were introduced by Gallager [1]. Modern research in MDPC codes is inspired by their role in post-quantum cryptography schemes based on the McEliece cryptosystem [2]. Further, quasi-cyclic parity-check codes are well-studied and widely used due to their simple and practical implementations.
Here, we explore a specific construction of parity-check codes motivated by a study of moderate-density and lowdensity parity-check codes from disjunct matrices [3]. This construction is inspired by Kautz and Singleton’s classical work on superimposed codes [4]. The Kautz and Singleton approach has seen renewed interest recently (e.g. [5], [6]), including a flurry of new work since being used for group testing for COVID-19 [7].
Kautz and Singleton’s original work used q-ary codes to create binary superimposed codes. One notable similar construction is for LDPC codes from 2-dimensional MDS codes

[8], which has also had ongoing impacts, including a special case being adopted as the standard for 10 Gigabit Ethernet over copper (10GBASE-T) [9]. Related LDPC codes from 2-dimensional MDS codes are presented in [10], [11]. The rank of QC-LDPC codes has been studied, e.g. in [12] and references therein.
In [3], it was shown that by taking a Reed-Solomon code of length n and dimension two, the parity-check codes obtained via the construction considered in this paper are free of 4cycles, which is advantageous for iterative decoding. However, specific parameters such as exact dimension and minimum distance were left as open questions.
The paper is structured as follows: Section II introduces definitions and the key constructions. For the main construction that uses a resulting binary matrix as a parity-check matrix, we provide an algebraic analysis of the codes and their parameters in Section III. Section III also shows that the main construction results in a quasi-cyclic code family with index equal to the characteristic of the base field. Results on the relationship between the primary constructions, as well as the impact of permutation and monomial equivalence in the starting q-ary codes on the resulting binary codes, are proven in Section IV. Section V gives a table of code parameters for small field sizes. Section VI concludes the paper.
II. PRELIMINARIES
We begin with some general notation. Throughout, [N ] denotes the set {1, 2, . . . , N }. The finite field with q elements is denoted Fq (q is always a power of a prime). Vectors are denoted in boldface, i.e. x. The support of a binary string x is the set of indices i where xi = 1. Finally, define [ei]n to be the ith standard basis column vector of length n, and define [ui]n to be the ith standard basis row vector of length n. If the length of the vectors is clear from context, we use shorthand ei and ui, respectively.

B. Disjunct matrices and the main constructions
Disjunct matrices, their applications, and their relationship to superimposed codes are detailed in [13]. Their application for MDPC and LDPC code constructions is discussed in [3].
Definition 3. A matrix M is D-disjunct if, for any D + 1 columns of M with one column designated, there is a row with a 1 in the designated column and a 0 in each of the other D columns.
Definition 4. Let Fq with q = pℓ be a finite field of characteristic p and let α be a primitive element of Fq. Then any element of Fq can be written as a0 + a1α + a2α2 + · · · + aℓ−1αℓ−1 for some collection of ai ∈ Fp. We will induce a p-ary lexicographic ordering on these elements where 0 < 1 < · · · < p−1 and α0 < α1 < · · · < αp−1. Similarly, for elements in Fq[x], order the coefficients of the polynomials in the p-ary lexicographic ordering and x0 < x1 < · · · .

We now describe the construction to create binary superimposed codes from q-ary codes.

code S(C) is formed by replacing each symbol αi with the column vector ei of length q.

Next we obtain a lower bound on the dimension of the code with parity-check matrix HC .
Proposition 4. Let C be an [n, k, d]q linear code. The binary code with parity-check matrix HC has dimension at least qk − (nq − (n − 1)).
Proof. The rank of HC is bounded above by nq−(n−1) since the sum of each block of q rows is the all-ones row vector. Thus the rank of the code with parity-check matrix HC is at least qk − (nq − (n − 1)).

When working with even characteristic, we can give an exact minimum distance in some cases.
Theorem 5. Let q = 2ℓ, and consider an [n, 2, n − 1] ReedSolomon code C over Fq with evaluation points from F∗q, with 2 < n < q. Then the code C(HC) has a codeword of weight q and thus d˜ ≤ q.
Proof. Assume that C has evaluation points from F∗q. Consider the set of polynomials S = {β(x − β) | β ∈ Fq}. Define the multiset ES = {(i, j) : p(i) = j for some p ∈ S} ⊆ F∗q ×Fq. Notice that (i, 0) appears twice in ES for each i ∈ Fq∗ since the zero polynomial has all zeroes and each of the other q − 1 nonzero polynomials has a unique root. Let (a, b) ∈ ES. Consider the polynomial β(x − β), for β ̸= 0. Then
β(a − β) = b, if and only if β2 + βa + b = 0 in Fq.
For a ̸= 0, the quadratic in variable β has either zero solutions or two solutions in terms of a, b (see, e.g., [18]). Thus for every pair (a, b), it must appear either zero or two times in ES. This implies the columns of HC indexed by S have an even number of 1’s in each row. Thus the set S yields a codeword in the nullspace of HC .

Theorem 6. Let C be a [n, 2, n − 1]pℓ RS code. Then C(HC ) is quasi-cyclic with index p.
Proof. Order the elements of Fpℓ as in Definition 4. We will show that, with this ordering, the matrix HC is quasi-cyclic with p × p circulant submatrices.
Let β ∈ Fq be arbitrary and let p0(x) = a + bx ∈ Fq[x] be arbitrary. Then p0(β) = a + bβ = γ0 + γ1α + · · · + γℓ−1αℓ−1 for some γi ∈ Fp. Now let pc(x) = p0(x) + c for c ∈ Fp. Notice that pc(β) = (γ0 + c) + γ1α + · · · + γℓ−1αℓ−1, where the addition γ0 + c is taken modulo p. In particular, because of our choice in polynomial and field element ordering, this shows that the polynomial immediately after p0 (modulo p) evaluates to the element immediately after β, β + 1 (modulo p). Hence, HC is made up of p × p circulants.

IV. EQUIVALENCE AND NON-EQUIVALENCE
We begin this section with a discussion of the nonequivalence of the codes from Constructions 1 and 2. While these codes share a common starting point of dimension 2 MDS q-ary codes, not only are the the final parity-check matrices different, the codes themselves are not equivalent.
Proposition 7. Constructions 1 and 2 produce nonisomorphic codes with different parameters and different quasi-cyclic indices. However, using an [n, 2, n − 1]q (n ≤ q) RS code CRS as the starting point, the equivalence classes of the codes C(HCRS ) from Construction 1 are in one-to-one correspondence with the equivalence classes of the codes C(HII) from Construction 2.
Proof. The proof is omitted for length. In brief, the argument is that column vectors [0]q−1, [ei]q−1 for i = 1, . . . , q − 1 in HII can be mapped to unit vectors of length q to obtain a permutation equivalent submatrix of the matrix HC.
Next we address notions of code equivalence for codes from Construction 1, given some equivalence of the starting codes.
Two codes C1 and C2 of length n are permutation equivalent if there exists a permutation π ∈ Sn such that π acting on the coordinates of C1 is C2.

Proposition 8. If C1 and C2 are permutation equivalent, then C(HC1 ) = C(HC2 ).
Proof. Codes C1 and C2 are permutation equivalent if there is a generator matrices G1 of C1 such that a permutation of columns of G1 generates C2. Equivalently, an n × qk array MC1 containing the codewords of C1 as columns can be transformed into an n × qk array MC2 containing the codewords of C2 as columns, via a permutation of the rows of MC1 . Expanding the MC1 array to an nq × qk matrix HC1 , we see that a permutation of the rows of MC1 is simply a permutation of the n × qk submatrices corresponding to each codeword position in C1. The nullspace of HC1 is the same as the nullspace of a block-permutation of HC1 , thus we have C(HC1 ) = C(HC2 ).
Recall that two codes C1 and C2 of length n over Fq are monomially equivalent if, given generator matrix G1 for C1, there exists a monomial matrix M such that G1M is a generator matrix for C2. A monomial matrix is a matrix M that can be decomposed as DP , where D is a diagonal matrix with entries in Fq and P is a permutation matrix. Note that in the case of binary codes, monomial and permutation equivalence
are the same.

Theorem 9. Suppose C1 and C2 are monomially equivalent. Then C(HC1 ) and C(HC2 ) are permutation equivalent and therefore monomially equivalent.

Proof. Let G1 be a generator matrix for C1. There exists a diagonal matrix D and permutation matrix P such that G1DP generates C2. In particular, if c ∈ C1, then cDP ∈ C2. Let αi be the ith entry on the diagonal of D and π be the permutation
corresponding to P . Under this operation, ci → αicπ(i). In HC1 , for all i ∈ [n], the column corresponding to c has a 1 in the row corresponding to ci of block row i and zeroes elsewhere in block row i. In HC2 , cDP , for all i ∈ [n], is such that the column corresponding to cDP has a 1 in the row
corresponding to αici and zeroes elsewhere in block row π(i). Because there is a bijection between codewords in c ∈ C1 and cDP ∈ C2, this is true for all codewords. So HC2 can be obtained via row and column permutations from HC1 and C(HC1 ) and C(HC2 ) are permutation equivalent (and hence monomially equivalent, as they are binary).

VI. CONCLUSIONS
This paper gives an algebraic approach to proving properties
of a family of quasi-cycle parity-check codes constructed using
RS codes as a starting point. Parameters were proven in some
cases and bounded in others. Some of these codes meet best
known parameters for binary codes. We note that starting with
evaluation codes or RS codes of dimension larger than two
results in codes with 4-cycles in the Tanner graph of HC [3]; exploration of such codes is left as a future research direction.
