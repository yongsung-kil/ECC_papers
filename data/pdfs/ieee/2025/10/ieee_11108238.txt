A Posteriori Guessing Random Additive Noise for Lossless Source Coding With Side Information
Javier Camino Treviño Graduate Student Member, IEEE, Meryem Benammar , Member, IEEE, and Damien Roque , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract— We propose a maximum a posteriori (MAP)approaching decoder, namely a posteriori guessing random additive noise decoding (AP-GRAND), which generalizes the existing maximum likelihood (ML)-approaching guessing random additive noise decoding (GRAND) to non-uniform sources. This decoder is notably useful for lossless source coding with side information (LSCSI) problems involving non-uniform binary sources. The proposed decoder is universal and can be applied to all channel codes. We illustrate the performance of AP-GRAND for short-blocklength Polar, BCH, and LDPC codes.
Index Terms— Lossless compression, side information, syndrome compression, maximum a posteriori, maximum likelihood.
I. INTRODUCTION
LOSSLESS source coding with side information (LSCSI), also known as the asymmetric Slepian–Wolf (SW) coding problem [1], consists of a transmitter that wishes to compress a source for a receiver that observes both the compressed source and a side-information statistically correlated to the information source (Fig. 1). lossless source coding with side information (LSCSI) is extensively used in video compression [2] and in the information reconciliation phase of secret key generation (SKG) from correlated sources [3].
Motivated by the latter application, we investigate a setting in which both the source and the side-information are binary sources, and are related by a binary symmetric channel, as is the case in static-RAM-based physically unclonable function (PUF) used in SKG [3]. Practical LSCSI schemes that operate close to the optimal theoretical compression rate of [1] consist in a syndrome-based compression [4] which relies on the analogy between the bins of random codes (resp. bin indices) and the cosets of linear block codes (resp. syndromes). This analogy allows building LSCSI schemes from powerful channel codes such as Turbo codes [5], [6], low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [7], Polar codes [8], and Bose-ChaudhuriHocquenghem (BCH) codes [9]. However, this calls for the adaptation of known channel decoders, namely the belief propagation (BP) decoder for LDPC codes, the successive cancellation (SC) decoder for Polar codes, and the Berlekamp– Massey (BM) algorithm for BCH codes, to account for the syndrome information available at the decoder.

III. A POSTERIOR GUESSING RANDOM ADDITIVE NOISE DECODING (AP-GRAND)
The MAP solution in Theorem 1 can be naively obtained via exhaustive search over the 2k sequences in the coset of the received syndrome sn−k, resulting in exponential complexity and rendering it impractical.
We propose a practical decoding algorithm, AP-GRAND, that approximates the MAP decoder via an ordered partial search. In the following, we formulate the MAP guessing random noise optimization problem, derive the corresponding loss function for sorting noise sequences, and present the final AP-GRAND algorithm.

IV. NUMERICAL RESULTS
In the following, we evaluate the BLER of AP-GRAND for the binary setting under investigation, and give some insights on its complexity. We consider three channel codes, namely a (256, 240) Polar code, a (255, 239) BCH code, and a (256, 240) QC-LDPC code with a base-graph B = [13, 14, 2, 14, 10, 1, 4, 8, 15, 15, 2, 15, 15, 7, 12, 2], expansion factor Z = 16, and girth of 16. We consider a non-uniform source (q = 0.01), and a quasi-uniform source (q = 0.49).
The MAP exhaustive search being impossible for such values of k, we choose as a theoretical benchmark the MAP bound of AP-GRAND. This bound is given by the BLER of a genie-aided decoder that produces by default the actual noise sequence as its estimate unless AP-GRAND produces a wrong estimate with a larger posterior than the actual noise sequence. By construction, the BLER of the MAP decoder lies between the BLERs of AP-GRAND and its MAP bound. In addition, we compare the performance of AP-GRAND to those of classical decoders (whenever possible) and to the ML and SO decoders of Section II-B obtained by setting respectively α = 0 (GRAND) and α = 1 (SO) in AP-GRAND. Note that, unlike the SC and BP decoders, the extension of both the BM and OSD decoders to a-posteriori decoders for BCH codes is a challenging task that we did not undertake in this work.
For all codes, when the source is non-uniform (q = 0.01), we can see in Figs. 2a–2c that AP-GRAND clearly outperforms each of GRAND and SO, as well as and SC for the Polar code and BP for the LDPC. In addition, the SO decoder outperforms the ML decoder for a wide range of values of p. Note also that, for the (255, 239) BCH code, AP-GRAND is very close to it MAP-bound, hence, it is arbitrarily close to the MAP decoder. This gap is slightly more significant for the Polar code in Fig. 2c and the LDPC code in Fig. 2b, and can be reduced by increasing the maximum number of queries gmax, which we fixed to 2n−k. When the source is quasi-uniform (q = 0.49), Figs. 2d–2f show that AP-GRAND, GRAND, SC, or BP are performing equally (and close to the MAP bound). The SO decoder is suboptimal in this case since it ignores the side-information.

V. CONCLUSION In this work, we proposed a MAP-approaching decoder that generalizes GRAND to LSCSI with non-uniform sources. The suggested decoder is universal and applies to all codes, including random and algebraic codes. Besides, the proposed AP-GRAND decoder outperforms the classical decoders for structured codes such as Polar and LDPC codes. However, similarly to GRAND, the large number of queries limits the competitiveness of the decoder to low-entropy noise scenarios and high code rates (small n − k). The theoretical complexity of AP-GRAND and the extension to soft-input decoders, such as OSD, are currently under investigation.
