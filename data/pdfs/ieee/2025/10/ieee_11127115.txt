Reduced Complexity Interval Passing for Sparse Signal Recovery

Salman Habib , Member, IEEE, Re´mi A. Chou , and Taejoon Kim , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—The reconstruction of sparse signals from a limited set of measurements poses a significant challenge as it necessitates a solution to an underdetermined system of linear equations. Compressed sensing (CS) deals with sparse signal reconstruction using techniques such as linear programming (LP) and iterative message passing schemes. The interval passing algorithm (IPA) is an attractive CS approach due to its low complexity when compared to LP. In this paper, we propose a sequential IPA that is inspired by sequential belief propagation decoding of low-density-parity-check (LDPC) codes used for forward error correction in channel coding. In the sequential setting, each check node (CN) in the Tanner graph of an LDPC measurement matrix is scheduled one at a time in every iteration, as opposed to the standard “flooding” interval passing approach in which all CNs are scheduled at once per iteration. The sequential scheme offers a significantly lower message passing complexity compared to flooding IPA on average, and for some measurement matrix and signal sparsity, a complexity reduction of approximately 36% is achieved. We show both analytically and numerically that the reconstruction accuracy of the IPA is not compromised by adopting our sequential scheduling approach.
Index Terms—Low-density-parity-check (LDPC) codes, compressed sensing, interval passing algorithm (IPA).

Reconstruction of signals from their samples is a common procedure in signal processing. Shannon has demonstrated that a signal can be accurately reconstructed from its samples as long as the sampling rate is at least half of the signal’s highest frequency [2], which is also known as the Shannon-Nyquist sampling theorem. Fortunately, it has been shown in [3] that CS requires a much smaller number of samples than required by the Shannon-Nyquist sampling rule to correctly reconstruct x, provided that the signal is k-sparse. Here, sparsity refers to the number of non-zero entries of x. Several techniques for solving this sparse signal reconstruction problem have been proposed in the literature. One approach focuses on finding a reconstructed version xˆ of x which has the smallest 0 norm [4]. However, this approach can be computationally infeasible for practical applications due to its NP-hard nature. An approximate solution to finding the smallest 0 norm, namely orthogonal matching pursuit, is proposed in [5]. Other compressed sensing approaches based on 1 norm minimization has been proposed and relies on linear programming (LP) for finding xˆ [6]. Nonetheless, this technique is also computationally intensive, especially for large measurement matrices.
Iterative message passing techniques for CS alleviates the complexity problem and has been extensively studied in prior work. Messaging passing algorithms operate on graphical structures, such as Tanner graphs of low-density-parity-check (LDPC) measurement matrices [7]. The Tanner graph of a (γ, ρ) regular LDPC measurement matrix consists of check nodes (CNs) with degree ρ and variable nodes (VNs) with degree γ, and edges connecting them. Messages or “beliefs” are propagated along these edges from CNs to VNs, and vice versa, in an iterative fashion via the belief propagation (BP) algorithm [8]. Due to its success in wireless communications, variants of the BP scheme have been envisioned for CS. Those methods include a BP based compressed sensing approach [9], [10], and the approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm [11], [12], [13], [14], [15], [16], [17]. Furthermore, an interval passing algorithm (IPA), suitable for reconstructing sparse and non-negative x, was developed in [18]. The construction of quasi-cyclic spatially coupled LDPC measurement matrices for the IPA was discussed in [19]. Although the IPA is less accurate compared to LP, its computational complexity is significantly lower than LP. In [20], a low complexity IPA is proposed for binary signals and measurement matrices. III. PROPOSED SEQUENTIAL INTERVAL PASSING ALGORITHM

In our proposed sequential IPA, abbreviated as SIPA(y, A), or SIPA, all the CNs in GA are serially scheduled in each iteration. Since a VN is connected to more than one CN, the VN can be updated more than once per iteration, unlike flooding in which a VN is updated only once per iteration. At a given time, a CN is scheduled randomly from C \ Ce, where each CN in Ce has been scheduled at an earlier time. It is worthwhile to note that the order in which the CNs are scheduled can be learned with the aid of reinforcement learning (RL), and it has been shown in [35] that an RL-based CN scheduling order outperforms a randomly generated one when soft valued sequential BP decoding of LDPC codes is considered. However, in this work, experiments reveal that a random CN scheduling order for SIPA performs just as well as an RL-based CN scheduling order. This is because in the case of SIPA, the long term rewards of the CNs, earned by the RL algorithm over many signals and iterations, are almost identical unlike channel coding. Hence, in the remainder of the paper we only consider random scheduling of the CNs for sequential interval passing.
