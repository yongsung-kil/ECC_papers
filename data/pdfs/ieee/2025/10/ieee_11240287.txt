Abstract—Recently, Huang and Zhang [1], [2] introduced the derivative as a fundamental structure of cyclic codes, based on which the derivative decoding was further presented. It is well known that some cyclic codes constructed based on finite geometries form a special class of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, which perform well under the sum-product algorithm. In this paper, we study the derivative structure of Euclidean geometry (EG) codes, with a special interest in EG-LDPC codes. It is proved that for µ = m − 2, the derivative ascendant of the extended (µ, s)th-order EG code of length 2ms is the extended (µ + 1, s)th-order twofold EG code of length 2ms. As a subcode of EG-LDPC code, the derivative descendant of the extend (1, s)thorder twofold EG code is characterized in terms of the roots of the generator polynomial. The new code relationship suggests that the twofold EG code can leverage the sum-product decoding of the derivative descendant for its derivative decoding.
I. INTRODUCTION
Recently, Huang and Zhang introduced the derivative as a fundamental structure of cyclic codes [1], [2]. For a cyclic code, two related cyclic codes, namely derivative descendant (DD) and derivative ascendant (DA), can be defined. Exploiting the code structure, a new decoding method, called derivative decoding, was further presented for cyclic codes. In a sense (more than conceptually), the derivative decoding can be viewed as a generalization of the recursive projection-aggregation decoding for Reed–Muller codes [3].
The derivative decoding is a general decoding framework for cyclic codes, supporting both hard-decision and soft-decision decoding. It is required that there be an available decoding algorithm for its cyclic DD. The performance of the derivative decoding for a cyclic code depends heavily on that of the available decoding for its cyclic DD. For most cyclic codes, however, the efficient decoding is not readily available, especially for soft decision decoding.
Codes based on finite geometries are an important class of cyclic codes [4]. Notably, some finite geometry codes form a special class of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [6], [7], which perform well under the sum-product algorithm. To verify the effectiveness of derivative decoding, finite geometry codes were used as the simulation examples in [1], [2].
In this paper, we study the derivative structure of cyclic codes constructed based on Euclidean geometries (EG), a large family of finite geometries. In particular, the subclass EGLDPC codes are of special interest. Through the derivative structure, we reveal a new connection between EG codes and

twofold EG codes [4], [8]. Consequently, a twofold EG code can be decoded with derivative decoding by leveraging the sumproduct decoding of its cyclic DD. This makes the derivative decoding a new efficient soft-decision decoding for twofold EG codes, which is different from the one that mimics the two-step majority-logic decoding [9].

B. EG Codes and Twofold EG Codes

A finite geometry has finite numbers of points, lines, and
flats. Two families of finite geometries over finite fields can
be defined, namely Euclidean and projective geometries. We
briefly review Euclidean geometries and related codes. For a positive integer m, let EG(m, 2s) denote the m-
dimensional Euclidean geometry over F2s . The finite field F2ms represents a concrete realization of EG(m, 2s). Let α be a primitive element of F2ms . Then, α∞ = 0, α0 = 1, α, · · · , α2ms−2 represent the 2ms points of EG(m, 2s), where α∞ denotes the origin of EG(m, 2s). A line in EG(m, 2s) is either a one-dimensional subspace or a coset of a
one-dimensional subspace of F2ms . For 0 ≤ µ ≤ m, a µ-flat in EG(m, 2s) is either a µ-dimensional subspace or a coset of a
µ-dimensional subspace of F2ms . For µ = 0, a 0-flat is simply a point of EG(m, 2s). For µ = 1, a 1-flat is simply a line of EG(m, 2s). Two flats that correspond to two cosets of the
same µ-dimensional subspace of F2ms are said to be parallel. For each µ-dimensional subspace of F2ms , there are 2s(m−µ) parallel µ-flats in EG(m, 2s), which form a parallel bundle of
µ-flats.

IV. DERIVATIVE DESCENDANTS OF TWOFOLD EG CODES
From Theorem 5 in Section III, we know that the cyclic DA of the extended (m−2, s)th-order EG code is the extended (m− 1, s)th-order twofold EG code. On the other hand, according to the definition of cyclic DA (refer to (19)), the cyclic DD of the twofold EG code is merely a subcode of the EG code, which is not necessarily the EG code itself.

It is known [4], [6] that if m = 2, the (0, s)th-order EG codes are LDPC codes, which can be efficiently decoded with sumproduct algorithm (SPA). Table I lists some EG-LDPC codes, twofold EG codes (which are cyclic DAs of the EG-LDPC codes), and cyclic DDs of the twofold EG codes (which are subcodes of the EG-LDPC codes). For the extended (0, s)thorder EG-LDPC code of length 22s, the dimension is [4, (8.33)]

k = 22s − 3s,

(54)

and the minimum distance is [4, Page 317]

d = 2s + 2.

(55)

For the extended (1, s)th-order twofold EG code of length 22s, the dimension is [4, (8.41)]

k = 22s − 3s + 2s.

(56)

The cyclic DD of the (1, s)th-order extended twofold EG code is a subcode of the (0, s)th-order EG-LDPC code. It can be proved that in terms of the roots of the generator polynomial, the cyclic DD of the (1, s)th-order extended twofold EG code has s more roots than the (0, s)th-order extended EG-LDPC code, which are given by

{α2i(2s+1) : 0 ≤ i < s}.

(57)

Therefore, the dimension of the cyclic DD of the (1, s)th-order extended twofold EG code is

k = 22s − 3s − s.

(58)

Remark 3: As a subcode of the EG-LDPC code, the cyclic DD of the twofold EG code has a slightly smaller dimension. Consequently, we can employ the SPA to decode the cyclic DD of the twofold EG code. The (64, 45) and (256, 191) codes in Table I were simulated in [2, Examples 3 and 4] to exhibit good performance under derivative decoding, but they were not identified as twofold EG codes in [2].
