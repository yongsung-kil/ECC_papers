Evaluation of 50G-PON FEC Tolerance to Receiver Impairments

Abstract We experimentally investigate the impacts of jitter and decision threshold resolution in a 50G-PON downstream scenario supported by an LDPC(17280, 14592) FEC. Both hard and soft input with a soft decoding scheme are evaluated in terms of BER and mean number of decoder iterations. © 2025 The Author(s)

Introduction
Amid increasing bitrates and a push to reduce CO2 footprints through network consolidation[1], the evolution of passive optical networks (PON) faces a true Cornelian dilemma: on the one hand, ensuring transmission over channels ever more degraded by intersymbol interference (ISI)[2]. On the other, enabling sufficiently high optical power budgets to allow connectivity of more clients in point-to-multipoint topologies while supporting coexistence with legacy generations.
ITU-T’s 50G-PON[3] tackles this challenge on different fronts, including operation in low chromatic dispersion wavelength windows, or the use of feedforward equalizers (FFE)[4] to mitigate residual link dispersion and bandwidth/chirp limitations of lowcost photodiodes and lasers. But the most disruptive of such measures might be the introduction of advanced forward error correction (FEC), in line with IEEE’s newest Ethernet PON (EPON)[5].
Low-density parity-check codes (LDPC)[6] have been chosen for this new generation thanks to performances allowing post-FEC error-free operation with optical power budgets high enough to sustain ⩾1:256 split-ratios over a few tens of kilometers of standard single mode fibers (SSMF). Both soft and hard input decoder types (SI and HI respectively, cf. Fig.1) are mentioned in the standard. In the first[7], the received optical signal POP T (t) is detected and converted with an analog-to-digital converter (ADC). This allows very good error correction since information is available on how close each detected bit is to the vertical decision threshold. The drawback of this approach is its complexity and cost compared to its HI counterpart since a high sampling rate or strongly parallelized ADC[8] is needed with, ideally, a high quantization resolution. In the HI solution, a simple decision operation (DEC in Fig. 1) is used with a comparator circuit, which allows a less expensive implementation. However, worst performances are expected since the decoder must correct errors using only the information on the signs of the detected se-

quence r(t). Note that soft decoding (SD) LDPC algorithms such as the minsum algorithm[9] are well capable of providing soft outputs (SO) when iteratively refining the estimations on the received samples, even with HI data.
Previously[10], we have demonstrated that E2 optical power budget class (20-35 dB) could be ensured with 3 and 4 dB margins at 50 Gbps in the downstream for HI and SI SD-LDPC decoders respectively. In a real receiver, these margins should account for implementation limitations coming especially from the optical network unit (ONU) side, where cost reduction is of paramount importance. Indeed, receiver hardware can be limited, for instance, by jitter and limited amplitude resolving capabilities. The former will cause deviations from the ideal decision instants upon detection which, in an ISI-distorted transmission, might severely impact post-FEC performances, as we will see later. The latter will impact HI FEC due to errors at the binary inputs of the decoder. Moreover, such vertical thresholds could also affect SI architectures with code rate matching, if used as channel output estimations of (untransmitted) punctured bits.
Several works have addressed LDPC codes for PON applications in the last years. Some of them focused on the optical channel characterization with FEC[11],[12] and on the gains in terms on optical power budget[13] but did not delve much into the details of degradations due to receiver implementation limitations. Here, we try to bridge the gap between both worlds by experimentally investigating the resilience of 50G-PON’s LDPC code to jitter and imprecise vertical thresholding in a 50 Gbps downstream transmission over 25 km SSMF and using a 25 GHz photodiode. We evaluate both SI and HI in terms of BER and mean number of decoder iterations, hopefully providing an useful insight on the ONU’s complexity and decoder power consumption and latency.
Experimental setup In our experimental setup (Fig. 2), a 215-1 pseudorandom binary sequence (PRBS) is systematically encoded using the protograph matrix from the standard[5]. We use the default FEC option without payload shortening and with puncturing of the last 384 parity bits to create an LDPC(17280, 14592) codeword with 84.44% code rate. The codeword is fed to a 50 Gbit/s real-time NRZ pulse pattern generator (PPG) and its output is electrically amplified before driving a 1342 nm transmitter optical subassembly (TOSA). The TOSA[19] consists of an integrated distributed feedback laser (DFB) and electro-absorption modulator (EAM) followed by a semiconductor optical amplifier (SOA) with an output power of 13.3 dBm under modulation. The optical signal propagates through 25 km SSMF and is directly detected by a 25 GHz Ge/Si avalanche photodiode (APD) with embedded transimpedance amplifier (TIA)[20]. The accumulated chromatic dispersion in the link is 76.1 ps/nm, close to the worstcase 77.1 ps/nm defined in the standard[21]. A variable optical attenuator (VOA) is used to change the mean received optical power (ROP) at the APD. To fully assess the performances of the decoders in the optical ISI channel, no scrambling, interleaving or equalization is used in our setup.
Since our LDPC decoding is done offline, we use a 33 GHz, 100 GSa/s digital sampling oscilloscope (DSO) followed by an oversampler (OVS) to recreate a 28 samples/symbol continuous-time equivalent of the detected signal. After the OVS, we perform synchronization, slicing, decoding and BER calculation. We use the computationallyefficient scaled minsum algorithm[9] with an optimized scaling factor of 0.75. Our layered decoder runs with double-precision calculations of

log-likelihood ratio (LLR)[22], in parallel for intralayer processing and in series for inter-layer operations and stops decoding if all parity-check equations are respected. A maximum of 14 iterations is assumed and three BER curves are compared, namely pre-FEC and post-FEC with SI and HI.
As an initial approach, we assume that a phaselocked loop (PLL) can compensate low frequency jitter (or wander) and we focus exclusively on shortterm decision instant variations, above 4 MHz as per 50G-PON’s jitter tolerance mask[21]. We consider a rather pessimistic case with a fixed jitter peak-to-peak amplitude ∆ = 71.4 mUIp-p around the optimal decision interval, close to the value defined in the standard (75 mUIp-p). As far as amplitude resolving is concerned, we base ourselves on the vertical resolution that can be obtained with a typical ADC. A 5 bits quantization resolution seems to be the most widely adopted choice[7],[18] here but recent works have successfully demonstrated 4 bits implementations with negligible penalties[17]. Working with 4 bits instead of 5, i.e., 16 quantization levels instead of 32, allows for a more conservative investigation of the impact of an imprecise vertical thresholding operation. We assume that automatic gain control is implemented and that quantization can be optimized over the full amplitude range of the signals, independently of the ROP. The least significant bit (LSB) amplitude obtained with an ADC in an SI architecture could be transposed without loss of generality to the voltage precision of the comparator in an HI approach. The imprecise vertical thresholds are used as references for the channel output of punctured (not transmitted) parity bits in the decoder as well as for the decisions on the decoder soft outputs. Results and Discussions
The shaded zones in Figs. 4a and 4b show the BER variations inside the considered receiver mask for the HI and SI cases, respectively. Each plot also shows the pre-FEC BER zone as well as post-FEC BER curves at other vertices of the mask as a reference. In a preliminary evaluation, we observe that the HI curve with optimal decision (left-most BER curve) imposes only 1 dB penalty in the ROP when compared to the best SI BER. Such low penalty is enabled thanks to the nature of our soft LDPC decoder and indicate a seemingly clear path towards the implementation of an E2compliant, low-cost ONU receiver, without ADC.
But as we dive deeper into the performances inside the receiver mask considered here, we notice that the HI solution is much less resilient to jitter and imprecise amplitude resolving. Indeed, the HI shaded zone is much broader than the SI’s, with an observable 2.5 dB power penalty range at BER = 10−2.5 against only 1.2 dB for the SI solution. We can also notice that imprecise slicing and decision might have a severe impact on the slopes of the post-FEC BER waterfalls, with the SI solution being more robust to such degradation than the HI. As far as the SI architecture is concerned, the penalties come from deviations of LLR distributions from their optimal Gaussian profiles[22]. This effect is further enhanced by the channel ISI coming from chromatic dispersion as well as the chirp of the laser source (linewidth enhancement factor αH = 0.3[19]) and the limited bandwidth of the receiver. For the HI architecture, the jitter effect coupled to a bad decision on the detected symbols will simply lead to more errors on the binary values used at the inputs of the decoder. Both HI and SI approaches are also affected in another way by imprecise amplitude thresholds due to rate-matching

since here those values are used not only at the decision of the LDPC decoder soft outputs but also as references for untransmitted, punctured bits.
From Fig. 4c, we can also see that such imperfections can potentially have a severe impact in terms of decoder power consumption and latency. As expected from the BER results, the SI solution allows for less variability of the mean number of decoding iterations within the considered receiver mask. For instance, the number of decoder iterations will vary between 4 and 11 on average at -25.2 dBm. The effects of an imprecise decision are, however, even more severe with HI data, going, for instance, from 4 to 14 iterations on average at -24.4 dBm and pushing the decoder from a comfortable high error-correction state with only a few decoding iterations to frequent decoding failures after the maximum number of iterations.
Conclusions We have experimentally evaluated the resilience of the default 50G-PON LDPC(17280, 14592) FEC to receiver jitter and amplitude resolution. We have compared post-FEC performance using hard and soft input decoding with the minsum soft-decoding algorithm over a 50 Gbps downstream link (25 km SSMF) and with a 25 GHz photodiode. Our main goal was to assess the feasibility of low-cost, ADCless, hard-input (HI) receivers for the ONU side, compliant to E2 power budget, to jitter & dispersion limits defined in the standard and close to an actual hardware implementation.
Results show that the HI approach incurs only 1 dB ROP penalty under ideal decision conditions when compared to the SI but offers lower robustness to receiver decision imperfections. Indeed, the SI solution yields lower power penalties and and fewer decoder iterations, suggesting potential benefits in power consumption and latency for the decoding block. This implies that eventual cost savings enabled by a HI receiver would likely be trade-off against a better decision circuitry and/or an additional (analog) equalization block.
