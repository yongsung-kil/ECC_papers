Abstract:- Burst errorsвАФextended runs of consecutive bit
corruptionsвАФpose a fundamental challenge for next-generation
systems such as 6G wireless networks and QLC NAND flash
memories. Conventional error correction codes (ECC), such as
LDPC and BCH, offer strong protection against random noise
but deteriorate markedly when faced with structured burst
patterns. Conversely, AI-based detectors can localize burst-prone
regions but lack mathematically grounded correction. To bridge
this gap, we introduce a hybrid error-control framework that
couples deep learning models (CNNs, LSTMs, and Transformers)
for burst-region detection with LDPC decoding to ensure reliable
correction. Evaluation was carried out using synthetic datasets
together with real-world traces covering wireless, optical, and
memory use cases. Experimental evaluations confirm consistent
gains in bit error rate (BER), burst coverage ratio (BCR), and
decoding latency, outperforming both ECC-only and AI-only
baselines. Beyond performance gains, the design emphasizes
explainability, scalability, and
hardware readiness,
thereby
positioning it as a strong candidate for real-time deployment in
future
communication
networks
and
high-density
storage
systems.

I. INTRODUCTION
Reliability in modern communication and storage
relies strongly on effective error-control methods, particularly
in the presence of burst errors, i.e., sequential clusters of bit
corruptions. Such errors often occur in advanced technologies,
including high-speed wireless links, optical channels, dense
storage media such as QLC NAND flash, and emerging 6G
networks. Unlike isolated bit flips, Burst errors may corrupt
several consecutive symbols or storage cells, which severely
reduces data integrity and overall performance [1], [2].
For many years, BCH and LDPC codes have played a
central role in maintaining data reliability in communication
networks. They are particularly effective against random bit
errors under AWGN environments, and therefore have been
widely adopted in standards such as 5G [3]. However, most
ECC schemes, optimized for random noise in memoryless
channels, often fail to adapt effectively to structured and
length-varying burst disturbances [4]. This limitation poses a
significant challenge in practical systems vulnerable to burst
errors.
Concurrent developments in deep learning (DL) have led to
the emergence of models such as Convolutional Neural
Networks
(CNNs),
Long
Short-Term
Memory
(LSTM)
networks, and Transformers, which are capable of capturing
intricate temporal and spatial patterns. These models are being
explored for tasks such as signal denoising, error detection, and
sequence prediction in both communication and storage
domains [5]. While effective at detecting burst patterns, they
often lack decoding guarantees, suffer from higher latency, and
function as black-box systems. This raises concerns in safety-
critical applications where interpretability and reliability are
essential.
This research addresses these limitations by proposing a
validated hybrid framework that integrates deep learning for
adaptive burst detection with ECC modulesвАФparticularly
LDPCвАФfor robust and explainable correction. Unlike earlier
approaches that separate detection and correction stages, our
design encourages real-time coordination between AI detection
and ECC-based correction. The framework is evaluated on
burst error patterns in wireless (6G, optical) and storage
(NAND flash) domains, using both synthetic and real-world
datasets.
The primary objectives of this research are as follows: (1)
to build a hybrid deep learningвАУECC architecture for burst
error detection and correction that can detect and correct burst
errors in dynamic environments, (2) to design an AI-based
detection mechanism using CNNs, LSTMs, and Transformer
models for variable-length burst patterns, (3) to guide LDPC
decoding with AI-predicted burst regions for precise correction
for accurate correction, (4) to benchmark the system in terms
of Bit Error Rate (BER), Burst Correction Rate (BCR), and
decoding latency, and (5) to ensure the solution is scalable,
explainable, and suitable for hardware-level deployment in

II. RELATED WORK
This section presents a comprehensive review of the key
developments in burst error correction. It is structured into
three subsections: (1) traditional error correction code (ECC)

and correction approaches, and (3) emerging hybrid AI ECC
models that combine both domains for improved burst error
resilience

Traditional ECC Techniques for Burst Error Correction

[15],

motivate the exploration of data-driven approaches such as

learning to identify structured error patterns.
B. Deep LearningвАУBased Burst Error Detection and
Prediction
Recent studies employ deep learning (DL) to analyze error
patterns in time-series and signal data, particularly in wireless
communication and memory systems. CNNs are useful for
identifying localized spatial error features, whereas LSTMs
capture temporal patterns in error sequences [5]. These
architectures have shown promise in tasks such as denoising,
symbol-level prediction, and error localization.
Transformer
models,
originally
designed
for
natural
language processing, extend these capabilities by effectively
modeling long-range dependencies. Through self-attention,
Transformers
capture
long-range
dependencies
more
effectively than recurrent models, providing stronger global
context, making them particularly effective for predicting burst
locations and durations. Despite these advances, DL-based
approaches face two fundamental challenges. First, they
function primarily as probabilistic classifiers and cannot
provide deterministic correction guarantees, thereby limiting
their reliability in safety-critical domains. Second, they require
large labeled datasets
and
remain prone
to
overfitting,
hindering
scalability
across
diverse
channel
conditions.
Hardware deployment is further constrained by power and
memory budgets, though even hardware-oriented techniques
such as quantization and pruning provide only partial relief.
Nonetheless, the adaptability of DL models to diverse burst
patterns
positions
them
as
strong
enablers
of
hybrid
frameworks when paired with conventional ECC. This synergy
has led to hybrid AIвАУECC architectures, which aim to harness
the predictive adaptability of DL alongside the proven
reliability of coding theory.
C. Hybrid AIвАУECC Models for Burst Error Resilience
To overcome the complementary limitations of ECC and
DL, recent research has explored hybrid architectures that
combine AIвАЩs adaptability with the mathematical rigor of
coding theory. In these designs, DL models first identify or
predict burst-prone regions, while classical ECC decodersвАФ
most often LDPCвАФsubsequently perform targeted correction
[21]. For example, CNN- or Transformer-based detectors can
flag burst-prone clusters, which LDPC decoders then exploit to
allocate correction resources more efficiently.
Hybrid systems consistently demonstrate measurable gains
in bit error rate (BER) and burst coverage ratio (BCR) under
burst-prone conditions [19]. Coupling AI-driven detection with
soft-decision LDPC decoding reduces unnecessary iterations,
lowers computational complexity, and enhances decoding
efficiency
[7],
[11].
Moreover,
hardware-aware
designs
selectively activate AI modules, thereby minimizing power
consumption and latency overhead. Despite these benefits,
hybrid approaches remain largely experimental. Most rely on
simulation-based evaluations rather than hardware prototypes,
and formal standardization is absent. Validation under realistic
burst conditions in wireless (6G) and high-density storage
scenarios is still limited. This study explicitly advances a
hybrid AIвАУECC framework that unites learning-based burst
detection with mathematically grounded LDPC decoding,
thereby enabling real-time resilience across next-generation
communication and storage systems.

III. PROPOSED METHODOLOGY
A. System Overview
Our framework combines deep learningвАУdriven detection
with classical ECC decoding, forming a hybrid burst error
correction approach. This design specifically targets the
difficulties
posed
by
dense
burst
errors
in
advanced
communication and storage scenarios. The design stems from
the observation that traditional ECC methods often fail to
detect dynamic burst error patterns reliably, while AI models,
despite their adaptability, lack the necessary correction
guarantees. We combine the adaptability of deep neural
networks for identifying burst locations with the proven
reliability of LDPC codes to handle the correction process.
Figure 1 provides an overview of the end to end pipeline. A
corrupted sequence is received from a noisy communication or
storage channel and passed into a deep detection module
composed of CNN, LSTM, and Transformer layers. This
module outputs a probabilistic mask indicating burst locations,
which then guides the LDPC decoder for focused correction.
The architecture supports modular deployment and scalability,
making it appropriate for applications in both communication
systems (e.g., 6G wireless) and memory platforms (e.g.,
NAND flash).

E.
Integration Mechanism
A shared attention-based probability interface links the AI
detection output with LDPC correction for seamless integration.
The soft mask output from the Transformer layer is post
processed via a softmax and thresholding operation to generate
a burst prior map. The resulting map adjusts the initial log-
likelihoods used by the LDPC decoder, improving its focus on
burst-affected regions [27]. Moreover, the LDPC decoder
utilizes a feedback loop, returning residual bitwise entropy to
refine AI learning during iterative training. Through this
collaboration,
the
AI
can
concentrate
on
uncertain
or
challenging regions, while the ECC logic ensures that final
corrections are grounded in formal decoding rules. Compared
to isolated modules, This integration shortens decoding
duration and strengthens reliability when implemented in
hardware systems.
F. Training and Optimization Strategy
The AI framework was optimized with both synthetic burst
datasets
and
real-world

derived
from
channel
simulations (AWGN, Rayleigh fading) and real world patterns
sourced
from
SSD
and
fiber-optic

Data
augmentation includes bit inversion, error stretch, and multi
length overlapping bursts. Model training employed the Adam
optimizer with a cosine-decay learning rate policy. To avoid
overfitting, dropout (set to 0.3) and batch normalization were
incorporated. Cross-validation is used to ensure generalization.
The LDPC decoder is initialized with standard 5G matrices and
adapted to align with the AI-predicted burst regions. This setup
reflects best practices seen in [21].
G. Adaptability and Scalability
Designed for modularity, the system allows updates to the
AI component without changing the LDPC logic, making it
suitable for evolving channels such as 6G wireless and flash
memory. Further, the decoder can swap out LDPC for BCH or
polar codes in the same pipeline. It also supports quantization-
aware training, enabling efficient deployment in memory-
constrained
embedded
devices.
This
flexibility
enables
deployment across diverse platforms, from low-power IoT
nodes to cloud-scale storage systems, aligning with scalability
objectives highlighted in [22]. Explainability is maintained by
visualizing the AI generated masks and decoder convergence
maps, aiding in debugging and certification.

Performance was assessed using four major metrics: Bit
Error Rate (BER), Burst Correction Rate (BCR), Localization
Accuracy (LA), and Decoding Latency. BER was calculated as
the ratio of incorrectly decoded bits to total transmitted bits.
BCR represented the proportion of burst error segments that
were successfully localized and cor- rected in entirety.
Localization Accuracy was determined using the Intersection
over Union (IoU) between predicted burst intervals and ground
truth error regions, following standard evaluation in object
detection-inspired burst prediction models. Decoding Latency
measured the total processing time required to convert a
corrupted input sequence into its corrected output. All metrics
were computed over varying SNR levels, burst lengths, and
dataset types to ensure robustness. These evaluation metrics are
widely accepted in burst error studies and are frequently
applied in ECC benchmarking for both communication and
storage systems [2], [22]. To establish the benefit of our hybrid
model, three baseline systems were compared: LDPC only

VI. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
We proposed a hybrid error correction strategy that merges
deep learning with LDPC coding to improve burst-error
robustness for future communication and storage platforms. By
combining CNN, LSTM, and Transformer architectures, the
system effectively identified burst-prone segments, which were
then corrected using LDPC decoding. Evaluations on datasets
emulating
6G
wireless
noise
and
NAND
flash
errors
demonstrated consistent performance improvements, achieving
a bit error rate near 10вБї¬≥, burst coverage above 93%, and
decoding latency below 3.5 ms. These results confirm the
effectiveness of uniting spatio-temporal deep learning with
coding theory, showing strong potential for deployment in
wireless transceivers, memory interfaces, and edge devices
under hardware constraints.
Looking ahead, this framework can be extended by
exploring other forward error correction codes such as ReedвАУ
Solomon, Polar, and BCH for domain-specific use cases,
including ultra-reliable low-latency communication and dense
storage. Future efforts will also focus on encoderвАУdecoder co-
optimization, self-supervised learning, and online fine-tuning
to adapt dynamically to changing channel conditions. Cross-
domain validation across environments such as underwater
acoustic links, satellite telemetry, and quantum memories will
further test the systemвАЩs robustness. Finally, integrating
interpretability tools will enhance transparency and trust,
supporting safe adoption in mission-critical applications. In
Overall, the framework offers a practical pathway to real-time,
explainable, and hardware-efficient burst error correction in
next-generation communication and storage systems.
