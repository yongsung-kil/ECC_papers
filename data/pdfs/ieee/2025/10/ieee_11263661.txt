Error Correction Code Acceleration in Future RAN: Challenges and Opportunities
Minwoo Kim∗ and Hyun Jong Yang† ∗Dept. of Electrical Engineering, POSTECH †Dept. of Electrical and Computer Engineering and Institute of New Media and Communications, Seoul National University
∗Pohang, †Seoul, Republic of Korea mwkim0210@postech.ac.kr, hjyang@snu.ac.kr

Abstract—With the proposal of new communication enhancement axes such as ultra-reliable low-latency communications (URLLC), researchers are targeting to develop a fast and errorfree communication technology in many aspects. In particular, this paper aims to provide a survey on the recent error correction code (ECC) optimization research. We focus on the acceleration of ECC using novel design of application-specific integrated circuits (ASICs), field-programmable gate arrays (FPGAs), and new encoding and decoding algorithms. We also address artificial intelligence (AI)-based ECCs. We discuss their advantages, limitations, and their potential in future network environments.
Index Terms—Channel coding, artificial intelligence (AI), lowdensity parity check (LDPC) codes, polar codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
Ultra-reliable low-latency communication (URLLC) has been proposed as one of the main targets of fifth generation (5G) mobile networks. Also, recent advancements of WiFi 7 bring innovations including higher-order modulation, multi-link operation, and enhanced multi-user multiple-input multiple-output (MU-MIMO). All of these innovations aim to support new applications such as virtual reality (VR), extended reality (XR) and vehicle-to-everything (V2X) which are gaining popularity. This leads to the urgent need for fast and reliable communication.
This has led to the need for advances in channel coding, which have played a crucial role in digital communications, developing a reliable but fast communication link. Following the need, advances on error correction code (ECC) schemes have been made, in codes such as turbo codes, low-density parity check (LDPC) codes, and polar codes. With their near-capacity-achieving performance, these codes have been implemented in standards to improve communication quality. Although many codes were proposed decades ago, they are still being actively studied. More specifically, acceleration of ECC has been extensively investigated.

II. ECC ACCELERATION
In this section, we briefly explain the coding schemes considered in this survey. Next, we describe how studies have accelerated and optimized ECC. The methods are divided into 3 classes: algorithmic, hardware (HW)-based, and neural network (NN)-assisted approach. We have summarized the various optimization methods in Table I.
A. Channel Coding Methods
In this paper, we focus on the acceleration of LDPC and polar codes, two main channel coding schemes in 5G NR (new radio). Here, we briefly introduce the schemes, along with their applications in modern mobile communication standards.
LDPC codes are linear block codes defined by a sparse parity check matrix. Initially proposed in 1962 by R. Gallager, LDPC was rediscovered decades later due to advancements in iterative decoding such as belief propagation (BP). LDPC codes have been widely adopted in recent standards such as IEEE 802.11 (Wi-Fi), DVB-S2, and 5G NR. The strength of LDPC codes lies in their efficient BP decoding, which uses the sparse structure of the LDPC Tanner graph for highthroughput parallel implementations. Its sparse parity check matrices allow for a highly efficient iterative decoding with fast convergence and low complexity.

Polar codes are the first codes proven to achieve channel capacity for binary-input discrete memoryless channels (BDMC) under successive cancellation (SC) decoding. Polar codes utilize channel polarization, which transforms a set of identical channels into ones that are highly reliable or completely noisy. Polar codes were selected for control channel in 5G NR due to their low decoding latency at short block lengths. SC decoding can be interpreted as a sequence of single parity check (SPC) decoding and repetition decoding on a binary tree illustrated as Fig. 1. Although SC decoding is suboptimal for practical use, enhancements such as successive cancellation list (SCL) decoding with cyclic redundancy check (CRC)-aided decoding significantly improve performance. As illustrated in Fig. 1, its decoding is inherently sequential, leading to limited parallelism and higher latency.
B. Algorithmic Approach for ECC Acceleration
Algorithmic approaches focus on building parallelizable structures for the decoder. [1] proposed a parallelization method using multiple sub-decoders. Two types of subdecoders are proposed, where one decoder uses the original parity check matrix, and the other uses a new parity check matrix generated from a sub-block of the original code.
Authors of [2] proposed using a set of parallel subdecoders for polar codes, where each sub-decoder decodes an independent sub-block of the original code. After such parallel decoding, bit-wise decoding is executed to obtain the final output. Given M sub-decoders where each sub-decoder decodes a polar code of block size 1/M , the authors achieved M times faster decoding speed. Another study investigated a SC decoding acceleration method by dividing the polar code tree into log2(N ) levels, where N is the codeword length [16]. [3] proposed a decoding acceleration method using a compact data structure in ARM platforms. By updating the message immediately after the data is loaded into cache, the authors reduced the memory requirement by approximately 50 % and achieved a three-time speedup in processing time.
C. Hardware-Based Acceleration of ECC
Some studies have implemented their acceleration approach on a HW device, effectively displaying their performance. We

focus on System on Chip (SoC) implementations, field programmable gate array (FPGA) design, and graphic processing unit (GPU)-specific algorithms for ECC acceleration.
Many studies have explored LDPC parallelization. Multicore implementations of the Gallager-E decoding algorithm have been proposed by exploiting multi-core central processing units (CPUs) and single instruction multiple data (SIMD) vectorization [4]. Also, SoC implementation of parallel LDPC decoder has also been presented [5].
Although polar decoding is tricky to parallelize due to its sequential nature, studies have explored various methods to parallelize polar codes [6], [17]. Authors of [6] proposed a parallelization method similar to LDPC decoding and implemented it on an FPGA. The authors bypassed the sequential decoding nature by generating a Tanner graph with sparse edges, therefore mimicking the Tanner graphs of LDPC code. The dense Tanner graph of polar code is carefully pruned by removing edges with less importance. The results show that the proposed parallelization is indeed effective, while displaying lower BER performance given the equal number of iterations under BP decoding. Recursive decoder design has been proposed [17], where special node types are implemented on an ASIC to facilitate parallelized processing at an SC decoder. [7] proposed a high-throughput and HW-efficient decoder architecture for both LDPC and polar decoding by using shared-memory HW.
GPU-based methods have also been addressed. [8] implemented a parallelized LDPC decoder by optimizing thread allocation. Decoding multiple codewords in parallel using GPU was also proposed, storing messages of multiple codewords on the same edge contiguously as a package [18]. [19] optimized GPU-based decoding using memory-reduced forward-backward approach for the check node update.
D. Neural Network-Assisted ECC Acceleration
AI has gained popularity due to its performance in nonlinear, complex tasks given sufficient data. More specifically, its ability to decode without explicit algorithms and its ability to optimize jointly with other communication elements make NN an appealing candidate for ECC acceleration. Following the success of deep learning (DL) in communication tasks, many studies have integrated NNs into channel coding, exhibiting good performance. NNs have been widely used in codeword construction. [9] proposed a DL-based reliability sequence for BP decoder, by representing the message and frozen bits of the code as a binary vector. Authors of [10] proposed a more systematic approach for polar code acceleration, where the polar code tree is divided into multiple types of nodes. Here, analytic acceleration methods for each node types are introduced along with convolution neural network (CNN), which decodes redundant nodes. Another approach for DLbased polar decoding is proposed in [11], where a bit-flipping metric is introduced and is applied to SC flip decoding. The new metric determines if additional computation is needed when the decoding fails. The method results in a big reduction in the number of multiplications and additions.

Recently, transformer architecture is gaining popularity in ECC studies. Initially developed for natural language tasks, transformers have been proven to show good performance in other sequential tasks. The parallelizable self-attention of transformers is the key feature behind the success in language processing. [12] proposed a novel error correction code transformer (ECCT) using code-aware masked attention, proving its performance in both LDPC and polar codes. Though the method may be inefficient compared to nonlearning methods, it still provides a solution with less time complexity compared to other learning-based methods. [13] used sequential transformer models to iteratively construct a polar code for any given length and rate. As ECCTs are computationally heavy, various attempts have been made to accelerate and optimize the solution. [20] addressed ECCT acceleration using quantization, optimized self-attention, and Tanner graph-dependent positional encoding. The error floor problem of ECCT have also been addressed [21], with a new loss function that lowers the floor. In addition to lightweight ECCT architectures, large language model (LLM)based communication [22] is being investigated and may also be a future solution to a reliable and fast communication.
NNs often struggle with codes of large code length due to its limited scalability, known as curse of dimensionality [14]. In short, as the number of information bits increases, the number of classes, or the size of the codebook space the NN has to distinguish increases exponentially. As a consequence, many works regarding NN-based decoding involves codes with short code length. Researchers of [14] bypassed the problem by replacing a smaller sub-block of the polar code decoder using an NN. The NN output is later jointly decoded using BP algorithm. It successfully exhibited that the proposed approach can reduce the latency of BP decoding, while being competitive with SC and BP in terms of BER performance. Also, [15] proposed a non-linear generalization of the polar code aided by parameterized neural networks with expandable polar kernel. l × l-sized kernel is proposed, which is an expanded version of the 2 × 2 conventional kernel, along with l decoding NNs on a decoding tree for every kernel.

III. ECC IN FUTURE RAN
With the anticipation of future networks, many architectures are being investigated [23], with many works focusing on accelerating the RAN functionalities and lowering user latency [24]. Here, we survey ECC in promising candidates of future radio access network (RAN) architectures.
Virtualized Radio Access Network (vRAN) and Open Radio Access Network (O-RAN) are gaining popularity as next generation architectures. Compared to traditional RAN structures where the RAN functionalities were tightly integrated into the HW, such architectures provide flexibility and scalability by decoupling the HW-dependent RAN functions from proprietary equipment. Instead, the new RAN architectures run the RAN functions as a software package on commercial off-theshelf (COTS) HW. More specifically, vRAN aims to fully virtualize the layer 1 and layer 2 functionalities of RAN, avoiding

Fig. 2 depicts the structure of the O-RAN architecture. ORAN is built on the vRAN architecture, with standardized interfaces and a disaggregated architecture, allowing multivendor interoperability. The acceleration abstraction layer (AAL) of O-RAN provides the application vendors the flexibility to integrate their applications with any type of accelerator HW [26]. O-RU (Open Radio Unit) takes charge of the low-PHY operations, whereas O-DU (Open Distributed Unit) executes the high-PHY functions, hence processing ECC functionalities. O-DU can be easily upgraded with newer modules without changing the HW of the unit.
AI-RAN is also being actively discussed [27], which integrates AI into the RAN infrastructure. In vRAN, one of the most compute-intensive tasks is the ECC of layer 1 [28]. More specifically, optimizing ECC is crucial when finding the most efficient performance of wireless communication systems in terms of throughput, retransmission, and cell capacity [28]. Integrating AI, which is computationally intensive in general, into latency-sensitive communication system is a challenging task. However, O-DU consists of a large computational capacity, sufficient to process the compute-intensive executions of AI-based ECC acceleration. Therefore, under the active development of AI-RAN and O-RAN, AI-based ECC acceleration is expected to play a major role in future RAN environments.
IV. CONCLUSION
This survey has explored diverse approaches for ECC optimization. We have analyzed some major optimization methods including algorithmic approach, HW-based approach, and AIbased approach. Future application of ECC acceleration in future RAN structure has also been investigated.
Future ECC acceleration research has a challenging goal of balancing performance, adaptability, and efficiency. While AI-based ECC optimization is a prominent solution for RAN acceleration, it faces challenges in developing a flexible, explainable solution. With the integration of AI and open communication architectures, bigger challenges and opportunities are imminent. Until these challenges are resolved, AI-based ECC optimization will stand as a prominent research area.
