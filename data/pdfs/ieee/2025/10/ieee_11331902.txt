Abstract: Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes have been 
extensively applied in wireless communications and data storage 
systems. However, their hardware encoder implementations often 
suffer from excessive memory consumption, limited parallelism, 
and high power dissipation. To address these issues, this paper 
presents an FPGA-oriented LDPC encoder architecture based on 
a hybrid sparse matrix. The proposed method decomposes the 
generator matrix G into a strictly sparse matrix and a small 
residual matrix, with the latter approximated through low-rank or 
sparse small-core techniques. A reconfigurable small-core 
computing module is introduced to efficiently process the residual 
part, minimizing redundant computations. Experimental results 
demonstrate that the proposed design reduces LUT and DSP 
utilization by approximately 20%, increases the operating 
frequency by 25%, and lowers power consumption by about 20%. 
Moreover, under the same signal-to-noise ratio, the bit error rate 
exhibits faster convergence compared to conventional encoder 
architectures. 
Keywords— LDPC coding ;Reconfigurable mapping; FPGA; 
Sparse matrix; Address generation. 
I. INTRODUCTION  
With the development of 5G/6G mobile communications, 
satellite links, and high-capacity storage systems, higher 
requirements are imposed on the error-correction capability and 
real-time processing performance of channel coding. Due to 
their sparse parity-check matrices and near-Shannon-limit 
error-correction performance, LDPC codes have become a core 
component of modern communication standards such as DVB-
S2 and 5G NR. However, compared with the extensive research 
on parallelization and architectural optimization on the 
decoding side, the hardware implementation of LDPC encoders 
also faces significant challenges in high-rate and long-code-
length 
scenarios: 
conventional 
direct 
multiply-and-add 
implementations based on the full generator matrix G require 
substantial storage and arithmetic resources; even exploiting the 
cyclic properties of QC-LDPC codes, the presence of residual 
matrices and irregular structures still leads to increased resource 
utilization and latency .In recent years, some works have 
attempted to reduce hardware complexity by sparsifying the 
generator matrix or introducing partially parallel architectures, 
but several limitations remain: High storage pressure: Directly 
storing the full generator matrix or large-scale lookup tables 
requires considerable BRAM resources; Conflict between 
parallelism and flexibility: Highly parallel architectures 
consume excessive resources, while low-parallel designs limit 
throughput; Complex handling of irregular residuals: Cyclic 
shift structures cannot cover all matrix elements efficiently. 
To address these issues, this paper proposes an LDPC 
encoder architecture based on a hybrid sparse matrix. The core 
idea is to decompose the generator matrix into a strictly sparse 
part and a small residual part according to their significance. 
The strictly sparse part is efficiently implemented using block-
level parallel shift/XOR networks, while the residual part is 
processed using low-rank or sparse small-core approximations 
with a reconfigurable small-core unit and low-precision or time-
multiplexed 
accumulators. 
Furthermore, 
a 
unified, 
parameterized parity-bit address calculation core is designed to 
support flexible configurations for multiple code lengths and 
code rates.

II. DERIVATION OF THE GENERATOR MATRIX PRINCIPLE  
Directly constructing a systematic LDPC code on FPGA 
may encounter two main issues: Loss of sparsity: Row and 
column transformations often densify the matrix, which is 
unfavorable for hardware implementation. High storage 
overhead: Directly storing the entire LUT consumes significant 
hardware resources. In this paper, we implement the encoder in 
hardware by preserving the original sparse structure and solving 
the corresponding linear equations [1]. 
Let parity-check matrix of an LDPC code be H ∈
{0,1}m×n ,where n is the code length, m is the number of 
information bits, and the code rate is R = k/n, satisfying  m =
n −k . The sparsity of H means that most elements are zero, 
with only a few non-zero entries (“1”) scattered throughout. We 
adopt a systematic codeword representation: let the information 
vector be u ∈{0,1}k, and the parity (check) bits be 𝑝𝑝∈{0,1}m, 
so that the final codeword is 𝑐𝑐= [𝑢𝑢 𝑝𝑝] ∈{0,1}n, satisfying the 
parity-check equations defined by H. During decoding, these 
equations are also used to verify received codewords and 
perform iterative decoding [2]. 
The generator matrix G is a k×n matrix (where k is the 
length of the information bits) used for encoding. An 
information bit sequence d of length k is linearly transformed to 
produce a codeword c of length n via modulo-2 operations c = 
d×G(mod2).LDPC codes are usually in systematic form, where 
the codeword c consists of the original information bits d 
concatenated with the computed parity bits p, c=[d p]. 
Correspondingly, the generator matrix can be written in 
systematic form:G = [I
P]where  I is a k times identity matrix 
and  P is a k×m matrix[3].

closely related and satisfy the orthogonality condition   H× Gᵀ

which may destroy the original sparsity of H and negatively

encoding—or carefully designed structures (quasi-bidiagonal 
structure) are often used to avoid direct encoding with a dense

III. OVERALL ENCODING PROCESS AND HARDWARE 
ARCHITECTURE DESIGN 
A. Residual Small-Kernel Approximation Processing 
In the LDPC encoder based on the hybrid sparse matrix, the 
generator matrix G is decomposed into a strictly sparse part and 
a small residual part. The former can be efficiently implemented 
directly through sparse matrix multiplication, while the latter, 
due to its small scale and low sparsity, would introduce large 
resource and delay overheads if directly computed using 
conventional hardware. To this end, this paper proposes a 
reconfigurable residual small-kernel approximation   
lengths and matrix modes, or reusing limited arithmetic 
resources through time-division multiplexing. Corresponding 
multiplication-addition templates or coefficient tables are pre-
generated for different residual matrix blocks and stored in on-
chip BRAM; when the FPGA needs to process a certain residual 
block, the corresponding table entries are loaded from the 
BRAM to achieve rapid switching.

summation of the residual part adopts low-precision 
approximate accumulation technology. Since the coefficient 
magnitude of the residual block is small, its impact on the 
encoding result is limited, allowing the following strategies to 
simplify the hardware in the FPGA:

3) Hardware Architecture Design 
This work proposes a hybrid sparse matrix–based LDPC 
encoder architecture optimized for FPGA implementation. The 
proposed design aims to maintain structural sparsity and reduce 
redundant 
memory 
usage 
by 
combining 
low-rank 
decomposition and reconfigurable small-core approximation 
for residual matrix processing. 
a) Generator Matrix Decomposition 
Let the generator matrix G of the LDPC code be partitioned 
as:G = [I_k | P] where I_k is the k × k identity matrix and P is a 
parity submatrix derived from the parity-check matrix H. To 
improve hardware efficiency, P is decomposed into a low-rank 
part P_L and a sparse residual part P_S:P = P_L + P_S where 
P_L captures dense, repetitive structures suitable for block-
based operations, and P_S maintains the original sparsity to 
minimize logic utilization. 
Hybrid Sparse Matrix Encoding 
Input: Information bits u [0:K-1], matrices P_L, P_S 
Output: Codeword c [0:N-1] 
1: for i = 0 to K-1 do 
2:     parity_L[i] = sum_over_j (u[j] * P_L[j][i]) 
3:     parity_S[i] = sum_over_j (u[j] * P_S[j][i]) 
4: end for 
5: parity = (parity_L + parity_S) mod 2 
6: c = concatenate(u, parity) 
b)  Reconfigurable Small-Core Design 
To handle different LDPC configurations dynamically, a 
reconfigurable small-core module is introduced. Each small-
core performs modular accumulation for a configurable subset 
of the parity matrix. Configuration parameters are stored in a 
lookup table (LUT) and can be updated at runtime. 
Pseudo-code 2: Reconfigurable Small-Core Operation 
Input: Partial matrix block B(q), configuration cfg 
Output: Parity segment y(q) 
1: Load parameters (shift_index, enable_mask) from cfg 
2: for each active bit b in enable_mask do 
3:     temp = (input_bit << shift_index) mod N 
4:     y(q) ^= temp 
5: end for

IV. TESTING AND VERIFICATION 
To verify the logical correctness and timing feasibility of the 
designed LDPC encoder, this paper conducts RTL-level 
functional verification in the vivado simulation environment. 
The input signal uses a random information bit stream, and the 
coding matrix is a sparse parity-check matrix constructed based 
on the DVB-S2 standard. The key control signals of the 
encoding module include: data_run_born_en (encoding enable), 
data_in_born_* (input data block), data_out_born_* (encoded 
output data block), and synchronization signals sync_in and 
sync_out. The figure shows the waveform simulation of the data 
flow and address mapping of the LDPC encoder on the FPGA, 
which outputs 12 channels of 14-bit encoded data and addresses 
in parallel, corresponding to different code rates. 
As shown in the Figure 2, when the data_run_born_en 
signal is valid, the input information block data_in_born_x is 
sequentially loaded into the encoder in consecutive clock cycles. 
After sparse matrix multiplication and reconfigurable logic 
mapping,the output terminal data_out_born_x generates 
corresponding encoding result after a fixed delay. Each output 
signal in the waveform remains stable during the valid period 
without glitches or misalignment, indicating that the logical 
structure is correct and the timing constraints are satisfied. At 
the same time, it can be observed that the timing intervals 
between different input blocks remain consistent, and the 
encoding results show obvious regular changes between 
different data frames, which conforms to the linear 
characteristics of LDPC encoding. The output data delay is 
consistent with the simulation expectation, verifying the 
feasibility and efficiency of the proposed "hybrid sparse matrix 
+ reconfigurable mapping structure" on FPGA. Through 
waveform analysis, it can be seen that the encoder can correctly 
encode continuously input data in a single-cycle pipeline 
structure; the output parity bits respond linearly with changes in 
input bits, which is consistent with the theoretical formula 
derivation; the overall timing of the module is synchronized 
with the simulation clock, with no logic hazards or race risks. 
To verify the reconfigurable performance of the LDPC encoder 
designed in this paper under multiple code rates, the system 
implements multiple sets of LDPC code rates and 
corresponding parameter configurations on the FPGA.

V. CONCLUSION 
Aiming at the problems of high computational complexity 
and high resource occupation in the FPGA implementation of 
traditional LDPC encoders, this paper proposes an efficient 
LDPC encoder based on a hybrid sparse matrix and 
reconfigurable mapping structure. By sparsifying the matrix 
structure and adopting a block-level parallel design, the 
encoding complexity is significantly reduced, and flexible 
switching of multiple code rates is realized.The synthesis results 
show that the proposed design requires only 2108 LUTs, 1219 
flip-flops, and 7 BRAMs, corresponding to utilization rates of 
0.96%, 0.28%, and 1.28%, respectively. In contrast, the 
reference design consumes 3665 LUTs, 2630 flip-flops, and 4.5 
BRAMs, resulting in utilization rates of 2.72%, 0.98%, and 
1.23%. The I/O utilization is also reduced from 14.74% in the 
reference to 12.00% in the proposed architecture. This 
demonstrates that the hybrid sparse-matrix construction and 
reconfigurable mapping strategy effectively improve hardware 
efficiency without compromising functional performance. In 
terms of bit error performance, the proposed scheme maintains 
better Bit Error Rate (BER) performance than the traditional 50-
time full-precision iteration scheme even under the conditions 
of only 10 iterations and 1-bit Log-Likelihood Ratio (LLR) 
approximation. At an Eb/N0 of 4.5 dB, the BER is about 1×10⁻², 
representing a performance improvement of approximately 2.7 
times. The results indicate that this structure significantly 
reduces hardware complexity while ensuring high throughput, 
and has excellent reconfigurability and scalability. In 
conclusion, the LDPC encoder proposed in this paper 
outperforms 
traditional 
implementations 
in 
terms 
of 
performance, efficiency, and flexibility, providing an 
engineering-practical and efficient encoding solution for high-
throughput FPGA communication systems (such as satellite 
communications and 5G NR physical layers).
