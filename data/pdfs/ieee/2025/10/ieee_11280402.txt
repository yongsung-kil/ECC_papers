BER Optimization in Low-SNR Regimes using LDPC Codes

Aarush Agarwal School of Electronics Engineering (SENSE)
Vellore Institute of Technology Vellore, India
aarush.ag02@gmail.com

Anand Mohandas 6G Innovation Lab Centre for Development of Telematics
Bangalore,India anandm@cdot.in

Abstract—In this study, we address the challenge of optimizing Bit Error Rate (BER) performance in lowSNR scenarios for 6G wireless communication systems. Initially, we establish a confusion area around the received BPSK symbols by defining the decision using the standard deviation of the AWGN noise. This probabilistic thresholding allows more accurate classifications of potentially incorrect bits. We suggest a novel Hybrid Bit-Flipping LDPC decoding technique that dynamically combines both hard and soft decision strategies to improve the decoding process further. Our approach dramatically lowers BER, particularly in very noisy environments, by using the probabilistic confidence of each bit in the confusion region. Lastly, simulation results show that under limited SNR conditions, the suggested hybrid decoder performs better than conventional LDPC decoding schemes in terms of preserving data integrity.
Index Terms—Bit Error Rate (BER), Low-SNR Regimes, LDPC Decoding, Confusion Region, Hybrid Bit-Flipping Algorithm
I. INTRODUCTION
Modern communication systems must operate effectively under stringent energy and spectral constraints due to the growing need for high-speed wireless connectivity. The sixth generation (6G) of wireless networks, which are designed to support extremely reliable and ultralow latency communication in scenarios such as the Internet of Things (IoT), smart grids, and mission-critical applications, require robust performance under low Signal-toNoise Ratio (SNR) conditions [1], [2].
This work proposes a hybrid decoding strategy tailored for LDPC codes in severely noisy environments. The term ’BER Optimization’ refers to a targeted reduction of decoding errors rather than a generic performance boost. Low-density parity check (LDPC) codes, first introduced by Gallager in 1962 [3], have become a cornerstone of modern error correction, offering nearcapacity performance and linear-time decoding capabilities. Wireless standards, including 5G NR, have incorporated LDPC codes because of their exceptional efficiency. Standard LDPC decoders perform less effectively in extremely low-SNR regimes, which are found in

deep fading, power-limited sensor networks, or mmWave backhaul [4], [5].
Among LDPC decoding methods, Bit-Flipping (BF) algorithms have gained popularity due to their low complexity and hardware efficiency. In particular, Noisy Gradient Descent Bit-Flipping (NGDBF) has shown improved convergence in noisy environments by perturbing the search space to escape local minima [5]. Further improvements have explored the use of symbol reliability and parity violations as metrics to guide bit flips, including Gradient Bit-Flipping (GBF) techniques that leverage signal gradients to make more informed decisions [9].
However, hybrid decoding approaches have emerged as a compromise, combining the speed of hard-decision decoding with selective soft-reliability metrics. Nonbinary hybrid LDPC codes extend this concept by processing a larger set of symbols to capture more nuanced noise characteristics [7]. Relaxed Min-Sum algorithms reduce decoding complexity by simplifying check node operations without significantly compromising BER [10].
Recent work has also embraced machine learning to optimize LDPC decoding. For example, neural-assisted decoders have shown promise in learning patterns in parity violations and bit reliabilities to adapt decoding paths [11]. Others, like [8], explore genetic algorithms to tailor code structures based on decoder behavior, pointing to more flexible and application-based coding strategies.
To further analyze LDPC performance, several studies have modeled error floors in LDPC decoding over additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels, which are critical to understanding the long-tail behavior of BER under harsh noise environments [13]. In mission critical scenarios, such as multi-user decoding [12] or satellite communications with dynamic channels [14], it becomes essential to maintain a low BER even under unpredictable conditions. Furthermore, studies on modulation schemes in fast-fading and frequency-selective channels such as π/4 D-QPSK, reveal how modulation

Building on these insights, we propose a novel hybrid Bit-Flipping LDPC decoding scheme tailored for lowSNR regimes. Our approach defines a ’confusion region’ centered around the origin, bounded by a multiple of the noise standard deviation ±kσ. Bits received within this region exhibit high uncertainty and are excluded or “frozen” during decoding iterations. The decoder then employs a hybrid metric that balances parity violation counts and soft reliability (absolute value of the received symbol) to compute flip scores. This prioritization of high-error-probability bits outside the confusion region enables the decoder to focus only on more reliable information, significantly reducing BER [6].

II. SYSTEM MODEL

A. Sigma Estimation
The communication system considered in this study follows a basic BPSK transmission framework subjected to Additive White Gaussian Noise (AWGN). The system begins by randomly generating a stream of binary bits, which are subsequently modulated using Binary Phase Shift Keying (BPSK). In BPSK, each bit is mapped to a distinct symbol in the constellation: logical ’0’ is represented by +1, and ’1’ by -1. The equation for BPSK modulation is as follows:

These symbols are transmitted over an additive white Gaussian noise channel (AWGN), where noise with zero mean and variance σ2 is added to each transmitted symbol. The received signal is modeled as:
y = x+n
where x ∈ {+1, −1} is the transmitted symbol and n ∼ N (0, σ2) is Gaussian noise.
Fig. 1 demonstrates an In-phase Quadrature (IQ) diagram for the BPSK signal added with AWGN noise. As noise increases, symbol clusters in the IQ plane begin to overlap, forming a region of intersection. This common region is where bit decision errors are the most likely to occur and is known as the confusion area.
To mathematically define this area, we introduce a probabilistic boundary of ±kσ around the origin (decision threshold). Any received symbol falling within the interval [−kσ, +kσ] is considered part of the confusion area, where the probability of incorrect bit decisions is the highest due to the significant overlap between the two symbol distributions. The region outside this interval is classified as the non-confusion area, where symbols can be classified with high confidence.

Fig. 2. BER Comparison for Confusion and Non-Confusion region
To analyze this further, we separately calculate the Bit Error Rate (BER) for both regions. Fig. 2 reveals that the BER within the confusion area is substantially higher, while the BER in the non-confusion area is nearly zero. This key observation motivates the use of specialized decoding strategies to handle the confusion area more effectively.
To quantify the impact of the confusion area on the overall performance of the system, we simulate the transmission of 105 randomly generated bits. These bits are segmented into 100 windows, each containing 1000 bits, to allow for windowed BER analysis.
We incrementally vary the value of k to adjust the width of the confusion area, defined as the range [−kσ, +kσ] around the decision boundary. At each value of k, the defined confusion area is identified and excluded from demodulation. The remaining bits in the non-confusion area are demodulated and used for BER calculation.

B. Hybrid Bit-Flipping LDPC Decoder
Although conventional LDPC decoders are known for their near-capacity performance, they tend to struggle under extremely low SNR conditions, often failing to reliably correct errors when noise levels are high. To address this limitation, we introduce a Hybrid BitSwitching LDPC decoding strategy specifically designed to enhance performance in such challenging environments. After segmenting each 1000-bit window into two halves of 500 bits each, every segment is independently encoded with an LDPC code featuring 2000 parity bits, thereby introducing strong redundancy to assist in error correction.
Fig.3 demonstrates the stepwise algorithm for the hybrid bit-flipping LDPC decoder, which is a custom decoding algorithm designed to enhance the Bit Error Rate(BER) in low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) regimes by combining the strengths of both hard and soft decision decoding.
In classical hard decision decoding, received signals are thresholded (typically at zero) to make a binary decision for each bit: if the received value |yi| > 0,

Accuracy is enhanced by isolating ambiguous bits (i.e., low-confidence bits falling in ±kσ) and excluding them from decoding. Only confident bits contribute to syndrome correction, improving overall decoding accuracy. To decide which bits to flip, it performs a hybrid scoring strategy. Performance is enhanced by freezing bits in the confusion region ±kσ where the bit reliability is statistically low. This prevents incorrect flips. The number of bits flipped per iteration was empirically selected on the basis of simulation results. We observed that flipping a small number of high-score bits (typically 5–10 per iteration) gave the best performance. Excess flipping caused instability and increased BER. The value of k is implicitly influenced by the distribution of flip scores, ensuring that only the most suspicious bits (high score, low reliability) are chosen dynamically in each iteration.

The simulated BER is calculated by dividing the number of incorrectly received bits by the total number of bits transmitted, as defined by the following expression.
BER = Ne Nt
Where: Ne = Number of bit errors (bits received incorrectly) Nt = Total number of bits transmitted

The shift in the optimal value of k as shown in Fig. 5, with an increasing number of transmitted bits, can be attributed to the formation of larger symbol clusters for both −1 and +1 in the BPSK constellation. As the bit count increases, the density of the received symbols around each ideal constellation point also increases. Consequently, the noise-induced overlap, or the confusion region—between the two clusters becomes more statistically populated and more widely spread. This requires a

The remaining bits are decoded using hard decision decoding for highconfidence bits and a modified bit-flipping strategy for bits closer to the decision boundary. High precision is achieved by restricting flips to bits that are both involved in unsatisfied parity checks and have low confidence. This selective bit flipping reduces false positives and avoids unnecessary modifications, thereby improving decoding precision.

IV. CONCLUSION
Numerous directions are still open for further research, and the proposed hybrid bit-flipping LDPC decoder greatly enhances BER performance in low-SNR regimes. The current implementation assumes an AWGN channel where the ±kσ confusion boundary is symmetric and optimal. Under non-Gaussian noise models (e.g., impulsive or colored noise), this assumption breaks down. Future work will investigate asymmetric or adaptive thresholding based on noise statistics, potentially using robust estimators like MAD (Median Absolute Deviation) to define more accurate confusion regions under skewed or heavy-tailed distributions. The current sigma estimation assumes a quasi-static channel over each decoding window. In dynamic or fast-fading environments, this assumption may not hold. To address this, sigma can be re-estimated at shorter intervals or tracked using channel estimation techniques, enabling dynamic adjustment of the confusion region.
