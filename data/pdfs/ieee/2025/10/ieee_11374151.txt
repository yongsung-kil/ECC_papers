Abstract—In this paper, we propose a decoding metric for the Sum-Product algorithm (SPA) that allows us to combine LDPC codes with spreading codes for underwater acoustic communications and directly use correlator values at the receiver to iteratively decode and retrieve the original LDPC-encoded messages. This variation of the SPA is known as Soft Distance Decoding (SDD). The conventional SPA requires accurate loglikelihood ratios to perform optimally but it is challenging to obtain this from correlator values. We consider M-ary spreading codes combined with several different CCSDS-standard LDPC codes and compare the BER performance of SDD with conventional SPA and also the Min-Sum algorithm (MSA) on a multipath fading channel. We find that the conventional SPA requires the despreading sequence values to be scaled to achieve optimal performance. However, this scaling varies depending on a number of factors such as the type of channel and LDPC code parameters, and therefore determining the optimal scaling is complicated. The Min-Sum algorithm (MSA) and the SDD do not require initial values to be scaled, but we find that SDD always achieves the optimal performance and outperforms the MSA.
Index Terms—Soft distance decoding, spread spectrum, sumproduct algorithm, LDPC codes
I. INTRODUCTION
Spread-spectrum systems are known for their resistance to interference and jamming, making them widely used in military, satellite, and wireless communications [1], [2]. However, they are not just limited to radio frequency signals and also have applications in modern underwater acoustic communication technologies frequently, which utilize spreadspectrum techniques to mitigate against the negative effects of such harsh channels [3]. Examples include: Binary Orthogonal Keying (BOK) using LFM chirps [4], [5]; M-ary Orthogonal Signalling (MOS) [6], [7], Direct Sequence Spread Spectrum (DSSS), and Multi Carrier Spread Spectrum (MCSS) [8]. The performance can be further improved in challenging conditions such as environments with multipath fading or low signal-tonoise ratios by incorporating modern error-correcting codes like Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes. Ding et al combined Low-Density Parity-Check Code with DSSS for iterative signal processing [9] in 2021. In 2023, [10] tackled

the issue of long decoding times for LDPC codes that causes inefficient communication and makes them unsuitable for short-block data transmission. They found that integrating a short-block LDPC code with direct sequence spread spectrum and soft spread spectrum in Underwater Acoustic Communication Systems (UWACS) effectively solved the limitations of traditional LDPC codes in such scenarios.
Although LDPC codes can achieve excellent performance, their effectiveness in M-ary Spread-Spectrum (MSS) systems is limited by the loss of channel information during symbol detection. Traditional decoders rely on accurate noise statistics, such as the sum-product algorithm (SPA) which requires knowledge of the noise variance and assumes the loglikelihood ratios (LLR) of the received symbols are Gaussian distributed. Inaccurate initial LLRs used in SPA decoding can severely degrade the error-correcting capabilities of the LDPC code. Consequently, obtaining accurate LLRs directly from the correlator values of each spreading codeword to feed into the SPA is extremely challenging. However, it is desirable to utilize LDPC codes with MSS spreading codes to take advantage of the excellent error correction, higher data rates and robustness to multipath fading that this combination offers. Motivated by this, we propose a different decoding metric for the SPA that does not require knowledge of channel statistics and can use correlator values directly while achieving an excellent performance. This decoding metric is known as a soft distance and was first proposed for the SPA by Farrell et al [11] resulting in a variation of the SPA called the soft distance decoder (SDD). It was later observed by [12] that this decoder could achieve a better performance than the conventional SPA when the noise was non-Gaussian since the SDD did not require knowledge of the noise statistics.
In this paper, we show how SDD can be employed with a joint LDPC and M-ary spreading coded system by directly taking correlator values at the receiver and iteratively decoding the soft distances to recover the LDPC-encoded messages.

A. M-ary Spread-Spectrum (MSS)
MSS integrates multiple bits of information into each spreading symbol, allowing for more efficient use of the available spectrum. In MSS, the input data is divided into groups of k bits, where M=2k. Each k-bit group represents one of M possible symbols, and each symbol is mapped to a unique spreading sequence. These sequences are designed to have good autocorrelation and cross-correlation properties for effective detection and low interference such as Gold codes and Walsh codes [13]. B. LDPC Codes
LDPC codes are very well-known class of powerful errorcorrecting code and in this paper we have chosen LDPC codes from the Consultative Committee for Space Data Systems (CCSDS) 231.1-O-1 standard [14]. The standard defines LDPC codes for use in space data systems to ensure reliable communication over noisy channels, such as those encountered in deep-space and near-Earth satellite communication. In this paper, we will apply three sizes LDPC code and they are the CCSDS (512,256), (256,128) and (128,64). These codes have been chosen for their short codeword lengths, which make them more suited to underwater acoustic communication.

III. SOFT DISTANCE DECODING
Soft distance decoding is a less commonly implemented decoding algorithm, but it is essentially the SPA with loglikelihood ratios replaced with squared Euclidean distances (or soft distances). This creates a locally determined noise variance from the initial soft distances of the received symbols, unlike the conventional SPA which requires knowledge of the noise variance for accurate initialization. Johnston et al [12] modified [11] by considering the difference of soft distances, called a differential soft distance (DSD), which allows for a single value to represent two soft distances and is analogous to the log-likelihood ratio.

IV. RESULTS
To evaluate the performance we serially concatenate the CCSDS LDPC encoder with an 16-ary spreading code whose values are then mapped to a BPSK constellation and transmitted on both an AWGN channel and multipath fading channel. The simulation result for MSS in AWGN channel is illustrated in Fig. 3. 16 5th-order Gold sequences with a spreading gain of 25 − 1 = 31 are selected as the spreading code bank. The simulations compare the uncoded MSS with the SPA, Min-Sum algorithm (MSA), which is a lower complexity approximation of the SPA, and SDD.
In MSS, the detection rule emphasizes identifying the peak with the maximum energy among the correlated signals. This means that the decoded information is represented as symbols, which are transmitted to the decoder. As a result, some channel information is lost. Hence, we use the output with maximum energy to initialize all decoders and the input to the decoder will only be 1 or -1.

In the simulation of soft distance decoding, the values of 1 or -1 from de-spreading are used to determine the soft distance. For example, if we receive a de-spreading sequence such as 1,1,-1,1, we will calculate the distances to 1 as (1 − 1)2 = 0,(1 − 1)2 = 0,(−1 − 1)2 = 4,(1 − 1)2 = 0 and the distance to 0 as (1 − (−1))2 = 4,(1 − (−1))2 = 4,(−1 − (−1)2 = 0),(1 − (−1))2 = 4. We find that the SDD always achieves the best performance, but this is not the case for SPA and we must scale the initial values to achieve a comparable performance. We have used integers from 2 to 10 to scale the values from the de-spreading signal with CCSDS (512,256) to determine the optimal scaling for this code in Fig. 4.
In Fig. 4, we find that the SPA decoding with the scaled initial values of 2 or 3 have similar results compared to the SDD results. From the scaled initial values of 5 to 10 for the SPA decoding, the performance are far worse than the SDD.
For the multipath channel, similar simulations have been applied with the SDD and SPA.
In Fig. 5, Fig. 6 and Fig. 7, the SDD still works optimally in all three figures, but the SPA still requires different scalings to achieve its best performance.
In summary, the SPA performs poorly using initial values of 1 or -1 from the de-spreading sequence since these are not accurate LLRs, but optimal performance can be achieved if these values are scaled. V. CONCLUSION
This paper has shown that a variation of the SPA, known as soft distance decoding allows us to decode LDPC codes combined with M-ary spreading codes without knowledge of the channel information. It is particularly useful for underwater acoustic communications where the spreading codes mitigate the frequency selectivity of the underwater channel.
The study evaluated the performance of CCSDS-standard LDPC codes using three decoding algorithms: Sum-Product Algorithm (SPA), Min-Sum Algorithm (MSA), and Soft Distance Decoding (SDD). The results demonstrate that in MSS systems, traditional SPA struggles due to the loss of channel information during symbol detection and the inability to use accurate LLRs, while MSA and SDD show improved performance due to neither requiring knowledge of the channel. Notably, SDD outperforms both SPA and MSA on the AWGN

and mutipath channel, making it a robust alternative for MSS. Although the SPA can achieve optimal performance with the scaling of initial de-spreading values, these vary depending on factors such as the codeword length and type of channel and cannot be determined without trial and error.
The study highlights the challenges of decoding LDPC codes combined with MSS due to the absence of accurate channel information, and proposes SDD as an effective solution that does not require this knowledge. It also investigates how initialization values affect the performance of SPA. It found that scaling the initial values can enhance results, but this changes depending on codeword length and also the type of channel. In contrast, SDD consistently achieves nearoptimal performance without the need for such adjustments, making it a promising decoding method for serially concatenated LDPC and MSS codes. The findings underscore the potential of soft distance decoding to not only enhance spread-spectrum communication combined with LDPC codes but also scenarios where the SPA is not suitable due to channel information not being available to obtain accurate LLRs.
