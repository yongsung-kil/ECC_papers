Parallelization Optimization and Performance Analysis of LDPC Decoding Based on Reconfigurable Logic Architecture

1st Yichen Wang
Communication Engineering , College of Engineering Yanbian University Yanji, China
cc20250808888@163.com
Abstract—Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) decoding performance is sought to be ameliorated via a parallelization regime that harnesses reconfigurable logic architectures. A non-uniform node grouping mechanism rooted in the density distribution of the parity-check matrix is proposed to boost parallelism while curbing decoding performance degradation, with a dynamically reconfigurable operation unit array constructed to accommodate diverse code lengths and rates and paired with an adaptive resource scheduling strategy that cuts down resource redundancy. Such a combined approach resolves key constraints of traditional decoding architectures, including insufficient parallelism for high-data-rate scenarios, excessive resource redundancy, and poor adaptability to varying code parameters. Extensive empirical tests demonstrate that a decoding throughput of 3.2 Gbps is achieved at a parallelism degree of 16 (a fourfold increase over serial decoding), alongside an 85.2% resource utilization rate and 51% improved energy efficiency, with bit error rate (BER) performance deterioration restricted to within 0.15 dB—thus validating the scheme’s effectiveness for high-speed communication systems.
Keywords-LDPC decoding; reconfigurable logic architecture; non-uniform node grouping; dynamic resource scheduling; decoding throughput
I. INTRODUCTION
Within the realm of modern information transmittance, the safeguarding of data dependability and operational efficacy remains a core preoccupation for researchers. The proliferation of 5G communication technology, alongside ongoing advancements in 6G research, has imposed increasingly stringent demands upon error-correcting coding methodologies. Owing to their capability to approach the Shannon limit, Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes have been widely deployed across various applications including satellite communications, optical fiber transmission, and mobile networks [1][3][7]. Nevertheless, the computational burden inherent in LDPC decoding remains considerable. Particularly in high-datarate communication environments, real-time processing requirements are frequently not satisfied by conventional serial decoding architectures, thus rendering the development of parallel decoder architectures a crucial pathway toward overcoming existing performance constraints.
Reconfigurable logic architectures, serving as a computational paradigm that integrates flexibility and efficiency, provide innovative insights into the parallelization of LDPC decoding procedures. The essence of such architectures lies in their capability to dynamically reconfigure operational elements and interconnection frameworks, thereby accommodating the decoding

In contrast to the rigid structures typical of Application-Specific Integrated Circuits (ASICs), reconfigurable architectures facilitate compatibility across multi-standard communication systems without compromising computational throughput, substantially reducing both development cycles and associated costs. Current investigations into LDPC decoding parallelization predominantly concentrate on devising parallel scheduling strategies for the updates of check nodes and variable nodes. Early methodologies frequently depended on statically designated uniform partitioning schemes, which, although capable of accelerating decoding, encountered difficulties such as uneven resource allocation and limited adaptability to heterogeneous code structures. In recent years, dynamically adaptive parallel methods founded upon graph coloring theory have gradually gained prominence [5][8]; however, the attendant complexity of their scheduling algorithms often impedes the efficiency of practical implementation.
Capitalizing on the inherent properties of reconfigurable logic architectures, a parallelization scheme for LDPC decoding that synthesizes dynamic resource allocation and optimized node grouping is presented in this paper. Lastly, comprehensive numerical simulations are executed to validate the proposed scheme’s advantages pertaining to bit error performance, throughput, and resource utilization [6][9]. Among the primary innovations of this work is the advancement of a nonuniform grouping strategy, predicated upon the density distribution of the parity-check matrix, to augment parallelism while maintaining bounded decoding performance degradation [6]; another lies in the conception of an adaptive resource scheduling mechanism that dynamically assigns operational units in accordance with group dimensions, thereby diminishing resource redundancy [9]; a third innovation involves the formulation of a quantitative model linking parallelism, bit error rate, and throughput, furnishing a theoretical foundation for the design of practical decoding systems [10].

A. Fundamental Principles of LDPC Codes LDPC codes, originally introduced by Gallager in
1962, are characterized by the use of a sparse parity-check matrix to enforce constraints on codewords. Let H denote a binary parity-check matrix of dimensions M×N where M<N. Here, M corresponds to the number of parity bits and N to the total code length. A valid codeword c=[c0,c1, … ,cN −1] must satisfy the condition HcT=0mod     2. The number of information bits is given by K=N−M, and the code rate is defined as R=K/N.
Sparsity in the parity-check matrix is typically quantified using row weight and column weight, which refer to the number of nonzero entries per row and per column, respectively. In conventional LDPC codes, these weights are significantly smaller than the dimensions of the matrix, a property that facilitates parallel decoding. ROUs typically comprise an array of arithmetic logic units (ALUs), multipliers, and register files, supporting combinatorial execution of operations such as addition, multiplication, and XOR. For LDPC decoding, special emphasis is placed on approximating nonlinear functions (e.g., tanh and tanh-1) using lookup tables (LUTs), balancing computational accuracy and efficiency.
The RIN mediates data transfer between operational units, with its topology significantly influencing communication latency. A hybrid interconnection strategy —employing a 2D mesh for local communication and a crossbar for global routing—is often adopted to align with the message-passing requirements of LDPC decoding, thereby optimizing both flexibility and performance.
The CC generates configuration bitstreams tailored to algorithmic needs, controlling both ROU functionality and RIN connectivity. Configuration words must account for data dependencies to ensure correctness in parallel execution. For dynamically grouped LDPC decoding, the CC must support rapid reconfiguration to accommodate varying group sizes and computation demands.
Performance of the reconfigurable architecture is evaluated via resource utilization rate and configuration overhead. The former is defined as the proportion of actively used operational units, while the latter refers to the clock cycles required per reconfiguration. In LDPC decoding applications, high resource utilization must be maintained while keeping configuration overhead below 5% of the iteration cycle to preserve real-time operation.
C. Theoretical Foundations of Parallel Decoding
The degree of parallelism achievable in LDPC decoding is constrained by the structure of the parity-check matrix. When two nodes—either variable or check nodes — share a connection, their updates exhibit data dependency and cannot be executed concurrently without risking message corruption. Such dependencies are captured using a node dependency graph, where vertices represent nodes and edges denote connections. Thus, the parallelization challenge reduces to identifying the maximum independent set within this graph.
The size of this set determines the theoretical maximum parallelism. For regular LDPC codes with fixed row weight r and column weight c, the maximum parallelism for check node updates is M/r, and for variable node updates N/c. In practice, however, reconfigurable resource limitations often necessitate a block-parallel approach, where independent node subsets are updated in parallel while subsets are processed sequentially. The number of blocks PP is inversely related to the degree of parallelism.
Block parallelism introduces performance degradation, primarily manifested as elevated bit error rate (BER) and

slower convergence. Let I and I′ denote the iteration
counts for serial and block-parallel decoding, respectively. The performance loss factor α=I’/I correlates positively with the number of blocks PP, typically satisfying α≤1+0.1P.

B. Architecture of the Reconfigurable Operational Unit Array
An array composed of P×Q reconfigurable operational units is architected, aligned with the computational characteristics of LDPC decoding. Here, P signifies the count of check node groups, and Q the quantity of operational units per group. Each unit encompasses the following constituent modules:
(1) A Log-Likelihood Ratio Register, responsible for storing LLR values of variable nodes along with intermediate message-passing results.
(2) A Non-Linear Operational Unit, effectuating tanh and tanh-1 functions via piecewise linear approximation, with approximation error constrained within 3%.
(3) An Accumulator, deployed for summation operations during variable node updates, endowed with saturation counting capability to preclude overflow.
(4) A Configuration Interface, which accepts directives from the configuration controller and enables dynamic switching of operational modes.
Inter-unit interconnection is realized through a hierarchical paradigm: operational units within a common group are interlinked via a ring bus to enable intra-group data sharing, whereas inter-group data interchange is effected through a global crossbar switch. The state of this crossbar is modulated in real time via configuration words.
To accommodate LDPC codes of divergent lengths, operational units are designed to support dynamic bitwidth adaptation: 8-bit fixed-point arithmetic is utilized for shorter codes (N≤1024), while 16-bit fixed-point operation is extended for longer codes (N>1024), thus harmonizing computational precision with resource expenditure.
C. Dynamic Resource Scheduling Mechanism
A dynamic resource scheduling algorithm, predicated on priority assessment, is devised to allocate operational units in accordance with group dimensions and computational complexity. The scheduling priority S is defined as:

The scheduling protocol unfolds through the following phases:
1. Initialization: Grouping information is ingested, and the priority S for each group is computed.
2. Allocation: Operational units are assigned in descending order of priority, with the number of units

3. Monitoring: The load status of operational units is tracked in real time, with units being released upon completion of a group’s operations.
4. Rescheduling: Should idle units and unallocated groups coexist, priority is recomputed and resources are reallocated accordingly.
This dynamic scheduling regimen elevates resource utilization beyond 85%, marking an approximate 20percentage-point improvement over static allocation. Concurrently, a pre-configuration mechanism is instituted: as the operations of the current group near completion, the operational units and interconnection pathways requisite for the subsequent group are pre-configured, thereby curtailing configuration overhead to beneath one clock cycle.
D. Refinement of the Parallel Decoding Process
Building upon the aforementioned architectural foundations, the refined parallel decoding procedure is articulated as follows:
1. Preprocessing of the Received Sequence: Initial LLR values are computed and deposited into variable node registers.
2. Configuration Loading: Operational unit functionalities and interconnection network parameters are configured in consonance with grouping information.
3. Check Node Update: Messages are computed in parallel within each check node group, with intermediate results disseminated via the intra-group bus.
4. Variable Node Update: Parallel computation of messages is conducted within variable node groups, with posterior probabilities updated incorporating LLR values.
5. Decision and Verification: Should termination criteria be satisfied, the decoding outcome is output; otherwise, the process reverts to Step 3 for continued iteration.
To attenuate iteration count, an early termination mechanism is incorporated: where the variation in posterior probability across two successive iterations falls beneath a set threshold (conventionally established at 103), iterations are prematurely concluded. This strategy achieves a 30% reduction in iteration number while confining BER performance degradation to within 0.1 dB.

For code length extension (1024→4096), entropy computation granularity is refined from 16-column to 8-column stats; for rate increments (0.5→0.9), greedy grouping’s max size threshold is truncated from 8 to 5. VI. CONCLUSIONS AND PROSPECTS
A parallelization optimization scheme for LDPC decoding, grounded in a reconfigurable logic architecture, is propounded in this research. Through the implementation of non-uniform grouping, a reconfigurable operational unit array, and dynamic resource scheduling, substantial enhancement in decoding throughput and resource utilization rate is achieved, while bit error performance is concurrently preserved. Empirical findings indicate that at a parallelism degree of 16, a throughput of 3.2 Gbps is attained, constituting a fourfold increase relative to serial decoding; meanwhile, resource utilization reaches 85.2%, energy efficiency is elevated by 51%, and BER performance degradation is confined within 0.15 dB, thereby affirming the scheme’s suitability for high-speed communication systems.
The merits of the proposed approach are principally manifested in three facets: firstly, the non-uniform grouping strategy capitalizes on the sparse attributes of the parity-check matrix, thereby striking a balance

between parallelism and performance degradation; secondly, the reconfigurable operational unit array affords adaptability to multiple code lengths, augmenting systemic flexibility; thirdly, the dynamic resource scheduling mechanism augments resource utilization while curbing energy expenditure.
Future investigations may proceed along the following trajectories: initially, the exploration of grouping optimization algorithms underpinned by machine learning, aimed at further mitigating performance loss; subsequently, the examination of adaptive iteration termination mechanisms capable of dynamically modulating iteration counts in response to channel conditions; thirdly, the extension of the scheme to multiuser MIMO systems, so as to bolster parallel decoding proficiency for multi-stream data. Moreover, in addressing the exigent demands for heightened data rates and diminished latency in 6G communications, the integration of quantum computing with reconfigurable architectures may be contemplated, potentially transcending the constraints inherent in conventional computational paradigms.
