ABSTRACT The rapid deployment of Internet-of-Things (IoT) devices for a few years has been impressive, and the progressive deployment of 5G will accelerate things even further. Indeed, this standard opens the door to a new generation of standards aimed at a convergence of networks and communication protocols (WiFi, LTE, 4G etc.). This results in the need for flexible implementations of different families of codes, such as, LPDC, NB-LDPC, turbo codes and polar codes. In this context, the work presented in this article proposes the design of a flexible instruction set processor for an IoT context. The objective is to improve the performance level of low-complexity processor cores through instruction set extensions for Error Correction Code (ECC) decoding. The approach discussed is supported by experimental results obtained based on a RISC-V architecture to which specific instruction sets have been added. The results demonstrate a reduction in the required processing clock cycles up to 44.1% for polar codes, 39.2% for LDPC codes, 21.8% for NB-LDPC codes, and 24.3% for turbo codes (4G LTE) codes with a classical Single Instruction Single Data (SISD) approach. Moreover, Single Instruction Multiple Data (SIMD) parallelization strategy enables execution time savings that are far more impressive. The number of clock cycles required to decode a data bit is reduced by 65.6% to 76.9%, with a limited hardware over-cost from 0.6% to 34% (depending on the error correction code family and the targeted RISC-V core).

II. BACKGROUND The potential to connect billions of heterogeneous devices is a challenge. Channel decoding is one of the most critical parts of a network, because it affects both the Quality of Service (QoS) and radio resource utilization. Thus the energy efficiency of the devices and networks is thus correlated with decoder performance. Theoretical advances in the field of ECC combined with application constraints have led to changes in their use. If we restrict ourselves to wireless communication standards, the most widely used ECC code families are:
• Turbo codes [19] are used in wireless 2G, 3G, and 4G standards [20],
• LDPC codes [21] are employed in Ethernet, WiFi, WiMAX, and 5G standards [1],
• Non-Binary LDPC (NB-LDPC) codes [22] selected in the CCSDS satellite communication standard [23] and Beidou GPS standard,
• Polar codes [24] recently adopted into the 5G standard. To enable real-time receiver development, a large number of studies are required to obtain receivers with high throughput, low energy consumption, or trade-off solutions. Historically, research has focused on efficient hardware implementation of single standard decoding circuits [25], [26], [27], [28], [29]. However, the development of such

architectures is tedious. Moreover, as these ECC decoding algorithms share few similarities, the design of efficient multi-standard decoders is complex or impossible. Consequently, typical multi-standard decoders consist of implementing several dedicated hardware decoders in parallel, which is a relatively fast and simple solution; however, it is a worst-case design approach in terms of area and power consumption.
One solution adopted in the past few years for digital communications systems is Software Defined Radio (SDR) [30], [31], [32], [33]. The feasibility of implementing such systems has recently been demonstrated for non-battery-powered embedded devices, i.e. in base stations with multicore Central Processing Units (CPUs) coupled with Graphical Processing Units (GPUs) or Field-Programmable Gate Array (FPGA) devices [34], [35], [36], [37]. The CPU/GPU platforms offer high levels of genericity and flexibility. These characteristics are achieved to the detriment of other factors such as energy consumption or peak performance when compared to ASIC/FPGA solutions [38]. However, for embedded systems in which energy consumption is not a critical constraint (i.e. cloud-based computing [34], [35] or base station design [36]), this drawback is currently largely counterbalanced by the lack of flexibility and scalability of ASIC/FPGA solutions and their long development cycles.
The design of these systems relies on the optimized software implementations of the algorithms used in the physical layer. To this end, specific parallelization strategies have been developed to exploit CPU/GPU features. Field studies have reported efficient ECC software decoders, in terms of throughput and latency on CPU/GPU targets for turbo codes [8], [11], LDPC codes [9], non-binary LDPC codes [12], and polar codes [7], [10]. Although more flexible than ASICbased systems, processor-based systems have limitations: the energy consumption and thermal dissipation of these architectures significantly exceed the embedded communication system constraints [38]. Part of this inefficiency comes from the lack of efficient support for ECC decoding operations by CPU/GPU ISAs, requiring a set of instructions to realize arithmetic computations that can be easily realized in one clock cycle in hardware.
Application-Specific Instruction set Processors (ASIPs) are a solution to increase processing efficiency while maintaining flexibility. Several academics [39], [40], [41] and industrial research [41] have explored this approach. Based on ASIP architectures, these studies provide the flexibility required for the ECC decoding process [42] and improve energy efficiency. As part of this effort, some studies have proposed the design of processor architectures with ECCoriented ISA. In [40], [41], and [43], the authors focused on the design of efficient programmable architectures for turbo codes, LDPC, and polar codes. In [40], [41], and [43], the authors proposed custom processor architectures for each standard, which are complex and time-consuming tasks. Studies [44] and [45] presented programmable architectures that support several distinct ECC families. These architectural

solutions that design processor ISAs and thus processor architectures from scratch, according to an application domain, are interesting but time-consuming, and require dedicated methodologies [43] and these architectures can not be used to execute other general-purpose applications.
A solution to reduce the development time and increase processor reusability consists of customizing existing processor cores by extending their ISAs to match the targeted application domain. This approach was applied to a custom soft-core processor design [4], and general-purpose CPUs [5], [6]. Typical examples include signal processing [46], [47], cryptography [48], [49], [50], and artificial intelligence [51], [52], [53], [54] domains.
In this study, we propose the use of the latter approach to improve the implementation of ECC decoders in programmable architectures. New hardware instructions dedicated to the ECC domain were identified and derived from an analysis of the commonly applied ECC decoding algorithms. These instruction sets will help improve the performance of software decoders, either (a) automatically by the compiler, which will be able to select these dedicated instructions, or (b) manually through the use of intrinsics by the developer in their software description. The relevance of the work was evaluated by extending the RISC-V ISA and hardware prototyping, as has been done in many previous studies [4], [55].
III. CONSIDERED ECC DECODING ALGORITHMS Error-correcting codes are well-known for their high computational complexity and irregular memory access [56], [57]. To obtain high decoding throughput, reusing ECC parallelization strategies applied in hardware decoders and finely tuned data quantization to fit processor features is essential. Indeed, it has been shown that for most ECC families, an 8-bit fixedpoint number representation [58], [59], [60], [61], [62], [63], [64] is an efficient trade-off between the error correction performance and memory and logic consumption. This data format is clearly adapted for scalar or SIMD decoder software implementation, as demonstrated in [7], [8], [9], [65], and [66].
In this study, the families of ECC used in the main digital communication standards are considered:
• Turbo codes - Turbo codes [19], [28] are found in 2G, 3G, and 4G LTE standards and are candidates for the future 6G [67]. Their decoding algorithm, explained in [8], consists of tracking/routing two mesh networks connected to a data interleaver [8], [11]. The decoding process is iterative, and the execution of the SISO_1 and SISO_2 tasks is often executed sequentially, whereas the exchanged information between them is interleaved (i.e. the and −1 boxes). SISO processing relies on BCJR or the forward-backward algorithm applied to a trellis. The latter relies on the transition probabilities in a particular trellis derived from information encoding rules. Fixed data widths of 8 or 16 bits could be used [28], [68]. The max-log-MAP decoding algorithm, selected for hardware decoder design, is also the most

suitable for software implementation, as highlighted in [11]. The fixed-point arithmetic operations involved are additions, subtractions, multiplications, minimum searching/computations, and maximum. • LDPC codes - Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) codes [21] are commonly found in ground radio frequency (RF) standards such as WiFi, WiMAX, and 5G. LDPC codes are specified owing to their low-density matrix, but they can also be represented using a bipartite Tanner graph. It represents the received information (VN - Variable Node) and parity equations (CN - Check Node) that should be validated. Iterative message passing algorithms between VN and CN elements are applied to decode LDPC codes [69], [70]. These decoding algorithms can be summarized as nests of loops, in which data and information are computed and shared across a permutation network. This network was implemented using bipartite graph topology [66], [71]. Traditionally, computations manipulate 8-bit integer data. In this work, the proposed decoding algorithm is the HorizontalLayered Min-Sum (HL, MS) algorithm [9], [11], which is also selected for high-efficiency hardware decoder design [72]. The arithmetic operations involved are addition, saturated subtractions, first and second minimum searching, masking, and bit-wise operations/selections. • Polar codes - Polar codes are a recent family of ECC [24]. These codes have been integrated into the 5G standard to protect control frames. Polar code decoding is performed using a recursive path over a binary tree [7], [24], [73]. During the tree traversal algorithm, the data accesses are mostly regular, but the computation parallelism decreases while the decoding tree is traversed. The algorithm considered in this study is a SuccessiveCancellation (SC) [7], [43] that can be applied directly for decoding or used in SC-Flip approaches [74]. The logical and arithmetic operations involved in the SC decoding process are mainly additions, saturated subtractions, minimum searching, and masking. • Non-binary LDPC codes - Instead of conventional binary coding, non-binary LDPC codes are transposed to the Galois Field, i.e. GF(n) with n > 2. This extension provides a significant improvement in terms of error correction performance but leads to an increase in computation complexity owing to GF(n) multiplications, divisions, and convolutions. However, their error correction performance level motivated their usage in the CCSDS satellite communication standard [23] and in the Beidou GPS standard. The decoding algorithms are composed of nested loops. It brings regularity in memory accesses by taking advantage of the GF representation except during GF multiplication and division. The proposed decoding algorithm is a Horizontal Layered Min-Sum algorithm [29]. The operations involved include additions, saturated subtractions, minimum searching, and XOR manipulating unsigned data on eight bits.

Since its origin in 2010, studies on RISC-V have generated a lot of emulation in scientific and industrial communities, leading to the development and availability of many RISC-V core architectures. Various architectures have been developed for various purposes and design constraints. Currently, a large set of RISC-V cores is available under open-source licenses for evaluation or experimentation purposes. Each architecture offers different features such as the number of pipeline levels, ALU complexity, availability, or size of memory caches. Moreover, some also provide out-of-order capabilities or computation parallelization features such as Data Level Parallelism (DLP), Thread Level Parallelism (TLP), or Instruction Level Parallelism (ILP). Although each core is theoretically extensible and customizable (to accommodate custom dedicated instructions), their relative architectural complexity and RTL source code quality may hinder this operation.
To evaluate the interest of the proposed ECC instructions, four 32-bit cores were selected from multiple carefully evaluated RISC-V processors: PicoRV32 [15], RISCY [16], IBEX [17], [55], and SCR1 [18]. This selection was based on different architectural characteristics, general performances, and relative hardware complexity. As our objective is to fairly evaluate the proposed ISA extension interest, these cores were benchmarked with the same protocol to measure the performance gains.
The four selected RISC-V architectures support the RV32IMC instruction set (I-Integer, M-Multiplier&Divider, CCompressed) and one to several pipeline stages. The RISCY core also benefits from a dedicated ISA extension named XpulpV2 to improve the execution of signal processing applications. A presentation of the four selected cores and their features is provided below:
• PicoRV32 [15] is a low-complexity core designed mainly for simple application contexts. The hardware implementation of the PicoRV32 core reaches high working frequencies on FPGA targets. Simultaneously, it has low hardware complexity because it has a single in-order pipeline architecture.
• The RISCY [16] core is a well-designed and more capable RISC-V core initially developed by MIT researchers and is currently a part of the PULP project. • The IBEX [17], [55] core was initially known as the micro-RISCY. This RISC-V core has a more balanced architecture providing an interesting trade-off between the performance and hardware complexity. It implements a two-stage in-order pipeline architecture in its basic version (a small configuration).
• Core SCR1 [18] is the latest evaluated core. It exhibits a high MIPS performances for reasonably low hardware

tested cores. The results demonstrate a latency reduction (in clock cycles) of 36.2% in the worst case on the IBEX core, up to 39.2% with PicoRV32. This improvement is achieved by (1) processing kernels that are clearly shortened by custom instructions and (2) the ratio of computations over memory accesses that are very low. The same trend can be observed with the polar code decoding algorithm, with results from 30.9% up to 44.1% latency fall.
It can also be observed that the latency gain for the NB-LDPC decoding algorithm is lower than that of the LDPC and polar codes. The IBEX core provides the best results with a reduction of 21.8% in the required clock cycles, whereas the worst case is observed with PicoRV32, with a reduction of only 17.3%. This observation can be explained by two factors:
• Extracting custom instructions that fit the 3-register format with the NB-LDPC algorithm is far more complex. Hence, only a few can be extracted for the SISD version.
• The number of memory access instructions is equivalent to the number of computation instructions. Consequently, the recurrence of custom instructions during the decoding process is low, leading to a poor reduction in terms of clock cycles.
The turbo code decoding algorithm presents results close to those of the NB-LDPC code in terms of performance improvement. The IBEX core provides the best results with a reduction of 24.3% in required clock cycles. The worst case is observed with the SCR1, with a reduction of only 14.8%. Here, the explanation is similar to the NB-LDPC: the turbo decoding algorithm performs a large set of memory accesses, so even if the instructions from our kit are used, their impact on execution time is limited due to the amount of memory accesses. Moreover, contrary to other evaluated ECCs, arithmetic operations are not saturated, consequently, the traditional ISA is already efficient for a part of the processing in the SISD context.
What can be said from these initial latency results is that the proposed approach always improves core efficiencies. As expected, the RISCY architecture exhibited excellent performances in terms of latency reduction. However, it can be observed that, depending on the error correction codes, these gains are not homogeneous across all the selected RISC-V cores. Indeed, if we analyze the average latency gain, the IBEX core benefits most from the new instructions.
This is one of the reasons for performing experiments on four different RISC-V cores: therefor, the volatility is explained first by their different internal architectures (e.g., the number of pipeline stages). Second, the performance also depends on the compilation tool-chains (e.g., compilers or compiler versions) and compilation scripts that generate quite different binaries.

B. EXPERIMENTS WITH SIMD INSTRUCTIONS Previous experiments focused on SISD implementation; however, the most efficient related work implementations were obtained with SIMD features. Consequently, the second experimental set focused on such architectures. The experimental results are reported in Tables 3 and 4 for execution time reduction and hardware complexity increase, respectively.
As ECC decoders process 8-bit information, the 32-bit width processing element of the RISC-V cores can process up to four data in a single clock cycle to improve the decoding throughput. Inter-frame parallelization has been deeply explored in multicore related works [7], [8], [11], [66].
This is a simple and straightforward approach for executing parallel computations by decoding multiple frames (e.g., 4×) frames at a time. However, to efficiently execute the same decoding computations over different frames, the data should be rearranged in memory at the beginning of the decoding process. To guarantee good system behavior, an opposite operation should be applied at the end of the decoding process. The required interleaving and inverse-interleaving steps are illustrated in Figure 5. As reported in related works, the execution time of interleaving and inverse interleaving stages is negligible compared to the execution time of the overall decoding processes. However, to manage conditional divergences over decoders at runtime, some custom instructions must be added, contrary to the SISD use case.

VI. CONCLUSION Digital embedded systems rely on reliable, flexible, scalable and energy-efficient communication. Software solutions are currently being studied, because they avoid pitfalls in terms of the flexibility and scalability of ASIC/FPGA solutions. However, their overall high-power consumption incurs a severe drawback in matching the energy requirements of the low-energy footprint of embedded systems. To improve their efficiency, an extension of their standard instruction set with carefully designed custom scalar and SIMD instructions is proposed for the main families of modern ECC codes. Results obtained by prototyping on four RISC-V cores demonstrate a huge improvement in terms of throughput up to 5.96× for a LDPC codes, 3.18× for polar codes, 3.84× for NB-LDPC codes and finally 4.5× for turbo codes.
• The experiments reported in the previous section manage inter-frame parallelism at the software level, such as in [7], [8], and [66], to obtain the maximum throughput efficiency. This efficiency is achieved at the cost of a high processing latency. Consequently, the first improvement consists of reducing these parameters by targeting intra-frame parallelization [9], [11], [12], [65], which is slightly more complex and requires code refactoring.
• The flexible nature of RISC-V’s ISA allows the designer to extend the instruction format and encoding beyond the standard format. Benefiting from this availability, one may consider using the empty field of instruction encoding as an extra space for carrying additional information, as seen in many designs such as RISCY’s ALU. In this case, related hardware must be compatible.
• Similarly, from a hardware perspective, the choices made regarding the integration of extensions in the ALU could be called into question. Indeed, other strategies for integrating instructions into the cores could be envisioned: the use of the P instruction format for vector instructions (SIMD) as in [85] and their generic register queue of up to several hundred bits, or the use of the CV-X-IF coupling interface for greater genericity.
