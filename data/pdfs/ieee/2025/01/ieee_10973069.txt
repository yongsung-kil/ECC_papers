Utilizing State Probabilities to Curb Consecutive Errors in Photonic THz Communications
Liga Bai , Lu Zhang , Senior Member, IEEE, Zhidong Lyu , Oskars Ozolins , Senior Member, IEEE, Xiaodan Pang , Senior Member, IEEE, Qun Zhang, and Xianbin Yu , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—The rapid evolution of high-speed communication technologies has brought serious inter-symbol interference (ISI). To cope with this issue, channel equalization techniques, such as feedforward equalization (FFE) and decision feedback equalization (DFE), are typically used to compensate for channel response to facilitate signal recovery, and forward error correction (FEC) code, such as low-density parity check (LDPC) codes, is widely deployed to improve link budget margin. However, the mutual influence between the equalizer and the decoder has yet to be thoroughly explored. In this paper, the state error decoding (SED) algorithm is proposed to enhance the decoding performance by effectively reducing the error probabilities and surpassing the limitations of traditional signal recovery methods. Experimental results in a photonic terahertz wireless communication system achieve error propagation mitigation and additional 0.2 dB link budget improvement by utilizing the proposed SED method, which confirms the effectiveness of the algorithm in reducing error propagation and improving system performance, paving the way for the development of broadband communications.
Index Terms—Decision feedback equalizer (DFE), error propagation, low-density parity check (LDPC), photonics terahertz communication.

II. PRINCIPLE

A. Frequency-Selective Channel
In conventional wireless communications, the inter-symbol interference (ISI) arises primarily from the multi-path effect. However, at the THz band, since the inherently narrow beamwidth of THz waves [29], [30], [31], the impact of multipath propagation is significantly reduced and thus can be neglected. Despite this advantage, ISI in THz systems stems from other factors beyond the wireless transmission link.
One main limiting factor originates from the THz channel itself. According to the free-space path loss theory, higher frequencies experience greater attenuation, worsening the channel environment [32]. As transmission rates increase, the channel response across the signal bandwidth can no longer be considered as flat or uniform. This frequency-dependent attenuation effect significantly narrows the effective bandwidth, limiting the achievable data rates [33]. Additionally, higher frequencies within the THz band are subjected to increased atmospheric molecular absorption which further constrain the usable frequency ranges, particularly by water vapor and oxygen [34], leading to considerable signal degradation and essentially dividing the vast THz spectrum into several gigahertz-wide transmission windows [32]. These intrinsic limitations are not addressable through simple system enhancements. Optimizing performance often necessitates precise control over the operational frequency bands to minimize atmospheric losses and the selection of suitable transmission windows.
Another constraint is resulted from the impairments of optical and electrical devices. Photonic-assisted THz transmission systems introduce further challenges such as chromatic dispersion and nonlinearity, which degrade signal integrity and limit bandwidth efficiency as well. Chromatic dispersion deteriorates the system response by introducing in-band frequency-selective attenuation. This effect is particularly problematic in THz systems due to the high frequencies and broad bandwidths involved, making it difficult to compensate for [35]. Besides, nonlinearity in photonic components, such as Erbium doped fiber amplifiers (EDFAs) and photodetectors (PDs), complicates system modeling and imposes additional constraints on signal fidelity. Furthermore, existing transceiver devices often have limited bandwidth due to cost constraints and incremental system upgrades [36], [37], [38]. As a result, current systems often fall short of THz communication requirements, particularly during the transition to fully THz-capable infrastructure. Recent studies on dispersion and nonlinear effects have explored novel approaches to enhance the performance of THz systems [39], [40]. However,

In an ideal memoryless channel, the channel impulse response (CIR) consists of a single unit-gain tap, with all other taps at zero. However, in practical THz systems, limited power response at higher frequencies causes energy leakage from the unit-gain tap to adjacent taps, effectively creating a multi-path channel, as depicted in Fig. 1. To optimize signal energy and improve the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), the tap with the highest energy—known as the main cursor—defines the main symbol location. Non-zero taps surrounding the main cursor contribute to ISI. Pre-cursor results from overlap with the main cursors of preceding symbols, requiring future equalizer inputs and a feedforward structure with delay. Post-cursor, caused by overlap with subsequent symbols, can be addressed using knowledge of previous inputs. These strategies for mitigating ISI are discussed in the following sections.

B. 3-State Transition Model
In real-world communication systems, the receiver has no knowledge of the transmitted symbols except for the training sequence used in modules like synchronizers and equalizers. Therefore, the reasonable scheme is that the receiver infers the transmitted symbol based on the decisions made.
In Fig. 4(b), we assume a received symbol yi−1 = +0.5, leading to the decision xˆi−1 = +1. The intersection point of the black vertical line and the two PDF curves reflects the probability of obtaining the received signal yi−1 = +0.5 from different transmitted signals. If xi−1 = +1 is transmitted (right curve), luckily no decision error occurs and the next input remains unbiased. On the contrary, if xi−1 = −1 is transmitted (left curve), a decision error ei−1 = −2 occurs, adding a left bias to the next input according to (6). Consequently, the next input is transited into l state. It is significant to note that no probability of transitioning to state r exists for the next input. Actually, only two state transitions are possible in BPSK modulation since each symbol has only one adjacent symbol in the constellations. When higher-order modulations like 4-level pulse amplitude modulation (4-PAM) are employed, the two outermost symbols, −3 and +3, show no possibility to transition into state l and r, respectively.

Another interesting conclusion is that error propagations result in much more l → r and r → l transitions than selftransitions, i.e., l → l and r → r. The explanation of this feature can be inferred from Fig. 4(a). Here, the left-biased PDF curve corresponding to xi−1 = +1 covers more area in the range of x < 0 than state r. Therefore, error propagations tend to alternate between the two states rather than stay in the same state.

We take the number of adders and multipliers used per symbol as an indicator to reflect the complexity of decoding schemes, as they are the most fundamental elements involved in the hardware implementation [44]. To ensure comprehensiveness, other advanced equalization methods such as maximum likelihood sequence estimation (MLSE) [45] and Bahl-Cocke-Jelinek-Raviv (BCJR) [46] have also been taken into consideration.

C. Experimental Setup
In our experimental verification, a THz 1.14 m wireless transmission link is established, as shown in Fig. 13. Firstly, a laser is employed to generate a narrow-linewidth continuous wave centered at ∼1550 nm. The output laser is then fed into a phase modulator (PM) to create a coherent optical frequency comb (OFC) with a frequency spacing of 27.7 GHz, which is determined by a high-power sinusoidal radio frequency (RF) signal also at 27.7 GHz. As shown in Fig. 14(a), we precisely optimize the power of the RF signal to maximize the power of the two 5th-order tones. Then, a programmable wavelength selective switch (WSS) filters out the two coherent optical tones spaced 277 GHz, to serve as the optical local oscillator (LO) and the optical carrier of the transmitted signal, respectively. Next, the optical carrier is amplified by an Erbium-doped fiver amplifier (EDFA) before being launched into an IQ Mach-Zehnder modulator (IQ-MZM) for QPSK baseband modulation. An arbitrary waveform generator (AWG) operating at 64 GSa/s is used to generate the QPSK signal (15 GBaud), which is then fed into the IQ-MZM as well. A subsequent EDFA amplifies the output after coupling the optical LO and the modulated signal by a 3 dB optical coupler (OC), as shown in Fig. 14(b). Afterwards, a variable optical attenuator (VOA) controls the optical power fed

into the uni-traveling carrier photodiode (UTC-PD), which emits the THz signal centered at 277 GHz into free space. A pair of THz lenses with 200 mm focal lengths collimates the THz beam over a 1.14 m line-of-sight link. A high-sensitivity Schottky diode mixer, which is driven by a 16-fold electrical LO at 277 GHz, is employed to down-convert the THz signal into baseband at the receiver side, which is depicted in Fig. 15(b), where a significant frequency-selective response can be observed compared to the transmitted signal in Fig. 15(a). In the end, the received baseband signal is captured by a real-time digital sampling oscilloscope (DSO) operating at 40 GSa/s and processed by offline DSP.
The DSP routine is listed in Fig. 16. At the transmitter side, the pulse shaping and resampling operation is appended after QPSK modulation to better confine the signal bandwidth and match the sampling rate of the AWG. At the receiver side, due to the impairment brought by system devices, IQ balancing, time recovery and synchronization are performed first. Furthermore, a carrier phase recovery (CPR) module uses the Viterbi-Viterbi algorithm [50] to compensate the phase noise which mainly stems from the differences of the two output ports and the mechanical vibration of voltage drivers in the WSS [51]. The AWGN variance used in DFE and LDPC decoder is estimated before the corresponding steps in the first transmission.

V. CONCLUSION
In this study, we demonstrate the effectiveness of the SED algorithm in mitigating equalizer-induced error propagation within a photonic THz coherent wireless frequency-selective transmission system. Both simulations and experiments confirm that the SED algorithm can significantly reduce error propagation and the occurrence of long consecutive errors, thereby enhancing overall system performance. Simulation analysis highlights the accuracy of the state-tracking module within the SED algorithm, demonstrating its robustness even under severe channel conditions. The study investigates both pre-FEC and post-FEC BER, achieving values as low as 1E-5, facilitated by the transmission of longer signal sequences. Experimental results further validate the algorithm’s potential, showing a 0.2 dB gain at a BER level of 1E-4, reinforcing its powerful performance. This research not only establishes the SED algorithm as a reliable solution for error mitigation, but also underscores its promise for enabling higher data rates and more reliable wireless communication at terahertz frequencies.

APPENDIX B DERIVATION OF TABLE I
According to Section III-C, an N-tap equalizer (including N1 feedforward taps and N2 feedback taps) with M states is required.
The maximum likelihood sequence estimation (MLSE) equalization consists of two steps, which are the branch metric calculation and the path metric update. In the branch metric calculation, each state requires N multiplications and N-1 additions. For the path metric update, each state requires consideration of all possible previous states, which involves M additions. Therefore, there are M×N multipliers and M×(N-1)+M×M = M×(M+N1) adders in total.
The Bahl-Cocke-Jelinek-Raviv (BCJR) algorithm uses forward recursion and backward recursion to equalize the received symbols. Each state requires N multiplications and N-1 additions for both forward and backward calculations. As a result, 2×M×N multipliers and 2×M×(N-1) adders are needed.
The FFE-DFE-MLSE structure in [47] reduces the needed number of MLSE taps to be equal to that of DFE taps. The FFE involves N1 multiplications and N1-1 additions. For the DFE, both the number of multiplications and that of additions equal to N2. The subsequent N2-tap MLSE requires M×N2 multipliers and M×(M+N2-1) adders. Consequently, the FFE-DFE-MLSE scheme involves N1+N2+M×N2 = N+M×N2 multipliers and N1-1+N2+M×(M+N2-1) = N-1+M×(M+N2-1) adders in total.
For our proposed scheme, which encompasses FFE, DFE with the state tracking module and the decoder with state demapping module, the number of states is fixed to 3. In the state tracking phase, (11) requires calculating the product of a 3-element vector (the previous state vector) and a 3 × 3 matrix (the state transition matrix) for both I axis and Q axis, which includes 2 × 9 = 18 multiplications and 2 × 6 = 12 additions. For the state demapping operation, (16) consists of 9 multiplications only. Therefore, α = 12 adders and β = 18+9 = 27 multipliers are required in SED. Considering the FFE and the DFE, N-1+α adders and N+β multipliers are involved in the proposed scheme.
