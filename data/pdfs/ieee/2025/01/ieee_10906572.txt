ABSTRACT 5G new radio supports a variety of new technologies and services, demanding significant improvements in radio access network (RAN) latency, throughput, and flexibility. Disaggregation addresses these challenges by splitting the RAN into network units–central (CU), distributing (DU), and radio (RU), enabling data processing virtualization and implementation on off-the-shelf hardware. However, implementing upper physical layer (PHY) processing on off-the-shelf hardware alone might cause inefficient usage of the server processors. Therefore, acceleration is often needed to offload heavy processing, focusing on low-density parity-check (LDPC) codec as the most compute-intensive task in the PHY. Additionally, LDPC coding is tightly coupled with rate-matching. This paper presents novel hardware architectures of rate-matcher and rate-dematcher, targeting field programmable gate array (FPGA) RAN accelerators. The presented solution’s approach to memory organization allows highly parallel operation with efficient hardware resource usage. The architecture is flexible, enabling a selection of various parallelism levels for instant integration with other PHY components, and achieves a throughput of up to 150 Gbps for ratematching, and up to 35 Gbps for rate-dematching. Both components have been integrated into a Peripheral Component Interconnect Express (PCIe) FPGA acceleration card with an LDPC encoder and decoder. The accelerator performance has been evaluated against OpenAirInterface PHY software. By measuring the acceleration impact on the processor load, it has been shown that with the proposed components, the acceleration efficiency can be increased by an order of magnitude compared to the LDPC-only solution.

INTRODUCTION Fifth generation (5G) new radio (NR) [1] is the latest generation of mobile network targeting enhanced mobile broadband (eMBB) along with novel use cases, such as massive machine-type communication (mMTC) and ultrareliable low-latency communication (uRLLC) [2], [3]. The new standard imposes strict requirements in terms of latency

Deployments supporting such features require high processing power hardware and systems with reliable processing latency. Further evolution of this concept is cloud RAN (C-RAN), where the RAN components are virtualized in the cloud [6], [7]. A 5G split RAN deployment is generally divided into a central unit (CU), distributed unit (DU), and radio unit (RU) [6], [8], [9], [10]. With the possibility of virtualizing both the CU and the DU, current RAN deployments can fully leverage the cloud infrastructure. Since the processing tasks for multiple cells can be aggregated, high-performance accelerators can further increase the energy efficiency and density of such deployments [9].
Several software solutions implement DUs on standard x86 CPUs, such as OpenAirInterface [11], srsRAN [12], or FlexRAN [13]. To increase the energy efficiency and throughput performance of DU implementations, computationally intensive tasks can be offloaded to GPUs [14], [15], FPGA accelerators [9], [16], [17], and specialized processors [18]. Since the low-density parity check (LDPC) codec accounts for most of the computational load in the physical layer processing chain, accelerators offloading the codec offer the highest gain in speed and energy efficiency [9], [19]. Therefore, most of the research on the topic of 5G functionality acceleration focuses on efficient LDPC codec implementations in various technologies [9], [14], [20], [21]. As stated in [9] and [19], the next candidates to offer significant offloading gain are the interleaving and bit selection, and their inverse counterparts, deinterleaving and inverse bit selection. Jointly, interleaving and bit selection form the rate-matching procedure, as defined in [22], while the inverse bit selection and deinterleaving form the ratedematching procedure. The work in this paper focuses on presenting novel rate-matcher and rate-dematcher architectures that specifically target FPGA or ASIC acceleration of DU functionalities.
Current implementations of rate-matchers and ratedematchers [23], [24] feature low parallelism levels and limited scalability. A low parallelism architecture can lead to a small hardware footprint but poses specific challenges in high-performance applications like DU accelerators. Specifically, low-parallelism blocks must be instantiated multiple times in parallel to achieve high throughputs. This would fundamentally increase processing latency, buffering, and logic overhead. It will be shown that blocks with higher processing parallelisms are a more efficient solution, both in terms of resource utilization and in terms of processing latency. Furthermore, the data chain parallelism flexibility can enable optimization of hardware usage efficiency for the specific use case, maximizing the processing throughput for the given constraint of the available FPGA resources, e.g., on cloud infrastructure such as [25].
Previous work that is directly comparable to the architecture presented in this paper includes [23], which covers implementations with processing parallelisms of 6-24 bits per transfer. In [24], an implementation with a fixed parallelism of 4 bits per transfer is presented. Both papers focus on bit address generation, which selects individual bits in memory. Achieving higher parallelism levels with bit addressing

increases the required number of memory access ports, logic complexity, and memory primitive utilization. Such implementations show high logic and memory resource usage for the achieved throughput and a considerably smaller range of supported parallelism levels. This makes them a suboptimal choice for both high-performance DU accelerators and lower throughput edge or embedded applications.
Our previous conference work from [26] introduced the basic concepts of the novel memory word addressing scheme, using the example of the rate-dematcher architecture. This paper further expands this addressing scheme to the downlink side, i.e., rate-matcher and presents a detailed architecture and implementation analysis. The proposed hardware architectures offer a high degree of flexibility in terms of throughput and resource utilization. The rate-matcher and rate-dematcher components have been integrated into a forward error correction (FEC) accelerator together with our LDPC encoder [27] and LDPC decoder [21] soft IP cores. Such an accelerator has been evaluated by comparing its performance with software implementations. Additionally, our previous conference work used the naming convention from [23] or [9], where rate-(de)matching and (de)interleaving represent separate functions. This paper aligns the nomenclature with the standard [22], where the rate-(de)matching represents the concatenated (inverse) bit selection and (de)interleaving operations.
The main contributions presented in this paper are as follows:
1) definition of novel rate-matcher and rate-dematcher architectures optimized for DU accelerators featuring low latency, scalable throughput, and high hardware usage efficiency (HUE);
2) flexibility of the developed components to provide various parallelism levels at compile time and hence enabling an optimal selection for the target use case; This flexibility allows the use both in low-throughput embedded applications and in high-throughput acceleration scenarios
3) the integration of developed components into the 5G NR FEC accelerator;
4) by an order of magnitude increased acceleration efficiency when compared to the currently presented accelerators in the scientific literature.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows: 5G NR transport block procedures relevant to the presented architectures are described in section II. The theoretical background of the novel addressing scheme that enables efficient implementations of the rate-matcher and ratedematcher is provided in section III. Hardware architecture and implementation details of the components are presented in IV. Results and discussion are presented in section V. Hardware usage efficiency, maximum operating frequency, and achievable processing throughput are evaluated for multiple parallelisms. An analysis of performance gains in acceleration scenarios is provided. In the end, the conclusion is given in section VI.

II. 5G NR TRANSPORT BLOCK PROCEDURES User data in 5G NR is transferred over a physical downlink shared channel (PDSCH) and a physical uplink shared channel (PUSCH), with data being divided into data units called transport blocks [22]. Transport block (TB) procedures for shared channels at the RAN side are presented graphically in Fig. 1 [22].
At downlink, a cyclic redundancy check (CRC) checksum is calculated and attached to the entire transport block. The stream of bits is then segmented into code blocks for LDPC encoding. A code block CRC checksum is calculated and attached to each information code block. Before LDPC encoding, filler bits are added to match the code block information sequence length with the available LDPC code. After the LDPC encoding, a systematic puncturing is performed. The puncturing is the removal of a number of first bits in the codeword, which correspond to high-weight
VOLUME 13, 2025

columns in the LDPC code’s parity check matrix (PCM). The encoded codeword is placed in a circular buffer which is used as the bit selection input. The bit selection step can systematically remove or repeat codeword bits, which matches the transport block size to the available resources allocated for transmission. Some encoded bits are repeated if the target code rate is very low. As defined in the standard [22], the rate-matching also includes bit interleaving. Bit interleaving step increases the SNR performance of the LDPC decoder by spreading the neighboring bits into separate quadrature amplitude modulation (QAM) symbols, thus reducing the effect of burst errors. Values from the bit interleaver are concatenated into a transport block to obtain a bit stream, which is later mapped, modulated, and transmitted.
On the uplink side, an almost symmetrical processing chain is used. The main difference is that the procedures are mainly performed on logarithm likelihood ratio (LLR) values instead of bits. The LLRs are received from the QAM demapper and represent the soft decisions whether the received bit is either ‘‘0’’ or ‘‘1’’. The LLR magnitude indicates the reliability with which the bit was received, whereas its sign represents the assumed bit value. LDPC implementations utilize these properties to achieve significantly better decoding performance when compared to hard decision decoding methods [28]. In the NR processing chain, the LLR stream is split (deconcatenated) into code blocks, which are then passed to the rate-dematching. The rate-dematching performs deinterleaving and the inverse bit selection, including the the LLR combining in case of low code rate repetition. Rowcolumn deinterleaving of LLRs produces the proper LLR sequence. If the bit selection on the transmit (user equipment - UE) side repeated some bits from the circular buffer, the corresponding LLRs on the receiver (g NodeB - gNB) are soft-combined in the inverse bit selection procedure. The obtained LLR stream is passed to the LDPC decoder, which outputs a stream of bits corresponding to the transport block segments. A CRC check of each code block and the entire transport block is performed.
A hybrid automatic repeat request (HARQ) retransmission is requested if an error is detected during the CRC check. A different set of bits is read out from the bit selection buffer [29] in the retransmission. In addition to the soft combining due to the low code rate, the receiver must support the soft-combining of the new set of LLRs with the previously received values to increase the effective SNR of the received sequence. The receiver must store LLRs received in previous transmissions in a HARQ buffer memory. Implemented components fully support this scheme.
The following subsections describe all rate-(de)matchingrelated TB procedures in more detail.

is written to the circular buffer. Bit selection enables fine adjustment of the code rate and direct control over the transmission sequence length [30]. The redundancy version index (RVid ) obtained from the medium access control (MAC) layer dictates which bits are selected for transmission. Each RVid index corresponds to a read start location in the circular buffer [22] and is unique for each HARQ (re)transmission.
LDPC codes in 5G are quasi-cyclic (QC). Their parity check matrix (PCM) H consists of circularly shifted identity submatrices (circulants) and zero submatrices. The parity check matrices are derived from one of the two base graph matrices (BG1 or BG2) by lifting them to a QC LDPC code PCM. The size of a circulant is called lift factor Z . Therefore, sequence lengths related to the 5G NR LDPC codec are calculated as multiples of Z . The read start location from the circular buffer during bit selection is also a multiple of Z . B. INVERSE BIT SELECTION OVERVIEW In 5G NR, the inverse of the bit selection operation is performed such that the stream of LLRs from the deinterleaver is reordered and recombined for LDPC decoding. This operation is symmetrical to the bit selection operation but processes LLR values instead of bits. LLRs have higher resource usage requirements, as they are stored in multiple bits. Typically, LLR values are stored in fixed-point format with bit widths ranging from 6 to 8 [21], [31].
Due to the nature of the bit selection mechanism on the UE side, there is a possibility that some bits have been transmitted multiple times. Corresponding LLRs on the gNB side must then be recombined into a single LLR to be sent to the decoder. This paper uses the bit-level Chace combining (BLCC) algorithm [32] for recombining. The component architecture is designed to be flexible, such that other soft-combining algorithms can be used as long as their processing pipeline is up to several clock cycles long. The BLCC algorithm calculates the LLR value by summing all the received demapper outputs corresponding to retransmitted bits. In fixed-point implementations, this summation must be performed with a saturation step to preserve the LLR sign and the appropriate dynamic range that is expected by the LDPC decoder.
Practically, the inverse bit selection procedure is executed by writing the LLRs starting from location k0mod to the circular buffer. If a particular buffer location is written to multiple times, the LLR values are soft-combined by summation. To simplify the logic, it is possible to initialize the entire circular buffer memory to 0 and always perform saturated additions. This value also facilitates the LDPC decoding process, as the parts of the sequence that are not received should be initialized to 0. LLRs equal to 0 represent a special case where both bit values are equally likely. The high-level overview of inverse bit selection is shown in Fig. 3.

III. EFFICIENT ON-THE-FLY RATE-MATCHING AND RATE-DEMATCHING ARCHITECTURES This section represents the theoretical foundation of the efficient hardware implementations of rate-matcher and ratedematcher blocks. To gain buffering and logic utilization efficiency, the functionality of the consecutive components is merged into a single monolithic block. This enables an addressing and shuffling scheme that operates on a single large buffer. The proposed novel approach also allows a processing throughput that does not depend on the selected modulation order Qm. The suggested method enables the design of scalable components that can process bits/LLRs in parallel with configurable parallelism levels. The parallelism level of the components will be denoted with W . This value specifies the number of bits/LLRs processed in a single transfer. It determines the maximum achievable throughput of the component, total resource usage, and resource usage efficiency.

This paper proposes reads of multiple bits in a single memory access cycle to obtain higher memory access efficiency. This is possible as columns are read out sequentially. As the window write and read rates are matched, the next window is available simultaneously as the current window is emptied. Physically, the window contents are stored in flip-flops, and the shuffling is executed by wiring the appropriate window locations to the output interface. Windows are stored in a double buffer. The window buffer will be called the shift out buffer, and the main memory that contains the codeword corresponds to the circular buffer. Hardware implementation of the component for ratematching is based on the architecture described in III-A. It facilitates operation in the accelerator environment, with features including code block by code block reconfiguration, low latency, and high memory utilization efficiency. Flexible throughput and utilization tuning are achieved by configuring the parallelism of the component W . An AXI4-Stream toplevel interface is used for data, and a separate port with a bit field handles configuration parameters. The configuration is evaluated on the first stream beat of the input codeword.
The design is subdivided into functional blocks, following the functional diagram from Fig. 5:
• Address generator - used for generating the read pointer values
• Circular buffer - handles data storage and write pointer generation. Two of them are instantiated to handle double buffering
• Shift aligner - aligns the bits to the exact pointer location for writing to the output value generating buffer
• Shift out buffer - reshuffles groups of Qm × W based on the Qm in a wired manner such that they are output in the proper order
Top-level control handles handshaking between components and input parameter synchronization. A custom parameter interface that captures parameters on the first AXI4-Stream stream beat is used. The logic is internally

2) CIRCULAR BUFFER
The circular buffer handles the storage of the encoded codeword for interleaving. Its functionality handles the operations with the MEM structure from Algorithm 1. The circular buffer stores the parts of the codeword marked for transmission, with puncturing and null bit removal already handled. As the data arrives from the encoder component via the AXI4-Stream interface, with a parallelism of W , it is written to consecutive memory addresses. Upon the last transfer, the component performs write finalization and then transitions to read mode. The write finalization extends the last memory write by appending the shifted values from the first transfer to the last valid bit of the last transfer. This enables simple handling of the pointer wrapping when reaching the top of the buffer. This process handles lines 19-22 from Algorithm 1. Externally passed parameters generate the control values required for proper circular repetition and state transitions.
For reads from the circular buffer, addresses are fed from the address generator. The component iterates through the generated row pointers sequentially. The rest of the components down the data processing pipeline (shift-align and shift-out) operate assuming this Round-Robin-like row read order. Since the top-level component works in a continuous streaming mode, the circular buffer is instantiated twice (with one operating in write and the other in read mode). Multiplexing and demultiplexing of control and data streams are handled on the top level.

For soft-combining, the write operation is performed in multiple steps:
1) Memory read from the write address; initializing address delay line
2) Soft combining with the set of W LLRs arriving from the shift aligner
3) Check for collisions in the address delay line. If there is a read from the same address in progress, tag that pipeline stage.
4) Write to the memory with collision check pipeline update
To maximize the operating frequency of the design and reduce the place and route effort, the memory primitives are instantiated with a 2 clock cycle read latency. This somewhat complicates the implementation of the component since there is a possibility of collisions when a relatively small Ncb is configured. A collision is a condition where the memory read has occurred, and a new request is issued for the same memory location with the old data still in the processing pipeline. This would cause the outdated memory contents to enter the processing pipeline, and give an invalid inverse bit selection output if not properly handled.
A collision occurs when there is a need to read from the memory address multiple times in a period shorter than 4 clock cycles for this implementation. In that case, the read pipeline is populated with an outdated value. Because of this, two separate delay lines follow the data flow. The first is populated with the set of memory addresses from which reads occurred. A read collision is identified if another read is issued from a memory address contained in the delay line. In that case, an appropriate stage in the collision identification pipeline is set to ‘‘1’’. When the data with an identified collision arrives at the soft combiner, it is exchanged with the value written to the memory and stored in a separate register.
For sending the data to the output, there is an internal counter for iterating through read memory addresses. When a memory address is read out, it is wiped by writing all ‘‘0’’. This means that the next word can be soft-combined with the emptied contents unconditionally.
This component is used in a double-buffered configuration, with one performing writes and the other performing reads. The top-level state machine takes care of multiplexing/demultiplexing data streams. Internally, the component has a read and a write state.

A. RATE-MATCHER IMPLEMENTATION AND PERFORMANCE
The rate-matcher component was synthesized for parallelism levels W ∈ {8, 16, 32, 64, 128, 256, 512}. The router of the Vivado tool could not complete the routing task due to extremely high routing congestion for W = 1024. If a very low latency is needed, higher parallelisms could offer faster processing, but with the rate-matcher hardware itself being underutilized.

VI. CONCLUSION The design of the 5G NR-compatible rate-matcher and rate-dematcher for use in FPGA-accelerated DUs was successfully implemented and verified. The scalability of the presented design was demonstrated by implementing all possible configurations. Implementation results that would be relevant for selecting the required parameters are given in great detail. This provides a good selection guide for the potential full integration of this design. Configurations ranging from a 1.3 kLUT and 4.3 Gbps up to 19 kLUT and 150 Gbps are available for use in the PDSCH datapath. For the PUSCH datapath, there is a selection of configurations ranging from 2.6 kLUT and 4.4 Gbps up to 32 kLUT and 35 Gbps. These values represent the highest reported throughputs as well as the widest available configuration ranges in literature.
The implemented components were tested in silicon, using field-proven 3GPP-compliant software for generating test vectors. The interfaces of the design were made with integration considerations, following industry-standard protocols and facilitating easy reconfiguration for real-world use cases.
To evaluate the performance increase due to code block processing offload, the rate-matcher and rate-dematcher were integrated into a standard-compatible FPGA design. It has been shown that offloading only the LDPC processing block to a dedicated accelerator has significantly lower throughput than offloading the entire code block processing. Furthermore, it has been demonstrated that even the low processing share of the rate-dematching on the uplink side becomes a bottleneck in an accelerated deployment. On the downlink side, it has been shown that rate-matching can take almost half of the transport block processing time in some configurations. This analysis highlighted the need for high-performance and scalable rate-matching and ratedematching processing blocks for hardware acceleration in 5G NR cloud-RAN network deployments.
The work presented in this paper presented a novel architecture of rate-matcher and rate-dematcher required for practical accelerated RAN deployments. It has been successfully demonstrated that significant performance gains can be obtained by offloading more functionalities to a dedicated FPGA or ASIC accelerator block. The presented architecture is designed in such a way that it can be deployed both on high-performance and resource-constrained parts, such as programmable SoCs.

As a future work, further modifications of the architecture to support additional channels in 5G NR, such as in [35], could increase the efficiency of the overall L1 processing acceleration. The introduction of parallelism levels that are not power of 2 could also increase the flexibility and further aid the integration. However, the implementation complexity in this case could also increase, requiring additional tradeoff analysis.
The proposed accelerator could be integrated into other systems or acceleration scenarios. For example, it is interesting to evaluate the acceleration efficiency in low-power resource-constrained deployments such as small cells. On the other hand, the system can be integrated into the inline accelerator for even higher speedups.
