ABSTRACT We propose a hardware architecture for 50G-PON LDPC decoder achieving high throughput and high error correcting capability while maintaining low level of resource utilization and implementation complexity. Our approach employs phased decoding as a key algorithm which effectively balances the competing goals of high throughput and low amount of resource utilization. We also propose a fixed-structured wiring network between variable nodes and check nodes, leveraging the quasi-cyclic property of parity-check matrix to significantly reduce implementation complexity. To further simplify the decoder structure and enhance the throughput, we propose a blockwise column cyclic shift in the paritycheck matrix, along with rules for normalizing and quantizing messages generated in decoder. Our design also incorporates a pipelined structure of computation units. By integrating the proposed design schemes, we draw a sophisticated architecture for high-speed and low-complexity LDPC decoder that achieves the seamless decoding throughput of 49.7664 Gbps, even on a single FPGA board.

Among the latest advances to meet this demand, 50G-PON, short for 50 Gigabits per second PON, is a cutting-edge technology poised to revolutionize fiber-optic broadband networks [2], [3]. 50G-PON operates on the principle of fiber-to-the-premises (FTTP), delivering ultra-fast broadband connectivity directly to end-users’ premises with minimal

Recently, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are chosen as a FEC scheme in modern PON systems, e.g., IEEE802.3 Ethernet [7] and 50G-PON [3], thanks to their superior error correction capability. LDPC codes are known to achieve near-optimal error correction capabilities with low redundancy by using sufficiently long codeword, making them highly efficient in terms of bandwidth utilization [8], [9], [10]. However, encoding and decoding of long LDPC codes are computationally intensive, requiring efficient algorithm and hardware architecture to be employed in a modem for real-time operation with limited resource usages.
LDPC codes can be represented by a bipartite factor graph composed of variable nodes and check nodes connected by edges [11], [12]. LDPC codes are decoded in an iterative manner, where messages updated in variable nodes are delivered to connected check nodes and messages updated in check nodes are delivered to connected variable nodes. Based on this characteristics, the LDPC decoder can be implemented with a parallel structure, in which message updates in all variable nodes are performed at the same time and message updates in all check nodes are conducted simultaneously. Such a parallel structure approach enables high decoding throughput, but at the expense of significant hardware resource utilization. To alleviate the heavy resource demands, LDPC decoders can be designed for sequential operations, allowing computational units to be reused over multiple time steps, albeit with degraded throughput. Therefore, an appropriate level of parallelization needs to be carefully considered to balance throughput and resource utilization. Layered decoding has been proposed as a viable solution for achieving this balance by enabling a group of nodes to update messages simultaneously [13], [14], [15], [16], [17]. QC-LDPC codes [18], [19], [20], whose parity-check matrix is composed of circulant submatrices, are suitable for layered decoding to be applied to. This implementation technique has been successfully applied to LDPC decoders in various wireless communication systems with throughput requirements in the hundreds of Mbps. Scheduling of message passing in QC-LDPC decoder based on layered decoding has been of great concern in many research works to improve throughput and reduce implementation complexity [13], [14], [15], [16], [17]. However, the LDPC decoder for 50G-PON modem necessitates a more sophisticated architecture, as achieving 50 Gbps throughput requires a higher level of parallelization and resource utilization. Many applications, including G-PON modems, needs to operate within limited computational resources, where the implementation on a field-programmable gate array (FPGA) board is an example. Thus, it is required to design LDPC decoder architectures that achieve high throughput while minimizing hardware resource usage. Although LDPC codes have been incorporated into the 50G-PON standard [3] and many efforts have been made to design high-speed LDPC decoders on FPGA platforms [21], [22], [23], [24], no previous work has demonstrated the implementation of an LDPC decoder on a single FPGA board that achieves

an actual 50 Gbps throughput in a seamless manner. For example, the demonstration in [24] attained 90 Gbps but required 75 FPGA boards.
In this paper, we propose a systematic and efficient mechanism for determining the design parameters of LDPC decoders, aiming to achieve an optimal balance among throughput, error correcting capability, and resource utilization. We also propose a sophisticated architecture of hard-decision LDPC decoder designed to deliver high throughput and low bit error rate (BER) while achieving low levels of implementation complexity and resource utilization. The proposed architecture includes phased decoding, low-complexity fixed-structured wiring networks, pipelined structure as well as normalization and quantization rules for messages exchanged during iterative decoding. By effectively leveraging the quasi-cyclic (QC) property of the LDPC code’s parity-check matrix, we design reusable computation units and introduce fixed wiring networks that improve interconnect efficiency. We analyze the performance of the LDPC decoder with our proposed architecture, demonstrating a significant reduction in resource utilization and a notable improvement in throughput compared to conventional designs. Synthesis, implementation, and post-implementation timing simulations confirm that the proposed architecture enables the LDPC decoder to achieve a seamless real-time throughput of 49.7664 Gbps on a single AMD Kintex UltraScale+ XCKU19P FPGA, while utilizing a low amount of computing resources.
As main contributions, • Phased decoding with pipelined structures achieves a
decoding throughput of 49.7664 Gbps while maintaining a low amount of FPGA resource utilization, requiring 429,785 LUTs and 485,147 flip-flops. • Fixed-structured wiring network significantly reduces wiring requirements for the LDPC decoder, utilizing only 12.8% of the wiring needed for conventional approaches. • Normalization and quantization rule for messages improves decoding performance without increasing complexity, providing high error correcting capability. III. LOW-COMPLEXITY ARCHITECTURE FOR HIGH-SPEED LDPC DECODER 50G-PON standard [3] requires the downstream throughput of 49.7664 Gbps while upstream throughput is chosen among 12.4416 Gbps, 24.8832 Gbps and 49.7664 Gbps. We aim to design LDPC decoder architecture achieving 49.7664 Gbps downstream throughput, which is the most challenging scenario in 50G-PON communications. Fig. 2 shows downstream PHY frame in 50G-PON data transmission. As depicted in Fig. 2, the downstream PHY frame is composed of 360 LDPC codewords and is transmitted for 125 µsec to achieve 49.7664 Gbps throughput, where each LDPC codeword is 17280 bit-length. It follows that the decoding process for each 17280-bit codeword must begin every 0.34722 µsec. To enable seamless decoding of continuously received codewords, multiple decoding submodules can be employed in the hardware, with each submodule decoding codewords alternately. Let Z , F and NI denote the number of submodules implemented in the decoder, the clock frequency in Hz and the number of decoding iterations, respectively. Then, in order to achieve the decoding throughput of 49.7664 Gbps using NI iterations at F Hz, each submodule must begin a new decoding iteration every 0.34722 × 10−6 × F × Z /NI cycles. The reduced resource usage of each decoding submodule allows for the integration of more submodules in the decoder hardware, which in turn increases throughput. With more submodules, additional decoding iterations can be performed, improving BER. Thus, our design begins with developing a low-complexity architecture for decoding submodules, followed by selecting proper values for Z and NI to achieve target throughput and BER by using the allowed amount of total resource utilization.
A. PHASED DECODING LDPC decoding submodule consists of variable node units (VNUs) and check node units (CNUs) connected via wiring networks. Each CNU corresponds to a row of H. Neighboring T columns of H are grouped, with a VNU responsible for updating the VCMs along these columns, where T ≥ 1 and

mod(256, T ) = 0. As a result, for a given H with the dimension of 3072 × 17664, 3072 CNUs and T ′ = 17664/T VNUs are defined as depicted in Fig. 3.
VCMs associated with columns located at the same position in each column group are updated simultaneously. In other words, for a given time period t, 0 ≤ t < T , VCMs associated with columns with indices t, t + T , t + 2T , · · · , t +(T ′−1)T are updated concurrently. We refer to such a time period as a phase. CNU stores the minimum and the second minimum absolute values as well as the product of sign bits obtained at the previous phase. Then, in the next phase, CNU compares newly incoming VCMs and stored values, then updates the minimum and the second minimum absolute values. Sign bits of newly incoming VCMs are multiplied to the stored sign-bit-product, by which the product of sign bits is updated. The minimum, the second minimum absolute values as well as the product of sign bits are combined into a compressed CVM (CCVM), where CCVMs updated at the final phase are delivered to VNUs. In VNUs, prior to updating VCMs, the minimum and the second minimum absolute values as well as the product of sign bits of VCMs are recovered from CCVM. A pair of column-wise VCM updates and row-wise CCVM updates composes one phase, and T phases compose one decoding iteration. The aforementioned decoding procedure will be called a phased decoding, where the detailed process is summarized in Algorithm 2 and described by an illustrative example in Fig. 4. In Algorithm 2, we use min1(i) and min2(i) to represent the minimum and the second minimum absolute values of VCM on the i-th row of H. We also use sb(i) = ±1 as the product of sign bits of VCMs on the i-th row of H and define M as an arbitrarily large number. Note that Vi(t) denotes a set of columns j, which are considered during the phase t, having hij = 1 in a given row i of H.
Phased decoding defines the level of parallelization of LDPC decoder and plays a crucial role in balancing throughput, resource utilization, and wiring overhead. The number of phases, T , must be carefully determined to meet the performance requirements of the decoder. When T = 1, the LDPC decoding submodule is implemented in a fully parallel structure, maximizing throughput but requiring significant computational resources. Such a design is often impractical for resource-constrained environments. Layered decoding, widely adopted in practical LDPC decoder hardware [13], [14], [15], offers an efficient alternative by reducing resource utilization through lower levels of parallelization. However, layered decoding relies on sequential operations of decoding units, inherently limiting throughput. As a result, layered decoding is unsuitable for high-speed applications such as 50G-PON LDPC decoder. For example, a previous implementation in [24] achieved a total throughput of 90 Gbps but required 75 FPGA boards, yielding just 1.2 Gbps per FPGA.
To address these limitations, we adopt phased decoding with an appropriately chosen T , which offers a partially parallel structure that balances high throughput and resource efficiency. By grouping columns based on the QC property of

B. LOW-COMPLEXITY WIRING

In LDPC decoder implementation, all pairs of column (or variable node) j and row (or check node) i of H having hij = 1 must be wired to exchange VCM and CVM correctly. Even in phased decoding, pairs of variable node and check node, which need to be connected, vary every phase so the wiring complexity is basically equivalent to that of fully parallel decoding. However, wiring all individual pairs of variable node and check node results in an extremely high level of complexity because 70400 ones exist in H of 50G-PON LDPC codes. Implementing too many wires may result in long wiring paths between basic components so that severe congestion and timing violation are highly probable especially when the decoder is implemented on FPGA. To alleviate such a wiring complexity, we design a fixed wiring network by utilizing the QC property of parity-check matrix and the reusable property of VNU and CNU in phased decoding.
For a general description, we define W × W as the dimension of circulant submatrices of parity-check matrix. Using phased decoding and the fixed wiring network introduced above, we propose an LDPC decoder architecture for each decoding submodule, as shown in Fig. 7. To reduce the number of wires connecting the block interleaver, located in the upper area of Fig. 7, and the VNUs, we in practice calculate channel LLRs directly inside the VNUs based on the received codeword, minimizing external wiring needs.
To further reduce wiring complexity between VNUs and CNUs, we propose blockwise cyclic shifts of columns in H as explained below. The number of VNU inputs is determined by the weight of the columns associated with each VNU. It is clear from Fig. 1 and Fig. 3 that all columns belonging to a given VNU have an identical weight. Thus, 17×256/T VNUs have 6 inputs, 50×256/T VNUs have 3 inputs, 256/T VNUs have 12 inputs and 256/T VNUs have 11 inputs. In order to reduce the implementation complexity of decoder, it would be better to maintain the regularity of the structure of CNU. Note that the maximum number of inputs to CNU i at phase t is equal to the maximum number of ones existing in the intersections of the i-th row and columns of H belonging to phase t. Thus, we define the number of inputs to CNU as the maximum cardinality of Vi(t) considering all i and t. D. PIPELINED STRUCTURE The throughput of decoding submodule can be improved by incorporating pipelined structures for both VNU and CNU. In this subsection, we present the pipelined structure of VNU and CNU and discuss pipelined phased decoding with respect to throughput and resource utilization.
1) VNU STRUCTURE Fig. 10 illustrates an example of a 3-stage pipelined VNU structure, which processes three incoming CCVMs along with their associated codeword bit concurrently. In the first stage, CVMs are recovered from CCVMs while propagating the codeword bit. To recover each CVM, the minimum absolute value in CCVM is compared to VCM obtained in the previous iteration. If the values differ, the CVM’s magnitude is set to the normalized minimum value based on our specified normalization formula in (7). Otherwise, the magnitude is set to the normalized second minimum. The CVM’s sign is computed by XORing the sign from CCVM with the sign of VCM from the previous iteration.
In the second pipeline stage of VNU, the 4-bit quantized LLR is derived from the propagated 1-bit codeword. Three CVMs are then summed with the derived LLR to obtain L(Qj′ ) as described in Algorithm 2. All of three CVMs and L(Qj′ ) are passed through flip-flops to be synchronized and prepared for propagation to the third pipeline stage.
28760

In the third pipeline stage of VNU, individual VCMs are updated by subtracting the corresponding CVMs from L(Qj′ ). During this process, VCMs are represented in two’s complement format for ease of arithmetic operations. After the subtraction, they are converted to sign-and-magnitude format to facilitate CVM updates by CNUs. The converted VCMs are then propagated to CNUs and inserted into a firstin-first-out (FIFO) queue for use in the first pipeline stage of the next iteration. Additionally, the third stage extracts the sign from L(Qj′ ), which eventually corresponds to the decoded codeword bit after the target number of iterations.
2) CNU STRUCTURE In this subsection, we present an example of a 2-stage pipelined CNU structure, as shown in Fig. 11, which processes two incoming VCMs, each represented in sign-andmagnitude format. In the first stage of CNU, the magnitudes of two VCMs are compared, and if necessary, swapped to ensure the larger magnitude is placed in the lower position. Additionally, the signs of two VCMs are XORed to be used in the next stage.
In the second pipeline stage of CNU, a pseudo rank order filter processes two pre-sorted pairs of VCM magnitudes in parallel, selecting the minimum and second minimum values. As shown in Fig. 11, one pair is sourced from the previous stage within the same CNU, while the other pair comes from a neighboring CNU. This configuration is based on the wiring equipped with a blockwise cyclic shift register between VNUs and CNUs, which leverages the QC property of H in our fixed-structure wiring network. Additionally, the sign bit from the previous stage is XORed with the sign bit from the neighboring CNU. The result at the final phase, combining the minimum and the second minimum absolute values as well as the XORed sign bit, forms a CCVM that will be used in the next iteration.

V. CONCLUSION We designed a hardware architecture for a high-speed and low-complexity hard-decision LDPC decoder tailored for 50G-PON modems. As key components of the LDPC decoder, we proposed phased decoding, fixed-structured lowcomplexity wiring networks, pipelined structures for VNU and CNU as well as normalization and quantization rules for LLR messages exchanged during iterative decoding. Additionally, we provided an efficient framework for determining the design parameters of LDPC decoder to achieve a balanced performance across throughput, error correcting capability, and resource utilization.

In phased decoding, each group of columns in parity-check matrix are processed simultaneously in each phase without being segmented into layers, which results in higher throughput than layered decoding. Since groups of columns are defined by considering QC structure of parity-check matrix, VNUs and CNUs can be reused multiple times resulting in low amount of resource utilization. By implementing VNU and CNU in a pipelined structure under phased decoding framework, we can run multiple phases in a staggered manner with one clock cycle offset and thus can improve significantly the decoding throughput. Based on QC property of parity-check matrix, the wiring network between computing units can be implemented in a fixed-structure, which reduces tremendously the wiring complexity of LDPC decoder. Applying blockwise cyclic shift to columns of parity-check matrix offers extra complexity reduction by reducing the number of inputs to each CNU. The proposed mapping rule applies a normalization factor with a rounding condition, which corresponds to NMSA, and then modifies the result additionally. This additional modification offers an implicit effect of NOMSA and results in enhanced BER performance.
The proposed architecture significantly reduces both the computation resources and wiring complexity required to achieve high throughput and high error correcting capability. The proposed LDPC decoder outperforms conventional LDPC decoders in view of throughput while maintaining low resource utilization. Implemented on a single FPGA, our architecture achieves a seamless actual throughput of 49.7664 Gbps, marking a significant advancement compared with previous works.
