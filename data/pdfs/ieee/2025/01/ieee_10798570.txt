Trapping-Set-Assisted Decoding for Short-Length Low-Density Parity-Check Codes
Asif Ali Zamzami , Zhu-En Yu , Yuh-Tser Wu, and Shih-Chun Lin , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—To guarantee that every device in industrial Internet of Things (IoT) is reliable for fast data exchange, ultrareliable and low-latency communication (URLLC) technology from 5G is crucial. To reduce latency, typically, only up to 1000 code blocklength is allowed. Indeed, a block error rate (BLER) of less than 10−5 and latency of less than 1 ms are achievable in 5G URLLC. The short code blocklength feature is also required in satellite IoT. Though low-density parity-check (LDPC) decoding via belief propagation (BP) shows promise for achieving low BLER with long blocklength, its BLER is high in short LDPC codes due to error-prone patterns, with the trapping set (TS) being one of the most significant patterns. To alleviate the damage of TS, which can be identified offline, we propose two decoding algorithms named dynamic TS-assisted reinforced BP (DTS-RBP) and TS-assisted relaxation for neural-network BP (TSR-NNBP), respectively. Compared with previous works, only proposed TS assisted decoders can meet URLLC requirements under 308 code blocklength at a 3 dB signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). Moreover, under 128 code blocklength for satellite IoT, our DTS-RBP decoder provides the best memory-performance tradeoff with approximately the same number of iterations as BP.
Index Terms—5G ultrareliable and low-latency communication (URLLC), Neural-network, short low-density parity-check (LDPC) decoder.

Though enhanced Mobile Broadband (eMBB) in 5G technology can also be applied in industrial IoT [1], its requirement for latency is much longer than URLLC to exchange for a higher data rate [5], [7]. Indeed, the device latency in the industrial IoT ecosystem may only be smaller than 1 ms and dropped to 10 μs [5], [9], [10]. When being integrated to 5G, communication over low Earth orbit satellite constellations (LEO-SatCons) can provide global coverage with high service density [11], [12], [13]. In satellite IoT communications, short low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are particularly useful for enhancing reliability in low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) conditions, as they can ensure robust communication and optimize spectral efficiency [14], [15]. Thus, the very short channel coding from the consultative committee for space data systems (CCSDSs) [16], which only has 128 code blocklength, is essential for the success of the satellite IoT.
The LDPC code is the channel coding recommended by the third generation partnership project (3GPP) for data transmissions in both 5G URLLC and eMBB use cases. Traditionally, the belief propagation (BP) decoder, or the so-called messagepassing algorithm, can have a low block error rate (BLER) even in the low SNR regime when the code blocklength is long [17]. As aforementioned, to satisfy the reliability and latency requirements of the industrial and satellite IoT, we focus on the short blocklength LDPC decoder, where the blocklength (in the number of symbols) can be only a few hundred. Under such a short code blocklength, the variable node decoder (VND) in the BP algorithm tends to exhibit erroneous bit value prediction for the data [18]. It has been observed that even when the SNR increases, the error-prone patterns in the decoding Tanner graph, such as stopping set [19] and trapping set (TS) [20], [21], seriously degrade the error-correcting capability in BP. Thus, for short BP decoders, the error-floor phenomenon exist even in a high SNR regime [22].
In order to improve the BP decoder in short LDPC codes, a deep learning-based approach known as neural-network BP (NNBP), performs weighted BP where the weights are found by stochastic gradient descent [23]. Though significant BLER improvements can be found for simple LDPC codes, there is only a slight improvement over BP for 5G LDPC in the URLLC target regime [24]. A. Error-Prone Patterns in Tanner Graphs of Short LDPC Codes
The short LDPC code, adopted in 5G and recommended by 3GPP [6] for industrial IoT applications, typically has a Tanner graph with error-prone patterns that lead to short cycles, which lower the error-correcting performance [22]. One of the important error-prone patterns is the TS, a subset of the VND set, denoted as T ∈ V. Typically, TS T can be defined according to the degrees, or the number of edges, of adjacent CNDs connecting to T [19], [20], [21], [31]. One example is as follows.

A single LDPC code can contain more than one stopping set; the error-correcting performance of the decoder will be further improved by dynamically selecting the worst stopping set and performing the reinforcement based on the selected one [26].
Specifically, once the original BP fails, the unsatisfied CNDs can be found such that corresponding check equations are not met. According to unsatisfied CNDs, the DSS-RBP decoder will dynamically select the worst stopping set from the prestored stopping sets list in [37] by [26, eq. (5) and (6)]. After selecting the worst stopping set S, the DSS-RBP will update the LLR via (5) by choosing different γi( ) according to whether VND vi ∈ S or not. Basically, for vi ∈ S, the LLR should be more reliable and have larger γi( ) than that for vi ∈ S. The LLR update from CND cj to VND vi at the th iteration will follow the original “product” rule as in (3). The LLR update of VND vi in the marginalization layer of DSS-RBP will be calculated as (8) but also with different γi( ) according to whether VND vi ∈ S. Finally, the decoded codeword can be obtained by converting the output LLRs of the marginalization layer to binary bits with hard decisions.

III. PROPOSED TRAPPING-SET ASSISTED DECODERS
Proposed decoders, namely DTS-RBP and TSR-NNBP, are presented now. Both decoders are based on 1-out TSs reviewed in Definition 2 of Section II-A. By the simulation in Section V, they can significantly reduce the error probabilities compared to all decoders reviewed in Section II for short LDPC codes.
A. Dynamic Trapping-Set Assisted Reinforced Belief Propagation
The key observation for designing the DTS-RBP decoder is that the LLRs of VNDs inside the TS tend to have a relatively low value and cannot be believed. It is well-known that the stopping set or the 0-out TS, used in our previous work [26], may not be so critical compared with 1-out TS in the Gaussian channel [18]. On the other hand, the LLRs for 1-out TS, which typically consists of a small group of VNDs connected to one degree-1 adjacent CND, cannot be trusted since it is hard to determine whether or not the CND has been satisfied [29], [34], [38]. The aforementioned observation drives us to pick the 1-out TS as the target errorprone pattern to be mitigated in proposed decoders.
The ideas of the new DTS-RBP come as follows, as depicted in Fig 2. If the LLRs of VNDs v2 and v4 outside 1-out TS T do not have large absolute values to affect the LLRs of VNDs inside T, then the LLRs of VNDs inside TS may not converge and be close to zeros. Consequently, the wrong belief is circulated within 1-out TS T all the time. IV. DECODER COMPLEXITY COMPARISON
First, the two proposed DTS-RBP and TSR-NNBP decoders have the same run-time complexities O(n) (in big-O notation [40]) and are essentially the same as those of standard BP [17], collaborative BP [25], NNBP [23], R-NNBP [23], and DSS-RBP [26] decoders. Here, the run-time complexity refers to the number of multiplications needed to execute an algorithm which properly reflects the required processing time. Despite the different iteration numbers in each decoding algorithm, all decoding algorithms also tend to have the same average iteration at a higher SNR, of which the error probabilities are lower enough to ensure reliable transmission. For example, all decoders will have almost the same iterations at the higher SNR [26], as shown in the simulation results in Section V-B. The main complexity differences for proposed algorithms are in the required memories and the corresponding performance-memory tradeoffs, as discussed in the following.
A. Memory Storage Requirement

V. SIMULATION RESULTS
In industrial IoT technology, a massive number of devices may be served; thus, it is essential to have high-reliability and short-length block transmission. Our design target follows the 5G URLLC standard [3], [42], where the BLER and the latency must be fewer than 10−5 and less than 1 ms, respectively. In practice, early stopping with CMM is crucial for reducing latency in IoT applications by enabling decoders to converge more rapidly. This approach can also ensure compliance with the LDPC IEEE 802.16e (WiMAX) standard, which typically achieves convergence within 10–20 iterations [43]. The LDPC decoding Tanner graph selected by 3GPP for the 5G URLLC is based on the base graph 2 (BG2) instead of BG1 [6] since under short code blocklength n ≤ 3824, it can still support rate R < 0.67 with high reliability. To be more specific, two main LDPC code construction methods are random [44], [45], [46] and algebraic constructions [47]. Though the random code construction typically performs better than the algebraic code when the code blocklength is long [47], the complexity and error performance may not suit for the short blocklength regime. Algebraic quasi-cyclic LDPC code [47] is then adopted in 5G URLLC, where the mother Tanner graph of the parity-check matrix H is transferred by a smaller BG, which is formed by a circulant permutation matrix or a cyclic and square permutation matrix. Since mother graphs based on BG2 [6] are used for short or medium length code constructions in 5G URLLC [48], in our simulation, the (n, n − m) = (308, 132), (406, 174), and (616, 264) BG2 quasi-cyclic LDPC codes will be used.
In the meanwhile, note that the very short block transmission LDPC code from the CCSDS [16], such as 128 code blocklength transmission using (128, 64) rate R = 0.5 LDPC code, may not exactly satisfy the industrial IoT requirement based on 5G URLLC standard. However, the very short blocklength of the aforementioned space data LDPC code makes it crucial for satellite IoT in low Earth orbit satellite access network (LEO-SAN), where a few bytes of missioncritical information must be sent. Supporting satellite IoT is important in the upcoming sixth-generation (6G) network [49] where the incorporation of space-based and ground-based cellular networks are anticipated [11], [12], [13]; therefore, we use the (128, 64) CCSDS LDPC code in our simulation.

D. Selection of Reinforcement and Relaxation Factor
The physical meaning of the reinforcement function (6) is to control the convergence of the less trustable LLRs inside the error-prone pattern by utilizing LLRs of previous iterations. In [35], it is observed that if γi,0 is small and γi,1 < 1, the LLRs dynamics will vary very fast and result in wrong decisions. In our article, we thus make only γi,0 large and equal to 1. The selection of γi,1 = 0.9983 for (308, 132) LDPC code is empirically trained by executing DTS-RBP with static 1-out TS, that is |T3| = 33, and 100 iterations as in Table IV. Note that when SNR changes, we still use this γi,1 = 0.9983 and thus the training overhead is negligible.
Contrary to the reinforcement factor γi,1, which derives its value from empirical training and has a nondecreasing function, the relaxation factor δi in (17), has a fixed binary value that reflects the knowledge of whether VND is inside or outside the TS. VI. CONCLUSION
Short length LDPC is crucial for future IoT, but the BLER of it could be high due to TS. We developed two TS assisted decoders, named DTS-RBP and TSR-NNBP, with the prestored TS. The DTS-RBP dynamically selected the worst 1-out TS from the prestored list and executed reinforced BP on the VNDs outside the TS. The TSR-NNBP employed the weighted LLRs from the previous iterations of VNDs inside the 1-out TS. Compared with previous works, simulation results showed that only proposed two TS assisted decoders met URLLC requirements for industrial IoT under 308 code blocklength at a 3 dB SNR. Moreover, under 128 code blocklength for satellite IoT, our DTS-RBP decoder provided the best memory-performance tradeoff with approximately the same number of iterations as BP. To further reduce the decoding complexity, the idea of check BP in [27] may be applied, which carefully select which CND can transfer LLR to adjacent VNDs. However, the wrong message will be circulated if error-prone patterns, such as 1-out TS are not considered in the selection process. How to integrate check BP in the proposed decoder will be left as a future work.

Besides the k-out TS, another TS defined as follows is also crucial for LDPC decoding, and the identified TSs via Algorithm 3 also belong to this family, as will be shown later.
Definition 3 (Elementary Trapping-Set [32]): A set TS T is considered as an (a, b) elementary TS (ETS) if VNDs inside TS T are properly connected by degrees-1 or -2 adjacent CNDs and there are precisely b degree-1 adjacent CNDs in the induced subgraph G(T), where b = |d(o=1)(T)| [20], [29], [32].
The ETS also contributes to the error-floor phenomenon, even though it is often composed of a small number of VNDs and a small number of adjacent CNDs in the induced subgraph [22]. This is because the ETS is connected by degrees-1 and -2 of adjacent CNDs, where degree-1 CND has no error protection capability and degree-2 CND is not strong enough to carry out meaningful linear operation [20], [34], [38].
