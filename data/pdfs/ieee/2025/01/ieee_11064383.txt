Efficient Bit-Flipping Decoding Algorithm for Structured LDPC Codes

Cheng He
School of Electrical and Electronic Engineering Huazhong University of Science and Technology, Wuhan, China
e-mail address: chenghehust@gmail.com

Yuzhu Wang
School of Electrical and Electronic Engineering Huazhong University of Science and Technology, Wuhan, China
Corresponding author: 2173090006@qq.com

Abstract: The Gradient descent bit flipping (GDBF) algorithm is a representative approach for decoding low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, which offers a good tradeoff between decoding performance and complexity. Unfortunately, the decoding performance of GDBF algorithm degenerates dramatically for structured LDPC codes due to their large column weight. To address the above issue, we propose to improve GDBF algorithm by introducing a weighted parameter in the energy function of each bit. Specifically, this parameter compensates for the computational error of the energy function due to the large column weight of the parity-check matrices of structured LDPC codes. Moreover, the flipping number of each bit in the iterative process is included in the energy function to avoid consecutive bit flips in the decoding. Simulation results on typical structured LDPC codes indicate that the performance of the proposed algorithm is better than the standard GDBF algorithm at the cost of slight increment in the algorithmic complexity. The increase operations can be easily implemented, which indicates the proposed algorithm is suitable for practical purposes.
Key words-component: Low-Density Parity-Check; Gradient descent; bit flipping; structured LDPC codes;
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-Density Parity-Check Codes (LDPC Codes) are a class of linear codes defined by sparse parity-check matrices, proposed by Gallager in his PhD thesis[1]. Due to their excellent error correction performance, LDPC codes have received wide attention from the academic community. LDPC codes can be divided into two main categories according to the construction method, i.e., Random LDPC Codes and Structured LDPC Codes. Random LDPC Codes and Structured LDPC Codes. These two types of codes have
their own advantages and disadvantages: (1) at longer code lengths, Random
LDPC codes have error correction performance close to the Shannon theoretical limit and can be analyzed in an asymptotic manner[3-6];(2) at medium code lengths, the performance of Structured LDPC codes is comparable to, or even exceeds, the performance of Random LDPC codes[7][8]. In practice, the check matrix of structured LDPC codes is usually constructed using algebraic or combinatorial methods, which can simplify the encoding and decoding process. Therefore, most of the LDPC codes used in practical systems are structured LDPC codes[9][10].
In order to satisfy the decoding requirements of LDPC codes, scholars have proposed two types of iterative decoding algorithms: Belief Propagation (BP) algorithm and Bit Flipping (BF) algorithm[1]. The BP algorithm has excellent performance,

but the complexity of each iteration is relatively high, and therefore it is not suitable for the decoding complexity of the restricted field. The BF algorithm, on the other hand, has the advantages of simplicity and low complexity per iteration, but the decoding performance decreases compared to the BP algorithm. In order to balance the decoding performance and algorithm complexity of practical applications, academics have proposed a series of improvements to the BF algorithm, aiming to improve the performance of the BF algorithm under the premise of ensuring the low complexity of the BF algorithm[1117]. Among them, the Gradient Descent Bit Flipping (GDBF) algorithm is a representative class of improved BF algorithms[18].
The GDBF algorithm is a class of BF algorithms using soft information, and its basic idea is to construct a nonlinear energy function as the objective function considering both decoding performance and decoding complexity, and to design the bit-flip rule based on the gradient descent method in order to maximize the objective function value. Simulation results for stochastic LDPC codes show that the GDBF algorithm is able to obtain performance gains relative to other BF algorithms (e.g., weighted BF algorithms) that introduce soft-judgment information corrections under the same number of iterations. In order to further improve the performance of the GDBF algorithm, some correction algorithms have been proposed to further improve the error correction performance of random LDPC codes using the BF algorithm[19-26]. However, the performance of the GDBF algorithm in decoding structured LDPC codes is not satisfactory. The analysis in the literature shows that the parity-check matrix of structured LDPC codes usually has large column weights, which destroys the balance of the ratio of the two values in the energy function of the GDBF algorithm, and thus leads to a degradation of the performance of the algorithm. For this reason, the exist works propose a weighted correction of the two terms in the energy function using soft verdict information. However, correcting the weights involves a comparison operation whose complexity increases linearly with the row weight of the parity-check matrix, and the parity-check matrix of structured LDPC codes usually has a large row weight, so this correction method increases the algorithm complexity greatly.
Aiming at the above problems of high complexity and performance degradation of the GDBF algorithm in decoding structured LDPC codes, this paper proposes an efficient BF decoding algorithm. The algorithm introduces a weighting coefficient related to the matrix column weight for each bit energy function of the GDBF algorithm to improve the

II. RELEVENT BACKGROUND A. LDPC code
It is assumed that the binary (n, k) LDPC code C is defined by the parity-check matrix H, where n and k are the code length and dimension of the code C, respectively, and the ratio R = k/n is called the code rate of the code C.
According to the difference of check matrix construction method, LDPC codes can be divided into two categories: random LDPC codes and structured LDPC codes. Under the condition of medium code length, structured LDPC codes have excellent performance, and the encoding and decoding process is easy to implement. Currently, structured LDPC codes are widely used in practical communication and storage systems. The parity-check matrix of structured LDPC codes usually contains a certain number of redundant rows, so its row number m is usually larger than n-k.
The parity-check matrix of an LDPC code can be equivalently described using a Tanner graph. Assume that the Tanner graph corresponding to the matrix H is G(H), containing m check nodes and n bit nodes , where the jth check node corresponds to the jth row of H, and where the ith bit node corresponds to the ith column of H. In addition, reference [26] analyzed the GDBF decoding process and found that the iterative processes of unsuccessful decoding are mainly classified into two main categories. The first type of iterative process is characterized by the fact that after a certain number of iterations, the number of errors contained in the decision result of each iteration oscillates, which in turn affects the convergence of the decoding process. This is mainly due to the fact that certain bits are repeatedly flipped during the decoding algorithm, which in turn causes the erroneous bits to be corrected and then flipped out of error. The second type of iterative process is characterized by the fact that after a certain number of iterations, the results of each iterative judgment fall into a specific error vector and cannot be further corrected. This type of error vector usually has a low Hamming weight and is mainly caused by the absorbing set of the LDPC code. Absorbing sets are a specific class of structures of LDPC code check matrices, which are important factors affecting the iterative decoding performance of LDPC codes (especially structured LDPC codes)[27-30].
To overcome this problem, the number of flips of bit zk during GDBF decoding is further introduced in the energy function of Eq. (8). At the initial time, set Fk.=0(1≤k≤n) At a certain iteration, if there is a flip of zk (i.e., the value of zk flips from 1 to -1 or from -1 to 1), then place Fk.= Fk.+1. Thus, the value of Fk. increases with the number of iterations of Fk. For the kth bit, if its corresponding value is larger, it means that the bit is always flipped during the iteration.
From the previous analysis, if a certain bit is always flipped during the GDBF decoding process, it will lead to the first type of unsuccessful decoding, and introducing Fk into the energy function of Eq. (8) can effectively suppress this phenomenon. For the second type of unsuccessful decoding, introducing the number of flips in the energy function can also reduce the possibility of the GDBF decoding process falling into lowweight error vectors, thus favoring successful decoding.

V. CONCLUSION
1)It has been shown that the decoding performance of the GDBF algorithm for structured LDPC codes is unsatisfactory, mainly due to the large column weight of the check matrix of such LDPC codes, which results in an unbalanced range of the two proportions of the per-bit energy function of the GDBF algorithm as well as consecutive flipping of the bits in the iterative process.
2)In this paper, an improved GDBF algorithm is proposed. The algorithm introduces a weighting factor to weight the two terms of the energy function of each bit of the GDBF algorithm, and the weighting coefficients are related to the column weights of the matrix, which avoids the complex operation of calculating the weights in the existing improved methods. In addition, the algorithm further introduces the number of flips of each bit during the iteration process into the energy function to avoid repeated flips of the bits during the decoding process.
3) Simulation results using typical structured LDPC codes show that the proposed improved GDBF algorithm obtains better error correction performance and faster convergence speed relative to the GDBF algorithm with very little increase in complexity, which makes it suitable for decoding in practical systems.
