Efficient ORBGRAND Implementation With Parallel Noise Sequence Generation

Chao Ji , Xiaohu You , Fellow, IEEE, Chuan Zhang , Senior Member, IEEE, and Christoph Studer , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract— Guessing random additive noise decoding (GRAND) is establishing itself as a universal method for decoding linear block codes, and ordered reliability bits GRAND (ORBGRAND) is a hardware-friendly variant that processes soft-input information. In this work, we propose an efficient hardware implementation of ORBGRAND that significantly reduces the cost of querying noise sequences with slight frame error rate (FER) performance degradation. Different from logistic weight order (LWO) and improved LWO (iLWO) typically used to generate noise sequences, we introduce a reduced-complexity and hardware-friendly method called shift LWO (sLWO), of which the shift factor can be chosen empirically to trade the FER performance and query complexity well. To effectively generate noise sequences with sLWO, we utilize a hardware-friendly lookup-table (LUT)-aided strategy, which improves throughput as well as area and energy efficiency. To demonstrate the efficacy of our solution, we use synthesis results evaluated on polar codes in a 65-nm CMOS technology. While maintaining similar FER performance, our ORBGRAND implementations achieve 53.6-Gbps average throughput (1.26× higher), 4.2-Mbps worst case throughput (8.24× higher), 2.4-Mbps/mm2 worst case area efficiency (12× higher), and 4.66 × 104 pJ/bit worst case energy efficiency (9.96× lower) compared with the synthesized ORBGRAND design with LWO for a (128, 105) polar code and also provide 8.62× higher average throughput and 9.4× higher average area efficiency but 7.51× worse average energy efficiency than the ORBGRAND chip for a (256, 240) polar code, at a target FER of 10−7.

C. iLWO Scheme

The difference between actual and queried noise sequences leads to inevitable FER degradation in LWO. D. sLWO Scheme
Compared with LWO, the advantage of iLWO stems from empirical observation of the distribution of nonzero elements in possible noise sequences, but lacks the statistical analysis of absolute LLRs, resulting in a limited FER improvement over LWO. Based on Monte Carlo simulations, [19] provides the sample curve between the reliability and bit index under different SNRs and a piecewise linear fitting model to better approximate the actual distribution of absolute LLRs rather than the simple one-segment approximation with zero intercept on (8), leading to a significant FER enhancement.
In order to fit the sample distribution of absolute LLRs well, [19] is engaged in calculating received LLRs to obtain the parameters of the piecewise linear ORBGRAND algorithm, including the slopes and intercepts. However, when the channel conditions change, different fitting parameters might be computed due to the randomness of simulated frames. Fig. 1(a) gives the sampling distribution of reliability |ri | of size-128 sequences under SNR = 5 dB, where the red, blue, and green lines represent the average results of 10 000 frames, the results of one random frame, and another random frame, respectively. Fig. 1(b) gives the sampling distribution of reliability |ri | of size-128 sequences under SNR = {1, 3, 5, 7, 9} dB, where each curve averages the results of 10 000 random frames. If each curve is processed individually, one could calculate different fitting parameters. In this case, a piecewise linear ORBGRAND architecture that supports the generation of noise sequences with varying order is required, since the fitting parameters directly determine the order in which noise sequences are generated, but will increase the implementation complexity if doing so. E. Simulation and Analysis
In order to compare the FER performance and query complexity of LWO, iLWO, and sLWO, Fig. 2 provides FER and average query complexity for a 5G CRC-aided polar code (128, 105) with an 11-bit CRC [13]. As shown in Fig. 2(a), when querying the same maximum number of noise sequences, denoted by Qmax, e.g., 213 or 217, both iLWO and sLWO (s = 7) provide almost the same FER performance and outperform LWO, resulting in a gain of about 0.5 or 0.4 dB at a target FER of 10−5. This FER improvement comes from iLWO’s empirical observation of sequences of which those with small Hamming weights are tested earlier, since they are more likely to be actual sequences under high SNR, while sLWO better emulates the statistical distribution of absolute LLRs. The average query number associated with these three schemes nearly coincides in Fig. 2(b), which reveals that iLWO and sLWO do not have an advantage in query complexity. It can also be observed that both iLWO and sLWO have an advantage in FER performance compared with the CRC-SCL algorithm if increasing the maximum number of sequences to Qmax = 217. To further illustrate these methods, we provide the first 50 queried ORBGRAND noise sequences under LWO, iLWO, and sLWO (s = 7) in Fig. 3, where each row represents a sequence with white being no bit flip and black being a bit flip. It can be seen that both iLWO and sLWO consider larger bit positions and give priority to the sequences with larger logistic weight and smaller Hamming weight, which is more likely to simulate actual noise effects, thus resulting in better FER performance than that achieved by LWO. In contrast, sLWO with a larger value of s reduces the worst case query complexity and average query complexity more. Specifically, sLWO with s = 6 reduces the worst case query complexity by 98.6%. In Fig. 4(b), the average query number of sLWO with s = 6 at Eb/N0 = 3 dB and Eb/N0 = 7 dB is 44 041 and 2.05, while the corresponding results of LWO are 34 4435 and 2.66, thus resulting in a 87.2% and 22.9% reduction in average query complexity. As a result, sLWO can significantly reduce query complexity while suffering a slight FER performance loss compared with LWO, which can achieve a good tradeoff between FER performance and query complexity if empirically choosing a suitable s. On the other hand, we observe that the lowest complexity sLWO with s = 6 achieves better FER performance at different SNRs than Step-GRAND with {α = 2, β = 6, HWmax = 8} and {α = 2, β = 8, HWmax = 8}, and the improved performance gain is about 0.5 and 0.35 dB at a target FER of 10−7. In addition, sLWO with s = 6 exhibits an advantage in query complexity, where the worst case query complexity is reduced by 4.4% and 77.2%. In terms of the average query complexity, sLWO with s = 6 presents a reduction of 82.9% and 90.5% at Eb/N0 = 7 dB.
Considering a slightly lower code rate, Fig. 4(c) and (d) compares the FER performance and average query complexity of LWO, sLWO, and Step-GRAND for a polar code (128, 99) with an 11-bit CRC, where all simulated decoders are the same as Fig. 4(a) and (b). It can be seen that the FER performance and average query complexity under (128, 99 + 11) follow almost the same trend as those under (128, 105 + 11). We also observe that all decoders with sLWO perform better than both decoders of Step-GRAND, but there exists an obvious difference between sLWO with s = 6 and Step-GRAND with {α = 2, β = 8, HWmax = 8} with respect to the FER, where the FER performance gap narrows as the code rate increases. One possible reason for this is that ORBGRAND generally requires higher query complexity for decoding lower rate codes; thus, the FER performance of sLWO with s = 6 would suffer a slight degradation as the code rate decreases while querying the small maximum number of noise sequences.

At the first step of decoding, the PCU tests y ⊗ H to determine whether y is already a valid codeword; if satisfied, the decoding process stops, and y is the detected codeword. Otherwise, sorting of |LLR(r)| in ascending order is performed in the sorter unit to obtain the permuted index vector. Following this sorting step, the nonzero noise sequences are generated using sLWO in a parallel fashion by employing the PGUs and NGUs. We adopt an LUT-based partition generation scheme and store a subset of precomputed partitions in dedicated memories; these pregenerated partitions are fed into the APCU and PGUs that will be detailed later and produce multiple parallel partitions. After that, the calculated partitions and sorted index vector are passed to the NGUs to recover the actual binary sequences, producing the corresponding

codewords by elementwise XOR operations between the noise sequences and y. Subsequently, the PCUs are invoked to verify whether these parallel codewords satisfy the parity checks. In the end, the outputs of the PCUs are passed to the codeword computation unit, which chooses the codeword with the highest priority among all of the constraint-satisfying codewords. Finally, x∗ is passed to the decoder output.
B. Sorting Unit
Sorting absolute LLRs is a necessary step in ORBGRAND. The nonzero noise sequences should be queried in decreasing order of their likelihood, which requires the index vector resulting from this sorting step. the wide disparity of used technologies (7 versus 65 nm).
Therefore, we maintain the original technology and supply for C2. Design C2 employs an LUT-based strategy to store all possible noise sequences in the memory and then develops
a highly pipelined ORBGRAND architecture with a fixed latency and throughput. When testing a total of Qmax = 213 sequences and loading QS = 256 of those into each stage from the memory, C2 provides an average as well as worst case throughput of 73.6 Gbps with a latency of 58.49 ns. Also, C2 achieves 19.9-Gbps/mm2 area efficiency and 2.32-pJ/bit energy efficiency, at the cost of 3.7 mm2 and 170.8 mW. On the other hand, design C1 utilizes a more efficient LUT-based architecture that stores a seed of base partitions at a low proportion of the total, thus occupying a low area of 1.15 mm2 and a low power of 162.4 mW. Besides, C1 exhibits a high average throughput of 53.6 Gbps and a high average area efficiency of 46.6 Gbps/mm2.
The final comparison is between D1 and D2, which are developed on (256, 240) polar code, and the normalization results are taken to evaluate both, since the used technology in D2 is close to the target technology (i.e., 40 versus 65 nm). Both designs can support highly parallel implementations. Specifically, design D2 employs multiple branches to improve the parallelism of noise sequence generation. However, in design D1, multiple PGUs are deployed to generate noise sequences in a parallel fashion. Attributing mainly to the optimized serial sorter, D2 consumes the lowest nonnormalized power of 4.9 mW among the existing ORBGRAND designs, and the normalized power of 11.47 mW is still 7.5 times lower than D1. In addition, D1 achieves 8.6× higher average throughput and 9.4× higher average area efficiency than D2 together with a comparable area and average energy efficiency with D2, as the resulting marginal gain from them could be lost when considering measurement results of a fabricated chip.
VI. CONCLUSION
In this work, we present an efficient ORBGRAND
VLSI architecture with the proposed sLWO. Compared with
LWO, sLWO can significantly reduce query complexity with
marginal FER loss by choosing a suitable shift factor that

can be determined empirically. To evaluate the efficacy of
our ORBGRAND architecture, we provide synthesis results
in a 65-nm CMOS technology. While maintaining similar
FER performance, our ORBGRAND implementations achieve
53.6-Gbps average throughput (1.26× higher), 4.2-Mbps worst case throughput (8.24× higher), 2.4-Mbps/mm2 worst case area efficiency (12× higher), and 4.66 × 104 pJ/bit worst case energy efficiency (9.96× lower) compared with the synthe-
sized ORBGRAND design with LWO for a (128, 105) polar
code and also provide 8.62× higher average throughput and
9.4× higher average area efficiency but 7.51× worse average
energy efficiency than the ORBGRAND chip for a (256, 240) polar code, at a target FER of 10−7.
