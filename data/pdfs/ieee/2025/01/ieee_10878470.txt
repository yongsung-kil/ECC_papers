Channel-Polarized Multilevel Coding for Low-Complexity Forward Error Correction
Takeshi Kakizaki , Masanori Nakamura , Member, IEEE, Fukutaro Hamaoka , Member, IEEE, and Yoshiaki Kisaka, Member, IEEE

Abstract—Digital coherent optical fiber communication systems adopt forward error correction (FEC) and multilevel modulation in accordance with transmission distance, data rate, and power consumption requirements. Bit-interleaved coded modulation (BICM) is practical coded modulation because of the simplicity of applying a binary code to converted binary-input memoryless channels (B-MCs) under a random bit-interleaver. The power consumption of soft-decision (SD) FEC has recently become a challenge due to transceiver power constraints. The multilevel coding (MLC) scheme efficiently reduces the decoding complexity, while the code design and complexity reduction depend on the modulation order. In this paper, we propose new binary codes for channel-polarized multilevel coding (CP-MLC) that convert multiple B-MCs to unreliable and reliable bit-channels and apply only SD-FEC to unreliable bit-channel to reduce the decoding complexity without depending on the modulation order. Systematic CP-MLC can also be used in probabilistically-shaped signals by using it in the component code of a probabilistic amplitude shaping scheme.
Index Terms—Forward error correction, optical communication, multilevel coding.
I. INTRODUCTION
D IGITAL coherent optical communication systems are expanding to data centers and metro networks in addition to sub-sea and long-haul networks. In sub-sea and long-haul networks, the systems use high noise-tolerance signals, such as quadrature phase shift keying (QPSK) or 16 quadrature amplitude modulation (QAM), and strongly soft-decision forward error correction (SD-FEC), which has a high net coding gain (NCG) and decoding complexity due to soft-decision decoding (SDD), to increase the transmission distance [1]. For data center and metro networks, the systems consider various multilevel modulation and a low-complexity FEC to optimize the power consumption and spectral efficiency [2]. For various applications, the coded modulation scheme is an important technique, combining a wide range of modulation formats with a binary error-correcting code.

In digital coherent optical communications, bit-interleaved coded modulation (BICM) has become one of the most practical coded modulation techniques because it can be adapted to various modulation schemes without changing the underlying binary codes. BICM uses a random bit-interleaver to convert channels with multilevel modulation to parallel binary-input memoryless channels (B-MCs) W : {0, 1} → R to raise the burst error tolerance [3].
Another method, the multilevel coding (MLC) [4] scheme with two-stage decoding (TSD), has been recently widely researched in optical communications to efficiently reduce the decoding complexity [5], [6], [7], [8], [9], [10]. TSD and non-Gray labelling make the channel capacity of the symbol bit-level non-uniform and only assign SDD to the unreliable least significant bit (LSB). An outer code with a lower overhead (OH) and hard decision decoding (HDD) corrects both the errors for most significant bits (MSBs) and the residual bit errors for LSB. MLC can reduce the decoding complexity while suppressing signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) degradation because the performance gap between HDD and SDD decreases in low-noise signals [11]. MLC on probabilistically shaped QAM (PS-QAM) signals has also been studied to increase the noise tolerance [12], [13], [14]. However, the design of binary codes and complexity reduction for MLC depend on the modulation order. Thus, MLC needs to use a different code for each modulation format with the total-OH fixed, and the reduction of its complexity decreases at a low modulation order. Therefore, there is a need for a useful method for reducing complexity that is independent of the modulation format.
For reducing the decoding complexity without depending on the modulation format, channel-polarized MLC (CP-MLC) was proposed [15]. CP-MLC converts d B-MCs (W, W, . . . , W ) into d − 1 reliable bit-channels (W2, W3, . . . , Wd) and one unreliable bit-channel W1, i.e., I(W1) ≤ I(W ) ≤ I(Wj) holds, and bypasses SDD in Wj for each 2 ≤ j ≤ d, where I(·) is mutual information (MI), and d is a division number of the bit sequence. CP-MLC flexibly reduces complexity by changing the division number d independently of the number of bits per symbol m, whereas the complexity reduction of MLC [5], [6], [7], [8], [9], [10] is equal to that of CP-MLC only when d is equal to m. CPMLC can also be adapted to PS-QAM by adopting component binary codes of the probabilistic amplitude shaping (PAS) [16] scheme. In this paper, we present the general form of CP-MLC under B-MC and also propose a form of systematic codes, clarifying CP-MLC as a binary code. To practically implement a decoding algorithm, we introduce a derivation of the log-domain calculation method for the log-likelihood ratio (LLR) in both unreliable and reliable bit-channels. Our contribution reduces the computational complexity of O(2d) to O(d), which is proposed in [20]. We present numerical simulation results for QAM and PS-QAM in the extension of [21]. Our findings demonstrate that CP-MLC can reduce our decoding complexity indicator up to 54% and 34%, respectively, while maintaining the SNR gap at 3.0 and 2.0 dB compared with conventional concatenated codes on QAM and PS-QAM signals. CP-MLC provides a new approach to reducing decoding complexity in addition to the conventional approach for each code.

In Section IV, we describe an evaluation of the decoding performance and the decoding complexity. Our work is concluded in Section V.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we showed that channel-polarized multilevel coding (CP-MLC) gives reasonable performance-complexity tradeoffs by changing the soft-decision forward error correction (SD-FEC) in the entire FEC frame under QAM and PS-QAM. We also derived the log-domain channel conversion method, which can reduce the computational complexity of O(d) compared with the conventional method O(2d) . The CP-MLC uses SDD only on an unreliable bit-channel, which is converted from the original B-MCs, taking advantage of the small difference in decoding performance for SD- and hard-decision (HD)-FEC for low noise signals. Our simulation results show a maximum complexity reduction of 54% and 34% when the SNR differences of

the Shannon limits are 3.0 dB in QAM and 2.0 dB in PS-QAM signals, respectively.
