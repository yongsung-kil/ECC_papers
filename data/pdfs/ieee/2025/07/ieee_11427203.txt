Abstract—LDPC codes, as an error correcting code close to the Shannon limit, are widely used in modern communication systems such as 5G, satellite communication, deep space communication, and other fields. Traditional LDPC decoding algorithms, such as belief propagation (BP) algorithm and its variants, have achieved good performance, but face challenges such as high complexity and slow convergence, especially when dealing with long code lengths and high noise scenarios. The rise of deep learning has brought new opportunities for LDPC decoding, and its powerful features learning and pattern recognition capabilities are expected to break through the bottleneck of traditional decoding. Based on the overview of deep neural network decoding methods for LDPC codes by data-driven and model driven approaches, this paper analysis and comparison of error rate performance of short code H (8,16) matrix by convolutional neural networks (CNN), residual network (ResNet), and Kolmogorov-Arnold Network (KAN).
Keywords—LDPC, decoding, deep learning
I. INTRODUCTION
In 1948, Shannon [1] proposed the famous Shannon theorem, which revealed the theoretical channel capacity of reliable communication, but did not provide a specific channel coding algorithm. After decades of development, channel coding technology started with Hamming codes, gradually developed into convolutional codes, and now widely used Turbo codes, LDPC codes, and Polar codes, which have become increasingly mature. Among them, LDPC codes have attracted much attention in domestic and foreign research due to their unique advantages, and have achieved rich results.
In 1962, Gallager [2] proposed LDPC codes, named after the sparsity of the parity check matrix. Gallager elaborated on its encoding method, iterative decoding algorithm, and error performance analysis in his doctoral thesis, but due to the limitations of computer hardware at the time, high complexity decoding algorithms were difficult to implement, and LDPC codes did not receive widespread attention. In 1981, Tanner [3] introduced a bipartite graph representation (later termed the Tanner graph) to model the interactions between variable nodes (VNs) and check nodes (CNs) within the parity check matrix of LDPC codes, thereby establishing a foundational framework for iterative decoding algorithms. In 1996, Mackay [4] played the role of LDPC's bit error rate,

demonstrating its performance close to the Shannon limit, which quickly made LDPC codes a research hotspot. In terms of constructing the parity check matrix and encoding algorithm for LDPC codes, early scholars used a random construction method to generate the parity check matrix. Later, scholars explored algebraic methods and conducted research using geometric analysis, graph theory analysis, and other techniques. M. G. Luby [5] proposed through graph theory analysis that irregular LDPC codes perform better than regular LDPC codes.
Although significant achievements have been made in theoretical research and practical applications, the decoding implementation of LDPC codes still faces the contradiction between complexity and performance, which limits their large-scale application in fields such as deep space communication and satellite communications.
Neural networks have achieved good results in Computer vision, natural language processing, and speech. Currently, common neural network models include: Deep Neural Network (DNN), Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) [6], Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) [7]. Nachmani E. [8] verifies the potential of deep learning in short code long channel decoding, but the training set requires an exponential number of codewords. Reference [9] proposes a BP-RNN decoder based on recurrent neural networks, introduces the structure of the BP-RNN decoder, and simulates QC-LDPC with code lengths of 128, 256, and 512, respectively. The results show that in the case of a large number of iterations, the computational complexity of the BP-RNN decoder increases, but its performance is significantly improved compared to the BP decoder. Due to the fact that the decoding performance of the BP algorithm for LDPC codes in AWGN channels is close to the Shannon limit, but its performance significantly decreases when dealing with related noise, while CNN performs well in image processing (such as image denoising). Authors in [10] proposed an iterative BP-CNN decoding framework that combines traditional BP decoding with CNN's noise estimation ability, achieving a gain of about 3.5dB compared to traditional BP.

Traditional decoding approaches for LDPC codes are broadly categorized into two primary types: soft-decision decoding and hard-decision decoding. Among them, hard decision decoding mainly includes bit flipping (BF) algorithm, while soft decision decoding algorithms mainly include probability-based belief propagation (BP) algorithm, log likelihood ratio based LLR-BP algorithm, minimum sum algorithm, normalized minimum sum algorithm, and bias minimum sum algorithm. The hard decision algorithm has low complexity and is easy to implement in hardware, but due to its low information utilization rate (based on receiving 0 or 1) and limited decoding performance, it is commonly used in environments with good channel conditions and low noise. In soft decision algorithms, the initial information obtained is the probability ratio of 0/1 information, and the information transmitted between nodes in each iteration is also a probability or confidence value. In each iteration, each verification node calculates the confidence values passed by all connected variable nodes, and similarly, variable nodes

also calculate the confidence values passed by all connected verification nodes.
III. LDPC DECODING BASED ON DEEP LEARNING In addition to traditional LDPC decoding algorithms, deep learning can also be applied to LDPC decoding. Deep learning-based LDPC decoding methods can be classified into two categories: data-driven deep learning and modeldriven LDPC decoding.
A. Data Driven LDPC Decoding LDPC decoding based on data-driven deep learning do
not need to consider the complex structure of information transfer between CNs and VNs in traditional algorithms. It only requires constructing a dataset and obtaining output information through a neural network model. The neural network learns the features of the dataset, iteratively trains the network, and obtains good training results. The network is then used to implement LDPC decoding. The basic idea is to let the deep learning model learn the mapping relationship between the received noisy LDPC codewords and the original transmitted information bits through a large number of training data. The model automatically extracts relevant features from the data to capture the statistical characteristics of the LDPC code and the channel noise, thereby realizing the decoding function.
Fig. 2. Data driven LDPC decoding.
As shown in Fig. 2 and Fig. 3, data driven LDPC decoding includes four steps. Firstly, a large number of LDPC - coded data sets need to be generated under different channel conditions. The dataset is the cornerstone of training, and the dataset that needs to be prepared is the original information bits as sample labels, and the LLR information after LDPC encoding and Gaussian channel as input for the network model sample data. The original information can be randomly generated in the program, and in this section, codewords with code lengths of 16 and 648 were used as the code length for data-driven training.
Fig. 3. LDPC Code Communication System Model Based on DL.
Then, Choose the appropriate network architecture based on the task type and data characteristics, such as multilayer perceptron (MLP), convolutional neural networks (CNNs), recurrent neural networks (RNNs), Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM), Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) or other networks. By empirical methods, grid search, random search, or Bayesian optimization, hyperparameters such as learning rate, batch size, and iteration times are determined. In this paper, CNN, Resnet, and KAN [11-12] networks are selected.

Commonly used loss functions include the cross entropy loss, which measures the difference between the predicted decoded result and the actual transmitted information bits. The goal is to minimize this loss function to make the model output the most accurate decoding result. In some cases, other loss functions such as the mean - squared error loss may also be used, or a combination of multiple loss functions can be designed to comprehensively optimize the decoding performance.
Finally, model evaluation and optimization are given. Simulink is performed and gets the Bit Error Rate (BER) and Frame Error Rate (FER) to assess the performance of the trained model. Compare the BER of the neural networkbased decoder with that of traditional LDPC decoding algorithms under the same channel conditions. This helps to quantify the improvement achieved.
B. Model Driven LDPC Decoding Another approach is model driven LDPC decoding that
combines neural networks with traditional decoding algorithms. In model driven decoding, a mathematical model is constructed to describe the relationship between the transmitted codeword, the received signal, and the noise. This model is then used to estimate the most likely values of the information bits. The process of iteratively transmitting information between CNs and VNs is transformed into a neural network to complete LDPC decoding, achieving higher bit error rate performance than traditional decoding algorithms at longer code lengths.
SNNMS is based on the mathematical model of the traditional LDPC decoding algorithm NMS, which corresponds the network structure and algorithm flow oneto-one (such as iteratively unfolding into layers), and the parameters have physical meanings (such as correction factors) to make the network more interpretable.

B. Model Driven LDPC Decoding Performance The main principle of data purification method [11] is to
use filters based on trustworthy clean datasets to remove training samples that deviate significantly from normal values [12]. This process filters out suspicious data before the training process. The current methods can effectively remove typical noise from data but may not be effective for carefully crafted adversarial noise. In addition, due to the unknown total number of contaminated samples, these methods cannot completely remove all contaminated points from the data stream. In order to remove as many outliers as possible, it is necessary to combine these methods with other methods.

V. CONCLUSIONS
An overview of deep learning LDPC decoding methods was provided. We compared the BER of H (8,16) and H (648， 108) matrices using CNN, Resnet, and KAN networks, and constructed a decoding network structure based on SNNMS algorithm using model driven algorithm. In deep learning implementation of LDPC decoding, model driven LDPC decoding is based on memory constraints, making it impossible to achieve model driven implementation of very long LDPC codes. In the future, we will consider optimizing the model and reducing the required parameters to achieve this.
