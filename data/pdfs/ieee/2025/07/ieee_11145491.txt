LDPC decoder based on a minimalist bipartite GCN

Abstract—This paper proposes a Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) decoder based on a minimalist bipartite graph convolutional neural network (GCN). The proposed method has been validated in a 16QAM coherent optical communication simulation system.
Keywords—LDPC, decoder, bipartite GNN, GCN
I. INTRODUCTION
As a critical component in high-speed communication systems, channel coding technology plays a pivotal role in enhancing communication system reliability and improving bit error rate (BER) performance. Forward error correction (FEC) codes are widely adopted channel-coding methods that have been successfully implemented in numerous communication systems [1]. Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes, subclass of FEC, are distinguished by the sparsity of their parity-check matrices, which enables nearShannon-limit performance potential [2].
With the advancement of communication technologies, increasingly powerful decoders have been continuously proposed [3], [4]. Current LDPC implementations predominantly employ belief propagation (BP) decoding algorithms and their derivatives. For short-length LDPC code, such iterative decoding algorithms based on Tanner graphs are susceptible to short-cycle phenomena, resulting in premature convergence of information exchange among nodes during iterations. This phenomenon can induce error propagation and severely degrades error correction capabilities.
Graph Neural Network (GNN) [5], a class of deep learning models specifically designed for modeling and processing graph-structured data, leverages the connectivity between nodes and edges to capture complex dependencies and irregular topological relationships inherent in such data.
This paper proposes an LDPC decoder based on a minimalist bipartite graph convolutional neural network (GCN). Compared to traditional BP decoding algorithms, the GCN decoder leverages its powerful nonlinear approximation capabilities to mitigate the short-cycle phenomena inherent in short-code decoding while reducing both decoding iterations and computational complexity. Simulation results under 16QAM modulation demonstrate that the GCN decoder outperforms BP decoders at equivalent complexity levels, achieving a gain of up to 3 dB in low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR).

B. GCN Decoder
The BP decoding algorithm for LDPC codes is an iterative decoding method operating on a Tanner graph, which consists of variable nodes (v), check nodes (c), and edges connecting them. A cycle in the Tanner graph is defined as a path starting and ending at the same node via distinct edges. During decoding, messages are alternately exchanged between v and c, errors in the received signal are progressively corrected. Notably, during each iteration, the information acquired by any node is exclusively derived from the extrinsic information values generated in the previous iteration.

It is evident from the messaging mechanism that the larger periods in the Tanner diagram allow mutable nodes to accumulate more extrinsic information from different paths. Conversely, short cycles can achieve self-reinforcing feedback of the node's own information in fewer iterations, resulting in error propagation and degraded decoding performance. However, neural networks mitigate the impact of short cycles through nonlinear optimization, data-driven training, and adaptive weight adjustment, thereby enhancing decoding performance.

For disjoint variable node set V and check node set C with edges existing exclusively between V and C, the Tanner graph formed by V and C is inherently a bipartite graph. RESULTS AND DISCUSSIONS To validate the performance of the GCN decoder, simulation comparisons were conducted with the traditional BP iterative decoding in both the AWGN environment and the 32GBaud 100km coherent optical transmission environment. For the simulation, a short-length LDPC code with a code length of n=648 and information length k=486 was selected, employing 16QAM modulation for signal transmission. The log-likelihood ratio (LLR) values of the preprocessed received signals served as inputs, with a batch size of 100 for each data group. Decoding was performed via the GCN layer defined in (6), where the network was minimally designed with only 1 GCN layer and a hidden feature dimension of hd = 2. The loss function was implemented as binary cross entropy with logits. Training utilized an Adam optimizer with gradient clipping to stabilize updates. The dataset was partitioned into training, validation, and test sets. For each SNR, the training process ran for a maximum of 1,000 epochs, with validation performed every 5 epochs using the validation set. An early stopping mechanism was applied, ensuring optimal training efficiency. To minimize testing variance, the test set comprised 10,000 epochs, equivalent to 1,000,000 codeword groups per SNR.

The training results of the GCN-based decoder are shown in Fig. 1. The trajectory of the loss function demonstrates a progressive improvement in decoding accuracy. The loss rapidly dropped to 0.1 within 150 epochs, after which it began to converge gradually. After 445 epochs, the early stopping mechanism is triggered to halt the training.
The trained GCN decoder was evaluated through simulations, with comparative analyses against the BP decoder in terms of computational complexity and BER. The complexity comparison results, illustrated in Fig. 2, demonstrate that the time complexity of a single GCN layer is approximately equivalent to hd iterations of BP decoding. Since the GCN decoder employs only 1 GCN layer with hd = 2, its overall complexity is roughly comparable to 2 iterations of BP decoding.

The BER comparison results between the GCN decoder and BP decoders are illustrated in Fig. 3. Under AWGN channels, the GCN decoder outperforms the 2-iteration BP decoder across all SNR, demonstrating its superior decoding accuracy at equivalent complexity. When compared to the 5iteration BP decoder, the GCN decoder achieves up to 3 dB SNR gain at SNRs below 11 dB. However, beyond 11 dB, the BP decoder surpasses the GCN decoder in performance. Under optical fiber channels, the BER curves exhibit trends similar to those in AWGN channels, with a maximum gain of approximately 2 dB.
At high SNR levels, the performance crossover arises because the BP decoder rapidly converges to precise solutions through iterative probabilistic message passing. In contrast, the GCN decoder’s nonlinear approximation capacity may introduce computational redundancy or converge to suboptimal local minima under high-SNR conditions. Additionally, the vanishing gradient of the loss function near convergence limits further optimization of the neural network, hindering its ability to refine predictions in high-SNR scenarios.
IV. CONCLUSION
This paper proposes a LDPC decoder based on a minimalist bipartite GCN and evaluates its performance through simulations under both AWGN channels and 32 GBaud 100 km coherent optical transmission systems. The results demonstrate that the GCN decoder consistently outperforms BP decoder at equivalent computational complexity. Moreover, compared to higher-iteration and more complex BP decoders, the GCN decoder achieves superior performance at low SNRs, with a maximum gain of 3 dB. Even under its extremely simplified architecture, the GCN decoder delivers competitive decoding accuracy, positioning it as a promising candidate for practical channel decoding applications.
