Abstract: Low-density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes have significantly been a vital resource in modern communication systems due to their close-to-capacity error-correcting capabilities and tendency to align well with various applications. The paper describes the architecture design and implementation of an LDPC encoder and decoder using Verilog. As for the state-of-the-art study on encoding and decoding, particularly referring to iterative methods for example, Bit-Flipping, Min-Sum, and Sum-Product algorithms, were considered. These methods are evaluated in terms of BER performance, computational complexity, and hardware efficiency. The proposed architecture is systematic encoder decoder integrated efficiently in such a way that it is optimized for high throughput and low resource utilization. Simulation results indicated the aptitude of the system in correcting transmission errors with respect to different noise conditions. It will depict its applicability in wireless communication in 5G networks and deep-space missions. These results show a possibility of LDPC codes to balance efficiency and reliability, hence provide significant contributions toward the advance of error-correcting systems.
Keywords: LDPC Codes, Verilog, Error Correction, Bit Flipping, Min-Sum, Sum-Product (SPA)
I. INTRODUCTION
Modern digital communication systems recognize that error correction is an essential requirement for ensuring reliable data transfer across noisy channels. Among the various recent, significant developments in error-correcting schemes, LowDensity Parity-Check codes are prominent because of their nearly capacity-approaching performance and scalability. LDPC codes rely on sparse parity-check matrices to identify errors during transmission so that transmission can be corrected; hence, these codes are extremely suited to a vast applications range, from wireless communications to deep-space exploration. The most distinctive feature of LDPC codes is the ability to bring the communication channels to near the Shannon limit. This enables efficient error correction with very low complexity. Parity bits are integrated into the original data during systematic encoding, whereas iterative algorithms for decoding, such as Bit-Flipping, Min-Sum, and Sum-Product, make them versatile. These have established LDPC codes as a milestone in the

development of standards like IEEE 802.16e, 5G NR, and CCSDS. This research designs and implements an LDPC encoder and decoder architecture in the Verilog language. Building on the algorithmic techniques and architectural innovation, this research has been directed toward system performance in terms of throughput and energy consumption. The approach used here has a focus on systematic integration of encoder and decoder modules and an evaluation of different decoding algorithms with regard to BER hardware efficiency.
The work focuses on closing the gap between theoretical developments in LDPC codes and practical hardware implementations, considering de- coder complexity challenges, resource constraints, and error correction performance. The contributions would fall in the line of developing scalable, robust, and efficient systems that meet the demands of contemporary communication standards.

III. METHODOLOGY
LDPC codes are linear error correcting codes used in Digital communication systems. They are a type of Error-correcting codes (ECC) that are used to detect and fix errors that occurs during data transmission.

Particularly used in Noisy channels like wireless transmission. A. Error Correcting Codes
When the data (like a message) is sent through a noisy channel, the message might get corrupted Figure 1. To solve this, we add extra bits to the message, called ‘parity-bits’, that help detect and correct any errors in the transmitted data as shown in Figure 2.

Fig. 2. Add Extra Bits called Parity Bits
Following are some important terminologies used in the system.
a. Codeword: The Full Encoded Message (data bits + Parity bits) sent over a channel.
b. Parity-check Matrix (H-matrix): A sparse (mostly zero’s and few One’s) matrix used to check errors and correct them.
c. Message bits (data bits): Original data that needs to be transmitted.
d. Parity bits: Extra bits added to the Message to help detect errors.
Now, the LDPC or any other error correcting codes have these two components called Encoder and Decoder as depicted in Figure 3. The Encoder is used to add those Parity bits and Decoder detects the errors and retain original message.
Fig. 3. Encoder and Decoder System
These codes are capacity-approaching codes, which means that practical constructions exist such that the noise threshold can be set very close to theoretical maximum - the Shannon limit- for a symmetric memoryless channel. Noise threshold is then defined an upper bound for a channel noise, up to which probability of lost information can be reduced. B. Parity-Check Matrix (H Matrix)
Parity check matrix 'H' is often an identifying characteristic of the LDPC code. H is a low-density matrix that uses most of its entries as 0's and just a few entries as 1's. For that reason, the term Low-Density Parity-Check Code is associated with it.

The Bit-Flipping Algorithm is a technique for LDPC codesdecoding at hard decisions, optimized for simplified hardware implementations. Unlike the Sum-Product Algorithm (SPA), relying on probabilistic soft information, the Bit-Flipping algorithm operates on binary values-0s and 1s-which makes it better suited for applications involving low power consuming requirements or hardware limitations. E. LDPC Decoder using Min-Sum Algorithm
The Bit-Flipping Algorithm is a hard-decision decoding method for LDPC codes optimized for reduced complexity in hardware implementations. Unlike the Sum-Product Algorithm, which relies on probabilistic soft information, the Bit-Flipping operates on the binary domain (0s and 1s). This makes the method applicable to low-power applications or hardwareconstrained applications. Hard-decision decoding is initiated with a hard-decision on the received bits assuming each received bit is correct or flipped. An algorithm works for every iteration. At every check node in the VNs, the syndrome will be checked by the algorithm; if that syndrome is satisfied, it means no error is associated with that bit. The connected check nodes are summed in the case of a VN not satisfying any check for a syndrome; if this number exceeds a set threshold, the corresponding bit is flipped. This iterative operation proceeds by flipping bits according to non-satisfied syndromic checks till all checks are satisfied, indicating that correct decoding took place, or until a predefined maximum number of iterations is reached. While the Bit-Flipping Algorithm can only provide worse error correction than soft-decision decoders, like SPA, its simplicity makes it a good candidate for hardware-constrained applications.
F. The Sum-Product Algorithm (SPA)
SPA is one of the most prominent soft-decision decoding algorithms for LDPC codes with a high error-correcting capability. It is based on an iterative process in which messages are exchanged between VNs and CNs in a bipartite graph that represents the parity-check matrix. The iterative messagepassing algorithm allows the decoder to estimate the probability of each bit's being correct from received symbols. In each VN, it calculates a log-likelihood ratio (LLR) during the initial assignment, which is calculated from the received encoded bits, and the LLR tells the likelihood that the bit is a 0 or a 1. In each iteration, VNs and CNs exchange messages: each VN updates

and sends an LLR based on inputs from neighboring CNs, and each CN computes and sends messages back to VNs by using received messages from neighboring VNs. A process of updates follows strict rules: CNs work according to a "sum" rule, and VNs apply a "product" rule; both in fact approximate Bayesian inference. The estimates are continued in the iteration across multiple iterations. Upon completion of a predetermined number of iterations or on coming into satisfaction of a convergence criterion, the hard decisions are made using the final LLR values: positive LLRs are decoded as '0' and negative ones as '1'. It achieves an attractive balance between computational complexity and error-correcting performance, making the sumproduct algorithm an attractive choice for hardware-based implementations such as FPGA applications.
G. Integration of Encoder and Decoder
The final integrated LDPC system would start by encoding the data at the transmitter side adding parity bits producing an encoded data stream that is transmitted. Then, the decoder modules a set of SPA, Bit-Flipping, or Min-Sum modules-would receive these encoded data streams and perform error correction as necessitated. Here, each decoder's performance will be assessed on its error correcting capability, computing complexity, and hardware efficiency. Now, it is quite feasible that their pros and cons may differ in those different criteria.
IV. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
The BER performance of various LDPC decoding algorithms unveils the merits and demerits of each method. Sum-Product Algorithm, in fact, illustrated the minimum BER value of all possible conditions, which is attributed to its softdecision decoding method. By iteratively refining the likelihood values, SPA yielded highly accurate decoding outcomes particularly for cases associated with multiple errors. This strong BER performance shows the robustness of SPA in a high-noise environment and makes it suitable for applications demanding high reliability. On the other hand, the Bit-Flipping Algorithm showed a moderate BER performance, which corrected most single-bit errors with difficulty with the multi-bit errors because of being a hard-decision type. This sensitivity to error patterns, especially when multiple bits are affected, limits the efficacy of Bit-Flipping in high-noise environments; however, in simpler noise conditions with minimal multi-bit errors, its BER performance was satisfactory. The Min-Sum Algorithm (MSA) achieved BER results close to those of SPA, though with a slight reduction due to its approximations in message computation. Despite this minor trade-off in accuracy, MSA's BER performance remained significantly better than that of BitFlipping and nearly equivalent to SPA, highlighting MSA as an effective balance between accuracy and resource efficiency.

V. CONCLUSION
In the current work, an LDPC encoder and decoder architecture was successfully implemented in Verilog. This shows efficiency in correcting errors for noisy systems of communication. Sum-Product Algorithm does better in error correction while Min-Sum and Bit-Flipping algorithms balance efficiency against simplicity. The architecture could therefore have high throughput, scalability, and is energy-efficient enough to make it suitable for applications such as 5G and deep-space communications. It makes clear the potential of LDPC codes in meeting modern communications tasks and puts this into perspective for further optimization and adaptation to emerging technologies.
