A Study of the Performance of LDPC Codes under Various Decoding Algorithms and Schedules

Sangwon Chae, Hyojeong Choi, Gangsan Kim, Hong-Yeop Song School of Electrical and Electronic Engineering Yonsei University Seoul, South Korea
{sw.chae, hjchoi3022, gs.kim, hysong}@yonsei.ac.kr
Jongsun Ahn KPS ground and user segment development division/KPS R&D Directorate
Korea Aerospace Research Institute Daejeon, South Korea jsever@kari.re.kr

Abstract—Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes have been widely used in modern communication standards due to their strong error correction capabilities. This paper presents an experimental evaluation of a (1200, 600) irregular LDPC code over an additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel, systematically comparing three decoding algorithms (Sum-Product, Normalized Min-Sum, and Offset Min-Sum) with three scheduling methods (Flooding, Layered Belief Propagation, and Residual Belief Propagation).
Parameter sweeps are performed to determine the optimal normalization factor (α) for the Normalized Min-Sum and the offset factor (β) for the Offset Min-Sum. A comparative analysis is then performed on the frame error rate (FER) and convergence behavior of each algorithm and scheduling configuration, considering the maximum number of iterations set at 10 and 25.
The results show that the Offset Min-Sum algorithm with β = 0.5 and Layered Belief Propagation scheduling provides an acceptable trade-off between complexity and error-correction performance, closely matching the Sum-Product algorithm but at a lower computational cost. Although Residual Belief Propagation converges quickly at lower signal-to-noise ratios, it exhibits a high error floor. These findings provide practical guidelines for selecting optimal decoding configurations in resource-constrained applications.
Index Terms—LDPC, Decoding Algorithms, Scheduling, Wireless Communication.
I. INTRODUCTION
Forward error correction (FEC) is a key technique for
correcting channel errors in communication systems. Among
the FEC schemes, LDPC codes [1] exhibit excellent error
correction performance and are now widely adopted in many
digital communication and broadcasting standards [2]. Due to
this wide practicality, there has been ongoing theoretical and
applied research on LDPC codes. In particular, the problem
of balancing computational complexity and error correction
capability by improving decoding algorithms and schedules is
still an active research topic [3], [4].
To address this design trade-off, the present work investi-
gates three widely used decoding algorithms (Sum-Product [6],

Normalized Min-Sum [7], and Offset Min-Sum [7]) in conjunction with three distinct scheduling methods (Flooding [1], [9], Layered Belief Propagation [10], and Residual Belief Propagation [11]). Our study focuses on the specific (1200, 600) irregular LDPC code, evaluated over an additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel. In addition, we adjust the Normalized Min-Sum and Offset Min-Sum parameters to determine the optimal normalization and offset factors. Finally, we compare the decoding performance under different iteration bounds (10 and 25) and investigate the error correction performance.

II. EXPERIMENTAL SETUP A. System Model and LDPC Code Configurations
In this paper, we model the AWGN channel as follows:

yi = xi + zi, i = 1, 2, . . . ,

(1)

where yi is the received signal, xi is the BPSK-modulated signal, and zi is the AWGN. The LDPC code used in this experiment is the (1200, 600) irregular LDPC code implemented in GPS L1C Subframe 2, as defined in [5], with a code rate of 1/2. The degree of the variable nodes ranges from 2 to 15, while the check nodes have a degree of 8 or 9.

B. Decoding Algorithms and Schedules
In this experiment, three decoding algorithms are considered: the Sum-Product, Normalized Min-Sum, and Offset MinSum algorithms.
The Sum-Product (SP) algorithm [6] computes and exchanges messages between variable nodes and check nodes using probability (or log-likelihood) information. This is done via functions such as tanh(·) and its inverse, making SP more computationally intensive than simplified alternatives. However, due to its accurate representation of the underlying probability distributions, it typically achieves excellent errorcorrection performance.

The Min-Sum (MS) algorithm [8] simplifies Sum-Product by replacing the more complex nonlinear operations with a minimum-based check node update. Although Min-Sum is faster, it tends to overestimate the magnitude of the messages and can lead to suboptimal performance. The Normalized Min-Sum (NMS) algorithm [7] addresses this by multiplying check node outputs by a normalization factor, α. This factor is chosen based on empirical or analytical methods to reduce the overestimation.
Finally, the Offset Min-Sum (OMS) algorithm [7] is another improvement on Min-Sum, where a constant offset, β, is subtracted from the magnitude of the check node outputs instead of applying a scaling factor. The offset helps mitigate message overestimation in a way that is sometimes more robust to different channel conditions than NMS.
We also explore three schedules for message updates: Flooding, Layered Belief Propagation, and Residual Belief Propagation.
The Flooding schedule is a traditional, two-step process in which all variable nodes first send messages to their connected check nodes, and then all check nodes respond to their connected variable nodes within each iteration [1], [9]. While conceptually simple and highly parallelizable, flooding can be slower to converge because it treats all edges equally in every iteration, regardless of the level of convergence on each edge.
The Layered Belief Propagation (LBP) schedule [10] divides the parity-check matrix into multiple layers (often corresponding to subsets of check nodes) and processes them sequentially. After each layer is updated, the newly updated variable node messages are immediately used for the next layer within the same global iteration. This leads to faster convergence than flooding since information propagates more rapidly after each layer update, which can result in enhanced performance for a given maximum number of iterations. However, it partially sacrifices the simplicity and parallelism of the flooding schedule.
Finally, the Residual Belief Propagation (RBP) schedule [11] assigns a priority to each message update based on the amount of change, or “residual”, from the previous iteration. The algorithm selectively updates messages with the largest change first. In principle, this approach enables the decoder to prioritize edges requiring refinement, thereby achieving faster convergence within a limited number of iterations. However, it must be noted that the RBP schedule involves the computation and comparison of these residuals, a process that can be computationally intensive in comparison to other schedules.
C. Parameter Optimization
In our experiments, we varied the normalization factor of the NMS algorithm and the offset factor of the OMS algorithm to identify the best-performing parameter values under each schedule.
For the NMS algorithm, we tested normalization factors (α) ranging from 0.70 to 0.95 in increments of 0.05. We observed that α = 0.85 and α = 0.90 yielded superior performance with both the flooding and LBP schedules, whereas α = 0.75, α =

0.80, and α = 0.85 performed better with the RBP schedule. In order to unify the choice of α across all three schedules, we selected α = 0.85.
For the OMS algorithm, we tested offset factors (β) ranging from 0.40 to 0.60 in increments of 0.05. We found that values between 0.40 and 0.50 delivered the best results across all schedules, and that performance diminished as β departed from this range. Consequently, we set β = 0.50 for all experiments.
III. SIMULATION RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
To evaluate the FER performance of the three decoding algorithms under different scheduling methods, we conducted Monte Carlo simulations over an AWGN channel. Each trial involved transmitting coded frames of 1200 bits (one codeword), then iterating the decoding algorithm until a specified number of error frames were observed. We assumed perfect Channel State Information at the receiver (CSIR).
Figure 1 shows the performance of the SP algorithm, while Figures 2 and 3 present the results for the NMS (α = 0.85) and OMS (β = 0.5) algorithms, respectively. In each figure, the Frame Error Rate (FER) is plotted versus Eb/N0 for a maximum number of decoding iterations of 10 and 25, using Flooding, LBP, or RBP scheduling.
A. Algorithm Comparison
Overall, the SP and OMS algorithms provided comparable FER curves, generally outperforming the NMS algorithm. While the SP algorithm exhibits a marginal performance advantage, OMS offers equivalent error correction capabilities at a reduced computational cost. Conversely, NMS exhibited a marginal decline in performance relative to both SP and OMS.
A more detailed examination of the performance reveals that under the flooding scheduling with 25 iterations, the SP algorithm attains 2.30 dB, while OMS follows closely at 2.25 dB. NMS lags behind at 2.45 dB. A similar pattern is observed with the LBP schedule, where both SP and OMS achieve 2.15 dB but NMS remains around 2.35 dB.
B. Scheduling Comparison
Figures 1–3 also highlight how each schedule converges under different maximum numbers of iterations (10 vs. 25). The Flooding schedule shows the largest performance gap between 10 and 25 iterations. For instance, at FER = 10−4 under the SP algorithm, Flooding exhibits a 0.75 dB improvement between 10 and 25 iterations, reflecting its slower convergence. By contrast, LBP shows only about a 0.3 dB gap under the same metric, indicating that it converges more quickly. RBP, on the other hand, demonstrates almost no difference between 10 and 25 iterations, suggesting it is effectively converged by iteration 10.
However, RBP shows its high error floor, which is especially evident when comparing it to the other schedules on the 25 iteration curve. For all three algorithms, the LBP achieves the best performance among the schedules at 25 iterations, followed by flooding, while RBP converges quickly but performs poorly at high Eb/N0.

IV. CONCLUSION
This paper investigated the performance of a (1200, 600) irregular LDPC code over an AWGN channel under three decoding algorithms: SP, NMS, and OMS. Additionally, three scheduling methods were considered: Flooding, LBP, and RBP. Parameter sweeps were performed for the NMS (normalization factor α) and OMS (offset factor β) algorithms to identify optimal values. Monte Carlo simulations were performed at varying values of Eb/N0 to evaluate the impact of maximum numbers of iterations (10 vs. 25) on the error correction capability.
The SP and OMS algorithms yield comparably low FERs, with OMS offering a simpler computational structure. In contrast, NMS exhibits higher FER.
Flooding demonstrates the slowest convergence, with significant improvement observed by increasing the iteration limit from 10 to 25. LBP generally converges faster than flooding, while RBP converges quickly, but its performance is compromised by a pronounced error floor.
Overall, the Offset Min-Sum algorithm (β = 0.5) combined with LBP scheduling has been shown to be an effective solution for balancing computational complexity and errorcorrection performance. This configuration competes closely with SP decoding and outperforms NMS.
