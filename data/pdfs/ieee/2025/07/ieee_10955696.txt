Effective IDS Error Correction Algorithms for DNA Storage Channels With Multiple Output Sequences
Caiyun Deng, Guojun Han , Senior Member, IEEE, Pengchao Han, and Yi Fang , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract— DNA data storage is a cutting-edge storage technique due to its high density, replicability, and longterm capability. It involves encoding, insertion, deletion, and substitution (IDS) channels for data synthesis and sequencing, and decoding processes. The IDS channels that feature multiple output sequences are prone to IDS errors, complicating the decoding process and degrading the performance of DNA data storage. To address this issue, we investigate effective IDS error correction algorithms considering two encoding schemes in DNA data storage. Specifically in the encoding process, we use marker codes (MC) and embedded marker codes (EMC) as inner codes, respectively, both connected to low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes as outer codes. First, we propose the segmented progressive matching (SPM) algorithm to infer the consensus sequence from multiple output sequences, thereby facilitating the decoding processes. Moreover, when using MC as the inner code, we propose a synchronous decoding algorithm based on the Hidden Markov Model (SDH) to infer the a posteriori probability (APP) of base symbols, which supports the external decoding algorithm. Furthermore, when the inner code is EMC, we propose the iterative external decoding (IED) algorithm. IED integrates synchronous decoding with embedded normalized min-sum decoding (ENMS) to achieve an enhanced APP for external decoding, enabling lower bit-error rate (BER) transmission. Meanwhile, we reduce the complexity of the external decoder by minimizing checksum node computations. Comparing the two schemes reveals that the SDH algorithm with MC as the inner code offers a lightweight solution for DNA data storage. In contrast, the IED with EMC demonstrates superior decoding performance with a linear complexity scale by the number of iterations. Compared with existing studies, simulation results show that our proposed decoding algorithm reduces the BER by 21.72% ∼ 99.75%.
Index Terms— DNA data storage, marker codes, lowdensity parity-check (LDPC) codes, insertion/deletion/ substitution (IDS) channel, normalized min-sum (NMS) decoding.

I. INTRODUCTION
T HE rapid advancement of information technology has resulted in an exponential increase in global data volume. Various storage media are developed to accommodate this vast amount of data, including mechanical hard drives, solid-state drives, Blu-ray discs, flash memories, etc. [1]. However, these media suffer from limited storage density, short retention life and environmental pollution. With the development of genome synthesis and sequencing technologies, the study of utilizing DNA molecules as storage media has received increasing attention [2], [3]. DNA, as a molecule encoding biological information, exhibits the advantages of high storage density, ease of replication, and long-term data preservation capabilities [4]. Thus, it is becomes a promising storage medium for archives.
DNA communication differs from binary communication because it involves four processes: encoding, synthesis, nanopore sequencing, and decoding [5], [6]. However, errors such as synchronization errors (including insertions and deletions) and substitutions, can occur during synthesis and sequencing [7]. Consequently, synthesis and nanopore sequencing processes in DNA data storage are modeled as insertion-deletion-substitution (IDS) channels [8]. The IDS error correction is an indispensable challenge for DNA data storage’s low bit-error rate (BER) transmission.
To achieve reliable storage, researchers have developed error-correcting codes in DNA data storage [9], [10]. Classical approaches introduced the finite-domain-based Bose-Chaudhuri-Hocquenghem (BCH) codes and ReedSolomon (RS) codes [11] into DNA storage. Others addressed the IDS errors by combining low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [12], [13] with watermarked, trellis, time-varying, and local interpolation synchronous coding [14], [15], [16]. However, these methods suffer from high decoding complexity with limited performance improvement. Furthermore, the multiple sequence alignment (MSA) algorithm analyzes homologous sequences of biological information to detect and correct gene errors [17], [18], [19]. By leveraging the easy replication of DNA storage, MSA infers near-consistent sequences from the multiple output sequences of the IDS channels [20]. DNA data storage comprises the following stages: encoding, IDS channels, and decoding, among which synthesis and sequencing happen in IDS channels. Mapping and segmentation operations are applied after encoding to prepare for synthesis and sequencing. The choice of decoding method is contingent upon the specific encoding scheme employed. The overall architecture of DNA data storage is shown in Fig. 1 and described in detail as follows.
1) Encoding: We apply two cascade encoding schemes to support IDS error correction: One employs LDPC code as external code in conjunction with MC code as internal code. The other utilizes EMC for internal code, followed by LDPC code for external code. These encoding schemes influence coding efficiency and yield varying decoding methods.
2) Mapping and Segmentation: The encoded binary sequence is mapped to a DNA sequence comprising four base symbols (A, T, C, and G). Current synthesis techniques can only handle short sequences of up to two hundred base symbols. Consequently, long DNA sequences must be segmented into shorter ones before synthesis.
3) Synthesis and Sequencing (IDS Channels): To model the probability of IDS errors during both synthesis and nanopore sequencing, we introduced the IDS channels in DNA data storage. Leveraging DNA’s ease of replication, we obtain multiple output sequences through parallel IDS channels.
4) Decoding: The decoding process includes SPM algorithm, internal decoding (also known as synchronous decoding) and external decoding. We use the SPM algorithm to compute a consensus sequence from multiple output sequences. If MC is the inner code, the SDH algorithm performs synchronous decoding followed by normalized min-sum (NMS) concatenation decoding. If EMC is the inner code, the IED algorithm integrates synchronous decoding with ENMS in an iterative manner. C. IDS Channel Model
To address the insertion, deletion, and substitution errors that may occur during the synthesis and sequencing processes, we use the IDS channel model to simulate these processes,

as illustrated in Fig. 2(a). Let w = (w1, w2, . . . , wl ) be a sequence of information to be transmitted over the channel and y = (y1, y2, . . . , yl′ ) be the received sequence. If the input symbol wi is queued for transmission over the channel, there are four cases at the received symbol upon entering the
channel. 1) An insertion event occurs with a probability of pi , where uniformly random symbols a ∈ {A, T, C, G} are appended to the received symbol yi . In this case, the input symbol wi remains in the input channel and corresponds to the received symbol yi+1. 2) The probability that the input symbol wi is deleted is pd , in which case no symbol is received and wi+1 is transmitted subsequently. 3) The input symbol wi is successfully transmitted with a probability of pt = 1 − pi − pd . However, there are also substitution errors with a probabil-
ity of ps. The probability that the input symbol is successfully transmitted and correctly received is therefore given by pt · (1 − ps), and the received symbol is yi = wi . 4) The substitution errors occur with the probability of pt · ps. In this case, a uniform random symbol a, where a ̸= wi , is received. The transmission of the input sequence finishes when the channel receives the last symbol wl . Note that the length of the output sequence y varies based on the deletion probability
pd and the insertion probability pi . In nanopore sequencing [12], there is a regional overlap
between the base symbols A, T, C, and G. This results in a high
probability of substitution errors of base symbols. The error transfer probability is illustrated in Fig. 2(b). Let β represent the parameter of nanopore sequencing, and p1, p2, and p3 denote the substitution error probability between base symbols. The relationship between them can be expressed as p1 = β, p2 = 3β, and p3 ≈ 0, with β ∈ (0, 1/3).

III. PROPOSED IDS ERROR CORRECTION ALGORITHMS
This section covers optimizing cascading decoding algorithms for multiple output sequences of IDS channels. First, we introduce the SPM algorithm to exploit the consensus information of multiple output sequences. Next, we synchronize the consensus sequence using the SDH decoding algorithm to get the APP of base symbols. Last, when using the EMC as the inner code, We propose the ENMS algorithm to correct the substitution errors, and a joint iteration of the internal and external decoders with the IED algorithm to compute the APP and LLR of the sequence more accurately.

A. SPM Algorithm
MSA techniques are frequently employed in biology to reconstruct the original sequence when presented with multiple corrupted sequences. The most commonly utilized MSA algorithm is the progressive matching (PM) algorithm proposed by T-Coffee and MUSCLE. Given the limitations of current synthesis techniques in sequence length, it is necessary to segment the transmitted long sequence into m short sequences. We propose the SPM algorithm, which first clusters and performs progressive matching to obtain short consensus sequences and splice them into long ones. The SPM algorithm, designed for short sequence comparisons, tackles the synchronization challenge in nanopore sequencing and remarkably simplifies the process of matching multiple sequences.

C. IED Algorithm

With an EMC as the inner code, the positions and LLR

values of the outer and inner codes influence each other.

We propose the IED algorithm that cascades synchronous

decoding with LDPC decoding and iterative exchanges of the

soft information. Concurrently, the embedded marker bits are

part of the LDPC information bits, with their position and LLR

value being determinable. According to this encoding scheme,

we introduce a low-complexity embedded-normalized min-

sum (ENMS) decoding algorithm for LDPC external decoding.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we propose efficient IDS error correction algorithms, addressing the issue of a DNA sequence through multiple parallel IDS channel outputs. First, we propose the SPM decoding algorithm for multiple output sequences to obtain a consensus sequence for decoding. Subsequently, the SPM algorithm connects to a synchronous decoder to identify the optimal base symbol APP. Moreover, the SDH algorithm is the foundation of the IED algorithm, corresponding to the internal coding schemes of MC and EMC. The IED algorithm achieves a lower BER by refining soft information iteratively
