Abstract: We demonstrate a low complexity LLR estimation method based on piecewise polynomial fitting for LDPC decoding in 100Gb/s bandwidth-limited flexible-rate IM/DD system, which reduces complexity by more than 90% while maintaining almost equivalent performance. Keywords: LLR estimation, LDPC decoding, Flexible-rate, IM/DD systems

INTRODUCTION
The rapid growth of 4K/8K video and cloud computing is driving passive optical networks (PONs) toward 100 Gb/s transmission, with two key technologies emerging: intensity modulation/direct detection (IM/DD) and coherent PON. While coherent solutions offer superior sensitivity and dispersion compensation, IM/DD has remained competitive in developing to 100G-PON through cost efficiency [1]. As an interim step toward evolving 50G-PON [2] to 100 Gb/s, flexible-rate IM/DD systems have been proposed in [3-5]. These systems maximize net rates under dynamic channel conditions by adapting forward error correction (FEC) code rates and modulation formats (non-return-to-zero or 4-level pulse amplitude modulation, NRZ/PAM4). A flexible-rate PON prototype demonstrated in [6-8] achieves 100 Gb/s with a total link budget of 31.5 dB – defined as the maximum allowable loss between transmitter power and receiver sensitivity, including fiber and connector losses.
In flexible-rate systems using low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [9] as FEC, achieving reliable soft-decision decoding with log-likelihood ratios (LLR) requires balancing two key factors: LLR accuracy for effective error correction and computation complexity for real-time operation. [10-13] explore this balance through different approaches: the machine learning method in [10] delivers near-optimal performance but requires significant computing power, whereas the Taylor series in [13] simplifies calculations with acceptable accuracy loss, though needing manual parameter adjustments. While these solutions show promise in simulations, their real-time performance remains to be tested.
In this paper, we simplify LLR Calculation from [13] by using extremum detection to automatically set key parameters that used to require manual tuning. Our piecewise polynomial fitting (PPF) LLR estimation method speeds up calculations while keeping accuracy, and works without human adjustments. By modeling the nonlinear relationship between received signal amplitudes and LLR values, our method reduces computational overhead by over 90%. Experimental validation in 100 Gb/s bandwidth-limited flexible-rate IM/DD system demonstrates negligible bit error rate (BER) differences (less than 1  10) between the PPF and conventional methods, with total link budget variations constrained below 0.1 dB.

EXPERIMENTAL SETUP
The experimental setup is shown in Fig. 1. A 1310 nm distributed feedback (DFB) laser generates a continuous optical carrier, which is fed into a 25 GHz Mach-Zehnder modulator (MZM). An electrical signal produced by a 25 GHz arbitrary waveform generator (AWG, 65 GSa/s) is amplified via a 45 GHz broadband electrical amplifier (EA, gain of 27 dB) to drive MZM for intensity modulation (NRZ/PAM4). Before being detected by a 20 GHz photodetector (PD), the modulated optical signal (8 dBm launch power) is transmitted through 20 km standard single-mode fiber (SSMF) to add dispersion and attenuated by a variable optical attenuator (VOA). The electrical signal out from PD is then captured by a 30 GHz digital oscilloscope (OSC, 80 GSa/s). Finally, the waveform data is processed offline in MATLAB.

Fig.1. Experiment schematics
Offline DSP is comprised of two main components: the transmitter (Tx) and the receiver (Rx). At Tx, a PRBS 15 binary sequence generated by MATLAB is encoded using the same FEC scheme as specified for 50G-PON (ITU-T G9804.2) [15], based on the 25G-EPON (IEEE 802.3ca) LDPC mother code (17664,14592) [16]. The encoded bit stream (11520 bits per frame) is subsequently mapped to NRZ/PAM4 modulation formats. After applying pre-equalization to compensate for bandwidth limitation, the data is resampled to match the AWG sampling rate. Before being converted into electrical signals, it is also formed by a root-raised cosine pulse shaping with a roll-off factor of 0.98. The actual symbol rate of the AWG output remains at 50 GBaud, both NRZ and PAM4. At Rx, waveform data captured by OSC is resampled to 1 bit/symbol. After a 31-tap FFE and a 5-tap DFE, the equalized signal undergoes LLR calculation using both the PPF and conventional methods. The resulting soft information is then fed into the LDPC decoder, which employs the sum-product algorithm (SPA) with 10 iterations. Finally, post-FEC BER statistics are collected.

Although minor fitting deviations exist at specific points (e.g., the gap between the blue curve and the circle at   1 in Fig. 2(d)), these errors remain within the error-correcting capacity of LDPC codes, thus ensuring no impact on decoding performance.

While preserving the total link budget, the PPF LLR estimation approach reduces the LLR computation complexity

per bit by more than 90%. This is because the conventional method employs fifth-order Taylor expansions to approximate

exponential and logarithmic operations, with each operation requiring 5 additions and 15 multiplications. Consequently,

LLR calculation per bit demands 21 additions and 56 multiplications. In contrast, the PPF method directly computes LLR values through quadratic polynomials (′      ), requiring only 2 additions and 3 multiplications per bit. This achieves 90.47% reduction in additions and 94.64% reduction in multiplications, resulting in overall computation

savings exceeding 90%.

CONCLUSION
This paper demonstrates a bandwidth-limited flexible-rate IM/DD system with a maximum bit rate of 100 Gb/s and a net rate up to 88.89 Gb/s, and verifies the feasibility of using PPF LLR estimation method. The experimental results show that, compared with the conventional method, the PPF method can ensure that the total link budget does not decrease by more than 0.1 dB. In practical application scenarios, a 0.1 dB power fluctuation is usually negligible. Additionally, this method can significantly reduce the LLR calculation complexity by over 90%. Future research should further explore the effect of this method in hardware deployment.
