Abstract—NAND flash memory is a pivotal technology in contemporary data storage, but its reliability diminishes due to retention noise and wear-out effects. This paper proposes a novel decoding optimization for Quasi-Cyclic Low-Density Parity-Check (QC-LDPC) codes tailored for NAND flash memory. The methodology incorporates Lookup Table (LUT)-based reconstruction and quantization, complemented by adaptive read threshold adjustments at the End-of-Life (EoL) stage. Key contributions include a Reconstruction Computation Quantization (RCQ) decoder, which achieves reduced computational and routing complexity through a two-stage optimization process, and a dynamic read threshold adjustment mechanism addressing layerto-layer variation to improve error correction under evolving threshold voltage distributions. Simulation results demonstrate enhanced reliability and performance, with improvements in Raw Bit Error Rate (RBER) of over 0.004 and a throughput-to-area ratio increase of up to 16%. Additionally, the proposed approach extends the endurance of TLC 3D NAND memory by over 30% at a fixed error probability compared to single-layer model and 12% compared to the Neural Network model. This work offers a step forward in improving SSD reliability at the EoL stage.
Keywords—NAND flash memory; LDPC codes; QC-LDPC; LUT-based decoding; read threshold adjustment; error correction, retention noise; end-of-life (EoL); dynamic thresholding;
I. INTRODUCTION
NAND flash memory underpins modern data storage, spanning applications from portable devices to enterprise-grade systems. Its advantages, including high storage density, low power usage, and rapid access speeds, have positioned it as a cornerstone in solid-state drives (SSDs). However, as fabrication technologies advance and memory cells shrink, error rates increase due to program/erase (PE) wear-out, retention noise, and cell-to-cell interference (C2CI) [1].
Retention noise, caused by charge leakage in floating gates, is a predominant error source in multi-level cell (MLC) and triple-level cell (TLC) NAND flash memory [2]. The limited resolution of read threshold levels further exacerbates this issue, leading to overlapping voltage distributions and elevated error rates [3]. Advanced error correction codes (ECCs), such as Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes, have been widely adopted to mitigate these challenges. LDPC codes enable softdecision decoding, leveraging log-likelihood ratios (LLRs) for superior error correction [4]. However, as threshold voltage distributions evolve due to retention noise, conventional static

thresholds fall short, necessitating dynamic adaptation. Recent advancements include Adaptive Read Threshold (ART) methods, which adjust read levels in response to retention noise, enhancing error correction without additional read cycles [5]. Similarly, Retention-Aware Belief-Propagation (RABP) decoding utilizes retention noise characteristics to adjust LLRs, improving decoding accuracy [6]. While detailed vendorspecific IBIS models combined with Verilog behavioral models [7], incorporating industry interface standards (ONFI, Toggle, [8], [9]) provide valuable insights into NAND flash memory behavior at the device level, they are insufficient for high-level prediction of optimal controller parameters. This limitation underscores the critical need for a comprehensive mathematical model of Flash memory to enhance design efficiency: enabling increased cost-efficiency through reduced reliance on time-consuming low-level simulations; facilitating analysis across diverse flash technologies without requiring specific IBIS models for each variant, and accelerating prediction of performance improvements through mathematical analysis. This paper introduces a novel framework addressing these challenges by focusing on LUT-optimized decoding of QCLDPC codes specifically tailored for NAND flash memory nearing its EoL. By integrating LUT-based reconstruction and quantization with dynamic read threshold adjustments, the proposed approach achieves significant reliability improvements. Key contributions include: 1) Application of the Reconstruction Computation Quantization (RCQ) decoder for 3D NAND flash memory leveraging pre-computed LUTs to map received signals to LLR values, thereby reducing memory usage and routing complexity; and 2) A dynamic read threshold adjustment mechanism that adapts to retention noise-induced threshold voltage variations, effectively addressing layer-tolayer heterogeneity inherent in TLC NAND flash memory.
The remainder of this paper is structured as follows: Section II introduces QC-LDPC codes and trapping set pseudocodewords. Section III describes the RCQ layered decoder and its implementation. Section IV discusses the Layer-to-Layer Variation Model for TLC NAND memory. Section V presents simulation results, including LUT construction for quantization and reconstruction. Finally, Section VI concludes with insights and future research directions for enhancing NAND flash memory reliability.

Fine-tuning stage
The optimized values for the R and Q tables, derived from the proposed method, are shown in Tables I and II. During the initial iteration of the message-passing decoder for quasicyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes with a girth g > 4, the decoding process operates without forming cycles. This allows the Density Evolution (DE)-optimized tables to provide near-optimal initial values for the quantized decoder’s R and Q tables. However, after approximately ⌊g/4⌋ iterations, the decoder begins to encounter cycles and trapping sets, which adversely affect performance. This results in the initial optimization becoming suboptimal for subsequent layers of the decoding process. To address this challenge, two strategies are employed for fine-tuning the R and Q tables.
Mutual Information-Based Optimization. This method, as proposed by Kurkovskiy, aims to maximize the mutual information between the high-resolution bit-wise decoder and the quantized layered min-sum decoder with non-linear framing (compression) functions. The R and Q tables are refined using Monte Carlo simulations to estimate the probability density functions (PDFs) of the decoder’s outputs. The optimization ensures that the quantized decoder retains as much information as possible from the high-resolution decoder. Key references for this approach include [17]–[30].
Error-Floor Fine-Tuning Optimization. This strategy focuses on optimizing the R and Q tables to mitigate the error floor at the target signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). By employing importance sampling techniques to simulate rare error events that contribute to the error floor, the tables can be adjusted to enhance decoder performance under practical operating conditions. Relevant references include [11], [31]–[42]. Both methods provide effective means of improving the performance of RCQ decoders. They address key challenges such as reducing the error floor and optimizing mutual information retention in quantized decoders. DE-Optimized Tables I, II presents the optimized Q, R values.

Two major factors affecting 3D NAND flash memory reliability are early retention loss and layerto-layer process variation. Early retention loss occurs as the charge stored in memory cells diminishes over time, shifting the threshold voltage distribution toward lower states. This phenomenon is particularly pronounced in 3D NAND flash memory, where substantial charge loss can occur within minutes after programming. The early retention noise is modeled as a Gaussian distribution: pnr(v) ∼ N (µr, σr2),where µr and σr represent the mean and standard deviation of the noise, respectively. The vertical stacking in 3D NAND flash memory introduces process variations between layers, resulting in distinct error characteristics for each layer. For the k-th layer, the error distribution is modeled as: pnlk (v) ∼ N (µlk , σl2k ),where µlk and σlk denote the mean and standard deviation for the k-th layer. Experimental analysis reveals that these parameters vary across layers and can be approximated using polynomial functions, with the first layer serving as the reference. The combined voltage distribution for each storage state is computed as the convolution of the initial voltage distribution with the other noise sources. Proposed Model for 3D NAND Layer Variation
To improve modeling accuracy, we propose replacing the cubic polynomial with a refined model that incorporates exponential decay to better capture retention loss and its variation across layers. The proposed model for variance Bk in layer k is:
Bk = (γk · exp(−βkt) + ζk) · A2 + τk · A + ξk, (4)
where γk, βk, ζk, τk, and ξk are layer-specific constants. The term γk · exp(−βkt) captures the exponential decay of charge over time t, with βk representing the rate of decay for layer k. The constant ζk adjusts the baseline decay for each layer. The polynomial terms τk · A + ξk model the residual dependence on A for greater flexibility. This hybrid approach provides a more accurate representation of early retention loss and layerspecific variations in 3D NAND flash memory, addressing both temporal and process-dependent error sources effectively.

V. SIMULATION RESULTS
Simulation experiments were conducted under the following conditions: 12 decoding iterations, QC-LDPC code parameters: protograph size 12 × 80, circulant size 128, degree distribution: λ(x) = 0.0125x2 + 0.9875x3, ρ(x) = 0.41667x25 + 0.58333x26. Key evaluations included optimizing the quantization table Q and the reconstruction table R. The proposed quantized decoder demonstrate, improved reliability, over 0.04 RBER gain and throughput-to-area ratio improvement up to a 16% increase due to reduced routing complexity, Fig. 1. The Fig. 2 compares the reliability improvements across various models and methodologies, highlighting the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The layer evaluation considered 3D NAND flash specifications with 128 (active) and 141 (full) layers, with a storage density of 8.54 Gb/mm2, WL pitch of 58 nm, BL pitch of 39 nm, auxiliary supply voltage of 1.2/1.8 V, and core supply voltage of 3.3/2.5 V. Compared to state-of-the-art solutions after 106 seconds the proposed solution, improved neural network-based method (Multi-Layer Perceptron, MLP) TLC flash reliability by 12%. VI. FUTURE WORK
This work lays a foundation for understanding fundamental characteristics of NAND flash cells. Future research will delve deeper into the nuances of write and read threshold voltage (Vth) histograms to elucidate noise mechanisms, wear-out

By comparing write and read threshold histograms, we aim to assess the susceptibility of stored data to bit-flips induced by wear-out mechanisms. The impact of neighboring cell interactions on individual cell behavior under varying Vth conditions will be rigorously investigated. VII. CONCLUSION
This work introduces the RCQ layered decoder, which enhances decoding efficiency through pre-computed reconstruction and quantization tables. By significantly reducing computational complexity and memory consumption, the RCQ decoder maintains robust error-correction performance, even in high-noise environments. Key improvements include optimized R and Q tables maximize mutual information, enhanced reliability of TLC flash memory by up to 30% through layervariation matching and RCQ decoders. These advancements contribute to the reliable operation of 3D NAND flash memory systems, ensuring their suitability for high-performance, highdensity storage applications.
