LDPC Decoding via Processing-in-Memory for Mobile and Wireless Communications
Mojtaba Mahdavi Ericsson Research, Ericsson, Lund, Sweden
mojtaba.mahdavi@Ericsson.com

Abstract—Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are widely used in modern communication systems, including 5G networks, due to their near-capacity error correction performance. However, conventional LDPC decoding implementations suffer from high memory bandwidth demands and computational complexity, particularly in high-throughput applications. Processingin-memory (PIM) has emerged as a promising computational paradigm to overcome the memory bottleneck by integrating computation within memory, thereby reducing data movement overhead. In this paper, we explore the realization of LDPC decoding using PIM architectures, where LDPC decoding computations are mapped onto arrays of memristor devices. Analytical results demonstrate that our proposed PIM-based LDPC decoder significantly improves throughput and latency compared to conventional architectures, highlighting the potential of PIM for next-generation error correction systems.
Keywords—Low-density parity-check (LDPC), Wireless Communication, Channel Coding, Signal Processing, 5G/6G Systems, Processing In Memory (PIM).

To address these challenges, we propose a processingin-memory (PIM)-based LDPC decoder that integrates computational operations within memory, thereby reducing data movement overhead and improving hardware efficiency [18]– [23]. Our approach maps LDPC decoding computations onto arrays of memristor devices, enabling parallel processing while significantly lowering latency and power consumption. By leveraging the inherent advantages of PIM, such as reduced memory bandwidth requirements and increased throughput, our proposed decoder provides a scalable and efficient solution for next-generation mobile and wireless systems. Analytical results demonstrate that the PIM-based LDPC decoder outperforms conventional architectures in terms of computational efficiency, latency, and throughput, highlighting its potential for future wireless networks and edge computing applications.

I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have become a fundamental component of modern error correction techniques due to their near-Shannon-limit performance in ensuring reliable data transmission [1], [2]. Originally introduced by Gallager, LDPC codes have gained widespread adoption in various high-speed communication standards, including 5G new radio (NR), Wi-Fi (IEEE 802.11ax), and satellite communications [3], [4]. Their ability to provide robust error correction while maintaining high spectral efficiency makes them ideal for applications requiring ultra-reliable and low-latency communication (URLLC), such as autonomous vehicles, industrial automation, and next-generation wireless networks [4]–[8]. Despite their advantages, implementing LDPC decoding efficiently remains a significant challenge due to the algorithm’s iterative nature and high computational complexity [9], [10].
Conventional LDPC decoding implementations primarily rely on digital signal processors (DSPs), application-specific integrated circuits (ASICs), and graphics processing units (GPUs) to achieve the required performance [11]–[13]. While these architectures can deliver high throughput, they are constrained by excessive memory access requirements and energy consumption, particularly in high-data-rate scenarios [14], [15]. The frequent data transfer between processing units and memory introduces latency and power inefficiencies, creating a significant performance bottleneck [16], [17]. II. PROCESSING IN MEMORY (PIM)
Analog memristive devices represent a cutting-edge technology capable of both data storage and in-memory computation within the analog domain [23], [24]. Unlike conventional architectures, these devices process data directly at the storage site, an approach known as processing-in-memory (PIM) [25]. By eliminating the need for continuous data transfers between memory and a separate processing unit, PIM significantly enhances efficiency and reduces energy consumption. Memristive devices come in various forms, each differing in material composition, switching mechanisms, and operational characteristics [26]–[28]. Among the most widely used are phasechange memory (PCM) and resistive random-access memory (ReRAM) [29]. Despite their advantages, memristors face limitations in bit precision due to a finite number of reliably programmable conductance levels. For example, a device with 32 discrete conductance states can store 5 bits.
A large number of memristors can be organized into an analog crossbar array [20], [30], [31], as illustrated in Fig. 1. The crossbar is typically integrated with peripheral components such as analog-to-digital converters (ADCs) and digitalto-analog converters (DACs) to enable seamless interaction with digital domain [32]. One of the key strengths of crossbars lies in their ability to efficiently perform large-scale multiplyaccumulate (MAC) operations in parallel. Specifically, they execute matrix-vector multiplication (MVM) by computing the dot product between an input vector, applied to the crossbar rows, and the conductance values stored in each column. III. PROPOSED PIM-BASED LDPC DECODING SCHEME
This paper presents a novel approach for implementing LDPC decoding schemes by leveraging PIM technique with multiple arrays of memristors. We demonstrate how LDPC decoding using belief propagation can be efficiently executed through iterative matrix multiplication and addition operations. The step-by-step procedure of the proposed memristor-based LDPC decoding scheme is illustrated in Fig. 2 and further explained in the following subsections. As shown in Fig. 2, in the proposed method, the decoding process starts by transforming channel decoding computations into matrix-vector and vector-vector multiplications and additions. Next, multiple sets of memristor devices are programmed with the appropriate values, as detailed below. The core computations for channel decoding are then executed using the intrinsic properties of memristors, applying Ohm’s law and Kirchhoff’s current law to generate the decoded data.
As a common practice in the context of LDPC codes, Tanner graph is used to represent LDPC codes. In Tanner graph two sets of nodes—variable nodes (corresponding to codeword bits) and check nodes (representing parity-check constraints)—are connected based on the parity-check matrix (H) of the code. An edge between a variable node and a check node exists if the corresponding entry in H is 1. An example of this concept is depicted in Fig. 3. Belief propagation (BP) or message passing algorithms use the Tanner graph structure to iteratively update and exchange probability-based messages between nodes, enabling efficient decoding of LDPC codes with near-optimal error correction performance.
Step 1: Initialization
The first step is to compute the Log-Likelihood Ratio (LLR), a fundamental concept in LDPC decoding. LLR plays a crucial role in belief propagation algorithms, facilitating the exchange of messages between nodes in a Tanner graph during the decoding process. The LLR value for each bit is defined as the logarithm of the ratio of the probabilities that the bit is 0 or 1, given the received noisy signal. IV. DESIGN COMPARISON AND KEY FEATURES
Several design proposals have been investigated for LDPC decoder implementations in literature. Table I presents a comparative analysis of the proposed PIM-based architecture alongside conventional approaches. To ensure a fair and technology-independent evaluation, latency and throughput are measured in cycles, used as primary performance metrics. Section II discusses the role of DAC/ADC integration, which varies by application; however, their characteristics are not included in the design comparison in Table I.
1) Supporting Binary and Non-Binary Codes: The proposed scheme is capable of realizing both binary and nonbinary codes, offering greater flexibility compared to existing designs in the literature, which are often limited to binary implementations. By leveraging the PIM technique, the architecture efficiently handles higher-order Galois field (GF) operations, enabling support for non-binary LDPC and other advanced coding schemes. This adaptability enhances error correction performance, making the design suitable for a wide range of communication standards and applications.
2) Ultra Low-Latency and High-Throughput: Overcoming extended critical paths in traditional LDPC decoding, especially for large code lengths, is key to improving throughput. Techniques like pipelining and register insertion help but increase latency. In contrast, the proposed PIM-based method leverages crossbar parallelism and fast read cycles to achieve high throughput and low latency. As highlighted in Table I, our design surpasses existing methods in both performance aspects. V. CONCLUSION
This paper presented a PIM-based approach for LDPC
decoding, where decoding operations were reformulated as
matrix-vector multiplications. These computations were effi-
ciently mapped onto memristor-based crossbars, significantly
reducing data movement and enabling parallel processing.
The proposed architecture effectively minimizes both latency
and energy consumption, showcasing its scalability and hard-
ware efficiency for future communication systems. This work
highlights the potential of PIM to address the challenges of
traditional LDPC decoding, offering a promising solution for
next-generation high-performance wireless networks.
