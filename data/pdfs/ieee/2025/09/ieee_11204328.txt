ABSTRACT
We consider the use of polar codes in edge computing and communications applications, and to achieve energy efficiency, we propose implementation of neural network-based decoders for them using analog in-memory computing with memristors. This approach is particularly suited for batteryconstrained edge devices, where the energy requirements of conventional deep learning-based channel decoders are prohibitive. We argue that integration of memristors into neural network-based successive cancellation decoders for polar codes may offer a promising approach for energy efficiency. Our results demonstrate that memristor-based NSC decoders achieve good decoding performance, although some degradation occurs over time due to conductance drift and noise in memristors. We note that the use of memristors can also be extended for decoding other types of channel codes that benefit from neural network-based solutions, such as convolutional codes decoded with recurrent neural networks or linear block codes decoded with transformer architectures.
Index Terms— Analog computing, memristors, neural networks, deep learning, in-memory computing, polar codes, neural successive cancellation.
1. INTRODUCTION
The rapid growth of wireless communication technologies has significantly increased the demand for efficient and intelligent signal processing capabilities at the edge [1]. While machine learning (ML) can significantly improve the capabilities of edge devices, deploying it on edge devices poses challenge since they are often energy-constrained, with limited computing capabilities. To address these challenges, combining ML solutions with in-memory analog computing emerges as a promising approach.

In-memory analog computing performs computations directly within memory, enabling highly parallel and energyefficient data processing. A key device advancing in-memory computing is the memristor, proposed in [2]. Memristors come in various forms such as metal-oxide, phase-change memory (PCM), and resistive RAM (ReRAM). Arranged in crossbar arrays, they efficiently perform matrix–vector multiplications (MVMs) making them well-suited as neural network accelerators.
At the same time, there has recently been a growing interest in integrating neural network (NN) based decoders for modern channel codes. A notable example is the neural successive cancellation (NSC) decoder [3], which integrates neural networks into successive cancellation (SC) stages to reduce latency.
In this paper, we propose the implementation of NSC for polar codes via analog computations using memristors. While analog computations introduce errors, which become worse as time progresses, we show that these errors do not preclude the use of neural networks in decoding. We focus on PCM and use statistical models to simulate its behavior during neural network based decoding in a software environment. The adopted model, specifically designed for simulating memristive neural networks during inference [4], accounts for the non-ideal characteristics of analog in-memory computing by using statistical models that represent conductance variations as normally distributed random variables, with parameters estimated from empirical measurements. We replace all layers of the NSC decoder with analog equivalents and perform MVMs using simulated memristors. Our results show that PCM-based analog NSC decoders maintain strong performance despite degradation over time. This study extends the initial work introduced in [5].
While we focus on decoding of polar codes, we note that other channel codes, which can be decoded with NNs, can also be implemented using memristors. For instance, convolutional codes can be decoded with recurrent neural network (RNN)-based architectures employing gated recurrent units (GRU) and long short term memory (LSTM) units [6]. Therefore, their NN-based decoders can also be implemented using

For a detailed explanation of how this decoder operates, we refer readers to [12].
SC decoders are serialized and may suffer from increased implementation complexity. On the other hand, one-shot decoders for polar codes have proven to be ineffective for long codewords. Therefore, combinations of different decoding techniques may be necessary. With this motivation, researchers have integrated neural networks with SC decoders, resulting in the NSC decoder in [3]. The NSC decoder leverages the parallelization capabilities of NNs to accelerate the decoding process while retaining the decoding capabilities of the original SC decoder.

When integrated into earlier stages, NNs have shown strong errorcorrecting capabilities since each network predicts a smaller number of information bits. In contrast, placing them in later stages is favorable for low-latency applications, as it enables the NSC decoder to make hard decisions more quickly, albeit with a significantly higher error rate compared to NSC decoders with neural networks embedded in earlier stages. One can choose where to place the networks based on these tradeoffs. As extreme examples, NNs put in stage Ocorrespond to the original SC decoder, and NNs put in stage log2 N correspond to the direct one-shot decoding of polar codes without SC.

3.3. Memristor Based NSC
Building on the NSC architecture described above, we now describe how it can be realized with analog in-memory computing using memristors. We enhance the NSC decoder architecture by replacing its NN layers with memristor-based in-memory computing blocks. This allows MVMs to be performed directly within memory using crossbar arrays, reducing data movement and improving energy efficiency and latency, key advantages for edge applications [13].
Multilayer perceptrons (MLPs) are adopted for the neural networks within the NSC decoder, as they have been shown to effectively decode sufficiently short codewords [14]. However, as the codeword length increases, decoding with a single MLP becomes impractical. The NSC decoder addresses this by partitioning the codeword and employing multiple small MLPs to decode segments of it. This modular approach enables the use of MLPs, which is why they are chosen for the NSC decoder architecture.
All neural networks within the NSC decoder are implemented as multilayer perceptrons (MLPs) with N h hidden layers. They share the same architecture hyperparameters, including the hidden layer sizes. The ith hidden layer has size hi for i E { 1, ... , N h}, forming the hidden layer size vector h = ( h1, ... , h N h). The weight matrices are denoted by {W1, ... , W Nh-d, where the weight matrix of the ith layer has dimensions (hi, hi+ 1) for i E {1, ... , Nh - 1}.

5. CONCLUSIONS
Polar codes with small lengths can be effectively decoded with NSCs leveraging the advantages of SC decoders and NNs. On the other hand, analog in-memory computing with memristors has been shown to be effective for real-time ap-

plications. In this work, we studied decoding of polar codes NSC implemented via memristors. Experiments with the analog NSC decoder for polar codes highlight its potential benefits, achieving error-rate performance that is competitive with conventional NN-based decoding approaches.
Further research could explore the development of NNbased decoder for other channel codes implemented using memristors. While the current approach utilizes MLPs, decoders for convolutional codes often incorporate bidirectional GRUs (BI-GRUs), which can also be implemented using memristor-based architectures. Additionally, LDPC codes can be decoded using MLPs that mimic the sum-product algorithm, which can also be mapped to analog memristor implementations. Transformer architectures have also been introduced for decoding various channel codes, expanding the potential applications of memristor-based decoder implementations.
