Ordered Reliability Bits Guessing Codeword Decoding of Short Codes
Qianfan Wang , Member, IEEE, Yiwen Wang , Xiangping Zheng , Student Member, IEEE, and Xiao Ma , Member, IEEE

Abstract—In this letter, we investigate the guessing codeword decoding (GCD) algorithm and the guessing noise decoding (GND) algorithm for short codes, revealing the universality (in terms of code rates) and efficiency (in terms of search numbers) of GCD over GND. As inspired by the ordered reliability bits guessing random additive noise decoding (ORBGRAND), we propose the ORB-GCD and the ORB ordered statistic decoding with local constraints (ORB-LC-OSD) for practical applications. For early termination, we introduce an integer-based dynamic approximate ideal (DAI) termination criterion. Simulation results show that: 1) With a limited maximum number of guesses, GCD offers better performance and lower complexity over GND in the high code rate region, which is widely accepted as the region suitable for the GND, and 2) ORB-GCD exhibits universality for various code rates and requires a smaller search number compared to ORBGRAND.
Index Terms—Guessing codeword decoding (GCD), ordered statistic decoding (OSD), ordered reliability bits guessing random additive noise decoding (ORBGRAND), locally constrained OSD (LC-OSD).
I. INTRODUCTION
U LTRA-RELIABLE and low-latency communication (URLLC) and massive machine-type communication (mMTC) are two major scenarios in 5G, which impose stringent requirements on reliability and latency [1]. In 6G, the potential hyper-reliable and low-latency communication (HRLLC) scenarios are expected to impose even more stringent demands on reliability and latency [2]. To support these scenarios, there is an urgent need for highperformance and universal decoding tailored to short codes, achieving maximum likelihood (ML) or near-ML performance.
In [3], the universal ML or near-ML decoding algorithms are distinguished into two classes: the guessing codeword decoding (GCD) and the guessing noise decoding (GND). The GCD generates a list of candidate codewords in a certain order and selects the most likely one as the decoding output. The OSD can be used for any short linear block codes, including LDPC codes, turbo codes, and polar codes, resulting in capacityapproaching performance [6], [7], [8]. Various works have been proposed to improve the OSD algorithm, such as employing polynomial interpolations to construct an expanded basis for BCH codes [9], or utilizing a quasi-reliable basis [10] for binary images of RS codes to avoid Gaussian elimination (GE), and representative OSD for staircase matrix codes to implement GE in parallel [11], [12]. Furthermore, to reduce the number of the re-encoding, several enhanced OSD algorithms have been developed, such as the OSD with local constraints (LCOSD) [13], the segmentation-discarding OSD [14], and the linear-equation OSD [15].
The guessing random additive noise decoding (GRAND) [16], referred to as GND for short, generates a list of noise sequences ordered from most likely to least likely until a valid codeword is found. Variations of GRAND have been developed, including soft-GRAND (SGRAND) [17], GRAND with symbol reliability information [18], and discretized soft GRAND [19]. These GRAND-like algorithms are universal and can be applied to any code (linear or nonlinear) because they do not depend on the code structure but require an efficient membership-verification (e.g., parity-check for linear codes). By exploiting code structure, GRAND can also be integrated with conventional decoders (e.g., SCL-GRAND for polar codes [20]) or improved GRAND using partial constraints (resulting in PC-GRAND [21]). However, due to complexity considerations, GRAND-like algorithms are only suitable for short codes with low or moderate redundancy [22].
It has been both theoretically and numerically demonstrated that, for most practical applications, the GCD is more efficient than the GND in the sense that it requires fewer guesses and each guessing has comparable complexity over the GND [3]. For practical use, efficient integer-based implementations have been developed for GND, leading to the ordered reliability bits GRAND (ORBGRAND) [22]. An immediate question arises: Is it feasible to implement ORB-GCD? The answer is affirmative, with the key being the design of an early termination rule necessary for GCD. The main contributions of this letter are as follows.
1) Inspired by the ORBGRAND [22], we present the ORBGCD and the ORB-LC-OSD. Different from the OSD, which requires online GE for each reception, the GCD only requires offline GE at most once.
2) For early termination, we introduce an integer-based dynamic approximate ideal (DAI) termination criterion, which can efficiently reduce the average number of test error patterns (TEPs) for the integer-based GCD algorithms.

Remark. It is worth noting that the GCD differs from the OSD in the sense that the GCD performs the offline GE at most once, whereas the OSD typically requires performing the GE for each reception of a noisy codeword. Thus, the complexity of transforming H into [I, P] of the GCD is not taken into account when compared with the GND. Also note that, without limited maximum list size, GCD and GND should have the same FER since both are ML decoding [3], [17]. However, it has been proved in [3] that GCD requires a less or equal number of guesses than GND. In other words, with a limited maximum number of TEPs, GCD (referred to as truncated GCD in [26]) can achieve better performance than GND (referred to as SGRANDAB in [17]), especially in the low and medium rate regions.

At the given SNR points, where the main complexity lies in generating TEPs and re-encoding, the ORB-LC-OSD algorithm exhibits lower number of operations over these two decoding algorithms because it operates with a significantly smaller average number of searches. While in the high SNR region, ORB-GCD will become more efficient since it has a similar number of TEPs to that of the ORB-LC-OSD and does not require GE. For all three performance metrics considered, the ORB-LC-OSD exhibits the best performance compared to the ORB-GCD and the ORBGRAND. However, the ORB-LC-OSD requires online GE for each reception. Alternatively, the ORB-GCD offers a better trade-off with moderate performance without the need for online GE.
IV. CONCLUSION
This letter has investigated the GCD and GND for short codes, revealing that, with a limited maximum number of guesses, GCD is applicable to a wide range of code rates and exhibits a lower average number of TEPs compared to GND. Inspired by the ORBGRAND, this letter has proposed the ORB-GCD and the ORB-LC-OSD for practical applications. For early termination, this letter has introduced an efficient integer-based DAI termination criterion. Simulation results reveal that the universality in terms of code rates and efficiency in terms of search numbers of GCD over GND.
