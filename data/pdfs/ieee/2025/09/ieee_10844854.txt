LVDE: A Lightweight Threshold Voltage Distribution Estimation Strategy for
High-Performance 3-D Nand Flash Memory
Zhelong Piao , Debao Wei , Member, IEEE, Huqi Xiang , Liyan Qiao , Member, IEEE, and Xiyuan Peng

Abstract— Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are now broadly employed as error correction code (ECC) solutions in NAND flash memories. With the rapid development of high-performance three-dimensional (3-D) NAND memories, mitigating the read latency caused by LDPC decoding attempts has emerged as an investigation focus. Existing investigations have demonstrated that efficient memory sensing is crucial to enhancing the efficiency of LDPC decoding attempts. The optimal memory sensing parameter options are determined by the threshold voltage distributions of NAND flash, which are difficult to be dynamically extracted during data retention. To address the issue, this article proposes an online lightweight strategy for threshold voltage distribution estimation, named LVDE. LVDE suggests a probe wordline design that uses one-read sampling to measure the threshold voltage distributions during longterm retention. Further, LVDE develops a methodology for the cross-layer estimation of threshold voltage distributions, thereby utilizing the measurements of the probe wordline to estimate those of each layer. LVDE is implemented on real flash chips to compare with numerous state-of-the-art strategies. Experimental results demonstrate that LVDE can significantly enhance the efficiency of LDPC decoding attempts and thus improve the read performance of 3-D NAND flash memory.
Index Terms— 3-D NAND flash memory, threshold voltage distribution, retention loss, optimal read voltage, LDPC code, read performance improvement.
I. INTRODUCTION
THREE-DIMENSIONAL (3-D) NAND flash is now receiving widespread attention as the mainstream non-volatile memory medium. To meet the exploding data storage demands in the consumer market and data centers, the storage capacity and density of 3-D NAND flash memories have been continuously improved in recent years. This development is mainly driven by the high-bit cell and layer stacking techniques [1], [2], [3], [4], [5]. However, advanced process techniques also induce significant data reliability degradation on 3-D NAND flash memories [6], [7], [8]. To address this

issue, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are broadly adopted as the error correction code (ECC) solutions on 3D NAND flash memories [9]. Except for providing a considerable error correction capability of hard decisions, LDPC can further perform soft decisions utilizing the read retry mechanism of modern 3-D NAND flash, thereby significantly enhancing the error correction capacity of a memory channel. Nevertheless, the read retries caused by LDPC soft decisions can significantly degrade the memory read performance [10]. On the other hand, the error correction capability of LDPC hard decision is maximized by applying accurate optimal read voltages (Vopt ), the tracking for which can also induce significant sampling latency [11]. In summary, diminishing the read latency caused by LDPC decoding attempts has emerged as a critical target in developing high-performance 3-D NAND flash memories [12], [13], [14], [15].
High-accuracy memory sensing, used to extract the raw information input to the LDPC decoder, is essential for improving the efficiency of LDPC decoding attempts [16]. The optimal parameters for memory sensing are determined by the threshold voltage (Vth) distributions of NAND flash. However, dynamically estimating the Vth distributions is difficult during data retention [17]. The conventional strategies generally use alternative approaches to estimate the optimal parameters, e.g., offline training a model (mapping table) between the program/erase (P/E) cycles and optimal parameters. When responding to a flash read request, this model (table) is first looked up to determine the optimal parameters based on the current P/E cycle. These approaches achieve a coarse-grained adaptivity of memory sensing to Vth distribution distortions. However, significant inter-block process variation (IBPV) has been demonstrated to exist in 3-D NAND flash memories [18], [19], [20]. When subjected to equal P/E cycles, vulnerable blocks exhibit more Vth distribution distortion due to their sensitivity to P/E cycles, whereas robust blocks exhibit less Vth distribution distortion due to their resilience to P/E cycles. In other words, IBPV causes the actual wear resulting from the P/E cycles to vary from block to block [21], [22]. As a result, a batch-level model or mapping table may cause a deviation between the predicted memory sensing parameters and the actual optimal options. On the other hand, the alternative approaches can generally only present partial memory sensing parameters for LDPC decoding, due to the lack of clear Vth distributions. As a result, few of these strategies can serve as a comprehensive solution for memory sensing.

To address the above issues, this article proposes an online lightweight Vth distribution estimation strategy, named LVDE, for the extraction of optimal memory sensing parameters throughout the LDPC decoding attempts. LVDE utilizes a two-step design. In the first step, the relationship between the cell count increments within each state window and the Vth distribution drifts during long-term retention is investigated. On this basis, LVDE implements the dynamic estimation of Vth distributions on the probe wordline with one read. In the second step, the relationship between the Vth distribution mean and retention length is exploited to implement cross-layer estimation of the Vth distribution drifts, based on which LVDE develops a method to estimate the Vth distributions on each layer utilizing the measurement results of the probe wordline. At the design level, LVDE offers the following advantages: First, the sampling overhead of LVDE only consists of one wordline read, which is appreciable in practical. Second, based on the dynamically estimated complete Vth distributions, LVDE can provide comprehensive optimal memory sensing parameters throughout the LDPC decoding attempts. Third, LVDE is designed to provide efficient memory sensing rather than optimization of LDPC codes. Thus, it can be combined with various existing LDPC decoder designs to improve the memory read performance. Compared with state-of-the-art Vopt tracking designs, LVDE can reduce the average frame error rate (FER) of LDPC hard decision from 61% to 19%. Compared with state-of-the-art soft memory sensing designs, LVDE can reduce the average read latency by 45% for aging NAND flash.
The main contributions of this article are listed as follows. 1) LVDE provides a probe wordline design where the Vth distributions over long-term retention can be measured with one read. 2) Utilizing the measurement results of the probe wordline, LVDE develops a method for estimating the Vth distributions on each layer over long-term retention without additional sampling overhead. 3) LVDE and multiple state-of-the-art designs are implemented on real flash chips for comparison. Experimental results demonstrate that compared with the state-of-theart designs, LVDE can significantly improve the efficiency of LDPC decoding attempts under various flash reliability statuses. The rest of the article is organized as follows. Section II describes the basis and background of this work. Section III elaborates the details of the proposed strategy. A series of evaluations are presented in Section IV, followed by the conclusion in Section V.

A. Basic of 3-D NAND Flash
Now describe the mechanism of 3-D NAND flash implementing data storage from the physical level [6]. The basic unit for 3-D NAND flash implementing data storage is called a cell. As shown in Fig. 1(a), cells are organized into the structure called block, which is the basic unit of data erasure in 3-D NAND flash. The position of a cell in a block is determined

In addition, a certain number of wordlines are arranged side by side parallel to the x-y plane forms a layer. As an example, the cell in Fig. 1(a) is indexed by the N -th cell of the 1st wordline on the 1st layer.
Now illustrate the logical level mechanism by which 3-D NAND flash implements data storage [9]. Modern 3-D NAND flash employ high-bit cell designs to expand storage capacity. Among them, the triple-level-cell (TLC) technique, which stores 3 bits per cell, is currently the most popular design [2], [14]. Therefore, this work is carried out based on a TLC NAND flash model. As shown in Fig. 1(b), the three bits stored in a TLC cell are called the most significant bit (MSB), the center significant bit (CSB), and the least significant bit (LSB), respectively. The flash internal circuits perform the programming and reading of information based on cell threshold voltage (Vth). Specifically, the TLC technique utilizes seven read reference voltages (RRVs) to divide the entire Vth range into eight intervals, called state windows [6]. For a fresh or erased block, all the cells are in the S0 state with negative Vth and the stored data is 111. Programming, i.e., the writing operation, is to raise the cell Vth to a target state window by injecting charges into the cell. Under ideal conditions, the charges in a cell remain constant during data storage, and thus its Vth corresponds to a constant state. The read operation refers to the identification of the cell Vth, which in turn outputs the 3-bit data corresponding to the Vth state. C. Read Latency Issues
Fig. 4 depicts a typical LDPC-based read process for NAND flash memories. Vth sampling is first performed to extract the optimal parameter options for memory sensing A series of progressive LDPC decoding attempts would be triggered if the initial decoding attempt fails, and the read level increases during this process until the error correction succeeds or the read level reaches the upper limit. Therefore, the read latency consists of two parts. The first part is caused by the Vth sampling (step ⃝1 ). The second part is caused by the LDPC decoding attempts (steps ⃝2 , ⃝3 and ⃝4 ).
The read latency issue due to Vth sampling is discussed first. Existing related works mainly focus on Vopt tracking, and thus the following uses the example of Vopt tracker designs to describe how to reduce the read latency caused by Vth sampling. Developing lightweight Vopt trackers has always been a critical objective in flash memory investigations. As shown in Fig. 2, the basic Vopt tracking scheme is to extract the complete Vth distributions by sampling point by point, layer by layer. For advanced high-density flash memory, where blocks are stacked in dense layers and the Vth is partitioned into multiple narrow state windows, the sampling overhead induced by this natural scheme is huge and thus not feasible in practical situations. Advanced Vopt tracer designs generally employ three approaches to reduce the sampling overhead, which are, 1) reducing read retries performed for a single errorprone area [3]; 2) utilizing inter-state estimation of Vopt [13]; and 3) utilizing inter-layer estimation of Vopt [31]. Ttotal is the sum of the latencies from L-step attemps. For step i therein, the decoding latency consists of three parts. Tsensing refers to the data output latency under one set of RRV offsets, where the stored data is read out of the flash array byte by byte. Further, Tsensing is multiplied by the read retry count Nrief to give the total memory sensing latency (step ⃝2 ). Ttransmission is the transmission latency of transferring data from the flash array to the flash controller (step ⃝3 ). Tdecoding is the latency caused by LDPC decoding itself (step ⃝4 ). On the other hand, the performance of LDPC sensing determines its capability for error correction, which in turn influences the read performance of the flash memory. Therefore, the issue of reducing the read latency induced by the LDPC decoding attempts is translated into applying efficient memory sensing.
Applying efficient memory sensing faces 3 major issues. 1) How to determine appropriate read levels for the initial LDPC decoding attempt [10], [12], [30]. Let the actual optimal read levels for the target stored data be Lo and the predicted read levels be L p. If L p<Lo, additional progressive decoding steps from L p+1 to Lo need to be performed, and the data transmission latency and decoding latency of this additional process are unnecessary and can be saved. On the other hand, if L p > Lo, then the sensing latency from the Lo+1 to L p step is redundant, which can also be saved, thereby improving the read performance. In summary, starting the decoding attempts of the LDPC with a more accurate initial read level is essential to reduce the read latency for data access. 2) How to apply accurate read voltage offsets [16], [26], [32]. Here also use Vopt as an example for illustration. Accurate Vopt tracking matters in two ways. If an LDPC decoder starts the decoding attempts with hard decisions, reading data with Vopt can minimize the RBER input to the LDPC decoder and thus improve the success rate of the LDPC hard decisions. Conversely, the probability of triggering LDPC soft decisions would increase, which in turn degrades the memory read performance due to the additional read retries performed. On the other hand, if an LDPC decoder starts the decoding attempts with soft decisions, Vopt as the baseline for all read voltages is also critical to maximize the error correction capability of the LDPC code under various read levels. The offset options for read voltages except for Vopt can cause a similar effect. 3) How to apply an accurate LLR for each bit [9], [27], [29]. Applying accurate LLRs can maximize the error correction capability of an LDPC code with the same read voltages, thereby diminishing the potential decoding attempts. Let the initial read levels be L in two cases, and in the latter case the LDPC has better error correction capability due to applying

accurate LLRs. In the former case, error correction fails with L read levels, and error correction succeeds until using L+ L read levels. In the latter case, the errors are corrected with L read levels. Then case 2 reduces the read latency due to the additional L-step attempts compared with case 1.
The above three issues can be summarized into one, i.e., the Vth distributions under various reliability stages are not clear. As previously mentioned, it is easy to determine the optimal memory sensing parameters with clear Vth distributions. Therefore, the issue of designing an efficient memory sensing strategy can be further converted into estimating the Vth distributions with a lightweight sampling overhead, which is the objective of the proposed LVDE, as shown in Fig. 4.
D. Related Works
There have been solid existing investigations that contribute to the read performance improvement of 3-D NAND flash memories. The following presents a brief description from the perspectives of 3D NAND characteristic investigations and optimization of LDPC hard and soft decision, respectively.
1) 3D NAND Characteristic: Luo et al. observed the phenomena of post-programming fast retention loss and cross-layer variation in Vth distributions on 3-D NAND flash [6]. Wu et al. comprehensively characterized the degradation patterns of various flash attributes over long-term retention [33], [34]. Saikia et al. investigated the Vth drift patterns over long-term retention based on cell-level charge loss simulations [35]. Shim et al. demonstrated that the wordlines within the same layer exhibit almost equivalent reliability characteristics attributed to the strong process similarity among the wordlines [36].
2) LDPC Hard Decision: Zhang et al. proposed to fit the Vth distributions with polynomials so as to track Vopt through the minimum points of the polynomial functions [11]. Li et al. proposed the concepts of sentinel cell and voltage to shave the read retries induced by Vopt tracking [13]. Based on this, Li et al. improved the two-state asynchronous estimation to single-state asynchronous estimation, thereby diminishing the computational overhead and space overhead incurred by algorithm implementation [37]. Ye et al. designed a method that combines the main table retry and proximal search to track Vopt , thus achieving near-zero read retry LDPC hard decoding [31]. Feng et al. investigated the relationship between random flip bit counts and Vopt offsets and proposed a design that tracks Vopt based on two consecutive page reads [38].
3) LDPC Soft Decision: Dong et al. raised the importance of non-uniform read voltages for LDPC soft decisions, where read voltages are concentrated on error-prone areas rather than being placed uniformly over the entire Vth range [16]. Li et al. designed a read level prediction scheme that improves the read performance of 3D NAND flash memory by exploiting the intra-state, inter-state, and inter-layer asymmetry patterns [10]. Du et al. proposed to determine the read levels of the initial LDPC decoding attempt using the cell counts of the errorprone areas [12]. Wang et al. designed an entropy-based dual non-uniform read voltage placement strategy to enhance the error correction capability of LDPC soft decisions [30].

III. THRESHOLD VOLTAGE DISTRIBUTION ESTIMATION
After extracting the Vth distribution parameters at the initial stage of retention, the parameters at any point during the retention period can be dynamically estimated based on the Vth distribution drifts up to that point. The LVDE elaborated in this section is designed to estimate the Vth distribution drifts during a long-term retention period, thereby implementing the Vth distribution estimation. First, the design of the probe wordline is introduced, followed by an elaboration of how to estimate the Vth distribution drifts across each layer using the probe wordline. Finally, the implementation overhead of LVDE is analyzed.
A. Research Motivation
The proposed LVDE exploits the following two Vth distribution characteristics of 3-D NAND flash, which have been demonstrated in a wide range of existing investigations.
Characteristic 1: During a long-term retention period, the Vth drifts across cells with the same state and wordline are uniform [13], [28], [36].
Characteristic 2: The mean of a Vth distribution is logarithmically related to the retention length [6], [31], [35].
B. Probe Wordline Desgin
Fig. 5 depicts how to measure the Vth distributions drift on the probe wordline during a long-term retention period. Let the start and end times of the period be t0 and t1, respectively. The measurement is implemented utilizing the following corollary.
Corollary 1: The distortion of a Vth distribution on one wordline can be considered as a simple leftward drift over long-term retention.
The explanation for Corollary 1 is provided below. As described by the first characteristic, the Vth drifts are uniform across cells of the same state on a wordline during long-term retention. Thus, the amplitude and variance of each Vth distribution remain constant for this wordline, while only the means move leftward as retention length increases.
Furthermore, after tuning each RRV to the right with an appropriate step, the cell count of the Si state window, denoted as Ni (t), is only dependent on the Vth distributions of states Si and Si+1 and is independent of the remaining Vth distributions. Therefore, the Vth distribution drifts can be measured utilizing the cell count increments of the state windows. For example, the cell count increment of the S0 state window is induced

by the leftward Vth distribution drift of the S1 state, and in turn, the cell count increment of the S0 state window can be used to calculate the Vth distribution drift of the S1 state. Similarly, with the Vth distribution drift of Si state and the cell count increment of the Si state window, the Vth distribution drift of Si+1 state can be calculated. The specific design is illustrated below.
Step 1: The Vth distribution parameters from the S1 to S7 states at the retention time t0 are obtained. Given that the amplitude ai and the standard deviation σi are considered to be constant over the retention, f [ai , µi (t0) , σi ] is abbreviated as f (µi (t0)).
Step 2: Cells within each state window are counted and denoted as Ni (t0), (i = 0∼7). As mentioned above, each RRV is tuned to the right with an appropriate step, denoted as V , thereby removing the right wing of the state Si within the Si+1 window. A too-low V may cause the Si+1 window to overlap the right wing of state Si . A too-high V may lead to the measurement accuracy being impacted by significant random noise at the top parts of the Vth distributions. In this work, each RRV is tuned 30 minimum steps to the right for LVDE
implementation. The cell count within each state window of
the probe wordline is determined using two FPGA FIFOs,
denoted as FIFO_XP and FIFO_UP. First, The XP data is
read and cached into the FIFO_XP, followed by the UP data
into FIFO_UP. Then, the LP data is read byte-by-byte, with

The following illustrates how to coordinate the above designs to implement the proposed LVDE. Algorithm 1 describes the metadata acquisition flow of LVDE. In this work, the metadata is stored in the spare single-level cell (SLC) area of the flash memory and is accessed before reading the test blocks. As seen in Algorithm 1, the Vth distribution extraction operation is executed only for states with positive Vth, i.e.,

from S1 to S7. The read margins between the S0 and S1 states are much wider than those of the remaining adjacent state pairs [37]. The cell counting operation is executed for all state windows. ta and tb are set to 1 hour and 1 day, respectively. The following analyzes the adaptability of LVDE under a broader range of flash noise types. As described in Section II, this work focuses solely on retention loss, which causes leftward drifts in the Vth distributions. However, state drift can also occur to the right, particularly in the presence of certain noise factors. The implementation of Algorithm 1 is not limited by the drift direction of the Vth distribution. Take Fig. 6 as an example. If LVDE detects a decrease in the number of cells with S0 state, the Vth distribution drift of S1 state can be determined by solving for the appropriate V1- µ1. The core design of Algorithm 2 can also be extended to more general scenarios. It should be noted that under different noise conditions, the metadata of LVDE may need to be updated regularly during the idle periods of the flash memory device, thereby optimizing the accuracy of the cross-layer estimation [25].
The implementation overhead incurred by LVDE is analyzed in the following, including the space overhead used for metadata storage and the additional runtime overhead due to applying the proposed strategy [31].
1) Space Overhead: Take the popular TLC flash block containing 62 layers with a capacity of 37MB as an example. As described by the Algorithm 2, the metadata consists of four types. One is the parameters of the Vth distributions on each layer. In this work, three 16-bit fixed-point numbers were used to record the amplitude, mean, and variance of each Vth distribution. Therefore, the space overhead taken up for recording the distribution parameters is 2604 (62 × 7×3 × 2) bytes. The second is the mean drift of each Vth distribution from ta to tb in Algorithm 1. This work uses one 16-bit fixed point number to record a drift. Thus the space overhead occupied by the second type of metadata is 868 (62×7×1×2) bytes. The two remaining types of metadata are the wordline index and the cell counts within each state window at tb for the probe wordline, which occupy one and eight 16-bit fixed-point numbers (18 bytes) to be stored, respectively. In summary, the total space overhead occupied by the four types of metadata is 3490 bytes. For a block of data, the metadata of LVDE only takes up 0.009%, which is appreciable in most applications.
2) Runtime Overhead: As Algorithm 3 describes, the runtime overhead of LVDE is incurred by the measurement for the probe wordline following Eqs. (19) and (23) and the

IV. EXPERIMENTAL ANALYSIS
This section first compares the sampling overhead of LVDE and multiple Vopt trackers. Subsequently, experiments are conducted on real flash chips to demonstrate LVDE’s effectiveness in enhancing the efficiency of LDPC decoding attempts.
A. Experimental Setup
1) Flash Setup: Micron B16A is used as the experimental chip model in this work due to its representativeness, which effectively reflects the typical characteristics and behavior patterns of mainstream flash devices. As described in Subsection III-A, the proposed LVDE is designed based on two fundamental NAND flash characteristics that have been demonstrated by solid studies. Therefore, theoretically, the proposed LVDE has strong adaptivity and can be applied to a broad range of 3D NAND flash models. The hardware differences across flash memories may lead to minor variations in strategy performance, which is tolerable generally.
The key structural parameters of B16A are as follows. The page size is 16kB. A block consists of 744 TLC wordlines, and every 12 wordlines form a layer, i.e., a block contains 62 layers. Further, 1008 blocks form a chip. Ten flash chips are used for the evaluation experiments. Specifically, 10 normal blocks are selected on each chip, i.e., a total of 100 experimental blocks are adopted. The recommended endurance in the Micron B16A datasheet is 1500 P/E cycles, while the actual endurance of a flash model can be much higher than the recommended endurance with a strong ECC [40]. Blocks on 5 chips are used for performance evaluation of LDPC hard decisions, withstanding preprocessing of 1k, 1.5k, 2k, 2.5k, and 3k P/E cycles, respectively. Blocks on the remaining 5 chips are used for performance evaluation of LDPC soft decisions, subjected to preprocessing of 2k, 3k, 4k, 5k, and 6k P/E cycles, respectively.
The flash chips are test with an FPGA-based platform, the photograph of which is shown in Fig. 8. Before performing each set of comparisons, random data was first written into the experimental blocks. The flash chips are placed in a

2) LDPC Code Setup: LDPC encoding and decoding are implemented on the PC side, thus handling the complex decoding calculations of soft decisions. The min-sum strategy is adopted as the LDPC decoding strategy for its efficiency and computational demands [28]. Each flash page contains seven codewords with a length of 18364 bits. This option can improve the efficiency of the decoding process by reducing computational complexity and isolating errors [30]. The code rate is selected as 8/9, providing strong error correction with relatively low space overhead [29]. The upper limits for the decoding iterations and the read level of the soft decision are set to 30 and 7, respectively. This is because continuing to increase these two parameters above this limit yields negligible improvement in error correction capability, but causes significant additional read latency, especially for increasing the read level. Therefore, an upper limit of 30 for the decoding iterations and an upper limit of 7 for the read level can balance the reliability and performance of a flash device, and have been adopted by numerous existing LDPC codes as typical options [12], [42].
The unit timing parameter of each operation during the LDPC decoding attempts is set according to the previous studies [30], [43]. The latency of the LDPC hard decision is set as 25µs, and the latency of the soft decision is set as 14µs. The transmission latency is set as 20µs, and the decoding iteration latency is set as 0.5µs. The total latency of each operation is calculated by multiplying the unit latency with the corresponding count.
3) Comparison Setup: The sampling overhead caused by LVDE is compared with that of a series of Vopt trackers, the details of which are elaborated in subsection IV-B. Layer read count is used as the quantitative evaluation metric of sampling overhead [25].
The state-of-the-art LDPC hard decision designs, i.e., Vopt trackers, to be compared with LVDE are DRRM (ASPLOS′24) [31] and RFBC (TCAS-I′24) [38]. The BER and frame rate error (FER) are used as the metrics for evaluation [13], [17].
The state-of-the-art LDPC soft decision designs selected for comparison are PreLDPC (TCAD′23) [12] and EBDN (TCAD′24) [30]. Specifically, PreLDPC is compared with PreLDPC-LVDE, a design with LVDE applied to PreLDPC. EBDN is compared with EBDN-LVDE, a design with LVDE applied to EBDN. Two metrics are used for comparisons. One is the performance of predicting the optimal read levels [10]. Three cases are included, where the predicted read levels are over than, equal to, or under than the actual optimal options, respectively. In this work, the predicted read level

for a wordline is considered equal to the actual optimal value when the total prediction errors across all error-prone areas do not exceed 3. Another metric is the effectiveness of reducing the total read latency by applying LVDE to each state-of-theart design [12].
B. Evaluation of the Sampling Overhead for Vopt Tracking
Now compare the sampling overhead of LVDE and several advanced Vopt tracker designs.
1) OCVTR [3]: OCVTR performs three reads for an error-prone area to estimate the Vopt . Thus, the sampling overhead is 1116 (62 × 3 × 6) layer reads.
2) ECRRV [11]: As recommended in the source article [11], ECRRV extracts 10 points for each error-prone area to fit the Vth distribution, thus reducing the overhead of pointby-point sampling. Therefore, the ECRRV-induced sampling overhead is 7440 (62 × 20 × 6) layer reads
OCVTR and ECRRV effectively address the problem of point-by-point sampling in a single error-prone area. However, since both are synchronous designs, the sampling overhead is exacerbated by the read retries performed in multiple errorprone areas.
3) SSAE [37]: SSAE employs two approaches suggested by TSAE to reduce the sampling overhead induced by tracking Vopt [13]. One is the concept of sentinel cell. A small number of sentinel cells are attached to the end of each wordline. By comparing the difference between the written and read data patterns of the sentinel cells, the Vopt of the entire wordline can be estimated based on one read. Another concept is sentinel voltage, which is an asynchronous design. As mentioned in subsection III-B, the drifts of different Vopt over a long-term retention period are linearly correlated. By pre-learning the linear models, only the Vopt of one RRV, the sentinel voltage, needs to be measured with the sentinel cells, and the Vopt of the remaining RRVs can then be calculated. Specifically, SSAE first reads UP and LP for a wordline to obtain the Vopt of RRV V4, which is further used to estimate the Vopt of the remaining RRVs. Therefore, the SSAE-induced sampling overhead is 41.33 (62 × 2.3) layer reads.
SSAE addresses the issue of multiple reads conducted for a single error-prone area (e.g., 3 reads for OCVTR compared to 1 read for SSAE) and the repetition of samples across multiple error-prone areas (e.g., 6 areas for OCVTR compared to 1 area for SSAE). However, sampling is still performed layer by layer, resulting in a significant read latency. Assume that LDPC hard decisions succeed on all layers with the Vopt provided by SSAE. One valid read, i.e., 62 layer reads, is essential to read out the stored data. And then, the additional read latency caused by SSAE is 67.7%.
4) DRRM [31] & RFBC [38]: To remove the layer-bylayer sampling overhead, the approach adopted by both DRRM and RFBC is to pre-train a table offline for the flash model, which is named as optimal read voltage difference (OVD) table in DRRM. When responding to a flash read request, the Vopt on one wordline is first obtained, based on which an additional offset compensation is applied to each layer (wordline) by looking up the table, thereby adapting to the inter-layer variation in Vopt .

The following elaborates how DRRM and RFBC calibrate the Vopt for a target wordline. DRRM implements a twostep design, named main read retry table (RRT) retry and proximity-search, respectively. The former first determines a coarse-accuracy Vopt interval and the latter further determines the exact Vopt offset. The Vopt tracking loop in both processes returns the result when detecting that the LDPC decoding syndrome has taken a valley value, which results in a variable sampling overhead. For example, when DRRM performs 3 RRT retries and 3 proximity searches to track the Vopt of a wordline, the resulting sampling overhead is 0.5 (1/12 × 6) layer reads. RFBC works by considering the Vopt offsets of each RRV on the same page type to be equal. Based on twice consecutive reads of a page, RFBC counts the flip bits between the two sets of data and uses it to estimate the Vopt of all RRVs corresponding to this page type. Therefore, the sampling overhead of RFBC is 6 page reads, i.e., 0.17 (2/12) layer reads.

D. Evaluation of LDPC Soft Decision Performance
This subsection evaluates the capability of improving the efficiency of LDPC decoding attempts by applying LVDE to state-of-the-art soft memory sensing designs. PreLDPC determines the read levels based on the cell counts of the errorprone areas. PreLDPC-LVDE follows the same approach as PreLDPC. The difference lies in the way of detecting the number of cells in an error-prone area. PreLDPC employs a method of counting the state shifts between twice reads whereas PreLDPC-LVDE employs a computation method based on the estimated Vth distributions, both of which are described in Section II. The EBDN pre-trains a batch-level mapping table between the entropy of an error prone area and read levels required, where the reliability status of the flash memory is divided into multiple stages according to the P/E cycles and the retention length. During data storage, the EBDN looks up the table with the P/E cycles and retention length the flash block undergoes to predict the optimal read levels. EBDNLVDE also determines the read levels based on entropy. The difference lies in the entropy value of an error-prone area is dynamically calculated by EBDN-LVDE, but not based on looking up a pre-trained table. V. CONCLUSION
This article proposes LVDE, an online lightweight strategy to estimate the Vth distributions of 3-D NAND flash over longterm retention. LVDE implements a dynamic extraction of the Vth distribution to provide optimal memory sensing parameters for LDPC decoding attempts, including the read levels of the initial attempt, the offsets of the read voltages, and the LLR on each Vth interval divided by the read voltages. Experimental results based on real flash chips demonstrate that applying LVDE can effectively improve the efficiency of the LDPC decoding attempts and thus improve the read performance of flash memory. In future work, we plan to optimize the flash memory behavioral model exploited by LVDE, thereby improving its adaptability in complex application scenarios.
