ELLR-BP: An Enhanced LLR-BP Algorithm Based on LDPC Coding for LoRa Physical Layer
Ruidongxue Wang and Jianhua Bao , Member, IEEE

Abstract—Wireless communication is highly susceptible to various forms of noise and interference, leading to potential errors in received data. Consequently, this study proposes a novel anti-interference LoRa physical layer communication model to reduce the Bit Error Rate (BER) under extremely low Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) conditions in LoRa communication. The model incorporates Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes as the channel coding scheme and integrates soft Chirp Spread Spectrum (CSS) demodulation with the Log-Likelihood Ratios Belief Propagation (LLR-BP) decoding algorithm. A key component of the model is the Enhanced LLR-BP (ELLRBP) algorithm, which dynamically adjusts LLR values during iterative decoding based on variations in SNR and Spreading Factor (SF), enabling enhanced BER performance in challenging environments. Additionally, the model introduces realistic channel effects, including fading and Co-SF interference, into the simulation framework. MATLAB simulations demonstrate the model’s effectiveness: LDPC coding improves the SNR threshold by approximately 1 dB and reduces the BER by more than 3% compared to traditional Hamming coding. When BER = 10−4, the proposed ELLR-BP algorithm achieves a gain of 0.4097 dB and reduces the BER by 0.54% to 0.81% compared with the LLR-BP algorithm. Similarly, the proposed anti-interference model significantly improves performance under Rayleigh channels and provides better resistance to Co-SF interference than standard LoRa.
Index Terms—IoT communication, LoRa, CSS, LDPC, LLRBP, Co-SF interference.
I. INTRODUCTION
L OW POWER Wide Area Network (LPWAN) is an emerging connectivity technology for the Internet of Things (IoT) that offers potential prospects for long-distance, energyefficient, and cost-effective communication. Long Range (LoRa) is one of the most notable LPWAN technologies, using the Chirp Spread Spectrum (CSS) technique to enable the receivers to decode signals with low sensitivity [1], which features low power consumption, a long transmission range, and flexible networking. LoRa is widely used in smart agriculture, transportation, smart grid, and additional sectors such as industrial automation, environmental monitoring, and urban infrastructure management.

The performance of the LoRa communication system has been affected by data collisions and IoT mutual interference. Due to the increasing demand for communication rate, communication distance, and power consumption in various applications, it is necessary to research LoRa communication technology further. Currently, the research on LoRa communication is primarily centered on Media Access Control (MAC) layer algorithms, such as multiple access techniques and adaptive data rate (ADR) algorithms. This allows multiple devices to transmit simultaneously on the same frequency without interference, thereby increasing network capacity. Further enhancing the efficiency of LoRa networks, [7] focuses on ADR algorithms, which dynamically adjusts spreading factors based on Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) measurements to optimize energy efficiency and ensure reliable message delivery. In conclusion, optimizing resource allocation and improving collision avoidance mechanisms can enhance system reliability and robustness, improving network efficiency and performance. Although this method offers certain performance gains, it remains unclear whether these marginal improvements can justify the additional complexity and potential latency they introduce.
Given that the reliability of data transmission is a key performance indicator in modern communication systems [12], effective error correction coding is essential for ensuring this reliability. Therefore, the improvements to the physical layer in this article primarily focus on optimizing the encoding scheme to enhance performance. Selecting an appropriate encoding scheme for LoRa networks requires careful consideration of coding complexity, error correction performance, and power consumption. First, coding complexity is directly associated with the computational resource and the real-time processing capability of devices. Second, error correction performance is crucial for the reliability of data transmission, especially in complex channel conditions. A robust error correction mechanism can significantly minimize data loss and reduce the frequency of retransmissions. Finally, choosing a lowpower coding scheme is essential for the design of LoRa networks, especially for battery-powered IoT devices, as it can substantially extend their operational lifespan.
Commonly used schemes include Convolutional, Turbo, Polar, and Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes [13]. Convolutional codes, while simple and well-suited for small data transmissions, provide limited error correction and are inadequate in highly error-prone environments. Turbo codes, although they offer error correction performance close to the Shannon limit, are unsuitable for power-sensitive applications like LoRa and IoT devices due to their high decoding complexity and power consumption. Polar codes generally outperform LDPC codes for short block lengths (less than 1 kbit), which is particularly relevant in LoRa systems where block lengths are typically small. Polar codes provide stronger theoretical error correction capabilities and lower bit error rates. However, their high encoding complexity poses significant challenges for resource-limited devices commonly found in LoRa networks. Since uplink communication dominates these networks, where devices prioritize low power consumption and computational simplicity, LDPC codes offer an optimal balance between performance and complexity, making them a promising solution. Moreover, the lower decoding complexity of LDPC codes makes them especially suitable for sporadic, short-packet communications in energy-constrained LoRa environments.
In conclusion, LDPC codes represent a more suitable choice for LoRa networks, offering an effective balance between error correction performance and computational efficiency in power-sensitive, low-data-rate applications. The feasibility of LDPC-based schemes within the context of CSS modulation has been demonstrated in [14]. The study emphasizes the

robustness and capacity-approaching potential of LDPC codes under both coherent and non-coherent reception scenarios, providing a solid foundation for their application in LoRa networks.
When using LDPC codes in LoRa, the Log-Likelihood Ratio Belief Propagation (LLR-BP) decoding algorithm relies on each received bit’s Log-Likelihood Radio (LLR) as the priori probability for decoding. However, CSS demodulation uses the hard output method based on the Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT), demodulating the symbols by searching for the location with the highest spectral amplitude [15]. Therefore, soft CSS demodulation is required to produce LLR values. Soft demodulation techniques for CSS-based LoRa systems have been investigated in [16]. A soft-output detection algorithm, integrated into a multi-layer modulation framework, was proposed to specifically support Turbo code decoding. Simulation results demonstrated significant improvements in BER performance compared to conventional hard demodulation methods, validating the effectiveness of soft demodulation in enhancing decoding performance for CSS-modulated LoRa systems.
A new approach developed by [17] applies LDPC to the LoRa communication system. It introduces a spectrum-based LLR extractor, which performs SoftMax operation on the amplitude spectrum to compute the LLR and outputs the probability of all frequency bins. Furthermore, analyzing the extracted amplitude spectral features for symbol demodulation prediction allows the adjustment of the LLR values to enhance system decoding performance. However, this adjustment leads to increased decoding delay. As a result, further research is needed to trade off decoding performance and latency.
This study establishes an LDPC-based interference-resilient LoRa physical layer communication model, introducing the soft CSS demodulation and LLR-BP algorithm. We introduce an enhanced LLR-BP algorithm named ELLR-BP to mitigate the BER system. This algorithm calculates the original BER according to SNR and SF. Then, it adjusts the LLR dynamically for iterative decoding. The model simulations are conducted in MATLAB under the Additive White Gaussian Noise (AWGN) channel and Rayleigh channel with introduced Co-SF interference. The results confirm the correctness and effectiveness of the model. Additionally, we analyze the influence of different conditions on the system’s BER.

C. LLR-BP Algorithm

LDPC decoding involves hard and soft decision methods, represented by the Belief Propagation (BP) and Bit Flipping (BF) algorithms. The BF algorithm corrects errors by flipping the bits that fail to satisfy the majority of check equations, making it suitable for applying hardware implementation due to its low computational complexity. However, the BP algorithm exchanges confidence information between the variable nodes and the check nodes of the Tanner graph for decoding through probabilistic information transfer, offering optimal performance at the cost of high computation complexity [22].
This paper used the LLR-BP algorithm based on bit LLR information, having certain advantages in decoding performance and computational complexity compared with the traditional BP algorithm [23]. This algorithm suits various communication systems’ channel decoding and information transmission tasks. Considering that the min-sum algorithm changes the original probability information from the check node to the variable node, it may lead to the loss of the original probability information, making it difficult to converge to the correct solution in the decoding process. To compensate for this performance degradation, several variants of the original minsum algorithm, such as the normalized min-sum algorithm (NMSA) and the offset min-sum algorithm (OMSA), have been proposed. These methods utilize fixed correction factors to mitigate overestimation issues in the min-sum framework, achieving certain improvements in decoding performance [25].
In [26], an exponential correction factor was introduced based on the min-sum algorithm, dynamically adjusting according to decoding conditions. This adaptive mechanism enhances decoding performance and outperforms fixed-factor methods such as NMSA and OMSA, particularly at higher SNRs. However, due to their dependence on correction factors, dynamic adjustments, or nonlinear computations in the node update processes, these methods often result in increased computational complexity, especially in hardware implementations.
To balance the trade-off between algorithmic error performance and complexity, we propose the ELLR-BP algorithm, which applies the correction factor directly to the LLR values. This approach avoids modifying the traditional node update framework, thereby reducing the complexity associated with iterative adjustments while also mitigating cumulative errors from approximate calculations. By focusing on LLRlevel corrections, the ELLR-BP algorithm ensures improved decoding performance with a more efficient computational structure.
In signal processing, the absolute value of the LLR represents the confidence level in the current bit decision, with larger values indicating a greater certainty that the bit decision is correct. However, the accuracy of the LLR value significantly impacts the overall quality of the decoding process:
1) Accurate LLR values: A larger absolute LLR corresponds to higher confidence in bit decision, improving the reliability of symbol detection. Consequently, the BER decreases as the absolute value of the LLR increases, reflecting enhanced decoding performance.
2) Inaccurate LLR values: If the LLR value is significantly biased or incorrect but has a large absolute value, it signifies high confidence in an erroneous decision. Consequently, the BER increases as the absolute value of the LLR increases, exacerbating the impact of erroneous decisions and potentially triggering cascading errors through error propagation.
As previously discussed, LLR directly influences the BER, allowing this relationship to be utilized inversely by employing BER as a feedback mechanism to adjust LLR values and regulate their influence on bit decisions, particularly in highBER environments.
By incorporating BER as a feedback mechanism, LLR values can be dynamically adapted based on the channel conditions, thereby enhancing reliability and minimizing the impact of errors. This adaptive strategy mitigates over-reliance

(32)

The motivation for introducing PK as a correction factor is closely related to the concept of mutual information in information theory, where the confidence in the received signal should decrease as channel reliability declines. In practical communication systems, the effective range of Pb typically falls between 0 and 0.5. This range ensures the applicability of the correction factor, preventing excessive amplification or attenuation of the LLR, thereby enhancing the robustness of the system.

In summary, the BER is high when the SNR is extremely low. In such a case, the PK tends to approach 0.5, resulting in a decrease in the absolute value of the original LLR. This mechanism facilitates error correction based on neighboring node information during iterations, potentially enabling a “zero crossing”, which means that it can flip from the wrong bit to the right bit. Conversely, under the appropriate SNR conditions, the BER approaches zero, the PK approaches 1, and the original LLR remains constant, ensuring the stability of the decoding process.

B. Embedded Algorithm Optimization
1) Embedded System Optimization: To reduce the algorithm’s reliance on complex mathematical operations, such as logarithms and exponentials, precomputed look-up tables can be utilized during iterative decoding. By replacing real-time calculations with pre-stored values, execution speed is significantly improved, and computational overhead is reduced. This optimization enhances the practicality of the algorithm in resource-constrained embedded systems, effectively balancing performance and resource usage.
2) Hardware Acceleration: Implementing iterative decoding steps on FPGA or DSP chips can significantly lower processing latency and energy consumption by leveraging their parallel processing capabilities. Additionally, custom application-specific integrated circuits (ASICs) implementations can further optimize power efficiency by tailoring the hardware specifically for the decoding process, eliminating unnecessary functionalities.
C. Network Deployment and Fine-Tuning
1) Dynamic Parameter Adjustment: To achieve optimal performance across different application scenarios, dynamic adjustments to key parameters can be made prior to actual deployment. By leveraging empirical judgment or test results, parameters such as the spreading factor, coding rate, and transmission power can be optimized to effectively balance data transmission rate and anti-interference capability, while conserving energy and ensuring efficient and reliable communication in various scenarios.
2) Anti-Interference Strategies: Devices should first be assigned different spreading factors to maximize the reduction of interference among devices operating on the same frequency. In scenarios with a large number of devices where SF overlap cannot be completely avoided, assigning different channels to devices with overlapping SFs can further mitigate Co-SF interference. Additionally, time synchronization among devices should be optimized to ensure that transmission times are coordinated and orderly, preventing signal conflicts or interference caused by uncoordinated or completely asynchronous transmissions.

VII. CONCLUSION
This paper presents the ELLR-BP decoding algorithm, integrated with LDPC coding, to improve the anti-interference capabilities of the LoRa physical layer. The algorithm optimizes the decoding process by dynamically adjusting LLR values based on SNR and SF. Simulation results show that LDPC coding improves the SNR threshold by approximately 1 dB and reduces the BER by over 3% compared to traditional Hamming coding. The proposed ELLR-BP algorithm further achieves an SNR gain of 0.4097 dB and a BER reduction ranging from 0.54% to 0.81% relative to the conventional LLR-BP algorithm. Additionally, the anti-interference model demonstrated significant performance gains under Rayleigh channel conditions, with SNR gains of up to 8 dB. Moreover, the BER for LDPC coding is 3.28% lower than that of Hamming coding under the same Co-SF interference conditions. These findings underscore the potential of the LDPC-based anti-interference LoRa physical layer model to enhance communication efficiency and system reliability, particularly in interference-prone environments. Future research will aim to improve the accuracy of BER calculations and introduce adjustment factors to better tune the confidence of BER estimates, thereby further optimizing the LoRa communication system model.
