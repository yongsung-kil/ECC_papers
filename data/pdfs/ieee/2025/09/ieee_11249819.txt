Single-Shot Decoding of Biased-Tailored Quantum LDPC Codes
Devon Campbell Dept. of Computer Science
Columbia University New York, NY, USA dec2180@columbia.edu

Abstract—Quantum processors often exhibit biased noise (dominant Z errors) and noisy readout, both of which degrade reliability and reproducibility. This work unifies two complementary strategies: bias tailoring, which realigns stabilizers to the dominant error basis, and single-shot (SS) decoding, which leverages metachecks to identify measurement faults from a single noisy round. We instantiate this in a four-dimensional lifted hypergraph-product (4D-LHP) LDPC code built from quasi-cyclic protograph seeds. Simulations show that tailoring reduces word-error rate (WER) by 20–60% across realistic Z:X bias ratios (1:1 to 103:1), with the largest gains at moderate bias. Under realistic q = 4% measurement noise, a single SS round recovers ∼ 33% of the performance lost to readout faults; metachecks detect > 99.8% of faulty syndromes, ffering near-complete fault visibility despite modest correction capability. These results indicate that 4D-LHP codes offer practical resilience under realistic noise, making them well-suited for integration into orchestrated QPU–CPU workflows.
I. INTRODUCTION
Quantum processors in hybrid quantum–classical workflows must deliver reproducible results under realistic device noise and tight latency budgets. In practice, performance is frequently constrained by (i) biased physical noise, where one Pauli error (often Z) overwhelms the others, and (ii) noisy readout, where faulty stabilizer measurements mask data errors and force repeated rounds that inflate depth, runtime, and failure probability [1]. Codes and decoders that are bias-aware and support single-round operation can substantially improve system resilience in orchestrated QPU-CPU workflows.
This work presents a unified architecture that combines biastailoring with single-shot (SS) decoding in a four-dimensional lifted hypergraph product (4D-LHP) quantum LDPC code. The construction embeds metachecks to evaluate the consistency of syndromes produced from each stabilizer layer, such that a single noisy syndrome suffices to identify measurement faults. Further, local Hadamard rotations realign stabilizers with the dominant error basis to raise the effective distance under dephasing-biased noise [2], [3]. In practice, avoiding repeated measurements lowers circuit depth and latency on the quantum side and reduces classical synchronization overhead during orchestration.
These 4D-LHP codes are instantiated from four quasi-cyclic protographs selected for large local girth and short-cycle suppression. An illustrative instance achieves [[384, 48, 6]] (rate

0.125), competitive with popular HGP baselines at similar distance while offering metacheck redundancy for SS decoding.
This paper offers the following key contributions:
• Unified, hardware-aware design. A 4D-LHP code that simultaneously supports bias tailoring and SS decoding with metachecks, enabling single-round operation under mixed Pauli noise.
• System-level gains. Simulations show up to 60% reduction in word-error rate (WER) from bias tailoring across realistic Z:X bias, and about one-third recovery of performance lost to q = 4% measurement noise via a single SS round. Metachecks detect > 99.8% of faulty syndromes and provide enough correction to materially lower WER.
• Engineering insight. Performance gains peak at moderate bias; at extreme bias, dense clusters of the dominant error surpass the twisted code’s protective advantage, leading to limited improvement. We outline decoder and seeddesign avenues to increase metacheck actionability.
These results target dependable quantum memories and near-term components within orchestrated QPU-CPU workflows: single-round readout lowers latency and noise accumulation; bias-aware stabilizers cut qubit overhead for a target logical error; and quasi-cyclic structure supports efficient BP+OSD decoding and hardware tiling.
II. BACKGROUND
CSS and homological products: CSS codes specify X- and Z-type stabilizers via binary parity-check matrices (HX , HZ ) with HX HZT = 0. Hypergraph-product (HGP) constructions form sparse quantum LDPC codes from two classical seeds and can be viewed homologically: a length2 chain with stabilizers adjacent to the qubit layer. Extending to length-4 introduces two additional layers whose boundary maps act as metachecks, enabling redundant consistency constraints on the measured syndromes. In 4D, one obtains independent metacheck matrices (MX , MZ ) for the two stabilizer sectors [1] (See Fig 1).

Bias tailoring: Many hardware platforms exhibit strongly biased noise—most commonly, dephasing (Z) errors occur far more often than X or Y flips [5]. Standard CSS codes waste half their checks on rare X faults, reducing effective distance for the common error. Bias tailoring addresses this by realigning stabilizers to the dominant basis.
A well-known example is the XZZX surface code: applying Hadamard gates to alternating qubits mixes X and Z within each stabilizer, forcing logical Z operators onto longer, diagonal paths and improving thresholds under Z-biased noise.
The same principle generalizes to LDPC codes. Algebraically, a Hadamard on a qubit swaps its columns in (HX , HZ ); applying H rotations to half the qubits exchanges the right halves of HX and HZ . The standard HGP form is:

Lifted hypergraph products: While standard HGP codes operate on arbitrary binary matrices, lifted hypergraph products (LHP) use quasi-cyclic protographs—compact templates whose entries are elements of the ring of circulants. Each nonzero entry represents a circulant permutation of the L × L identity matrix, so expanding the protograph with a lift parameter L yields a large, sparse parity-check matrix while preserving structure. The construction uses small seed matrices whose connectivity strongly affects local girth and decoder behavior; choosing seeds systematically is key to avoiding trapping sets and ensuring good distance scaling [6].

III. METHODOLOGY

4D construction: We instantiate a length-4 homological product from four classical seeds. The five layers C−2 → C2 play distinct roles: C0 holds qubits; C±1 are Z- and Xtype stabilizers; C±2 provide metachecks that constrain the measured syndromes. This redundancy enables single-round identification of measurement faults in both sectors.
Operators: The 4D chain induces four sparse matrices:

Here, HX , HZ define the stabilizers and MX , MZ the metachecks that enforce syndrome consistency. The chain relations δiδi−1 = 0 guarantee commutation and ensure that, in the absence of measurement faults, valid syndromes satisfy MX sX = 0 and MZ sZ = 0.
Bias tailoring model: Hadamard rotations are implemented at simulation time without altering matrix structure: the qubits are partitioned into two equal sectors matching the right/left halves of (HX , HZ ). In the “rotated” sector, the X and Z error channels are swapped, emulating local H gates on those qubits.

Each stabilizer measurement flips independently with probability q. Each data point aggregates N = 104 Monte Carlo trials (median and IQR reported); code and configurations are available in the cited repositories [8]–[10].
Single-shot decoding pipeline: We use a two-stage BP+OSD decoder [11]. (i) Syndrome cleaning: treat the observed syndrome as a codeword of the metacheck code and run BP+OSD with MX/Z to locate/correct measurement faults, yielding a cleaned syndrome. (ii) Data decoding: run BP+OSD with HX/Z on the cleaned syndrome to infer a low-weight recovery. Success is declared if the residual anticommutes with no logical operator. The approach exploits the syndrome redundancy of the 4D construction and handles both data and measurement noise efficiently.
Implementation: This work extends the LDPC protograph framework and BPOSD decoder to a 4D-LHP class that operates on symbolic (quasi-cyclic) seeds and then expands to binary matrices with lift parameter L (see code repository references [8]–[10]).
Seeds and parameters: Seeds were chosen via a progressive edge-growth (PEG) search targeting high local girth and ACE constraints, which are known to reduce short cycles and improve BP convergence [12]. We prioritized PEG heuristics

IV. RESULTS
This section reports the word error rate (WER)—the probability that decoding fails—computed over N = 104 Monte Carlo trials for each parameter set. Simulations span physical error rates p ∈ [0.01, 0.08], measurement error rates q ∈ [0, 0.04], and bias ratios η = pZ /pX from 10−3 to 103.
Bias tailoring: Figure 2 shows the relative WER change between untailored and Hadamard-tailored codes. The tailored code improves in all tested settings. Gains are largest for moderate bias, peaking at up to 60% WER reduction around η ≈ 10−2–10−1. At extreme bias (η 102), the benefit of tailoring remains substantial (up to 40%) but tapers as η grows, which we attribute to decoder saturation (see Sec. V). Notably, the tailored code improves performance even under unbiased noise, reducing WER by about 10%.
Sensitivity to measurement noise: Figure 3a plots WER vs. p for several q. With ideal measurements (q = 0), WER stays below 10−2 up to p 0.06 and below 0.1 at p = 0.08. Even modest measurement noise rapidly degrades performance: at q = 0.02 the WER rises across the full p range; at q = 0.04 it nearly saturates to 1. This underscores the need to correct measurement faults rather than rely on repetition.
Single-shot recovery: Figure 3b compares three modes at a representative point (p = 0.04): baseline (q = 0),

noisy measurements without SS, and noisy with SS. Noisy measurements raise the median WER from < 0.02 to ∼ 0.11. A single SS round lowers the median to ∼ 0.08 (–28%), recovering about one third of the penalty from measurement noise and narrowing the variance.
Metacheck visibility vs. actionability: Figure 4 reports metacheck behavior across trials. Detection is near-perfect for both sectors (most trials > 99.8%), confirming that measurement faults reliably imprint a metasyndrome. Correction is more dispersed, typically 5–10% with a mass near zero, reflecting decoder sensitivity to local structures. Despite limited correction, the visibility provided by metachecks is sufficient to lower WER materially (Fig. 3b). Improving actionability—via seed design or metacheck-aware decoding—offers a clear path to further gains.
Summary: Across parameters, the 4D-LHP architecture delivers (i) strong bias responsiveness (up to 60% WER reduction at moderate bias), (ii) robustness to measurement noise via single-round decoding (∼33% recovery), and (iii) near-complete fault detection (> 99.8%). Performance reflects structural advantages of the 4D-LHP design and seed choices; while not globally optimal, PEG-based seeds provided strong empirical reliability. These results show that combining bias tailoring with metacheck-enabled single-shot decoding yields practical reliability improvements under circuit-level noise.
V. DISCUSSION
This work presents a unified architecture—a 4D lifted hypergraph product (4D-LHP) code with bias tailoring and embedded metachecks—that mitigates biased noise and enables single-shot (SS) decoding of measurement faults. Unlike prior studies that treated these techniques separately, this approach combines them in a low-overhead, hardware-friendly framework: tailoring aligns stabilizers with dominant dephasing to reduce logical errors, while metachecks add redundancy to detect and partially correct measurement faults in one round.
Bias tailoring delivers the largest benefits at moderate bias, reducing WER by up to 60%. At extreme bias (η 102), improvements persist (up to 40%) but plateau, as the overwhelming density of Z-type errors produces correlated clusters that exceed the protection afforded by the twisted code, leading to decoder saturation despite longer logical Z operators.
Even under unbiased noise, bias-tailoring improves performance, reducing WER by about 10%. This likely reflects structural benefits of the Hadamard rotation: column swaps increase Tanner graph girth, reduce short cycles to aid BP+OSD, and lengthen certain logical operators, raising minimum weight and spreading errors across more qubits [3].
Under realistic readout noise, a single SS round recovers roughly one-third of the performance loss. While metachecks provide near-perfect detection (> 99.8%), their correction capability remains limited (5–10%), indicating that decoder performance, not metacheck coverage, is the main bottleneck.

Promising directions for future work include:
• Sustained Thresholds: Perform multi-round simulations to assess stability under repeated noise–decode cycles [13].
• Joint Decoders: Investigate one-stage BP methods that integrate stabilizers and metachecks into a single Tanner graph to reduce message-passing conflicts and improve convergence [14].
• Bias-Adaptive Protocols: Design adaptive schemes that dynamically reconfigure Hadamard-tailored sectors based on real-time noise statistics, enabling the code to maintain efficiency under time-varying bias.

VI. CONCLUSION
Combining bias tailoring with SS decoding in a 4D-LHP
framework yields robust, hardware-aware improvements: sub-
stantial WER reductions across bias, recovery from measure-
ment noise, and near-complete fault visibility. These properties
make the approach a practical building block for dependable
quantum memories and other pre-fault-tolerant modules within
orchestrated QPU–CPU systems.
