End-to-end Neural-Classical Concatenated Feedback
Coding with OFDM Guidance
Adnan Can Buyuksirin, Hun-Seok Kim, Senior Member, IEEE
Department of EECS, University of Michigan, Ann Arbor, MI
{adnancan, hunseok}@umich.edu
Abstract—In this paper, we propose an end-to-end (E2E)
optimized concatenated feedback coding scheme that combines a
neural inner encoder/decoder with a classical low-density parity-
check (LDPC) outer code for reliable feedback-based communi-
cation over multipath fading channels. A key contribution of
this work is the inclusion of the LDPC decoder within the
training loop by exploiting the differentiability of the sum-
product algorithm (SPA), allowing gradient-based optimization
to flow across the entire feedback-based communication chain. To
enhance robustness over multipath fading channels, we integrate
orthogonal frequency-division multiplexing (OFDM) guidance as
well as the bit-interleaved coded modulation (BICM) paradigm
into the proposed concatenated feedback coding approach. The
proposed framework supports practical blocklengths and fading
scenarios, significantly outperforming both fully neural and non-
E2E concatenated baselines.
Index Terms—End-to-end learning, feedback coding, concate-
nated codes, LDPC codes, OFDM, deep learning
I. INTRODUCTION
The concept of a communication system with receiver
feedback dates back to the earliest days of information theory.
Shannon [1] showed that for a discrete memoryless chan-
nel with noiseless feedback, the channel capacity remains
unchanged. Nonetheless, while feedback does not increase
the theoretical maximum rate, it significantly improves the
achievable error exponent, reducing error probabilities at rates
below capacity and enhancing system performance.
Inspired by the recent remarkable progress of deep-learning
(DL) based physical layer communication and coding schemes
[2]–[6], researchers have specifically investigated DL-based
feedback coding schemes. Deepcode [7], for example, em-
ploys recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to jointly optimize
encoding and decoding strategies with feedback, significantly
outperforming classical schemes by effectively shaping trans-
missions based on prior feedback. Building on the TurboAE
[3] framework, the Feedback Turbo Autoencoder (FTAE)
[8] explicitly incorporates receiver feedback at each iteration
of decoding, further enhancing reliability compared to its
feedforward counterpart.
Recent advances have leveraged powerful neural architec-
tures to enhance feedback-based coding performance. At-
tentionCode [9] introduced a Transformer-inspired architec-
ture that dynamically aligns transmitted symbols with prior
feedback using self-attention, exploiting temporal correlations
across rounds and significantly improving reliability over

earlier recurrent approaches. However, AttentionCode pro-
cesses each message bit individually, resulting in long symbol
sequences and frequent feedback exchanges. Building on this,
Generalized Block Attention Feedback (GBAF) codes [10]
adopt a Transformer encoder-based design to jointly encode
groups of bits in a block-wise manner, substantially reduc-
ing forward-feedback interactions while capturing temporal
and spatial dependencies more efficiently. Complementary to
these, LIGHTCODE [11] focuses on computational efficiency,
achieving performance comparable to GBAF with a much
smaller neural architecture, though requiring a substantially
larger training batch size for similar reliability.
Despite significant advancements, current DL-based feed-
back coding schemes are primarily constrained to very short
block lengths and show performance degradation as codeword
lengths increase, even when the coding rate is held constant
[9]–[11]. Additionally, these schemes are typically designed
for simple channel models like additive white Gaussian noise
(AWGN) or flat fading channels, and they lack evaluation
under more realistic models such as frequency-selective multi-
path fading channels. Although the concept of concatenating
a classical outer code with an inner neural code has been
explored (e.g., CCN codes [4]), previous schemes have not
been optimized through fully end-to-end (E2E) learning across
the concatenated classical-neural coding design.
In this work, we propose a unified framework to address
these limitations. First, inspired by [5], we develop an OFDM-
guided feedback transmission model and evaluate feedback-
based codes over multipath fading channels with longer
blocklengths. Building on this model, we introduce a novel
concatenated feedback coding scheme, where a neural inner
code manages feedback utilization and a classical outer code
ensures overall reliability. Our main contribution is an E2E
trainable system that integrates LDPC codes as an outer code
by leveraging the differentiable structure of the sum-product
algorithm (SPA), enabling gradient-based optimization across
the entire communication chain. To further enhance robustness
over frequency selective fading channels, we incorporate the
bit-interleaved coded modulation (BICM) [12] paradigm to
introduce diversity to further enhance the performance. Our
results demonstrate that the proposed methodology signif-
icantly outperforms purely neural and direct concatenation
counterparts that do not utilize E2E optimization.

III. PROPOSED CONCATENATED FEEDBACK CODING
This section presents the proposed concatenated feedback
coding scheme, which integrates a classical outer channel
code with an inner feedback-based neural encoder and decoder
pair. Our E2E training methodology enables joint optimization
of the entire communication system over multipath fading
channels. Instead of appending an outer LDPC code after an
independently trained neural encoder/decoder, the outer code
is embedded within the computational graph by incorporating
a differentiable LDPC SPA decoder. The overall system archi-
tecture is illustrated in Fig. 1, highlighting both the trainable
and non-trainable components involved in the transmission,
feedback, and decoding processes.
A. Concatenated Feedback Encoding
The transmitter first encodes the information vector u ∈
{0, 1}k into a codeword c ∈{0, 1}n using a binary outer
encoder e : {0, 1}k →{0, 1}n with rate R1 = k/n. We
adopt the 5G New Radio (NR) LDPC code as the outer code,
with rate matching applied as specified in [15]. Since the
LDPC encoding operates over the finite field GF(2), where
all operations are performed modulo-2, it is inherently non-
differentiable and thus detached from the computational graph.
During training, the original information vector u serves
directly as the supervised learning target.
To improve robustness against frequency-selective fading,
we employ BICM [12], which introduces diversity by dis-
persing adjacent coded bits across different fading subcarriers.
In BICM, the codeword c ∈{0, 1}n is first permuted by an
interleaver represented by a permutation matrix π ∈Rn×n,
producing the interleaved sequence ˜c = πc. Each row of
π contains exactly one nonzero entry, ensuring a bijective
and lossless mapping. The interleaved bit vector ˜c is then
partitioned into n/m groups of m bits, where each group
is mapped to a complex-valued modulation symbol via a
memoryless modulation function µ : {0, 1}m →C, resulting
in the modulated symbol vector z ∈Cn/m.
The modulated vector z is partitioned into N message
blocks, each associated with one of the N available OFDM
subcarriers, resulting in M = (n/m)/N symbols per block.
Such block-wise processing enables a sequence-to-sequence
encoding paradigm [16], wherein the encoder progressively
refines its transmission strategy across multiple communica-
tion rounds with feedback. We adopt the Transformer encoder-
based GBAF model [10] for the design of the inner neural
encoder and decoder. We refer the interested reader to [10]
for a detailed description of the model and the sequence-to-
sequence encoding paradigm, due to space limitations.
During each communication round ρ ∈{1, . . . , T}, the
inner neural encoder generates one complex-valued parity
symbol per message block, resulting in the OFDM frequency-
domain transmit vector x(ρ) ∈CN. Since exactly one parity
symbol per block is transmitted at each round, the inner
code rate is R2 = M/T, leading to an overall system rate
R = R1R2 = (k/n)(M/T).

vector at the transmitter, defined following the structure

IV. NUMERICAL RESULTS
In this section, we present numerical evaluations of the
proposed BICM-enhanced E2E optimized concatenated feed-
back coding scheme. We compare its performance against
three baselines under the same system coding rate constraint:
(i) a fully neural feedback-based model without any outer
code, (ii) a direct concatenation of a neural inner encoder
and an LDPC outer code without E2E training, and (iii)
5G LDPC forward error correction (FEC) scheme without
feedback. All models employ OFDM at the physical layer
and are assessed over multipath fading channels. While all
feedback schemes employ ZF equalization, the FEC baseline
uses MMSE equalization.
A. Experimental Setup
All models are evaluated at a system coding rate of
R = 1/3, with outer LDPC code rate R1 = 1/2 and inner
neural code rate R2 = 2/3. The information vector has length
k = 384, resulting in a codeword of n = 768 bits, which is
interleaved and mapped to 384 4-QAM symbols (m = 2 bits
per symbol) across N = 64 OFDM subcarriers with M = 6
symbols per block. The inner encoder transmits one symbol
per block per round over T = 9 rounds, satisfying R2 =
M/T = 2/3. For the fully neural model, the input consists
of 384 bits, mapped to 192 4-QAM symbols and distributed
over N = 64 subcarriers (M = 3 symbols per subcarrier),
with one symbol per subcarrier transmitted per round over 9
rounds, thus achieving R = M/T = 1/3. Transmission occurs
over the 3GPP EVA channel [18] with subcarrier spacing
∆f = 30 kHz, assuming a constant frequency response across
communication rounds. We also assume that forward and
feedback links share the same/reciprocal channel realization,
reflecting time-division duplexing (TDD) operation. All neural
models adopt the GBAF [10] architecture.
All models are trained using the AdamW optimizer with
an initial learning rate of 0.0025, linearly decayed over
150,000 iterations, and a batch size of 4096. For the proposed
E2E scheme, the outer LDPC decoder is incorporated during
training via a differentiable SPA, whereas for the direct
concatenation baseline, the inner neural encoder/decoder is
trained independently to produce correct LDPC codewords
using BCE loss. The fully neural baseline is similarly trained
using BCE loss without any outer code. During training, the
feedforward signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) is uniformly sam-
pled from 5 to 11 dB to compute σ2
ff, whereas evaluation
is performed at fixed SNR values between 6 and 10 dB
using Monte Carlo simulations. The SPA decoder runs for
10 iterations for the proposed E2E scheme and 20 iterations
for the direct concatenation and FEC baselines, highlighting
the superior performance of the proposed scheme even with
fewer decoding iterations. The SPA decoder is implemented
with numerical stability enhancements following the guide-
lines in [19], ensuring optimized performance. We consider
two feedback conditions: (i) noisy feedback, where feedback

B. Experimental Results
To assess the impact of interleaving, we evaluate two BICM
configurations. In the first, a fixed interleaver is applied during
both training and inference, allowing the E2E model to adapt
to the interleaved structure. In the second, interleaving is
applied only at inference. For comparison, both the proposed
E2E scheme and the direct concatenation baseline are also
trained and tested without interleaving. Additionally, the direct
concatenation model is evaluated with interleaving applied
only at inference. Training-time interleaving is unnecessary
here, as the neural decoder directly targets LDPC codewords,
making interleaving ineffective during training. The 5G LDPC
system inherently adopts BICM, while the fully neural base-
line does not use interleaving, as it offers no benefit due to
architectural differences.
Fig. 2(a) demonstrates that under noiseless feedback, the
proposed E2E concatenated scheme consistently achieves the
lowest BER across all configurations. Among the baselines,
the direct concatenation model with test-time interleaving
outperforms both its non-interleaved counterpart and the fully
neural model, achieving a BER of ≈3.6 × 10−6 at 10 dB.
The fully neural baseline performs slightly better than the
direct concatenation setups in the low SNR regime from 6
to 8 dB, with a BER of ≈1.7×10−3 at 6 dB. In contrast, all
E2E variants substantially outperform the baselines across the
entire SNR range. The proposed E2E scheme with test-time-
only interleaving achieves a BER of ≈5.7 × 10−9 at 10 dB,
offering more than two orders of magnitude improvement over
the best-performing baseline. Among the E2E configurations,
the no-interleaving variant yields the highest BER, reaching
≈3.5 × 10−8 at 10 dB, indicating that interleaving plays
a crucial role in enhancing robustness. Performance with
test-time-only and train/test-time interleaving remains nearly
identical. Lastly, all feedback-based methods significantly
outperform the 5G LDPC FEC baseline, which achieves
≈5.4 × 10−3 at 10 dB, confirming the benefits of feedback-
based communication.
Fig. 2(b) shows that under noisy feedback, the proposed
E2E concatenated scheme consistently outperforms all base-
lines across the entire SNR range as well. Among the base-
lines, the fully neural model performs best at low SNR,
achieving a BER of ≈1.9 × 10−3 at 6 dB and ≈4.0 × 10−4
at 8 dB. However, at higher SNRs, the direct concatenation
model with test-time interleaving becomes more effective,
achieving ≈1.5 × 10−4 at 9 dB and ≈3.6 × 10−5 at 10 dB,
surpassing the neural baseline. The direct concatenation model
without interleaving yields the highest BER across all SNRs,
starting at ≈7×10−3 at 6 dB and improving to ≈1.5×10−4
at 10 dB, showing a promising error decay but remaining
inferior to all other configurations. In contrast, the E2E
train/test-time interleaving configuration consistently achieves
the best performance, reaching a BER of ≈8.3 × 10−9 at
10 dB and ≈1.5 × 10−8 at 9 dB, achieving approximately
the same performance compared to its noiseless behavior.
Notably, this configuration is the only one that maintains near-
identical performance across noisy and noiseless, with just
a minor degradation at 6 dB where the BER increases from
≈5.1×10−7 to ≈8.3×10−7. This confirms that exposing the
decoder to interleaved bit structures during training enables
it to effectively interpret soft feedback even when corrupted
by multipath distortion and noise. On the other hand, no-
interleaving and test-time-only interleaving variants lack this
level of robustness, showing greater sensitivity to feedback
channel. Overall, these results highlight that while all E2E
variants provide significant gains over traditional baselines,
the train/test-time interleaving configuration offers unmatched
robustness across feedback conditions and SNR regimes.

V. CONCLUSION
We introduced a BICM-enhanced, E2E optimized concate-
nated coding framework that integrates a neural inner en-
coder/decoder with a classical LDPC outer code for feedback-
enabled OFDM communication over multipath fading chan-
nels. By leveraging a differentiable SPA decoder, the entire
system is trained jointly, enabling seamless integration of
neural and classical components. Our results show that all
E2E variants significantly outperform traditional baselines,
with the train/test-time interleaving configuration offering
the most consistent performance across varying SNR and
feedback conditions, while also exhibiting strong resilience to
distortion, making it a practical and robust solution for future
feedback-based communication systems.
