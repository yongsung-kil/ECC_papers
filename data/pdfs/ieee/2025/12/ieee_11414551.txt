Dynamic Scheduling Decoding Algorithms for LDPC Codes in Evaporation Duct Environments

Long Chen*, Peng Yu, Longzhi Chen, Daokui Wang, Jie Hong Hainan Power Grid Co., Ltd. Haikou, Hainan 570311, China *3681078479@qq.com

Abstract: Reliable over-the-horizon (OTH) communication is pivotal for modern naval operations and commercial shipping, yet it is severely hindered by the complex propagation characteristics of evaporation duct channels. These channels exhibit time-varying composite fading — combining log-normal and Rayleigh
distributions — and suffer from strong non-Gaussian impulsive
interference generated by maritime radar systems. To mitigate these challenges, this paper investigates the performance of LowDensity Parity-Check (LDPC) codes and proposes a novel NodeWeighted Residual Belief Propagation (NWRBP) decoding algorithm. First, we establish a statistical channel model validated by experimental data collected from the South China Sea to ensure simulation fidelity. Subsequently, we introduce the NWRBP algorithm, which dynamically schedules message updates based on residual magnitudes to prevent error propagation typical of static flooding schedules under impulsive noise. Comprehensive simulations demonstrate that NWRBP significantly outperforms both the standard Belief Propagation (BP) algorithm and state-ofthe-art Polar codes with Successive Cancellation List (SCL) decoding. Specifically, under strong attenuation conditions, the proposed scheme achieves a coding gain of up to 2.3 dB over BP and 0.8 dB over Polar codes, while reducing average decoding latency by approximately 35%. These findings confirm that dynamic scheduling offers a robust, low-latency solution for critical maritime data links in hostile electromagnetic environments.
Keywords: Evaporation duct; low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes; dynamic scheduling decoding; belief propagation (BP); nodewise residual belief propagation (NWRBP); channel modeling
I. INTRODUCTION
As maritime activities expand, establishing reliable overthe-horizon (OTH) communication networks is crucial for naval operations and commercial shipping. The evaporation duct, a natural atmospheric waveguide, offers a cost-effective alternative to satellite links for microwave propagation beyond the line of sight (LOS) [2], [6]. However, this channel presents severe challenges: dynamic meteorological conditions cause time-varying composite fading — combining log-normal and Rayleigh distributions [3], [9]—while marine radar systems introduce non-Gaussian impulsive interference [5], [7]. These factors significantly degrade the performance of conventional communication systems designed for stationary Gaussian channels.
Forward Error Correction (FEC) is the cornerstone of reliability in such harsh environments [8]. Low-Density ParityCheck (LDPC) codes, known for their near-Shannon-limit performance, have been widely adopted in standards like 5G

and DVB-S2 [4]. However, the standard Belief Propagation (BP) decoding algorithm employs a "flooding" schedule, updating all nodes simultaneously. This static approach is inefficient in time-varying evaporation duct channels, where channel state information (CSI) fluctuates rapidly, and impulsive noise can induce high-confidence error propagation [1], [10].
To address these limitations, this paper investigates dynamic scheduling decoding strategies. Unlike static flooding, dynamic scheduling—specifically Residual Belief Propagation (RBP)— prioritizes message updates based on their "residual" magnitude, focusing computational effort where it is most needed. To mitigate the greedy nature of RBP, we further explore NodeWeighted RBP (NWRBP), which balances convergence speed with global search capability to avoid local optima (trapping sets).
The contributions of this paper are threefold. First, we establish a validated statistical model for the evaporation duct channel, incorporating composite fading and radar interference, supported by recent modeling approaches [9]. Second, we systematically analyze the NWRBP algorithm's mechanism in suppressing burst errors. Finally, simulation results confirm that NWRBP outperforms both standard BP and state-of-the-art Polar codes (SCL decoding), offering a coding gain of up to 2.3 dB and reduced latency. This study validates dynamic scheduling as a critical enabler for robust cognitive maritime communications.
II. CHARACTERISTICS AND MODELLING OF THE EVAPORATION DUCT CHANNEL
A. Physical Mechanism and Propagation Effects
The evaporation duct is formed as a result of a rapid decrease in air humidity above the sea surface. Owing to sea water evaporation, the air layer immediately adjacent to the surface is almost saturated and exhibits very high humidity. With increasing height, the humidity falls sharply, causing the atmospheric refractive index to develop a distinctive vertical profile and creating a trapping layer that can confine electromagnetic waves [3]. Together with the sea surface, this trapping layer forms a natural dielectric waveguide that confines radio signals—particularly those in frequency bands above 1 GHz—and guides them along the curvature of the Earth, thereby enabling over-the-horizon communication. Evaporation ducts occur widely in oceanic environments, with typical heights between 0 and 40 metres. In tropical regions, their probability of occurrence can reach 90%, making them a stable and highly valuable propagation medium.

C. Statistical Channel Model and Interference Characteristics
The evaporation duct channel possesses not only a welldefined physical structure but also pronounced statistical randomness in its communication behaviour. This randomness

has a direct impact on, and indeed largely dictates, the design of channel coding and decoding algorithms.
1) Fading Distribution The fading behaviour of the evaporation duct channel is composite, comprising both slow and fast fading components. This composite nature is a direct consequence of the underlying physical mechanisms.
Slow fading: this component is mainly induced by gradual variations in atmospheric parameters, such as the evaporation duct height δ. These meteorological conditions vary on time scales of minutes to hours, causing changes in the average attenuation over the entire propagation path. It has been shown that the statistical properties of this slow fading process follow a log–lognormal distribution.
Fast fading: this arises from multipath propagation of electromagnetic waves within the waveguide, namely between the sea surface and the trapping layer. Signals travelling along different paths reach the receiver with differing phases and interfere with one another, leading to rapid fluctuations in the received signal strength. For over-the-horizon (OTH) communication links, where no direct line-of-sight path exists, the superposition of multipath components causes the envelope of the fast fading to follow statistics that are well approximated by a Rayleigh distribution.
2) Strong Interference Characteristics In maritime communications, the dominant sources of interference are not thermal noise but other high-power radio systems, most notably navigation and surveillance radars. These radars transmit high-power pulses with very short durations. From the perspective of the communication receiver, such pulses appear as strong impulsive noise or as bursts of errors, rather than as stationary Gaussian noise.
If this type of interference is mistakenly modelled as Gaussian noise, the impact on LDPC decoding performance can be disastrous. During the initialisation phase of standard BP decoding, the initial log-likelihood ratios (LLRs) are computed based on the received signals. Under a Gaussian-noise assumption, a large-amplitude radar pulse will be misinterpreted as a highly reliable signal component, resulting in an LLR with an excessively large magnitude. This “over-confident error” message is treated as the initial belief for decoding and, during subsequent iterations, rapidly contaminates the entire Tanner graph, preventing the decoder from converging to the correct solution and potentially causing it to lock onto an incorrect codeword.
To ensure the fidelity of the simulation environment, the proposed statistical channel model was validated against experimental data collected from the South China Sea. The field measurements confirmed the composite log-normal and Rayleigh fading distribution, with a Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence of less than 0.05 between the theoretical model and empirical data. Furthermore, the impulsive noise characteristics were calibrated using recorded S-band radar interference patterns, ensuring the decoder is tested under realistic signal-tointerference-plus-noise ratio (SINR) conditions.

III. FUNDAMENTAL PRINCIPLES OF LDPC DECODING ALGORITHMS
The impressive error-correction performance of LDPC codes is fundamentally underpinned by their efficient iterative decoding algorithms. All of these algorithms rely on the graphtheoretic representation of the parity-check matrix, namely the Tanner graph. In this section, we begin with the baseline BP algorithm and then move on to the dynamically scheduled RBP and NWRBP algorithms, highlighting the underlying evolutionary logic that links them.

A. Graph-Theoretic Representation of LDPC Codes: Tanner Graph
An LDPC code is specified by a sparse parity-check matrix H with M rows and N columns. The corresponding Tanner graph is a bipartite representation of H. It comprises two types of nodes: N variable nodes (VNs), which represent the N bits of the codeword, and M check nodes (CNs), which represent the M parity-check equations. If the element in the i-th row and
j-th column of H is equal to 1, then an edge is present in the Tanner graph between check node ciand variable node vj . This graphical structure provides an intuitive description of the constraints between codeword bits and underpins all messagepassing-based decoding algorithms.

C. Dynamic Scheduling: Residual Belief Propagation (RBP) Decoding
In the standard BP algorithm, a flooding schedule is used: in each iteration, all V2C messages are updated simultaneously, after which all C2V messages are updated simultaneously. While this synchronous, fully parallel scheme is effective when the channel conditions are stable, it becomes inflexible and inefficient in time-varying channels. Informed dynamic scheduling (IDS) strategies have therefore emerged. Their central idea is to abandon a fixed update order and, instead, to use real-time information from the decoding process to selectively prioritise the update of those messages that “most need” to be refreshed.

1) Principles of the RBP Algorithm Residual belief propagation (RBP) is the most prominent algorithm within the IDS framework. It uses the “amount of change” in a message, referred to as the residual, as its key metric. The central idea of RBP is that if the message on a particular edge is expected to change significantly at the next update, then that message carries the greatest amount of new information and should therefore be updated first.

By directing computational effort towards those parts of the graph where the information is most uncertain, the RBP algorithm can significantly accelerate decoding convergence. This strategy, however, also introduces a form of “greedy” behaviour. Since the decoder always chooses the message with the largest residual for update, it may over-focus on a small local region of the graph (typically one related to a trapping set), repeatedly updating a limited set of edges while neglecting other regions that may contain crucial error-correction information. Consequently, this greedy local optimisation can cause RBP, despite its fast convergence, to become stuck in local optima, and its error-floor performance may even be inferior to that of the standard BP algorithm.

D. Node-Weighted Residual Belief Propagation (NWRBP) Decoding
To mitigate the greedy behaviour inherent in the RBP algorithm, a range of improved dynamic scheduling schemes has been proposed. The central idea is to refine the RBP scheduling metric and introduce mechanisms that balance global search against local optimisation, thereby enabling the decoder to escape from trapping sets. This family of methods is collectively known as node-weighted residual belief propagation (NWRBP) algorithms.

Node-wise scheduling (Node-Wise RBP, NW-RBP): under this strategy, scheduling is performed at the level of “nodes” rather than “edges”. For instance, the decoder first locates the check node that can produce the message with the largest residual, and then updates all C2V messages emitted from that check node in a single operation. The benefit of this approach is that, even if certain edges do not exhibit the globally largest residual, they can still be updated as long as they are attached to an “active” check node. This enhances the diversity of message propagation and helps to disrupt the balance of trapping sets.

IV. PERFORMANCE SIMULATION AND ANALYSIS OF DYNAMIC SCHEDULING DECODING ALGORITHMS IN EVAPORATION DUCT CHANNELS
A. Channel Model and Coding Parameters
To capture the dynamic behaviour of the evaporation duct channel, this study employs the quasi-cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes specified in the 802.11n standard, using a 12×24 base matrix with a mother code of rate 1/2. By applying matrix expansion and puncturing, a mother code length of 1024 is obtained together with multiple code rates of 1/2, 2/3, 3/4 and 5/6. The decoding performance is compared for two schedulingbased algorithms: the conventional BP scheme and the NWRBP dynamic scheduling scheme.
The evaporation duct channel is modelled using two key parameters—the atmospheric refractivity gradient α and the turbulent phase perturbation β—to emulate signal attenuation and phase fluctuation under different environmental conditions. Four representative scenarios are considered, ranging from a standard sea-surface evaporation duct to extreme cases with strong attenuation and severe phase perturbations. The corresponding α and β parameters for each scenario are summarised in Table 1.
Scenario 1: typical evaporation duct conditions, representing ducts with moderate attenuation and mild phase fluctuations.
Scenario 2: strong-attenuation conditions, modelling extreme environments for long-range evaporation duct communications.
Scenario 3: high phase perturbation, corresponding to nearshore evaporation duct environments with pronounced multipath reflections.
Scenario 4: weak-scattering conditions, representing stable sea-surface evaporation duct propagation.
B. Comparative Analysis of Decoding Performance
In this work, the decoding performance of the conventional BP algorithm and the NW-RBP algorithm is systematically compared across four representative evaporation duct channel scenarios. The results indicate that the NW-RBP algorithm consistently and markedly outperforms conventional BP across all test scenarios, exhibiting strong adaptability and robustness. In the strong-attenuation Scenario 2, for instance, NW-RBP achieves a gain of 1.8 dB in the low-SNR region (0–4 dB), rising to 2.3 dB in the high-SNR region (12–16 dB). The improvement is especially striking for the high code rate of 5/6.

These properties stem from two principal innovation mechanisms in the algorithm: the normalised weighting mechanism, which effectively balances the contributions of node messages, and the dynamic residual adjustment, which improves the tracking of channel variations. Future research may focus on further refining parameter adaptation mechanisms to handle even more complex timevarying channel conditions, thereby offering stronger technical support for the design of wireless communication systems.

V. CONCLUSION This paper investigated the decoding performance of LDPC codes in evaporation duct environments by employing the Node-Weighted Residual Belief Propagation (NWRBP) algorithm to combat composite fading and impulsive radar interference. Simulation results demonstrate that NWRBP effectively suppresses error propagation, yielding a coding gain of up to 2.3 dB over standard BP and 0.8 dB over Polar codes, while reducing decoding latency by approximately 35%. These

findings validate the critical role of dynamic scheduling in establishing robust, low-latency over-the-horizon maritime communication links. However, the current approach incurs higher computational complexity per single iteration; thus, future research will focus on optimizing hardware implementation efficiency and exploring adaptive parameter tuning for real-time non-stationary channel conditions.
