An FPGA-Based LDPC Decoder for Smart-NICs
Hassan Chreif, Lounis Zerioul, Julio C. Pe´rez-Garc´ıa, Stefano Secci, Sami Taktak CNAM, Paris, France
{hassan.chreif, lounis.zerioul, julio.perez-garcia, stefano.secci, sami.taktak}@cnam.fr

Abstract—Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes are a key component of modern wireless standards. Most FPGA-based implementations emphasize decoding performance while overlooking system-level concerns such as network integration and autonomous operation. This paper introduces a configurable FPGA-based LDPC decoder with an integrated UDP/Ethernet stack, enabling direct offloading in real 5G New Radio (NR) deployments for 5G access networks equipped with FPGA-based smart-network interface cards (Smart-NICs). The architecture supports multiple base graphs, variable lifting sizes, and diverse code rates through parameter-driven synthesis, while a dual-clock design ensures reliable timing across heterogeneous interfaces. Implemented on the NetFPGA-SUME platform, the proposed decoder achieves efficient resource utilization (15.09% of FPGA resources), a sustained throughput of 181.51 Mbps, and an endto-end decoding latency of 423.1 microseconds. Experimental evaluation further reports a bit error rate of 4.688 × 10−5 at 6 dB of Eb/N0, confirming good performance and deployment readiness.
Index Terms—FPGA, LDPC, Decoder, 5G, New Radio, dualclock, Smart-NICs.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) coding is a fundamental component of 3GPP 5G New Radio (NR) [1], providing robust forward error correction (FEC). The effectiveness of an FEC scheme is usually evaluated based on two main criteria: complexity, which includes area and power consumption, and performance, typically measured in terms of coding gain. LDPC codes excel in both aspects, enabling efficient implementations with high coding gains, which explains their extensive adoption in modern communication standards [2]. More recently, they have also emerged as suitable workloads for in-network acceleration on Smart-NIC platforms [3].
Many wireless standards use LDPC codes for FEC, including IEEE 802.11 [4], Digital Video Broadcasting (DVB) [5] and 10 Gigabit Ethernet [6]. However, the more demanding requirements of 5G NR and future networks require greater innovation in LDPC code design. Currently, the LDPC decoder is one of the most computationally demanding components of a 5G modem, often accounting for up to 40% of the hardware resources required to implement the physical layer (PHY). To address these challenges, the decoder must be designed with a high degree of flexibility and adaptability [7].
With the shift toward software-defined radios (SDR) [8], specialized hardware accelerators are increasingly used to offload LDPC encoding and decoding from general-purpose CPUs. LDPC decoding is highly parallel and computationally intensive, making CPU-only execution inefficient. Offloading to FPGA, ASIC, or GPU-based accelerators reduces latency,

improves energy efficiency, and frees CPU resources. Among these, FPGAs are particularly attractive, offering a balance of performance, energy efficiency, low cost, and flexibility, enabling high throughput while adapting to evolving standards and deployment scenarios. These properties also make them an excellent fit for Smart-NICs, where packet-level programmability and reconfigurable acceleration are critical.
A. Research Motivation and Contributions
In the literature, most FPGA-based offloading solutions rely on PCI Express (PCIe) interfaces to transfer LDPC encoding and decoding tasks from the CPU. While PCIe offers high bandwidth and low latency, it is inherently limited in physical reach, typically supporting connections of up to one meter (PCIe 7.0). By contrast, modern data centers and edge computing infrastructures increasingly demand offloading over longer distances, which can be achieved using high-speed network interfaces such as Ethernet or optical links. Smart Network Interface Cards (Smart-NICs), capable of line-rate innetwork computing, are emerging in mobile access networks, disaggregated Radio Access Networks (RANs), and Multiaccess Edge Computing (MEC) platforms.
We propose a configurable FPGA-based LDPC decoder system that integrates network processing with the LDPC decoder. The design includes a full UDP/Ethernet stack on FPGA, covering Ethernet MAC, IP processing, and UDP handling. This integration removes the boundary between signal processing and network interface, allowing deployment in networked systems without extra hardware or software.
In summary, the specific contributions of this paper are:
• A flexible and configurable 5G NR LDPC decoder system, accommodating multiple base graphs, variable lifting sizes, and a wide range of code lengths and rates.
• A production-ready FPGA implementation that ensures full protocol compliance and reliable timing across dualclock domains. The implementation supports autonomous operation, including flow control, error detection and correction, and end-to-end Ethernet-to-UDP processing, making it directly applicable for Smart-NIC deployment.
• Comprehensive performance evaluation, reporting decoding latency, sustained throughput, and benchmarks under both standalone and network-integrated scenarios. Experimental results demonstrate real-time operation in practical 5G NR environments.

II. RELATED WORKS
This section reviews key FPGA-based LDPC decoder designs and their integration into communication systems. We first cover recent high-throughput, resource-efficient implementations for 5G NR. Next, we summarize flexible architectures enabling runtime adaptability to different codes. Finally, we examine network-enabled FPGA systems, highlighting challenges in integrating LDPC decoding with real-time Ethernet. These studies provide context for our approach, bridging algorithm-level decoders and deployable Smart-NIC solutions.
A. FPGA-based LDPC Decoder Implementations
Recent FPGA-based LDPC decoders have aimed at higher throughput for 5G NR. Tran-Thi et al. [9] achieved 2.82 Gbps on Xilinx Kintex UltraScale+ using the HOMS algorithm, improving resource efficiency and reducing check node memory by 10%. Mejmaa et al. [10] proposed a fully parallel Quasi-Cyclic Low-Density Parity-Check (QC-LDPC) decoder with the SOLL technique, validated via HDL co-simulation, showing BER performance across multiple code rates. Pourjabar and Choi [11] presented a multi-mode decoder achieving 2.1 Gbps on FPGA (13.46 Gbps in 28 nm synthesis) with adaptive parallelism and multi-frame support, though limited to single-packet scenarios without network interface handling.
On the other hand, Subhi and Al-Doori [12] presented a comparative study of LDPC decoder implementations using both direct VHDL and MATLAB-to-VHDL conversion approaches. Their results showed that direct VHDL implementation achieved 37.13% reduction in logic gates and improved thermal power dissipation, while MATLAB conversion provided 52.19% reduction in pin usage. However, both approaches were evaluated exclusively in controlled synthesis environments without consideration for network integration or real-world deployment scenarios.
The authors in [13] implement a QC-LDPC decoder with the SOMS algorithm, reaching 13.3 Gbps peak throughput and 0.77 µs latency on Xilinx Zynq-Ultrascale+. Sun et al. [14] achieved 7.76 Gbps with a modified 2-bit Min-Sum algorithm, reducing resource usage by two-thirds.
Other implementations prioritized efficiency and resource optimization. Chen et al. [15] reached 27.85 Mbps decoding throughput on FPGA for irregular QC-LDPC codes with dualdiagonal parity, supporting multiple code rates, while AbdelMomen et al. [16] achieved 224 Mbps with 50% area reduction on NI myRIO 1900 ZYNQ FPGA. Despite these improvements, all designs were limited to algorithm-level evaluation or

controlled testbench environments, lacking network integration and practical deployment capabilities.
B. Flexible and Stochastic Decoder Architectures
Recent research has explored flexible decoder architectures that can adapt to different code configurations. Tera et al. [17] introduced a flexible FPGA-based stochastic decoder for 5G LDPC codes, achieving runtime flexibility for different code rates without FPGA reprogramming. Their automated design flow enabled support for multiple 5G NR code configurations and demonstrated 26% reduction in hardware utilization and 52% increase in energy efficiency compared to state-of-theart designs. The decoder runtime adaptability represented a significant advancement in operational flexibility, though the implementation remained confined to controlled testbench environments.
Benhayoun et al. [18] focused on ultra-reliable low-latency communications (URLLC) requirements, presenting a parallel software implementation achieving 150 µs decoding latency for 288 bits with 50% memory size reduction and threetime speedup compared to previous software implementations. Their work addressed the computational complexity challenges for IoT devices but remained focused on software implementation rather than complete hardware-software integration.
C. Network-Enabled FPGA Systems and Integration Challenges
Although FPGA-based LDPC decoders have achieved high performance, integration with network processing remains largely unaddressed. Most designs focus on testbench validation with direct log-likelihood ratios (LLRs) inputs, leaving a gap between prototypes and deployable systems. Le Gal and Je´go [19] demonstrated that CPU-based decoders can achieve 50 to 100 Mbps throughput, but their approach does not exploit FPGA capabilities or real-time network protocols, relying on pre-formatted LLR data.
Existing implementations assume idealized I/O conditions, often using simulation interfaces or PCIe, and fail to address challenges such as Ethernet packet reception, protocol parsing, and real-time LLR extraction. Commercial FPGA platforms like Xilinx Alveo and Intel Stratix provide network acceleration, but integration with custom LDPC decoding requires extensive effort. Network and processing functions typically operate in separate domains, lacking the specialized pipeline management needed for continuous LDPC processing with live network data.

B. 5G Quasi-Cyclic LDPC Codes Background
Quasi-Cyclic (QC) LDPC codes represent an important structured LDPC code family extensively used in modern communication systems [21]. These codes offer significant advantages over random LDPC codes, including comparable decoding performance with simplified and structured design, excellent suitability for hardware implementations through parallelizable architectures, simple interconnection networks, and support for flexible block lengths that are essential for adaptive communication systems.
1) QC-LDPC Code Structure: QC-LDPC codes are mathematically defined by a base matrix B of size L×C with entries bi,j ≥ −1, where i ∈ {1, 2, . . . , L} and j ∈ {1, 2, . . . , C}. The parity check matrix (PCM) H of a QC-LDPC code is constructed by expanding the base matrix using an expansion factor Z. Each element in the base matrix is systematically replaced with sparse matrices of size Z × Z according to specific rules: elements with bi,j = −1 are replaced by zero matrices, while elements with bi,j > −1 are replaced by circulant permutation matrices created by right-shifting the identity matrix by bi,j positions.
This construction results in a PCM H with M = Z ×L rows and N = Z × C columns, providing the structural regularity that enables efficient hardware implementation.
2) 5G NR LDPC Code Specifications: The 5G LDPC code design supports extensive flexibility in code sizes and code rates, making it suitable for diverse communication scenarios. The base matrix is extensible by expansion factors up to Z =

C. Layered Decoding Architecture with Min-Sum Algorithm
Quasi-Cyclic Low-Density Parity-Check (QC-LDPC) codes enable efficient layered decoding due to their circulant matrix construction. In this approach, the parity-check matrix H is partitioned into L horizontal layers, where each layer consists of Z consecutive rows corresponding to a row of the base matrix B. This structure guarantees that each column of H contains exactly one non-zero element per layer, permitting intra-layer parallelism while preserving sequential inter-layer processing.
Layered decoding relies on iterative message passing between variable nodes (VNs) and check nodes (CNs). Variableto-check (VTC) messages αm,n are sent from VN n to CN m, while check-to-variable (CTV) messages βm,n flow in the opposite direction. The a priori information from the channel is denoted γn. This framework supports efficient realizations of decoding algorithms such as the Min-Sum algorithm.
As illustrated in Fig. 1, the Min-Sum algorithm approximates belief propagation while reducing computational complexity, making it well-suited for hardware implementation. In the LDPC processing domain, unpacked log-likelihood ratio (LLR) inputs are decoded using the layered Min-Sum algorithm, fully compliant with 5G NR specifications. The decoded output is serialized, formatted into AXI streams, and repackaged into UDP packets for transmission. Auxiliary modules such as debug monitors, counters, and pipeline registers facilitate system verification, status monitoring, and error recovery. The modular architecture ensures robust operation across clock domains, enabling high-throughput communication and decoding.
1) Dual-Clock Domain Strategy: The system employs a dual-clock domain architecture to balance performance and resource utilization. The network domain handles Ethernet MAC processing, IP/UDP protocol handling, and flow control, while the LDPC domain performs all algorithmic decoding tasks, including LLR handling, iterative message passing, and output serialization. This separation allows each domain to operate at an optimized frequency, ensuring efficient resource allocation and continuous data flow across heterogeneous subsystems.
2) Network Interface Architecture: The entire network processing stack is implemented on an FPGA, eliminating the need for external network interface hardware. The interface integrates with SFP+ transceivers, providing a direct connection to network infrastructure without additional protocol conversion. Full IEEE 802.3 Ethernet MAC functionality is supported, including frame validation, CRC checking, and automatic error detection and recovery.
On top of the Ethernet layer, the system implements complete IP and UDP processing, encompassing header parsing, checksum verification, and protocol compliance. Configurable packet filtering based on UDP destination ports allows se-

lective processing of LDPC-encoded data while discarding unrelated network traffic.
3) Clock Domain Crossing Architecture: Data transfer between the network and LDPC domains is managed through asynchronous FIFO structures with integrated flow control. On the ingress path, high-capacity FIFOs buffer UDP payloads containing LLR values, absorbing burst traffic and frequency variations while maintaining uninterrupted decoding. Optimized data packing enables multiple LLRs per word, maximizing inter-domain bandwidth. Backpressure mechanisms signal upstream modules to prevent data loss under high traffic.
On the egress path, dedicated FIFOs buffer decoded output bits prior to network transmission. Automatic UDP packet reconstruction, including header generation and checksum calculation, ensures standards compliance. Coordinated scheduling aligns LDPC output with network timing constraints, preserving throughput and data integrity.
4) LDPC Processing Pipeline Implementation: The LDPC pipeline consists of three stages: input parsing, core decoding, and output serialization. The input stage extracts LLRs from UDP payloads, performs header validation, and aligns data for decoding. The core stage executes layered Min-Sum decoding, parameterized for all 5G NR base graphs, lifting sizes, and code rates. Configurable iteration limits with early termination provide a trade-off between error-correction capability and decoding latency. The output stage serializes the decoded bits, validates their order, and constructs UDP packets for transmission.

B. Critical Path Analysis and Timing Performance
The Network domain operates reliably up to a frequency of 156.25 MHz (6.40 ns period), providing adequate margin for 10-Gigabit Ethernet packet processing. The LDPC domain runs at 75 MHz (13.33 ns period), accommodating iterative decoding computations while ensuring reliable throughput. Overall, these results validate the dual-clock architecture and confirm reliable operation of both the processing and communication pipelines within their respective frequency domains.
C. Network Protocol Integration
A UDP/IP/Ethernet stack based on verilog-ethernet [22] was integrated to provide autonomous packet handling. Ethernet, IP, and UDP modules were reused with minor buffer adjustments, while the LDPC processing pipeline was fully developed in VHDL, available online1 for reference. The system selectively filters LDPC traffic, generates response packets, and employs multi-level buffering to absorb network jitter. Comprehensive error detection, checksum verification at all layers, and AXI4-Stream flow control ensure reliable, lossless operation under high-throughput conditions.

VII. CONCLUSIONS
We introduce an FPGA-based integration of a configurable 5G NR LDPC decoder with a full UDP/Ethernet network stack, with target integration in a Smart-NIC in disaggregated RAN environments. The system supports all 5G NR base graphs, lifting sizes, and code rates through parameter-driven synthesis, achieving 181.51 Mbps throughput, 423.1 µs end-toend latency, 1.19 Gbps LDPC core throughput, 100% packet reliability, and efficient resource utilization (15.09% LUTs, 69.12% BRAM) with dual-clock operation (156 MHz network, 75 MHz LDPC) at 5.731 W. BER evaluation confirms robust error correction: at Eb/N0 = 3 dB, the FPGA-based decoder achieves a BER of 1.34×10−3, outperforming uncoded BPSK over AWGN (3.87 × 10−3) and delivering more than an order of magnitude gain over uncoded Nakagami-m fading (3.81 × 10−2). No error floor was observed up to 6 dB. These results demonstrate the feasibility of deploying LDPC decoding as an in-network function on FPGA-based SmartNIC platforms.
Future work will focus on porting the proposed architecture to NetFPGA-Alveo platforms based on UltraScale+ FPGAs, aiming to enhance throughput by leveraging higher operating frequencies, parallel LDPC cores, and optimized scheduling for multi-stream decoding. We will also explore runtime resource allocation to enable dynamic switching between LDPC configurations, as well as integrating machine learning for channel estimation and adaptive decoding to enhance performance for next-generation applications. Additionally, the architecture will be extended to operate under more demanding and realistic network conditions, including burst traffic, concurrent decoding streams, and packet loss scenarios typical of Smart-NIC and edge computing environments. The implementation of adaptive buffering and lightweight reliability mechanisms, such as sequence numbering over UDP-lite, will be investigated to improve robustness during high-throughput operation.
