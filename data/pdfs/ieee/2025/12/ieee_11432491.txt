GLDPC Codes Based on Polar Constraints and Their Near-Optimal Decoding
Binghui Shi, Yongpeng Wu, Yin Xu, Xiqi Gao, Xiaohu You, and Wenjun Zhang

GLOBECOM 2025 - 2025 IEEE Global Communications Conference | 979-8-3315-7781-0/25/$31.00 ©2025 IEEE | DOI: 10.1109/GLOBECOM59602.2025.11432491

Abstract—In this work, we introduce the integration of generalized low-density parity-check (GLDPC) codes with short polar component codes, termed GLDPC codes with polar component codes (GLDPC-PC). A recently proposed soft-input soft-output (SISO) decoder for polar-like codes enables effective iterative belief propagation decoding for GLDPC-PC. This SISO decoder after a post-processing exhibits little performance loss to the optimal SISO decoder when all the variable nodes have relatively low degrees. A three-step method is introduced to design protograph-based GLDPC codes. The constructed GLDPC codes are compared with 5G LDPC codes. They exhibit little performance loss in the waterfall region and possess better error floor with less iterations.
Index Terms—Generalized LDPC codes, soft-input soft-output decoding, polar codes, density evolution.
I. INTRODUCTION
Generalized low-density parity-check (GLDPC) [1] codes generalize low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [2] by replacing the single parity-check (SPC) with more general constraints based on stronger component codes. The belief propagation (BP) decoding of GLDPC codes iteratively pass extrinsic information between the variable nodes (VNs) and check nodes (CNs) in the Tanner graph where every CN represents a component code. The soft-input soft-output (SISO) decoder for the component codes is necessary for their BP decoding. One of the major obstacles in the development of GLDPC codes is the lack of low-complexity SISO decoders.
The optimal SISO decoding, called Bahl, Cocke, Jelinek and Raviv (BCJR) algorithm, is proposed in [3]. However, its computational complexity grows exponentially for linear block codes in the number of parity checks when the code rate is more than half. Reference [4] proposes an approximation based on list decoding but lacks precision and requires optimization of a weight factor and a saturation value. Reference [5] proposes the soft-output guessing random additive noise decoding and applies it in several GLDPC

Polar-like codes [6] represent a wide range of modifications of polar codes, whose codebook is obtained by performing a linear transformation to the polar codebook [7]. A recent work [8] proposes soft-output SCL (SO-SCL) to extract the bitwise soft information for polar-like codes. Based on this method, we are able to decode GLDPC with polar code as component code (GLDPC-PC) under low complexity. Postprocessing of the soft output is widely applied to achieve better performance for a sub-optimal SISO decoder [4], [9], [10].
Protograph-based LDPC code is introduced in [11] and is extended in [12], [13]. GLDPC code can also be constructed from a protograph. In this paper, we introduce our three-step method to construct a protograph-based GLDPC code. The SISO decoder of the polar component code adopts SO-SCL and BCJR algorithm. We apply a post-processing to SO-SCL and it demonstrates performance close to BCJR algorithm when all the VNs have relatively low degree. The constructed GLDPC codes are compared with the the 5-th generation wireless system (5G) LDPC codes. They outperform 5G LDPC codes in the error floor region and only exhibit little loss in waterfall region. Therefore, these codes are suitable for scenarios requiring high reliability.

III. DESIGN OF GLDPC-PC CODE
In this section, the design method of the GLDPC-PC codes are introduced. The criteria of code design is based on minimizing the iterative decoding threshold obtained by the Monte Carlo (MC) density evolution (DE). Exhaust search of the protograph is adopted and a three-step method is proposed to reduce the search space.

A. Protograph-Based GLDPC codes
A protograph [11] is a small Tanner graph with nc CNs and VNs. The biadjacency matrix H of a protograph is called its protomatrix. The process that turns a protograph into a Tanner graph is called lifting. During the process of lifting, the protograph is firstly replicated for M times. Then, every edge in the protograph has M copies and they are permuted among the M disconnected copies of the protograph. This procedure connects the M graphs and results in a Tanner graph with M nc CNs and M nv VNs. M is called the lifting factor.
We can obtain a GLDPC code from a protograph and lifting. Every CN in a GLDPC code represents a specific code called component code. The code length of the component code must be the same as the number of VNs connected to the CN. If two CNs connect to one VN, different random column permutation should be conducted to their component codes to eliminate the potential correlation of the two codes. Let Hi denote the parity-check matrix of the component code of the i-th CN in the protograph. Then, the parity check matrix of a GLDPC code is obtained via replacing the j-th 1 in i-th row of H by the j-th column of Hi [15]. If the component code of the i-th CNs has code dimension ni and information dimension ki, the GLDPC codes from this protograph have rate R = 1 − (Σni=c1(ni − ki)) /nv.
Furthermore, if the permutations applied in lifting are restricted into circulant permutations, then quasi-cyclic (QC)

GLDPC code is obtained. It is easy to show that the paritycheck matrices of QC GLDPC code consists of circulant permutation matrix (CPM) of size M × M .
B. Monte Carlo Density Evolution
The iterative decoding threshold [16] is defined by the minimum SNR to ensure that the expected bit error rate (BER) approaches zeros as the blocklength grows to infinity within a ensemble of codes.
The conventional way to compute threshold is known as DE [16]. For GLDPC codes, extracting the closed-form solution of extrinsic LLRs is hard. Therefore, the MC DE [9] is proposed to approximate the threshold for protographs of GLDPC codes. Taking AWGN channel as an example, MC DE can determine whether an SNR Eb/N0 is beyond the threshold or not. For a protograph of a GLDPC code of rate R, this algorithm has the following steps:
1) Replicate the protograph for M times. Randomly generate M nv Gaussian distributed channel LLRs with mean µch = 4REb/N0 and variance σc2h = 2µch and assign them to every VN in the M protographs.
2) Randomly connect the M protographs and perform the BP decoding for one iteration. If the SISO decoder adopts BCJR algorithm, no extra operation is needed. If BCJR is unrealizable and a near-optimal decoder is adopted, an operation termed as ”make extrinsic” should be performed [9].
3) Determine if the extrinsic LLRs of every VN is greater than 0. If this is true, Eb/N0 is beyond the threshold. If not, go back to step 2. If the number of iteration achieves an upper bound, terminate the algorithm and determine that Eb/N0 is below the threshold.
C. Code Design
For the design of LDPC codes, the exhaust search of their protograph [12] is adopted since the complexity of their threshold computation is low. Subject to the high complexity of MC DE, this is impossible for GLDPC codes. To limit the search space, we adopt a three-step method to design the GLDPC codes.
The first step is CN configuration selection and GLDPC code rate determination. We firstly choose CN configuration, i.e., the value of nc and the component code for every CN. Once the CN configuration are chosen, the GLDPC code can only perform well around a certain code rate, which can be explained by the extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) function [17]. The chosen CN configuration have a known CN EXIT function IA,c = f (IE,c) and matching the VN EXIT function to it restricts the code rate around a fixed value Rbest. This value can be approximated by Rbest ≈ f (ϵ) for sufficient small ϵ > 0 if f is the CN EXIT function in binary erasure

channel (BEC). In BEC, the area between the VN and CN EXIT functions is equal to the rate loss [17]. If we assume that a VN degree distribution (d.d.) whose EXIT function IE,v = g(IA,v) is very close to with the known CN EXIT function is obtained. Then code rate of the GLDPC code with this VN d.d. and CNs is very close to the channel capacity, which is revealed by the start point of the VN EXIT function g(0). Since we assume the two EXIT functions are close, we obtain Rbest ≈ g(0) ≈ f (ϵ).
The second step is enumerating the VNs d.d.. To decrease the search space, we constrain the maximum number of parallel edges in the protograph to be 3. So the maximum degree of a VN is 3nc. To fully utilize the error-checking capability of polar codes, the degree-1 VN is allowed. As demonstrated above, the VN d.d. should produce a GLDPC code of rate around Rbest. To satisfy this, most of the VNs are of degree 1 or 2 and only a few VNs are of degree greater 2. Therefore, we only need to enumerate the part of d.d. of degree greater than 2 and the number of degree-1 VNs and set the degrees of other VNs to be 2. Among these VNs of degree greater than 2, selecting one VN to have relative high degree tends to improve the threshold.
The third step is searching the protographs according to the VNs d.d. and selecting the one with lowest threshold gap. In this step, We decompose the degree of every VN into a column vector of length nc. Every degree has many decompositions and their combinations form a huge search space. However, only a small fraction of these combinations can satisfy the requirement of the code length of the CN configuration. Indeed, we only need to enumerate the combination of the column vectors of super VN and degree-1 VNs and determine whether the remaining VNs can meanwhile be of degree 2 and satisfy the code length. Allocating degree-1 VN to one type of CN as uniformly as possible tends to improve the threshold, which can further restrict the search space. Once a valid combination is obtained, a protograph is obtained and its threshold is computed by MC DE. The protograph whose threshold has the smallest gap to AWGN channel capacity is the final adopted protograph.
D. Adjustment for SO-SCL
Through simulations, we obverse that SO-SCL exhibits significant performance loss when there are high-degree VNs. Therefore, we additionally regulate that the degree of every VN shall not exceed 4. Under this regulation, the constructed code has greater threshold gap and the code rate may change. This will be illustrated in the simulations.

Different from optimizing parameters for each iteration in [9], we set the parameters for each iteration to be consistent to reduce the number of parameters. V. NUMERICAL RESULTS
In this section, we design several GLDPC-PC codes and demonstrate their performance in AWGN channel. When executing MC DE, we set M to 105. The polar codes adopted in GLDPC codes are Reed-Muller (RM) codes. The MC simulation for FER terminates once 50 errors are collected.
The iterations of BP decoding is set to 40 and 100 for GLDPC codes and LDPC codes, respectively. The computational complexity of the CNs in GLDPC codes is higher than LDPC codes, while GLDPC codes have less CNs. Together with less iterations, we believe that the BP decoding complexity of the two codes is comparable.
For each CN configuration, we will show two codes: typeI code and type-II code. Type-I code only restricts that the maximum number of parallel edges in the protograph is 3. Only the performance of it under BCJR decoding is shown as explained in Section III-D. Type-II code further restricts that the degree of every VN shall not exceed 4. The performance of it under BCJR and SO-SCL is provided.
We consider QC GLDPC codes. We adopt a lifting method combining circulant progressive edge growth (CPEG) algorithm [18] and approximate cycle extrinsic message degree (ACE) [19] algorithm. Notice that the protograph of GLDPC codes possesses many degree-2 VNs and any circle that is solely composed of these VNs has ACE of zero. Therefore, we only calculate ACE for circles not only composed of these VNs when determining one circulant permutation.

VI. CONCLUSION
In this work, we first introduce the GLDPC-PC codes. These codes exhibit significantly lower error floors, which

brings performance gains in the low FER region. We adopts
the low-complexity SO-SCL decoder as the SISO decoder for
polar codes. We also introduce a three-step method to con-
struct the protograph of the GLDPC code. SO-SCL exhibits
little performance loss to the BCJR algorithm when all the
VNs have relatively low degree.
