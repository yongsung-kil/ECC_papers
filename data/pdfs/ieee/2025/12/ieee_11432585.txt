Abstract—This paper proposes an enhanced guessing codeword decoding (GCD) algorithm, termed GCD with most reliable cyclic basis (MRCB-GCD), specifically tailored for BCH codes. Unlike original GCD, the proposed method selects the k consecutive bits with the highest aggregate reliability as the re-encoding basis, leveraging the cyclic structure of BCH codes. To further enhance search efficiency, a local constraint (LC) mechanism is introduced, where the extended reliable bits of length k+δ enable the algorithm to skip numerous unnecessary test error patterns (TEPs). To stop the search process, we introduce two termination criteria and prove that the proposed approach with the trivial termination criterion forms a non-exhaustive-search maximum likelihood (ML) decoding algorithm. A saddlepoint-based numerical method is developed to approximately calculate the upper bound on the performance gap to the ML decoding and estimate the average number of searches, showing the advantages of the proposed MRCB-GCD over the original GCD in terms of both decoding performance and search efficiency. Simulation results demonstrate that: 1) MRCBGCD outperforms original GCD in both frame error rate (FER) and number of searches, 2) the LC mechanism and termination criteria further reduce the number of searches, and 3) the proposed approach achieves finite-length capacity across various code rates but without requiring Gaussian elimination.
Index Terms—BCH codes, guessing codeword decoding, ordered statistic decoding, saddlepoint approximation.
I. INTRODUCTION
Emerging 5G ultra-reliable low-latency communication [1] and envisioned 6G hyper-reliable low-latency communication [2] scenarios impose stringent performance and latency requirements. It is expected that the potential applications of 6G demand extremely high reliability (with block error rates from 10−5 to 10−7), and ultra-low latency (end-to-end latency from 0.1 ms to 1 ms) [3, 4]. This motivates the investigation of error-correcting codes, particularly short-length codes.
Among various short codes, BCH codes are well-known for their strong error-correcting capabilities, attributed to their large minimum distances [5]. Notably, in the short-length regime,

BCH codes with near-maximum likelihood (ML) decoding can outperform LDPC codes, CA-polar codes and TBCCs, approaching the finite-length capacity [6]. However, an efficient (in terms of complexity) ML or near-ML decoding algorithm for BCH codes remains a challenging goal1.
A well-known universal near-ML decoding approach is ordered statistics decoding (OSD) [10]. This approach focuses on flipping the most reliable positions, which typically contain fewer errors, and then generating a list of codewords by reencoding, from which the most likely codeword is selected. If the flipping patterns are based on Hamming weight, the method corresponds to OSD algorithm [10], while if they rely on soft information, it corresponds to Dorsch’s algorithm [11]. As noted by Fossorier in [12], the hardware implementation of OSD is not friendly, mainly due to the Gaussian elimination (GE) part. The limitations of GE include: 1) The number of steps of GE depends on the received sequence and hence is unpredictable; 2) GE is inherently serial2 and difficult to parallelize, resulting in high latency; 3) GE has cubic computational complexity and requires quadratic memory.
For BCH codes, GE can be avoided by using interpolation polynomials, but re-encoding relies on the associated high-rate RS codes, requiring extensive search and re-encoding [15]. By using the prestored systematic matrix [12] or iterative basis update algorithm [16], the operations of GE can be partially reduced but cannot be avoided. Recently, it has been proved that guessing codeword decoding (GCD) [17] without online GE is an ML decoding algorithm. The GCD is universal since it can be applied to any codes. This, however, usually leads to a relatively large average number of searches due to the suboptimal quality of re-encoding basis. In this paper, we leverage the cyclic structure of BCH codes by selecting k consecutive bits with maximum aggregate reliability as re-encoding basis to improve the quality of the re-encoding basis, thereby enhancing the original GCD while still avoiding GE. III. MRCB-GCD AND PERFORMANCE ANALYSIS

A. MRCB-GCD Algorithm

In contrast to the original OSD, the MRCB-GCD avoids

GE by leveraging the cyclic structure of BCH codes. The key

idea is to select k consecutive bits with maximum aggregate

reliability as the re-encoding basis. Given the basis, we generate

partial TEPs (of length k) in increasing order of soft weight

and perform re-encoding, until either the maximum number of

searches is reached or an early stopping criterion is satisfied.

To further improve the decoding performance, we can extend

the basis from k to k + δ dimensions [18], which introduces

local constraints that enable to skip numerous unnecessary

TEP candidates. In particular, similar LC mechanisms have

been applied to JSCC [19, 20], polar codes with transformed

generator matrices [21], all achieving good performance. Here,

we use subscript I to denote the extended set {1, . . . , k} for the

information part and subscript P for its complementary paritycheck part, resulting in eI ∈ F2k and eP ∈ Fn2 −k. In particular, if δ > 0, the algorithm is referred to as LC-MRCB-GCD,

while if δ = 0, the LC-MRCB-GCD is reduced to the MRCB-

GCD. For notational simplicity and generality, all subsequent

analyses implicitly adopt this unified δ-extended framework

unless otherwise specified.

C. Performance Analysis

1) Upper bound on the performance gap to ML decoding: Formally, the error event of the proposed decoding algorithm can be classified into two sub-events:

• E0: The true TEP (corresponding to the transmitted codeword) is in the list but is not the most likely one. This implies that ML decoding also incurs an error.
• E1: The true TEP is not included in the decoding list. This occurs when the rank of the true TEP is larger than ℓmax, denoted as L(r) > ℓmax and L(r) is a random variable for the rank of the true TEP.

The FER, denoted as ε, of the proposed decoding is

ε = Pr{E0} + Pr{E1},

(19)

max(εML,Pr{L(r) > ℓmax}) ≤ ε ≤ εML+Pr{L(r) > ℓmax}, (20)

FER − εML ≤ Pr{L(r) > ℓmax}.

IV. NUMERICAL RESULTS
In this section, we present numerical results of the proposed algorithms in comparison with other decoding algorithms. The codewords are transmitted over BPSK-AWGN channels. Without loss of generality, we set ℓmax = 106.
Example 4 (Proposed decoding algorithm versus conventional decoding algorithms): Consider BCH code C [127, 64]. Five decoding algorithms are employed for decoding: 1) original OSD algorithm with order t = 4; 2) original GCD [17]; 3) LC-GCD with δ = 12 [26]; 4) proposed MRCB-GCD; 5) proposed LC-MRCB-GCD with δ = 12. The last four decoding algorithms employ the DAI stopping criterion. The results are shown in Fig. 4, from which we observe that the proposed MRCB-GCD achieves better FER performance and requires fewer searches compared to the original GCD. Similarly, the proposed LC-MRCB-GCD outperforms the conventional LCGCD in both aspects. These improvements are attributed to the proposed MRCB manner, which enhances the quality of the re-encoding basis. We also observe that LC-MRCB-GCD achieves comparable performance to original OSD, approaching the RCU bound [27, Theorem 16]. Notably, the proposed LCMRCB-GCD requires significantly fewer searches and avoids the hardware-unfriendly GE compared to the original OSD.
Example 5 (Proposed decoding algorithm versus finite-length capacity): Consider BCH codes of length n = 127 and dimensions k ∈ {22, 36, 50, 64, 78, 92, 106}. Three decoding algorithms are employed for the decoding: 1) original GCD algorithm; 2) proposed MRCB-GCD with δ = 0; 3) proposed LC-MRCB-GCD with δ = 12. All three algorithms employ the DAI stopping criterion. The required SNRs are obtained by simulations for these decoding algorithms and the finitelength capacity is obtained from the RCU bound and the metaconverse (MC) bound [27, Theorem 27], where the target FER is 10−3 for both the simulation and the finite-length capacity. The results are shown in Fig. 5, from which we see that, the LC-MRCB-GCD can approach the finite-length capacity across almost all code rates, demonstrating significant decoding gains over MRCB-GCD and original GCD across a broad range of code rates, excluding the extremely high and low ends.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we proposed an enhanced GCD algorithm for BCH codes by using MRCB instead of trivial basis, thereby improving the quality of the re-encoding basis. Two early stopping criteria were presented to stop the search process. The proposed decoding algorithm with the trivial stopping criterion was proved to be non-exhaustive-search ML. Furthermore, a saddlepoint approximation was derived to approximately calculate the upper bound on the performance gap to the ML decoding and estimate average number of searches. Numerical calculation and simulation results demonstrated that the LCMRCB-GCD outperforms the GCD and LC-GCD, in both decoding performance and search efficiency, and also outperforms the OSD in terms of the average searches. Moreover, for short BCH codes across almost all code rates, the LC-MRCB-GCD approaches the finite-length capacity, but without GE.
