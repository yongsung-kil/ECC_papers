Abstract—As a classic Maximum Distance Separable code, 
Reed-Solomon (RS) codes have been an indispensable core 
technical support in digital communication, data storage and deep 
space exploration since their proposal in 1960, thanks to their 
optimal algebraic error-correcting performance and flexible 
construction. This paper systematically sorts out the theoretical 
evolution and algorithmic innovation of RS codes: starting from 
finite field algebra foundations and two classical constructions—
one based on evaluation mapping and the other as a special case of 
BCH codes—it analyzes three key breakthroughs in decoding 
theory. Classical algebraic decoding including the PGZ, 
Berlekamp-Massey and Euclidean algorithms lays the foundation 
for engineering applications, Guruswami-Sudan list decoding 
breaks the half-minimum distance limit, and the Kotter-Vardy 
algorithm integrates soft information with algebraic decoding. The 
paper focuses on cutting-edge progress from 2021 to 2025, 
covering efficient algorithms such as FFT-based fast joint 
decoding, symbol-level iterative information set decoding and tree-
based Chase-type soft-decision decoding, as well as engineering 
breakthroughs including domestic FPGA-adapted low-resource 
encoders/decoders and RS-LDPC probabilistic joint decoding. 
Finally, it summarizes challenges such as complexity-performance 
balance, emerging coding competition and probabilistic decoding 
framework construction, and prospects future directions including 
AI integration, extreme scenario optimization and deepened 
domestic adaptation, aiming to provide a comprehensive technical 
landscape for researchers and engineers.

I. INTRODUCTION  
In the digital information age, the reliability of information 
transmission and storage is the core cornerstone supporting 
various technological applications. Since Claude E. Shannon 
proposed information theory and established the channel coding 
theorem in 1948, channel coding technology has become a key 
means to approach the channel capacity limit and resist noise and 
interference by introducing controlled redundancy [1]. Among 
numerous error-correcting code schemes, Reed-Solomon (RS) 
codes, with their rigorous algebraic structure, optimal error-
correcting potential (MDS property), and strong robustness 
against burst errors, have undergone more than 60 years of 
evolution and still occupy a core position in the fields of digital 
communication and data storage, demonstrating strong technical 
resilience and application vitality. 
RS codes were first proposed by Irving S. Reed and Gustave 
Solomon in their 1960 paper "Polynomial Codes over Certain 
Finite Fields" [2]. Their core advantage lies in: given the code 
length n and information length k, they can achieve a maximum 
error-correcting capability of t = (n - k)/2. This MDS property 
makes them the preferred coding scheme for many international 
standards—from deep space communication [3], optical disc 
storage [4], to modern data center storage [5], mobile 
communication, and digital television, RS codes all play an 
irreplaceable role in error control. 
The technical vitality of RS codes stems not only from their 
inherent algebraic advantages but also from the continuous 
innovation in decoding theory and engineering implementation. 
Their development process clearly presents a three-stage 
evolutionary logic of "theoretical foundation—boundary 
breakthrough—engineering optimization": the first stage  
focused on classical algebraic decoding, with the successive 
proposal of the Peterson-Gorenstein-Zierler (PGZ) algorithm, 
Berlekamp-Massey (BM) algorithm, and Euclidean algorithm 
[7], which solved the problem of efficient decoding of RS codes 
and laid the theoretical foundation for engineering applications; 
the second stage achieved two key theoretical breakthroughs: the 
Guruswami-Sudan (GS) list decoding algorithm broke the half-
minimum distance limit of traditional hard-decision decoding 
[8], and the Kotter-Vardy (KV) algorithm integrated channel 
soft information into the algebraic decoding framework, 
enabling RS codes to approach the maximum likelihood 
decoding limit; the third stage focuses on the "performance-
complexity" 
balance 
and 
engineering 
implementation, 
witnessing the emergence of innovative algorithms such as fast 
joint 
decoding, 
symbol-level 
iterative 
decoding, 
and 
probabilistic joint decoding, while engineering optimization for 
scenarios such as domestic FPGA adaptation and high-speed 
communication has become a research hotspot [11-21]. 
Despite decades of research on RS codes, driven by 
emerging scenarios such as 5G-Advanced, 6G, and distributed 
storage, they face new technical challenges such as high 
throughput, low latency, and low resource occupancy, which 
also give rise to new research directions.

A. Mathematical Foundation: Finite Fields and Polynomial 
Rings  
The operations of RS codes are performed over a finite field 
(Galois Field), which is a core feature distinguishing them from 
other codes. Specifically, a finite field GF(2m) with 2m elements 
constitutes the operational platform for RS codes [10]. Over this 
field, addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division are 
closed and well-defined, providing a complete mathematical 
foundation for algebraic operations of error-correcting codes. 
For an RS(n,k) code over GF(2m), each symbol in the codeword 
is taken from this field, endowing it with an inherent strong 
capability to correct burst errors. Let n - k = 2t where t denotes 
the error-correcting capability. That is, if the number of 
erroneous data in a set of encoded data does not exceed t, the 
erroneous data can be corrected using n - k parity data. 
B. Two Classical Definitions of RS Codes and Their 
Equivalence 
The construction method of RS codes profoundly influences 
the development of their decoding algorithms, and their two 
main definitions converge theoretically. The first is the 
definition based on evaluation mapping, i.e., the original 
definition. This is the method proposed by Reed and Solomon in 
their original paper [1]. Given k information symbols over 
GF(2m), a (k-1)degree message polynomial m(x) can be formed. 
By evaluating m(x) at n distinct field elements, the resulting 
sequence of nfunction values constitutes an RS codeword. This 
definition intuitively reflects that the encoding process is 
analogous to function interpolation. 
Later, researchers found that RS codes can also be regarded 
as a special class of BCH codes [10]. Under this definition, an 
RS code is determined by its generator polynomial g(𝑥𝑥). For 
an RS(n,k) code defined over GF(2m), the generator polynomial 
is generally taken as g(𝑥𝑥) in (1):

iterative-based BCH decoding. 
C. Systematic Code Encoding Theory 
In practical applications, the systematic code form is highly 
favored for its convenience in directly extracting information 
bits. The encoding of systematic RS codes converts the message 
polynomial m(x)  into a codeword polynomial c(x)  through 
polynomial operations, such that the first k  coefficients 
of c(x)  are the message symbols themselves, and the 
latter n-k  coefficients are parity symbols. This is usually

As shown in Fig. 1, if the k information symbols in the

In summary, the encoding theory of RS codes is built on the 
solid algebraic foundation of finite fields. Their multi-angle 
definition methods not only enrich their theoretical connotation 
but also pave the way for their strong error-correcting capability 
and flexible practical application paths. 
III. EVOLUTION AND BREAKTHROUGHS IN RS DECODING 
THEORY 
The decoding theory of RS codes is the most dynamic and 
creative part of their entire technical system, and its development 
process can be regarded as an epitome of the evolution of coding 
theory. This chapter elaborates on this process in detail, from 
classical algebraic decoding that laid the foundation, to list 
decoding that broke traditional limits, and then to soft-decision 
decoding that utilizes channel soft information, aiming to reveal 
its inherent development logic and theoretical innovation.

A. Classical Algebraic Decoding: From Direct Solution to 
Iterative Approximation 
Early decoding theory's core is to transform the decoding 
problem into solving algebraic equations over finite fields. Its 
standard process includes syndrome calculation, key equation 
solving, error location determination which is Chien search, and 
error value calculation which is Forney algorithm. Among these, 
the solution efficiency of the key equation—the error-locator 
polynomial—directly determines the decoder's performance. As 
shown in Fig. 2, the complete process of classical algebraic 
decoding of RS codes is clearly illustrated. 
 
Fig. 2. Flowchart of classical algebraic decoding for RS codes. 
The PGZ algorithm, proposed by Peterson, Gorenstein, and 
Zierler, is the first complete algebraic decoding framework [13]. 
Its idea is to directly obtain the error-locator polynomial by 
solving a system of linear equations composed of syndromes. 
The algorithm is conceptually clear, but its computational 
complexity grows as O(t3) with the increase of error-correcting 
capability t, making it difficult to meet the practical application 
requirements of high error-correcting capability.

algorithm proposed by Berlekamp and Massey from different 
perspectives [9]. The ingenuity of the BM algorithm lies in 
skillfully transforming the problem of solving the key equation 
into an iterative process of constructing the shortest linear 
feedback shift register. Through a series of iterative steps, the 
algorithm gradually refines the estimation of the error-locator 
polynomial and finally obtains the correct solution with a 
complexity of O(t2). Its standard iterative implementation steps

B. Soft-Decision Decoding: Theoretical Breakthrough 
Beyond Half-Minimum Distance 
Soft-decision decoding utilizes the probabilistic reliability 
information output by the channel instead of the quantized 
values of hard decisions, which can significantly improve 
decoding 
performance. 
Compared 
with 
hard-decision 
algorithms with an error-correcting radius limited to half the 
minimum distance t=(d-1)/2 , soft-decision algorithms can 
achieve a gain of approximately 2–3 dB by utilizing symbol-
level or bit-level reliability information, approaching the 
Shannon limit. This section systematically elaborates on three 
core soft-decision algorithms—Guruswami-Sudan (GS) list 
decoding, Kotter-Vardy (KV) soft-decision decoding, and Chase 
decoding algorithm—focusing on analyzing their theoretical 
principles, algorithm steps, and performance comparisons. 
The GS list decoding algorithm breaks the error-correcting 
boundary of traditional hard decisions, allowing the decoder to 
output a finite-sized list of candidate codewords when the 
number of errors exceeds t , ensuring that the transmitted 
codeword is included with high probability. Its theoretical core 
is based on algebraic interpolation and factorization [8]. First, a 
bivariate polynomial Q(x,y) is constructed that passes through 
all received points (xi,yi) and is assigned higher multiplicities at 
high-reliability points, where the multiplicity assignment 
strategy determines the error-correcting capability of the 
algorithm. Then, Q(x,y) is factorized into linear factors of the 
form y-p(x), where each factor corresponds to a candidate 
codeword polynomial p(x) with degree less than k. The error-
correcting radius of the GS algorithm is increased 
from (1-R)/2 for hard decisions to (1-√R)/2 where R is the code 
rate, 
significantly 
enhancing 
robustness 
in 
high-noise 
environments. Theoretical analysis shows that as the list size 
approaches infinity, the GS algorithm can asymptotically reach 
this bound.

Simulation results for the Reed–Solomon code with 
rate R=143/255 are summarized in Fig. 4. One can see from Fig. 
4 that at codeword error rates of 10-3 and lower, algebraic soft-
decision decoding provides a coding gain of about 1.5 dB, 
whereas GMD decoding and Guruswami–Sudan decoding 
achieve coding gains of about 0.2 and 0.4 dB, respectively, 
compared to conventional hard-decision decoding. Although the

evident from Fig. 4 that most of this gain can be obtained with 
very small list sizes. A list of size 4 already outperforms both 
GMD and Guruswami–Sudan decoding by a substantial margin, 
while a list of size 32 approaches the asymptotic performance to 
within 0.1 dB. 
The KV soft-decision decoding algorithm extends the GS 
framework to the field of soft decisions, converting channel soft 
information into a multiplicity assignment strategy in the 
interpolation process through a reliability matrix, realizing the 
integration of probabilistic information and algebraic decoding. 
The core steps of the algorithm include reliability matrix 
construction, 
multiplicity 
assignment, 
and 
weighted 
interpolation and factorization. First, a q×n  matrix Π  is 
constructed 
based 
on 
the 
posterior 
probability Πi,j=Pr(X=xi|Y=yj) to quantify the reliability of each 
symbol. Then, an iterative algorithm is used to assign 
multiplicities to each received point, with high-reliability points 
obtaining higher multiplicities to guide the interpolator to focus 
on 
credible 
information. 
Finally, 
the 
bivariate 
polynomial Q(x,y) is solved under the multiplicity constraint, 
and factorization is performed to obtain candidate codewords. 
The asymptotic performance of the KV algorithm is determined 
by a geometric condition: if the angle θ between the received 
vector rand the true codeword c satisfies cos θ ≥√R+o(1), then 
decoding is successful. 
The Chase decoding algorithm realizes soft decisions by 
generating a set of test patterns combined with a fixed-distance 
decoder. Its core idea is to perturb the low-reliability symbols in 
the received vector to generate multiple candidate versions, 
decode them using a hard-decision decoder such as the 
Berlekamp-Massey algorithm, and finally select the optimal 
candidate codeword. The algorithm flow is as follows: first, 
screen the e symbols with the lowest reliability in the received 
vector (where  e≥t) to identify low-reliability symbols; then 
generate multiple candidate test patterns by flipping the values 
of low-reliability symbols; next, apply a hard-decision decoder 
such as the BM algorithm to each test pattern to obtain candidate 
codewords through hard-decision decoding; finally, select the 
optimal candidate codeword based on the simulated weight, 
which is a weighted distance integrating symbol reliability. 
Chase-like algorithms have controllable complexity, which 
grows linearly with the number of test patterns, and low 
hardware implementation difficulty, making them widely used

IV. RECENT RESEARCH HOTSPOTS AND ENGINEERING 
BREAKTHROUGHS 
In recent years, research on RS codes has focused on three 
core goals: "complexity reduction", "performance optimization", 
and "scenario adaptation", leading to a number of achievements 
with both theoretical innovation and engineering value—from 
fast joint decoding algorithms to symbol-level iterative 
optimization, from domestic FPGA adaptation design to RS-
LDPC joint decoding, continuously promoting the in-depth 
application of RS codes in scenarios such as high-speed 
communication, distributed storage, and domestic hardware. 
This section systematically summarizes the key progress of this 
stage. 
A. Fast Decoding Algorithms: Complexity and Efficiency 
Optimization 
The research "Fast Error and Erasure Decoding Algorithm 
for Reed-Solomon Codes" addresses the problem of joint error 
and erasure decoding of RS codes and proposes an efficient 
decoding algorithm based on Fast Fourier Transform [11]. The 
paper first defines the syndromes for error and erasure decoding 
and derives the complete key equation, solving the defect that 
existing FFT-based decoding algorithms have incomplete key 
equations and cannot cover all decodable modes, thus extending 
the existing algorithms applicable only to error decoding to the 
scenario of joint error and erasure decoding. Through FFT 
optimization, modular arithmetic methods, and partial 
transformation techniques, the algorithm achieves an optimal 
time complexity of O(nlog( n-k)+(n-k)log2 ( n-k))  and breaks 
the constraint that the parameter (n-k) must be a power of 2, 
making it applicable to RS codes with arbitrary parameters [12].

The research "Symbol-level iterative information set 
decoding of Reed-Solomon Codes" addresses the problems of 
high complexity in bit-level soft-decision decoding and 
excessive iterations in traditional symbol-level decoding such as 
PTA in existing iterative decoding of RS codes . The study 
proposes a low-complexity symbol-level iterative information 
set decoding algorithm (kSD) [14]. The core innovation of the 
algorithm lies in extending the symbol-level dense parity-check 
matrix, introducing low-weight parity-check equations to 
increase matrix sparsity, and integrating the idea of information 
set decoding. Through steps including symbol reliability 
evaluation, extended matrix column permutation, syndrome 
checking, symbol voting, and confidence update, soft 
information is used to iteratively optimize the decoding accuracy 
of k information set symbols. A dual stopping criterion—where 
the syndrome is 0 or the number of parity-check equations meets 
the threshold—is designed to achieve fast convergence and 
complete codeword reconstruction.

B. Domestic FPGA-Adapted Low-Resource Encoder/Decoder 
Design 
The research "BIST on Domestic FPGA" addresses the 
problems that some domestic FPGA lack RS encoding/decoding 
IP cores and cross-platform transplantation and debugging are 
complex. The study designs a low-complexity, low-resource 
RS(36,20) 
encoder/decoder—operating 
over 
the 
Galois 
field GF(28) with a maximum capability of correcting 8 symbol 
errors—and implements the Built-In Self-Test (BIST) function 
by integrating a Pseudorandom Binary Sequence module [16]. 
The design adopts a multiplier factor matrix simplification 
technology to convert finite field multipliers into XOR 
operations and reduces hardware resource consumption by 
combining an improved Berlekamp-Massey  algorithm. Through 
a noise injection module, a comparison and decision module, 
and switch switching, flexible switching between service mode 
and self-test mode is achieved: in self-test mode, the design can 
automatically compare the decoded output with the original 
PRBS sequence, greatly simplifying the cross-platform 
transplantation and verification process.

V. CONCLUSION 
After more than 60 years of evolution, Reed-Solomon codes 
have built a complete technical system of "theoretical 
foundation—algorithmic 
breakthrough—engineering 
implementation". Based on finite field algebra, the MDS 
property and two classical constructions of RS codes were first 
established. The proposal of the PGZ, BM, and Euclidean 
algorithms solved the engineering problems of algebraic 
decoding, laying the foundation for the wide application of RS 
codes. Subsequently, GS list decoding broke the half-minimum 
distance limit, and the KV algorithm realized the deep 
integration of soft information and algebraic decoding. These 
two paradigm shifts drove RS codes to approach the Shannon 
limit. Nowadays, focusing on the "performance-complexity" 
balance and scenario adaptation, algorithms such as fast joint 
decoding 
and 
symbol-level 
iterative 
decoding 
reduce 
complexity, 
while 
breakthroughs 
such 
as 
domestic 
encoders/decoders and RS-LDPC joint decoding break through 
engineering application bottlenecks, enabling RS codes to 
continue playing a core role in scenarios such as high-speed 
communication, distributed storage, and domestic hardware. 
Facing the future needs of digital communication and data 
storage, the research on RS codes will develop in the directions 
of "cross-integration, scenario deepening, and technical 
collaboration". As a classic type of error-correcting code, the 
theoretical value and application potential of RS codes have not 
been weakened by the emergence of emerging codes, but have 
continuously expanded their application scenarios through 
continuous innovation. In the future, through theoretical 
breakthroughs, engineering optimization, and cross-field 
integration, RS codes will continue to play a core role in the 
reliable transmission and storage of digital information, 
providing solid guarantees for various technological applications.
