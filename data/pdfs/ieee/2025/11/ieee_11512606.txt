Abstract-The rapid expansion of the Internet of Things (IoT), robotics, and automated control systems has created a critical demand for high-throughput, low-latency wireless communication. This paper presents the hardware implementation of an efficient parallel decoding algorithm for regular Low-Density ParityCheck (LDPC) codes, constructed with a girth of eight to avoid short cycles. The codes are constructed using a geometric approach based on balanced incomplete block designs (BIBD) to ensure a minimum girth of eight. The design was developed using geometric methods, implemented on the Quartus platform with Verilog Hardware Description Language (HDL), and includes the circuit design and simulation for core modules. Synthesized and deployed on a Stratix II FPGA (EP2S90F1020C3), the decoder achieves a maximum data processing rate of 10 Mbps with robust error correction capabilities. A key innovation is the standardization of all internal information in the decoding process to a uniform format, which eliminates the need for additional data conversion, thereby reducing hardware complexity and computational delay. This makes the design particularly suitable for real-time applications in IoT and robotic systems. In summary, the proposed decoder achieves a 25% reduction in clock cycles for check node updates and a processing throughput of 10 Mbps on FPGA hardware. This work demonstrates a practical and efficient decoding solution that meets the stringent requirements of modern real-time communication systems in IoT and robotics.
Keywords-Girth; LDPC Code; FPGA; Decoding; IoT; Robotics
I. INTRODUCTION
In the burgeoning era of the Internet of Things (IoT) and intelligent automation, the demand for robust, reliable, and efficient data transmission has become a foundational imperative. This is particularly critical for latency-sensitive and safety-conscious applications such as autonomous robots navigating dynamic environments, industrial control systems orchestrating precision manufacturing, and vast networks of smart sensors monitoring infrastructure. These systems operate in often hostile and unpredictable channel conditions, where interference, noise, and signal fading can severely corrupt data integrity. Consequently, the choice of error-correcting codes

Against this backdrop, Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes, a class of linear block codes first introduced by Robert Gallager in his seminal 1962 doctoral dissertation at MIT, have experienced a remarkable renaissance. Their defining characteristic is a sparse parity-check matrix H, meaning the matrix contains very few '1's relative to '0's. This sparsity is the key to their exceptional performance and practicality. LDPC codes are renowned for their capacity-approaching capability; when configured with large block lengths and sophisticated iterative decoding algorithms, they can perform within a fraction of a decibel of the theoretical Shannon limit, the ultimate boundary for reliable communication over a noisy channel.
Perhaps their most significant advantage for modern hardware applications lies in their inherent parallelism. Unlike sequential decoding algorithms used for other codes (such as the Viterbi algorithm for convolutional codes), the iterative belief propagation decoding for LDPC codes can be structured to perform many operations simultaneously. This architectural flexibility makes them exceptionally well-suited for hardware implementation on platforms like Field-Programmable Gate Arrays (FPGAs) and Application-Specific Integrated Circuits (ASICs). It is this combination of near-optimal performance and parallelizable decoding that renders LDPC codes particularly attractive for real-time systems in robotics and IoT. In these domains, the conflicting demands for low-power operation (to extend battery life in remote sensors) and highthroughput decoding (to enable rapid control-loop cycles in robots) are simultaneously met by efficient LDPC decoder designs.
Recognizing these strengths, the telecommunications industry has firmly adopted LDPC codes as a core channel coding scheme for the 5G New Radio (NR) standard, specifically for its enhanced mobile broadband (eMBB) data channel. This standardization has further accelerated research and optimization efforts around the code [1-4]. Recent studies

Gallager's original formulation defined a regular LDPC code of length n by a parity-check matrix H where the number of 1s in each column and each row is fixed. Specifically, each column contains a small, constant number λ (lambda) of 1s (the variable node degree, with λ ≥ 3), and each row contains a constant number ρ (rho) of 1s (the check node degree). A crucial property for avoiding short cycles and ensuring good performance is that the number of overlapping 1s between any two columns is no greater than one. This structural constraint helps to maximize the "girth" of the associated Tanner graph, which is a bipartite graph representation of the code, leading to more independent and reliable information exchange during the iterative decoding process[4]. Specifically, a larger girth, such as eight compared to six, reduces the negative impact of short cycles, leading to more accurate belief propagation, faster convergence (fewer iterations), and ultimately better Bit Error Rate (BER) performance [15]. For instance, codes with girth 8 typically require 10-15% fewer iterations to converge than those with girth 6 under the same channel conditions, while girth 10 offers diminishing returns for the increased structural complexity in many practical, finite-length codes.

A regular LDPC code with a parity-check matrix H=(hi, j)m×n can be represented by a bipartite graph known as a Tanner graph. The codeword vector X=(x1,x2,..., x n ) is represented as a set of variable nodes {x j, j=1,2,...,n}, while the redundancy vector Z=(z1,z2,..., z n) is represented as a set of check nodes {zi, i =1,2,...,m}. In H, each variable node corresponds to the check nodes in its column, and each check node corresponds to the variable nodes in its row. An edge connects variable node xj and check node zi only if hi, j=1, and these two nodes are called adjacent nodes, with the edge being their adjacent edge. The number of edges connected to a node is called the degree of that node. For an (n, λ, ρ) regular LDPC code, each variable node is connected to λ check nodes, giving it a degree of λ, while each check node is connected to ρ variable nodes, giving it a degree of ρ[4]. IV. HARDWARE CIRCUIT DESIGN OF THE LDPC DECODER
The hardware architecture for the LDPC decoder is designed for compactness and efficiency to facilitate integration into IoT and robotic platforms. The design employs a partially parallel structure and is partitioned into several key modules:
⚫ A front-end input signal processing module.
⚫ A partially parallel variable node update (VNU) module.
⚫ A partially parallel check node update (CNU) module.
⚫ An interleaving module that manages data exchange between the VNU and CNU.
⚫ A decoding decision output module.
The VNU and CNU modules operate with different data widths and sequences. The VNU processes data in a width of 36 (performing 36 node updates in parallel, each with a data length of 28 bits), while the CNU operates on a width of 18. The interleaver ensures correct data routing and synchronization between these two modules. The overall block diagram of the log-domain sum-product algorithm decoder is depicted in Figure 5.

V. CONCLUSIONS
This paper has presented the design and hardware implementation of an efficient parallel decoder for regular LDPC codes with a girth of eight. The proposed algorithm improves upon traditional tree-based architectures by optimizing the check node update process, reducing the number of core operations and clock cycles required. A unified quantization scheme was developed and shown to have negligible performance impact while significantly simplifying the hardware data path. The decoder was successfully implemented on an FPGA using Verilog HDL and the Quartus platform, achieving a processing rate of 10 Mbps with excellent error correction performance. By standardizing information processing and optimizing the architecture, the design reduces hardware complexity and computational latency, effectively meeting the stringent demands of real-time IoT and robotic applications. However, the current design is optimized for regular LDPC codes with specific code rates and lengths. Its performance with irregular codes or under very high noise conditions (e.g., very low SNR) may be suboptimal. Future work will focus on extending the architecture to support irregular LDPC codes, exploring adaptive quantization schemes for wider dynamic ranges, and investigating low-power techniques for energy-constrained applications.
