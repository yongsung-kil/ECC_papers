On the Correcting Capability of Hard-Decision Error-and-Erasure Iterative Decoding of LDPC
Codes for Data Storage

Abstract‚ÄîModern storage systems such as NAND flash solidstate drives are increasingly exposed to channels where both errors and erasures coexist. Classical Reed‚ÄìSolomon (RS) codes provide strong error-and-erasure protection but suffer from high decoding complexity and latency, limiting their applicability in high-throughput storage architectures. Low-Density ParityCheck (LDPC) codes, with iterative message-passing decoders, offer near-capacity performance at low complexity and are therefore the preferred solution in practice. While the decoding behavior of LDPC codes has been well studied for pure error or pure erasure channels, the mixed error-and-erasure case has received less attention, despite its direct relevance to storage. In this work, we analyze the potential correction capability of iterative error-and-erasure decoding for LDPC codes under harddecision channels. Using density evolution, we derive recursion equations for the joint evolution of error and erasure probabilities and establish bounds on the region of correctable error‚Äìerasure patterns. The presented results provide theoretical correction bounds that complement existing studies on error-only and erasure-only channels, and offer guidelines for LDPC code and decoder design in storage systems where errors and erasures naturally coexist.
Index Terms‚ÄîLDPC codes, iterative decoding, error-anderasure channels, hard-decision decoding, density evolution, correction capability bounds, data storage systems.
I. INTRODUCTION
Modern high-density storage systems, such as NAND flash SSDs, face growing reliability challenges due to various impairments. Two dominant failure mechanisms can be distinguished: random errors, arising from cell wear, retention loss, or inter-cell interference, and erasures, caused by unreadable memory cells, synchronization losses, or uncorrectable channel distortions. Consequently, practical storage channels often exhibit ternary outputs: correctly received symbols, erroneous hard decisions, and erasures. While error-control coding for pure-error [1] and pure-erasure [2] channels has been extensively studied, scenarios where errors and erasures coexist have received comparatively little attention.
Classical storage systems relied heavily on Reed‚ÄìSolomon (RS) codes, exploiting their maximum distance separable

(MDS) property to correct combined errors and erasures efficiently [3]. However, algebraic RS decoding has quadratic complexity and exhibits high decoding latency, making it increasingly impractical for modern large-block storage and high-throughput systems. Furthermore, the rigid block structure of RS codes limits their scalability to the very long block lengths demanded in current devices.
Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes [4] have become the coding method of choice in advanced storage systems. LDPC codes achieve high performance using iterative decoding with low complexity [5]. In practice, SSD controllers typically adopt a two-phase decoding strategy: a fast harddecision LDPC decode is performed first, and if decoding fails, the controller initiates soft-decision LDPC decoding using multiple threshold reads. This design balances reliability and latency: hard-decision decoding ensures high throughput in most cases, while soft-decision decoding is invoked only rarely, when the bit error rate (BER) becomes too high [6].
In practice, a channel containing both errors and erasures poses special challenges. Preliminary analyses of LDPC iterative decoding in hybrid error/erasure environments [7] suggest that introducing erasures can either help or harm decoding, depending on their distribution relative to the code‚Äôs Tanner graph structure. For example, assigning unreliable symbols as erasures rather than hard decisions may reduce the impact of trapping sets, thereby improving decoding convergence, but excessive erasures may instead stall decoding.
This paper investigates the iterative error-and-erasure correction algorithm for LDPC codes under hard-decision channels. Unlike soft-decision decoding, which relies on detailed reliability metrics, we assume only ternary hard outputs, reflecting the operational constraints of storage devices aiming to minimize read latency and controller complexity.
By systematically addressing the joint error-and-erasure setting in hard-decision LDPC decoding, this work closes an existing gap between theory and practice. The theoretical predictions obtained via density evolution (DE) are compared with Monte Carlo decoding simulations. For all experiments, the decoding process is limited to 10 iterations, and a bit error rate (BER) threshold of 10‚àí7 is used to determine convergence. Performance is analyzed under varying channel error and erasure probabilities in order to illustrate the correcting capability of the decoder.

V. CONCLUSION
This paper investigated the correcting capability of iterative error-and-erasure decoding for LDPC codes under a mixed error‚Äìerasure channel, a model of practical relevance for modern data storage systems. Using density evolution, we derived explicit recursion equations governing the evolution of error and erasure probabilities, enabling an analytical characterization of the decoder‚Äôs asymptotic behavior. The analysis revealed how erasures can either impede or facilitate convergence, depending on their interaction with erroneous messages, and showed that moderate erasure levels may improve decoding performance by suppressing harmful error propagation.
The theoretical predictions were validated through Monte Carlo simulations for both regular and irregular LDPC codes. For the regular (3, 6) ensemble, the density evolution results closely matched experimental data across a wide range of channel parameters. We further demonstrated that an optimized irregular degree distribution, obtained via differential evolution, provides improved robustness to mixed errors and erasures and performs closer to the ideal density evolution bound than the regular code. Experimental comparisons confirmed the superiority of the optimized irregular ensemble at all considered operating points.
Overall, the results illustrate the potential of densityevolution-guided design for enhancing the correction capability of LDPC codes in scenarios where errors and erasures coexist. Future work may include analyzing more sophisticated update rules, extending the framework to spatially coupled and other LDPC code constructions.
