Abstract—NAND flash is the primary storage technology in most modern electronic devices. Its evolution has been driven by scaling to increase bit density per cell, which has led to high raw bit error rates (RBER) and reduced data integrity. Multi-level flash devices have greater complexity and even higher RBER. Addressing this requires designing accurate channel models and error correction coding (ECC) algorithms tailored to these models. Our research focuses on modeling the NAND flash channel and synthesizing, implementing both classical and modern ECC techniques optimized for NAND flash architectures, enhancing reliability in contemporary storage systems.
Index Terms—NAND flash memory, Channel codes, SSRS codes, RS codes, FPGA, LDPC codes, product codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
NAND flash represents a form of non-volatile storage widely used in modern data systems. Traditionally, these memories were based on the Single Level Cell (SLC) structure, where a single cell stores a one bit of data (either 0 or 1). With continuous device miniaturization, the introduction of multilevel cells (MLC) capable of storing multiple bits per cell has greatly improved storage density. For instance, MLCs hold two bits, Triple Level Cells (TLCs) reserve three bits, Quadruple Level Cells (QLCs) accommodate four bits, and Penta Level Cells (PLCs) can store five bits in each cell [1]. However, such multilevel configurations lead to greater design complexity and higher RBER. To maintain data reliability, advanced ECC mechanisms have been developed in this study, incorporating precisely selected parity bits to preserve information accuracy [2]. Consequently, the Undetectable Bit Error Rate (UBER) achieved after channel code application is considerably lower than the RBER.
The key contributions of our research work are listed below.
• Determining the probability channel matrix for NAND flash devices such as MLC, TLC, and QLC.
• Synthesizing and validating Subfield- Subcodes of ReedSolomon (SSRS) codes and RS codes for Multilevel NAND flash device [2].
• Synthesizing and validating Euclidean Geometry LowDensity Parity Check (EG-LDPC) codes, Quasi-Cyclic

(QC)-EG-LDPC codes, and QC-LDPC codes for different memory architectures.
II. MODELLING OF THE MULTILEVEL MEMORY CELL
The threshold voltage distributions can be modeled using the Gaussian distribution, the Normal Laplace distribution, and the Student-T distribution. Deviations in threshold voltage distributions arise from multiple noise sources, including programming errors, retention errors, and charge leakage. Hence, accurate modeling of the NAND flash memory channel must account for these noise factors. Such noise induces nonuniformity in the voltage distributions. Channel matrices for MLC, TLC, and QLC NAND flash devices have been derived across different program/erase (P/E) cycles to represent distinct stages in the device’s lifetime.
III. SYNTHESIS AND IMPLEMENTATION OF SSRS AND RS
CODES
A. SSRS and RS codes
An SSRS code is defined as a subset of the parent RS code over GF (qm) where all codeword components are constrained to reside within a fixed vq-dimensional vector subspace S of GF (qm) [3].
The SSRS codes are synthesized over subfield GF (4) of GF (47) for MLC, SSRS over GF (8) of GF (85) for TLC, GF (16) of GF (164) for QLC and GF (32) of GF (325) for PLC NAND flash memory [2]. Similarly, the RS codes are designed over GF (164) matching multi-level device architectures.
From the range of available NAND flash memory architectures, this work focuses on two device models produced by Micron Technology, which were utilized to design and analyze different SSRS and RS coding schemes for the intended use. [1].
B. Design of SSRS and RS code in MLC
An MLC flash based on Architecture-1, single sector, comprises 4096 cells used to hold two bits of data, along with an additional 128 cells allocated for parity-check information.

IV. DESIGN AND IMPLEMENTATION OF LDPC CODES
The LDPC codes are synthesized from the Euclidean geometry EG∗(2, 28). Further, we have designed and implemented QC-LDPC codes from EG∗(2, 26), EG∗(2, 27), and from finite fields GF (67) and GF (73). We have designed appropriate decoders for these codes using the min-sum algorithm.
The encoders and decoders for the synthesized codes are implemented using the Vivado 2022.2 tool with the Intel(R) Xeon(R) E-2224G CPU at 3.50GHz and a RAM size of 80GB. The encoder for QC LDPC codes is designed using a Cyclic Shift-Register-Adder-Accumulator (CSRAA) circuit [3].
V. PERFORMANCE ANALYSIS
The SSRS (4224, 4096) dibits with t = 12 dibits and the SSRS (4320, 4096) dibits with t = 21 dibits are designed for MLC employing both device architectures. Similarly, RS (528, 512) and RS (540, 512) codes are designed over GF (164) for both device models. Performance of the designed two SSRS codes and two RS codes for MLC is presented in Fig. 1. Since RS codes can detect and correct a larger number of errors for read data from flash, they demonstrate better performance than SSRS codes in this context. However, the main drawback of RS codes lies in their higher encoding and decoding complexity. The SSRS and RS codes designed for Architecture-2 demonstrate superior performance, as this model provides a larger parity-check space of 224 cells.

The RS encoder, configured for a (528,512) code designed for MLC ( Architecture-1) with 16-bit symbols, utilizes 16 multipliers, 16 adders, and 16 shift registers, each operating on 16-bit data. In contrast, the SSRS encoder, designed for a (4222,4096) code with dibit (2-bit) symbols tailored to the same MLC architecture, requires a higher count of smaller units — specifically, 69 multipliers, 101 adders, and 126 shift registers, all handling 2-bit data. Although the SSRS encoder involves a greater number of arithmetic and storage elements, each unit processes fewer bits, resulting in potentially lower overall hardware complexity and power consumption. VI. CONCLUSION
This research work has focused on modeling the NAND flash memory channel, followed by synthesis, implementation, and performance analysis of suitable classical and modern error control coding techniques for these devices. The designed channel codes improve the reliability of stored information and potentially extend the useful working life of these devices.
