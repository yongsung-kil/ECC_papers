Protomatrix-Based LDPC Codes for Continuous Phase Modulation

Erik Perrins, Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—This paper develops a design methodology for constructing protomatrix-based low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes that are paired with (matched to) continuous phase modulation (CPM) waveforms. We show how a protomatrix extrinsic information transfer (PEXIT) based search can yield lower decoding thresholds than previously reported, and how a single constraint added to this search is effective at preserving the linear minimum distance growth property while remaining within one dB of capacity. We show that a two-stage lifting procedure plays an essential role in completely eliminating error floors at very low error rates. Our high-throughput joint LDPC–CPM decoder is demonstrated to be practical with execution speeds comparable to a standalone LDPC decoder. The global iterations of the joint decoder are shown to provide natural protection against undetected errors due to small minimum distance, and this protection is further enhanced by a novel check node “splitting” technique. The diverse family of three CPM waveforms that are used in aeronautical telemetry are considered as design examples.
Index Terms—Channel coding, concatenated coding, continuous phase modulation, iterative methods.
I. INTRODUCTION
C ONTINUOUS phase modulation (CPM) [1] has the advantage of joint power and bandwidth efficiency, which stems from its constant signal envelope. This property is essential in applications that require the use nonlinear amplifiers operating in full saturation, which in turn enables miniaturization and/or the ability to meet strict size weight and power (SWaP) constraints; example applications include aeronautical telemetry [2], Bluetooth [3], and satellite communications [4].
Protomatrix-based low density parity check (LDPC) codes [5] form an important subclass of structured codes that, when carefully designed, have many desirable features (e.g. [6]), including low decoding thresholds, linear minimum distance growth, low encoding complexity, and fast decoding. The design of LDPC codes has received some study for general types of CPM. An extensive study for designing unstructured and structured LDPC codes for CPM was undertaken in [10] and [11], which, importantly, included the first application of PEXIT analysis to CPM. The study in [12] is somewhat similar to [11] and used EXIT concepts to design nonbinary LDPC codes for CPM. Additional studies (e.g. [13], [14]) are applicable only to the family of minimum shift keying (MSK) type CPMs, which are popular in part because they permit an offset quadrature phase shift keying (OQPSK) interpretation [15], thus sidestepping the CPM perspective entirely. This was used in [14] to pair an MSK-type CPM with the accumulate-repeat-4-jagged-accumulate (AR4JA) LDPC codes (designed originally for BPSK in [6]), and this pairing was subsequently adopted into the IRIG-106 telemetry standard [2]. While effective, the MSK-type approach does not generalize to all CPMs.
The main contributions of this paper are (i) to organize a cohesive design framework for constructing protomatrix-based LDPC codes that are matched to a desired CPM waveform, and (ii) to apply this framework effectively to yield finished LDPC–CPM codes that meet the design criteria for a standard. At the core of our approach is protomatrix optimization using PEXIT analysis as applied to CPM, as such we acknowledge the dependence of our methodology on [9], [10], and [11] and related works. However, we demonstrate that decoding thresholds lower than those reported in [10] and [11] can be achieved via a purely unconstrained protomatrix optimization. Likewise, we demonstrate how a single constraint can be included in the optimization to preserve the highly-desirable linear minimum distance growth property while still remaining within one dB of capacity [16], [17]. Thus, we show that the techniques in [10] and [11] can be improved with broader considerations.
Because the scope of our contribution includes code construction and is highly-concerned with the low-error-rate performance of the finished codes, there are several considerations herein that were not present in the studies cited above. The CPM modulator can be viewed as a continuous phase encoder (CPE) [21], which is a finite state machine that can be demodulated/decoded using trellis processing. The sequence {Un} is the input to the CPE and the sequence {Cn} is its output, which is also denoted in Fig. 1. The trellis has NS = pM L−1 states and NE = pM L edges (branches), where each edge is labeled with a unique value of Cn; thus, we have Cn ∈ {0, 1, . . . , NE − 1}.
The subject of complexity reduction for CPM has received much study [1] and several relevant approaches are surveyed in [22]. In this paper, we limit ourselves to using the frequency pulse truncation (PT) technique, which models the CPE as having a shortened (truncated) value of LRx, i.e. LRx < L. With the PT approximation, the transmitter uses the (exact) CPM model as described above; however, the demodulator/decoder uses the smaller (approximate) value of LRx in place of L in the expressions for NS and NE.

Due to the spectrum inefficiency of PCM/FM, the Advanced Range TeleMetry program (ARTM) developed two “tiers” of spectrum-efficient CPM waveforms, which were adopted into the IRIG-106 telemetry standard [2] in the early 2000s. C. Receiver Model
The receiver model is shown in Fig. 3. The various modules exchange vectors (sequences) of soft information in the form of L-values whenever the underlying variable is binary (i.e. u and y, but not {Cn}). An L-value is defined as the log of the ratio of the two elements in a binary probability mass function (PMF). Input vectors are denoted with “I” and contain a priori information. Output vectors are denoted with “O” and contain a posteriori information. In cases where an output is used as another module’s input, we compute the extrinsic version of such outputs. The traditional CPM demodulation process is well understood [1]. We assume that symbol, carrier, and frame/ASM synchronization [26] are perfectly known, which are reasonable assumptions given the similarities between the transmission model in Figs. 1 and 3 and the existing AR4JA–ARTM1-NR pairing in the telemetry standard [2], which has been in operation for over a decade. It is important to emphasize that the most computationally-intensive parts of the demodulation process take place only once [14] and are positioned outside the iterative loop, as shown in Fig. 3 with the CPM demodulator soft-input soft-output (SISO) module [27]. This module produces a metric increment for each edge in the trellis (e) at each time step (n), {pn(C(e); I)}, which are viewed as NE-ary PMFs [28]. These log-based PMFs are preserved for future decoding iterations, as shown in Fig. 3. The remaining task of the CPM demodulator SISO is identical to the sole task of the CPM decoder SISO, which is to generate the soft output for u as described in [27]. Because of the presence of the known ASM sequence on either side of the code word, the CPM SISO forward and backward recursions can take place over a brief “warm-up” interval which obviates the need for any additional trellis termination/tail sequence.

As shown in Fig. 3, the SISO operations take place within a larger “global” iterative decoding loop that performs a defined maximum number of iterations, Itmax. For the first global iteration, the CPM demodulator SISO is fed with r(t) and zero (no) a priori information for u; this task is completed outside the loop and the result is passed forward via the switch in position “A.” For successive global iterations, the switch is placed in position “B” so that the CPM SISO decoder’s updates are used. For these operations, the CPM SISO decoder accepts the non-zero input sequence {λi(u; I)} and returns the extrinsic update for u, {λi(u; O)}. The SISO module’s completion step does not need to compute outputs corresponding to the warm-up intervals.
As shown in Fig. 3, the LDPC decoder has an a priori input, {λi(y; I)}, and its own internal state memory in the form of “edge buffers,” {η(e)} (initialized to zero prior to the first global iteration and preserved from one global iteration to the next). During one global iteration, the LDPC decoder update consists of Itloc “local” (i.e. internal) iterations between variable and check node updates (e.g. [29]). The LDPC decoder produces an extrinsic a posteriori output, {λi(y; O)}, and a full (non-extrinsic) a posteriori probability (APP), {Λi(y; O)}, which serves as the basis for the final hard decisions, yˆ.
III. PROTOMATRIX AND EXIT BACKGROUND
Our approach to designing LDPC codes for the CPM-based scheme in Fig. 1 draws primarily from [6], [8], [9], [11], and [30] with modification and adaptation as needed. We consider only quasi-cyclic codes because they have demonstrated very good decoding thresholds with low encoding complexity and fast decoding, e.g. [6].
The underlying design philosophy involves EXIT [31] dynamics between the CPM scheme and the variable and check nodes of the LDPC code. With EXIT, L-values are modeled as if they were the outputs of a binary AWGN channel. As explained in [32], the conditional mean and variance of this Gaussian “channel” are parameterized by the variable σ, 0 ≤ σ ≤ ∞, which in turn can be quantified in terms of mutual information (MI), I, 0 ≤ I ≤ 1. The functions I = J(σ) and σ = J−1(I) are used to go back and forth between the σ and I domains, and very simple piecewise approximations of these functions are given in the Appendix of [31].
The protomatrix EXIT (PEXIT) analysis [8] builds on the original EXIT principles by modeling the flow of MI within a protomatrix, which is a smaller template for the larger parity check matrix that will eventually be constructed. A

major incompatibility with the PEXIT analysis as originally formulated in [8] and our current application is that the CPM modulator and demodulator are situated between the LDPC encoder/decoder and the AWGN channel, with the interleaver/deinterleaver playing a role in this separation. These differences fundamentally alter the flow of extrinsic MI in the system. The PEXIT modifications we need were first developed in [9] for partial-response channels. The applicability of these modifications to CPM was recognized in [10] and [11]. As such, the entire framework needed for PEXIT analysis for CPM can be assembled from existing elements in the published literature.
The primary contribution herein is to organize these elements into a cohesive design framework and then to apply this framework effectively to yield finished LDPC–CPM codes that meet the design criteria for a standard. In particular, we will show that proper consideration must be made to (i) constraints on the PEXIT optimization, (ii) the role of the interleaver, and (iii) the lifting procedure that follows the PEXIT optimization. These issues were not fully considered in [10] and [11] when PEXIT was first applied to CPM and they have considerable impact on the effectiveness of the finished LDPC–CPM codes.

C. Role of the Interleaver/Deinterleaver
The interleaver and deinterleaver are integral to the function of the entire LDPC–CPM code. At the deinterleaver input, the CPM SISO produces L-values with a modest amount of time correlation, which stems from the correlation in the CPM waveform itself. Fig. 6 (a) shows the sample autocorrelation of these L-values for the ARTM2 waveform. Given the relatively large size of the LDPC code words, the deinterleaver is more than capable of producing an output that appears to be statistically uncorrelated. It is not clear that this is even necessary, however, because LDPC codes themselves can be interpreted as having an inherent degree of interleaving, e.g. [31, Fig. 1], which further disperses inputs that were initially adjacent.
At the interleaver input, however, there is significant variation in the amplitudes of the L-values (variation in MI) produced by the LDPC decoder, an example of which is shown in Fig. 6 (b). This variation is due to irregular variable node degrees and the manner in which check and variable nodes are connected. Thus, in a quasi-cyclic code this variation is exhibited on a per-circulant basis (per protograph variable node, which are indexed by j). In [10] and [11], partial interleavers were used (i.e. a separate interleaver per circulant/protograph variable node), whereas [9] used a global interleaver (i.e. a single interleaver for the entire LDPC code word). The former leaves the per-circulant variation seen in Fig. 6 (b) intact at the interleaver output while the latter disperses (averages) these variations over the entire code word. The CPM SISO module thus receives inputs with measurably-different statistical (MI) behavior depending on which type of interleaver is used.

The algorithm essentially starts “somewhere” by being supplied with an initial (unoptimized) protomatrix B, a sufficiently large “guess” for a starting SNR that is above the decoding threshold, and a given column upon which to focus the current pass of the optimization effort. When the algorithm emerges from its outermost loop, the set Sprev contains (possibly several) candidate columns that resulted in the PEXIT analysis’ convergence at (Es/N0)prev. It is readily apparent that this approach does not lead to a unique result; however, we have observed some reassuring consistency in its operation. In repeated optimization runs, we did not observe any particular sensitivity to the initial choice of B. Rather, we have found that a consistent final decoding threshold value (Es/N0)∗ is achieved with B having a consistent distribution of variable and check node degrees. This is in harmony with other studies on LDPC code design, cf. e.g. [6], [9], [11], [31].

Algorithm 1 Line 5 allows for the application of constraints in the column search, a variety of which have been considered in prior studies. The work in [10] and [11] is most relevant to the present study because it was devoted to code design for CPM, but it was also much broader in that it considered unstructured LDPC codes and several types of structured codes beyond protomatrix-based. In the next section, we will show that preserving the linear minimum distance growth property has a significant impact on the final results. While perhaps unsurprising, this at least underscores the fact that additional study was needed beyond [9], [10], [11] for CPM, as undertaken herein. To preserve this property, we will impose one simple constraint on our designs, which is that all variable nodes must have a degree of 3 or more, i.e. dv(j) ≥ 3, ∀j. This has been shown to guarantee linear minimum distance growth for irregular unstructured LDPC code ensembles [34]. In what follows, we refer to an unconstrained (“U”) design as one that initially uses all 255 possible columns at Algorithm 1 Line 5, and a constrained (“C”) design as one that initially uses only the 241 possible columns that have degree 3 or higher.
We note that we tried the broader approach used in [9] of preserving the linear minimum distance growth property (controlled use of degree-1 and degree-2 nodes, with degree-3 or higher thereafter). However, this resulted in no appreciable gains in decoding thresholds and we achieved better performance results with the final codes when we used our one simple constraint.

Looking ahead to the performance of the final, lifted codes, we found it necessary to take additional measures to address the impacts of low girth for the shortest of our desired codes: the R = 2/3 and R = 4/5 codes with information block length K = 1024. Recall that girth is defined as the shortest cycle in the Tanner graph. As shown in the analysis of [19], the presence of element 3 in the AR4JA protographs negatively impacts the girth of the derived codes. This can cause a BER/FER slope that becomes more shallow as Es/N0 increases (sometimes referred to as an “error floor” in the literature, although in this case it does not stem from the presence of undetected errors); this shallowing is visible in the BER/FER slope for the original pairing of AR4JA with BPSK (see [25, Figure B-2]), and we have replicated this in Fig. 9 for the AR4JA–ARTM1-NR pairing.
We take the following measures for ARTM0 and ARTM1, but we have found this to be unnecessary in the case of ARTM2, for reasons that are discussed in Section VI-D. For the R = 4/5 case, we are able to eliminate element 3 from the final protomatrixes in (9) and (10) (i.e. we reduce the alphabet size to B = 3) at the expense of increasing the decoding threshold by only 0.05 dB above what would have otherwise been the minimum value. B. Details of the Pre-Lift by 2

The pre-lift by 4 is described in detail in [6], so repetition here is unnecessary. While the pre-lift by 2 is similar, there are several differences worth mentioning.
Recall that the elements of B are drawn from a 4-ary alphabet, i.e. bi,j ∈ {0, 1, 2, 3}. For the pre-lift by 2, we replace each bi,j with an expanded 2 × 2 sub-matrix whose row and column weights are equal to bi,j. This results in an “intermediate” protomatrix, D, with dimensions MD = 2MB and ND = 2NB. The 2 × 2 sub-matrixes are weight-bi,j circulants. A circulant is a square matrix where each row is a right-hand circular (barrel) shift of the row above. As such, the entire circulant can be specified by providing only the first row; the “weight” of the circulant is the sum of the first row. The location of a non-zero element is referred to as its “phase.”
Because of the smaller pre-lift by 2, it is not possible to remove all parallel edges in D (parallel edges exist when protomatrix elements have values greater than 1). The progressive edge growth (PEG) algorithm [35] may be used in this lifting stage (as it was for the pre-lift by 4 in [6]), however, the PEG algorithm invariably returns the following 2 × 2 expansions:

C. Second-Stage Lift and Removal of All Parallel Edges
In the second and final lifting stage, D is lifted by a factor of ML by assigning random phases to ML × ML circulants using the ACE algorithm [30]. Because of the pre-lift, the entries of the intermediate protomatrix are drawn only from the alphabet {0, 1, 2}, and so this second lifting stage results in weight-2, weight-1, or weight-0 (all-zeros) circulants. A weight-1 circulant can be specified simply by its “phase,” which is the location of the non-zero element in the first row and is an integer in the set {0, 1, . . . , ML − 1}. We simplify

D. Observations About the Concatenated LDPC–CPM Code While our proposed methodology focuses on the design of
the LDPC code, it must not be overlooked that the LDPC code is concatenated with the CPM waveform, via an interleaver. This concatenation lessens the consequences of low girth and minimum distance, as we now discuss.
The girth of an LDPC code is traditionally viewed as relating to the smallest number of (local) iterations for a message sent from a node (belonging to the shortest cycle) to propagate back to itself; this in turn violates the statistical independence assumptions for extrinsic information from one (local) iteration to the next, and an error floor can result. Because the global iterations in Fig. 3 take place between the LDPC iterations, they provide an opportunity for the SISO module to deliver “fresh” extrinsic information to the LDPC decoder, which does not exist when the LDPC decoder iterates by itself. Thus, the traditional impact of low girth becomes more subtle. For the shortest of our desired codes (R = 4/5 and R = 2/3 with K = 1024) our numerical results show that AR4JA-ARTM-NR (a traditional LDPC configuration with no global iterations) is most sensitive to low girth effects [see the shallow slope at high SNR in Fig. 9]; ARTM0 and ARTM1 are less sensitive [see the steeper slope at high SNR for these codes in Figs. 12(a) and (b), respectively]; and ARTM2 is the least sensitive of all [see that the slope grows steeper with SNR for these codes in Fig. 12 (c)]. Our conjecture for ARTM2, which has M = 4 and two bits per CPM symbol, is that the bit-based global interleaver followed by the symbol-based

With the global iterative arrangement in Fig. 3, it is possible to isolate the LDPC decoder and encourage undetected errors, simply by selecting a large value for the parameter Itloc (i.e. a large number of local iterations per global iteration). A brute-force method of exploiting agreement/disagreement is to require an additional global iteration after the parity check passes. This is computationally expensive because undetected errors are hardly ever present, thus in the vast majority of cases the additional iteration agrees with the previous one, producing a second passed parity check. In the rare case when an undetected error is present, we have observed that the disagreement from the SISO update is almost always successful in pulling the LDPC decoder away from the erroneous neighboring code word, resulting in a failed parity check, and thus allowing regular decoding to resume. This outcome can be accomplished more elegantly with check node splitting.
Fig. 10 (a) shows the Tanner graph of a high-degree check node where, for convenience, half of the edges are drawn with solid lines and the other half with dashed lines. It is wellknown that the edges connecting such a node (in this case the dashed and solid edges) can be “split” to form two new lowerdegree check nodes, as shown in Fig. 10 (b). These new check nodes can then be connected to each other via a punctured variable node [drawn with an open circle in Fig. 10 (b)].
Although this new code is equivalent to the original, its iterative decoder behaves differently. Focusing on the upper check node in Fig. 10 (b) at the current iteration, the current solid edges participate in the check, along with the dashed edges from the previous iteration, which propagate through the punctured variable node. Likewise, the lower check node receives input from the current dashed edges and the previous solid edges. Meanwhile, a global update from the SISO may take place between the current and previous (local) iterations. If the global update disagrees with its predecessor, as it will when convergence is moving toward an erroneous neighbor, then half of the edges will be influenced by this disagreement, increasing the likelihood the checks will fail. If there is agreement between the global updates, the only penalty is a slight “drag” on the speed at which the messages are passed to the current iteration (which we quantify in Section VIII-C).

VII. HIGH-THROUGHPUT DECODER
An essential part of our design process has been the availability of a high-throughput, parallelized, joint decoder for LDPC–CPM. This has aided in both revealing and addressing the challenges that manifest themselves at low BER/FER.
Our software-based implementation makes use of the singleinstruction multiple-data (SIMD) instruction set extensions that are widely available on general purpose processors. These instructions are capable of processing 128-bit “vectors” in parallel. We implemented our SISO and LDPC decoders using 16-bit fixed-point arithmetic with 4 bits reserved for the fractional part. We formulated all calculations to take place in the log-domain and used the “double-line” approximation shown in [6, Fig. 19] to implement the function y(x) = ln(1+e−|x|). With 16-bit arithmetic and a 128-bit vector width, our system is thus capable of parallelization by a factor of P = 8, though it could realize higher factors if supported by the hardware.
To parallelize the LDPC decoder, we used a structured protomatrix-based decoder (see [6, Fig. 20]), which passes length-ML vectors of edge memory, {η(e)}, back and forth between the variable and check-node updates. This implementation operates directly on the modestly-sized protomatrix D. It offers a straightforward means of implementing P ≤ ML because the length-ML edge vectors can be processed in parallel. While that exceeds the capability of our system, it is convenient at least that the values of ML in Table III are all integer multiples of our design target of P = 8.
We combined two strategies to parallelize the SISO decoder. The first is the “algorithm-level” parallelization technique from [37], which we refer to as A-type. The basic idea is to divide the received code word into PA time segments, which overlap to a small degree, and then to process these segments in parallel; thus achieving a net parallelization factor of approximately PA. The small overlap allows the forward/backward recursions in the SISO algorithm to “warm up” so that the SISO completion step yields an accuracy approaching that of a single (long) time segment.
For the second strategy, we organized the state/edge processing of the trellis in ways that can be processed in parallel. For example, Fig. 11 shows a partial diagram of the 64-state, timevarying trellis for ARTM2 (which uses LRx = 2). The entire trellis can be decomposed into 16 separate 4-state “butterflies,” the first two of which are shown in different colors to highlight the fact they are disjoint. It is straightforward to formulate the SISO equations to process an entire butterfly at once, i.e. 4 states in parallel. We refer to this as B-type parallelism by a factor of PB. For all three of our modulation types there are natural trellis structures that can be parallelized

by using PB = M . We complemented this by selecting a degree of A-type parallelism in order to achieve a final value of P = PA ·PA = 8. This hybrid parallelization scheme results in an effective reduction in both the breadth (the number of states, via B-type) and depth (the number of time steps, via A-type) of the trellis. We have already applied the PT technique to reduce the number of states in the trellis, and other applicable trellis-reduction techniques are surveyed in [22].

IX. CONCLUSION
This paper shows a step-by-step procedure on how to construct capacity-approaching protomatrix-based LDPC codes that are matched to a given CPM waveform. Our results demonstrate that error floors can be eliminated by proper consideration of protomatrix optimization constraints and the lifting procedure that follows. In spite of the relatively small minimum distance for the LDPC codes, we have shown how the joint LDPC–CPM decoder can be very robust against undetected errors due to a novel application of check node splitting. We have implemented a high-throughput decoder, showing the practicality of the LDPC–CPM system as a whole while demonstrating low-error-rate performance. These LDPC–CPM codes have been recommended for adoption in aeronautical telemetry and the procedure presented herein is available for other CPM-based applications.
There are additional capacity-approaching coding schemes that have received little or no attention in connection with CPM, such as spatially coupled LDPC codes, polar codes, and rate compatible configurations. The capacity-approaching results presented above serve to motivate these as promising topics for future research.
