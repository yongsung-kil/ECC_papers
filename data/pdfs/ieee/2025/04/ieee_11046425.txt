Abstract: We experimentally assess the impacts of different LDPC decoding parameters with both soft- and hard-inputs in 50G-PON. We also derive the mean number of decoder iterations per received optical power over 25 km SSMF. © 2024 The Author(s)

1. Introduction

We have witnessed a consistent trend in Passive Optical Networks (PONs) indicating a fourfold increase in bitrates with every new generation. The severe cost constraints are another particularity of the optical access and have been the main drivers behind its architectural and technological choices. PONs allow operators to mutualize the deployment costs between several clients while minimizing energy consumption and costs thanks to a fully passive fiber infrastructure and Point-to-Multi-Point (PtMP) network topology. Also, intensity modulation with direct-detection (IMDD) using Non-Return-to-Zero (NRZ) has been the standard transmission option here and will continue to be used for the next generation thanks to its simplicity and low cost.
However, as bit-rate increases, the optical access must cope with more stringent Intersymbol Interference (ISI) coming from the interplay between fiber chromatic dispersion, laser chirp [1] and the limited bandwidths of lasers and photodiodes available on the market at reasonable costs. The next generation must also ensure sufficiently high optical power budgets for reliable PtMP connectivity, or even increase them to enable energy savings through network consolidation [2]. For its next generation, known as 50 Gigabit capable Passive Optical Network (50G-PON), the ITU-T has defined both upstream and downstream wavelengths inside the O-band to reduce the impacts of chromatic dispersion. The downstream is fixed at 1342±2 nm and three options have been defined for the upstream, enabling different coexistence scenarios with legacy PON.
Maybe the most interesting novelty of this new generation is the use of advanced Forward Error Correction (FEC). Heavily inspired by the IEEE, the ITU-T has adopted the same quasi-cyclic Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) code as in 25G/50G-Ethernet PON (EPON) [3] with minor adjustments on the shortening and puncturing structure (Fig. 1) to allow an integer number of 360 codewords (777600 bytes) within 125 µs of a downstream PHY frame at 49.7664 Gbit/s. Other rate matching options have later been added for the upstream [4] to provide operators the ability to dynamically choose between higher code rates (higher net throughput) or error correction improvement for different burst sizes and distances between clients and the central site [5]. All things considered, FEC will be a key technology allowing all PON generations to achieve optical budgets up to 35 dB (class D) while complying with the differential optical path losses and reaches defined in the standards.
Several research works have addressed LDPC in 50G-PON. In [6], real-time Bit Error Rate (BER) values as low as 10−15 are demonstrated at 50 Gb/s but over an electrical channel and without any indications on the LDPC decoder parameters. Another real-time implementation is evaluated in [7] with a decoding weight factor of 0.75 but at a lower bit-rate and once again in the electrical domain. Finally, in [8], the authors evaluate the error correlation characteristics at the input of an LDPC decoder for different equalization schemes after propagation through an optical channel and a model is used to simulate the post-FEC performances. To the best of the authors’ knowledge, none of those works focused on the optimization of the LDPC decoder parameters or evaluated the mean number of decoding iterations per Received Optical Power (ROP) for both soft- and hard-input decoding. The later could provide valuable insights into the complexity, energy consumption and cost of the receiver, which could be particularly useful for the Optical Network Unit (ONU) side. Those are precisely our objectives here.

2. Experimental Setup
Fig. 2 shows our experimental setup. A 215-1 Pseudo-Random Binary Sequence (PRBS) is systematically encoded using the protograph matrix given in [3]. After 384 bits parity puncturing, an LDPC(17280, 14592) codeword with 84.44% code rate is created. In order to fully contrast the performances of the LDPC decoders against channel ISI, we do not scramble or interleave the binary sequence after the LDPC encoder. The codeword is fed to a 50 Gbit/s real-time NRZ Pulse Pattern Generator (PPG). The PPG output is electrically amplified (EA) before driving a Transmitter Optical Subassembly (TOSA) module consisting of an on-chip Distributed FeedBack laser (DFB) followed by an Electro-Absorption Modulator (EAM) and a Semiconductor Optical Amplifier (SOA) [9]. An extinction ratio of 7 dB and a mean optical power of 13.3 dBm are measured at the output of the TOSA. The optical signal propagates through 25 km of Standard Single-Mode Fiber (SSMF) and is directly detected by a 25G-class Ge/Si Avalanche Photodiode (APD) with embedded Transimpedance Amplifier (TIA) [10]. The accumulated chromatic dispersion in the link is 76.1 ps/nm at the operating wavelength of 1342 nm, which is close to the worst-case scenario defined in [11] (77.1 ps/nm). A Variable Optical Attenuator (VOA) and a powermeter (PM) are used to vary and measure the mean ROP at the APD. Equalization is not used in our setup.
Since the LDPC decoding here is not performed in real-time, we use a 33 GHz, 100 GSa/s Real-Time Oscilloscope (RTO) followed by an oversampler (OVS in Fig. 2) to recreate a continuous-time equivalent of the received signal with 16 samples/symbol. After the OVS, we perform synchronization, slicing, LDPC decoding and BER calculation. Two decoding approaches are evaluated in this work. In the first one, soft-inputs yˆSI with floating-point double-precision are given to the decoder. This corresponds to the best case in terms of performance but the worst in terms of complexity and cost. Indeed, for this reference SI scenario, we expect very good error correction capabilities at the cost of a high amplitude resolution. In the second approach, a less performing yet less complex, hard-input (HI) approach is used. In this case, the decoder must perform the same task using only the information on the signs yˆHI of the received sequence. The decoding architectures used in the three BER calculations (SI and HI post-FEC and pre-FEC) are depicted in Fig. 2. We use a minsum algorithm, which provides a less complex implementation of the belief-propagation solution and we evaluate our iterative, layered decoder for weight factors α varying between 0.55 and 0.85 and a maximum of 14 iterations. Our LDPC decoder runs with double-precision calculations in parallel for intra-layer processing and in serial for inter-layer operations and stops decoding whenever all parity-check equations are respected.

3. Results & Discussions

Fig. 2: Experimental setup.

Fig. 3a shows a set of BER curves as a function of the ROP for 0 and 25 km and for both SI and HI approaches using an optimum α = 0.75. Results for other values of α are not shown for the sake of concision. The less steep waterfall slopes for the 25 km SI scenario are probably a consequence of the ISI caused by chromatic dispersion, as we could anticipate from the received eye-diagram (inset ⃝A in Fig. 2). We observe that the HI approach only inflicts around 1 dB penalties in performances compared to the SI case. Interestingly, the steeper slopes in the HI BER curves compared to their SI counterparts could indicate that some sort of Gaussian process is taking place upon decision on the ISI distorted samples, which facilitates the decoding procedures. This shows that HI decoding could be a very interesting approach not only for the relatively low penalties despite its less complex and less costly implementation compared to an ideal SI case but also because it enables steeper BER curves despite the distortions caused by the channel on the transmitted signal. It should be noted that since we perform our measurements over only 270 codewords (492480 Bytes), we are not able here to reliably reach the error-free levels defined in the standard (10−12). However, we expect our assessments to give us a fairly good estimation on the positions of the post-FEC BER waterfalls. Due to the sharp slopes in those zones, a small margin in the ROP should be sufficient to allow error-free operation.
In Fig. 3b, we show the mean number of iterations of the LDPC decoder as a function of the ROP for different values of α and, once again, for both SI and HI approaches over 0 and 25 km. Such an analysis could be very useful to help us infer the decoding capabilities and even estimate the power consumption of ONUs situated at different locations from the central side. As it could be expected from Fig. 3a, for ROP levels below -24 dBm, the HI decoder needs, on average, a higher number of iterations than the SI decoder at a same power level. The yellow dash-dotted lines also depicted in Fig. 3b indicate power levels below which we expect to reach error-free operation for the HI and SI approaches. In order to find them, we base ourselves on the ROP below which we were unable to measure errors after the 14th LDPC decoder iteration in Fig. 3a and we take an extra margin of 0.25 dB. A mean number of only 6 iterations for 25 km and 4 iterations for 0 km should be enough to ensure full error-correction in our setup. The zone in Fig. 3b between the yellow and orange curves tells us that the decoder will be capable of providing better performances than the pre-FEC BER by correcting some but not all errors. However, the mean number of iterations in this case will exponentially scale as ROP decreases until it eventually plateaus at the maximum number of 14 iterations in our decoder. For this zone, we expend a relatively large amount of decoding processing power for a relatively low BER amelioration. Fig. 3b also shows that the α parameter only provides a slight improvement below our reference yellow dash-dotted line but can decrease the mean number of iterations above it, specially after 25 km with soft-inputs.

4. Conclusions
We have evaluated the performances of 50 Gbit/s IMDD-NRZ over 25 km SSMF using different decoding coefficients of an LDPC(17280, 14592) FEC for both soft- and hard-input approaches. Post-FEC SI performances are clearly affected by ISI caused by chromatic dispersion and the limited bandwidth of the optical components used in the transmission. We observe only ∼ 1 dB penalties between the optimal SI decoders and their HI counterparts. Interestingly, the latter allows for steeper waterfalls, which could indicate that some sort of Gaussian process might be taking place upon the decision operation on distorted samples thus helping the decoding procedure. Furthermore, we also investigated the variations of the mean number of decoding iterations for different received optical powers, in an attempt to provide some insight on the complexity and costs of ONUs located at different distances from the central. A maximum number of 6 decoding iterations is enough in our experimental setup to ensure error-free operation for SI and HI decoders after 25 km SSMF. Finally, we observe that the LDPC decoder weight coefficient will have a larger influence on the mean number of decoding iterations for lower ROP values.
