Toward Universal Belief Propagation Decoding for Short Binary Block Codes
Yifei Shen , Member, IEEE, Zongyao Li , Student Member, IEEE, Yuqing Ren , Graduate Student Member, IEEE, Emmanuel Boutillon , Senior Member, IEEE, Alexios Balatsoukas-Stimming , Senior Member, IEEE, Chuan Zhang , Senior Member, IEEE,
Xiaohu You , Fellow, IEEE, and Andreas Burg , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract— Belief propagation (BP) decoding has been recognized for its capacity-approaching performance and high throughput when decoding long low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. However, the application of BP decoding for short codes is hindered by dense parity-check matrices (PCMs) and prevalent short cycles in the Tanner graph. In this paper, we introduce a general method to extract an optimized sparse PCM for short binary block codes, which removes length-four cycles and enhances the connectivity of short cycles to enable BP decoding with improved performance. Notably, for short binary codes with lengths up to 64, our BP decoding performance approaches the maximum likelihood bound and surpasses the best-reported BP results with reduced computational complexity. Compared with other universal decoding algorithms, BP decoding using our extracted sparse PCMs is competitive in terms of both error-rate performance and computational complexity. These promising results suggest that our method to improve BP decoding for short codes is a step toward a practical universal BP decoder for next-generation communication systems.
Index Terms— Belief propagation, universal decoding, short block codes, next-generation communication system.

These requirements are critical for short-packet transmissions [4] in emerging applications like Internet of Things and ultra-reliable low-latency communications (uRLLC). For these scenarios, which involve block code lengths of tens to hundreds of bits, classical codes like ReedMuller (RM) codes [5] and Bose-Chaudhuri-Hocquenghem (BCH) codes [6] have demonstrated the capability to approach the sphere packing bound (SPB) [7] when employing softdecision decoding.
As suggested in recent surveys toward next-generation communications [8], [9], [10], [11], the re-consideration and synthesis of various requirements offer an opportunity to unify decoding algorithms, which could increase the hardware efficiency and compatibility across standards and scenarios. The decoding algorithm of such a universal decoder should be code-agnostic and should support a wide range of channel codes. Summarized in [9], research on code-agnostic decoding algorithms includes ordered reliability bit guessing random additive noise decoding (ORBGRAND) [12], ordered statistics decoding (OSD) [13], [14], and BP decoding [15]. Though approaching the maximum likelihood (ML) decoding performance for short codes, ORBGRAND and OSD face challenge in decoding long codes and low-rate codes due to excessive bit-flipping trials. BP decoding is effective with 5G long code scenarios, but struggles with poor error-rate performance for short codes due to length-four cycles (4-cycles) in Tanner graphs. To meet good performance across both long and short codes in a practical universal decoder, one solution is to exploit the strengths of ORBGRAND and OSD with short codes by concatenating short codes to construct long codes [16], [17]. However, altering long code construction may affect compatibility with existing standard codes. Given the recognized capabilities of BP decoding for current long code scenarios, focusing on its improvement for short codes presents a more practical solution. Additionally, the BP algorithm benefits joint and iterative detection and decoding receivers in next-generation wireless networks [18].

However, the direct application of BP decoding to Tanner graphs for non-LDPC codes often results in very poor performance, due to the prevalence of 4-cycles in the parity-check matrix (PCM). Over the past 20 years, many advanced BP decoders have been proposed to address this performance issue, which perform multiple BP decoding attempts using different PCMs or adjusted input soft messages. Specifically, adaptive BP decoding (ABP) [21] permutes the PCM based on the message reliability after each iteration, at the cost of time-consuming Gaussian elimination processes. Random redundant decoding (RRD) [22], [23] initiates multiple BP procedures in a serial manner, deriving the diversity of PCMs from random permutations. In contrast, multi-bases BP (MBBP) decoding [24], [25] sets up several BP sub-decoders, each tailored to distinct PCMs for parallel decoding. A renowned variant of MBBP, automorphism ensemble decoding [26], [27], gains the matrix diversity from different bit shuffles on a single PCM. Augmented BP (AugBP) decoding [28], [29] imposes bit guesses for a few bits and runs a list of BP sub-decoders with distinct input messages.
Though some of the aforementioned decoders exhibit nearML performance, they still present challenges to be further addressed. Specifically, for non-LDPC short codes, the PCMs utilized in those algorithms retain 4-cycles, which negatively impacts each BP decoding attempt. Moreover, the complexity of processing such dense PCMs is further amplified by multiple attempts. Therefore, it is necessary to first optimize a single BP decoding attempt by mitigating the effect of 4-cycles in the original PCM before further optimizations. As indicated by [30], eliminating all 4-cycles from the original Tanner graph is achievable, by splitting check nodes (CNs) and adding extra variable nodes (VNs). The authors of [30] proved that the performance after transforming the graph is improved over the binary erasure channel (BEC). However, for additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels, these added VNs do not contribute useful messages to the decoder, potentially degrading the BP performance [31]. Therefore, this strategy for eliminating 4-cycles was rarely studied for BP decoding. In 2016, the neural BP (NBP) decoding method was proposed to compensate for short cycles by setting message-passing weights via neural network training [32]. However, the performance gain brought by these weights is limited, because short cycles in the Tanner graph are not essentially eliminated. Although the authors of [33] additionally tried to optimize the original PCM to a cycle-reduced PCM, the improved performance is still insufficient to approach the ML bound for short codes.
In fact, the impractical performance of BP decoding for short codes is not solely due to short cycles. Small trapping sets also play a critical role in degrading the performance. Typically, trapping sets arise from combinations of short cycles, and the occurrence of small trapping sets indicates low connectivity between short cycles [34]. It has been pointed out in [35] that adding one redundant parity-check row can break certain trapping sets. We respond to these observations by proposing a methodology to extract a sparse PCM with enhanced cycle connectivity, while maintaining the inherent

encoding constraints. Our method optimizes existing 4-cycle removal strategies to minimize the number of the added auxiliary VNs (AVNs). Then, we propose a progressive row growth (PRG) scheme, which constructs a PCM with a row size exceeding the rank and low row weights. This constructed PCM is then transformed into a 4-cycle-free PCM using the optimized 4-cycle removal strategy. The resulting matrix, characterized by a girth of 6 and enhanced cycle connectivity, exhibits reduced matrix density and avoids both 4-cycles and small trapping sets. This optimized PCM improves the performance of a single BP attempt and provides a fundament for combining with advanced BP decoding methods.
A. Our Prior Work and Contributions
The concept of constructing a low-weight PCM and then removing 4-cycles was first proposed for the component convolutional codes of 4G LTE Turbo codes [36], where the extraction process is based on the polynomial field. In this new work, we focus on block codes and explore the application of this concept in the matrix field. A preliminary experiment applied to 5G NR polar codes in [37] has shown a promising result for certain extremely low-rate codes. However, in this early study, parity-check rows are randomly selected, and the excessive number of AVNs restricts the performance improvements. Compared with our previous work [36], [37], the novel contributions of this paper are summarized as follows.
1) We explore the relationship between the number of AVNs and short-cycle connectivity in 4-cycle-free PCMs. Based on this analysis, we propose an optimized 4-cycle removal strategy that avoids the formation of degree-1 and degree-2 CNs. Moreover, we prove that the complexity of BP decoding is reduced by employing the 4-cycle-free PCM.
2) We propose the PRG scheme that incorporates the optimized 4-cycle removal strategy. This scheme can systematically extract sparse PCMs tailored to binary short block codes. Utilizing the PRG scheme, we can obtain 4-cycle-free PCMs characterized by enhanced cycle connectivity and reduced number of small trapping sets.
3) We evaluate the performance of BP decoding on a single PCM, specifically contrasting our extracted sparse PCMs with state-of-the-art (SOA) PCMs for short RM, BCH, extended BCH (eBCH), and 5G NR polar codes. Notably, our extracted sparse PCMs outperform the best-reported BP results for the RM(32, 16) code in [38] and the eBCH(64, 24) code in [39] with lower complexity.
4) We compare our improved BP performance with other universal decoding algorithms and discuss strategies to further enhance this performance. A single BP decoding with our extracted PCMs outperforms ORBGRAND at low-to-medium code rates, and it matches the performance and complexity of order-2 OSD. Our trials with MBBP indicate that just three BP decoding attempts on our sparse PCMs are sufficient to reach ML performance for the RM(32, 16) code and the BCH(63, 45) code.

C. Small Trapping Sets and Cycle Connectivity
Another factor that impairs the performance of BP decoding is the presence of trapping sets, defined by [46]:
Definition 1: A trapping set is a set of VNs that are not eventually correct.1 It is denoted by T (a, b) if it includes a VNs and connects to b odd-degree CNs within the subgraph induced by this trapping set. Since VNs within small trapping sets struggle to receive accurate messages through a limited number of odd-degree CNs, these sets prevent the escape from false candidate codewords and lead to the error floor phenomenon [48]. Once trapped in a false codeword, adjusting the messages of the involved VNs allows the decoder to escape from the trap. In fact, the effectiveness of advanced BP decoding algorithms can be explained in terms of trapping sets. ABP decoding avoids trapping sets that lie in unreliable bits, and these bits are linked to an identity sub-matrix. MBBP and RRD explore multiple trapping set distributions by instantiating different PCMs. Aug-BP decoding facilitates the escape of certain VNs from trapping sets by providing them with reliable messages.
Inspired by the construction of finite-length binary LDPC codes, our goal is to develop high-quality PCMs for any short binary block code that can be used for high-performance BP decoding. These PCMs will eliminate 4-cycles and reduce the number of small trapping sets. To achieve this goal, we specifically target attenuating the impact of short cycles and trapping sets by transforming PCMs. Before introducing the proposed methodology, we outline techniques for identifying trapping sets and assessing their impact on decoding performance.
1) Enumeration of Trapping Sets: Not all trapping sets are harmful to BP decoding. In practice, the most harmful trapping set is known as the elementary trapping set (ETS) [49], characterized by all connected CNs to the set of a VNs having degrees of 1 or 2. Essentially, the parameter b records the number of degree-1 CNs. An efficient algorithm to enumerate ETSs was proposed in [50]. Starting with a batch of small ETSs, this approach expands them to uncover larger ETSs through connections to odd-degree CNs. Focusing on a girth of 6, we illustrate examples of ETSs induced by 6-cycles in Fig. 1, assuming VNs have a degree of 3. A single 6-cycle can be identified as a T (3, b) trapping set. From such a cycle, expansion through an odd-degree CN allows for the exploration of connections to VNs within another cycle, potentially creating larger trapping sets such as T (6, 4) or T (4, 2). Furthermore, bridging the two odd-degree CNs of T (4, 2) results in T (4, 0), which is formed by three 6-cycles. It should be noted that the value of b may vary from the examples in Fig. 1 if the degrees of VNs change.

III. PROPOSED METHODS TO EXTRACT SPARSE MATRICES FOR SHORT BINARY BLOCK CODES
Inspired by the methods for constructing finite-length LDPC codes, we aim to extract an equivalent Tanner graph for non-LDPC codes that features a large girth and minimal small trapping sets. For LDPC codes, the construction relies on the degree profile of CNs and VNs determined by density evolution [54], and the target matrix size is (N − K) × N . In contrast, when extracting a high-quality PCM for nonLDPC codes, we start from a given PCM which provides N − K fundamental parity-check constraints. To elevating

2) Avoidance of Degree-1 and Degree-2 CNs: During the process of removing 4-cycles among rows {i1, · · · , is}, if row i1 is a subset of all the other rows,2 then adding the corresponding AVN converts row i1 to a degree-1 CN. Given that degree-1 CNs are not helpful for the parity check, the added AVN and resulting degree-1 CN can be omitted. This operation is functionally equivalent to directly adding row i1 onto the other s − 1 rows.
Moving on to degree-2 CNs, our focus is on those degree-2 CNs that appear in the 4-cycle removal process. If the intersection of rows i1 to is differs from row i1 by a single VN vj, then adding the corresponding AVN turns row i1 into a degree-2 CN. It is known that a degree-2 CN connects two equivalent VNs so that we can merge these two VNs to decrease the number of AVNs. This merging operation may increment the degree of vj, which is essentially equivalent to directly adding row i1 onto the other s − 1 rows.
This optimized 4-cycle removal strategy is outlined in Alg. 1, where the size of the transformed matrix H˜ is denoted as M˜ × N˜ . The basic steps (Lines 1 − 16) are as presented in [56], with additional steps (Lines 17 − 23) integrated to prevent the formation of degree-1 and degree-2 CNs. These additional steps reduce the number of required AVNs, which leads to a higher density within the AVN columns and allows more messages from information bits through connected CNs to help overcome their possible trapping state. Conversely, if such CNs are retained after eliminating 2 × t cycles, their connected AVNs have a degree of 3, potentially resulting in a small ACE value when involved in a 6-cycle. This strategy will be employed in our proposed PRG scheme where selecting redundant parity-check rows generates frequent degree-2 CNs when removing 4-cycles, which can minimize the number of AVNs of the final extracted PCM. The details will be introduced in Section III-B.

B. Part Two: Progressive Row Growth Scheme
An exhaustive approach is to select a matrix with rank N − K from all non-zero codewords of the dual code C⊥, which ideally minimizes the number of small trapping sets after removing 4-cycles. However, this approach implies a large search space. As a more practical alternative, we focus on a search space composed of low-weight parity-check rows and propose a PRG scheme to select parity-check rows and further extract the sparse matrix. The low row weights in this defined search space can ensure a reduced number of 4-cycles to be eliminated, thereby preventing the addition of too many AVNs. Also, the low row weights can effectively limit the BP decoding complexity with the extracted 4-cycle-free matrix at a low level, as substantiated by Theorem 1.
The PRG scheme is inspired by the ACE-constrained PEG algorithm used in LDPC design [20]. Given the predetermined degree profile and the desired girth, the PEG algorithm progressively assigns dvj edges to each VN vj, while ensuring that no edge introduces cycles that are shorter than the girth. An edge is determined by selecting a CN that satisfies the girth restriction and currently has the lowest degree. If there are multiple candidate CNs, preference is given to the CN that maximizes the minimum ACE for its newly involved cycles, which effectively selects the CN whose associated cycles have

the highest connectivity to the rest of the graph. If several CNs meet the above criteria, a CN is randomly chosen from the preferred candidates for connection to vj. This algorithm incorporates the restrictions on the girth and ACE for the whole LDPC matrix into the selection process for every edge.
Compared to the construction of LDPC matrices, where the flexible design constraints allow for progressive edge selection, extracting girth-6 sparse PCMs for non-LDPC codes faces challenges due to the fixed parity check constraint. In preparing the PCM for 4-cycle removal, we can only select entire rows from a predefined search space matrix, with selection criteria related to the cycle connectivity of the transformed girth-6 matrix rather than the constructed matrix. For simplicity, the PCMs of the search space, alongside the PCMs before and after the 4-cycles removal, are labeled as Hs, Ha, and Hb, respectively. The solution to address these challenges is the proposed PRG scheme, which is depicted in Fig. 2 and detailed as follows.

of degree-2 CNs. Since the elimination of 4-cycles splits rows in Ha, row weights in Hb(i) are generally less than the weight of hk′ . The message updates in our simulations are conducted using the NMS algorithm in a flooding schedule. The same damping factor, γ = 0.7, is employed across all simulated channel codes, enhancing consistency in our results [37]. The two parameters in Alg. 2 are set to σ = 5 and p = 2. For simplicity, the transformed matrix from the standard PCM through our optimized 4-cycle removal strategy is abbreviated as PCM-I, and the sparse matrix extracted by the PRG scheme is denoted as PCM-II. Since all compared decoders are BP decoders, in this section, we measure the computational complexity by counting the number of edges |E| in the matrix to simplify the evaluation.

C. Comparison With NBP and NBP-Based Auto-Encoder
In [33], the authors proposed NBP and applied it to short BCH codes, based on which an auto-encoder was designed in [58] to construct short linear block codes beyond BCH codes. We will present our bit error rate (BER) performance on the BCH(63, 45) code and compare it with results in [33] and [58]. In [33], NBP decoding is applied to the standard PCM Hstd and the cycle-reduced PCM HCR of the BCH(63, 45) code. The auto-encoder proposed in [58] constructs a new (63, 45) code featuring a more sparse PCM, denoted as HAE in the following.
Consistent with the configurations in [33] and [58], we set Imax = 5 to compare the BER performance of various BP decoders, as shown in Fig. 7. Based on the original Hstd, adjusting message-passing weights via neural networks contributes to an approximate 0.8 dB gain at a BER of 10−4, and optimizing Hstd to HCR achieves an additional gain of 0.67 dB. The gated NBP decoding of the HAE constructed in [58] provides an extra 0.5 dB gain compared to decoding the HCR of the BCH code. Although this auto-encoder represents a significant improvement, further enhancement is possible by increasing the number of redundant rows. Specifically, nonzero codewords of the dual code C⊥ of cyclic codes can be decomposed into multiple sets consisting only of cyclic shifts of one codeword [24]. For example, we can translate the 189 × 63 PCM Hs into 3 cyclic PCMs of 63 × 63. Thanks to the enhanced 4-cycle connectivity, each cyclic PCM potentially offers better performance than the 18 × 63 HAE at high Eb/N0 values, but at the cost of processing more edges.
In our work, we extract a 63 × 72 PCM based on the PRG scheme, with 567 edges and a uniform row weight of 9. As shown in Fig. 7, this PCM-II provides a 0.9 dB gain over cyclic PCMs of [24], with no 4-cycles and 43.75% fewer edges. Moreover, using this PCM-II reduces the gap between BP and ML decoding to just 0.35 dB at a BER of 10−6.

In this work, we focus on improving BP performance with single PCMs and propose a methodology to extract sparse 4-cycle-free PCMs for binary short block codes. The results indicate that the sparse PCM developed in this work outperforms the SOA PCMs for short RM, BCH, eBCH, and NR polar codes. In this section, we compare the improved BP performance with other universal decoding algorithms and explore further optimization approaches for our work.

B. Further Optimization on Our Work
When the code length is short enough, the manageable size of the search space PCM enables efficient identifications of near-optimal PCMs through greedy heuristics. As code length further increases, the expanded row size of Hs introduces substantial complexity for the extraction of a high-performance sparse PCM. This is exemplified by the RM(128, 64) code, where the search space matrix expands to

94488 × 128. The reliance on greedy heuristics to remove 4cycles further diminishes the matrix optimality with longer codes. Despite this challenge, the PCM extracted through the proposed PRG scheme still shows improved sparsity and performance compared to cyclic PCMs in [24] for BCH codes and sparse PCMs in [61] for polar codes. Nevertheless, there remains a non-negligible performance gap to achieve the ML bound, which suggests exploring advanced BP decoding schemes as an open research. Some possible solutions are outlined as follows.
1) Optimized Message-Passing Schedule: Apply smarter message-passing schedules than the flooding schedule, such as layered schedule [63] and informed dynamic schedule [64].
2) Optimized Message-Passing Equation: Design advanced message-passing equations beyond SPA or NMS equations, with approaches like NBP decoding [33], adjusted min-sum decoding [65], or finite alphabet decoding [66].
3) Multiple Decoding Attempts: Engage in multiple BP decoding attempts with different PCMs or varied input LLRs, including RRD [22], MBBP [24], and aug-BP [28].
Subsequently, we experiment with a few approaches to illustrate the further enhancement.
In the first trial with MBBP decoding, we generate a list of PCM-II matrices for multiple BP decoding attempts, all extracted with a constant value of Ma. After identifying the first H(bMa), its corresponding Ha(Ma) is excluded from Hs. The process repeats until no further H(bMa) can be derived from the current Hs, forming a list of PCM-II. The results of this MBBP trial are appended to Fig. 9. The PCM-IIbased MBBP trial with a list size of 3 almost matches the ML performance for the RM(32, 16) and BCH(63, 45) codes, which just triples the number of processed edges compared to the standard PCM. VI. CONCLUSION
Toward next-generation communications, this work proposes a methodology to enhance BP decoding for short binary block codes. The key ingredients are a refinement of current 4-cycle removal strategies and the introduction of progressive row growth scheme for extracting sparse PCMs with high cycle connectivity. Simulation results show that our BP decoding is promising to approach ML performance for short block codes, while maintaining low computational complexity. Compared with other universal decoding algorithms, BP decoding using our extracted PCMs outperforms ORBGRAND at lowto-medium code rates and is competitive with OSD in terms of both error-rate performance and computational complexity.
