A Serial Concatenated Scheme for LDPC Code to Achieve Better Error Correction Performance

Zhe Wang1, Meng Zhang1 ( 1): National ASIC Research Center, Southeast University, Nanjing, China
e-mail: wangzhe0543@126.com

Abstract—Low Density Parity Check(LDPC) Codes is a class of approaching limits error correction codes which has gained widely application in communications. The excellent performance, however, is the result of a large number of repeatedly conducting iterative decoding algorithm, and is apt to be seriously affected by small girth in the parity check matrix. In this paper we present a novel serial concatenated scheme for LDPC codes which utilize much smaller generation matrix and the interleaver to reduce possibilities of girth, thus reducing the number of iterations in decoding. Simulation shows that the new codes have better performance while less requirement of iterations.

I. Introduction
Since its rediscovery in 1996[1], LDPC codes have been a research concentration because of its approaching limits performance and flexibility in construction. Many communication standards such as WiMAX and DVB-S2 have adopted LDPC as the error correction codes in data transmission[2,3]. For WiMAX, the adopted (576,288) QCLDPC codes could reduce the bits errors rate(BER) to 10-8 at the signal noise ratio(SNR) of 3.5dB after 15 iterations of decoding process[4]. Compared to traditional RS(255,239) codes, the LDPC codes obtain a gain of more than 3 dB.
Despite the excellent performance, there are still some limitations which affect the decoding algorithm, the Belief Propagation(BP) to be fully exploited, especially the "" in the generation matrix. The "" could bring loses to the code gain in that it makes the likelihood information propagated on the Tanner graph un-independent. Eliminating the "" has become a prime goal in designing new LDPC codes, and a variety of methods have been developed including OCS-LDPC and PEG QC-LDPC[3]. These methods have made notably advances, while without exclusion, all have introduced a large parity check matrix. In this paper, we tried another direction to adopt smaller matrix and introduce a scheme of concatenation. In the new scheme, decoding iterations are carried out in both the whole codes and the component codes. Simulations show that compared to traditional LDPC and the simple serial concatenated LDPC codes, the new scheme achieved much better performance under smaller iteration numbers.

III. The new serial concatenated scheme
Here we present the detail of the new scheme. Since large parity check matrix can by no means totally eliminate the small girth, we now try a opposite way, i.e., shrinking the matrix by using less parity check equations for the code. Meanwhile, to compensate performance's decrease due to the shrinking, two constant codes are used rather than one. Between these two encoders are a random interleaver to scramble the coded bits from the first encoder[6,7]. The schematic graph is shown in picture 3:

In the receiving side, iterations are carried out to decode each of the component LDPC codes. To fully take advantage of the code structure, outer iterations are carried out after the fulfilling decoding the first component code, that is, to send to decoded likelihood information back to decoder 2 and repeat the same decoding process. The outer iterations would be important in that it could further reduce the possibility of error bits and decrease the error platform, which may appear in many sparse graph code including LDPC and Turbo codes. For the component code, the number of iterations for decoding is set to be 8 and that of the outer iteration is 4. Now the simulation results are presented here. Figure 5 shows the comparison of several different structure LDPC codes. As can be seen clearly, the serially concatenated structure LDPC codes have a clear advantage over traditional (576,288) and (576,384) LDPC codes. For example, at a Signal Noise Ratio(SNR) of 3.0dB, under 4 outer iterations and 8 decoding iterations, the serial concatenated LDPC code's BER is 8.68*10-9 while the regular (576,288) QC-LDPC code's BER under 15 iterations is about 1.0*10-6. Further comparison is provided with two times of encoding scheme using the same (576,384) LDPC code. As shown in figure , merely combining two time of LDPC without any outer iteration shows much deficit, as the BER in 2.5 dB's SNR is 8.0*10-8, which is much worse than that of the purposed code. The reason is that compared to simple LDPC codes, the serial concatenated structure reduces the possibility of small girths, while makes better use of the reliability likelihood information
