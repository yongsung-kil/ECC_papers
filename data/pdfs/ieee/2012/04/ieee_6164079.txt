Maximum-Girth Cylinder-Type Block-Circulant LDPC Codes
M. Gholami and M. Esmaeili, Member, IEEE

Abstract—In this paper, a particular class of block-circulant low-density parity-check (BC-LDPC) codes referred to as cylindertype BC-LDPC (CTBC-LDPC) codes is studied. We represent a cylinder-type parity-check matrix H by a graph called the block-structure graph of H and denoted by BSG(H). Using the properties of BSG(H) we show that CTBC matrices with column-weight two and girth an arbitrary multiple of 8 can be constructed, while for a CTBC matrix H with column-weight
≥ 3 this girth cannot exceed 12. An algorithm generating CTBC-LDPC codes of arbitrary possible girth is given. The algorithm produces a large group of codes with flexible rate, length and girth. From performance perspective over AWGN channel, the maximum girth-12 CTBC-LDPC codes with columnweight at least three generated by the given algorithm are at least as good as their random-like counterpart codes and outperform the codes of the same size constructed by successive-level-growth, progressive-edge-growth, and partition-and-shift methods.
Index Terms—LDPC codes, block-structure graph, cylinder form matrix, maximum girth.
I. INTRODUCTION AND BACKGROUNDS
L OW-DENSITY parity-check (LDPC) codes [1][2], represented by their Tanner graph [3], can be decoded with iterative decoding algorithms such as the sum-product [4] algorithm for which the decoding complexity grows linearly with the code length and yet achieves error-performance very close to the theoretical limits set by Shannon [5]. Among other references, construction of random-like LDPC codes has been addressed in [6]-[9]. Due to the encoding complexity of random-like LDPC codes, construction of well-structured codes is of interest [10]-[25]. In particular, construction of quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes, due to their simple encoding process using linear shift registers, is of great interest.
An LDPC code C represented by a parity-check matrix (PCM) H is called a block-circulant LDPC (BC-LDPC) code if, for some positive integer m > 1, the matrix H consists of m × m circulant permutation matrices and m × m allzero matrices. Obviously, any BC-LDPC code is quasi-cyclic.
