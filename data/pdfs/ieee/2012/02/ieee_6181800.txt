A Transform Approach for Constructing Quasi-Cyclic Euclidean Geometry LDPC Codes
Qiuju Diao, Wei Zhou, Shu Lin and Khaled Abdel-Ghaffar Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering University of California Davis, CA 95616, USA
Email: {judiao, weizhou, shulin, ghaffar}@ucdavis.edu

Abstract—A method for constructing quasi-cyclic Euclidean geometry (QC-EG) LDPC codes in the Fourier transform domain is presented. Given a Euclidean geometry over a finite field of characteristic 2, base matrices in the Fourier transform domain are first constructed. Then the inverse Fourier transforms of these base matrices, combined with row and column permutations, result in low-density arrays of circulant permutation matrices and/or zero matrices. The null spaces of these low-density arrays give a family of QC-EG-LDPC codes. Codes in a special subclass have large minimum distances and their Tanner graphs contain no harmful trapping sets with sizes smaller than their minimum distances.
I. INTRODUCTION
Quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes have advantages over other types of LDPC codes in hardware implementation of encoding and decoding. Encoding of a QC-LDPC code can be efficiently implemented using simple shift registers with complexity linearly proportional to its number of parity-check symbols (or its length) [1]. In hardware implementation of its decoder, the circular structure of the parity-check matrix of the code simplifies the wire routing for message passing [2] and allows partially parallel decoding [3] which offers a tradeoff between decoding complexity and decoding speed. Well designed or constructed QC-LDPC code can perform as well as other types of LDPC codes. Over the last 12 years, many methods for constructing QC-LDPC codes have been proposed. Several QC-LDPC codes have been adopted as standard codes for various next generations of communication systems and they are appearing in recent data storage products. More applications are expected to come.
Major methods for constructing LDPC codes can be divided into two general categories: graph-theoretic-based methods and algebraic methods. Each type of construction has its advantages and disadvantages. In general, algebraically constructed LDPC codes have lower error-floors and their decoding using iterative message-passing algorithms converges faster. Furthermore, it is much easier to construct algebraic LDPC codes with large minimum distances. Algebraic constructions of LDPC codes are mainly based on finite geometries, finite fields and combinatorial designs. These constructions result in either QC-LDPC codes or cyclic LDPC codes.
Recently, we have proposed a transform domain approach for constructing algebraic QC-LDPC codes [4], [5]. This

approach makes code construction much easier and simplifies the computations of the dimensions of the constructed codes (or the ranks of their parity-check matrices) and the girth of the codes’ Tanner graphs, as demonstrated in [4], [5]. Furthermore, this approach puts all the algebraic constructions presented in [6]-[12] under a single framework.
In this paper, we are only concerned with constructions of binary QC-LDPC codes. A binary QC-LDPC code is given by the null space of an array H of sparse circulants over GF(2) of the same size. If the array H, viewed as a matrix, has constant column weight γ and constant row weight ρ, then the code given by the null space of H is said to be (γ,ρ )regular, otherwise it is said to be irregular.
In almost all of the proposed constructions of LDPC codes (quasi-cyclic or not), the following constraint on the rows and columns of the parity-check matrix H is imposed: no two rows (or two columns) can have more than one place where they both have non-zero components. This constraint on the rows and columns of H is referred to as the row-column (RC)constraint. This RC-constraint ensures that the Tanner graph of the LDPC code given by the null space of H has a girth of at least 6 and that the minimum distance of the code, if (γ,ρ )-regular, is at least γ + 1 [13], [14]. This distance bound is tight for regular LDPC codes whose parity-check matrices have large column weights and row redundancies, such as the algebraic LDPC codes constructed using finite geometries, finite fields and combinatorial designs. A parity-check matrix H that satisfies the RC-constraint is called an RC-constrained parity-check matrix and the code given by its null space is called an RC-constrained LDPC code.
In this paper, we first give a review of our earlier construction of QC-LDPC codes in the Fourier transform domain [4], [5]. Then, we show that QC-LDPC codes can be constructed from Euclidean geometries in the Fourier transform domain. Two large classes of quasi-cyclic Euclidean geometry (QCEG)-LDPC codes are constructed. Before we conclude the paper, we will show that codes in a special subclass of QCEG-LDPC codes have no harmful trapping sets with sizes smaller than the minimum distances (or minimum weight) of the codes. Consequently, their error-floors are dominated by their minimum distances.

III. CONSTRUCTION OF QC-LDPC CODES IN THE FOURIER TRANSFORM DOMAIN
The one-to-one correspondence between an array of circulants and/or ZMs and a diagonal array of matrices of the form given by (11) or (14) is our basis for constructing QC-LDPC codes in the Fourier transform domain. In the following, we are considering only construction of binary QC-LDPC codes whose parity-check matrices are arrays of CPMs and/or ZMs.

To construct a QC-LDPC code, we begin with an appropriately chosen m × n matrix B over a finite field GF(2r). Form a (2r − 1) × (2r − 1) diagonal array H˜ = diag(B◦0, B◦1, . . . , B◦(2r−2)) of the form given by (14). Next, we apply the permutation π−1 = (πr−o1w, πc−o1l ) on the rows and columns of H˜ to obtain an m × n array H˜ π−1 = [AiF,j ]0≤i<m,0≤j<n of diagonal matrices AFi,j of size (2r −1)× (2r − 1). Then, we take the inverse Fourier transform of H˜ π−1 to obtain an m×n array H˜ π−1,F−1 = H = [Ai,j ]0≤i<m,0≤j<n of CPMs and/or ZMs of size (2r − 1) × (2r − 1). H is an m(2r − 1) × n(2r − 1) matrix over GF(2). For r ≥ 3, H is a low-density matrix. The null space of H gives a QC-LDPC code Cqc. Since the array H is constructed from B, we call B the base matrix for code construction.
If the base matrix B satisfies the condition given by the following theorem, then the parity-check matrix H of the QCLDPC code Cqc satisfies the RC-constraint and its Tanner graph has a girth of at least 6. We will state the theorem without a proof. A proof can be found in [4].
Theorem 1. A necessary and sufficient condition for an array H of CPMs and/or ZMs to satisfy the RC-constraint is that every 2 × 2 submatrix in the base matrix B contains at least one zero entry or is non-singular.
The condition on B given by the above theorem is referred to as the 2 × 2 sub-matrix (SM)-constraint. A base matrix B that satisfies the 2 × 2 SM-constraint is called a 2 × 2 SMconstrained base matrix.
Next, we show that the construction of an RC-constrained low-density parity-check matrix of a QC-LDPC code which consists of an array of CPMs and/or ZMs can be done directly from a 2×2 SM-constrained base matrix B without forming the array H˜ = diag(B◦0, B◦1, . . . , B◦(2r−2)), taking the inverse row and column permutations π−1 = (πr−o1w, πc−o1l ) and the inverse Fourier transform F −1.

If H is an RC-constrained array of CPMs and/or ZMs constructed from B, then the RC-constrained array constructed from BT is the transpose HT of H.
The above construction puts all the constructions of QCLDPC codes based on finite fields given in [6]–[12] under a single framework. In these papers, the base matrices are constructed based on finite fields and combinatorial designs.
IV. CONSTRUCTION OF QC-LDPC CODES IN FOURIER TRANSFORM DOMAIN BASED ON TWO-DIMENSIONAL EUCLIDEAN GEOMETRIES
In this section, we show that QC-LDPC codes can also be constructed from finite geometries using the Fourier transform domain approach presented in the last section. We first consider code construction based on two-dimensional Euclidean geometries over finite fields of characteristic 2. Construction of QC-LDPC codes based on Euclidean geometries with dimensions higher than 2 is similar and will be considered in the next section.
Consider the two-dimensional Euclidean geometry EG(2, 2r) over GF(2r) [14]. This geometry consists of 22r points and 2r(2r + 1) lines. A point in EG(2, 2r) is simply a two-tuple a = (a0, a1) over GF(2r) and the zero two-tuple (0, 0) is called the origin. A line in EG(2, 2r) is a one-dimensional subspace or a coset of a one-dimensional subspace of the vector space of all the 22r two-tuples over GF(2r). A line consists of 2r points. If a point a is on a line L in EG(2, 2r), we say that the line L passes through the point a. Any two points in EG(2, 2r) are connected by one and only one line in EG(2, 2r). For every point a in EG(2, 2r), there are 2r + 1 lines that intersect at (or pass through) the point a.

The field GF(22r), as an extension field of the ground field GF(2r), is a realization of EG(2, 2r). Let α be a primitive element of GF(22r). Then, the powers of α, α−∞ = 0, α0 = 1, α , α2, · · · , α2r−2, give all the 22r elements of GF(22r) and they represent the 22r points of EG(2, 2r). The 0-element represents the origin of EG(2, 2r). Factor 22r − 1 as 22r − 1 = (2r+1)(2r −1). Let β = α2r+1. The order of β is 2r−1. Then, 0, 1, β , β2, . . . , β2r−2 form a subfield, GF(2r), of GF(22r) and can be regarded as the ground field for GF(22r).
Let EG∗(2, 2r) be the subgeometry obtained from EG(2, 2r) by removing the origin and the 2r + 1 lines passing through the origin. This subgeometry consists of 22r − 1 non-origin points and 22r − 1 lines not passing through the origin. Let a and b be two non-zero independent points in EG∗(2, 2r).
These points can be represented by two non-zero elements of GF(22r) such that a+ηb = 0 for all η ∈ GF(2r). Then, a+ηb, η ∈ GF(2r), is a line in EG∗(2, 2r) consisting of 2r points of EG∗(2, 2r), a, a + b, a + βb, . . . , a + β2r−2b, which are represented by powers of α. Let L = {αj0 , αj1 , . . . , αj2r−1 } be a line in EG∗(2, 2r). For 0 ≤ i < 22r − 1, let αiL = {αj0+i, αj1+i, . . . , αj2r−1+i}. Then, αiL is also a line in EG∗(2, 2r) and α0L, αL, . . . , α22r−2L give all the 22r − 1 lines in EG∗(2, 2r) [14]. This structure of lines is called cyclic
structure. Let L be a line in EG∗(2, 2r). Based on L, we define the fol-
lowing (22r − 1)-tuple over GF(2), vL = (v0, v1, . . . , v22r−2), whose components correspond to the 22r −1 non-origin points α0, α , α2, . . . , α22r−2 of EG∗(2, 2r), where vj = 1 if αj is a point on L and vj = 0 otherwise. It is clear that the weight of vL is 2r. This (22r − 1)-tuple vL over GF(2) is called the
incidence vector of the line L [14]. Due to the cyclic structure of the lines in EG∗(2, 2r), the incidence vector vαL of the line
αL is the cyclic-shift (one place to the right) of the incidence
vector vL of the line L. Form a (22r −1)×(22r−1) matrix HEG over GF(2) with the
incidence vectors of the 22r − 1 lines, α0L, αL, . . . , α22r−2L, of EG∗(2, 2r) as rows. Then, HEG is a (22r − 1) × (22r − 1) circulant over GF(2) with both column and row weights 2r.
HEG can be obtained by using the incidence vector vL
of the line L as the generator and cyclically shifting vL 22r − 2 times. Since two lines in EG∗(2, 2r) have at most one
point in common, their incidence vectors have at most one
position where they both have 1-component. Consequently,
HEG satisfies the RC-constraint and its null space gives a cyclic EG-LDPC code CEG of length n = 22r − 1 [13], [14], whose Tanner graph has girth of at least 6. The minimum distance of CEG is exactly 2r + 1 and the rank of HEG is equal to 3r − 1 [13], [14]. Extensive simulation results
have shown that the cyclic EG-LDPC codes perform very

codes are cyclic, their encoding is very simple and can be
implemented with a simple feedback shift-register [14].

V. CONSTRUCTION OF QC-LDPC CODES IN THE FOURIER TRANSFORM DOMAIN BASED ON
MULTI-DIMENSIONAL EUCLIDEAN GEOMETRIES
Consider the m-dimensional Euclidean geometry EG(m, 2r) over GF(2r) with m ≥ 3. This geometry consists of 2mr points and 2(m−1)r(2mr − 1)/(2r − 1) lines. Each point is an m-tuple over GF(2r). Each line consists of 2r points. The field GF(2mr) as an extension field of the ground field GF(2r) is a realization of the geometry EG(m, 2r) [14]. Let α be a primitive element of GF(2mr). Then, the powers of α,α −∞ = 0, α0 = 1, α, . . . , α2mr−2, represent the 2mr points of EG(m, 2r).
Let EG∗(m, 2r) be the subgeometry obtained by removing the origin, represented by α−∞ = 0, and the lines passing through the origin from EG(m, 2r). This subgeometry consists of 2mr −1 non-origin points and (2(m−1)r −1)(2mr −1)/(2r − 1) lines not passing through the origin of EG(m, 2r). The lines in the subgeometry EG∗(m, 2r) also have cyclic structure. The lines in EG∗(m, 2r) can be partitioned into K = (2(m−1)r − 1)/(2r − 1) cyclic classes, each class consisting of 2mr − 1 lines, α0L, αL, . . . , α(2mr−2)L. We denote these K cyclic classes of lines by Q0, Q1, . . . , QK−1. Any line L in a cyclic class can be used to generate all the 2mr − 1 lines in the class by multiplying the points in L by α0 = 1, α, . . . , α(2mr−2) in turn.

In fact, the null space of any subarray of HEG,qc(t) gives a QC-EG-LDPC code.
The transpose B(t)T = [BT0 BT1 . . . BtT−1] of B(t) can also be used as the base matrix for code construction. Based on B(t)T , we can construct a c × ct array [HEG,qc(t)]T of CPMs and ZMs of size of (2r − 1) × (2r − 1). [HEG,qc(t)]T is a (2mr −1)×t(2mr −1) matrix with column and row weights 2r and t2r, respectively. The null space of [HEG,qc(t)]T gives an RC-constrained QC-EG-LDPC code with rate at least (t−1)/t and minimum distance at least 2r +1. Of course, the null space of any subarray of [HEG,qc(t)]T also gives a QC-EG-LDPC code.
The above construction based on multi-dimensional Euclidean geometries gives a large class of QC-EG-LDPC codes with various lengths and rates. Notice that for a twodimensional Euclidean geometry, the base matrix is constructed based on any single line not passing through the origin of the geometry. However, for a Euclidean geometry with dimension higher than 2, multiple base matrices can be constructed from multiple lines.
VI. TRAPPING SET STRUCTURE OF A SUBCLASS OF QC-EG-LDPC CODES
Most LDPC codes have a common severe weakness, known as error-floor [18], which is mostly caused by an undesirable structure, known as a trapping set [19] in the Tanner graph of the code. Error-floor may preclude LDPC codes from applications where very low error rates are required. In this section, we show that a subclass of QC-EG-LDPC codes have very good trapping set structure. As a result, codes in this subclass have very low error-floors.

1 ≤ κ ≤ n and 0 ≤ τ ≤ m, a (κ,τ ) trapping set, denoted by T (κ,τ ), is defined as a set of κ variable-nodes (VNs) in G which induces a sub-graph of G with exactly τ odd-degree check-nodes (CNs) and an arbitrary number of even-degree CNs. The parameter κ is referred to as the size of the trapping set. A trapping set is said to be elementary if all CNs in the induced sub-graph have degree one or degree two and there are exactly τ CNs of degree one.
Iterative decoding, such as the SPA and MSA, is very susceptible to trapping sets. Extensive studies and simulation results show that the trapping sets that result in high decoding failure rates and contribute significantly to high error-floors are those with small values κ and small ratios τ / κ, say τ /κ < 1. These trapping sets are commonly referred to as small trapping sets. The most harmful ones are elementary trapping sets with τ/κ< 1.
Besides small trapping sets, undetected errors caused by small minimum weight of a code also contribute considerably to the error-floor of the code. If there are no harmful trapping sets with size κ smaller than the minimum weight of an LDPC code, then the error-floor of the code is dominated by the minimum weight of the code.
In a recent paper [20], we proved the following theorem.
Theorem 2. For an RC-constrained (γ,ρ )-regular LDPC code with γ > 1, its Tanner graph contains no trapping set of size smaller than γ + 1 for which the number of odd-degree CNs is smaller than γ.
Consider the QC-EG-LDPC code CEG,qc constructed using the two-dimensional Euclidean geometry EG(2, 2r). The parity-check matrix HEG,qc of the code is a (2r +1)×(2r +1) array of CPMs and ZMs of size (2r − 1) × (2r − 1). This array is a (22r −1)×(22r −1) matrix over GF(2) whose column and row weights are both 2r. As pointed out in Section IV, the rank of HEG,qc is 3r − 1. The null space of HEG,qc gives a binary (4r − 1, 4r − 3r) QC-EG-LDPC code with minimum distance exactly 2r + 1. Since HEG,qc satisfies the RC-constraint, it follows from Theorem 2 that the Tanner graph of the QC-EGLDPC code CEG,qc has no (κ,τ ) trapping set of size κ smaller than 2r +1 with the number τ of odd-degree CNs smaller than 2r. This implies that for κ ≤ 2r, there is no (κ,τ ) trapping set with the ratio τ /κ < 1.
Summarizing the results given above, we have the following parameters for the cyclic EG-LDPC code CEG,qc constructed using the two-dimensional Euclidean geometry EG(2, 2r) over GF(2r): (1) length 4r −1; (2) dimension 4r −3r; (3) minimum distance 2r + 1; (4) girth at least 6; and (5) no trapping set of size less than 2r + 1 with number of odd-degree CNs less than 2r. Consequently, the error-floor is mainly dominated by the minimum weight 2r + 1. If r is large enough, say r ≥ 4, the QC-EG-LDPC code CEG,qc has a large minimum weight. Hence, the code decoded with the SPA or MSA should have a very low error-floor.

VII. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we presented a method for constructing algebraic QC-LDPC codes using a transform domain approach. This approach puts most of the previous algebraic constructions of QC-LDPC code under a single framework. We also showed that QC-LDPC codes can be constructed from finite Euclidean geometries using the Fourier transform domain approach. In addition, we also showed that the Tanner graphs of codes in a subclass of QC-EG-LDPC codes have no harmful trapping sets with sizes smaller than their minimum distances. Since they have relatively large minimum distances, their error performances are expected to have very low errorfloors.
