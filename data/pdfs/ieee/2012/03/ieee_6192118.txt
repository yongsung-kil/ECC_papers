LDPC Convolutional Codes using Layered Decoding Algorithm for High Speed Coherent Optical Transmission

Deyuan Chang1, Fan Yu1, Zhiyu Xiao1, Nebojsa Stojanovic2, Fabian N. Hauske2, Yi Cai3, Changsong Xie2, Liangchuan Li1, Xiaogeng Xu1, Qianjin Xiong1

Abstract: We successfully realized layered decoding for LDPC convolutional codes designed for
application in high speed optical transmission systems. A relatively short code with 20% redundancy was FPGA-emulated with a Q-factor of 5.7dB at BER of 10-15.
OCIS codes: (060.2330) Fiber optics communications; (060.4510) Optical communications

1. Introduction

High speed coherent optical transmission systems are being intensively investigated to meet significantly increased traffic demands, including speeds of 100Gb/s, 400Gb/s and beyond (super channel). High order phase modulation formats, such as QPSK, 16QAM and even 64QAM [1], are considered for increasing spectral efficiency and transmission capacity. However, higher optical signal-to-noise ratio (OSNR) values are required for more complex modulation formats to reach the same transmission distance as those of systems with less constellation points. To address this issue, soft decision forward error correction (SD-FEC) codes providing a high net coding gain (NCG) performance play an essential role.
In recent years, several 20%-overhead SD-FEC schemes using low-density parity-check block codes (LDPCBCs) have been proposed [2, 3]. A single LDPC code with 20% overhead and the codeword length of nearly 20000 was reported in [4]. The code enables NCG of 11.3dB without error floor down to BER of 10-15 which was verified by FPGA emulation. Two main implementation challenges should be considered in case of such a long code. First, the well known routing congestion problem becomes severe as the codeword length grows, and the second one is that the feedback-loop architecture of an LDPC-BC decoder incurs more global wiring overhead. Both of them limit the clock frequency and require larger space and power consumption than the initial estimation.
LDPC convolutional codes (LDPC-CCs), as the counterpart of LDPC-BCs, have the potential to overcome the two implementation drawbacks, as investigated in [5]. The comparable coding performance can be obtained by LDPC-CCs with shorter codeword length. Moreover, LDPC-CCs are suitable to be implemented in pipeline architecture (one iteration corresponds to one decoder processor). Unfortunately, LDPC-CCs are traditionally decoded by a message passing algorithm (MPA) [6]. Tens of iterations (or decoder processors) are needed for decoding convergence, which incurs high latency and large implementation area.
In this paper, we investigate LDPC-CCs using a layered decoding algorithm (LDA) [7], and show FPGA-based verification of a 20%-overhead LDPC-CC specially designed without error floor down to BER of 10-15. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first report of the LDA application to LDPC-CCs with 50% reduction of number of decoding iterations. By the use of LDA, LDPC-CCs appear as most powerful FEC with extremely reduced complexity for high speed optical transmission systems.

To facilitate understanding the MPA and LDA decoding process of an LDPC-CC, the number of accumulated
layer messages updating operations on each received codeword block wt (corresponds to one vt transmitted through the channel, with the length of c) in the Decoder Unit k (DUk, corresponding to the kth decoding iteration) is illustrated in Fig. 1. When the DUk receives the new block wt from the DUk-1, it simuteneously sends the processed block wt-m-1 received (m+1)-block times earlier to the DUk+1. Every time when one layer VN or CN messages of wt are all updated, the corresponding accumulation counter in the DUk is incremented by one. In the MPA decoding, the new received block wt is processed by one layer CN updating and neither VN updating nor LLR updating is executed, as represented by wt(1,0,0) shown in Fig. 1(a). 3. Results and Discussion

To verify that the LDA can speed up twice the decoding convergence of LDPC-CCs, we studied the two decoding algorithms for the same LDPC-CC. By careful selection of sub-matrices [5], an LDPC-CC(18360,4,24) was constructed with the constraint length equal to the codeword length of LDPC-BC(18360,4,24) presented in [4]. The performance of the LDPC-CC(18360,4,24) was derived for AWGN channel using fixed point computer simulation. In simulation, 4-bit quantization was used and the CN updating operation was replaced by the offset minimum operation with multi-thresholds [4]. In Fig. 2 we plot the BER performance of LDPC-CC(18360,4,24). The LDA achieves almost the same performance as MPA while the maximum number of iterations Imax is halved. The decoder of LDPC-CC is naturally implemented in pipeline architecture. Therefore the LDA can reduce memory and logic resources by half to achieve the same performance as the MPA.
The FPGA emulated BER performance of the LDPC-BC(18360,4,24) are also shown in Fig. 2. One can easily notice that one LDA iteration of LDPC-CC(18360,4,24) has the same number of computational operations as LDPC-BC(18360,4,24) iteration. Since the quantization scheme and the node operations are also exactly the same, the fair comparision between the two counterpart codes can be made. The LDPC-CC has steeper performance curves than its block counterpart. It can be seen that with the same Imax of 12, about 0.3dB higher NCG can be obtained by LDPC-CC(18360,4,24) at a post-FEC BER of 10-15. Similarly, to achieve the same performance as LDPC-BC, the constraint length of LDPC-CC can be shorter, which helps to revolve the routing congestion problem of logic implementation.
An LDPC-CC(10032,4,24) with the constraint length about half of 18360 was constructed. Its performance was evaluated on the FPGA emulation system extended to 8 circuit boards [4]. Due to the shorter constraint length, the routing network of the decoder was easier for implementation. The total throughput of the FPGA emulation easily reached 40Gb/s. Fig. 3 shows the FPGA emulated BER performance of LDPC-CC(10032,4,24). When Imax is set to 12, the code achieves a Q-factor of 5.7dB at a post-FEC BER of 10-15, which corresponds to an NCG of 11.5dB.

4. Summary

For the first time, we applied layered decoding for LDPC-CCs. Using half the number of decoding iterations, the
layered decoding achieves the same performance as the traditional message passing algorithm. As a result, the area consumption and the decoding latency of the LDPC-CC decoder are reduced by half, which relaxes requirements for
the implementation of powerful LDPC-CCs in high speed optical systems using digital signal processing. The LDPC-CC(10032,4,24) was FPGA emulated with NCG of up to 11.5dB and no error floor down to BER of 10-15.
