An Improved LDPC Decoding Scheme with Reverse Bit Order Mode for Distributed Video Codec

Bin Li, Yumei Wang, Yu Liu, Lin Zhang
Key Lab of Universal Wireless Communications, Ministry of Education, Beijing University of Posts and Telecommunications, Beijing, China Email: muziwenwul986@gmail.com

Abstract-In traditional distributed video coding (DYC), considering the correlation between the adjacent bitplanes, the LDPC-based joint bitplane decoding algorithm prefers to exploiting the decoded more significant bitplanes as the prior knowledge to decode the other less significant bitplanes for the better system performance. However, the prior knowledge does not always work well, especially when the quality of the side information (SI) is not so well. Therefore, in this paper we propose a reverse bit order mode for those blocks with poor quality in the SI to improve the LDPC decoding performance. For the pixels in these blocks, their corresponding binary representations will adopt reverse bit order to calculate the likelihood-ratio (LLR) values for LDPC iterative decoding. The experimental results show that compared with the joint bitplane decoding algorithm in [5], the PSNR gain for our proposed decoding scheme can reach up to 2.4 db.
Keywords-reverse bit order mode; LDPC; likelihood-ratio; DVC

I.

INTRODUCTION

In the conventional video coding architecture like H.2641AVC, the video encoder needs to explore complex motion estimation and compensation solutions for better rate­
distortion (RD) perfonnance. As a result, the computing power
of the encoder is more complex than that of the decoder. However, this architecture has being challenged by some emerging applications (e.g. wireless video surveillance, multimedia sensor networks) which need an encoder with significantly lower complexity while pennit a decoder with higher complexity. Distributed video coding (DYC), a new video paradigm, allows for shifting the computing complexity from the encoder to the decoder while maintaining the efficient compression. So it can well fit for these applications. However, there is still a significant compression efficiency gap in practice between the conventional video coding and DVe.

Basically, this gap is due to many specific aspects of DYC, such as the generation of side infonnation (SI) frames [1], the estimation of the correlation noise model (CNM) parameters [2], the utilization of the reconstruction approach [3] and the design of the decoding algorithm [4-5]. Among these aspects, the design of the decoding algorithm plays an important role to improve the overall decoding performance. In the original DYC solutions, such as [6], the authors propose to convert the quantized coefficients of the Wyner-Ziv (WZ) frame into their

corresponding binary representations. Then, the same-position bits of the binary representations for the coefficients are used to construct a serial of bitplanes, and each bitplane will be independently coded with the WZ codec based on binary LDPC code. In [7], the authors propose to exploit a Q-LDPC­ based WZ codec to encode two correlated non-binary sources. Although this codec can fully use the correlation of the sources and achieve better perfonnance, hugely increase the computing complexity of the system. In [4] and [5] the authors reveal that decoding each bitplane independently will neglect the correlation among different bitplanes, and then the system perfonnance will also be affected. Therefore, exploit the decoded bitplanes as the prior knowledge to help decoding the other bitplanes is proposed for the better compression performance. Furthermore, the authors in [8] indicate when the quality of SI is not so well, decoding some bitplanes with intra mode could get better compression performance than the joint decoding algorithm. In addition, in [9] we set different confidence areas to partition the coefficients in the Sl. For the better quality of the blocks in the SI, the corresponding binary representations of the coefficients will set fixed likelihood-ratio (LLR) values as prior infonnation for decoding other bits.
However, these methods do not fully exploit the correlation between the binary representations of the pixels in the blocks with poor quality of the SI to help decoding. In this paper we propose an improved LDPC decoding algorithm with reverse bit order mode for DYC. Considering the movement of the objects in the video sequence, all of blocks in the frame are classified as Reverse bit order Mode (RM), Zero Mode (ZM), and Classical Mode (CM). For the blocks in these three different modes, the corresponding coefficients will have different encoding and decoding methods. Especially for the RM, the corresponding binary representations of the coefficients will be coded from the least significant bit to the most significant bit. In this way, we can offer more accurate prior knowledge to the LDPC decoder and reach better decoding perfonnance.

11. PROPOSED DVC ARCHITECTURE
In this section, we propose a modified DVC architecture for our improved decoding scheme. In this architecture, the main functionalities and the coding process will be described in detail in the following.
Fig. 1 illustrates the basic architecture with our proposed decoding scheme, which works as follows. We adopt a fixed Group of Pictures (GOP=2) in this architecture, which means that the video frames are divided into key (odd) frames and WZ (even) frames. The key frames are intra coded by using a traditional video coding approach like H.2641AVC, while the WZ frames are WZ coded [10].
At the WZ decoder, with the help of the adjacent decoded
key frames (XR and XF ), we adopt the method in [1] to generate the side infonnation Y and the method in [2] to estimate the residual frame R between the encoded WZ frame and the ST. Subsequently, the residual frame R is sent to the "Mode decision" unit for setting three different modes, i.e. ZM, eM and RM, depending on the movements of the objects in the video sequences. In our system, each frame is partitioned into non-overlapping blocks of size 16 by 16 and all these blocks are classified as one of the three modes. If the movement of the objects is complex or nonlinear, such as curving and zooming, the corresponding blocks are classified as the RM; if the objects in the blocks are stationary or the movement of the objects is translational, these blocks pertain to the ZM; otherwise, the other blocks correspond to the eM. All of the pixels belonging to the three different modes, their binary representations will have different bitplane extraction methods at the encoder and the LLR calculation methods at the decoder. Therefore, the block mode information need also be sent to the encoder through the feedback channel. In addition, when the decoder receives the transmitted syndrome bits from the encoder, with the help of the SI and the mode information, the LPDCA decoder will proceed iterative decoding. During this process, if the Hamming distance between the received syndrome bits and

that calculated from the decoded bitplane is not equal to zero, it means that the bitplane can not be decoded successfully. At this time, more syndrome bits from the LDPCA encoder will be requested until there are enough syndrome bits for successful decoding.
2M At the WZ encoder, we adopt the unifonn scalar
quantizer to partition the WZ frames. Then, According to the block mode infonnation from the "Mode decision" unit of the decoder, the quantified coefficients are converted to their binary representations with different methods. If the pixels correspond to the RM, their binary representations will be reversed. If the pixels belong to the ZM, the corresponding binary representations are set to 0; if the pixels pertain to the eM, the binary representations keep unchanged. Then, a series of bitplanes are constructed by the same coefficient location bit of them. These bitplanes are encoded using a rate-compatible Low Density Parity Check accumulate (LDPCA) encoder [11]. For every encoded bitplane, the corresponding syndrome is stored in the LDPCA encoder and will be transmitted depending on the requirements of the decoder through the feedback channel. With this method, we can transmit as fewer syndrome bits as possible while guaranteeing the successful decoding.
