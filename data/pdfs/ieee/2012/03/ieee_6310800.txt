On Threshold Prediction of Low-Density Parity-Check Codes with Structure
Laurent Schmalen, Stephan ten Brink, Gottfried Lechner, and Andreas Leven

Abstract‚ÄĒLow-density parity-check (LDPC) codes based on structured parity check matrices are widely used due to their favorable implementation properties. Often, however, the convergence threshold is optimized based on the general LDPC code ensemble (i.e., the degree profile only) without taking into account the imposed structure, leading to a mismatch of anticipated and actual threshold performance. In this paper, we study this mismatch in more detail and restrict ourselves to the simplest yet most popular constraint: the dual-diagonal structure of the parity-check matrix of a repeat-accumulate code, enabling linear-time encoding. We show that convergence threshold predictions based on density evolution (DE) or EXIT charts need an appropriate choice of component code models. We quantify the threshold prediction error for both BEC and AWGN channels over a wide range of code rates and degree profile optimization techniques. Finally, we provide an explicit example illustrating the achievable gains by using properly designed degree profiles.

I. INTRODUCTION
I N recent years, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have found numerous applications in various communication standards, including, e.g., WLAN, WiMAX, DVB-S2 and many more.
Code design for both new and proprietary communication links is a two-step procedure, which is comprised of 1) finding degree profiles (row/column weights of the paritycheck matrix) to ensure good convergence behavior, as well as 2) defining a suitable graph structure that, e.g., avoids short cycles, and facilitates convenient hardware implementation. We observed that, often (e.g., [1]), degree profiles are optimized for general LDPC code ensembles, thus ignoring the imposed structure, while the final implementation is based on an irregular repeat accumulate (IRA) code, in order to allow for efficient linear-time encoding. This leads to a mismatch of anticipated and actual threshold performance.
IRA codes have the appealing property of being linear-time encodable. They are frequently used in modern communication standards, such as, DVB-S2 and IEEE 802.16 (WiMAX). The parity check matrix H of an IRA code is in the form H =

III. A CLOSER LOOK AT THE THRESHOLD BEHAVIOR
In the following, we seek to quantify how the threshold of the general ensemble and the corresponding IRA ensemble (which is a subset of the general ensemble) differ. We utilize two different tools: density evolution with explicit consideration of the accumulator, and multi-edge-type (MET) density evolution, which covers a broader class of codes.

Note that although the performance of the codes should concentrate around the performance of the general ensemble, systematic IRA codes present a specific instance where this is not the case (note that the fraction of all the codes within the ensemble that are systematic IRA codes is vanishingly small). As the threshold is slightly worse than the ensemble average, this means that there have to be (a family of) codes that have better thresholds than the ensemble average. One example of such a family of codes are terminated LDPC convolutional codes [8], also referred to as spatially coupled LDPC codes [9].

B. Multi-Edge Type Representation
Another way of describing the difference between the general LDPC ensemble and the IRA ensemble is to consider the multi-edge type (MET) representation [10, Sec. 7]. Such a representation is shown in Fig. 3. The left graph corresponds to the ensemble of LDPC codes; edges originating at variable nodes of all degrees are interleaved and every check node connects to these edges.
The right graph corresponds to the IRA ensemble where edges from the systematic part and from the accumulator are fed into two individual interleavers and each check node has exactly two connections to the accumulator. Hence, the IRA ensemble is a subset of the general LDPC ensemble.
While the multi-edge type representation does not explicitly give closed form expressions, it enables us to obtain results for a broader class of channels including the AWGN channel.

V. CONCLUSIONS
We compared codes defined by parity-check matrices of the same degree profiles, but with and without structure, representing general versus IRA code ensembles. While different decoder structures do not have an influence on the bit error rate performance, the threshold predictions based on such decoder models (representing parity-check matrices with/without structure) do have an impact. Special care has to be taken for low-rate codes while the effect is almost not visible for high-rate codes. Taking into account the structure in the underlying model of the degree profile optimization thus permits to find profiles which are better sutied to realize codes with IRA structure allowing for linear-time encoding. In a simulation example, we have confirmed that a properly optimized degree profile permits gains of about 0.05 dB for a rate 1/2 code over an AWGN channel.
