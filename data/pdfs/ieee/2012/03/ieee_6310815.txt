Performance of efficiently-encodable iteratively-decodable block codes

Tingjun Xie, Stephen G. Wilson Charles L. Brown Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering,
University of Virginia, Charlottesville, VA, U.S.A E-mail: {tx4c, sgw}@virginia.edu

Abstract—Principles for designing efficiently-encodable (EE) iteratively-decodable block codes are investigated. We assumed encoding is performed by an erasure-filling decoder and are interested in minimizing encoding time, subject to maintaining good error performance in decoding. We focus on a design parameter, called the recovery vector n, which controls the encoding speed of EE codes. When designing powerful block codes, this vector affords great flexibility in setting the encoding speed. Both analytic and simulation results indicate that faster encoding speed eventually implies a degraded error floor performance, and in practice the tradeoff between encoding speed and error floor performance should be evaluated to meet the actual application. In addition, an extra design rule is proposed for EE low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, and we show that the popular irregular repeat accumulate (IRA) codes and efficientlyencodable rate-compatible (EERC) codes can be obtained by using such rule.
Keywords: Efficient encoding, encoding speed, error floor
I. INTRODUCTION
The discovery of iterative decoding has brought the renaissance of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] in the last two decades. LDPC codes have shown their great potential to approach the Shannon limit on the noisy channel [2], and have been used in many state-of-art communication systems.
LDPC codes use a single parity-check (SPC) code as the check node (CN) constraint and its variable nodes (VN) can be regarded as repetition (REP) nodes. In fact, any linear code C(n, k) can be adopted as either a CN or VN constraint. If a linear code C(n, k) other than a SPC code is used as CN constraint, it is referred to as a generalized LDPC (GLDPC) code, and a doubly-generalized LDPC (DGLDPC) code is obtained if, in addition, some linear C(n, k) code other than repetition (REP) code is used as variable node (VN) constraint. Such C(n, k) codes are usually called component codes in the literature, or also super CN (SCN) or super VN (SVN). The the increased types of the VN’s and CN’s in GLDPC and DGLDPC codes expand the scope of LDPC codes; when properly designed, they can show better performance over LDPC codes [6][7], making them competitive error-control schemes under many scenarios.
Though most of the focus on LDPC codes and variants has been on error performance, encoding is an important issue in practice. The encoding complexity using a bruteforce technique is O(N 2) for ordinary codes, where N is the code length; nonetheless some carefully-designed codes enjoy

linear or sublinear time complexities. For example, repeataccumulate codes and variations have a simple accumulator circuit that computes parity bits in linear time, and quasi-cyclic codes [3] can be linear-time encoded using shift registers. Furthermore, EERC codes [9] have O(log2 N ) encoding time complexity, and here an erasure-filling decoder is employed to encode, an attractive engineering advantage. We assume this encoding-with-decoder procedure as well.
The H matrix of IRA and EERC codes can be written as H = [H1 H2], where the unique structure of the H2 matrix enables the efficient encoding. In [4] and [5], the H2 matrix is generalized to design EE-GLDPC and EE-DGLDPC codes. In this paper, we investigate the effect of what we call the recovery vector n, and show the competing nature of a fast encoding and the error floor. For EE-LDPC codes, an extra design rule is proposed, we show that IRA and EERC codes are special classes of codes generated by applying such rule.
The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. In section II we briefly review the principles for efficiently-encodable, iteratively-decodable block codes (including LDPC, GLDPC and DGLDPC codes), and give the design rule for EE-LDPC codes. The opposing effects of rapid encoding time and poor error floor performance are analyzed in section III. Section IV presents simulation results and discussion, and section V concludes the paper.
II. REVIEW OF EE PRINCIPLES FOR BLOCK CODES
In describing the codes, we employ an adjacency matrix H where H(i, j) = 1 indicates a VN type in column j is connected to a CN type in row i. The adjacency matrix of EE codes described in this paper have the form H = [H1 H2]. We assume H2 an iterative erasure decoder is used to do encoding. During the encoding, the message bits are presented to the iterative decoder and the values of parity bits are sequentially determined until the entire codeword is encoded. In the following, for the sake of completeness we will summarize the principles of EE; for more details readers are referred to [5].
We assume that the adjacency matrix H = [H1 H2] has a dimension Mc × Nv, so Mc (or Nv) represent the number of CN’s (or VN’s) respectively, whether standard SPC (REP) node types, or pertaining to a general component code. We call an edge ‘on the envelope’ iff its corresponding H(i, j) = 1 is an element of the envelope. An edge is ‘below the envelope’ iff the corresponding H(i, j) has the property that i = i0, j > j0, and ‘above the envelope’ iff i = i0, j < j0, where H(i0, j0) is the edge on the envelope. Figuratively speaking, the envelope is a diagonal (or diagonallike) structure dissecting H2 matrix into two disjoint parts: a lower triangular (triangular-like) matrix L and the other an upper triangular (triangular-like) matrix U . The edges in L are called ‘below the envelope’, and ‘above the envelope’ if they are in U .

B. Free and constrained edges of VN
Let us suppose a VN uses C(n, k) as its component code, and it corresponds to one of the columns of H2. Of k edges receiving messages from the channel, (k−1) edges correspond to the information bits and one edge the parity bit. We define free and constrained edges as follows: after (k−1) information bits are chosen, we call an edge e connecting to the CNs a free edge if the message carried by e can be linearly represented by (k − 1) information bits with known values; otherwise it is called a constrained edge. As an example, all n edges of REP(n, 1) codes are constrained edges. It should be noted the number of constrained and free edges depends on the specific choice of (k − 1) information bits.

A. The shape of H2 of EE-LDPC codes
Based on the discussion in the previous section, IRA and EERC codes are also special members in Π. In the following we propose an extra rule on H2 of EE-LDPC codes, which simplifies the H2 construction, and show that IRA and EERC codes are obtained by applying this rule under some specific n. For the sake of simplicity we only consider the case that all parity bits, except the last nD positions, are degree-2 VNs.
Without loss of generality, it is assumed that when constructing the H matrix of EE-LDPC codes, the envelope of H2 is first generated and we proceed to append the second edges of parity bits from left to right. The extra rule is as follows: the second edge is appended to the candidate CN with the smallest number of edges; if ties exist, choose the CN with the smallest number of index. (this guarantees the uniqueness of H2 matrix; if not done by so, consider many possible variations of H2 in EERC case)

B. The shape of H2 of EE-GLDPC and EE-DGLDPC codes
For GLDPC and DGLDPC codes, H2 itself must contain cycles. We usually do not adopt the simple rule as for LDPC codes, for two reasons. First, SCNs have fixed numbers of sockets to connecting edges, so it is preferable to have SCNpreferred PEG [5]; due to the existence of free edges in DGLDPC codes, the simple rule above usually cannot guarantee the best cycle property. In practice, the H2 matrix of EEGLDPC and EE-DGLDPC codes have more irregular structure compared with EE-LDPC codes. In Fig. 1, the blowup of the lower right corner of the H2 matrix of an EE-GLDPC code using n = [..., 16, 8, 8, 8, 4, 4, 4] is shown: the red dots correspond to ‘1’s in H2 matrix. It can be observed that the distribution of red dots is fairly irregular. Also note that n imposes some ‘triangular forbidden zones’ below the envelope and no ‘1’s are allowed to appear in the zones, with the dimension of the triangle determined by the elements of n. It is guaranteed by such structure that the encoding will finish in at most D iterations, where D is the length of n.

C. Tradeoff between the encoding speed and the error floor performance

Compared with ordinary iteratively-decodable codes, the

recovery vector n imposes added constraints on the available

positions where ‘1’s can be placed. There are two major

effects: degree-1 VNs and a dispersive CN distribution. The

number of degree-1 VNs is nD, given n = [n1, n2, ...nD],

whereas the dispersive CN distribution is produced by a

fast-diminishing sequence n (e.g. n for EERC codes) [10].

The design of iteratively-decodable codes with EE property are presented and examined in this paper. The encoding speed of EE codes is controlled by a recovery vector n, and the flexibility of encoding time of EE codes can be easily realized by modifying the length of n. The tradeoff between the encoding speed and the error floor performance is an interesting and important issue in practical applications: the encoding speed can be maximized given the required error rate under a certain SNR, or the error floor performance can be optimized once the encoding speed fixed. Finally, EE codes are systematic codes, therefore they are suitable for rate-compatible data transmission scenarios; in addition, for EE code, a preliminary categorization of k-SR nodes is immediately obtained once n given, which could possibly greatly facilitates the design of the puncturing schemes.
