Counting Short Cycles of Quasi Cyclic Protograph LDPC Codes
Mehdi Karimi, Student Member, IEEE, and Amir H. Banihashemi, Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—An efficient method for counting short cycles in the Tanner graphs of quasi cyclic (QC) protograph low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes is presented. The method is based on the relationship between the number of short cycles in the graph and the eigenvalues of the directed edge matrix of the graph. We demonstrate that for a QC protograph LDPC code, the complexity of computing such eigenvalues can be reduced significantly by representing the directed edge matrix as a block circulant matrix. Numerical results are presented to show the lower complexity of the proposed method compared to the existing algorithms for counting short cycles. These results also reveal that QC LDPC codes on average have a superior short cycle and girth distribution compared to similar randomly constructed codes.
Index Terms—Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, quasi cyclic (QC) LDPC codes, protograph LDPC codes, girth, short cycles, short cycle distribution, girth distribution, counting short cycles.
I. INTRODUCTION
A MONG the different constructions of low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes, quasi cyclic (QC) protograph codes are among the most popular, and appear in a number of standards [9], [10]. This is mainly because the encoder and the decoder for these codes are easier to implement. The performance of iterative algorithms, used to decode LDPC codes, in general, and QC LDPC codes, in particular, closely relates to the short cycle distribution of the code’s Tanner graph, with the general belief that for good performance, short cycles should be avoided in the graph. The relationship between the short cycle distribution and the code’s performance has been established, e.g., in [13] and [7]; has been used to design codes, e.g., in [8] and [2], and to analyze the performance of coding schemes, e.g., in [20]. It is thus important to search for efficient algorithms that can count short cycles of different length in the code’s Tanner graph.
Counting the number of cycles in a general graph is known to be a hard problem [6]. More efficient algorithms have been devised for counting short cycles in general graphs [1], [11], and in Tanner graphs [4], [3], [7]. Among these algorithms, the most competitive, outperforming even [7], is that of [11], which is capable of counting short cycles of length g, g + 1, . . . , 2g − 1 in a general graph, and short cycles of length g, g + 2, . . . , 2g − 2 in a Tanner graph, where g is the girth of the graph. The complexity of the method of [11] is O(g|E|2), where |E| is the number of edges in the graph.

In this paper, our focus is on the short cycle distribution of the Tanner graphs of QC protograph LDPC codes. Using the structure of the codes, we first propose an efficient algorithm (less complex than that of [11]) for counting the short cycles of the corresponding Tanner graphs. We then use the proposed algorithm to investigate the short cycle distribution of a few ensembles of QC protograph LDPC codes and demonstrate that, on average, they have a superior short cycle and girth distribution compared to similar randomly constructed codes.

Consider a binary LDPC code C of length n with an m × n parity check matrix H. Corresponding to H, we have the Tanner graph G(V, E) of C, where G is a bipartite graph, with the set V partitioned into two disjoint subsets U and W such that every edge in E connects a node from U to a node from W . Sets U and W are referred to as variable nodes and check nodes, and represent the code bits and parity check equations of C, respectively. Clearly, |U | = n and |W | = m.

IV. NUMERICAL RESULTS
The following results are obtained using MATLAB on a PC with 2.2 GHz CPU and 8GB RAM.
Example 1: In this example, we consider the Tanner (155, 64) code [15]. For this protograph code, the base graph is a fully connected 3 × 5 graph, and the lifting degree is N = 31. The code has a girth 8 and thus the proposed algorithm can count the number of short cycles up to the length 2g − 2 = 14 : N8 = 465, N10 = 3720, N12 = 22630 and N14 = 156240. It took less then 0.5 second for the proposed method to count these cycles.
Example 2: In this example, we still use the 3 × 5 fully connected graph as the base graph and lift it with degrees N = 400, 1000 and 2000, by selecting the cyclic permutations randomly. Table I shows the number of short cycles of the constructed graphs. The results demonstrate that the number of shortest cycles reduces by increasing the lifting degree. For the three graphs, the proposed method counted the cycles in 1.1, 6 and 18 seconds, respectively. This is while the algorithm of [11] took 3.5, 20 and 119 seconds, respectively, to perform the same task. It is also worth noting that the algorithm of [7] took 126 and 1847 seconds for the first two codes, respectively, and ran out of memory and was unable to find the results for the third code. In the last column of Table I, we have given the short cycle distribution of a randomly constructed (3, 5) regular Tanner graph in which 4-cycles are avoided. As can be seen, the short cycle distribution of this code is significantly inferior to that of the similar randomly constructed QC code (N = 2000). In particular, the former graph has g = 6 while the girth of the latter is 12.
Example 3: In this example, we consider the (irregular) LDPC codes adopted in IEEE 802.11 standard [9]. There are 12 codes in the standard with rates R = 1/2, 2/3, 3/4 and 5/6.
