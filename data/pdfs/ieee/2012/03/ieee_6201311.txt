Rate-Compatible QC-LDPC Codes Design in Powerline Communication Systems
Zaishuang Liu, Kewu Peng, Weilong Lei, Chen Qian, and Zhaocheng Wang Department of Electronic Engineering, Tsinghua University, Beijing 100084, P. R. China Tsinghua National Laboratory for Information Science and Technology (TNList), Beijing 100084, P. R. China
Email: liuzs09@mails.tsinghua.edu.cn

Abstract—Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are an important choice of the error correction codes in broadband powerline communication (PLC) systems. In this paper, a "column extension" design method for rate-compatible quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes is proposed, which can provide ef¿cient multi-rate QC-LDPC schemes for PLC systems. Based on the optimization of degree distributions among multi-rate LDPC codes, excellent performance can be achieved at each code rate. Bene¿ting from the nested matrix structure, the designed QC-LDPC codes facilitate the implementation of multi-rate encoder/decoder and offer the advantage of low-complexity. A comparison with the QC-LDPC codes in ITU-T G.9960 speci¿cation is performed, where the effectiveness of the proposed method and the superior performance of our designed codes are validated via extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) chart analysis and bit error rate (BER) simulations.
Keywords—Rate-compatible, QC-LDPC, PLC, multi-rate, degree distribution, EXIT chart.
I. INTRODUCTION
Powerline communication (PLC), as an emerging and promising technology for data transmission in smart grid, internet access, in-home networking and many other applications, has gained a lot of attention and investigations in recent years. Since the existing powerline network is also capable of supporting high data rate transmission, the advantage that there is no need for new infrastructure makes PLC a wellknown cost effective choice. The current PLC systems are divided into narrowband and broadband, wherein the latter usually can provide the bandwidth up to 30 MHz or even wider. The G.hn standard developed by ITU Telecommunication Standardization Sector (ITU-T) is a representative of broadband PLC standards [1].
Since the powerline cables were originally designed for a far different purpose than the desired signal transmission, the PLC channel unfortunately performs as a harsh and complex environment suffused with variable noises. Thus, a robust and powerful forward error correction (FEC) scheme is essential for a PLC system. Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, first proposed by Gallager [2] and rediscovered by Mackay and Neal [3], [4], are linear block codes which can achieve near-capacity performance. Besides their popularity in wireless communication and broadcasting fields, LDPC codes can also be used in PLC systems. A lot of previous work has been done to investigate the LDPC applications in PLC, including the evaluation of their performance, the comparison

with other commonly used error correction codes such as the concatenation of Reed-Solomon (RS) and convolutional codes, the discussion of decoding algorithms suited for PLC scenarios, and the introduction of the combined FEC schemes consisting of LDPC and other codes [5]–[10]. In addition, ITU-T Recommendation G.9960 [11], as the physical layer specification of G.hn standard, has employed a set of multirate quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes as its FEC scheme.
As an important family of LDPC codes, QC-LDPC codes can provide excellent error-correcting performance with relatively simple structures [12], [13]. Benefiting from the regular array structure of their parity-check matrices, QC-LDPC codes can not only be encoded efficiently via simple circuits such as shift-register-adder-accumulators (SRAA) [14], [15], but also be decoded delicately with semi-parallel decoding architectures [16], [17], which significantly decrease the implementation complexity of the encoder/decoder. Therefore, QC-LDPC codes have been employed by several communication/broadcasting standards such as IEEE 802.16e and Digital Television Terrestrial Multimedia Broadcasting (DTMB), besides ITU-T G.9960.
Nowadays, multi-rate is a necessary requirement for most integrated FEC schemes, to provide flexible spectrum efficiency in the physical layer. Considering the implementation issues of the multi-rate LDPC encoder and decoder, ratecompatible scheme is a quite efficient hardware-friendly solution, which allows a single "mother" encoder and decoder to be good across a wide range of rates. There have been many ratecompatible LDPC code design schemes [18]–[20], which have promoted the research on how to ensure good performance at each code rate with the structure constraints. The most common rate-compatible technique is puncturing [18], where the transmitter punctures specific check bits in a codeword according to well-designed puncturing patterns. Other representative schemes include the "row hiding" [19] and the "matrix extension" [20], which have their own particular nested matrix structures but both with different check bit number of each rate.
In this paper, a new design method for rate-compatible QCLDPC codes is proposed. With the optimization of degree distributions, the multi-rate QC-LDPC codes restricted by a nested matrix structure can achieve excellent performance at each code rate, wherein extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) chart [21]–[23] is adopted as a powerful analysis tool. H shown as (1), which is composed by an array of circulant

sub-matrices. Each sub-matrix Ai,j, with a uniform size of b × b, could be either a zero matrix or a nonzero circulant matrix.

In most QC-LDPC cases, the nonzero circulant sub-matrices

B. EXIT Chart and QC-LDPC Code Design
EXIT chart, first proposed by ten Brink [21], is a powerful and visualized tool to analyze the convergence of iterative systems with serially concatenated inner and outer decoders. The inner decoder’s EXIT curve Ci and the outer decoder’s inverted EXIT curve Co-1 make up of a typical EXIT chart. The performance analysis of an iterative decoding system usually relies on the area property [22] of the EXIT chart, i.e., successful decoding is predicted by a tunnel between Ci and Co-1, whereas the area between the two curves represents the capacity loss. It is recognized that EXIT curve-matching is an essential way to design a good iterative decoding system.
The observation in [23] demonstrates that the LDPC decoding operation can be characterized by an EXIT chart. For an irregular LDPC code, the EXIT curve Ci of the inner variable node decoder (VND) is a weighted summation of different repetition decoders’ curves, according to the degree distributions of the variable nodes. Similarly, the inverted EXIT curve Co-1 of the outer check node decoder (CND) is a weighted summation of different parity-check decoders’ inverted curves, according to the degree distributions of the check nodes. Thus, as the first step of the LDPC code design in this paper, we can design the degree distributions of an irregular LDPC code and predict its performance under the given degree distributions via EXIT chart analysis. By decreasing the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) as low as possible while still keeping the EXIT tunnel open, i.e., keeping the two curves not intersecting until (1,1), we can find an SNR threshold of successful decoding, which represents the theoretical SNR threshold of the LDPC code under the given degree distributions.
Once the degree distributions are determined, the progressive edge-growth (PEG) algorithm [24]–[26] can be adopted to construct the T matrix by progressively adding edges. In particular, [26] proposed a design method of QC-LDPC codes based on the PEG algorithm, and validated that such designed QC-LDPC codes can achieve as good performance as random LDPC codes. The PEG algorithm usually has two kinds of constraints, i.e., on minimum girth and on degree distributions. Since the short girth can be eliminated via the design of shifting values, the constraint on degree distributions is often set as higher priority when constructing a QC-LDPC code.
The final step is to design the shifting values of the rotated identity sub-matrices. Random construction method [13] can avoid the short girth somehow, while it is not very satisfying in some cases. Another method, which has better availability and more robust performance, is suited to construct the shifting values with the expansion factor b = pm − 1 or pm, wherein p is a prime number [27], [28]. When the shifting values are finalized, the QC-LDPC code design is completed.

C. Multi-Rate QC-LDPC Scheme in ITU-T G.9960
The QC-LDPC scheme in ITU-T G.9960 [11] is a typical paradigm of the LDPC codes applied in broadband PLC systems. It supports mother codes with rates 1/2, 2/3 and 5/6, in both short and long families. From the mother codes, codes with higher rates such as 8/9 and 20/21 are obtained through puncturing. In this paper, we tentatively care about the three long mother codes, whose parameters are listed in Table I.
It is observed that these multi-rate QC-LDPC codes, with the same value of K and t, provide different codeword length through different b. Since their T matrices are not within a nested structure, but designed independently at each rate, the three mother codes are not naturally rate-compatible.
Besides, in this scheme, the T matrices have relatively few rows and each element 1 of T represents a rotated identity submatrix, which makes the choices of the variable node degrees fewer, and the optimization of degree distributions is restricted accordingly. For example, the variable node degrees can almost only be chosen as (2, 3, 4) for a 4 × 24 T matrix of this kind.
The EXIT chart of the mother code with R = 2/3 is depicted in Fig. 1 to predict the SNR threshold of successful decoding under the given degree distributions, while the EXIT charts of the other two codes are omitted. As shown in the figure, the predicted Eb/N0 threshold of the code with R = 2/3 is about 1.61 dB. The narrow but open EXIT tunnel is shown in the enlarged figure. III. PROPOSED RATE-COMPATIBLE QC-LDPC DESIGN
A. Proposed Rate-Compatible Method
In this section, a new rate-compatible QC-LDPC design method is developed. First of all, we predetermine the parameters of the multi-rate codes such as the sizes of T matrices and the expansion factor b, according to the specific scenarios and the required code rates. The T matrices of all the rates are expected to follow a nested matrix structure, which is quite different from other rate-compatible schemes. The parameter c, i.e., the row number of T, is constant, while the parameter t, i.e., the column number of T, varies with different rates. The T matrix of the lowest rate is the shortest, and the higher the rate is, the longer the T becomes.
Once the basic parameters are determined, the degrees and their distributions of each rate need to be fixed. The choices of the check node degrees are relatively simple. Since they have less impact on the code performance, they could be chosen approaching the average of the whole nodes. However, the variable node degrees and their distributions impact the performance significantly. So we carefully choose the variable node degrees and design the near-optimal distributions via EXIT chart analysis.
This "column extension" design method is simply shown in Fig. 2. A set of good degrees and their distributions of the lowest rate are firstly searched out, and then the corresponding Hc matrix is constructed. From the first matrix, new columns are progressively added to the matrix, to obtain the extended matrices of higher rates, and the longest mother Hc matrix of the highest rate is finally constructed. The design procedure of the new columns follows the PEG algorithm [26], while the shifting values within the new columns must be designed carefully to avoid the short girth [27], [28].
Benefiting from the constant row number of the multi-rate T matrices, the variable node degrees of the longer matrix always contain those of the shorter, which is good for the optimization of degree distributions around all the rates. V. CONCLUSION
This paper proposes a new method for the rate-compatible QC-LDPC codes design, where a nested T matrix structure with column extension is carefully constructed and the optimization of degree distributions is carried out through EXIT chart analysis. The proposed method can provide lowcomplexity implementation of multi-rate encoder/decoder as well as excellent performance of each code rate. In order to get a superior FEC scheme for broadband PLC systems, a set of multi-rate QC-LDPC codes is designed by using the method.
