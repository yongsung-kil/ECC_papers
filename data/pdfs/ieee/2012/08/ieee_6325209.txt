Abstract‚ÄĒWireless and PLC standards have to cope with noisy channels. The advanced FEC codes like LDPC and turbo codes are the most competitive ones. Multi-standards devices (such as mobile phones with cellular and WiFi) are becoming more and more attractive but raise the issue of duplicating hardware. Trellis decoding is a good solution to share material and to achieve good throughputs on FEC multi-standard decoders. Such a technique added with natural parallelism underlines access memory conflicts on the LDPC decoding process, which decreases the decoding performance. This paper presents an optimized trellis design to avoid these access conflicts with no loss in decoding performance.
Index Terms‚ÄĒArchitecture, FEC decoding, hardware implementation, iterative decoding, LDPC codes, turbo codes, parallelism, trellis decoding.
I. INTRODUCTION
The discovery of turbo codes by C. Berrou [1] in 1993 has promoted the research of codes able to reach the Shannon limit. Turbo codes have been improved to increase throughputs and to decrease the error floor. Nowadays, such solutions are selected in standards: 8-state binary turbo codes (bTC) in UMTS and Advanced-LTE [2] or 8-state double-binary turbo codes (dbTC) in DVB-RCS and the PLC standard HomePlugAV [3]. In the meantime, Mackay has rediscovered the Low-Density Parity -Check (LDPC) codes which are the best concurrent for TC. Quasi-Cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes are standardized in IEEE802.11n [4] as well as IEEE802.16m [5]. This family is especially interesting from the viewpoint of implementation. Both codes show great performance and low decoding complexity.
Current devices communicate more and more through several media, and so, have to deal with several Forward Error Correction (FEC) strategies. For a constructor, the easier way to meet specifications in FEC decoding is to include several specific decoding chipsets. Several teams are studying methods to save chipset area by mutualizing functions. The Radix-4 [6] method is particularly interesting to implement a bTC/dbTC dual mode chipset with high throughputs (appx. 100 Mbps at 140 MHz). Other teams [7] [8] are working on an ASIP chipset able to decode LDPC, bTC and dbTC but requiring to

be reconfigured between different usages. [9] proposes a turbodecoding architecture to merge a LDPC and a bTC decoder.
Our interest here is focused on a generic decoder, able to decode both the LDPC codes from WiFi and the bTCs from LTE, or both the LDPC codes from WiFi and the dbTCs from HPAV. These architectures should be optimized in terms of complexity, but should also be competitive compared with a single-standard decoder solution. Contrary to TCs, LDPC decoding updates variables several times during an iteration of the process. The LDPC decoding structure needs this result with a delay called the reuse delay in the following. The purpose of this paper is to define a scheduling which improves this reuse delay and so avoids memory access conflicts, with no impact on the performance.
This paper is structured as follows. Section II will describe the generic decoding architecture trellis. Section III will focus on the way to optimize the LDPC scheduling without any loss of performance. Section IV will optimize the scheduling applied to a WiFi LDPC matrix and will describe the impact in performance. Section V will conclude the paper.

B. TC/LDPC common trellis
A hardware generic decoder shall decode both LDPC and TC codes on the fly. Reconfiguration is not possible because it requires too much time (a few microseconds). This kind of architecture shall switch from one application to another without reconfiguration, and so, the routing of the decoder shall be optimized according to the codes. Here is proposed an architecture according to the 802.11n matrix given by Fig.2 (N = 648, R = 1/2) and LTE 8-state bTC.
The Convolutional Code (CC) associated with the LTE bTC is a Markov chain with 8 states. Each section uses variable ci ‚ąą {0, 1}. From each state only 2 paths are possible in accordance with the possibility of ci. A trellis section gives exactly 16 paths.

IV. A WIFI/LTE USE CASE
The architecture defined in Section II allows a few number of parity equations to be decoded in parallel. In a context of WiFi/3G convergence (LDPC and bTC trellis structure), the trellis core can decode four equations in parallel. IEEE802.11n standard defines 12 different QC-LDPC matrices with an expanding factor z = {27, 54, 81} and parity degrees from 7 to 22. Each matrix row of an LDPC matrix is an independent subgroup. The following part focuses on optimizing the scheduling of the first Wifi LDPC matrix illustrated in Fig.2 for a codelength of 648 bits and a rate 1/2.
The LDPC code is made up of 12 successive independent subgroups with dcL = 7 or dcL = 8. This means that an iterative decoding step with the generic decoder will decode a generic trellis composed by 12 subgroups. Update messages will be transferred from one place to an other during each iteration. No update message is reused during the processing of one subgroup, but it is reused between subgroups. Border effects shows that the end to end trellis will reuse some variables with delay m‚Ą¶ = 5. It means that the micro-trellis processing delay shall be ‚ąÜ ‚Č§ 5. This delay is not possible to reach due to the micro-trellis length, and so the scheduling of this matrix has to be optimized to avoid latency and memory conflicts.
The shift permutation order defined by (13) is favored, because it has no impact on the message address calculation complexity and it guarantees no performance loss.
Between subgroups L1 and L2, there are four common variable groups, named V1, V5, V9 and V14. With the trellis configuration defined in section II, two variables (c24 and c25) are distant by m‚Ą¶ = 7 trellis sections. The optimal repartition of the delay is obtained when u = 15. In this case, the minimal trellis distance is mS‚Ą¶ = 21 and is reached by three variables (c367, c368 and c369). The scheduling of the subgroup L2 is replaced by its optimal permutation. This paper has put in light a question about the data reusing access due to the topography of LDPC codes. Small rescheduling shows no impact on the performance, but largely reduces the latency between micro-trellis decoding and avoids memory access conflicts. By shifting lines with a circular permutation in independent QC-LDPC subgroups, the new scheduling has no impact on the memory address computation, but still improves the decoder efficiency.
