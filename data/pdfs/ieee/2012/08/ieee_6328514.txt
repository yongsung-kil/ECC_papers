Abstract—A hybrid decoder (HD) for LDPC codes in the
presence of additive white Gaussian noise is proposed. This HD
employs a min-sum belief propagation (BP) decoding to correct
errors, combined with erasure iterative decoding. Whenever
BP decoding fails, its output is modified and artificially
created erasures are introduced. The proposed HD employs
an overall reduced number of iterations and achieves a similar
performance to that of traditional BP decoding schemes, espe-
cially in the error-floor region, with equivalent implementation
complexity. Results of HD performance obtained by computer
simulation are presented.
Index Terms—LDPC codes, BP iterative decoding, erasures
decoding, hybrid decoding.
I. INTRODUCTION
In this article we investigate a hybrid decoder (HD) for
LDPC (low density parity-check) [1] codes in the presence
of additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN), with the goal
of reducing the overall number of iterations while keeping
a performance equivalent to that attained by a sum-product
iterative decoder [2].
The proposed HD operates in two stages, the first of
which employs an iterative min-sum belief propagation (BP)
decoder to handle binary codewords affected by AWGN, and
the second stage is used when the BP decoder fails. The
second decoder stage employs an erasure iterative decoder
which operates over the output of an artificially made binary
erasure channel (BEC), and interacts with the first stage.
The idea of applying iterative erasure decoding after the BP
decoding is related to ordered statistic decoding (OSD), and
the reader is referred to [3], [4] for details on OSD. In the
sequel we shall refer to HD cycles, instead of HD iterations,
in order to avoid confusion with the use of the word iteration
as, for example, for BP iterations.
One cycle of the HD operation includes at least one
passage through the BP decoder and if necessary it also
includes a passage through the erasure decoder. The effi-
ciency of this approach is demonstrated through computer
simulations, where the HD system performance is compared
with that where just a BP decoder is employed. Iterative
decoding schemes available in the literature consider er-
rors, either random or in bursts, and employ soft-decision
decoding associated with hard-decision decoding [5]. The
trick of correcting erasures after a decoding failure with
a BP decoder has received little or virtually no attention,
either in AWGN channels or other channel models. The
remaining parts of this article are organized as follows.
In Section II we describe the proposed HD system. In
Section III we present the reasoning behind the idea of an
artificially created BEC, to handle errors remaining after an
unsuccessful BP decoding. In Section IV we describe our
approach to determine an adequate value for the number
of artificially created erasures, and the article is closed in
Section V with the presentation of the computer simulation
results and some comments.
II. HYBRID DECODING SYSTEM
We consider a binary LDPC code 𝒞for a communication
system as follows. At the transmitter a binary information k-
tuple u = (u1,u2,...,uK) feeds the encoder for 𝒞to produce
the codeword c = (c1,c2,...,cN). Each codeword is then
modulated before being sent through the channel using,
for example, binary phase-shift keying (BPSK) modulation,
thus producing the N-tuple x = (x1,x2,...,xN), where xi,
1 ≤i ≤N, is a real number. The N-tuple x is sent through an
additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel the output
of which is denoted by
y, expressed as y = x + n, where
n = (n1,n2,...,nN) denotes a vector whose coordinates are
AWGN samples introduced by the channel, with zero mean
and variance σ2. Decoding consists of processing y to
recover u.
On receiving the N-tuple y the decoder converts y into
a vector 𝝃= (ξ1,ξ2,...,ξN) whose coordinates are values
of a quantized logarithmic likelihood ratio (QLLR) [6]. Let
IHD denote the number of cycles used for executing the HD
algorithm.
A number IBP of iterations is specified for the first
decoding stage which employs a min-sum BP decoder. The
choice of a value for IBP must be such that if IBP iterations
have been performed and the decoding was not successful
then it is likely that the BP decoder has reached a trapping
set [7]. Once a trapping set has been reached it is not
possible, by performing more iterations with the BP decoder,
to get out of it and successfully decode the received word.
After each iteration the BP decoder generates an N-

Whenever there is a decoding failure on the first decoding
stage then the second decoding stage is employed to process
the output of an artificially created BEC. The input to the
BEC is the binary sequence ˆc produced by the BP decoder
at the end of the IBP-th iteration. Our motivation to employ
an iterative erasure decoding stage is the following. For
a given block code with minimum Hamming distance d,
it is well known that any erasure pattern containing up
to d −1 erasures [8] can be corrected, as well as a large
fraction of patterns containing d or more erasures, as long
as the number of erasures in a codeword does not exceed
the number of parity-check digits and these erasures do not
cover a codeword [9].

An erasure decoding iteration consists in examining each
parity-check equation counting the respective number of
erasures. If a parity-check equation contains a single erasure
among the positions checked then the decoder can correct it.
A parity-check equation is ignored by the decoder whenever
it contains no erasure or at least two erasures. Whenever
one or more erasures are corrected then the erasure iterative
decoding procedure is repeated. Decoding is interrupted
whenever either all erasures have been corrected or else all
parity-check equations are tested and not a single erasure is
corrected [10].
In case no erasure has been corrected after the first itera-
tion the HD operation is stopped, and a decoding failure is
declared. However, if at least one erasure has been corrected,
when the erasure decoding operation is finished the N-

III. THE ARTIFICIAL BEC
A critical aspect for the success of the HD rule introduced
concerns the choice of the cardinality of 𝕊𝒳, i.e., the rule
for introducing erasures on the N-tuple ˆc produced by the
BP algorithm, after reaching IBP iterations without success.
The output symbols of a BEC, i.e., 0, 1 and Δ, are either
correct (0’s and 1’s) or unknown (Δ’s). In the case of our
artificially introduced BEC, the output symbols are 0’s, 1’s
and Δ’s, however the rule to produce them must be such that
the reliability of 0’s and 1’s are as large as possible and the
number 𝒳of erasures be as large as possible, as long as it
does not clutter erasure correction by the erasure decoder.
Obviously, what we desire with this artificial BEC is that
the set 𝕊𝒳should contain all positions responsible for the
failure of the BP decoder. This condition assumes that the
0’s and 1’s at the BEC output are correct. In the iterative
BP decoding process, as more iterations are executed, in
average more and more QLLR values of the reliable digits
in the received N-tuple move away from the zero value [11].
However, we observe that in a AWGN channel, for example,
the presence of cycles in an LDPC code Tanner graph [12]
causes some positions in error to take relatively high QLLR
values.
IV. THE CHOICE OF A VALUE FOR 𝒳
As we already mentioned, the parameter 𝒳represents the
number of bits to be erased at the end of an unsuccessful
BP decoding, using the criteria of erasing those digits with
lowest QLLR absolute values. An adequate determination
of 𝒳is essential for obtaining a satisfactory overall perfor-
mance since, in the erasure iterative decoding process, as
the number of introduced erasures increases, it is less and
less likely to find parity-check equations containing a single
erased bit which thus could be solved. The set of erased
bits which can not be decoded with the code parity-check
equations is called a stopping set [13].

by increasing 𝒳, there is a corresponding increase in the

by a decrease in the correction of erasures due primarily to
the presence of stopping sets.
