New Quasi-Cyclic LDPC Codes with Girth at Least Eight based on Sidon Sequences

Guohua Zhang†*, Rong Sun†, Xinmei Wang†
† State Key Lab. of Integrate Service Networks, Xidian University, Xi’an, P.R.China * China Academy of Space Technology (Xi’an), Xi’an, P.R.China
Email: zhangghcast@163.com, rsun@mail.xidian.edu.cn, xmwang@xidian.edu.cn

Abstract—A new family of quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes of girth at least eight is explicitly constructed from Sidon sequences. These codes can be encoded using simple shift-registers. Furthermore, for short to moderate lengths these quasi-cyclic codes perform close to random progressive edge-growth (PEG) LDPC codes in terms of bit-error probability and block-error probability, collectively.
Key words-channel coding; LDPC codes; girth; construction; Sidon sequence.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have been one of the major research topics for many coding theorists for the last decade. In an effort toward the algebraic constructions of LDPC codes, quasi-cyclic (QC-) LDPC codes are getting more attention due to their linear-time encodability, small size of required memory and good performance [1-12]. Since Tanner derives a lower bound on the minimal distance which increases exponentially with the girth of the code, Tanner graphs with large girth are particularly desirable. A necessary and sufficient condition for a QC-LDPC code to have large girth was presented by M.P.C.Fossorier [1]. Recently, an alternative condition was proposed by Xiaofu Wu [7]. So far, most of the methods for constructing QC-LDPC codes of girth larger than six are based on extensive computer search [1,5,7,8], and most of the explicit constructions of QCLDPC codes have been focused only on girth of six [3,6,9]. A natural question is whether it is possible to connect the necessary and sufficient condition to some well-developed mathematical problems, such that the QCLDPC codes of girth at least eight (or more) can be constructed explicitly. The only construction along this line, to the best of our knowledge, is due to B.Vasic who introduced a family of QC-LDPC codes of girth eight using arithmetically constrained sequences [2]. In this paper, we provide a new explicit construction of QCLDPC codes with girth at least eight by Sidon sequences. Simulation results show that for short to moderate code lengths our new QC-LDPC codes can perform nearly as well as the random codes generated by the celebrated progressive edge-growth (PEG) algorithm. Since the new codes are quasi-cyclic, they even have an advantage over the PEG-LDPC codes in the sense that the former can be encoded using simple shift-registers while the latter cannot.

We close this section by pointing out the following remarks.

(A) To construct codes of higher rate, larger values of m are needed. For example, to construct a rate-0.9 ((L3)/L, L=30) code, block length of at least 27000 (L3) is required. This imposes restrictions on the achievable rates at a given block length. Using the method of arithmetically constrained sequences presented by Vasic [2], a girth-8 (3,30)-regular QC-LDPC code with length 7110 (the size of CPM is 237) can be constructed based on the earliest sequence {0,1,3,4,…,112,117,118}. The length of this code is about 3.80 times shorter than the block length of 27000. On the other hand, Lu [15] derives a conclusion which states that if the size of CPM is equal to or larger than (J-1)(L-1)(g/2-1)/(JL-J-L), a (J,L)-regular QC-LDPC code can be necessarily constructed with girth g. By letting g=8, J=3 and L=30, it is readily seen that the shortest block length corresponding to this lower bound is about 3.80 times larger than the block length of 27000.

(B) In general, the proposed construction cannot be generalized to other (four and above) column weights by splitting a Sidon sequence into multiple sub-sequences that pair-wisely satisfy the Condition (1) or (2). We give two counterexamples below.

Let m=84. {0,1,3,7,12,20,30,51} is a Sidon sequence

Let m=168. {0,1,13,19,76,79,87,114,116,123,138,
148,164} is a Sidon sequence over Z168. The exponent matrix E2 corresponds to a Tanner graph with girth only six.

Therefore, the scope of the method presented in the paper is currently limited to codes with column-weight three.

(C) In Section 8 of Chapter 12 of Ryan/Lin's book [12], two algebraic constructions of QC-LDPC codes with girth 8 are presented based on the superposition code-construction method (also called generalized product code). The first construction gives girth-8 (3,L)regular QC-LDPC codes with block length of L2(q-1), where q is a prime power. The second construction generate girth-8 (J,L)-regular LDPC codes with block length of LJ and rate of ((L-1)/L)J .
In addition, there are several proposed methods for constructing algebraic QC-LDPC codes with girth at least six using finite fields, finite geometries and experimental designs. These methods are well documented in the book by Ryan and Lin [12]. Most of the codes constructed by these methods are closely related to GF(q). Therefore, the block lengths of the resulting codes are largely constrained by the integer q, which must be a prime power.
For the Sidon-LDPC codes, their lengths are of the form Lm, where m is not necessarily a prime power. Therefore, the Sidon-LDPC codes are more flexible in the selection of block length.
V. SIMULATION RESULTS
A. Comparison with progressive edge-growth (PEG) codes
As illustrative examples, two Sidon-LDPC codes of designed rate 1/2 are constructed based on Cond.(1) and Cond.(2), respectively. The well-known rate-1/2 (504, 252) and (1008,504) progressive edge-growth (PEG) LDPC codes [16] are used as the counterparts of the two new codes. For comparison purposes, m is set to 84 and 168 respectively. Greedy algorithm gives G(84)={0,1,3,7,12,20,30,51} for m=84 and G(168)={0,6,7,9,17,21,37,50,55,77,102} for m=168.The detailed parameters of the two Sidon-LDPC codes are listed in Table 2. The belief propagation algorithm is used to decode these codes and the maximal number of iterations is set to be 80. BPSK modulation is assumed over an additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel. At least 100 block errors are collected per simulation point. Fig.2 shows that the performance difference between the two Sidon-LDPC codes and their corresponding PEG counterparts is minor. The significance of the nearly identical performance should not be underestimated, considering that random PEGLDPC codes are among the best codes for short to moderate block lengths, and that Sidon-LDPC codes can be encoded using simple shift-registers owing to their quasi-cyclic structure.

Before closing this section, we point out the following remarks. (1) Although Margulis construction [17] gives a girth-8 LDPC code but with poor performance, generally speaking, there is high probability that LDPC codes with large girth imply good codes [2,5,7,11,15,16]. (2) Compared with randomly generated girth-8 QC-LDPC codes, explicitly constructed girth-8 QC-LDPC codes can be well used as component codes in other general construction skills for large-girth QC-LDPC codes, for example, in the Chinese Remainder Theorem (CRT) method [4,10,11]. (3) While the new construction method bears some similarities to the construction proposed in Section 2 of Chapter 11 of Ryan/Lin's book [12], there are three major differences between them. Firstly, the size of CPM is an arbitrary integer m for the new method, while the latter needs a specific type of integer q-1, where q is a prime power. Secondly, the a-multiplied row constraints 1 proposed in the latter method are not needed in our new construction. Finally, our new construction guarantees a girth at least eight, while the latter guarantees a girth only at least six.
VI. CONCLUSION
Based on Sidon sequences over Zm, a class of QCLDPC codes of girth at least eight is presented. The new codes are called Sidon-LDPC codes. Experimental results show that for short to moderate lengths, Sidon-LDPC codes can perform nearly as well as random PEG-LDPC codes. Therefore, in the applications where the block length is not too large, Sidon-LDPC codes are likely to be one of the competitors to random codes, due to their
