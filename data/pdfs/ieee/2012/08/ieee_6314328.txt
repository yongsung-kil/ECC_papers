Rate-Compatible QC-LDPC Codes Design Based on EXIT Chart Analysis
Zaishuang Liu, Kewu Peng, Weilong Lei, Chen Qian, and Zhaocheng Wang Department of Electronic Engineering, Tsinghua University, Beijing 100084, P. R. China Tsinghua National Laboratory for Information Science and Technology (TNList), Beijing 100084, P. R. China
Email: liuzs09@mails.tsinghua.edu.cn

Abstract—This paper proposes a "column extension" design method for rate-compatible quasi-cyclic (QC) low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes, which can provide efficient multi-rate coding schemes for communication systems with flexible spectrum efficiency. The optimization of degree distributions among multiple code rates makes the designed QC-LDPC codes achieve excellent performance at each code rate, while the nested paritycheck matrix structure of the designed codes facilitates the implementation of multi-rate encoder/decoder and offers the advantage of low-complexity. A comparison with the multi-rate LDPC codes in DVB-S2 specification is addressed. Extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) chart analysis predicts the superiorities of the optimized degree distributions in Eb/N0 thresholds, and the bit error rate (BER) simulation results show that the designed codes perform 0.03-0.1 dB better than the DVB-S2 64K codes at each typical code rate, with even shorter code length.
Keywords—Rate-compatible, QC-LDPC, multi-rate, degree distribution, EXIT chart.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, first proposed by Gallager [1] and rediscovered by Mackay and Neal [2], [3], are linear block codes defined by sparse parity-check matrices H. Because of their design flexibility, decoding simplicity, high-throughput and especially the capability of achieving near-capacity performance, LDPC codes are gaining increased attention in communication standards and literature. As an important progress of the channel coding applied in communication standards, a powerful multi-rate LDPC scheme has been employed by DVB-S2 [4].
To provide flexible spectrum efficiency in the physical layer, most integrated forward error correction (FEC) schemes require multi-rate. As a competitive choice, multi-rate LDPC scheme is applied widely in the last decade. However, the codes with high-performance at each rate are usually difficult to achieve a single low-complexity encoder/decoder structure fully compliant with all the rates. Example for the DVB-S2 multi-rate LDPC scheme, it defines different LDPC codes for each rate. Although the codes share a similar structure and properties, it still poses an enormous challenge on the development of a single encoder/decoder structure for all the rates. Considering the implementation issues of the multi-rate LDPC encoder and decoder, rate-compatible scheme [5]–[7] is a quite efficient hardware-friendly solution, which allows a single "mother" encoder and decoder to be good across a wide range of rates.

Quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes, as an important family of LDPC codes, can provide excellent error-correcting performance with relatively simple structures [8], [9]. Benefiting from the regular array structure of their parity-check matrices, QC-LDPC codes can not only be encoded efficiently via simple circuits such as shift-register-adder-accumulators (SRAA) [10], [11], but also be decoded delicately with semi-parallel decoding architectures [12], [13], which significantly decrease the implementation complexity of the encoder/decoders. Therefore, QC-LDPC codes have been employed by several communication standards such as IEEE 802.16e, ITU-T G.hn and Chinese Digital Television Terrestrial Multimedia Broadcasting (DTMB).
In this paper, a new method for rate-compatible QC-LDPC codes design is proposed. With the optimization of degree distributions, the multi-rate QC-LDPC codes restricted by a nested matrix structure can achieve excellent performance at each code rate, wherein extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) chart [14]–[16] is adopted as a powerful analysis tool. A set of multi-rate QC-LDPC codes is constructed and compared with the multi-rate LDPC scheme of DVB-S2. EXIT chart analysis and bit error rate (BER) simulations demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method and the superior performance of the designed codes.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. Section II briefly introduces the related backgrounds, including QCLDPC code as well as its design method based on EXIT chart tool, the existing rate-compatible LDPC schemes, and the multi-rate LDPC scheme of DVB-S2. In Section III, our design method is addressed with an example of proper degree distributions, the Eb/N0 thresholds comparison results obtained from EXIT chart analysis are provided, and finally the designed multi-rate QC-LDPC codes are given in the form of template matrices. Simulation results and conclusions are drawn in Section IV and Section V, respectively.

B. EXIT Chart and QC-LDPC Code Design

There are various approaches to design LDPC codes,
wherein EXIT chart tool is chosen as the starting point in this paper. EXIT chart, first proposed by ten Brink [14], is
a powerful and visualized tool to analyze the convergence of
iterative systems with serially concatenated inner and outer decoders. A typical EXIT chart is composed by the inner decoder’s EXIT curve Ci and the outer decoder’s inverted EXIT curve Co-1. The performance analysis of an iterative decoding system usually relies on the area property [15] of
the EXIT chart, i.e., successful decoding is predicted by a tunnel between Ci and Co-1, whereas the area between the two curves represents the capacity loss. It is recognized that
EXIT curve-matching is an essential way to design a good iterative decoding system.
As observed in [16], an iterative decoder for an (N, K) LDPC code can be viewed as a bipartite graph that has N variable nodes, an edge interleaver, and N − K check nodes. The sets of variable and check nodes are referred to as the
variable node decoder (VND) and check node decoder (CND), respectively. As shown in Fig. 1, The decoder structure is
regarded as a serial concatenation of these two component

The observation above indicates that the LDPC decoding operation can be characterized by an EXIT chart. For an irregular LDPC code, the EXIT curve Ci of the inner decoder VND is a weighted summation of different repetition decoders’ curves, according to the degree distributions of the variable nodes. Similarly, the inverted EXIT curve Co-1 of the outer decoder CND is a weighted summation of different paritycheck decoders’ inverted curves, according to the degree distributions of the check nodes. Thus, we can firstly design the degree distributions of an irregular LDPC code and predict its performance under the given degree distributions via EXIT chart analysis. By decreasing the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) as low as possible while still keeping the EXIT tunnel open, i.e., keeping the two curves not intersecting until (1,1), we can find an SNR threshold of successful decoding, which represents the theoretical SNR threshold of the LDPC code under the given degree distributions.
Once the degree distributions are determined, we adopt the progressive edge-growth (PEG) algorithm [17]–[19] to construct the T matrix by progressively adding edges. In particular, Li and Kumar [19] proposed a design method of QC-LDPC codes based on the PEG algorithm, and validated that such designed QC-LDPC codes can achieve as good performance as random LDPC codes. The PEG algorithm usually has two kinds of constraints, i.e., on minimum girth and on degree distributions. When constructing a QC-LDPC code, since the short girth can be eliminated via the design of shifting values, the constraint on degree distributions is often set as higher priority. Thus, a T matrix fully compliant with the given degree distributions can be constructed via the PEG algorithm.
The final step is to design the shifting values of the rotated identity sub-matrices. Random construction method [9] can avoid the short girth somehow, while it is not very satisfying in some cases. Another method, which has better availability and more robust performance, is suited to construct the shifting values with the expansion factor b = pm − 1 or pm, wherein p is a prime number [20], [21]. When the shifting values are finalized, the QC-LDPC code design is completed.

C. Review of Rate-Compatible LDPC Schemes



Rate-compatible LDPC code schemes, with typical application in hybrid automatic repeat request (HARQ) systems, can support multi-rate encoding/decoding operations by using only one set of encoder and decoder corresponding to the largest H matrix. The most common rate-compatible technique is puncturing [5], where the transmitter punctures specific check bits in a codeword according to well-designed puncturing patterns, to provide the compatibility from the high rates to the low rates. In such a codeword, the information bit length at each rate is constant, while the lower rate provides higher redundancy with more check bits. It is suitable for the HARQ systems with incremental redundancy.
Other representative schemes include "row hiding" [6] and "matrix extension" [7]. The former first constructs a mother code at the lowest rate, then gradually hides the uppermost rows of the mother matrix, to obtain the daughter codes of higher rates, with the same codeword length of all rates. Differently, the latter keeps the same length of information bits, extends the row and column of H matrix synchronously to construct larger matrices of lower rates step by step, and finally gets the largest mother matrix of the lowest rate.
The two schemes above have their own nested matrix structures, and can provide particular rate-compatible LDPC codes in specific scenarios. However, since the number of check bits always changes with different rates, the variable node degrees of each rate are definitely different, which means it is difficult to design the optimal or near-optimal variable node degree distributions for all the rates, under the constraint of the nested matrix structure. Therefore, the performance of these multi-rate LDPC codes usually cannot be guaranteed in all the scenarios.
D. DVB-S2 Multi-Rate LDPC Scheme
The multi-rate LDPC scheme of DVB-S2 [4] is a typical paradigm of the LDPC codes applied in communication standards. It supports 11 code rates from 1/4 to 9/10, in both normal and short FEC frame modes. The codeword lengths of the normal and short modes are uniformly specified as 64,800 bits (64K for short) and 16,200 bits (16K for short), respectively. In this paper, we tentatively care about the DVBS2 LDPC codes at 7 typical rates from 1/3 to 5/6, whose parameters are listed in Table I.
It is observed that these multi-rate LDPC codes within a mode have the same codeword length but different numbers of information bits and check bits. Since the H matrices are designed individually at each rate, they are not rate-compatible though they share a similar structure and properties.

III. PROPOSED RATE-COMPATIBLE QC-LDPC DESIGN
A. Proposed Rate-Compatible Method
A new rate-compatible QC-LDPC design method is developed in this section. First of all, the parameters of the multirate codes such as the sizes of T matrices and the expansion factor b are predetermined according to the specific scenarios and the required code rates. We expect the T matrices of all the rates to follow a nested matrix structure, which is quite different from other rate-compatible schemes. The parameter c, i.e., the row number of T, is constant, while the parameter t, i.e., the column number of T, varies with different rates. The T matrix of the lowest rate is the shortest, and the higher the rate is, the longer the T becomes.
Once the basic parameters are determined, we need to fix the degrees and their distributions of each rate. The choices of the check node degrees are relatively simple. Since they have less impact on the code performance, they could be chosen approaching the average of the whole nodes. However, the variable node degrees and their distributions impact the performance significantly. So we carefully choose the variable node degrees and design the near-optimal distributions via EXIT chart analysis.
This "column extension" design method is simply shown in Fig. 3. We firstly search out a set of good degrees and their distributions of the lowest rate, then construct the corresponding Hc matrix. From the first matrix, new columns are progressively added to the matrix, to obtain the extended matrices of higher rates, and the longest mother Hc matrix of the highest rate is finally constructed. The design procedure of the new columns follows the PEG algorithm [19], while

Benefiting from the constant row number of the multi-rate T matrices, the variable node degrees of the longer matrix always contain those of the shorter, which is good for the optimization of degree distributions around all the rates. Typically, the types of variable node degrees for all the rates could be the same to simplify the design. The relatively large design freedom could make the degree distributions of all rates near-optimal and guarantee the excellent performance at each rate.
Another kind of flexibility is provided by the expansion factor b. Since the T matrices are getting longer with the rate increasing, the multi-rate codes have different codeword lengths but the same number of check bits if b is constant. Relatively, the requirements of the same codeword length or information bit length could also be met by varying the values of b, under the same nested T matrix structure.

B. EXIT Chart Analysis of Degree Distributions
Considering the code length and code rate requirements of the communication systems similar with DVB-S2, the parameters c and b are determined as c = 24, b = 256 and b = 1024, and the multi-rate codes are derived from a code with the rate of 1/3, namely, with a 24 × 36 T matrix. According to the analysis, the variable node degrees are chosen as (2, 3, 12) for all the rates, which could form excellent distributions in most cases. Finally, the designed multi-rate QC-LDPC codes are determined to be across 10 rates from 1/3 to 5/6. Each time the rate rises to the next higher level, the T matrix increases 12 columns, while its column number of degree 2 keep unchanged, and its column numbers of degree 3 and degree 12 add 10 and 2 respectively. This feature not only provides optimized degree distributions, but also takes account of the design convenience of the multi-rate LDPC codes. IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
In this section, the designed rate-compatible QC-LDPC codes with 7 typical code rates out of the total 10 are compared to the codes in DVB-S2 at the same code rates, through BER simulations. The sum-product algorithm (SPA) [3] is used for the LDPC decoding with the maximum iteration of 50. QPSK modulation is adopted. The channel model is AWGN channel.
The BER performance comparison of the two multi-rate LDPC schemes is shown in Fig. 6 and Fig. 7. When the rate is 3/5 or lower, the codeword lengths of our designed codes with b = 256 are even shorter than that of the short code in DVB-S2. As shown in Fig. 6, at the rate of 1/3 and 1/2, our designed codes with b = 256 perform worse than the DVBS2 codes because of their short codeword lengths. However, when b = 1024, the performance of the designed codes are improved significantly. At the rate of 1/3, our (36864, 12288) code performs extremely close to the 64K code of DVB-S2. At the rate of 1/2, the performance of our (49152, 24576) code is even 0.03 dB superior to the 64K code of DVB-S2, at the BER of 10-5. At the rate of 3/5, our (15360, 9216) code performs close to the 64K code and much better than the 16K code of DVB-S2, as the latter displays an error floor. Besides, our (61440, 36864) code performs about 0.1 dB better than the 64K code of DVB-S2, at the BER of 10-5.

V. CONCLUSION
This paper proposes a new method for the rate-compatible QC-LDPC codes design, where a nested T matrix structure with column extension is carefully constructed and the optimization of degree distributions is carried out through EXIT chart analysis. The proposed method can provide lowcomplexity implementation of multi-rate encoder/decoder as well as excellent performance at each code rate. A set of multi-rate QC-LDPC codes is designed by using the method, and compared to the DVB-S2 multi-rate LDPC scheme. The effectiveness of the proposed method and the superior performance of our designed codes are validated via both EXIT chart analysis and BER simulations.
