OPPM and PWM Concatenated Modulation for LED Indoor Communication

Yongsheng Wu, Chunxue Yang, Aiying Yang*, Yu-nan Sun
Department of Opto-electronic Engineering Beijing Institute of Technology Beijing, China
yongshengs.wu@gmail.com, yangcx0301@126.com, yangaiying@bit.edu.cn, syn@bit.edu.cn

Abstract—In this paper, a kind of concatenated modulation with OPPM and PWM for LED indoor communication is proposed. 400 Mbit/s bit rate is achieved in analysis and 50 Mbit/s transmission rate is demonstrated in experiments. The Monte Carlo simulations are applied to get the BER curves of the system based on the AWGN channel. The modified bit-flipping algorithm could get 2dB coding gain more than the standard bitflipping algorithm at the BER level of about 10-5.
Keywords-Visible Light Communication (VLC); Overlapping Pulse Position Modulation (OPPM); Pulse-Width Modulation (PWM); Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) codes
I. INTRODUCTION
Nowadays, the visible light communication (VLC) has attracted much more attentions in various fields of applications, such as lighting, automobiles, transportation, communication, imaging, agriculture, and medicine[1]. The VLC system employs a signal transmitter as an illumination module such as light emitting diode (LED) lighting[2]. Due to the constraint of low bandwidth illumination LEDs, many methods are adopted to achieve the high transmission rate. Using discrete multitone (DMT) modulation, VLC systems can provide a 200+ Mb/s transmission rate with commercial high-power lighting LEDs[3]. Based on WDM and DMT modulation, a VLC system with a single RGB-type white LED operating at an aggregate rate of 803 Mbit/s has been reported[4].
The DMT modulation needs an arbitrary waveform generator and a complex calculation with IFFT and FFT. However, the overlapping pulse position modulation (OPPM) method could increase the throughput with the same complexity as the OOK modulation[5]. In this paper, we propose an OPPM and pulse-width modulation (PWM) concatenated modulation to obtain a higher transmission rate for the bandwidth limitation of LEDs. Transmission rate of 400 Mbit/s has been achieved in analysis and 50 Mbit/s has been demonstrated in experiments. With the low density parity check (LDPC) coding, we could have more than 4 dB coding gain while the BER value maintains about 10-5[6].
II. OPPM AND PWM CONCATENATED MODULATION
From the datasheet of RC-LED-650-02 (ROITHNER LASERTECHNIK), we can see that the rise time and fall time

are both 3 ns. Using OOK modulation, we could achieve a transmission rate no more than 166 Mbit/s as one bit takes 6 ns. OPPM allows pulses to overlap when they represent different information such that each pulse carries increased amount of information on the average. We assume the resolution of system to be 1 ns, and then we could divide the minimum pulsewidth of RC-LED-650-02 into 6 slots. When the symbol of OPPM represents 2 bits data, 9 ns with 9 slots are needed. In this case, the period of one bit signal is decreased from 6 ns to 4.5 ns. As the pulsewidth is not less than 6 ns, we could expand the pulsewidth to 7 ns or more. We make the OPPM concatenated with PWM and then extend the symbol to 10 lots. In this way, we get a higher bit rate as one symbol represents 4 bits. The detailed description of the modulation is given in table 1. With this kind of modulation, the transmission rate is increased to 400 Mbit/s. Because the VLC system is also considered as the illumination system, the brightness should be the key factor of the system. The proposed modulation could have 68.75% brightness level while OOK only has 50% in average.

The Monte Carlo simulations are applied to analyze the performance of the algorithm for LDPC coded transmission and the maximum iteration value is 30 in simulations. According to the Mackay’s Construction 1A, the low density parity-check matrices H (512, 256, 0.5) are created[7]. A 256 by 512 matrix (256 rows, 512 columns) is created at random with weight per column 3, and weight per row as uniform as possible, and overlap between any two columns no greater than 1. The additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel adds the noise in the
transmission system, such as yi = xi + ni , where yi is the received signal, xi is the signal from the transmitter, and ni is
the AWGN with zero mean. When the error is occurred for modulated symbol, 4bits would be affected rather than one bit. Here we use the grouped bit-flipping (GFP) decoding algorithm which has the similar complexity as standard bitflipping algorithm [6]. Detailed discussion of GFP algorithm could be found in another paper which is in reviewing. Fig .4 shows the results of BER curves at different SNR values. From the curves it can be seen that the GFP algorithm could get 2dB coding gain more than the standard bit-flipping algorithm with the BER value of about 10-5; and 4 dB coding gain more than the uncoded transmission. It could be expected that the GBF algorithm could get more coding gain compared with the standard BP decoding algorithm as the number of bits with the modulated symbol is increased with the same decoding complexity.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, a kind of concatenated modulation with OPPM and PWM for LED indoor communication has been discussed. 400 Mbit/s bit rate has been achieved in analysis and 50 Mbit/s transmission rate has been demonstrated in experiments due to the limitation of devices. Considering the structure of the modulated symbol, the modified bit-flipping algorithm could get 2dB coding gain more than the standard bit-flipping algorithm at the BER level of about 10-5 with the similar complexity. Moreover, the performance of the system could be improved when the block length of LDPC code was larger and more bits could be represented as one symbol
