AbstractвАФIn this paper, we propose a simple mathematical structure to improve the BER (bit error rate) performance of quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes. Since the proposed structure can be defined in-between those of random codes and structured codes, these proposed QC-LDPC codes not only perform as well as random-LDPC codes, but also preserve the advantage of structured LDPC codes which is easier to implement over coding hardware. Moreover, the proposed structure, constructed from balanced incomplete block design (BIBD), can reduce the density of parity-check matrix and hence reduce the number of short cycles, which generally lead to better performance. At the end, we show that the error floor performance of QC-LDPC codes can be improved by proposed hybrid structures and perform well over binary-input additive white Gaussian noise (BI-AWGN) channel.
I. INTRODUCTION (HEADING 1)
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes were originally invented by Gallager [1] in the early 1960s and resurrected in the mid of 1990s with the work of MacKay and Neal [2], since its performances can approach to Shannon limit by utilizing the iterative message passage decoding [2], [3]. Based on the methods of construction, LDPC codes can be divided into two main categories: random codes [4], [5] and structured codes [6]-[8]. In general, the structured LDPC codes reduces its storage memory requirements, hence they have drawn much attention in the sense that they facilitate low-complexity encoder and decoder designs, especially quasi-cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes [6], [9]-[10].
A regular QC-LDPC code [1], [11] is defined as the null space of a sparse parity-check matrix H over GF(2). The codes satisfy the following properties: 1). each column has constant weight s; 2). each row has constant weight t; 3). no two rows (or two columns) have more than 1-component in common; 4). Both s and t are small compared to the length of the code and the number of rows in H.
Previously, we proposed classes of QC-LDPC codes generated from modified Welch-Costas codes (MWC) [7] and

one-coincidence sequences (OCSs) codes [9], which will be respectively named MWC-LDPC codes and OCS-LDPC codes in this letter. In MWC-LDPC codes and OCS-LDPC codes, by randomly selecting elements of the seed sequences, the parity-check matrix H can be generated from utilizing simple equations. We showed the performance of LDPC codes was as good as the random LDPC codes with short to moderate block lengths, and had almost the same performance as the QC-LDPC codes proposed in [8] for different block lengths and rates.
Recently, BIBD's were used to construct LDPC codes. Several classes of LDPC codes were constructed based on special types of BIBD's. Several subclasses of BIBD-LDPC codes were quasi-cyclic (QC). In [13], Honary et al. proposed BIBD-constructions to build QC-LDPC codes. The method in [13] reduced the density of parity-check matrices and hence reduced the number of short cycles. Lin et al. [11], [12] presented a symbol dispersion method to construct QC-LDPC codes based on Bose-BIBD's, then dispersed the parity-check matrix into specific structure for correcting erasure-bursts (binary erasure channel, BEC). However, the families of QCLPDC codes in [11] are restricted to (4, 4k) and (3, 3k), where k is a positive integer. Compared with the construction in [11], our approach provides QC-LDPC codes with greater flexible and selectable code rates and lengths.
In this letter, we propose a simply mathematical scheme to construct the hybrid structure of parity-check matrices for QCLDPC codes. By utilizing the elements of modified prime sequences (MPS) and modified Welch-Costas codes (MWC), the proposed QC-LDPC codes have Tanner girth greater than or equal to 6. And also the proposed hybrid structure based on BIBD's also reduces the density of the parity-check matrix and hence reduces the number of 6-cycles, which generally lead to better performance of LDPC codes. In section II, we illustrate the proposed construction in detail. In section III, we show the performance of the proposed QC-LDPC codes, and compare it to the Shannon limit. Finally, we draw some concluding remarks in section IV.

In part (a), the (c, p1, пБђ) is a well-known notation for BIBD's families. IV. CONCLUSION
In this letter, we presented a method for constructing QCLDPC codes based on a hybrid structure of the parity-check matrix. By utilizing modified prime sequences and modified Welch-Costas codes, it ensures the Tanner graph of codes has

a girth of at least 6. Because of the zero matrices in the proposed hybrid parity-check matrix, we reduce the number of 6-cycles. Moreover, our approach provides QC-LDPC codes with greater flexible and selectable code rates and lengths. The simulation results show that the proposed QCLDPC codes can efficiently improve the error floor performance of regular QC-LDPC codes over the BI-AWGN channel. And no error floor is observed even at BER of 10-8.
