Abstract—Estimating accurately channel noise has significant impact on the performance of channel coding. In this paper, we present a low complexity online estimator at the decoder side for low-density parity check (LDPC) codes over additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels with a time-varying noise. The proposed algorithm is obtained by modeling the channel statistic as a Bayesian inference problem on a factor graph. Since BP algorithm cannot adapt efficiently to the statistical change of SNR in an AWGN channel, we apply the Laplace propagation (LP) algorithm on the proposed algorithm. Through simulations, we show that the proposed algorithm can simultaneously decode LDPC codes and estimate the time-varying SNR at the bitlevel, which enhance the BP decoding performance. Moreover, the proposed LP estimator shows a very low computational complexity.
Index Terms—Channel coding, Estimation, Adaptive decoding
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, as a type of errorcorrecting codes, were first proposed by Gallager in the early 1960s [1] and revived by Mackay and Neal in 1996 [2]. Since then, LDPC codes have attracted wide spread interests in the research community. For LDPC decoding, factor graph representation and inference techniques including the belief propagation (BP)-like algorithms such as the sum-product and the max-product algorithms provide a general and powerful framework. In the context of Bayesian inference, BP (i.e., sumproduct) based LDPC decoding corresponds to the computation of posterior probabilities over hidden variables (original channel input) given the received signal (channel output) on the factor graph. The posterior probability over hidden variables can be obtained exactly through BP algorithm on tree-structured graphs. For graphs with loops, such as LDPC decoding [3] in practice, BP algorithm (or loopy BP algorithm) still shows a good performance.
Since LDPC decoding is based on an iteratively softdecision BP algorithm, it requires the knowledge of signal to noise ratio (SNR) at the decoder. In many previous studies, the SNR is assumed to be perfectly known prior to decoding,

such that LDPC decoding can achieve the best performance. In reality, however, the perfect knowledge of the SNR may not always be available at the decoder, as the channel SNR may vary over time. In the presence of an SNR mismatch, the studies in [4]–[6] showed that the decoding performance of BP can be degraded. Thus, many SNR estimation algorithms [6], [7] were proposed to alleviate the decoding performance loss caused by SNR mismatch. While one may argue that the actual SNR could be obtained through a pilot signal or feedback channel under varying channel conditions, a fast varying channel implies potentially a large communication overhead if we want to take full advantage of the channel state information.
The problems of channel SNR estimation have attracted increasing interests in literature. For example, Summers et al. in [6] proposed a lightweight SNR estimator for Turbo decoding, which is based on the sums of squared receiver values and sums of their absolute values. Moreover, Ramon et al. presented an EM-based estimation of carrier phase, amplitude and noise variance in multiuser turbo receivers in [7]. In both of [6] and [7], it was observed that the channel state information extracted by their proposed estimators effectively improved the decoding performance. However, in all above studies, the basic assumption is that the SNR is fixed for all codewords. In practice, communication systems such as orthogonal frequency division multiplexing (OFDM) and synchronous code-division multiple-access (CDMA) systems usually involve time-varying noise, where the papers [8] and [9] consider a randomly varying noise variance in OFDM and CDMA systems, respectively. In contrast with the aforementioned works [6], [7], they assume that different subcarriers have different SNRs, while the SNRs within a subcarrier are the same for all codewords. To better capture the time-vary SNR property, it is more reasonable to consider the change of SNR differs at the bit-level for each codeword block. Recently, a particle based online SNR estimator is proposed in [10], which performs online SNR estimation at the bitlevel and improves BP decoding performance by updating the estimated SNR iteratively. However, the particle based bit-level SNR estimator in [10] are usually computationally demanding, which results a significant decoding delay. In the rest, we

In Bayesian inference, the estimation of time-varying SNR in an AWGN channel corresponds to the estimation of the posterior probability of channel noise variance or precision (i.e., the reciprocal of variance). In this paper, we are interested in the statistical knowledge of time-varying channel parameter, which is typically continuous variables. Although, BP algorithm is a powerful probabilistic inference technique, it works well only for efficiently computing posterior probability over discrete variables of small alphabet sizes or continuous variables of linear Gaussian distributions. Therefore, BP algorithm cannot handle the problem of time-varying SNR estimation, which involves continuous variables with nonGaussian distribution (i.e., Gamma distribution for precision in this problem). The workaround, proposed in this paper, is to incorporate LP algorithm for channel parameter estimation. In contrast with standard BP, LP algorithm propagates and updates an approximate distribution in the context of Gaussian distribution instead of the original non-Gaussian distribution, where the approximate and original distributions have the same modes. Through experimental results, we show that the proposed algorithm no longer depends on the initial estimate of noise precision, and offers a lower decoding error rate than the traditional BP decoding.

A. Factor graph construction
A factor graph is a particular type of graphical model representing a joint distribution of all the variables and enables efficient computation of marginal distributions through BP algorithm. Factor graph is a bipartite graph, which comprises of two different kinds of nodes: factor node (depicted by small squares) and variable node (usually depicted by a circle), as shown in Fig.1. In factor graph, each edge must connect a factor node and a variable node. Moreover, each factor node represents one of factors over a subset of some variables in a factorized joint distribution. Then, we investigate the decoding performances of BP decoder, LP based BP decoder, and PBP decoder with timevarying SNR, where σnoise = 0.2. By changing σ0, different time-varying SNRs are sampled from the aforementioned model in the study of Fig. 2. The mean values of different time-varying SNRs, as shown in Figs. 4 and 5, change from 0.8 dB to 1.9 dB. Moreover, we introduced 0 dB and +2 dB mismatches of these mean values as initial values for all three decoders in the Figs. 4 and 5, respectively. In our experiments, the LP/PBP estimator starts working after 50 BP iterations. Moreover, we perform LP/PBP estimator periodically for every 20 BP iterations until BP decoder successfully decodes the codeword or reaches its maximum number of iterations (i.e., 150 in our experiment).
Fig. 4 depicts that the proposed LP based LDPC decoder slightly outperforms the ordinary BP decoder, when the mean of true SNR is used as initial value. Moreover, Fig. 5 shows

that our proposed LP based BP decoder obtained a much better performance than that of BP decoder using initial SNR with a +2 dB mismatch of the true mean of time-varying SNR. The gap between BP with and without the knowledge of true SNR is about 0.6 dB, however, the gap between a known true SNR BP decoder and the LP based BP decoder is less than 0.35 dB. This result indicates that knowing only the +2 dB mismatch above the true mean of the time-varying SNR is not enough for a standard BP decoder to achieve its best decoding performance, when the SNR in a channel varies at the bit-level. In addition, the BP decoder with known time-varying SNR is shown in Fig. 5 as benchmark performance. Finally, although a 0.15 dB gap is observed between the proposed LP based BP decoder and PBP decoder, the proposed LP based BP decoder offers a quite lower computational complexity than that of PBP decoder.
V. CONCLUSION
This paper proposed an adaptive LDPC decoding with LP estimator over time-varying AWGN channels. By incorporating the LP algorithm in Region I of the proposed factor graph, the estimate of channel SNR can be updated periodically. Finally, the time-varying channel SNR can be precisely tracked. Thus, our algorithm yields a better decoding performance (in terms of lower BER) than the standard BP algorithm, when the true knowledge of SNR is not available at the decoder. Furthermore, the proposed LP estimator has a lower computational complexity than that of PBP estimator with the cost of a small performance loss (i.e., 0.15 dB).
