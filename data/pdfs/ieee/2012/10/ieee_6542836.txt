Abstract—In this paper, we propose an efficient Ordered statistic decoding (OSD)-aided turbo decoding algorithm for LTE turbo codes to improve the error performance of conventional turbo decoding. More significant performance gain can be achieved for short codes and high-rate punctured codes. Considering the Loglikelihood ratio (LLR) oscillation, we introduce a more reliable method to reconstruct ordered information sequence in terms of the accumulated LLR of each bit in the codeword. Additionally, we present a simple encoding method based on bit flipping to decrease the computational complexity. The simulation results show that noticeable performance gains are achieved by the proposed OSD-aided schemes.

I. INTRODUCTION
As an important component of the Shannon-limit approaching codes, the turbo codes are selected as the coding scheme for data transmission in LTE systems. Both the encoding and decoding of the LTE turbo codes can be efficiently implemented with low complexity.
In order to further increase the spectral efficiency of the LTE systems, both the rate-compatible puncture turbo coding and Hybrid automatic repeat request (HARQ) are employed. Besides, according to the LTE standard, the block length of turbo codes covers a wild range from 40 to 6144 [2].
The highest rate of the punctured turbo codes is almost close to 0.9 and the shortest block length is 40. Therefore the original rate-1/3 turbo codes in LTE suffer severe performance degradation for the widely varying rates and lengths, resulting in great loss in the data throughput of HARQ.
To improve the performance of the turbo codes especially for the scenarios with short block lengths and high punctured rates, we propose an efficient ordered statistics decoding (OSD)-aided turbo decoding (turbo-OSD) algorithm. The OSD algorithm, originally used in soft-decision decoding for linear block codes, is used to assist in further improving the error performance of conventional turbo decoding. Motohiko Isaka once proposed the OSD algorithm in conjunction with iterative decoding for turbo-like codes in [3]. III. TURBO DECODING WITH OSD ASSISTANCE

The OSD-aided turbo decoding scheme is that the OSD algorithm is performed when the conventional turbo iterative decoding fails, which is detailedly described as follows. Firstly, the magnitudes of the soft outputs of the turbo decoding are set to be the reliability values, which are the inputs of the OSD processing. Then, sort the reliability values in a decreasing order and permute the hard-decision bits and the columns of the generator matrix G according to that order. After that, apply Gaussian elimination on the permuted matrix G˜ in order to obtain the generator matrix G˜sys in the systematic form [4]. Finally, the encoding processes are carried out according to the matrix G˜sys and the hard-decision bits with high reliabilities, where the original information bits extracted from each encoded sequence are detected by the cyclic redundancy check (CRC). The OSD algorithm is based on the idea of algebraic decoding and the whole codeword is re-coded by the info-bits with high reliabilities.
After each iteration in turbo decoding, the soft outputs are usually represented in the log-likelihood-ratios (LLR) forms, where the absolute values of the LLRs are taken as the input reliabilities of the OSD process. The Max-Log-MAP [5] algorithm is used for our turbo decoding. Hence, the LLR outputs of both the systematic bits d1, ..., dK and the parity

In the multiple encoding stages of OSD process, we propose a bit-flipping method to further decrease the complexity of the turbo-OSD scheme. The message sequence m consists of the permuted K bits after sorting by reliabilities and Gaussian elimination according to [4], then a standard encoding process via the permuted matrix G˜sys requires O(K2) times multiplyadd operations. For the flipping of the i-th bit of m, only the elements in the i-th row of G˜sys are involved, so just a simple bit-flipping operation is needed for the encoding. If the bit in the i-th row and j-th column of G˜sys is one, the j-th bit of the codeword should be flipped, which at most takes O(K) times flipping operation decreasing the complexity of the turbo-OSD scheme.
Assume that the conventional Max-Log-MAP algorithm is applied for the turbo decoding and the OSD process is carried out among the T iterations, where the parameter T is the maximum number of iterations. According to these assumptions, we propose two schemes for OSD process to assist the turbo decoding. Introducing a parameter f , the frequency of the OSD process performed in turbo decoding, then the schemes can be represented as follows:

1) f = 0. The turbo decoding is carried out without OSD assistance.
2) f = 1. If the turbo decoding fails after the last iteration, the OSD is performed with the soft values out from the second constituent decoder after the T -th iteration.
3) f = T . OSD is carried out using the reliability values out from the second constituent decoder after each iteration.

Obviously, the last scheme can achieve more performance gain, but it is very difficulty to perform the OSD process after each iteration due to the significant cost of the Gaussian elimination. We need to find a tradeoff between the decoding performance and the complexity.
Due to the LLR oscillation phenomenon during the iterative decoding observed by [6], the last LLR outputs are not always credible measurements for the reliabilities. In [7], the LLR accumulation is introduced in the iterative decoding for LDPC codes. Considering the LLR output from the first several iterations are not reliable enough, we denote the parameter p as the number of iteration from which the LLR accumulation process is invoked. IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
In this section, we give the simulation results of different turbo-OSD schemes for short or medium length (N,K) LTE turbo codes, such as (132, 40), (924, 304) and (3084, 1024) LTE turbo codes. The decoding performance is simulated over the Additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel, using Binary phase shift keying (BPSK) modulation.
For the different values of the parameter f and p, we mainly consider three schemes for OSD-aided turbo decoding and the specific explanations are as follows
• f = 1, p = 8: Named as tailed-OSD, the OSD process is invoked after the last iteration of Max-Log-MAP turbo decoding;
• f = 8, p = 8: Named as repeated-OSD, the OSD process is invoked after each iteration of Max-Log-MAP turbo decoding;
• f = 1, p = 3: Named as acc-tailed-OSD, the LLR accumulation is invoked from the 3-rd iteration and the OSD process is performed after the last iteration of MaxLog-MAP turbo decoding. For the value of the parameter p, we take it from 1 to 8 in simulation and the error performance is best when p is taken to 3.
Fig. 3 illustrates the performance of the conventional trubo decoding (f=0) and three OSD-aided turbo decoding schemes for (132,40) LTE turbo codes, which exhibits obvious performance gain with OSD assistance. At block error rate of 1e-2, tailed-OSD-aided turbo decoding achieves about 0.25dB gain compared with the conventional turbo decoding. Moreover,

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we propose several turbo-OSD schemes to improve the performance of the conventional turbo decoding including tailed-OSD, repeated-OSD and acc-tailed-OSD algorithms. To decrease the computational cost of the turboOSD algorithm, we present a simple encoding method based on bit flipping. Simulation results verify the performance gain achieved by the assistance of the OSD algorithm especially for short codes and high-rate punctured codes.
