ERROR FLOOR COMPENSATION FOR LDPC CODES USING CONCATENATED SCHEMES
G. Spourlis, I. Tsatsaragkos, N. Kanistras and V. Paliouras
University of Patras Electrical and Computer Engineering Department
26500 Patras, Greece

ABSTRACT
This paper quantitatively investigates the trade-offs in the
compensation of error floor on iterative decoders. The
characterization of iterative decoding systems prone to error
floor at low noise is a great challenge as techniques based on
software simulation are inadequate due to the extremely long
simulation time required. We compare the BER performance
as measured at very low BER using hardware accelerators
and study the cost of compensation techniques in terms of
hardware complexity and throughput. Specifically, techniques
based on the use of diversity in LDPC decoders and the
use of concatenated BCH-LDPC codes are considered and
corresponding hardware optimizations are discussed. It is
shown that an introduced synergy of error-floor compensation
techniques achieves substantial coding gain up to 1 dB at low
noise, not possible by conventional LDPC or BCH decoders.
In addition, hardware reductions are achieved in the BCH
subsystem, due to simple post-processing in the LDPC decoder.
Index Terms— FEC, BCH, LDPC, error floor.
1. INTRODUCTION
An LDPC code is a linear error-correcting code, fully defined by a sparse binary parity check matrix H. A bipartite Tanner graph [1], which consists of two sets of nodes (check and variable nodes), is a widely used way to represent a parity check matrix H. Each row of H corresponds to a parity check equation, graphically represented as a check node of the Tanner graph, while columns correspond to the codeword bits (variable nodes). An ace in the H matrix indicates a connection between the corresponding variable and check nodes. Message passing algorithms for decoding LDPC codes operate by iteratively passing information along the edges of the Tanner graph. The existence of cycles in the Tanner graph affects the error correction capability of an LDPC code.
The region where the bit-error rate (BER) steadily decreases as the noise level decreases is called the waterfall region. The error correcting capability of LDPC-based systems at low-noise levels is often dominated by the error floor. We call error floor a region where BER reduction slows down as the noise level decreases [2]. This behavior is commonly observed in decoding algorithms on graphs with cycles, where the decoding process becomes sub-optimal and there exist low-weight patterns uncorrectable by the decoder. Several authors have investigated the error floor behavior and proposed compensation solutions. Trapping sets [2], absorbing sets [3] or stopping sets [4], near-codewords [5] and pseudo-codewords [6] are mainly responsible for the error floor effect, that degrades the decoding performance

of LDPC codes. Kang et al. propose a two-stage iterative decoding procedure [7], while Han et al. present three classes of decoders for lowering floors induced by trapping sets [8]. Zhang et al. [3] propose a post-processing technique which reduces the reliability of messages that correspond to falsely satisfied checks, aiming to reverse wrong bits of absorbing sets. The impact of the quantization scheme is studied in [9] and an adaptive quantization approach is proposed.
The authors have recently proposed two LDPC decoding schemes which improve BER performance in the error floor region of operation. The first one is a Multiple decoder architecture [10], which exploits the diverse behavior of different decoding algorithms. A different decoding scheme, based on syndromes calculation, succeeds to eliminate the performance degradation caused by oscillatory behavior of specific LDPC decoding algorithms [11].
A different way to improve the behavior of LDPC codes at low noise levels is by using other codes externally. External codes used in these cases do not suffer from error floor and aim at the correction of any error that is not corrected by LDPC codes. In this study we use binary BCH codes as external codes. This method has already been used in standards like DVB-S2 [12]. Binary BCH codes are relatively simple in implementation. They do not suffer from error floor although they have inferior error correction performance at high noise in contrary with LDPC codes. A binary BCH code is a cyclic linear error-correcting code. Generator polynomial is a primitive one and is constructed by minimal polynomials, as the LCM polynomial. An encoder can be constructed, which uses the generator polynomial g(x) to compute a codeword by multiplying it with the information word. For binary BCH codes, the coefficients of g(x) lie in GF (2), but its roots receive values at GF (2m). These roots, which are 2t in number, are also used in the first part of decoding process in order to detect if we have encountered errors in the received word or not. The heart of the decoding procedure in classic approaches is the finding of the errors locators, i.e. the locations where errors have occurred.
This paper focuses on the use of binary BCH codes externally of the LDPC codes to improve performance in the error floor region. The use of a conventional LDPC decoder as part of the BCH-LDPC system is a case. Another case of great interest we investigate here is the use of an LDPC decoder which takes advantage of the calculation of syndrome weights in order to reduce the number of decoding errors in cases of unsuccessful decoding. This reduction of decoding errors facilitates a BCH decoder of a given correcting capability t to find the valid codeword. Conversely, the use of post-processing can allow the reduction of t and therefore of the BCH hardware complexity. Main novelties in

2. REVIEW OF ERROR FLOOR AND COMPENSATION TECHNIQUES
2.1. Parallel decoders
Trapping of the decoder depends on several factors, including the decoding algorithm, the particular noise to which data were exposed, the properties of the LDPC code and numerical issues in the decoder implementation. This result is strongly associated with the ability of the algorithm to successfully decode the particular distorted received word, and not with the average decoding capability of the algorithm as quantified by the BER metric. A decoding algorithm may succeed to avoid particular trapping sets, while another fails to reach the correct solution. Exploiting this diverse behavior, we have investigated the effect of parallel decoding ideas on the decoding capability of LDPC architectures, implementing variations of the Min-Sum algorithm. The Multiple LDPC decoder simultaneously processes every received word in N different decoding scenarios [10]. For each received word that enters the Multiple decoder, the final estimated codeword is the one of the N sub-decoders outputs that achieves the best result. Some easy-to-implement techniques have been proposed to resolve possible disagreement among decoders. A Multiple decoder implementation composed of two sub-decoders, one of which performs better in the waterfall region and the other in the error floor region of operation, achieves good BER performance both for low as well as high SNR levels. The appropriate selection of incorporated algorithms improves the BER results. The drawback of this method is that the concurrent decoding of every particular word by at least two implemented algorithms at no additional delay penalty imposes a significant hardware overhead. This increase of hardware cost is justified by the substantial BER and FER (frame-error rate) improvement in the error floor region, an improvement which is not possible with conventional single decoders.
2.2. Syndrome-based decoder
The authors have proposed a low-cost decoding scheme targeting a particular type of errors and particular decoding algorithms. The Syndrome-based LDPC decoder improves the performance of iterative algorithms dominated by oscillatory behavior in case of convergence failure [11]. Experimental results show that offset Min-Sum is such an algorithm. 2.3. Complexity and BER performance
Syndrome-based decoder reduces the decoding errors in case of unsuccessfully decoded codewords, while Multiple decoder targets to successfully decoding of such codewords. Thus, Syndrome-based decoder achieves BER performance improvement only, while Multiple decoder enhances FER performance as well. Authors have presented analytical experimental BER results in the corresponding published works [10] [11], which manifest the BER performance improvement arising from these two decoding schemes.

3.3. Implementation and experimental results
We have implemented a concatenated BCH-LDPC FEC system to conduct hardware emulations at various levels of noise and examine the behavior in the error floor region. The architecture of the overall system is shown in Fig. 1. Two BCH encoders of the same length operating in parallel are used. An LFSR feeds encoder’s inputs with random information words. The BCH encoder’s outputs are connected to the LDPC encoder which produces the final codeword. Adjacent bits of the information part of this codeword come from different BCH encoders thus implementing single interleaving. The output of the LDPC encoder is BPSK-modulated and transmitted through an Additive White Gaussian Noise (AWGN) channel emulated in hardware [15]. LLRs are computed for the samples obtained from the channel and are subsequently processed by an iterative LDPC decoder. The output of the LDPC decoder is processed by two BCH decoders operating in parallel. Using two smaller BCH units, instead of one larger, for encoding and decoding process, we aim at higher throughput rate and greater correction ability on the average. The Syndrome-based decoder (SB) significantly improves the error correcting capability of the MS and OMS decoding algorithms [11]. A single Syndrome-based LDPC decoder succeeds to reduce the errors in case of unsuccessfully decoded words, not to successfully decode these words. Reducing the number of decoding errors in values less than the BCH correcting capability t, the syndrome-based post-processing assists BCH decoder to successfully decode such words. We here show that the particular combination of decoding techniques achieves a very effective compensation of the error floor effect that is not possible by single LDPC or BCH decoder architectures. Moreover, SB decoding gives the designer the option to implement a BCH of lower correcting capability, hence smaller, without overall BER performance degradation. The experimental results show that the BCH-1 code, which requires less area resources, achieves similar BER performance to the BCH-2 code, when we use a syndome-based post-processing.

Multiple decoder, on the other hand, imposes a significant hardware overhead.
