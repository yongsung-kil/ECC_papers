AbstractРђћA class of short binary (3, ¤Ђ)-regular quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) code called cyclotomic coset (CC) code is constructed on the basis of cyclotomic coset. The length of the CC codes is less than 200 bits. The CC codes perform reasonably well over the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel with iterative decoding in terms of bit-error rate and decoding convergence.

A new approach to the construction of short binary QCLDPC codes is proposed. The shift matrix is obtained by cyclotomic coset, and then expanded to parity check matrix by substitution of corresponding CPMs for elements in the shift matrix. The characteristic of cyclotomic coset excludes 4-cycles in the Tanner graph of the parity check matrix, which is of great benefit to the error performance of LDPC codes decoded by sum-product algorithm (SPA) [1].

Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are currently the most promising coding technique to achieve the Shannon capacities for a wide range of channels. LDPC codes were invented by Gallager in his 1960s doctoral dissertation [1] and were mostly ignored during the 35 years that followed. One notable exception is the important work of Tanner in 1981 [2], in which Tanner generalized LDPC codes and introduced a graphical representation of LDPC codes, now called a Tanner graph. The study of LDPC codes was resurrected in the mid 1990s with the work of MacKay, Luby, and others [3РђЊ5], who noticed, apparently independently of GallagerРђЎs work, the advantages of linear block codes with sparse (low-density) parity-check matrices. Recently, LDPC codes have attracted many attentions for their capacity approaching performance, linear decoding complexity, flexibility, low error floor, and so on. Now, the construction for LDPC codes is one of hot spots in LDPC research area [6РђЊ10].
An LDPC code is a linear block code given by the null space of an m ├Ќ n parity-check matrix H that has a low density of 1РђЎs. A regular LDPC code is a linear block code whose paritycheck matrix H has column weight ╬│ and row weight ¤Ђ, where ¤Ђ = ╬│n/m and ╬│ m. In the case of general LDPC codes, a significant amount of memory is required to store their paritycheck matrices. As an attractive subclass of structured LDPC codes, quasi-cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes, whose paritycheck matrices consist of circulant matrices or zero matrices, are getting more attention due to their easy implementation and other graceful features [11]. Thus, QC-LDPC codes may be a good candidate to solve the memory problem for their algebraic structures. B. Girth

An LDPC code can be described by a bipartite graph, called a Tanner graph [2]. The length of the shortest cycle in a Tanner graph is referred to as its girth. In almost all of the proposed constructions of LDPC codes, the following constraint on the rows and columns of the parity-check matrix H is imposed: no two rows (or two columns) can have more than one position where they both have 1-components. This constraint on the rows and the columns of H is named the row-column (RC)constraint. The RC-constraint on H ensures that the Tanner graph of the LDPC code given by the null space of H is free of cycles of length 4 and hence has a girth of at least 6 [14].
A cycle of length 2i in H is defined by 2i 1РђЎs such that 1) two consecutive positions are obtained by alternatively changing the rows or columns only, and 2) all positions are distinct, except the first and the last ones. It follows that two consecutive positions in any cycle belong to distinct CPMs, which are either in the same row, or in the same column. IV. CONCLUSION
The construction of binary QC-LDPC codes based on cyclotomic coset naturally eliminates cycles of length four in the Tanner graph. In comparison with the state-of-the-art binary random PEG and MacKay codes or structural array codes, the CC codes have comparably better error performance with length up to 155 bits. However, the method to the construction of short CC codes with high rates is still under consideration.
