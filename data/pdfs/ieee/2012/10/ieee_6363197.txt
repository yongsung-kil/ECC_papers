ABSTRACT
This paper introduces clockless stochastic decoding for high-throughput low-density parity-check (LDPC) decoders. Stochastic computation provides ultra-low-complexity hardware using simple logic gates. Clockless decoding eliminates global clocking, which eases the worst-case timing restrictions of synchronous stochastic decoders. The lack of synchronization might use outdated bits to update outputs in computation nodes; however, it does not significantly affect output probabilities. A timing model of clocklesscomputation behaviours under a 90nm CMOS technology is used to simulate the BER performance of the proposed decoding scheme. Based on our models, the proposed decoding scheme significantly reduces error floors due to the “lock-up” problem and achieves superior BER performance compared with conventional synchronous stochastic decoders.
Index Terms— stochastic computation, iterative decoding, forward error correction codes, clockless computation, circuit implementation
1. INTRODUCTION
Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) [1, 2] codes are forwarderror-correcting codes that perform very close to the Shannon limit. LDPC codes have been used in digital communication standards, such as IEEE 802.16e (WiMAX), IEEE 802.11n (WiFi), IEEE 802.3an (10GBASE-T), and DVB-S2. Parallel decoder implementations tend to achieve high throughput [3– 5], but the wiring complexity and large area consumption lead to an increase in the maximum wire length, which imposes an upper limit on achievable speed [6].
Stochastic decoding [7–10] is a technique where probability messages are represented by the statistics of a Bernoulli sequence. The computation blocks are implemented using simple binary gates and data transmission between the nodes is performed serially, which leads to low complexity decoders. Generally, stochastic decoders are designed using synchronous circuits, but for large LDPC codes, this can lead to potential problems: large power dissipation due

to high-speed clocking and lower throughput due to wiring complexity in the chip implementation.
We have previously reported a method for asynchronous stochastic decoding of LDPC codes that uses local clocking instead of global clocking [11]. In the decoder, all computational blocks and the interleaver operate based on their local delays, which eases the worst-case timing restriction while maintaining comparable error-correcting performance to a synchronous stochastic decoder. However, there are speed and hardware overheads due to local handshaking. In this paper, we report a clockless stochastic LDPC decoder that completely eliminates any global and local clock signals and evaluate its BER performance using a timing model.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. A review of stochastic decoding is in Section II. Section III describes the proposed clockless stochastic decoding scheme. Section IV describes the hardware implementation. Section V describes our timing model and how it is used to simulate bit-error rate (BER) performance. Section VI provides performance comparisons with conventional decoding under a 90nm CMOS technology. Finally, we conclude this paper in Section VII.

2.2. Stochastic decoding

3. CLOCKLESS STOCHASTIC DECODING
3.1. Synchronous decoding
Stochastic LDPC decoders are usually implemented by using synchronous circuits [9, 10], where all the variable-node outputs are updated simultaneously using a global clock signal.
Fig. 2 shows a block diagram of a two-input variable-node and its timing diagram in synchronous stochastic decoding. All computation and data-transmission delays are restricted by the worst-case delay (Tsync), which is related to the longest wire in the interleaver. Even if the data-transmission delay (TA) is smaller than the others, the next data transmission A has to wait until the next clock cycle to be processed. The shaded blocks indicate that the waiting time of each block. The worst-case restriction degrades the throughput of stochastic decoding.
3.2. Asynchronous decoding
To alleviate the clock-related problems, we have proposed asynchronous decoding for min-sum computation [13–16] and for stochastic computation [11]. Fig. 3 shows a timing diagram of the two-input variable node in asynchronous stochastic decoding. TA and TB are data-transmission delays between variable and check nodes, and TOUT is a variablenode computation delay. In asynchronous stochastic decoding, since node computations are performed by using the most recent available messages rather than all updated messages at a global level that are required for stochastic decoding, nodes do not need to wait for the all updated messages including from tardy long wires, but can proceed when ready. Asynchronous stochastic decoding can be implemented by using asynchronous circuits [17, 18]. In asynchronous circuits, local control signals are generated based on handshaking and are used instead of a global clock signal. Since the asynchronous circuits are controlled using a requestacknowledge-based handshaking protocol, the transmission delay via the interleaver is determined by local wiring delay characteristics. However, one operation generally takes four steps because of handshaking [18], which degrades the speed. Moreover, the hardware due to the local handshaking generally becomes larger than the synchronous circuits.
3.3. Clockless decoding
In clockless stochastic decoding, we completely eliminate the local handshaking of asynchronous stochastic decoding. The update algorithm of variable and check nodes is the same as for asynchronous stochastic decoding: computations are performed by using the most recent available messages. 4. HARDWARE IMPLEMENTATION
Fig. 5 shows a circuit diagram of 3-input variable node in clockless stochastic decoding. There are two operation modes based on U. If U is ‘1’, a new bit is generated through a three input AND gate and becomes the output using a multiplexer. It corresponds to the 3-input extension of equation (1). In addition, an edge memory (EM) is updated based on an input condition. The EM [8, 9] generates a random value based on the stored data to reduce the possibility of “lock-up”, which degrades a stochastic decoder’s error-correcting capability. In clockless stochastic decoding, there is no clock signal to control the EM. A pulse generator generates a pulse signal to control the EM using U. If U is ‘0’, the output bit is randomly selected from EM. The random signal is usually generated by a linear feedback shift register (LFSR).

5. TIMING MODEL FOR BER SIMULATION
In this section we describe our timing model for BER simulations using clockless stochastic decoding. In clockless stochastic decoding, there is no periodic timing that can generally model behaviours of circuits in higher level than gate-level descriptions [19]. 7. DISCUSSION
Error floors in stochastic decoding occur due to “lock-up” that stops decoding before the convergence. The “lock-up” problem comes from short cycles in LDPC codes and timing randomness of updating outputs in computation nodes. Ideally, each stochastic stream has to be independently generated [7], but they are not actually independent due to the short cycles. Once a synchronous stochastic decoder is in a “lock-up” state, it uses the same pattern of random inputs due to simultaneous updating in all computation nodes. As the “lock-up” state strongly depends on input patterns, the synchronous stochastic decoder tends to be in a “lock-up” state even if it uses EMs that operates on a specific cycle delay.
The reduction of error floors in the proposed decoding method is closely related to parallel asynchronous iterative algorithms [20–22] in parallel and distributed computing. In that field, asynchronous methods have demonstrated better efficiency than synchronous ones. The update timing of the asynchronous iterative algorithms is related to data arrival and has similar characteristic to the proposed stochastic decoding. The lack of synchronization gives a chance for the decoder to use different input patterns that might get rid of “lock-up” in conventional stochastic decoding. Note that asynchronous stochastic decoding has local update timing, but still has local synchronization, which indicates the update timing is independent of data arrival.
To compare with the previous work [11], the proposed stochastic decoder would be implemented with less hardware area than that of the asynchronous stochastic decoder. It is because the asynchronous stochastic decoder requires additional local controler circuits that the proposed decoder doesn’t require, which would also reduce power dissipation. Since there are not handshake operations in the proposed stochastic decoder, the throughput would be improved compared with that of the asynchronous stochatic decocer.
8. CONCLUSION
We have proposed a clockless stochastic decoding technique for parallel LDPC decoding. We model clockless stochastic decoding in a BER simulation and evaluate the BER performance, where the transistor-level simulation results under a 90nm CMOS technology are used for the timing model. The proposed decoding scheme achieves superior BER performance compared with the conventional synchronous decoding in regular (32, 16) and (1024, 512) LDPC codes due to the lack of synchronization, which would eliminate “lockup”. In the future prospect, we will design and implement the circuit of the proposed stochastic decoder and compare with other related works.
