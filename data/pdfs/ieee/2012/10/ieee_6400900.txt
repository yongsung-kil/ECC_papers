Mixed-Scheduling Belief Propagation for LDPC Decoders in the Magnetic Recording Systems

Watid Phakphisut and Pornchai Supnithi
Faculty of Engineering, King Mongkut’s Institute of Technology Ladkrabang, Bangkok 10520, Thailand s4610134@kmitl.ac.th, ksupornc@kmitl.ac.th

Abstract— Serial belief propagation is known to improve the decoding performance of the low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes through the modified sequence of check node or variable node update. The sequence updates or scheduling can be performed in two directions: a left-to-right (LR) direction or the right-to-left (RL) one. In this work, we show that even when both update directions yield comparable bit error rate (BER) performances, the error locations may differ. Therefore, we propose a mixed-scheduling belief propagation (MBP) which combines the reliability information obtained from the LR and RL directions. Simulation results show that the MBP outperforms the current serial schedulings by providing faster convergence as well as lower bit error rates in the bit patterned media system which is a candidate for future magnetic recording systems.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] is shown to exhibit the performance approaching the Shannon limit in an additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel [2]. These codes have been widely employed in digital communication systems such as wireless LAN [3], and magnetic recording systems [4] among others.
An LDPC code, a kind of linear block codes, is based on a sparse parity-check matrix. To decode the LDPC codes, the belief-propagation (BP) algorithm is a well-known soft iterative decoding algorithm [2]. To improve the convergence speed of LDPC decoding, serial schedulings [5-10] and informed dynamic schedulings [11-14] have recently been proposed. For the serial schedulings, the check nodes are updated according to the numbered sequence of check nodes, while the updated check nodes can be performed in the increasing or decreasing orders in every iterative decoding proceeds. In [10], the authors further improve the performance of the serial schedulings by applying the opposite check node update directions for the even and odd iterations. For the informed dynamic schedulings, the updated check nodes are indicated by the residual (the difference of information from check node to variable node in the current iteration from the previous iteration). In [7], the serial scheduling (also known as serial belief propagation) is shown to have the same decoding complexity per iteration as flooding scheduling. However, the informed dynamic scheduling which requires the residual generation and ordering of the residual has a very high complexity [14].

Recently, in [15], the serial belief propagation such as layered belief propagation (LBP) [5-7] and shuffled belief propagation (SBP) [8-10] have been proposed in an ultra-high density magnetic recording system called the bit patterned media (BPM) system. The results show that both the LBP and SBP give the fast convergence speed and they significantly improve the bit error rate performance under various media noise levels. However, the performance of LDPC decoding in the written-in error channel [16-18] that is an important challenge in the ultra-high density magnetic recording system has not been studied. In [19], it is also shown that LBP for nonbinary LDPC codes over GF(q) provides the better performance than the conventional LDPC decoding in conventional magnetic recording systems.
In this work, we analyze the reliabilities obtained from a left-to-right(LR) and right-to-left (RL) directions of the sequential updates. In the channel with and without written-in error situation, the results show that even for comparable bit error rates, the error locations may be different. Therefore, we propose the mixed-scheduling belief propagation (MBP) that uses the mixture of these reliabilities of variable nodes. Our work differs from [10] in that the check updates in the RL and LR directions are made at all iterations, only when making hard decision, the mixture of reliabilities from the opposite directions are obtained. The performances of the proposed algorithm show that the MBP gives the faster convergence speed as well as the better bit error rate performances than the serial belief propagation in the BPM system with and without written-in error.
This paper is organized as follows. In Section II, we review the serial belief propagation for LDPC decoder. In section III, the reliabilities of serial scheduling are analyzed and the mixed scheduling method is proposed. The simulation results and discussions are in Section IV. Finally, in Section V, we give the conclusions.
II. SERIAL BELIEF PROPAGATION FOR LDPC CODES

The ith codeword bit is represented by the variable node vi , and the jth parity bits are represented by the check node cj. In the BP algorithm (or the flooding scheduling), the check nodes propagate the information or reliabilities to all the variable nodes simultaneously. To improve the convergence speed of LDPC decoding, serial belief propagation [5-10] as an alternative to the BP algorithm has recently been studied. The serial scheduling improves the decoding performance of the LDPC codes through the modified sequence of check nodes update which are different from the flooding scheduling. The serial scheduling can be divided into 2 main types: layered belief propagation (LBP) [5-7] and shuffled belief propagation (SBP) [8-10].
In the LBP algorithm, the reliabilities propagated from the check nodes are sorted by the numbered sequence of the check nodes. Each check node is first updated completely, then the message of the connected variable nodes will propagate to the check node before the remaining check nodes can start. On the other hand, for SBP algorithm, the reliability updates of check nodes are indicated by the numbered sequence of the variable nodes. The specific message from the check node will propagate to the variable nodes. Unlike the LBP, all messages from all the check node propagates simultaneously. An iteration of LBP and SBP will be counted after each message from check node is updated completely. Both the LBP and SBP algorithms can be summarized in Table II and III.

III. MIXED-SCHEDULING FOR BELIEF PROPAGATION
In the serial belief propagation, the most recent information (from the previous check node update) is used to change the reliability in the last of a series. Hence, LBP and SBP have more information than BP when propagating the reliability values to variable nodes. For the LBP with left-toright direction of check node update (LBP-LR), the reliability on the left side will affect the reliability on the right side following the direction of the sequential update. On the other hand, for the LBP with right-to-left direction of check node update (LBP-RL), the reliability on the right side will affect that on the left side. In Fig. 1, The LBP-LR passes the information from c1 to v1, v6 and v9. Afterward, the information is sent from the variable nodes connected to c3, c4 and c6. Therefore, the reliability of check node c1 is hidden inside of the message that is propagated from c3, c4 and c6. Figure 2 shows the relationship of the check nodes in the updated procedures of LBP algorithm based on Fig. 1. The solid line is a route that is used to pass the reliabilities from a check node to its neighborhood. In the updated procedures of LBP-LR, the check node update proceeds from c1 to c6, the reliabilities of the node c1 and c2 are used to modify the reliability of c3. Hence, the check node c3 may not account for some important information from the check nodes c4, c5 and c6. On the other hand, for LBP-RL decoding, the node c3 will obtain information from the node c5 and c6.

A. Complexity Issues
For efficient implementations, the forward-backward algorithm [21] is the method used to compute the information from between the check nodes and the variable nodes. The hyperbolic tangent core operation and addition are required to compute the information from the check node and variable node. Both the BP and LBP decoding require 3(dcí2) hyperbolic tangent core operations and 2dsí1 additions, but the SBP requires dc(dcí2) core operations and 2dsí1 additions for the same task. For the MBP algorithm, since the decoding is based on the serial scheduling of belief-propagation, it requires a similar hardware complexity to LBP and SBP, but MBP exhibits more latency in computing the updated check nodes and variable nodes than both the LBP and the SBP. To have comparable latency to the serial schedulings, the MBP

can implement the updates in both directions simultaneously, but will need about 1.5 times of the computational operations of the LBP or SBP. Specifically, the MBP-C method requires 6(dcí2) core operations and 2dsí1 addition, while MBP-V requires 2dc(dcí2) core operations and 2dsí1 addition.
IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
This section shows the performance of the proposed MBP method for the LDPC decoder. In the simulation, we use the binary random LDPC code that is constructed using the progressive-edge-growth algorithm [22] with the degree of variable nodes of 3 and the degree of check nodes from 26 to 29. The block size is 4,608 and the code rate considered is 8/9, both of which approximate the sector size and the code rate used in magnetic recording system [4]. All the simulations are plotted in terms of the bit error rate (BER) versus signal-tonoise ratio (SNR) in terms of Eb/N0, where Eb is the average bit energy and N0 is a one-sided power spectral density of an additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN).
We first make the performance comparison of various decoding schemes in an AWGN channel when the SNR is fixed at 4.25 dB. In Fig. 3, the results reveal that the serial belief propagation yield the better performances than the conventional LDPC decoding. It can be seen that both the LBP-LR and SBP-LR perform almost indistinguishably from LBP-RL and SBP-RL, respectively. The MBP shows the bit error rate improvement over other types of LDPC decoding at all iterations. The performance of MBP after 10 iterations is equivalent to that of LBP after 20 iterations at the BER = 2×10-6. Figure 4 shows the comparison of the probability of bit in error at various bit locations of LBP-LR, LBP-RL and MBP-C after 20 decoding iterations. The probability of bit in error is obtained by simulating many data sectors and keep track of the errors at each location. Even though LBP-LR and LBP-RL exhibit similar bit error rates, the errors may appear at different locations. Some bits are less likely to be corrected than others. For example, the bit #1030 shows the probability of bit in error of 1.12×10-5 and 0.56×10-5 when using LBP-LR and LBP-RL, respectively. On the other hand, from the simulation, no error occurs at this particular bit when the mixture of reliabilities from opposite directions is used in the decoding of the same LDPC code.

The bit pattern media (BPM) recording system is a promising option for future ultra-high density magnetic recording system [16]. The user data bits are stored on magnetic islands isolated by non-magnetic material. Due to the size and position fluctuations of the islands, the write field gradients and the demagnetization variability, two error conditions may occur. One is the switching of the previously written bits, the other is the failure to write the desired bits. These errors are known as written-in errors [16]. In [17, 18], the written-in error channel is modeled as a BSC channel with the crossover probability p. A discrete-time approach for the BPM read/write process can be modeled as BSC-AWGN channel [18].
In Fig. 5, we evaluate the performance of the MBP in a cascaded BSC-AWGN channel with p = 0.0005 at SNR = 5.5 dB, the MBP offers the significant BER improvement over the serial scheduling. For example, at BER = 2×10-6, the MBP requires 5 iterations as opposed to 20 iterations for serial belief propagation (or less than 4 times). In Fig. 6, for the probability p = 0.001 at SNR = 6.65 dB, the performance of the MBP after 5 iterations is equivalent to that of LBP and SBP after 30 iterations (or less than 6 times) at BER = 2×10-6. Herein, the errors in the BSC channel directly affect the reliabilities of the sum-product algorithm; however, these effects can be tackled by the mixed reliability technique in the MBP. The MBP requires a comparable hardware complexity but more latency than the LBP and the SBP. For comparable latency, the number of iterations of MBP should be divided by a factor of 1.5 as discussed in the previous section.
The performances of the BP detector and its variants in the cascaded BSC-AWGN channel are shown in Fig. 7. The simulation shows that the MBP slightly outperforms the rest. Without written-in errors, the crossover probability is 0, then the SNR gains of the MBP over the LBP and the SBP are about 0.1 dB at BER = 4×10-7. With p = 0.0005 and 0.001, MBP offers significantly improved bit error rate performances having the SNR gains of 0.3 dB and 0.4 dB, respectively, at BER = 2×10-6.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we propose a mixed scheduling for belief propagation (MBP) suitable for the improvement of LDPC decoder. The method is based on a mixture of reliabilities from left-to-right and right-to-left directions of the serial scheduling strategies. We have analyzed the difference of reliabilities in left-to-right and right-to-left sequences of the sequential update algorithm. Even when both types exhibit comparable bit error rate performances, the error locations may differ. The simulation results show that when compared with the LBP and SBP, the MBP yields a faster convergence speed and better bit error rate performances in bit patterned media recording system with and without written-in error situations.
