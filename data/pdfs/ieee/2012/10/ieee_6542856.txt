Combined Modified Weighted Bit-Flipping Decoding of Low-Density Parity-Check Codes
Haiyi Huang, Yige Wang and Gang Wei School of Electronic and Information Engineering
South China University of Technology Email: huanghaiyi2012@gmail.com, eeygwang, ecgwei@scut.edu.cn

AbstractŌĆöIn this paper, we propose a combined modified weighted bit-flipping (CM-WBF) algorithm for low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. The new algorithm combines modified weighted bit-flipping (M-WBF) with low complexity weighted bit-flipping (LC-WBF). Simulation results show that the new algorithm offers a better trade-off among performance, complexity, and convergence compared with M-WBF, LC-WBF and RR-WBF algorithms for finite geometry LDPC (FG-LDPC) codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes were first proposed by Gallager in 1960s [1][2] and brought into prominence by MacKay and Neal [3][4]. They are currently one of the best codes that can achieve near-Shannon-limit error performance. LDPC codes can be decoded by hard-decision, soft-decision or hybrid decoding algorithms. Among these schemes, weighted bit-flipping (WBF) algorithms offer a good tradeoff between performance, complexity and speed, and have received significant attention.
Several WBF algorithms have been proposed in recent years, such as standard weighted bit-flipping [5], modified weighted bit-flipping (M-WBF) [6], low complexity weighted bit-flipping (LC-WBF, also called LP-WBF in some literature) [7], reliability ratio based weighted bit-flipping (RRWBF) [8], fast modified weighted bit-flipping (FM-WBF) [9], improved modified weighted bit-flipping (IM-WBF) [10] and fast improved modified weighted bit-flipping (FIM-WBF)[11]. Except FM-WBF and FIM-WBF algorithms, all weighted bitflipping algorithms generally flip only one bit per iteration. This sequential process often results in slow convergence. The IM-WBF and FIM-WBF algorithms offer better performance compared with other WBF schemes, but they require higher computational complexity. Therefore, an improved WBF algorithm that achieves a better trade-off among performance, convergence, and complexity is desired.
In this paper, we propose a combined modified weighted bit-flipping (CM-WBF) algorithm for LDPC codes. The new algorithm combines M-WBF with LC-WBF. Simulation results show that CM-WBF offers better performance, and in the meantime the average number of iterations for convergence is reduced by half compared with M-WBF, LC-WBF and RRWBF algorithms.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. In Section II, we review some existing WBF algorithms, including standard WBF, M-WBF, LC-WBF, etc. The CM-WBF algorithm is

introduced in Section III. In Section IV we show some simulation results which verify that CM-WBF offers better performance and faster convergence compared with other WBF algorithms with similar decoding complexity. Finally, conclusions are drawn in Section V.

During decoding, CM-WBF usually finds two bits to flip at each iteration, especially for the first few iterations. This benefits decoding from two aspects. First, compared with other WBF algorithms that flip one bit each time, CM-WBF corrects more errors at each iteration with high probability. Therefore, it converges faster. Secondly, an important reason for decoding failure of the WBF algorithms is that the decoding process is trapped into an infinite loop, i.e., selecting and flipping the same bit back and forth until the maximum number of iterations is reached [7]. The CM-WBF algorithm uses two criteria to search for the least reliable bit, which means that it can break the infinite loop with high probability. Compared with other WBF algorithms, CM-WBF apparently has higher computational complexity. Table II shows the computational complexity comparison of different WBF algorithms for (N, K)(dv, dc) FG-LDPC codes. Here, we focus on decoding complexity at each iteration. The complexity of initialization has not been taken into account since it is only performed once. For all WBF algorithms, decoding at each iteration consists of three steps: (1) calculating the syndrome; (2) updating En; (3) selecting bits to flip.
Use standard WBF for example. In Step 1, after bit n is selected and flipped at the previous iteration, smŌĆÖs that correspond to the dv checks in which bit n participates should be updated. Then, for each m Ōłł M(n), we update En for all n Ōłł N (m) in Step 2. Then the computational complexity of Steps 1 and 2 is dv ┬Ę dc additions. Next in Step 3, one bit of the length-N bit vector z is flipped. To select this bit based on En, (N ŌłÆ 1) times of comparison are needed. Then the computational complexity of Step 3 is (N ŌłÆ 1) since comparisons can be considered as additions. In total, we need dv ┬Ę dc + N ŌłÆ 1 additions. We could use similar analysis to obtain the complexity of other WBF algorithms.
Although the computational complexity of CM-WBF at each iteration is about twice as much as that of LC-WBF or M-WBF, it is shown in Section IV that with the same total complexity, CM-WBF offers better performance compared with M-WBF, LC-WBF and RR-WBF.
