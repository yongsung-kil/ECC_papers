How far are LDPC codes from fundamental limits on total power consumption?
Karthik Ganesan‚Ä†, Pulkit Grover‚Ä°, Andrea Goldsmith‚Ä° ‚Ä†-University of California, Berkeley ‚Ä°-Stanford University karthik.ganesan@berkeley.edu, {pulkit@,andrea@wsl.}stanford.edu

Abstract‚ÄĒ In order to minimize the transmit power in a communication system, it is well known that it is optimal to operate close to the channel capacity. Recently derived fundamental limits show that this strategy is not optimal when the goal is to minimize total power consumption, i.e., transmit as well as processing power, because of high decoding complexity close to capacity. This paper investigates how close LDPC codes with corresponding message-passing decoders get to the fundamental limits. We focus on some one-bit decoding algorithms, namely Gallager-A and Gallager-B, to decode LDPC codes. We compute the minimum total (transmit and decoding) power consumed using these two decoding algorithms for two decoding models: first where only the computational nodes consume all the power (the ‚ÄúNode Model‚ÄĚ), and next where wires consume all the power (the ‚ÄúWire Model‚ÄĚ). For each model and each decoding algorithm, we compare this minimum total power with the (transmit) power required for uncoded transmission and with fundamental lower bounds on power consumption for these models. In some cases, we observe that the transmit power needs to increase unboundedly in order for the total power to be asymptotically (as Pe ‚Üí 0) smaller than that for uncoded transmission. We also observe that the power consumed in the Wire Model asymptotically dominates the power consumed in the Node Model, which suggests the importance of characterizing the wiring complexity of decoding, and not just the number of operations.
I. INTRODUCTION
In his seminal paper [1], Shannon‚Äôs focus on channel capacity under a transmit power constraint helped to illuminate the long-distance communication problems of the day. Today, communication is often over significantly shorter distances. Empirically, it has been observed that at these short distances, the power required for processing the signal can dominate that required for transmission, often by orders of magnitude [2]. Thus, the focus on the transmit power constraints, typically captured via SNR, does not abstract all the relevant power consumed in today‚Äôs systems. There is a very practical need today to model and analyze the effects of processing power.
Processing power is a strange beast: it depends strongly on the communication strategy (modulation choice, constellation-size, coding strategy, equalization strategy, etc.). Even for a fixed communication strategy, it depends strongly on the implementation technology (e.g. 45 nm CMOS) and choice of circuit architectures (e.g. placement of computational nodes and routing of wires). Understandably, there has been little theoretical work in understanding this

processing power. The first simplifying perspective was adopted in [3], [4], where all of the processing power terms at the transmitter and the receiver are lumped together and are assumed to be constant independent of the communication strategy. It was shown that the transmissions need to be ‚Äúbursty,‚ÄĚ but not too ‚Äúbursty,‚ÄĚ in order to balance between transmit and processing power. But the devil is in the details: system designers do change the coding strategy with the distance, which is an evidence of the fact that the coding strategy strongly influences the processing power. For instance, at small distances, e.g. on chip communication, inter-processor communication, short distance wired communication [5], and even indoor wireless communication [6], system designers shun capacity-approaching codes (e.g. the [5]), and sometimes even use uncoded transmission [6]. To develop a thorough theoretical understanding of processing power and its tradeoffs with transmit power, we need more refined models that abstract the impact of code-choice on processing power.
In practice, code-choice has a strong impact on the encoding and decoding algorithms and implementations, and thus abstracting encoding and decoding power is a reasonable point to start. What would be a good model for encoding/decoding power? Turing‚Äôs abstraction suggests one way forward: it is not difficult to count the number of operations (in an order sense) required to decode a given code with a specified decoding algorithm. It is standard in coding theory to quantify the required number of operations for many codes and encoding/decoding algorithms (e.g. see [7], [8], [9]). But how close is the connection between number of operations and power consumed in the encoding/decoding circuitry?
Towards obtaining an answer for this question, in this paper we focus on low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes introduced by Gallager in his Ph.D. thesis [10]. The choice is motived by practical utility as well as ease of modeling. LDPC codes, and related sparse-graph codes, are widely used in practice today, both over short (e.g. the 10-GBASE-T standard for communication within a data-center [2]) and long distances (e.g. deep-space communication [11]).
Within our restriction of coding family and decoding algorithms, and under our simplifying assumptions, we provide the following insights:
