ABSTRACT
Optimal quantizers for channel output are especially necessary for very high speed and memory-cell sensing applications where high-precision analog-to-digital converters are not available. This paper investigates a method to find optimal output quantizers for a binary input (BI) additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel when regular low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are used with belief-propagation (BP) decoding. The optimal quantizers are found by uniparametric and multi-parametric optimization methods based on the discretized density evolution (DE). The capacity of various LDPC code ensembles are also provided with the BI-AWGN channel outputs quantized using these optimal quantizers. It is demonstrated that only 3 bits of quantization precision performs almost within 0.1 dB of the infiniteprecision channel output. The effect of the code rate and the degree distribution of LDPC codes on the optimal quantization boundaries is analyzed.
Index Terms— LDPC codes, belief-propagation, AWGN channel, quantization
1. INTRODUCTION
The belief-propagation (BP) decoding of low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes shows extremely good error performance when channel produces high-precision output [1]-[2]. In very high speed applications, however, few bits of precision is only available for channel output due to the limitation of analog-to-digital converters (ADCs). For example, the read latency of NAND flash memory increases exponentially with the sensing precision as a consequence of fully serial sensing operations [3][4], and thus not more than three bits

are desired in general for the sensing precision. Also, for forward error correction (FEC) of optical transmission systems, a 2-bit quantizer operating at 32 Gsample/s is the fastest up to date [5]. Therefore, designing an optimal low-precision quantizer is of great significance to achieve the best error correcting performance. In [6], low-precision non-uniform quantizers attaining the AWGN channel capacity have been presented for various signal-to-noise ratios (SNRs), but no error control coding was considered, which must be incorporated in reliable communication systems.
In this paper, we present the optimal erasure, 2-bit, and 3bit quantizers1 for BP decoding of regular LDPC codes when using the BI-AWGN channels. The post-FEC bit error rate (BER) of a specific quantizer is evaluated utilizing the density evolution (DE) [7][2][8], and the hill climbing method is applied to optimize the quantizer. It is demonstrated that these quantizers are superior to the Lloyd quantizers, which are generally regarded as optimal in the sense of mean square error (MSE) and in the absence of FEC. Furthermore, it is shown that the optimality is greatly dependent on the code rate and degree distribution.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. We briefly introduce the BI-AWGN channel model and find the 2-bit optimal Lloyd quantizer for the BI-AWGN channel output in Section 2. In Section 3, the discretized DE for BP decoding is described and optimal quantizers are exhaustively searched for using the DE. The dependency of optimal quantizers on the code rate and the degree distribution is analyzed in Section 4. Finally, Section 5 concludes this paper.

3. OPTIMAL QUANTIZERS FOR BP DECODING
For BP decoding, we suppose infinite block length and infinite decoding iterations. Also we assume infinite-precision (realized by floating-point arithmetic) messages exchanged between variable and check nodes; this is because much more bits can be generally exploited within a decoder than those for the channel output. The discretized DE is utilized to evaluate quantizers, which is an efficient tool to estimate the asymptotic performance of BP decoding [8]. A particular noise variance can be examined by the DE whether to arrive at an arbitrarily small decoding error rate with the iterative message passing algorithm. The noise threshold, i.e., the maximum noise variance reaching error-free decoding, can be used as an explicit performance metric for the quantizer.

3.2. Multi-Parametric Search for Optimal Non-uniform Quantizer
Finding an optimal non-uniform 3-bit quantizer is a multiparametric optimization problem of q1, q2, and q3. As a good starting point, we set q2 of the non-uniform 3-bit quantizer by q1∗ of the 2-bit quantizer. The other parameters q1 and q3 are set to some real numbers within (0, q2) and (q2, ∞), respectively; e.g., we set q1 = 0.5 × q2 and q3 = 1.5 × q2. Starting from these initial parameters, we tweak q1 first to find a local minimum σ existing on a uni-parametric subspace of q1, then sequentially vary q3 and q2 with the other parameters fixed. By repeating such a uni-parametric optimization for all parameters in rotation, we can find an optimal 3-bit quantizer. Fig. 4 illustrates the concave-like noise threshold surface for the rate-1/2 (3, 6)-regular LDPC codes with the fixed q2 = 0.74 and varying q1 and q3, where the first two optimization iterations are designated by the arrow lines. We can find a higher-precision quantizer in such a hill-climbing manner from the initial boundaries including those of the lowerprecision quantizer.

4. ANALYSIS OF OPTIMAL QUANTIZERS
In this section, we investigate why different quantization boundaries perform best for different code rates. It is apparent that the noise power is a significant factor that changes the optimal quantizer. For the case of the Lloyd quantizers, the MSE is determined by the pdf of y and therefore the small noise threshold of high rate codes naturally leads to narrow optimal quantization boundaries. The 2-bit Lloyd quantizers yield q1∗Lloyd = 1.30 and 1.02 for the noise thresholds of the rate-0.33 (4, 6)-regular and the rate-0.875 (3, 24)regular LDPC, respectively. On the other hand, the DE-based searches produce q1∗ = 0.82 and 0.41 for these codes as can be seen in Table 1. The ratio of the optimal boundaries are 1.30/1.02 = 1.27 for the Lloyd quantizer and 0.82/0.41 = 2 for the DE-based quantizer. Therefore, when comparing those ratios as well as the absolute boundary values, there must be more than the noise power that affects the quantizer’s optimality.
Another reason of finer optimal boundaries for higher-rate codes illustrated in Fig. 6 can be more clearly explained by the min-sum (MS) approximation of the BP algorithm [11]. In the MS decoding, the outgoing message of a check node has the minimum magnitude of all incoming messages except that from the variable node to receive the message. Consider 2-bit quantization for example. Then, at the first iteration, the outgoing message of a check node has the large magnitude r2 only if all incoming messages have the magnitude r2.

In other words, if any variable node has a magnitude r1, the check node has the magnitude r1. Therefore, since more variable nodes are associated with a check node in a higher rate
code, the outgoing message of a higher rate code has a larger probability of having magnitude r1 unless we use different quantizers. For example, when we fix the variable node degree at 3, and if ϕ−1 + ϕ1 = 0.274 and ϕ−2 + ϕ2 = 0.726 for some q1, the probabilities that an outgoing message is r2 are 0.20 and 2.9 × 10−7 for the check node node degrees of 6 and 48, respectively. It can be easily predicted that a similar behavior is observed for the case of BP decoding, though the
concentration of check node messages near origin is somewhat relieved. This suggests that q1 should be decreased for high check node degrees to avoid the concentration of check
node messages near origin, which results in denser quantiza-
tion boundaries. Illustrated in Figs. 8 and 9 are the pmf’s p(v2) of the outgo-
ing messages v from a variable node after the second BP decoding iteration for the (3, 6) and (3, 60)-LDPC codes, respectively. Several q1’s are tested under the maximum AWGN channel parameters σ∗’s. The concentrated input pmf’s p(u1) caused by too large q1’s for the check node degree dc produce repetitions of concentrated pulse as the output pmf’s pv(2), since the outgoing messages are obtained by dv −1 times convolution of pu(1) with pu0 , where dv is the variable node degree. On the other hand, the reduced optimal q1∗’s (i.e., 0.74 and 0.34 for the (3, 6) and (3, 60) codes, respectively) result in Gaussian-like output pmf’s which are much similar to those of the unquantized channel output3, which then leads

5. CONCLUDING REMARKS
In this paper, we presented the optimal erasure, 2-bit, and 3bit quantizers for the BI-AWGN channel output when LDPC codes are used in conjunction with BP decoding. It was found that the optimal choice of quantization boundaries greatly varies depending on the degree distribution as well as the code rate. Furthermore, we demonstrated that with only 3-bit precision of the channel output the BP decoding shows very close performance of the unquantized channel output for a wide range of degree distribution pairs. On the other hand, the Lloyd quantizer with the minimum MSE leads to far from the optimal error performance. With the results presented in this paper, we can achieve the highest reliability of very high speed communication and storage systems when LDPC codes are used and low precision is only available for the channel output.
