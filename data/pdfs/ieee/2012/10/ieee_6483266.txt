Abstract—We consider the decoding of regular low density parity-check codes with a Gallager-B message-passing algorithm built exclusively from faulty computing devices. We propose an extension of the Gallager-B algorithm where messages can be repeated to provide increased fault tolerance, and use EXIT functions to derive its average performance. Thresholds are obtained both for the channel quality and the faultiness of the decoder. We argue that decoding complexity is central to the analysis of faulty decoding and compare the complexity of decoding with a faulty decoder instead of a reliable decoder, for a fixed channel condition and residual error rate. Finally, we show that when the message repetitions in the extended Gallager-B algorithm are scheduled optimally, a small complexity overhead with respect to a reliable decoder provides large gains in fault tolerance.
I. INTRODUCTION
The problem of computing with unreliable devices was first studied in the early days of computers [1], but subsequently lost some of its importance as techniques were developed to build integrated circuits that were sufficiently reliable for most applications. Recently, the topic has received increased attention for a variety of reasons. Sophisticated computing devices are making their way into various domains where a very high reliability is required, such as in medical, aerospace, or automotive applications. The main existing technique for achieving a very high reliability is modular redundancy, where computing modules are duplicated to form a repetition code. This is costly in terms of circuit area and power consumption, and there is an incentive for finding more efficient approaches. More generally, allowing higher error rates in computing devices is seen as a promising way of improving their energy efficiency. For instance, as feature size shrinks in CMOS circuits, the variability inherent to the fabrication process becomes harder to manage, and relaxing the reliability requirement could allow designing circuits more aggressively to save power. Another related approach is to design a circuit such that timing constraint violations are permitted for some input sequences, which allows to reduce the supply voltage, and therefore the power consumption [2]. Furthermore, variability issues in integrated circuits are expected to keep worsening, and fault tolerance could become a required property for all circuit designs [3].
It seems likely that error correction codes such as low density parity-check (LDPC) codes could be used to decrease the cost of achieving a certain reliability in an information

processing system. For circuits that only perform an identity function, such as buses and memories, the application of error control coding is straightforward. However, if coding is used to provide fault tolerance in a circuit, it is necessary to understand how the performance of the code is affected by a faulty decoding device.
The case of a one-step majority logic decoder built using faulty XOR gates, but reliable majority circuits, was considered in [4]. This decoder is equivalent to a single iteration of the Gallager-A algorithm. Thanks to the relative simplicity of the decoder, the authors were able to derive an analytical expression for the bit error rate of any finitelength LDPC code having a girth of at least 6. The problem was also considered in [5] in the context of designing faulttolerant memories built exclusively out of faulty devices. Some numerical results for a faulty message-passing decoder were presented. A detailed asymptotic analysis of LDPC code ensembles under faulty decoding was performed in [6]. One of the decoding algorithms considered is “Gallager-A”, where deviations occurring in the decoder are modeled by considering that messages are passed over binary symmetric channels with cross-over probability α. Using density evolution, the author shows that for α ą 0, it is not possible to achieve an arbitrarily small error probability. Instead, the channel threshold is characterized for a decoder achieving a small error probability η. Finally, an analysis of faulty decoding of regular LDPC codes using Gallager-B was presented in [7], where an approximate expression is provided for the residual error rate achieved by the decoder. However, in the model proposed therein, the residual error rate is measured immediately after considering the deviations in the variable node processor, which is not equivalent to the model used in [6] or to the model that we will use. Our model appears somewhat more flexible as it allows considering the case of a decoder that computes unreliably while iterating, but that can still generate a reliable final output.
In this paper, we use error-probability EXIT functions to study regular LDPC ensembles decoded with a faulty Gallager-B decoder. We present an extension of the GallagerB algorithm that can trade decoding complexity for reliability by repeating the messages. Underlying our approach is the belief that decoding complexity is central to the analysis of faulty decoders. Decoding complexity is also a concern when the decoder is assumed reliable, but it should be

To characterize the performance of message-passing LDPC decoders, we are interested in tracking the average error correction performance on an iteration-by-iteration basis, which can be achieved by assuming that the code graph is cycle-free, and by tracking the distribution of the messages. C. Thresholds

The Gallager-B algorithm admits a number of variable node functions, depending on the value of b, and a different choice of function can be made at each iteration. We can express this choice by using a sequence S of parameters, where Srts specifies the parameter to be used in decoding iteration t. This sequence will be referred to as a rule schedule. We will say that the decoding algorithm achieves an error probability pres if and only if there exists a rule schedule with finite length L such that prLs ď pres.
For reliable decoders, the channel threshold is defined as the worst channel parameter ˚ for which the decoder can achieve an arbitrarily small error probability [10]. In the case of a faulty decoder, it is not necessarily possible to achieve an arbitrarily small error probability. Instead, we will define the threshold of a faulty decoder as the worst channel parameter ˚ppresq for which the decoder can achieve a given residual error rate pres. IV. OPTIMAL REPETITION SCHEDULE
For some n and pres, the curve po˚pφeq defines a region in terms of pφe, poq where the faulty decoder can achieve the error rate target. We would now like to consider how the decoding complexity compares for a reliable and a faulty decoder for some point pφe, poq inside this achievability region.

A simple yet meaningful measure of decoding complexity is to consider the total number of arithmetic operations that are required to decode one codeword, on average. In this section, we use this measure to compare the decoding complexity for a reliable and a faulty Gallager-B decoder, when both decoders share the same channel error probability po and the same residual error rate constraint pres.
Let An be the number of arithmetic operations required to compute one iteration of the Gallager-B algorithm with n message repetitions. We can express this number in terms of the number E of edges, the number Nv of variable nodes and the number Nc of check nodes. A decoding iteration can be split into three parts: 1) message exchange, 2) check node function evaluation, 3) variable node function evaluation. Even though sending a message is not, strictly speaking, an arithmetic operation, we count two operations for sending a one bit message on a given edge, from a variable node to a check node, and back to a variable node. The number of operations for the message passing is therefore 2nE. The motivation for including the message passing in the number of operations is that it is an important contributor to the complexity of a circuit implementation. For both the check node and the variable node functions, an efficient implementation will compute all output messages simultaneously. Also, when repeating messages, each function is used n times. At a check node, we compute the total parity using pdc ´ 1q operations, and then subtract the intrinsic parity in order to send the extrinsic parity on each of the dc outgoing edge. The number of operations used for the check node computation is therefore nNcp2dc´1q. The computation at the variable node is given by (12). First, dv “edge sums” (the sums in j) are computed, which requires dvpn ´ 1q operations. We then use pdv´1q operations to compute the total of all inputs, followed by dv subtractions to obtain the extrinsic sums, followed by dv comparisons with b to generate the output messages, for a total of nNvppn ` 2qdv ´ 1q operations. B. Overhead of Faulty Decoding
We are interested in evaluating the complexity of decoding with a faulty decoder, as opposed to a reliable decoder. To this end we define the cost CS of some schedule S of length L as the sum of the cost of each of the L decoding rules, where the cost of a rule is as defined in the first paragraph of IV-A. The overhead of a schedule S2 over a schedule S1 is then CS2 {CS1 ´ 1.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we considered the problem of decoding an LDPC code when all computations performed in the decoder are faulty, concentrating on the Gallager-B binary message passing algorithm. Under a cycle-free assumption, the average performance of this decoder can be derived exactly using EXIT functions. We presented an extension of the GallagerB algorithm that can trade complexity for reliability at every iteration, and derived its EXIT function. The performance of the extended algorithm was characterized in terms of a channel threshold and of a decoder-deviation threshold. We then proceeded to find how to optimally schedule the decoding rules in every iteration to minimize the decoding complexity. Our optimization results show that despite the simplicity of the fault tolerance method introduced in the decoder, a small complexity overhead with respect to a reliable decoder can provide an increase of several orders of magnitude in the maximum deviation probability that can be tolerated. It is likely that a more complex scheme for coding internal messages would bring increased fault tolerance, but our results suggest that such a scheme should retain the ability to adjust its complexity and fault tolerance at every iteration.
