Design of Irregular LDPC Codes with Degree-Set and Degree-Sum Distributions
Min Jang, Jin Whan Kang and Sang-Hyo Kim School of Information and Communication Engineering, Sungkyunkwan University
Suwon, 440-746 Republic of Korea Email: iamshkim@skku.edu

Abstract—In this paper, we introduce new attributes of lowdensity parity-check graphs, degree-set and degree-sum, which explains the connectivity of a check node with more detail. Then, we consider more specified ensembles defined by degreeset/sum distribution of the graph than one defined by the degree distribution. As the degree set/sum distribution varies, tradeoff between the performance at the waterfall and that at the error-floor is observed. The trade-off point can be managed by controlling degree-set/sum distribution.
I. INTRODUCTION
Wave effect, which is first mentioned by Luby et al. in [1], is a good interpretation about the excellent performance of well-designed irregular low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. This effect is a consequence of the fact that belief flows from high-degree variable nodes to low-degree variable nodes in the irregular graphs as water flows form high to low places. In the iterative belief propagation decoding of irregular codes, variable nodes with higher degrees rapidly enhance the reliabilities of their corresponding codeword bits. Afterwards, they propagate their strong belief to low-degree variable nodes through the connected edges so that low-degree ones could also correct errors. Finally, after a sufficiently large number of iterations, all variable nodes in the graph result in a similar level of error correction despite the difference in degree. Therefore, for irregular graphs design, we give a kind of structural advantage, that is to say high degree, to some portion of variable nodes, and we impose a duty of helping others on high-degree variable nodes.
For better performance we have to control the wave effect such that belief flows efficiently in the graphs, and it means the degree distribution should be carefully determined. In this respect, it is theoretically proved that the degree distribution determines the asymptotic performance of LDPC ensembles by using the density evolution technique [2]. The density evolution quantifies the wave effect in terms of the degree distribution, and we can design and optimize the wave effect of ensembles [3].
In this paper, we explore another way of designing the wave effect beyond the degree distribution. We introduce new attributes, degree-set and degree-sum, which measure the number of nodes that are as far as one and two edges away from a certain check node. Since the degree-set and the degree-sum reflects the connectivity of a node in greater detail than the degree, one can define specified ensembles by

setting a degree-set distribution or a degree-sum distribution of a graph like the multi-edge type framework [4], [5] and joint degree distributions [6]. Then, we provide several construction algorithms, which scale the irregularity of degree-set/sum distributions.
According to numerical results, the waterfall performance varies with the degree-set/sum distributions. It is observed that there is a trade-off between the performance at the waterfall region and the error-floor region. Formerly, there have been some reports that show the trade-off. Richter in [7] and Mateo et al. in [8] modified the progressive-edge-growth (PEG) algorithm [9], to construct LDPC codes with better waterfall region performance while the error-floor performance is sacrificed. However, in this work, it is shown that the trade-off point can even be controlled by various construction algorithms.

B. Finite-Length Performance and Design Problem
Since there is a limit on the length of the codes that we practically use, it is also important to analyze the performance of short-to-moderate length LDPC codes. The concentration theorem is not so for the finite-length LDPC codes, and the cycle-free assumption is no longer valid. Therefore, there must be the performance variation among the instances in a finitelength ensemble.
Fundamentally, the finite-length performance curve such as bit error rate (BER) and frame error rate (FER) is divided into two regions, the waterfall and the error-floor. In the waterfall, the error rate curves falls sharply as the channel noise decreases. In the error-floor region, on the other hand, the error rate slope becomes moderate due to the structural weaknesses of the graph such as small-size trapping set (stopping set for BEC) and pseudo-codewords [11], [12].
It appears to be a trade-off between the performance at the waterfall region and that at the error-floor region among the instances in a finite-length ensemble C(n, λ(x), ρ(x)). It is shown that the design methods for improving the performance at the waterfall region worsen the error-floor region performance as one [7], [8]. In this respect, designing a finitelength code means picking up a instance that has proper trade-off characteristic from the ensemble depending on the requirements of applications.

C. Specified Ensembles with Degree-Set/Sum Distribution

Since the degree-set and the degree-sum are more detailed attributes to represent the connectivity of check nodes than the degree, we can define more specified ensembles C(n, λ(x), ρ(x), {(Sk, σk)}dks=1) and C(n, λ(x), ρ(x), τ (x)) by supplementing C(n, λ(x), ρ(x)) with the degree-set/sum distribution. Note that C(n, λ(x), ρ(x), {(Sk, σk)}kds=1) ⊆ C(n, λ(x), ρ(x), τ (x)) ⊆ C(N, λ(x), ρ(x)), where τ (x) is derived from {(Sk, σk)}kds=1.
Our concern in this paper is to find degree-set distribution and degree-sum distribution for good practical performance. We were first inspired by the fact that irregularity in the graph elevates the capacity of the code. Luby et al. pointed out in [1] that high-degree variable nodes converge quickly and propagate their high belief to low-degree variable nodes, thereby improving the performance. Under the definition of the degree-set/sum distribution, we could expect that high-degree variable nodes with good networks (connected to large degreesum check nodes) may converge much faster, and then they will help low-degree variable nodes even with poor networks (connected to small degree-sum check nodes). Therefore, it is reasonable to infer that not only irregular degree distribution but also irregular degree-set/sum distribution may help the codes enhance their performance. We verify this hypothesis on the relationship between the performance and the diversity of degree-sets/sums by finite-length code constructions.

A. Original-PEG with Unified Degree-Sets/Sums
The original-PEG algorithm is devised to avoid short cycles in the graph [9]. In this algorithm, block length n, code rate r, and variable node degree distribution λ(x) are determined prior to the design. Check node degree distribution ρ(x) is formed as regularly as possible, and the size of cycles in the graph is locally maximized during design. Readers may refer to [9] for more information on the basic PEG procedure. We just briefly review the important characteristics of the PEG algorithm.
In the initialization step of the PEG algorithm, the order of variable nodes is determined depending on the desired degree of each variable node. Since the low-degree variable nodes are more prone to error than high-degree ones, a creation of the cycle of which low-degree variable nodes consist should be preferentially avoided. The PEG algorithm maximizes local cycles in a greedy manner. Compensating the greedy nature of the PEG algorithm, edge placement begins from low-degree variable nodes after variable nodes are listed in the nondecreasing order of the desired degree. We refer to this type of ordering as the low-degree VN first criterion.
After initialization, the algorithm selects check nodes to connect edges for each variable node in an edge-by-edge fashion. A variable node is connected with a check node farthest from it under the current graph setting for each single edge construction. If there is more than one check node farthest from the variable node, the one with the lowest degree in the current connection status is linked to the variable node to make regular ρ(x). This selection method is called the low-degree CN first criterion.
One thing to note is that all check nodes in the PEG graph have similar degree-sets/sums due to the two criteria. Because of the low-degree VN first criterion, the edges which belong to all degree-i variable nodes are successively linked to check nodes until they are all connected. Afterwards, the edges are evenly distributed over all check nodes owing to the lowdegree CN first criterion. Due to such behavior, all check nodes connect with a similar number of degree-i variable nodes. However, the construction algorithms in [7] and [8] relaxed the low-degree CN first criterion, thereby the degree-sets/sums are more diversified in the designed graphs.

V. NUMERICAL RESULTS
The structure and the performance of five different LDPC codes G1, G2, G3, G4, and G5, which are constructed by algorithms described in Table I, are compared in this section. The G1 is a reference code, which is designed by original PEG algorithm [9] with additional error-floor lowering criteria [13], [14]. The G5 is another reference code, and the algorithm PEG5 employs the highest free CN degree criterion instead of the low-degree CN criterion for better waterfall performance.
The block length of every code is 10000, and the code rate is one-half. All codes are designed with the same variable node degree distribution λ(x) = 0.21991x1 + 0.23328x2 + 0.0258x3 +0.0853x5 +0.06540x6 +0.04767x7 +0.01912x8 + 0.08064x18 + 0.22798x19, which is given in [3], and the check node degree distribution is desired to have the most concentrated form of ρ(x) = 0.71876x7 + 0.28124x8. In contrast, their degree-set/sum distributions are dissimilarly controlled as our intension by using different criteria.

First of all, the error rate curves demonstrate that the performance at the waterfall region is determined by how many types of degree-sets the code has. The code with more types of degree-set and wider degree-sum distribution shows better performance at the waterfall region than those with fewer types of degree-sets and narrower degree-sum distribution. For example, G1 even consumes more bit energy than G4 by approximately 0.16dB for achieving the same frame error probability of 1 × 10−1. On the contrary, the code that has few types of degree-sets and small variance of degree-sum distribution has lower error floor.
It is observed that there is a trade-off between the performance at the waterfall and that at the error-floor. One can manage the trade-off point by controlling the diversity of degree-sets/sums in the graph. The trade-off can be regarded as one between the asymptotic threshold maximization and finitelength problems such as cycles, pseudo-codewords avoidance. Based on the fact that the best waterfall performance code has the degree-sum distribution very similar to that of the random graph. We conjecture that the randomly constructed graph falls into a nearly best specified ensemble within C(n, λ(x), ρ(x)) in terms of waterfall performance.

VI. CONCLUSIONS
New concepts, degree-set and degree-sum for a node, were defined as features of bipartite graphs of LDPC codes. It is shown that the degree-set/sum distribution is closely related to the waterfall performance of LDPC codes. We provided several modified-PEG construction algorithms which scale the diversity of degree set/sum of the designed graphs. The waterfall performance improves as the diversity increases. However, the error-floor performance degrades since the structural weaknesses is increases by the diversification. That leads

to the trade-off between waterfall and error-floor performance of LDPC codes.
Based on the results concerning the trade-off, we can improve the practical performance of some applications by sacrificing the performance at a certain region. Especially, there are applications for which error requirement is relaxed so that we get a chance of improving waterfall performance at the price of making error-floor higher. Inner LDPC codes of concatenated coding systems are less strictly demanded with respect to error rate. For example, the error-floor is mitigated by error protection of retransmissions in Hybrid ARQ (HARQ) systems. We may be able to improve the system performance by designing irregular LDPC codes with proper trade-off between waterfall and error-floor, which makes the whole body of this work more valuable.
