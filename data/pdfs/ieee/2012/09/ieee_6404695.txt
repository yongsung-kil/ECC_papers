Abstract—In this paper, we present a new class of decoders for low density parity check (LDPC) codes. We are motivated by the observation that the linear programming (LP) decoder has worse error performance than belief propagation (BP) decoders at low SNRs. We base our new decoders on the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) decomposition technique for LP decoding. The ADMM not only efficiently solves the LP decoding problem, but also makes it possible to explore other decoding algorithms. In particular, we add various penalty terms to the linear objective of LP decoding with the goal of suppressing pseudocodewords. Simulation results show that the new decoders achieve much better error performance compared to LP decoder at low SNRs. What is more, similar to the LP decoder, no error floor is observed at high SNRs.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low density parity check (LDPC) codes, when paired with message passing decoding algorithms, can perform close to the Shannon limit. However, message passing is not the only choice for decoding LDPC codes. The linear programming (LP) decoder proposed by Feldman et al. [1] brings LDPC decoding to the realm of convex optimization. It has been shown that LP decoding is better than message passing algorithms in many aspects. First, the LP decoding has an “ML certificate” property. That is, if the solution from an LP decoder is an integral solution, it is the maximum likelihood (ML) solution. Second, the LP decoder provides better theoretical guarantees. Message passing algorithms are not guaranteed to converge and are observed to suffer from an “error floor”. LP decoding, on the other hand, is guaranteed to correct some fraction of errors in bit flip channels (see e.g. [2]–[4]) and, empirically, has not been observed to suffer from an error floor.
Despite having these advantages, LP decoding has much higher computational complexity when implemented with general LP solvers. Some efficient LP decoding algorithms have been proposed, such as [5]–[10]. Among these works, [9], [10] developed a distributed, scalable, approach based on the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM). In [9], [10], the authors exploit the geometry of the LP decoding constraints and develop a scalable decoding algorithm that has a message passing interpretation. The simulation results from [10] reveal a drawback for LP decoding: for some large blocklength codes LP decoding performs worse than BP decoding at low SNRs. This suggests that an ideal decoder

should perform like BP at low SNRs and have no error floor effects at high SNRs.
To achieve this goal, we introduce a penalty method in this work. In a nutshell, we start with LP decoding and penalize non-integral solutions by adding a nonlinear penalty term to the objective. We then apply ADMM and obtain a low complexity algorithm as in [9]. This idea is inspired by the observation that the BP is related to minimization of the Bethe Free Energy at temperature T = 1 [11]. This objective is the sum of an LP decoding objective and the Bethe Entropy Function. Our methods replace the Bethe entropy function with a tractable penalty term to retain the high-SNR performance of LP decoding.
The paper is organized as follows. Section II introduces the background of LP and ADMM decoding. Section III introduces the methods of decoding using penalty functions. We explore three penalty functions, 1, 2 and log-penalty functions, and present the corresponding ADMM algorithms. Simulation results are presented in Section IV. Conclusions are drawn in Section V.

III. INCORPORATING A PENALTY TERM
Linear Programming Decoding fails when it outputs a fractional solution. Such a solution is called a pseudocodeword. Many prior works have sought to reduce the impact of pseudocodewords on decoding. These works can roughly be classified into two categories: (a) change the constraint set of LP decoding by tightening the relaxation, e.g. [13], [14] or (b) change the objective functions, e.g. [15]. We present a class of methods that fall in the second category. We add a penalty term to the objective to penalize fractional solutions. The result is that the output of the problem is more likely to be an integral solution.

First, we see that the WER drops quickly as we start to penalize fractional solutions (increasing the αi above zero). Similarly, decoding time also improves. But the WER increases when we penalize too heavily, e.g., when α1 >

0.6, α2 > 0.8 or α3 > 0.2. The WERs then drop back to a local optimum as the αi are increased and finally increase again up to unity. The globally optimal parameter choices are α1∗ = 0.6, α2∗ = 0.8 and α∗3 = 0.2. We simulated other SNRs and the optimal parameters were almost the same. Second, although different values for Tmax give almost the same performance for LP decoding (see points at αi = 0), Tmax does affect the performance when a penalty term is added.

B. WER performance
We again simulate the Margulis code in AWGN channel with same μ and settings. We set Tmax = 1000 and use optimal αi parameters identified in Section IV-A. In Fig. 4, we plot the WER as a function of SNR. Fig. 4 shows an interesting comparison between different decoders. The “nonsaturating BP” is a robust BP implementation that avoids saturating the log-likelihood ratios of the sum-product BP decoder (see [17] for details), thereby improving decoding performance in the error floor regime. We note that the ADMM LP decoder displays worse performance than BP at low SNRs. However BP has an error floor at high SNRs. Even with the non-saturating BP implementation, the slope of WER tends to be a constant at high SNRs (Eb/N0 = 2.3, 2.4, 2.5). However the slope of the WER corresponding to ADMM LP decoder continues to increase at Eb/N0 = 2.5 and 2.8.
Our proposed decoders combine the good characteristics of the LP and BP decoders. The decoders that uses the 1 and 2 penalty function both perform as well as BP decoder at low SNRs. In addition, the slope of the WER curves of all three proposed decoders continue to increase at high SNRs. This is similar to the LP decoder. We observe that all three decoders run almost as fast as our baseline sum-product BP implementation. We also note that decoding using the 2 penalty has the best error performance and time efficiency among the three penalty functions presented. Thus, we consider the decoder that uses the 2 penalty to be a strong competitor to the classic BP algorithm.
