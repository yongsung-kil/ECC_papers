A Fast Searching Method for the Construction of QC-LDPC Codes with Large Girth
Francis C. M. Lau and Wai Man Tam Department of Electronic and Information Engineering, Hong Kong Polytechnic University, Hong Kong
Email: encmlau@polyu.edu.hk

Abstract—In this paper, we propose an effective and efficient searching method for constructing quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes with a desired girth g. We begin with an arbitrary QC-LDPC code with girth-4 and we evaluate only the number of cycles with length 4. When all the cycles with length 4 are removed by adjusting the elements of the QC-LDPC code, we form a QC-LDPC code with girth-6. Subsequently, we consider only the numbers of cycles with length 4 and length 6. In general, knowing that the current QC-LDPC code has a girth of g , we only consider the numbers of cycles with length up to g even though g may be smaller than the desired girth g. By using an adaptive cost function, which is defined as the number of cycles of length g , in the optimization/searching process, we are able to reduce the computational effort tremendously compared with Wang’s searching algorithm [1]. Consequently, our proposed method can generate QC-LDPC codes with the desired girth much more efficiently.
I. INTRODUCTION
Quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes have attracted much interest because of their special properties — flexible design and ease of implementation. They can also be constructed from different structured codes such as finite-geometry codes [2], balancedincomplete-block-design (BIBD) codes [3] and circulantpermutation-matrix-based codes [4], [5], [6]. Among the different structured QC-LDPC codes, those consisting of cyclically right-shifted permutation matrices are the most desirable codes in practical applications. Typical code examples include array codes [4], [5] and Fossorier’s codes [6].
Basically, the structured codes are designed free of cycles of length 4. In other words, the structured codes have a girth of 6. However, the structured codes, especially those with high code rates, cannot guarantee a larger girth value due to the limitation of the code structures.

Although the progressive-edge-growth (PEG) algorithm is a widely-accepted method to construct large-girth random codes [7], [8], it cannot guarantee achieving the largest possible girth when applied to generating the QC-LDPC codes [9]. Consequently, efficient computer searching mechanisms are still regarded as a powerful tool to finding the optimal solution.
We consider QC-LDPC codes with a J × K base parity-check matrix and a sub-block (also called submatrix) size of Z × Z. Then, there exists a total of ZJ×K possible combinations of elements for the base matrix. Even for a moderate value of Z, J and K, such as Z = 432, J = 3 and K = 18, there is an enormous number of possible combinations. Using an exhaustive computer searching method in finding codes with large girth is therefore extremely time-consuming and is not feasible.
The hill-climbing mechanism is an effective searching method, in which the elements in the base matrix are modified iteratively in order to find a better solution. In [1], the numbers of cycles smaller than the desired girth passing through each individual element are first evaluated. Then, the cost of each element is calculated with a weighted sum of the corresponding numbers of cycles. Finally, the element with the largest cost improvement is to be modified so as to lower the “overall” cost of the code design. The process repeats until the cost cannot be further reduced. In each iteration, all the cycles with length no larger than the desired girth have to be counted for all the elements. Consequently, the computational complexity is very large. This issue is particularly problematic to high-rate, large-submatrixsize QC-LDPC codes requiring a large girth.
In this paper, we propose a simple and fast searching method based on the hill-climbing algorithm. Our aim is to reduce the computational intensity while trying to achieve the desired girth at the same time. In our proposed method, the elements in the base matrix are modified and optimized in a pre-assigned order iteratively. IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
We denote the desired girth by g and the girth of the current QC-LDPC code by g where g ≤ g. In the proposed algorithm, the update operation is based on evaluating the numbers of cycles of length no larger than g that pass through the element. In Wang’s hill-climbing algorithm [1], however, the numbers of cycles of length no larger than g passing through each element need to be computed. Thus, in each iteration, the computational intensity of Wang’s algorithm is at least J ×K times that of our proposed algorithm. To make a fair comparison, we use the computational intensity of Wang’s algorithm as a reference. We approximate J × K iterations of our proposed method as one equivalent iteration time using Wang’s algorithm. In Fig. 3, we plot the equivalent number of iterations used by the two algorithms in searching for a girth-8 QC-LDPC code with a base matrix size of 3 × 18. Figure 4 further plots the results when the base matrix size equals 4 × 24. For the former case, the number of “equivalent” iterations used by our proposed algorithm is around 10% to 20% of that needed by the Wang’s algorithm. For the latter case, i.e., with a 4 × 24 base parity-check matrix, our proposed algorithm requires no more than 10% of the number of iterations used by the Wang’s algorithm. Moreover, we observe that the percentage reduction in the number of iterations is larger when Z is smaller.
V. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we have proposed a fast searching method for constructing QC-LDPC codes with a given girth. Due to its low computational complexity, the proposed method can accelerate the searching speed for QCLDPC codes with large girth. Simulation results have

shown that compared with Wang’s searching algorithm, our proposed method can improve the speed by 5 to 10 times when searching for a girth-8 QC-LDPC code with a base matrix size of 3 × 18, and by at least 10 times when base matrix size becomes 4 × 24.
