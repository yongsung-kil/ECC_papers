Pipelined Architecture for Low Density Parity Check Encoder

Silvia Anggraeni, Fawnizu Azmadi Hussin and Varun Jeoti
Electrical and Electronics Engineering Department Universiti Teknologi PETRONAS
Bandar Seri Iskandar, 31750 Tronoh, Perak, Malaysia silvia31804@yahoo.com, fawnizu@petronas.com.my and varun_jeoti@petronas.com.my

Abstract—This paper proposes a pipelined architecture for low density parity check encoder by pipelining information bits and sub-matrices of parity check matrix (H) using two bit-wise operations. The two bit-wise operations are multiplication and exclusive-OR. The investigation is done by exploring two methods of pipelining using the two bit-wise operations in the proposed architecture. The first method of pipelining uses combination of shift register and memory for the sub-matrices of H while the second method of pipelining uses shift register for the information bits and the sub-matrices of H. It is shown that the second method of pipelining increases the throughput but has the largest units of shift registers and flip-flops in the design.
Keywords: low density parity check (LDPC); encoder; architecture; pipelining.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low density parity check (LDPC) codes are known for the error performance which is closest to the Shannon’s limit. These codes have low density of 1s in their parity check matrix (H). The sparseness of H leads to a low decoding complexity. Even though LDPC codes have a sparse H, encoding complexity of LDPC codes may not be as low as the decoding part.
The common method of encoding in LDPC codes is by converting H into a generator matrix (G). If the matrix G is dense, the encoding complexity may be quadratic in the code length denoted by O(N2). If we are able to utilize the right matrix H, there is a possibility that the encoding complexity is linear using this method.
Since there is a chance that we get a dense matrix G from the chosen H, some researchers chose to avoid this method. Many preferred to utilize the sparseness of H to be used in encoding like Richardson and Urbanke [1] and Neal [2]. The encoding method proposed by Richardson and Urbanke utilizes an approximate lower triangular form of H which is divided into small sub-matrices to get almost linear encoding complexity. Another method introduced by Neal applies lower and upper (LU) triangular matrix decomposition. However, it is not easy to find a good sparse matrix with lower and upper triangular sub-matrices for H.
An encoder is a device which implements the process of encoding. The purpose of an encoder is to add some redundant bits to the information bits which are going to be used for

detecting and correcting an error. Sometimes in the encoding, there is an idle time between the current with the next process of computation for getting the redundant or the parity bits. In order to reduce the idle time in the process of encoding, pipelining can be applied in LDPC encoding.
Pipelining is an implementation technique where multiple instructions are overlapped in execution and exploit parallelism among the instructions in a sequential stream [3]. Pipelining is a common method to increase the throughput of the system.
There are some works on pipelined LDPC encoder such as [4], [5], [6], [7] and [8]. Pipelined LDPC encoders in [4] and [6] apply the encoding method of Richardson and Urbanke while [7] encodes using generator matrix G. The pipelined LDPC encoder in [5] and [8] are not using the G matrix or the H matrix for encoding since this work utilizes specific LDPC type. Hardware pipelining in [4] divides pipelining all computations of matrix vector multiplication (MVM) and inverse operation in the encoding method of Richardson and Urbanke while the work in [6] is pipelining the memory read, computation and memory write operations.
The work in [5] uses pipelining for MVM of sub-matrices of H and some part of parity bits while [7] applies pipelining for MVM of long vector and large matrix into short vector and small matrix based quasi-cyclic matrix. The work in [8] applies wave-pipelined architecture using different stages of buffer insertion between puncturing, repetition and interleaving for accumulate repeat accumulate (ARA) LDPC code.
In this paper, we propose a pipelined architecture for LDPC encoder by pipelining the information bits and submatrices of H. The operation done in the proposed architecture is a bit-wise operation. The investigation is done by exploring two methods of doing the BM and the BXOR operation in the proposed architecture. It is shown that the second method of pipelining in the proposed architecture increases the throughput of register RP1 but has the largest units of shift registers and flip-flops. The rest of paper is organized as follows. Section II discusses the proposed architecture of LDPC encoder. We describe results and discussion of the proposed architecture in section III. The last section is the conclusion.

II. PROPOSED ENCODER
The framework of the proposed encoder is shown in Fig. 1. Our framework of encoder is divided into software and hardware parts with information bits that need to be encoded as inputs.
The software part generates a non-singular matrix H with an approximate lower triangular form based on a codeconstruction method which we have previously developed [10]. The chosen H is used to generate sub-matrices P1 and P2 based on [9].
In order to get the sub-matrices of P1 and P2, the chosen H is divided into small sub-matrices of A, B, C, D, T, E according to method of Richardson-Urbanke. An example of sub-matrices of A, B, C, D, T, E is described by (1). The submatrices of A and C represent the information part while the others sub-matrices (B, D, T and E) are the parity part. The modification to get sub-matrices P1 and P2 derived from [9] is described in (3) and (4).
The software part in Fig. 1 can be easily implemented and will work together with the hardware in the system. The

information bits received as inputs from the applications that use the proposed encoder are applied in the hardware part.
The sub-matrices of P1 and P2 and the information bits will be used to get parity bits p. The parity bits p consists of parity p1 and p2 which are derived from (5) and (6). The next step of hardware encoding is combining the parity bits p1 and p2 with the information bits to get the coded information bits or codeword c = [s p1T p2T]. The value of codeword c = [s p1T p2T] is valid only if c satisfies the condition HcT = 0.
We propose a pipelined architecture for the hardware part of our parity generator in order to reduce the idle time in the process of encoding and to improve the throughput of encoder. The proposed architecture for the parity generator is shown in Fig. 2 which can be used for any code rate and code length. In Fig. 2, there are labels of K, 1, mP1, mP2, a, b, c and d which indicate the length of bit used in the design.

The proposed architecture is pipelining the information bits and the sub-matrices of P1 and P2. The reason for doing this is the parity bits are influenced by information bits and these submatrices as given in (5) and (6). This architecture consists of three stages of pipelining which require operations of bitwise multiplication (BM) and bitwise exclusive-OR (BXOR).
In the first stage, the (1 × K) information bits are loaded into the register RS1 while the sub-matrices of H are inserted into the registers RP1 and RP2. In the second stage, the pipelining BM for the sub-matrices P1, P2 and the information bits are taken place then these results are stored into the register RS2A and RS2B. In this stage, the operation of BM is performed by using AND gate between the 1-bit of information and pipelined P1 and P2.
In the third stage, the pipelining of BXOR is performed then the results are inserted into the register RS3A and RS3B. The operation of BXOR in the stage 3 has a feedback loop which works as binary accumulation for the pipelining technique. Inside the BXOR, there is operation of AND-gate between the result of XOR-gate with count. The element of the count is only 1 and the value can be 1 or 0.

III. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
In this section, we would like to investigate methods on how to incorporate pipelining techniques in the proposed architecture. The investigation is done by exploring two methods of doing the pipelining for the BM and the BXOR operations in Fig. 2. Both methods of pipelining applies K-bit shift register using parallel-in and serial-out.
The operation of BM is performed by 1-bit of information and 1-bit of sub-matrix P1 or P2 while the operation of BXOR is determined by XOR-gate, flip-flop as feedback, AND-gate with the count. If the count is 0, then the result of BXOR is 0 while if the count is 1, then the result is also 1.
In the first method of pipelining, the labels of a, b, c, d in Fig. 2 represent K-bit, 1-bit, K-bit, 1-bit length in the architecture. The architecture of the BM in the first method of pipelining for parity p1 is presented in Fig. 3. The information bits is represented by K-bit shift register and is loaded into the register RS1. In this architecture, we use memory to store the element of sub-matrix P1 using some addresses. If one address is loaded, the data of this address will be transferred to the shift registers RP1. The BM operation will execute 1-bit from the RS1 and the RP1 register. The result of this operation which is only 1-bit element will be stored in the register RS2A.
The architecture of the BXOR operation in the first method for parity p1 is described in Fig. 4. In the BXOR box, there is one XOR-gate, one flip-flop as feedback and one AND-gate. The feedback loop indicates the use of the previous result of the XOR operation which works as binary accumulation for the pipelining technique.
The result of the BXOR operation will be stored into the register RS3A which is a mP1−bit shift register. The input of the mP1−bit shift register is entered serially and the parallel output of mP1 bits are taken out of the register. If the cycle of pipelining is below K, the count is 0. This causes the output of the shift registers are equal to zeros. If it reaches to K-cycle, the count is 1 which generates one input of the RS3A to be the same as binary accumulation. After mP1 times of K-cycle, the parity p1 of mP1 bits will be generated into the output of the RS3A.

The length of a, b, c, d in Fig. 2 for the second method of pipelining are mP1-bit, mP1-bit, mP2-bit, mP2-bit. The second architecture is developed since we would like to increase the throughput of the system. The BM operation for the second method of pipelining is depicted in Fig. 5.
This method uses serial bitwise operation for doing the multiplication. The difference from the BM architecture in the first method is on the representation of sub-matrix P1. This method applies all elements of sub-matrix P1 using mP1 units of K-bit shift registers.
The registers RP1, RS1 store all elements and shift the required elements for the BM operation. The two inputs of the BM-box are the pipelined vector P1 with the size of mP1×1 and the 1-bit of information. The BM uses the same value of information bit for all AND gates. The output is the pipelined parity p1 which will be stored into the register RS2A. The process of shifting does the pipelining for the (mP1×K) submatrix P1 into the size of (mP1×1) and the (1 × K) bits of information into 1 bit.

The architecture of the BXOR operation in the second method for parity p1 is described in Fig. 6. In the BXOR box, there are some XOR-gates, flip-flops as feedback and ANDgates. The input of the BXOR is the pipelined parity p1 with the size of (mP1×1) from the register RS2A while the final result is stored into the RS3A.
The difference from the BXOR architecture in the first method is the number used for the XOR-gate, the flip-flop, the AND-gate. In the BXOR box of the second method, there are mP1 units of XOR-gates, flip-flops and AND-gates while in the first method, we use only one for all gates and flip-flop. The type of register applied in the RS3A of this method is different. The register RS3A are mP1 flip-flops while in the first method, there is a mP1−bit shift register.
The comparison of the units of shift registers, flip-flops and memory required in both methods of pipelining are given in Table I for parity p1. It is shown that the first method of pipelining has the largest area of memory while the second method has the biggest units of K-bit shift registers and flipflops.

IV. CONCLUSION
We have presented a pipelined architecture for LDPC encoder which can be achieved by two methods of pipelining. The differences between both methods are the shift registers, the flip-flops and the memory used. In this paper, we have shown that the method of pipelining in the proposed architecture influences the throughput of the register RP1 and the units of shift registers, flip-flops and the memory required in the design.
