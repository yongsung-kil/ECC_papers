7.7Gbps Encoder Design for IEEE 802.11n/ac QC-LDPC Codes

Yongmin Jung, Chulho Chung and Jaeseok Kim
School of Electrical and Electronic Engineering Yonsei University
Seoul, Republic of Korea {jymsoc, jaekim}@yonsei.ac.kr

Yunho Jung
School of Electronics, Telecommunication and computer Engineering
Korea Aerospace University Goyang-si

Abstract—This paper proposes a high throughput encoding process and encoder architecture for quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes in IEEE 802.11n/ac standards. In order to achieve the high throughput with low complexity, a partially parallel processing based encoding process and encoder architecture are proposed. Forward and backward accumulations are performed in one clock cycle to increase the encoder throughput. A low complexity cyclic shifter is also proposed to minimize the hardware overhead of combinational logic in the encoder architecture. The proposed encoder is implemented with 130-nm CMOS technology. For (1944, 1620) irregular code, 7.7 Gbps throughput is achieved at 100 MHz clock frequency. The gate count of the proposed encoder core is about 96 K.

I. INTRODUCTION
Quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes defined by a sparse parity check matrix have received much attention as a forward error correction code due to their excellent error correction performance [1]. Some wireless communication standards, such as IEEE 802.11n, IEEE 802.11ac and IEEE 802.16e, employ the QC-LDPC codes as an error correction code [2]-[4]. These wireless communication standards support a very high data rate over hundreds of Mbps. IEEE 802.11ac especially supports about 7 Gbps data rate. These standards also support various code rates and block lengths. Therefore, a rate compatible QC-LDPC encoder that supports the high throughput is required.
In order to achieve high throughput, an encoding process can be performed in the small number of clock cycles. In many literatures, LDPC encoders for QC-LDPC codes were presented [5]-[13]. The LDPC encoders presented in [5]-[11] can support various code rates and block lengths. However, these LDPC encoders cannot support the high data rate over Gbps. The LDPC encoders introduced in [12][13] are designed for the LDPC code of IEEE 802.11n standard. Although the LDPC encoder in [12] can support the high throughput over Gbps, a fully parallel architecture causes high hardware complexity. Since the LDPC encoder in [13] requires many clock cycles for encoding, it is hard to achieve Gbps throughput.

The parity check matrix of QC-LDPC code is composed of square sub-matrices. Each sub-matrix is an identity matrix with a cyclic shift or a zero matrix. During an encoding process, information bits are multiplied by the parity check matrix. Due to the property of the parity check matrix of QC-LDPC code, the multiplication is implemented by a cyclic shifter.
In this paper, we propose a high throughput LDPC encoder that requires the small number of clock cycles based on a partially parallel architecture. The proposed LDPC encoder also supports various code rates and block lengths. A low complexity cyclic shifter is also proposed to reduce the hardware overhead of the cyclic shifter in the partially parallel architecture.

II. BACKGROUND
A. QC-LDPC Codes in IEEE 802.11n/ac standards
The parity check matrix of QC-LDPC codes can be described as a base parity check matrix. Fig. 1 shows an example of the base parity check matrix defined in IEEE 802.11n/ac standards [2][3]. The digits indicate the cyclic shift values of identity sub-matrices. The dash, '-', indicates a zero matrix. QC-LDPC codes of IEEE 802.11n/ac standards support four code rates, i.e., 1/2, 2/3, 3/4 and 5/6, and three block lengths, i.e., 648, 1296 and 1944. In order to support three block lengths, three kinds of sub-matrix size Z are defined as 27, 54 and 81.

IV. PROPOSED HIGH THROUGHPUT ENCODER ARCHITECTURE
A. Overall Architecture of Encoder
In IEEE 802.11n/ac standards, LDPC encoder not only generates codeword block from information bits, but also adds pad bits to the information bits, and performs puncturing and repeating the codeword block [2][3]. Fig. 2 shows the overall architecture of the proposed QC-LDPC encoder including encoder core, padding stage and puncturing and repeating stage. Adding the pad bits to the information bits is performed before the LDPC encoder core, and puncturing and repeating the codeword block are performed after the LDPC encoder core. The padded information bits and the punctured or repeated codeword are temporarily saved in the buffers. The size of input bits of the proposed encoder architecture is set to 8. The size of output bits varies depending on the modulation scheme and the several output steams are output for multiple spatial streams. Control signals for the overall encoder architecture are generated by the controller. In this encoder architecture, the sizes of buffers are optimized to minimize the hardware overhead.
B. Proposed High Throughput Encoder Core
As shown in Fig. 2, the LDPC encoder core can be divided into two steps. The first step consists of N-M cyclic shifters (CSs) and the accumulation logics including the adders and the flip-flops (FFs). From the buffer of the padding stage, the encoder core reads the Z bits information sub-sequence m(j) per clock cycle. Since the sub-matrix H1(i,j) is the identity matrix with cyclic shift or the zero matrix, H1(i,j) · mT(j) can be implemented by a cyclic shifter. Thus, Ȝi(j) of (5) is obtained by accumulating the cyclic shift results of m(j). Since there is no dependency between Ȝi and Ȝi-1, all Ȝi‘s can be simultaneously obtained by the (N-M)-ways partially parallel processing as shown in Fig. 2. N-M CSs, adders and FFs are also required. The final results of the first stage, Ȝi(M-1)’s, can be obtained after M information sub-sequences are read. Therefore, the required number of clock cycles at the first stage is M.
The second step consists of the vector adders and the forward and backward (FW/BW) accumulators. According to (6), the parity sub-subsequence p(0) can be obtained by the vector addition of all Ȝi’s.

VI. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we proposed the high throughput QC-LDPC encoding process and encoder architecture. By using the partially parallel processing in the column direction and the accumulations of data, 7.7 Gbps throughput was achieved with 96 K gate counts. The low complexity cyclic shifter was also proposed. Therefore, the proposed encoder can be applied to encoding of QC-LDPC codes in IEEE 802.11n/ac standards.
