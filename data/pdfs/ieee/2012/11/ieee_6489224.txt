Quantization. Absorbing Regions and Practical 
Message Passing Decoders 
Behzad Amiri, Shayan Garani Srinivasa*, and Lara Dolecek 
Electrical Engineering Department, University of California, Los Angeles 
*Western Digital Corporation

Abstract-Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes and accom­
panying message passing decoding algorithms are a popular 
choice for data encoding and decoding in modem communica­
tions and storage systems. To reduce implementation complexity, 
the messages in a practical message passing decoder are necessar­
ily quantized. It is well known that the performance of practical, 
quantized message passing decoders in the high-reliability regime 
is governed by non-codeword decoding errors, typically described 
via trapping/absorbing sets. Absorbing regions act as "decoding 
regions" around absorbing sets. In this work, we take a closer 
look at the interplay between quantization and absorbing regions. 
We provide a study of a range of quantization choices, describe 
the impact of quantization on the candidate absorbing regions, 
and derive guidelines for practical finite-precision decoders. 
In particular, we show that, depending on the choice of the 
quantization allocation, different absorbing sets emerge as dom­
inant: even though the overall performance of two quantization 
schemes can be similar, the distribution of decoding errors across 
possible absorbing sets can be substantially different. We take 
the advantage of disjointness of error profiles of two carefully 
chosen quantized decoders to design a decoder that is a series 
of these two decoders. T he result is a performance improvement 
of at least an order magnitude relative to constituent decoders 
without increase in complexity. 
I. INTRODUCTION 
Sparse graph-based codes such as low-density parity-check 
(LDPC) are a class of codes that have high performance close 
to the Shannon limit. It is well known that the outstanding 
performance can be achieved by practical but mathematically 
SUboptimal message-passing decoding algorithms. Prior works 
have designed message passing decoders for improved perfor­
mance and reduced complexity, e.g., [3], [13]. In the imple­
mentation of message passing decoders, due to the floating­
point arithmetic in digital computing machines, messages are 
necessarily quantized. 
In many applications such as emerging data storage systems, 
the region of very low frame error rates (FERs) is of primary 
interest. In this high-reliability regime, certain non-codeword 
objects - referred to as absorbing sets [5] - start to become a 
dominating cause of the decoding errors. Each such absorbing 
set has an "absorbing region" associated with it. If a noise 
realization falls into the absorbing region of a given absorbing 
set, the decoder converges to this absorbing set. The relative 
effect of a given class of absorbing sets on the decoder 
performance (captured by the shape and size of the associated 
absorbing regions) is a complex function of the quantization 
choices. In our recent works we focused on the combinatorial 
analysis of absorbing sets to design good codes [9], [10]. 
It is convenient to use the notation Qa.b to denote the 
quantization of messages exchanged between variable nodes 
and checks nodes during the iterative decoding: the parameter 
a stands for the integer part and the parameter b stands for 
the fractional part of the messages. An additional bit is used 
to represent the sign of the message. For example, QO.O 
corresponds to the bit flipping decoder which only has two 
levels for messages. It is obvious that for Qa.b, we are using 
a + b + 1 bits to represent each message in the decoder. 
As we increase the values of a and b, the complexity of 
the computations increases. Faster message computations and 
more compact decoder implementations can be achieved by 
utilizing fewer bits for message quantization. Therefore, it 
is desirable to use fewer bits in quantization to reduce the 
complexity of the decoder. 
In this work, we provide a study of the impact of quantiza­
tion on the performance of practical finite-precision iterative 
decoders (e.g., popular soft-xor decoders [8]). We take a closer 
look at the quantization choices, investigate the relationship 
between absorbing set errors and quantization levels in prac­
tical decoders, and establish decoder design guidelines based 
on the analysis of relevant absorbing regions. By performing 
extensive simulations over a range of code parameters of 
array-based LDPC codes (to be defined shortly), taken as a 
representative of structured quasi-cyclic codes, we make the 
following qualitative observations: 
• 
When a ::; 3, soft-xor decoder with Qa.b performs better

When a > 3, Qa.b and Qa.b' decoders perform about the

under Q5.1, (8,2) absorbing sets emerge as dominant at 
the same SNR points. 
• A series of decoders with different quantization parame­

C. Classification of errors 
We assume transmission of the all-zeros codeword over an 
AWGN channel. Because of the noise in the channel, the 
received sequence may not always be decoded as the all­
zeros codeword. The output of an iterative decoder can be 
categorized into following four different disjoint classes: 
• The all-zeros codeword: no decoding error occurs. 
• ( c, d) absorbing set errors: decoder f) converges to a 
( c, d) absorbing set. 
• Non-absorbing set errors: decoder f) converges to a 
combinatorial object, which is not an absorbing set. The 
set of all noise realizations which result in non-absorbing 
set errors is referred as the non-absorbing region. 
• Non-converging errors: decoder f) does not converge 
within the last ten iterations2. The set of all noise real­
izations which result in non-converging errors within the 
prescribed number of iterations is referred as the non­
converging region.

B. Bipartite graph, absorbing sets and absorbing regions 
Let G = (V, c, F) denote a bipartite graph (Tanner graph) 
describing an LDPC code, with the usual notation of V being 
the set of bit nodes, C the set of check nodes, and the set F 
describing the edges between the nodes in V and C. For a 
given D <;; V, we say that a check node C E C is satisfied 
(unsatisfied) with respect to D if c is connected to D even 
(odd) number of times. For any subset D <;; V, let E(D) 
(resp. O(D) be the set of neighboring satisfied (unsatisfied) 
checks in C. 
An Ac,d absorbing set [5] of size (c, d) is a subset D <;; V 
with size c that connects to a subset O(D) <;; C with size d, 
where each element of D has strictly fewer neighbors from 
O(D) than from C\O(D). 
The set of all noise realizations which the decoder f) 
decodes as a (c, d) absorbing set is called a (c, d) absorbing 
region of f). Note that absorbing region is a function of both 
the code and the decoder. Absorbing regions are illustrated in

IV. GUIDELINES FOR PRACTICAL DECODERS 
As previously mentioned, we observed that although the per­
formance of different decoders for a + b = 6 and a E {4, 5, 6} 
are similar in terms of the overall frequency of decoding errors, 
the sets of noise realizations which caused decoding errors are 
disjoint for different values of a. This observation suggests that 
a noise realization which causes a decoding error for Q4.2 
decoder, may in fact decode correctly under Q5.1 or Q6.0 
decoders. Interestingly, this property holds for all different 
code parameter choices tested in our simulations. 
This insight motivates us to propose a decoder consisting 
of a series of decoders with partially disjoint error sets. In 
the series of two decoders, all of the received sequences from 
channel are decoded by decoder 1. Decoder 2 is only applied to 
those sequences that caused a decoding error under decoder 
1. Therefore, it is desirable to have a less computationally

recently proposed by Zhang and Schlegel based on boosting

V. CONCLUSION 
In this paper, we studied the effect of the quantization 
parameters on the performance and on the error profile of prac­
tical soft-xor decoder by performing extensive simulations. 
By taking a closer look at the error profiles, we observed 
a dependency of the absorbing regions on the quantization 
choice. Based on the error profile analysis, we devised a new 
decoder as a series of two carefully chosen decoders wherein 
the constituent decoders have the same implementation com­
plexity but disjoint error profiles. The resulting decoder is 
simple to implement and offers an order of magnitude and
