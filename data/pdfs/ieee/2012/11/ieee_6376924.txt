I. INTRODUCTION
Modern coding theory deals with finding good error correcting codes that have efficient encoders and decoders [1]. Message-passing iterative decoding algorithms based on belief-propagation (see e.g., [2]) are examples of suboptimal decoders (i.e., may fail to correct errors that are corrected by a maximum-likelihood (ML) decoder).
Wiberg HW DO [3], [4] developed the use of graphical models for systematically describing instances of known decoding algorithms. In particular, the ‚Äúsum-product‚ÄĚ algorithm and the ‚Äúmin-sum‚ÄĚ algorithm are presented as generic iterative message-passing decoding algorithms that apply to any graph realization of a Tanner code. Wiberg HW DO proved that the min-sum algorithm can be viewed as a dynamic programming algorithm that computes the ML-codeword if the Tanner graph is a tree. For LDPC codes, Wiberg [4] characterized a necessary condition for decoding failures of the min-sum algorithm by ‚Äúnegative‚ÄĚ cost trees, called PLQLPDO GHYLDWLRQV.
Various iterative message-passing decoding algorithms are derived from the belief propagation algorithm (e.g., maxproduct [3] attenuated max-product [5], tree-reweighted beliefpropagation [6], etc.). The convergence of these BP-based iterative decoding algorithms to an optimum solution has been studied extensively in various settings (see e.g., [3], [5]‚Äď[10]). However, bounds on the time and message complexity of these algorithms are not considered. The analyses in these works often rely on the existence of a single optimal solution in addition to other conditions such as: single-loop graphs, large girth, large reweighting coefficients, consistency conditions, etc.
Jian and Pfister [10] analyzed a special case of the attenuated max-product decoder [5], for regular LDPC codes. Using contraction properties and consistency conditions, they proved

sufficient conditions under which the message-passing decoder converges to a locally optimal codeword. This convergence also implies convergence to the LP-optimum and therefore to the ML-codeword.
&RQWULEXWLRQ This paper presents the first decoding algorithm for finite-length (regular and irregular) LDPC codes over MBIOS channels with bounded time complexity that combines two properties: (1) it is a message-passing algorithm, and (2) the algorithm has an ML-certificate.
We present a new message-passing iterative decoding algorithm, called QRUPDOL]HG ZHLJKWHG PLQVXP (NWMS) algorithm. NWMS algorithm applies to any irregular Tanner code with single parity-check (SPC) local-codes (e.g., LDPC codes and HDPC codes). The characterization of local-optimality for Tanner codes with SPC local-codes has two parameters: (i) a certificate height h, and (ii) a vector of layer weights w ‚ąą R+h \ {0h}. We prove that, for any finite h, the NWMS decoder is guaranteed to compute the ML codeword in h iterations if an h-locally optimal codeword exists (Theorem 7). Furthermore, the output of NWMS can be efficiently MLcertified. The number of iterations, h, may exceed the girth. Because local-optimality is a pure combinatorial property, the results do not rely on convergence. The time and message complexity of NWMS is O(|E| ¬∑ h) where |E| is the number of edges in the Tanner graph. Local optimality is a sufficient condition for successful decoding by our BP-based algorithm in loopy graphs. We discuss on bounds on the threshold of ML-certified NWMS decoding over MBIOS channels. We refer the reader to [11, Section 7] for the full details.

IV. NUMERICAL RESULTS
We chose a (3, 6)-regular LDPC code with blocklength N = 1008. We ran h = 200 iterations of the NWMS decoder for received words over an AWGN channel. The decoded words were checked by the local optimality verifier. Three choices of level weights w were considered: (1) Unit level weights, w 1. This choice reduces local optimality to [12], [14] (although in these papers h is limited by a quarter of the girth). (2) Geometric level weights w 3 ¬∑ 2 ‚ąí1. In this case NWMS reduces to the standard min-sum algorithm [4]. (3) Geometric level weights w 3 ¬∑ 1.25 ‚ąí1. In this case NWMS reduces to normalized BP-based with őĪ = 1.25 [8]. The choice of weights in [8] was obtained by optimizing DE w.r.t. minimum bit error probability.
Figure 1 depicts the word error rate of NWMS with respect to these three level weights by solid lines. The ratio of received words for which NWMS decoding failed to return a codeword certified as locally optimal (and hence ML-optimal) is depicted by dashed lines. The results show that the choice of unit level weights minimizes the gap between NWMS decoding failure and non-certified decoding. That is, the main explanation for decoding failure with unit level weights is the lack of a locally optimal codeword. Moreover, there is a tradeoff between maximizing the rate of certified NWMS-decoding and minimizing the word error rate.
V. CONCLUSIONS AND DISCUSSION
We present a new message passing algorithm, called NWMS, with the following properties: (1) NWMS applies to regular

and irregular Tanner codes with SPC local codes. (2) NWMS
has a decoding guarantee stated as follows. If there exists
a locally optimal codeword, then NWMS finds it (recall that
a locally optimal codeword has an ML-certificate). (3) The
decoding guarantee of NWMS is not bounded by the girth.
Namely, the height parameter in local optimality and the
number of iterations in the decoding is arbitrary. (4) The
decoding guarantee of NWMS is not asymptotic nor does it
rely on convergence. Namely, it applies to finite codes and
decoding with a finite number of iterations. (5) NWMS applies
to any MBIOS channel (including unbounded LLRs).
In the special case of regular LDPC codes, the NWMS
algorithm is a generalization of BP-based decoding algorithms
simply by the choice of the level weights. This includes the
min-sum algorithm [3], [4], attenuated max-product [5], and
normalized BP-based [8]. Hence, ML-certificates for these
decoding algorithms follow from NWMS.
Analysis of successful LP-decoding of regular LDPC codes
is based on local optimality [12], [14], [15]. Hence, all these
results apply immediately to NWMS-decoding provided that
the number of iterations is bounded by a quarter of the girth
of the Tanner graph.
