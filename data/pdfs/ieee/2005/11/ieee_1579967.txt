Coset-Based Quasi-Cyclic LDPC Codes for Optimal Overlapped Message Passing Decoding
Yongmei Dai and Zhiyuan Yan
Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering Lehigh University, PA 18015, USA E-mail: {yod304, yan}@lehigh.edu

Abstract— Due to the regular structures of quasi-cyclic (QC) low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, the message passing decoding of these codes not only has efficient hardware implementation, but also can be overlapped to improve throughput and hardware utilization efficiency (HUE). In previous work, we proposed a scheduling scheme for the overlapped message passing (OMP) decoding of the SFT codes. In this paper, we first show that our scheduling scheme produces the minimum intraiteration waiting time for QC LDPC codes. Then we propose a coset-based construction of QC LDPC codes, and study the girth and minimum distance properties of our new codes. Given the same parameters, the ensemble of the SFT codes is a subset of the ensemble of our coset-based QC LDPC codes. Our coset-based QC LDPC codes allow the OMP decoding to achieve greater throughput gain and higher HUE while maintaining the same theoretical performances as the SFT codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1,2] have attracted a lot of attention due to their near-capacity performances when decoded with the iterative message passing algorithm (see, e.g., [3]). Various (fully parallel, fully serial, and partly parallel) decoder architectures have been proposed [4–8]. In general, random code structure, which is used to enhance the theoretical performances, often causes problems in the implementation of such codes, whereas regular code structure often eases the hardware implementation, which motivates the joint code-decoder design methodology [6, 7, 9].
Quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes1 [7,10–15] lend themselves conveniently to efficient hardware implementation [7]; hence construction of QC LDPC codes with good performances is of both practical and theoretical interest. Various construction methods [7, 10–13] have been proposed. In particular, the QC LDPC codes based on multiplicative structure of finite fields (often referred to as the SFT codes) are proposed in [11, 13], and their girth and minimum distance properties have been investigated in [11,13–15]. For short to medium block length, the SFT codes perform comparably to or even better than the randomly constructed regular LDPC codes [13].

Thus, two types — intra- and inter-iteration – of data dependencies exist since the variable (check) node updates of any iteration utilize the results of the check (variable) node updates of the same (previous) iteration. Hence, check node function units (CNFUs) and variable node function units (VNFUs)—the two kinds of hardware units that implement the check and variable node updates, respectively—often work one after another. This leads to low hardware utilization efficiency (HUE) and low throughput. This problem can be solved by either folding [16] or data interleaving [5, 7], but both approaches have their drawbacks. Recently, an overlapped message passing (OMP) decoding algorithm, where the check and variable node updates are (partially) overlapped, has been proposed for QC LDPC codes [8, 17]. The OMP decoding improves both throughput and HUE while using the same memory and introducing no performance degradation. In [18], we have shown that maximizing the throughput of the OMP decoding of QC LDPC codes using the hardware structure described in [8] can be formulated as a data dependency and hardware constrained optimization problem, and thus amounts to minimizing the intra- and inter-iteration waiting times. In particular, when the intra-iteration waiting time is small enough, the HUE is improved to 100% and the maximum throughput gain of approximately 2 is achieved. We have also proposed a scheduling scheme to determine the intra-iteration waiting time and the corresponding starting addresses in [18].
In this paper, we first prove that our scheduling scheme proposed in [18] gives the minimum intra-iteration waiting time and then compare our scheduling scheme with that in [8]. Although the OMP decoding algorithm can be applied to any QC LDPC codes, previous studies have focused mostly on the SFT codes [8, 18]. However, the maximum throughput gain of roughly 2 prescribed by the optimal OMP decoding cannot always be achieved by the SFT codes. In order to achieve the maximum possible throughput gain, we propose a new construction method of QC LDPC codes that is based on cosets. We show that the QC LDPC codes so constructed have similar girth and minimum distance bounds to those in [13] for the SFT codes. Furthermore, given the block length and rate of the code, our coset-based QC LDPC codes include the SFT codes [11,13] as subsets. Hence, given any SFT code, there exists a group of coset-based QC LDPC codes (including the given SFT code) with the same parameters and similar performances as opposed to the given SFT code. B. Relation to Other Constructions
The coset-based construction is a generalization of both the construction for the SFT codes and that based on cyclotomic cosets. Fix the design parameters m, j, and k, the ensemble of the SFT codes is a subset of the ensemble of the QC LDPC codes based on cosets. We also show that when cyclotomic cosets are well defined, using cyclotomic cosets instead of cosets in the above construction leads to the same class of QC LDPC codes up to permutations of coordinates.
To understand the differences between these construction methods, the effects of two operations on the parity check matrix H have to be discussed. First, note that block row and block column permutations can be performed on H. Block row and column permutations are simply permutations of the check and variable nodes respectively in the Tanner graph, and result in either the same or equivalent codes. Hence, these operations do not affect the minimum intra-iteration waiting times. Second, cyclic shifts can be performed on block rows. This operation essentially amounts to choosing different coset leaders in the coset-based construction. This operation yields different codes and hence can potentially change the minimum intra-iteration waiting times. However, the girth and minimum distance bounds of the coset-based QC LDPC codes, given by Lemmas 1 and 2 below, are not changed by these operations.
The construction for the SFT codes first chooses b ∈ Zm∗ , an element with order j (j|m − 1), and then sets xs,t = bsat (mod m) in (1). If we use the j cosets with {1, b, b2, · · · , bj−1} as the leaders, the resultant code constructed using these j cosets is the SFT code constructed using a and b. Thus, given m, j, and k, the SFT codes form a subclass of QC LDPC codes based on cosets. Furthermore, when k, j are given, we can find φ(k), φ(j) elements with order k, j in Zm∗ , where φ(·) is the Euler function [19]. Each of the φ(k)φ(j) combinations of a and b can be used to construct an SFT code. Since the parity check matrices of these φ(k)φ(j) SFT codes are equivalent up to block row and block column permutations, these codes are equivalent codes and have the same minimum intra-iteration waiting times.
In [7], Mansour and Shanbhag proposed a construction of QC LDPC codes based on cyclotomic cosets. The construction in [7] is the same as our coset-based construction described above except for the usage of the term “cyclotomic cosets” in [7]. Cyclotomic cosets, to the best of our knowledge, are defined with respect to GF(a) [19, page 62], which requires a to be a prime power. The example given in [7] is indeed based on cyclotomic cosets with respect to GF(2). However, the construction in [7] did not explicitly require a to be a prime power. Despite the ambiguity in terminology, we show below that when cyclotomic cosets exist, the two constructions lead to the same class (up to permutations of coordinates) of codes. When a is a prime power, GF(a) exists. The cosets with respect to a as defined in (6) are also cyclotomic cosets modulo m with respect to GF(a). In this case, coset-based

Coset-based construction using a non-prime-power element a ∈ Zm∗ of order k leads to a QC LDPC code. Since a and a are of the same order, they are conjugates to each other and a is simply a permutation of a . When using the same coset leaders, each of the cosets with respect to a is a permuted version of the corresponding cyclotomic coset with respect to GF(a). Therefore, the codes obtained using the two constructions are equivalent. However, it is possible that none of the elements with order k is a prime power and hence cyclotomic cosets cannot be defined. Thus, it suffices to focus on only the QC LDPC codes constructed using cosets.
C. Properties of Coset-Based QC LDPC Codes
In the construction of QC LDPC codes, girth and minimum distance are the two most important measures of performances. A relatively large girth is important for the iterative message passing decoding algorithm to converge to the optimum performance, while a large minimum distance is important to mitigate the error floor effects at high signal to noise ratio (SNR) in the decoding [20]. It has been shown that (j, k) regular SFT codes, where j ≥ 2 and k ≥ 3 have girth 6 ≤ g ≤ 12 [11] and the minimum distance lower bounded by dmin ≥ j + 1 for j odd and dmin ≥ j + 2 for j even [14] and upper bounded by dmin ≤ (j + 1)! [15]. The following two lemmas give the girth and the minimum distance bounds of the coset-based QC LDPC codes (the proofs are omitted due to limited space).
Lemma 1: A coset-based (j, k) regular QC LDPC code has a girth g ≥ 6.
Lemma 2: A coset-based (j, k) regular QC LDPC code has a minimum distance j + 1 ≤ dmin ≤ (j + 1)!. Furthermore, all codewords have even Hamming weights and hence dmin ≥ j + 2 for even j.
Therefore, the coset-based QC LDPC codes have similar girth and minimum distance bounds to those for the SFT codes. Thus, we conjecture that, given any SFT code, it is easy to find a coset-based QC LDPC code with the same parameters and similar performances as opposed to the given SFT code. We have simulated the bit error rate (BER) performances over additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel with BPSK modulation of the SFT codes and the QC LDPC codes constructed using cosets and cyclotomic cosets, and our simulation results confirm our conjecture. In Fig. 3, we show the BER performance comparisons among a family of (4, 9) regular QC LDPC codes constructed using the three different methods. The three groups of curves shown in Fig. 3

correspond to m = 109, 181, and 397 (block length N = 981, 1629, and 3573) respectively. Note that in each group the codes constructed using cyclotomic cosets and cosets use different coset leaders. A maximum number of 50 iterations of message passing decoding is performed. All these codes have 3 redundant rows in their parity check matrices and hence all have roughly the same code rate of R ≈ 0.56. For given length and rate, the BER performances of the three codes constructed using different methods are almost the same. Therefore, the SFT codes or the cyclotomic-coset-based codes do not appear to be advantageous in terms of BER performances over the coset-based codes.
