Improvements on Accelerating Iterative Decoding Using Eigenmessages
Todd K. Moon, John S. Crockett, Jacob H. Gunther Utah State University

AbstractвҖ” The eigenmessage decoder has been shown to reduce the number of decoding iterations required for LDPC and other iteratively-decoded codes by introducing a degree of nonlocality into the decoding algorithm. In this paper, the multiple loop eigenmessage approach is extended using normalized matrices. Performance is examined via EXIT charts, showing that eigenmessage algorithms have wider channels in the chart. Performance as a function of the girth of the graph is examined, showing the performance to be largely invariant to girth. Finally, performance on matrices denser than typical вҖңlow densityвҖқ parity check matrices is examined, showing that eigenmessage methods perform better than message passing, but still break down as the matrix density increases.
I. INTRODUCTION AND SUMMARY
Maximum likelihood decoding on a graph is a global problem and suffers from complexity. Message passing decoding algorithms [1], [2], [3] reduce the global problem to a set of local problems which have lower complexity, but do not always find the global solution. It is well known that for a graph without cycles (i.e., a tree), the message passing algorithm terminates in a number of iterations proportional to the diameter of the graph. However, some graphs, in particular those associated with iteratively decoded codes such as turbo codes, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, or repeat-accumulate codes, do have cycles. In such cases, the performance of the message passing algorithm has been successful, but the decoding may take many iterations. For example, for a moderately large LDPC code, many hundreds of iterations may be necessary for decoding at low SNR. This high number of iterations diminishes the potential utility of such codes by increasing decoding complexity or latency. Recent work [4], [5] has developed decoding algorithms which are less local than conventional message passing in an attempt to reduce the number of iterations. This paper examines some incremental advances on the performance and understanding of the eigenmessage approach, including normalized operations, EXIT charts, and the performance as a function of the girth of the Tanner graph.

B. Results
To examine the effectiveness of the normalized multiloop and single loop eigenmessage algorithms, we present simulation results for regular LDPC codes with column weight 3. The results were averaged across ensembles of five random matrices of equal size and girth. The simulations were run for three ensembles: five (N, K) = (15000, 5000) вҖңlargeвҖқ codes, five (5000, 1666) вҖңmediumвҖқ codes, and five (1000, 333) вҖңsmallвҖқ codes. All simulations were limited to a maximum of 100 iterations.
As figures 2, 3 and 4 show, the multiloop-5 and multiloop10 algorithms converge in 60% to 80% fewer iterations than message passing over the range of SNRs studied, with more pronounced reduction in iterations occurring as SNR increases. The single loop algorithm shows even more remarkable acceleration, consistently reducing iterations by 70% to 80% and nearly always outperforming the multiloop algorithms. For example, for large matrices at 1.2 dB SNR the single loop algorithm requires 75% fewer iterations than message passing!

III. EXIT CHARTS The trajectory defined by the EXIT chart [6] seems to define an unavoidable limit on the number of iterations required by the iterative decoding algorithms: the algorithm must iterate enough times to move through the channel of the EXIT chart. However, the nonlocality introduced by single- and multiple-eigenmessage approaches actually opens up the EXIT chart channel somewhat. With the wider EXIT channel, the number of iterations is reduced. The effect on the decoding threshold will also be examined. In figure 5 the EXIT charts are shown for MP decoding. At higher SNR the path or channel is wider, allowing decoding

in fewer iterations. At lower SNR the channel almost pinches off, requiring many more steps for the algorithm to converge. Compare this MP decoder behavior to the EXIT charts for single loop eigenmessage decoding in figure 6. At higher SNR the eigenmessage algorithm opens up the channel so that decoding can take place in very few steps. As the SNR decreases, the channel pinches off (as in the message passing case), but the part of the channel that gets pinched off is very short. The rest of the channel is still relatively wide, allowing the decoding before and after the pinchoff to progress rapidly. The EXIT charts for multiloop eigenmessage decoding are qualitatively similar to those for single loop decoding. EXIT charts were created in many trials and those shown here demonstrate typical behavior.

We also studied the effects of girth (length of shortest cycle) on the decoding behavior of the algorithms. Since the eigenmessage algorithms are based on cycles in the graph, it is reasonable to suppose that changes in girth would effect the decoding. V. MOVING TOWARD DENSE MATRICES
Finally, we present some experiments on the ability of eigenmessage decoders to decode on matrices which are not totally sparse. (A motivation for such an exploration arises in the interest of a general linear decoder, or for an attack on a McEliece public key encryption system [7].)
It is known [8] that as the parity-check matrix becomes more dense, the BP decoding moves further and further away from ML or optimal decoding. Not only does the number of calculations needed grow as an obvious result of the increase of edges in the graph, but the probability of decoding error also grows, so much so that BP is no longer worthwhile as a decoding algorithm for any but the sparsest matrices.
While ML decoding is independent of the particular equivalent parity-check matrix employed, BP decoding is not. In spite of the invariance of the matrix rowspace, the BP decoding effectiveness quickly deteriorates as the density increases.
We tested the effectiveness of three decodersвҖ”BP, singlecycle eigenmessage, and multiple-cycle eigenmessage with group size fiveвҖ”on an ensemble of matrices. We created five random matrices of reasonably non-trivial yet manageable size 667 Г— 1000. Each of these matrices had a constant column weight of three. Each matrix was then incrementally row reduced, saving at intervals along the way. We thus had five sets of matrices, each set containing a traditional sparse matrix

VI. CONCLUSION
In summary, the following observations may be drawn from these experimental results:
1) Single and multiple eigenmessage approaches reduce the number of decoding iterations required, while also slightly reducing the probability of error. These experiments are further substantiated by EXIT charts, which exhibit wider channels for eigenmessage decoding than MP decoding.
2) The decoder performance seems largely invariant to the girth of the graph of the code.
3) The eigenmessage decoder performs somewhat better for denser matrices than MP decoding, but still breaks down as the matrix weight increases.
