Quasi-Cyclic Codes from Extended Difference Families

Tao Xia University of Arizona Email: xt9410@yahoo.com

Bo Xia Intel Corporation Email: bo.xia@intel.com

Abstract— Quasi-cyclic codes are renowned for their structural design and low-complexity shift register encoding. However, existing design techniques based on difference families have limited code size options due to algebraic constraints. We introduce in this paper a notation of extended difference family (EDF) and provide a systematic code design based on EDFs with a high degree of flexibility in code size. Short/medium-length codes of high rate (≥ 0.9) can be easily constructed.
I. INTRODUCTION
Since the introduction of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes by Gallager [1] and their rediscovery [2], [3], the battlefield of code design has been divided into two opposite regimes: random constructions [3], [4] and structured algebraic constructions [5]–[11]. Random constructions usually specify some design constraints such as the absence of length-four cycles (i.e., at least of girth six) and check/variable degree distributions (for irregular codes) or parity-check matrix row/column weights (for regular or near-regular codes), and randomly search for a parity-check matrix until the specified constraints are satisfied. Though flexible in design and generally good in performance, random constructions do not facilitate performance analysis for finite-length codes due to their randomness and difficulties exist for constructions of very-high-rate short/medium-length codes.
Algebraic constructions, on the contrary, are usually specified by a small number of parameters. The codes designed can be highly regular and are absent of length-four cycles. These codes lend themselves to a very low-complexity shift-register encoding implementation [5]–[10]. Performance analysis, or at least a minimum distance bound, is made easier due to the algebraic structure, and high-rate short codes can also be easily constructed. These designs, however, are only capable of designing codes of certain lengths/rates, and hence are not sufficiently flexible for practical applications.
Kirkman triple systems based on difference families (DF) (m, 3, 1)-DF have been used in [8]–[10] to construct codes with column weight 3. Constructions of (m, w, 1)-DF with a prime power m ≡ 1 mod w(w − 1) and w = 4, 5 have also been presented in [12] which may lead to quasi-cyclic codes of column weight w = 4 and 5. A major drawback of this design, however, is the lack of flexibility in the code size selection due to the constraint on m.
In order to achieve an increased code size flexibility, we propose a notation extended difference family (EDF) and construct quasi-cyclic codes based on this notaion. The general

idea behind this is to have a family of t sets of w integers whose intra-set distances are distinct, and a rate (t − 1)/t mt, m(t − 1) quasi-cyclic code of column weight w can be constructed from this EDF. It can be shown that, as long as m is greater than twice the maximum integer in this EDF, the code is absent of length-four cycles. High-rate codes (rate ≥ 0.9) can be easily constructed with this design.
The paper is organized as follows. Section II gives a short tutorial on quasi-cyclic codes based on difference families. The notation of extended difference family is introduced in Section III and we provide a heuristic algorithm to search for EDFs. We discuss the construction of quasi-cyclic codes based on extended difference families in Section IV, and show the highest code rate achievable with this design. Performance results of some high-rate quasi-cyclic codes are shown in Section V, and concluding remarks are given in Section VI.

II. QUASI-CYCLIC CODES FROM DIFFERENCE FAMILIES
Definition 1: Let F = {B1, . . . , Bt} be a family of wsubsets of an additive group G of order m. We say that F is a (m, w, 1) difference family, or (m, w, 1)-DF in short, if any nonzero element of G can be represented exactly in one way as a difference of two elements lying in a same member of F. The members of a DF are called base blocks, and obviously m ≡ 1 mod w(w − 1) [12].
We define an intra-set difference as the difference of two elements lying in a same member of F . The above definition therefore implies that
• Any two intra-set differences are distinct. • The set of all intra-set differences comprise the non-zero
elements of the group G.

The easy construction of quasi-cyclic codes and their lowcomplexity shift register encoding have drawn much interest among researchers [5]–[10]. The most well-studied quasi-cyclic codes are derived from (m, 3, 1)-DF, also known as Kirkman triple systems. Very high rate codes of medium lengths have been reported in [8] which is not possible with random constructions.
It has been shown in [13] that a (m, 3, 1)-DF always exists in GF(m) for a prime power m ≡ 1 mod 6. It was further shown in [12] that a (m, w, 1)-DF also exists for w = 4, 5 when

Despite its low encoding complexity and easy construction, this method is rather inflexible due to the restriction on m. Consider a design with w = 5. Then it is required that m = 20t + 1 be a prime power according to (3). The only integers within [100, 200] that satisfy this condition are 101, 121, and 181, and it is not possible to design DF-QC codes with a different number of check equations than these integers.
In the next few sections, we will discuss constructions of quasi-cyclic codes from extended difference families toward the goal of increased flexibility.

III. EXTENDED DIFFERENCE FAMILIES
Definition 2: Let F = {B1, . . . , Bt} be a family of sets of w non-negative integers. We say that F is a (w, t) extended difference family, or (w, t)-EDF in short, if all intra-set difference magnitudes (distances) are distinct. The largest of all distances is denoted by dmax.
Lemma 1: A (m, w, 1)-DF is a (w, t)-EDF when m = tw(w − 1) + 1.
This is evident since any intra-set distance of a (m, w, 1)-DF is uniquely mapped to an element in GF(m). Thus the distances are distinct.

The restrictions on extended difference families are rather
loose compared with difference families in that distances in N rather than differences in the group GF(m) are considered (so m is not necessary in the definition) and that the set of distances is not inclusive of all integers from 1 to dmax as is in difference families due to the lack of algebraic structure. An example (3, 3)-EDF is {0, 1, 10}, {0, 2, 8} and {0, 3, 7} whose intra-set distances are {1, 9, 10}, {2, 6, 8} and {3, 4, 7}. The dmax of this EDF is 10, and we observe the absence of distance 5. We have the following lemma about dmax for an EDF.
Lemma 2: For a (w, t)-EDF, dmax ≥ dm0 ax = tw(w − 1)/2. This is evident since the total number of different distances in a (w, t)-EDF is tw(w − 1)/2.
The equation in Lemma 2 is in general not satisfied except for a few special cases. For example the (2, t)-EDF, Bl = {0, l}, l = 1, . . . , t. Another example is a (3, 4)-EDF {0, 1, 12}, {0, 2, 8}, {0, 3, 10} and {0, 4, 9}. As will be seen in the following section, minimizing dmax is beneficial to a more flexible code design and may result in codes of higher rates.
We propose the following heuristic while simple algorithm to find a (w, t)-EDF for w ≥ 3. We claim that this algorithm may not be optimal in the sense that the value dmax it finds may not be the smallest possible.
1) Initialize for each set Bl = {0, l}, the maximum element ml = l, l = 1, . . . , t, and the distance set as D = (1, . . . , t).
2) Sort {Bl} according to ml in descending order. 3) For each set Bl in the sorted order, find the smallest
positive integer d ∈/ D and the candidate integer c = d + ml. Compute the new distances D = |c − Bl|. If D ∩ D = φ, insert c into Bl as ml = c, and merge D into D. Otherwise, repeat this step with the next smallest integer d ∈/ D until this step succeeds. 4) If all Bl are of order w, a (w, t)-EDF has been found. Otherwise, go back to step 2.

Statistically, Ht−1 has a high density of ‘1’ despite the low density of Ht. Thus the encoding has a complexity in the order of m2. A simple solution is to force Ht into a lower-triangular
form by erasing all 1’s in its upper triangle excluding the diagonal. The parity vector is hence pT = Ht−1cT , where c is the output of the shift register. Since Ht is lower triangular, the equation HtpT = cT may be solved recursively with a

lower-triangular form guarantees that Ht is invertible and that
the new parity-check matrix H has a girth no smaller than the

V. SIMULATIONS
We constructed three rate-0.9 EDF-QC codes (63, 3, 10), (139, 4, 10), and (247, 5, 10). Their length parameters are (630, 567), (1390, 1251), and (2470, 2223), respectively. Fig. 3 plots error performance results for these codes in additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels with binary signaling. In simulations, the encoding process is bypassed by using all-zeros input words. A maximum number of 100 decoding iterations is used in the message-passing decoding. The error probabilities of codewords (Pcw) and bits (Pb) are recorded for information bits only. With the help of importance sampling technique for low-density parity-check codes [16], we have been able to simulate the code performance to as low as Pcw = 10−8 (7 and 7.5 dB for (63, 3, 10) code, 6 dB for (139, 4, 10) code) as an attempt to locate their error floors. VI. CONCLUSIONS
We have presented in this paper a structural design of quasi-cyclic codes based on extended difference families. A generalization of quasi-cyclic codes based on difference families, this design inherits the ease of construction that is not enjoyed by random code constructions, while stands out with its great flexibility in the code size selection.
