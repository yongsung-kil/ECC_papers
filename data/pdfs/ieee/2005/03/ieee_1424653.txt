Abstract— The evaluation of the minimum distance of LowDensity Parity-Check (LDPC) codes remains an open problem due to the rather large dimension of the parity check matrix H associated with any practical code. In this article, we propose an effective modification of the Error Impulse (EI) technique for estimation of the minimum distance of the LDPCs. The EI method is successfully applied to sub-optimum decoding algorithms such as the iterative MAP decoding algorithm for Turbo Codes. We present novel modifications and extensions of this method to the suboptimum iterative sum-product algorithm for LDPCs. Simulation results validate the functionality of the proposed technique. Simulations focus on a particular class of LDPC codes, but our approach is general and applies to any LDPC code.
I. INTRODUCTION AND PROBLEM FORMULATION
The problem of evaluating the minimum distance of a LDPC code remains open due to the high dimension of the matrix Hn−k,n specifying the parity check equations defining the code. Indeed, in [1] it has been shown that determination of the minimum distance cannot be accomplished with a polynomial time algorithm.
The information about the minimum distance of a linear code can be useful to predict its performance in the lowBER/FER region where one must inevitably resort to extremely long simulations to verify performance.
An effective algorithm for evaluating the minimum distance of turbo codes, called the Error Impulse (EI) was proposed in [2], [3]. Although the rationale behind the method hypothesized a ML decoder, the algorithm has shown reasonably good performance with the sub-optimal iterative decoding algorithms used for turbo-codes. In order to keep the presentation concise, we refer the interested reader to [3] for details on the theoretical rationale beyond the algorithm. Suffice it to say that the algorithm exploits the capabilities of a ML soft decoder to prevent EI input patterns. With a reasoning based on the Euclidean space geometry, in [3] it was shown that under the hypothesis that the all-zero codeword is transmitted, the minimum distance of a linear code is equal to the minimum error impulse strength Ai over all the codeword bits i = 1, . . . , n which is able to make the iterative decoder diverge from the detected all-zero vector. A drawback of this algorithm for LDPCs is that it could converge toward a minimum distance higher than the actual one because of

the sub-optimality of the iterative decoder and presence of

decoding cycles.

In this paper, we propose a different approach for the esti-

mation of the minimum distance dm which has its basis in the EI technique proposed in [3]. Other authors [4] have proposed

methods based on EI for minimum distance estimation, but

our method is different and employs application of the error

impulse twice in a two level search. Unlike the EI technique

whereby one can only obtain information about the minimum

distance of the code, the proposed algorithm evaluates the

minimum distance by enumerating the codewords, along with

their multiplicities and the Hamming weights of the input error

events, to which the sum-product decoder for LDPCs converge

when perturbed by an error pattern constituted by at most

two EIs located in two different codeword positions. The idea

is to perturb the all-zero transmitted codeword with a noise

pattern able to make the iterative decoder diverge from the

all-zero decoded word toward a codeword c that with high

probability, would be the minimum distance codeword (i.e.,

the codeword with the smallest Hamming distance from the

all-zero codeword). Then, knowing the strength and position

i (with i = 1, . . . , n) where the impulse is applied in the span

of the codeword that makes the decoder diverge from the all-

zero codeword, in the proposed algorithm we apply a second

impulse spanning all the codeword bit positions j (∀i = j and

j = 1, . . . , n) in order to list all the detected codewords close

to c and different from the all-zero codeword.

As far as the validity of the results furnished by the algorithm is concerned, we should say that at least theoretically, there is no guarantee of a correct result since the proposed algorithm as well as the original EI technique is based on the assumption of a true maximum-likelihood decoder, while the sum-product algorithm is sub-optimal specially when the Tanner graph of the code contains small length cycles as it is the case in a practical setting. We verified experimentally that good results can be obtained by keeping iterating the LDPC iterative decoder until the maximum number of iterations Nit, even in cases in which a valid codeword is found before reaching the maximum number of iterations Nit.
We have verified the performance of the proposed technique by checking that the minimum distance furnished by the algorithm was not above the bound dm,b, and by verifying the agreement between simulation and the upper-bound curve as shown in the next section. Furthermore, we have made many other tests not reported here with small size LDPCs (e.g., H having dimension on the order of n − k ∝ 10 to 40), and rate Ro = 1/2 LDPCs. In such cases, we evaluated the true dm of the code by inspection of the matrix H, and we verified that the algorithm always furnished the correct dm. Nevertheless, one must recognize that in a practical setting, the matrix H is often so large that it is nearly impossible to evaluate the minimum distance of the LDPC using a classical approach.

IV. CONCLUSIONS
This article has focused on the problem of estimating the minimum distance of a LDPC using a variant of the EI technique already successfully applied to sub-optimum decoding algorithms such as the iterative MAP decoding of Turbo Codes. We proposed a modification of the EI algorithm applicable to the suboptimum iterative sum-product decoder for LDPCs. Simulation results have confirmed the functionality of the proposed algorithm.
