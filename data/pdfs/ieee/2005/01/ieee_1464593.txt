A Memory-Based Architecture for FPGA Implementations of Low-Density Parity-Check Convolutional Decoders
Stephen Bates and Gary Block Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering
University of Alberta, Edmonton, Canada. Email: {stephen.bates,gblock}@ece.ualberta.ca

Abstract— Low-density parity-check convolutional codes complement

their popular block-oriented counterparts and may be more suitable in

certain communication applications. These include streaming voice and

video and packet switching networks.

In this paper we introduce these codes and propose a memory-based

decoder architecture that is well suited for implementation on Field-

Programmable Gate Arrays. We present an overview of the architecture

and demonstrate its efficiency over register-based architectures.

We then discuss a realization of this architecture that can trade per-

formance for throughput and can achieve up to 120 Mb/s of information

I. INTRODUCTION

Research into the implementation of low-density parity-check

encoders and decoders has, to date, focused exclusively upon block

oriented codes (LDPC-BCs). Implementations of LDPC-BC encoders

and decoders have been reported in industry and academia for both

ASICs and FPGAs (e.g. [1], [2] and [3]). These implementations

can achieve good performance with respect to power and area when

compared with other coding schemes [4].

However Low-Density Parity-Check Convolutional Codes (LDPC-

CCs) [5] may be better suited to certain applications than their block

counterparts. For example LDPC-CCs are able to encode and decode

arbitrary lengths of data without the need to fragment them into fixed-

sized blocks. This may be advantageous for streaming applications

such as streaming video, audio and satellite radio.

Another potential application of LDPC-CCs is packet switching

communication systems such as those based on the Ethernet frame

format [6]. The Ethernet frame can vary in its length and this makes

the integration of a LDPC-BC difficult. Using LDPC-CCs removes

the need to accommodate randomly-sized packets into fixed-sized

blocks.

However, until LDPC-CC encoder and decoder architectures have

been implemented in ASICs and FPGAs, the true benefit of adopting

them is unclear. Architectures suitable for ASIC implementations of

LDPC-CC decoders tend to be register-intensive and as such are not

optimal for FPGA implementations [7]. In this paper we focus on a

LDPC-CC decoder architecture that is more suited to implementation

on modern FPGAs where Logic Elements (LEs) are complemented

by large amounts of on-chip memory [8]. This architecture takes

advantage of the dual-port nature of much of this memory and leads

to a design that balances the utilization of the FPGA’s memory and

LEs.

This architecture was implemented on an Altera Stratix S80 devel-

opment board donated by the Canadian Microelectronics Corporation.

The design achieved an information throughput of up to 120 Mb/s

and a BER as low as 2 × 10−6

at an

to trade throughput for performance in a dynamic fashion.

We introduce LDPC-CCs in Section II. In Section III we present some simulation results to show that LDPC-CCs have the same desirable code properties as their block counterparts. In Section IV we show that register-based LDPC-CC decoder architectures are not well suited to FPGAs. In Section V we present a memory-based architecture for a LDPC-CC decoder that is well suited to most modern FPGA devices. We go on to present synthesis results and performance results for a realization of this architecture and show that it performs as predicted by simulations. We finish in Section VII with conclusions and a discussion of ongoing and future work.
II. AN OVERVIEW OF LDPC-CCS
LDPC-CCs were first proposed by Felstro¨m and Zigangarov [5]. They are similar to LDPC-BCs in that they generate code-bits based on parity-check operations [4]. However they differ in that any given code-bit, v(t), is generated using only previous information bits and previously generated code-bits. This implies that the parity-check matrix for LDPC-CCs is inherently lower diagonal and this simplifies the encoding process and reduces encoding latency [4].
An important parameter of an LDPC-CC is its memory, M . The performance of well designed LDPC-CCs increases with M but so too does the encoder and decoder complexity.

LDPC-CCs can be decoded using the message passing algorithm

similar to that used for LDPC-BCs [4]. The decoding operation is per-

formed by units termed processors [5]. Each processor is comparable

to an iteration in an LDPC-BC and, as such, performance improves

as we add more processors until some limit is met. However, unlike

an LDPC-BC iteration, each processor is a physically independent

unit consisting of delay elements, a single check node and a single

variable node. Each processor is identical and the outputs of processor

i − 1 are the inputs to processor i. These inputs and outputs are

Log-Likelihood Ratios (LLRs) and for the first processor the inputs

are the channel LLRs. For more information of the structure of the

LDPC-CC decoder see [5].

IV. REGISTER BASED LDPC-CC DECODERS FOR FPGAS
LDPC-CC decoders are very register-intensive because they require a delay line of at least length M + 1 per processor [5]. In addition, this delay line must be wide enough to store all the partial LLRs for the information bits and the code-bits. This results in at least 8 × (M + 1) × W registers per processor where W is the wordlength of the LLR.
If a register based LDPC-CC decoder design, suitable for a highspeed ASIC implementation, is synthesized for an FPGA, the results are disappointing. As summarized in Table I, the utilization of Logic Elements (LEs) on the target device for a single processor is quite high. This is due to the fact that the synthesis tool has no choice but to implement the registers using LEs.

A register-based LDPC-CC decoder must pass LLRs along a FIFO delay line. However, an alternative to doing this is to place the LLRs into a circular buffer whose current position is tracked using a pointer.
This circular buffer can be implemented using on-chip RAM in an FPGA. In addition most modern devices allow this RAM to be configured to have multiple ports [8]. Therefore multiple memory read and write operations can be performed in a single clock cycle.
To reduce the number of read and write operations required per memory entity we split the circular buffer into eight columnar RAMs (see Fig. 3). Each RAM is of height P words and of width W bits. In reality FPGA tools constrain us to choose the width to be a multiple of 8. Hence we set the LLR precision to W = 8. The eight columnar RAMs store the four information LLRs and four code LLRs from time t back to time t − P + 1.
In order to perform the decoding operation we are required to perform a certain number of operations on this circular buffer. These operations are discussed in Sections V-A and V-B
A. Input and Output LLR Update
It can be seen from Fig. 2 that each processor requires input LLRs and must generate output LLRs. The input LLRs for processor i come from either the channel (if i = 0) or from processor i − 1. The outputs from processor i either go to the decision device (i = I − 1) or processor i + 1. Here, I represents the total number of processors in the decoder.
At any given time, t, the address to which the input LLRs must be written can be generated using a modulo P counter. The output LLRs are the oldest LLRs in the buffer which are therefore located at p (t) = (p(t)+1) mod P where mod represents the modulo operator. The pointer can then be updated such that p(t + 1) = p (t). Note that the time between t and t + 1 should be equal to the time taken to receive one codeword. We refer to this time as the baud period.
The writing (reading) of the input (output) information LLRs is shown in phase (a) of Fig. 3. Note that if the memory is dual-port that we can perform both the read and write operations in a single clock cycle. The LLRs output from the memory are then processed by the variable node before being output to processor i + 1 or the decision device (see Fig. 2).
B. Parity-Check Node LLR Update

C. Over-clocking the Circular Buffer
The fact that we are required to perform all the memory operations in Sections V-A and V-B in a single baud period implies we require quad-port RAM or a clock to address the circular buffer which is ×2 the speed of the base clock.
However a further complication exists. As can been seen on the righthand side of Fig. 3, any given parity-check read (write) operation many involve up to two reads (writes) of a given column of RAM. For example, in phase (b) of Fig. 3 memory bank RAM3 is read twice. Therefore the worst case total number of reads (writes) required in a single baud period can be up to three. Hence the clock driving the RAM circuitry is required operate at ×3 the baud period.
In order to co-ordinate the circular buffer operations some additional multiplexors are required (labeled A to E in Fig. 2). These assist in the transfer of memory addresses and read or write values from one clock domain to another.

VI. A MEMORY-BASED LDPC-CC DECODER REALIZATION

As noted in Section V, the memory architecture is quite complex.

In order to validate the simulation and synthesis results presented in

Sections III and V it was important to implement this architecture on

an actual FPGA.

The Canadian Microelectronics Corporation supplied an Altera

Stratix DSP development board which consists of a Stratix EP1S80

FPGA as well as on-board RAM and interfaces. A 10-processor

decoder was implemented on the FPGA along with a test pattern

generator and a pseudo-AWGN (Additive White Gaussian Noise)

generator. The performance of this AWGN generator was validated

via simulation. The Stratix device was provided with a 80 MHz clock

from a crystal and an on-chip PLL was used to generate synchronous

40 MHz and 120 MHz clocks.

VII. CONCLUSIONS AND ONGOING WORK

In this paper we have focused on an implementation of a LDPC-

CC decoder that is well suited to implementation on FPGAs. This

implementation is memory-based and employs a RAM and a ROM in

a co-ordinated fashion to perform both processor input-output LLR

update and parity-check input-output update. We showed how this

can be accommodated by clocking the RAM and its interface at ×3

the frequency of the remaining circuitry. Synthesis results show that

this architecture is over five times more efficient than a register based

approach when targeting FPGAs. We also showed that it achieves a

good balance of logic element and memory utilization on an Altera

Stratix EP1S80.

We presented a realization of this architecture which can achieve

up to 120 Mb/s of throughput and a BER as low as 2 × 10−6 at an

3 dB.

An alternative architecture is currently being developed which will

permit the clocking of memory at ×2 the baud rate. This will increase

the throughput of the device. In addition, the application of this

memory-based architecture to more modern FPGAs and to ASICs

It is difficult to compare the decoder presented in this paper with existing LDPC-BC implementations due to the differing target hardware. In [3] a slower (54 Mb/s) decoder was implemented on a Xilinx Virtex-E FPGA. However its BER performance was better (10−6 at 2 dB). In [1] the decoder throughput was much higher (512 Mb/s) because the design was implemented as an ASIC. The block error rate was about 10−5 at 3 dB. However LDPC-BCs have been the focus of research for several years where as LDPC-CCs are much more recent. To the authors’ knowledge this is the first hardware LDPC-CC decoder to be implemented. Our results are encouraging enough to suggest that further investigation is warranted.
