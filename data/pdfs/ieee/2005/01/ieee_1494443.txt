Noise Predictive Belief Propagation

Mustafa N. Kaynak Tolga M. Duman Department of Electrical Engineering Arizona State University Tempe, AZ 85287-5706 {kaynak, duman}@asu.edu

Erozan M. Kurtas Seagate Technology 1251 Waterfront Place Pittsburgh, PA 15222-4215 erozan.m.kurtas@seagate.com

Abstract— We introduce iterative noise whitening for belief propagation (BP) based channel detectors over inter-symbol interference (ISI) channels with correlated noise. Called noise predictive belief propagation (NPBP), the new scheme iteratively whitens the noise samples by modifying the edge probability computation of the BP algorithm. NPBP detectors based on finite impulse response (FIR) and infinite impulse response (IIR) prediction filters are introduced. In addition, we propose a novel prediction filter optimization method leading to a better noise whitening performance. Simulation results for both coded and uncoded systems and comparisons with maximum a posteriori (MAP) and BP detectors show that, significant improvements can be obtained.
I. INTRODUCTION
ISI channels are encountered in many applications, including wireless communications and magnetic recording. To recover ISI channel inputs, usually trellis based MAP and Viterbi algorithms are used. More recently, BP algorithm has been proposed for this purpose as well [1]-[2]. When the factor graph of the ISI channel is length 4 cycle free, BP gives a similar performance with the MAP detector, while it has the advantage of having a parallel structure.
Some ISI channels encountered in practice do not have additive white noise. For instance, magnetic recording channels equalized to a partial response target have correlated noise due to the linear equalizer used to shape the channel impulse response. Over these channels, channel detectors assuming additive white Gaussian noise are not optimal.
A trivial way to improve the detector performance over the ISI channels with correlated additive noise is to whiten the noise. In [3]-[4], noise predictive maximum likelihood (NPML) detection is proposed by modifying the branch metric computation of the Viterbi algorithm by using an FIR prediction filter and the “tentative” hard decisions from the surviving path of each state. Moreover in [5], NPML detection based on IIR filters is proposed. In general, these detectors utilizing FIR or IIR prediction filters improve the performance significantly over recording channels.
In this paper, we propose noise predictive belief propagation detector to iteratively whiten the additive noise over ISI channels with correlated noise, as an extension of the classical BP algorithm. We consider NPBP detection based on both FIR and IIR prediction filters.

The main difference between NPML and NPBP detection is that, the decision feedback is local for NPML detection, while it is global for NPBP detection. Another difference is that, unlike the NPML detector which generates hard decisions, the proposed detector is a soft-input soft-output algorithm, which is very advantageous for the coded systems.
Over magnetic recording channels, we show that, the proposed detector effectively whitens the additive noise and improves the bit error rate (BER) performance significantly, outperforming both BP and MAP detectors when noise prediction is not performed. The complexity does not increase significantly either. In addition, the performance improvement is preserved when an outer error correcting code is used. Since the NPBP detector has a parallel and iterative structure, it results in simplified and fast hardware implementations. It also has a much shorter decoding delay, which reduces the memory requirements significantly.

In Section IV, the NPBP detector is introduced. Simulation results are given in Section V and the paper is concluded in Section VI.

III. FACTOR GRAPHS AND BELIEF PROPAGATION
ISI channels can be represented using factor graphs [1]. For instance, Fig. 2 shows the factor graph of the PR4 channel having an impulse response of H(D) = 1 − D2. In the factor graph, the circle shaped variable nodes represent the channel inputs and the triangle shaped function nodes represent the channel outputs. An edge between these nodes represents a conditional dependency. In a factor graph, a “cycle” refers to a loop starting and ending at the same node. Clearly, the PR4 channel factor graph shown in Fig. 2 is cycle-free.

Belief propagation is an iterative algorithm based on the information exchange between the variable and the function nodes. The algorithm works briefly as follows. At the first half of each iteration, function nodes calculate the a-posteriori probability for the connected variable nodes, using the extrinsic a-priori information passed from the connected variable nodes as well as the channel output and the ISI channel constraints. Then at the second half of the iteration, the variable nodes update their a-priori information based on the a posteriori information passed from the connected function nodes. After a number of iterations, the bit decisions are made. The details of the algorithm can be found in [1].
BP algorithm is commonly employed in the artificial intelligence community and it is also known as the “message passing” or the “sum-product” algorithm. BP represents a broad set of decoding algorithms and the original turbo decoding algorithm is shown to be a special case [6].
Compared to the MAP algorithm which has a serial structure, the main advantage of the belief propagation algorithm is its parallel structure, which is very advantageous for practical systems. Furthermore, BP achieves the optimal MAP performance if the factor graph is cycle free and near optimal performance if the cycles are not short [2].

B. NPBP Detection with IIR Prediction Filters
In [5], IIR noise prediction filters are proposed to achieve the performance of the NPML detector utilizing long FIR prediction filters. One problem is that, long IIR filters may cause error propagation. Therefore, it is convenient to use short IIR filters.
For NPBP detection, we consider a single pole IIR prediction filter, with E(D) = 1/(1 + αD), |α| < 1. Assuming error free decision feedback, the D-transform of the whitened noise sequence is given as

e(D) = w(D)/(1 + αD) = w(D) − w˜(D), (7)

where w(D) and w˜(D) represent the original and the predicted noise sequences respectively. In time domain, w˜(D) satisfies w˜n = α.(wn−1 − w˜n−1) = α.en−1. For BP algorithm, wn − α.en−1 is used as the whitened noise sample. The derivation of the IIR prediction filter coefficients is given in [5].

D. NPBP Detection Implementation Issues
Regarding the NPBP detection, there exist some important implementation issues arising from its parallel and iterative structure. One issue is at which iteration to start the noise prediction. In order to avoid error propagation caused by the incorrect decision feedback, the hard decisions should be very accurate. Therefore, first NP step should be performed after the BP detector converges. For instance, BP detector converges after approximately 10 iterations for the PR4 channel.
Another implementation issue is the frequency of the NP steps. Our simulations have shown that, NP should be performed at every 5 to 10 iterations, instead of every iteration, as it usually takes 5 iterations for the BP algorithm to converge after each NP step. Moreover, the first 3-4 NP steps improve the BER performance significantly and after that, additional NP steps improve the performance only slightly. Therefore, it is sufficient to perform 3-4 NP steps.
The reference channel output that will be used for noise prediction is another implementation issue. At the first NP step, the whitened channel output is obtained as r˜n = rn −w˜n, where w˜n is calculated using Eq. 4. At the second NP step, still the original channel output rn should be used as the reference, not the whitened channel output r˜n obtained in the previous step. The reason is that, the prediction filter coefficients are calculated from the autocorrelation of the original additive noise wn. The operation of the NPBP detector is illustrated in Fig. 3.

We also study a coded system, where an outer LDPC code is used over the longitudinal recording channel (Dn = 2.86) equalized to the PR4 target. In particular, we consider (L, K, M, cw, rw, Rc) = (1010, 709, 303, 3, 10, 0.7) (code 1) and (L, K, M, cw, rw, Rc) = (1107, 986, 123, 3, 27, 0.89) (code 2) LDPC codes, where L, K and M denote the number of coded, information and parity bits respectively. Rc is the code rate and cw and rw are the column and the row weights. Fig. 8 shows the simulation results for both codes as well as the uncoded results.
As expected, outer codes introduce significant coding gains. At a BER of 10−5, for the lower rate code, the coding gain is 3.8 dB with the BP detector, compared to MAP or BP detection for the uncoded case. Length one FIR prediction filter based NPBP detector introduces an additional 0.4 dB gain, leading to an overall coding gain of 4.2 dB. Compared to the NPBP detector utilizing a length 2 FIR prediction filter, the coding gain is 3 dB with the NPBP detector utilizing a length 1 FIR prediction filter. For the higher rate code, at a BER of 10−4, coded system utilizing a BP detector achieves a 2.8 dB coding gain. If a length one FIR prediction filter is used, then the overall coding gain is around 3.3 dB.
Since the lower rate code is very powerful, improvement offered by the NPBP detector is smaller. However, the higher rate code is not as powerful, therefore noise prediction leads to a higher BER improvement. Since higher rate codes are used for recording channels, NPBP detection is very advantageous.

For our examples we use the PR4 channel as the target. Since its factor graph is cycle free, BP performs well. We also studied the use of other targets, such as general partial response targets, and observed similar performance improvements when the NPBP detector is employed. However, for some targets the BP detector performance may degrade due to the short cycles in the channel factor graph, but noise prediction will still improve its performance.

VI. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we introduced noise predictive belief propagation utilizing FIR and IIR prediction filters. We showed that, the proposed NPBP based channel detector improves the BER performance significantly for both uncoded and coded magnetic recording systems. The parallel and iterative structure of the NPBP detector leads to simple and fast hardware implementations. We also proposed a novel prediction filter optimization method by taking the bit errors into account. The proposed method leads to better performing prediction filters. Extensions of the NPBP detection scheme is possible. For instance, pattern dependent NP algorithms can be employed for channels with signal dependent media noise.
