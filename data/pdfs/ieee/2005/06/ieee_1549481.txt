Abstract- Several combinatorial properties of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, such as minimum distance, diameter, stopping number, girth and cycle-length distribution of the corresponding Tanner graph, are known to influence their performance under iterative decoding. Recently, a new class of combinatorial configurations, termed trapping sets, was shown to be of significant importance in determining the properties of LDPC codes in the error-floor region. Very little is known both about the existence/parameters of trapping sets in structured LDPC codes and about possible techniques for reducing their negative influence on the code's performance. In this paper, we will address both these problems from an algorithmic and combinatorial perspective. We will first provide a numerical study of the trapping phenomena for the Margulis code, which exhibits a fairly high error-floor. Based on this analysis, conducted for two different implementations of iterative belief propagation, we will propose a novel decoding process, termed averaged decoding. Averaged decoding provides for a significant reduction in the number of incorrectly decoded frames in the error-floor region of the Margulis code. Furthermore, based on the results of the algorithmic approach, we suggest a novel combinatorial characterizations of trapping sets in the class of LDPC codes based on finite geometries. Projective geometry LDPC codes are suspected to have extremely low error-floors, which is a property that we may attribute to the non-existence of certain smaUl trapping sets in the code graph.
I. INTRODUCTION
For many practical applications, including storage and
deep-space communication systems, one requires ex-
tremely reliable signalling techniques with bit-error rates (BERs) below 10-15. Despite the fact that long LDPC
codes can achieve excellent perfornance for low to
medium signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), sometimes these
codes tend to exhibit a sudden saturation in the BER curve
for sufficiently high SNRs. For many codes, including those based on Cayley graphs [9] or certain types of designs, error-floors exist for relatively low BERs, usually of the order of 10-8. For many other codes, error-floors cannot be observed directly, because they are out of reach
of standard simulation techniques. Certain classes of codes with error-floors below 10-11 BER were recently reported, most notably codes based on finite geometries. For these
codes it is claimed that they should not exhibit an error-
floor above a BER of 10-23 [11]. These conjectures
are largely based on approximation techniques utilizing computer-based trapping-set classifications.
The notion of a trapping set1 was first described in
'initially referred to as a near-codeword

the context of error-floor analysis in [7]. This concept
was further developed in the seminal paper [8], in which
trapping set configurations were shown to exhibit a strong influence on the point of onset as well as on the slope of the error-floor curve of LDPC codes. Since the methods described in [8] usually do not guarantee the identification of all trapping sets, and furthermore, since they are based on computer-search strategies, it is of interest to develop alternative techniques for analyzing trapping sets and error-floors. In this paper, we propose to investigate two related problems pertaining to trapping set analysis. The first problem is concerned with identifying iterative decoding algorithms that have the potential of preventing erroneous information from being "trapped" in the code graph. As will be shown in this paper, simple messageaveraging strategies can be used in such a setting. The second problem is concerned with analytically identifying classes of LDPC codes which do not contain small trapping sets, and hence are not likely to exhibit low error-floors. The best performing codes that satisfy this constraint are based on projective geometries [4]. We will show that the exceptional error-floor characteristic of projective geometry codes can be justified combinatorially, due to the fact that the line-point incidence graphs of projective geometries do not contain small trapping sets.
Finally, we will propose the study of several other code parameters related to trapping set structures, such as the spread of a trapping set.
The paper is organized as follows. Section II contains a brief description of trapping sets. Section III presents a comprehensive set of numerical results related to the existence and properties of trappino sets in the Margulis code. The same section also contains the description of a novel generalization of belief propagation decoding that leads to significant reductions in the number of erroneously decoded frames. Section IV provides a novel classification of possible trapping sets in LDPC codes based on projective geometries.

(caps) - i.e. arcs and caps not contained in any larger

arc or cap. Results pertaining to the enumeration of these

configurations will be presented elsewhere.
