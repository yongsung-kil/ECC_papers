Abstract- In this paper we construct a novel family of (3 ,p) -regular quasi-cyclic (QC) low-density parity-check
(LDPC) codes, derived from permutation theorem for Latin squares, when used over binary input Additive White Gaussian Noise (BIAWGN) channel. The codes have high code rate, girth absence of 4, good minimum distance, and sparse parity check matrices. They are designed to perform well when iteratively decoding with sum-product decoding algorithm and to allow low complexity encoding.
Keywords- quasi-cyclic, iterative decoding, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, Latin squares.
1. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes were invented by Gallager [2] in 1962, but were nearly forgotten for more than 30 years until after the introduction of turbo codes by Berrou et al [3], LDPC codes were rediscovered by Mackay [4] in 1996, and were shown to demonstrate the properties of good codes capable of asymptotically approaching Shannon limit. In recent years, LDPC codes have attracted a great deal of interest. For various channels including binary input Additive White Gaussian Noise (BIAWGN) channel, it was demonstrated [1] that with properly designed code construction parameters, LDPC codes can be used to transmit information reliably at rates which can be higher than those achievable with turbo codes. In fact, the state of art in the coding field is currently achieved by LDPC, which was accomplished by Chung et al [ 1] showing that LDPC codes can be within 0.0045dB from the
Shannon limit at BER 1O- in BIAWGN channel using a block length of 107.
The main contribution of this paper is to generalize a large
class of the construction (3, p) -regular quasi-cyclic (QC)
LDPC codes using permutation/interleaving mapping to connect the Latin squares and the block LDPC codes overGF(p), instead of the linear mapping method in [5]. The
benefit of our codes have included the [5] as its small subset and constructed more randomly. Through the simulation result, the codes have excellent BER performance over BIAWGN channels even with short block length. So for the small block

II. BACKGROUND
A. LDPC Codes
LDPC codes are designed by specifying a parity check matrix H to optimize the flow of information in the decoding process. In paritucular, H is chosen to be sparse so that the calculation of each check sum depends on few code word bits and the evaluation of code bit validity on few check sums. Using this property of LDPC codes Gallager presented iterative sum-product decoding algrithms whose complexity remains linear in the block length [2], with performance remarkably close to the Shannon limit. Recently it has been shown that the encoding complexity of LDPC codes can also be linear in the block length.
A cvcle in a Tanner graph is a sequence of connected code bits and parity checks which start and end at the same vertex in the graph and contain no other vertices more than once. The length of the cycle is simply the number edges it contains and the girth of a Tanner graph is the size of its smallest cycle. It is known that the iterative sum-product decoding algorithm converges to the optimal solution provided that the Tanner graph of the code is free of cycles. The shorter the cycles in the graph, the sooner the algorithm breaks down. To date, our proposed (3,p) LDPC codes avoid short cycles in the Tanner
graph.

IV. PERMORMANCE ANALYSIS
We employed sum-product decoding, also known as belief propagation, as presented in [6]. In Fig.5 shows the Bit Error Ratio (BER) performance of the proposed optimum codes compared with the codes using the construction method from
[5]. We analyze the proposed short block length regular QC
LDPC codes (blocklength < I0') with n = 20, (3, 20) - regular
LDPC code with code rate R = 0.8500 and redundancy of 14.50%, and when n = 28,(3,28) - regular LDPC codes with code rate R = 0.8928 and redundancy of 10.33%. Notice that the definition of redundancy commonly used for optical communications, see [5]. From the simulation results, we can conclude that our proposed codes have better BER performance than the MOLS method, even it is in the short block length. This is perhaps the primary advantage of proposed LDPC codes over random constructions and the reason we attribute to their improved performance.
