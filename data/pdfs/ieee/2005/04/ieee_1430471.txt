A Decoding for Low Density Parity Check Codes over Impulsive Noise Channels
Hiroki Nakagawa, Daisuke Umehara, Satoshi Denno, and Yoshiteru Morihiro
Graduate School of Informatics, Kyoto University Yoshidahonmachi, Sakyoku, Kyoto 606-8501 Japan
Tel: +81–75–753–5960, Fax: +81–75–753–5349 E-mail: hiroki@forest.kuee.kyoto-u.ac.jp, {umehara, denno, morihiro}@i.kyoto-u.ac.jp

Abstract— Power line channel often suffers from impulsive interference generated by electrical appliances. Therefore, power line communication makes degradation due to such impulsive interference. We introduce Middleton’s Class A noise model into a statistical model of impulsive noise environment.
In this paper, we apply LDPC (Low Density Parity Check) codes and sum-product decoding to additive white Class A noise (AWAN) channels. We propose a sum-product decoding which is suitable for AWAN channels. In addition, we show the BER (Bit Error Rate) performance of the proposed sum-product decoding in Class A noise environment by computer simulation.
I. INTRODUCTION
By the spread of the Internet, home networking attracts the attention of many people. Residential power line is one of the most attractive communication media for home networking, since almost all rooms in a house have its outlets. However, many electrical appliances frequently cause man-made electromagnetic noise on power line channels. Such man-made noise has the impulsive characteristics. This is one of technical problems to realize the power line communications with high rate and high reliability. We introduce Middleton’s Class A noise model [1] into a statistical model for such impulsive noise. Class A noise model proposed by Middleton is one of non-Gaussian noise models and is currently applied to the modeling of man-made impulsive noise channels, for example, wireless channels, power line channels and so on.
The statistical feature of Class A noise is much different from that of Gaussian noise. Therefore, the conventional receivers optimized in additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels, in general, are not suitable for Class A noise environments. Spaulding and Middleton proposed a number of optimum or sub-optimum receivers for coherent and incoherent detections in Class A noise environments [2], [3]. Besides, Umehara et al. investigated an optimum decoding for turbo codes in Class A noise environments [4]. However, no one has ever discussed the sum-product decoding for LDPC codes in Class A noise environments.
In this paper, we apply LDPC codes and sum-product decoding [5] to the AWAN channels. Gallager invented LDPC codes in 1962 [6], which are linear codes and are defined by parity-check matrices with very few non-zero entries. LDPC codes have many good characteristics. LDPC codes and sumproduct decoding are known as one of the methods obtaining high coding gain. Also, LDPC codes can be decoded in linear

time by sum-product decoding which is suitable for parallel implementation. For these reasons, we expect that LDPC codes are one of the most effective error correcting codes for power line communications with high rate and high reliability.
Many conventional digital communication systems are designed against AWGN channels. The conventional sumproduct decoding for LDPC codes is also designed against AWGN channels. Therefore, input values of the sum-product decoding are the log likelihood ratio (LLR) of received signals which is supposed to AWGN channels. If we apply the conventional sum-product decoding on power line channels, it will suffer from degradation of BER performance, because power line channels are often modeled by AWAN channels. Therefore, we propose the LLR for sum-product decoding which is suitable for Class A noise. Then, we compare the performance of the proposed sum-product decoding with that of the conventional sum-product decoding by computer simulation over AWAN channels.
In Section II, we explain the Middleton’s Class A noise model, LDPC codes, LDPC coding and sum-product decoding. Also, we propose the optimum sum-product decoding for LDPC codes over AWAN channels. In Section III, we show the BER performance of the proposed sum-product decoding over AWAN channels by computer simulation and compare the BER performance of the proposed sum-product decoding with that of the conventional sum-product decoding.

C. Tanner Graph
LDPC codes can be represented effectively by a bipartite graph called “Tanner graph” [9]. The bipartite graph is a graph whose nodes are separated into two classes and whose edges only connect two nodes between different classes. The Tanner graph corresponds to the parity-check matrix H. On the Tanner graph, nodes in one class whose number is N is called “message nodes” and nodes in another class whose number is M is called “check nodes”. The message node n corresponds to the n-th column of H for n = 1, 2, · · · , N . The check node m corresponds to the m-th row of H for m = 1, 2, · · · , M . The message node n is connected to the m-th check node if and only if the (m, n) element Hmn of H is equal to “1”. We define that A(m) is the set of message nodes which connect with check node m and B(n) is the set of check nodes which connect with message node n. B. LDPC Codes and Encoding
In this paper, we apply LDPC codes and sum-product decoding to AWAN channels. LDPC codes are invented by Gallager in 1962, which are binary linear block codes and are defined by binary parity-check matrices with very few non-zero entries. There are two types of LDPC codes. One is regular LDPC codes, and the other is irregular LDPC codes. A regular LDPC code has a parity-check matrix whose column weight is constant and an irregular LDPC code does not have constant column weight. The column weight means the number of “1” included in each column of the parity-check matrix. Generally, it is known that a regular LDPC code whose column weight is 3 has the best BER performance regardless of the code length.

IV. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we have discussed the sum-product decoding for LDPC codes over AWAN channels. First of all, we have

confirmed that the BER performance of the conventional sum-product decoding suffers from degradation over AWAN channels. The conventional sum-product decoding is designed against AWGN channel and the input value of the sumproduct decoding is the LLR of the received signal which is supposed to AWGN channel. Therefore, we have proposed the LLR for sum-product decoding which is suitable for AWAN channel. Also, we have investigated the characteristics of the LLR for AWAN channel. We have compared the BER performance of the proposed sum-product decoding with that of the conventional sum-product decoding and uncoded BPSK. Consequently, we have shown that the proposed sum-product decoding has good BER performances over AWAN channel.
