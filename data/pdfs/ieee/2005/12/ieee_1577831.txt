Generalized Quad, Hex, and Octagon LDPC Codes∗

Zhenyu Liu and Dimitris A. Pados† Department of Electrical Engineering State University of New York at Buffalo
Buffalo, NY 14260 E-mail: {zliu2, pados}@eng.buffalo.edu

Abstract— We use the theory of finite classical generalized polygons to derive and study low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. The Tanner graph of a generalized polygon LDPC code is highly symmetric, inherits the diameter size of the parent generalized polygon, and has minimum (one half) diameterto-girth ratio. We show formally that when the diameter is four or six or eight all codewords have even Hamming weight. When the generalized polygon has in addition equal number of points and lines, we see that the non-regular polygon based code construction has minimum distance that is higher at least by two in comparison with the dual regular polygon code of the same rate and length. A new minimum distance bound is presented for codes from non-regular polygons of even diameter and equal number of points and lines. Finally, we prove that all codes derived from finite classical generalized quadrangles are quasi-cyclic and we give the explicit size of the circulant blocks in the parity check matrix. Our simulation studies of several generalized polygon LDPC codes demonstrate powerful bit-error-rate performance when decoding is carried out via low complexity variants of belief propagation.
Keywords— Belief propagation, cyclic codes, generalized polygons, low-density parity-check codes, sum-product algorithm.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1], [2] are currently capturing the interest of coding theory researchers and communication systems practitioners alike. LDPC codes can be very long, and yet can be decoded effectively and practically via the iterative belief-propagation (BP) algorithm (also known as sum-product). “Good” LDPC codes are usually generated randomly by computer. Decoder-aware (BP processing) generator tuning [3] has produced codes performing extremely close to the Shannon limit. These codes, however, also exhibit several disadvantages. Frequently, they have small minimum distance inducing an early error floor. Most are irregular codes with no easy way to describe. Their generator matrices are not sparse at all, hence the encoding complexity is by all accounts high.
Lately, more and more LDPC codes with explicit algebraic/combinatorial construction are being reported [4], [5]. Compared with random LDPC codes, algebraic LDPC codes have a more symmetric structure, better minimum distance, and are easier to describe and encode. A commonly used approach when searching for good LDPC code designs is the maximization of the girth of the associated Tanner graph [5], [6]. It is well known that high girth decreases the dependence

between passing messages in the BP algorithm and improves the minimum distance of the code. An opposing, less used, objective may be the minimization of the diameter of the Tanner graph. Low diameter enables efficient BP message passing and reduces the required number of decoding iterations.
Obviously, maximum girth and minimum diameter are conflicting requirements since in any graph the girth is always upper bounded by two times the diameter length. The graphs achieving exactly this bound are called generalized polygons (GPs). According to the celebrated theorem of Feit and Higman [7], non-trivial finite GPs exist only with diameter 3, 4, 6 or 8. In this paper, we call these objects finite generalized triangles, quadrangles, hexagons, and octagons, respectively, and use them to derive corresponding LDPC codes. Interestingly, among them the finite generalized triangle codes are precisely the two-dimensional projective geometry LDPC (PG-LDPC) codes in [4]. Some codes based specifically on generalized quadrangles were discussed earlier in [8].
In this paper, we derive and study LDPC codes based on all classical generalized polygons [9]. In particular: (i) We show that even diameter (4, 6 or 8) polygons define codebooks of even weight codewords. (ii) We observe that even diameter polygons with equal number of points and lines have a regular/non-regular dual form that gives rise to two LDPC codes of the same length and rate but of different minimum distance. Theoretically, we see that the non-regular polygon code has minimum distance that is better at least by two over its regular dual. Numerical upper-bound studies indicate that the difference in minimum distance may be increasing significantly in favor of the non-regular polygon code as the code length increases. (iii) For LDPC codes from even diameter non-regular polygons with equal number of points and lines we produce a tighter minimum distance bound than the one in [10]. (iv) Using the intersection of the Singer group and the automorphism group [11] of the polygon, we prove that all codes based on classical generalized quadrangles are quasi-cyclic and give the explicit size of circulant blocks in the parity check matrix. Our simulation studies demonstrate that the LDPC codes that we derived from generalized polygons, especially from split Cayley hexagons, have superior performance, fast convergence, and very low decoding complexity (due to the low weight of the parity check matrix). We also find that many GP codes can be decoded very effectively by the low complexity normalized BP-based algorithm of [12] and, somewhat surprisingly, the induced biterror-rate (BER) can be even lower than full BP decoding.

II. GENERALIZED POLYGONS: DEFINITIONS AND NOTATION
A. Finite Fields as Projective Spaces
Consider a finite field K0 = GF (q) and its (n + 1)dimensional vector space K = GF qn+1 . If α is a prim-
qn+1 −1
itive element of K, then β = α q−1 is a primitive element of K0. Define on K the n-dimensional projective space over K0 denoted by PG (n, q) or PG (n, K0). The points of PG (n, q) are the equivalence classes [x] = {y | y = tx, t ∈ K0} = x, βx, . . . , βq−2x for all x ∈ K − {0} (all one-dimensional subspaces). The lines of PG (n, q) are the two-dimensional subspaces {t1x + t2y | t1, t2 ∈ K0} =
[y], [x], [x + βy], . . . , [x + βq−1y] , x = y ∈ K − {0}.
B. Incidence Structures
We say that a point p ∈ P is incident to a line ∈ L if and only if (p, ) ∈ I ⊆ P × L and we call Γ = (P, L, I) an incidence structure. The point-line incidence graph or simply incidence graph of Γ is an undirected bipartite graph G with vertex partitions the sets P and L and edges the elements of I. We say that Γ is a weak generalized polygon if and only if the diameter of G is half of its girth. Γ is a generalized polygon if and only if it is a weak generalized polygon and each vertex of G has degree at least three. If all point vertices have the same degree t + 1 and all line vertices have the same degree s+1, then Γ has order (s, t). Generalized polygons with finite point and line sets P, L are called finite. It can be proved that every finite generalized polygon has an order. Therefore, a finite weak generalized polygon is a finite generalized polygon if and only if s > 1 and t > 1. The incidence matrix H of Γ is the matrix with rows labeled by the lines of Γ, columns labeled by the points of Γ, and entries 1 or 0 depending on the corresponding point-to-line incidence.
For each incidence structure Γ = (P, L, I), by exchanging the roles of P and L we obtain a dual structure Γ¯ = (L, P, I¯), I¯ ⊆ L × P, where ( , p) ∈ I¯ if and only if (p, ) ∈ I. It is obvious that the dual of a (weak) generalized polygon is again a (weak) generalized polygon. The incidence matrices of Γ and Γ¯ are the transpose of each other.
Finally, we say that two generalized polygons Γ and Γ are isomorphic if there exists a one-to-one mapping η such that ηP = P , ηL = L , and (p, ) ∈ I if and only if (ηp, η ) ∈ I ; η is called an isomorphism. The automorphism is an isomorphism from Γ to itself. All automorphisms of Γ put together form the automorphism group Aut (Γ).
C. Finite generalized polygons
Finite generalized polygons with diameter n = 2 are trivial (each point is incident to every line). By Feit and Higman [7], finite generalized polygons with diameter n >= 3 and order (s, t) exist only in the following cases: 1) n = 3, s = t; 2) n = 4; 3) n = 6, st is a square; 4) n = 8, 2st is a square. If n = 3, then Γ is a projective plane. This case encompasses the two-dimensional projective geometry developments (and derived codes) in [4], as explained in the introduction. We will focus in the sequel on the cases n ≥ 4.

D. Classical Finite Generalized Quadrangles
The so called classical finite generalized quadrangles (GQs) are constructed based on either the totally isotropic subspaces of a non-degenerate (unitary or symplectic) polarity π of index two or a non-degenerate quadric q of index two on some finite projective space PG (n, q). The point set P is the set of all totally isotropic points (also called absolute points) of π or the set of points of q, respectively. The line set L is the set of all totally isotropic lines of π or the set of all lines that are contained in q, respectively. The incidence set I is the set of all pairs (p, ) ∈ P × L such that in PG (n, q) point p is on line . In the case of a unitary polarity, symplectic polarity, or a quadric, we have unitary, symplectic, or orthogonal classical GQs, respectively.
For every finite field K0, a non-degenerate quadric of index two on PG (n, K0) exists (uniquely) only if n = 3, 4, or 5 and the quadric is of hyperbolic, parabolic, or elliptic type, respectively. The GQs generated from such orthogonal polarities are usually denoted by Q(n, q) and have order (q, qn−3) [9]. Note that when n = 3 the order becomes (q, 1); therefore, Q(3, q) are weak.
A non-degenerate unitary polarity of PG (n, q) exists if and only if q is a square. Assume then that q = q02. The polarity has index 2 if and only if n = 3 or 4 and is again unique for each q up to isomorphism. The generated GQs are denoted by H(n, q) and have order (q, q0qn−3) [9]. It can be shown that H(3, q2) is isomorphic to the dual of Q(5, q).
A non-degenerate symplectic polarity of PG (n, q) with index 2 exists (uniquely) only when n = 3. The generated GQs are denoted by W (q) and have order (q, q) [9]. W (q) is isomorphic to the dual of Q(4, q). W (q) is self-dual (isomorphic to its dual) if and only if q is even.
E. Classical Finite Generalized Hexagons and Octagons
The classical generalized hexagons H(q) known as split Cayley hexagons and T (q3, q) known as twisted triality hexagons have order (q, q) and (q3, q), respectively [9]. A split Cayley hexagon is self-dual if and only if q is a multiple of three. The classical Ree-Tits generalized octagons O(q) have order (q, q2) and exist only when q = 22e+1, e = 0, 1, 2, . . . . Explicit constructions for these hexagons and octagons can be found in [9] and will not be needed herein.
III. LDPC CODES FROM GENERALIZED POLYGONS
In the rest of this paper, Γ = (P, L, I) is always a finite generalized polygon with order (s, t) and even diameter d, and K0 = GF (q) is the base field of the corresponding underlying projective space.

IV. SIMULATION RESULTS

In this section we examine the error rate performance of a few selected generalized polygon LDPC codes. In Fig. 1 we consider the H(4) (1365, 378) code with rate 0.27. Decoding is performed by either BP or the low complexity “normalized BP-based” procedure of [12] (the normalization factor is set to 2.0). For code comparison purposes, we generate a second (1365, 378) LDPC code using the Progressive EdgeGrowth (PEG) algorithm [6]. The PEG code generator has been known to produce some of the best LDPC codes. The bit node degree sequence used in the construction is density evolution optimized with maximum degree 10 and was seen by experimental trials to offer the best performing PEG LDPC codes for this setup. The exact degree sequence used, as returned by [20], is 0.6101x2 + 0.2495x3 + 0.0488x5 + 0.0046x7 + 0.0667x8 + 0.0128x9 + 0.0075x10. We can see that at BER 10−5 the BP decoded generalized polygon code H(4) is only 2.7dB away from the Shannon limit and 0.25dB away from the PEG code. Moreover –and quite interestingly– the normalized BP-based algorithm works exceptionally well on H(4) and outperforms BP decoding in the high SNR area with significantly lower computational cost. For illustration purposes, Fig. 1 also includes the regular polygon dual of H(4), H(4), which is of the same length and rate (decoded by BP). The minimum distance of H(4) is 2(42 +4+1) = 42. Using the algorithm provided in [17] to search for the small weight codeword, we see that the minimum distance of H(4) may be as high as 64 (returned upper bound). While the biterror-rate difference of H(4) and H(4) in Fig. 1 is not that pronounced, we do observe an error floor forming in the high SNR area for H(4). Fig. 2 shows that the block-error-rate of H(4) is clearly inferior to H(4). In fact, in decoding H(4) we find a small amount of undetected errors (incorrect codeword decisions) while in decoding H(4) all errors are detected errors (decoding failures).
Since GP-LDPC codes have the strongest connectivity in their Tanner graph for a given girth, convergence of the BP decoder is very fast. In Fig. 3 we plot the BER curve of H(4) under maximum BP iteration number 10, 15, and 50. At BER 10−5 the performance gap between 10 and 50 iterations is about 0.2dB; the gap between 15 and 50 iterations narrows down to about 0.1dB. Rapid BP convergence is a GP-LDPC property shared with some of the other algebraically generated codes such as EG-LDPC codes [4]. GP-LDPC codes, in addition, have small row and column weight (for example, five for this H(4) code) which coupled with normalized BP-based decoding makes the decoding cost exceptionally low.
