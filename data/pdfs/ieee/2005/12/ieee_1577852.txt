Low-Density Parity-Check Convolutional Codes Applied to Packet Based Communication Systems
Zhengang Chen, Stephen Bates and Xiaodai Dong Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering University of Alberta, Edmonton, T6G 2V4, Canada Email: {zgchen,sbates}@ece.ualberta.ca, xdong@ece.uvic.ca

Abstract— In this paper, the application of low-density paritycheck convolutional codes (LDPC-CCs) to packet based communication systems is studied. By presenting analysis of encoder and decoder design as well as simulation results for such systems, we show that LDPC-CCs are more suited to packet based applications than LDPC block codes (LDPC-BCs). This is due to their ability to operate on arbitrary lengths of data, lower encoder complexity and the performance gain of presetting and termination. Moreover, in this paper, we propose an algorithm for terminating the LDPC-CC encoder from any state to the all-zero state.
I. INTRODUCTION
Due to their near Shannon-limit performance, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have attracted a lot of interest since their rediscovery in the 1990s [1]. As very powerful LDPC codes are constructed and simulated, the incorporation of these codes into existing or future communication systems needs to be considered. Since many of today’s communication systems are packet based, we are required to organize the physical layer data sequences into frames1 for transmission. However, applying LDPC block codes (LDPC-BCs) to such systems presents several challenges. For example, how to accommodate the random packet size within a fixed LDPC block size? What happens if the frame size to be transmitted is only slightly larger than the LDPC block size? These are only a few of the questions that need to be considered.
To avoid these problems, which are specific to block codes, an alternative approach is switching to convolutional codes. Fortunately, there is a convolutional version of LDPC codes, proposed in [2], called LDPC convolutional codes (LDPCCCs). Instead of dividing the input data sequence into blocks, LDPC-CCs can operate over arbitrary lengths of data. They can be considered as convolutional codes defined by a semiinfinite and periodic low-density parity-check matrix and decoded iteratively. The periodicity of their parity-check matrices makes LDPC-CCs more structured and easier to implement. It has been shown that LDPC-CCs with memory M (see Section II for details) can achieve better performance than LDPC-BCs with block length N comparable to M [2].
LDPC-CCs have simple encoding and decoding structures that lend themselves well to ASIC implementation. The encoding complexity is only linearly proportional to the memory of
1For the purpose of this paper we used the term packet to represent a certain length of data bits to be transmitted. frame is used to refer to a specific physical layer packet (e.g. Ethernet).

the matrix. The LDPC-CC decoder uses the same message passing algorithm as LDPC-BCs. It can be split into a concatenation of identical processors, and perform the decoding continuously [2].
Another advantage of LDPC-CCs in packet based applications is that the packet encoding and decoding can be performed from a known state. Starting from the all-zero state, both the encoder and the decoder assume all previous bits are zero. Then the error probability over the first hundreds of bits is greatly reduced, because they have parity-check relations with bits of infinite log likelihood ratio (LLR). Since the information carried by leading bits of a packet is usually very important (e.g., source and destination addresses), this property is helpful to packet transmission. Similarly, at the end of a packet, the LDPC-CC encoder can be forced to the allzero state to facilitate good performance, which is not a trivial problem due to the special encoder structure of LDPC-CCs. In this paper, we show that such sequences can be determined by solving a linear system over GF(2).
This paper is organized as follows: In Section II we give the LDPC-CC definition and analyze the encoder and decoder structures. In Section III simulation results are given to show that LDPC-CCs have comparable performance to LDPC-BCs. In Section IV the application of LDPC-CCs to packet based communication systems is discussed, and presetting and termination techniques are focused upon. Some simulation results of LDPC-CCs applied to packetized transmission are presented in Section V. Conclusions are given in Section VI.
II. LDPC CONVOLUTIONAL CODE DEFINITION
Both the LDPC-CC and the LDPC-BC are defined by a parity-check matrix (denoted by H, here we use the transposed form H , in conformity with the notation in [2]). The code is defined as the null space of H, i.e., vH = 0, where v is the row vector of coded bits. The parity-check matrix H for LDPCCCs is semi-infinite and periodically time-varying. To make it easier to understand, we focus upon rate-1/2 systematic codes defined over GF(2).
Suppose the data bit sequence before encoding is u = {u(0), u(1), · · · , u(t), · · · }, then the systematic rate-1/2 coded bit sequence can be written as v = {u(0), v(0), u(1), v(1), · · · , u(t), v(t), · · · }. The typical structure of H is shown in (1) [2]. The rows with subscript u (u-rows) correspond to information bits in the codeword.

A. Encoding

The encoding of LDPC-CCs is much simpler than that of LDPC-BCs due to the “upper triangular” property (see (1)) of H . Given the information bit sequence u, the systematic codeword v can be directly obtained from the parity-check matrix. The encoding equation for parity bits is given by [2]

In the encoder equation (2), the coefficients (hu(i), h(vi)) are time-varying with period T . At time instant t = nT + φ , the phase parameter φ determines the involved coefficients. To generate the parity bit v(t), the encoder has to know previous information bits as well as previous parity bits. With
LDPC-CCs encoders, there is no need to implement a control
mechanism to divide the input sequence into fixed-length blocks.The encoder output v(t) at time t is a function of the present input u(t) and the encoder state (S). According to (2), the encoder state can be defined as the set of the last

M information bits {u(t − 1), . . . , u(t − M)}, the last M parity bits {v(t − 1), . . . , v(t − M)}, and the phase (φ ) of H . This phase parameter φ specifies current parity-check equation.
The LDPC-CC encoder can be implemented using shift registers, multiplexers and XOR gates. It is analogous to the encoder of convolutional codes. The encoding complexity for regular LDPC-CC is independent of M. For LDPC block codes, the encoding complexity can be as high as O(N2). Although linear encoding complexity LDPC-BCs can be constructed [3], the LDPC-CC encoder is still easier to implement.
B. Decoding
The LDPC-CC can be represented by an infinite Tanner graph. Although there are infinite number of nodes, each variable node is only connected to a finite number of check nodes which lie within a time span of (M + 1), and vice versa. Therefore, LDPC-CCs can also use the message passing (MP) algorithm [4] for decoding. A pipelined decoder structure was proposed in [2] for LDPC-CCs. The iterative decoding process can be divided into a concatenation of I identical processors (Fig. 1). A processor is a check/variable node processing unit, with some first-in first-out shift registers inside to contain intermediate statistics (LLRs). Each processor corresponds to a single round of iteration in the MP algorithm. (More detailed information on the LDPC-CC decoder structure can be found in [2], and we will not elaborate it in this paper.) With this pipelined structure, LDPC-CC can be decoded continuously after an initial delay. This is a good feature for many applications. In ASIC implementation, this pipelined structure is also easy to handle. The design effort can be spent on developing one processor which can then be tiled as many times as permitted or required. In addition there is no global routing required, which is necessary for LDPC-BC implementations.

IV. LDPC CONVOLUTIONAL CODES IN PACKET BASED
SYSTEMS
In packet based communication systems, the data bits are divided into frames for transmission. The frame size can vary randomly between fixed limits. This makes it difficult to employ LDPC block codes, because the overhead of fitting random sized frames into fixed-length blocks is high, especially for large block lengths. LDPC-CCs are better candidates for these systems in the sense that they can deal with arbitrary lengths of data bits.
Many packet transmission systems use Start-Of-Frame (SOF) and End-Of-Frame (EOF) delimiters in order to assist the receiver in determining where a packet begins and ends (e.g. 1000BASE-T Ethernet [7]). In these systems, the correct detection of these delimiters is straightforward. For instance, they are often chosen from the signal constellation such that they have a large minimum distance from other symbols.

Therefore it is legitimate to assume that the SOF and EOF delimiters can be correctly detected without the decoder and the beginning and ending of a coded packet is known to the decoder.
A. Presetting from the all-zero state
In section II-A we defined the state of the LDPC-CC encoder, which can be considered as a convolutional encoder with large memory and time-varying wiring between the encoder shift registers [2]. The all-zero state of the LDPCCC encoder is the state where both information bit and parity bit delay lines are filled with zeros. This is obviously a selfperpetuating state in which the encoding of an information bit equal to zero maintains the all-zero state.
Consider a received packet isolated from any others. If this packet is encoded from the all-zero state, it means all previous information and parity bits are assumed to be zero. Meanwhile, if the receiver can detect the SOF delimiter and set all the LLRs in the LDPC-CC decoder to ∞, the code can be effectively strengthened over the first bits of the packet. This is because any parity check involving infinite LLRs has a lower degree and becomes a stronger parity check. We call this encoder and decoder initialization process presetting.
In most packet formats, the header field of a packet carries important address information. Take Ethernet frame format as an example, the destination and source Media Access Control (MAC) addresses are located in the beginning of a frame. Enhanced error protection over these address fields is advantageous for low latency cut-through switching [8], where packets are routed as soon as their destination address is evaluated and before error checking. When the header of a packet is correct, it can be forwarded to the correct recipient, and a retransmission from the correct source address can be requested even if the whole packet is not error-free. On the other hand, wrong header field may result in much more unpredictable consequences.
B. Termination to the all-zero state
The performance of LDPC-CCs towards the end of a packet depends on how we conclude the packet. For convolutional codes, it is well known that the preferred way is to terminate and return the encoder to the all-zero state. Similarly, we want the LDPC-CC encoder to return to the all-zero state (called termination). However, LDPC-CC encoder termination is not trivial due to the encoder state definition in Section II-A. In the following discussion, we assume the end of the packet is known to the decoder (i.e., the EOF delimiter is detected correctly).
Suppose no termination is employed at the end of a packet, the bit sequence is simply truncated there. After the last bit of the coded packet, certain LLRs still have to be shifted into the processors of the LDPC-CC decoder. Otherwise, the bits at the end of the packet can not be decoded as well as earlier bits due to lack of information about the subsequent bits. In this case, even if all the following information bits are assumed to be zero, the corresponding parity bits will not be

If termination is applied, the encoder will end at the all-zero state, which implies that all the following bits are assumed to be zero. Then the decoder can set all the subsequent LLRs to ∞. Based on the same rationale for presetting, termination can improve the performance at the end of a packet. Since the input data can be random, the encoder can be at any state at the end of a packet. It is now clear that the termination problem is equivalent to solving the linear equation xD = β , where β can be determined by the codeword to be terminated. The matrix D is a submatrix of H and of size 2L by L + M. If this equation has one or more solutions, a termination sequence x can be found and can take the encoder back to the all-zero state. It is easy to show that for this linear system, if 2L ≥ L + M and the matrix D is full rank, then a solution always exists [9]. Another important fact is that given L, the matrix D is not always the same. Due to periodicity, it has T different possible forms, depending on the starting phase at the beginning of the matrix D. Therefore, the existence of a solution is largely dependent on the parity-check matrix H . This analysis also suggests that L should be no less than M, which is intuitively correct because the memory of the code is M.
There are two ways of selecting L. One of them is to choose L such that the matrix D is full rank for all possible phases. For the (128,3,6) LDPC-CC simulated in this paper, we find that when L ≥ M + 6, D is full rank for all the 129 possible starting

phases, which implies that there always exists a termination sequence of length at least M + 6. The other way is to set L to a predefined value, and check the starting phase of D for each packet. If there is no solution for that phase, padding bits are inserted between the packet and the termination sequence until there is a solution. This approach is easier but has variable length of termination overhead.
The termination sequence is definitely an overhead because it does not carry any user data. Its length depends on the memory of the LDPC-CC. For convolutional codes, this termination overhead is also determined by the memory or the constraint length. What’s different here is that the memory is much larger. The actual code rate is reduced by this overhead. When the packet is long enough, this rate loss should be acceptable. Simulation in Section V will address the effects of this overhead.
Another issue is that if many packets are sent to the LDPC-CC decoder, an Inter-Packet Gap (IPG) of at least 2M codeword bits is required to allow for sufficient resetting of the LLRs. However, such IPGs already exist in most modern packet communication systems, and this requirement can be easily satisfied.
V. SIMULATION RESULTS
Packet transmission over the AWGN channel is simulated using the regular (128,3,6) rate-1/2 LDPC-CC and the (1024,3,6) LDPC-BC mentioned in Section III. More comparisons between LDPC convolutional and block codes can be found in the literature [2], [10]. In this paper we focus on its application to packet based systems, so the code with M = 128 is taken as an example. LDPC-CCs with larger memory should behave similarly in such systems, with better BER performance.
The packet length is set to 2048 bits (uncoded). The data sequence is divided into packets of the same length for both LDPC-CC and LDPC-BC. For LDPC-CC each packet is encoded and decoded from the all-zero state, i.e., with presetting. Termination is also applied to these packets. The length of termination sequence is M + 1 (using the second approach in Section IV-B). The information bit SNR (Eb/N0) is normalized by a factor to take into consideration the termination overhead and make the comparison fair.

Here we also use header error rate (HER) to illustrate the advantage of LDPC-CC in protecting the packet header fields. Take the IEEE802.3 Ethernet frame as an example, the header is of size 14 × 8 = 112 bits, containing source and destination MAC address. VI. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper we have introduced LDPC convolutional codes and argued that they are more suited to packet based communication systems than LDPC block codes. This is because they can accommodate packets of arbitrary length, have simple encoder and decoder structures, and can obtain extra performance gain by presetting and termination. They achieve all these and still provide the coding gain comparable to LDPC codes with block length larger than the memory. Presetting and termination of the LDPC-CC encoder are discussed in this paper due to their importance in packetized transmission . A feasible method of termination is proposed for LDPC-CCs. Further study will include irregular LDPC-CCs and packet switching network simulation of LDPC-CCs.
