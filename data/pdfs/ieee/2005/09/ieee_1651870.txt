A PERFORMANCE IMPROVEMENT AND ERROR FLOOR AVOIDANCE TECHNIQUE FOR BELIEF PROPAGATION DECODING OF LDPC CODES

Enver Cavus, Babak Daneshrad
Wireless Integrated Systems Research Group University of California
Los Angeles, CA, 90095-1594 gcavus, babak@ee.ucla.edu}

Abstract-In this work, we introduce a unique technique that improves the performance of the BP decoding in waterfall and error-floor regions by reversing the decoder failures. Based on the short cycles existing in the bipartite graph, an importance sampling simulation technique is used to identify the bit and check node combinations that are the dominant sources of error events, called trapping sets. Then, the identified trapping sets are used in the decoding process to avoid the pre-known failures and to converge to the transmitted codeword. With a minimal additional decoding complexity, the proposed technique is able to provide performance improvements for short-length LDPC codes and push or avoid error-floor behaviors of longer codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) codes [1] have been attracted great attention due to its near Shannon Limit performance and low complexity message-passing decoding based on Belief Propagation (BP) Algorithm. Although BP decoding provides remarkable bit error performance with practical decoding complexity, it does not achieve Maximum Likelihood Decoding (MLD). For LDPC codes of lengths between hundred and few thousand bits, BP decoding introduces observable performance loss compared to MLD in waterfall region, whereas for longer size codes, large performance gaps are introduced in the error floor regions.
In the last decade, various researches have been studied to bridge the gap between BP decoding and MLD and to push error floors of LDPC codes lower. One common approach has been to construct LDPC codes with good cycle properties [2][5] in contrast to random constructions [6]-[8]. In [2], the progressive edge-growth construction was proposed on an edge-by-edge basis to maximize the local girth at variable nodes. A heuristic approach that uses the average of the girth histogram to select good codes from random graphs at short lengths is presented in [3]. In [4], Tian et al. showed that not all the short cycles are equally harmful and constructed irregular LDPC codes by ensuring good connectivity of the short cycles with the rest of the graph. A following work that merges the ideas from [2] and [4] is also proposed in [5].
In addition to optimized code construction techniques, post signal processing techniques or slightly modified messagepassing algorithms are also utilized to compensate the performance loss of BP decoding [9]-[12]. In [9], a probabilistic scheduling instead of conventional flooding

schedule is proposed. Reliability based decoding of LDPC codes, which combines BP decoding and ordered statistic algorithm, is developed in [10] to bridge the gap between BP decoding and MLD. In [11], an initial bootstrap step is introduced where a number of less reliable messages are replaced by messages from reliable check equations. A trial and error guessing methodology for solving undecoded bits of BP decoding is proposed in [12] for binary erasure channel and then the proposed method is generalized to other memoryless channels. Based on optimized code construction approaches and modified message passing techniques, BER improvements from a fraction of decibel to several orders of magnitudes are demonstrated though a considerable additional decoding complexity is also introduced.
In this work, we introduce a unique technique that uses the cycle properties of the code and a post processing technique in order to improve the performance of the BP decoding. It is well known that medium to long block size LDPC codes have large minimum distances and, the decoder failures (also known as detected errors) are mainly due to near codewords [13][15]. The combinations of bit nodes that prevent the LDPC decoder from converging to the transmitted codeword are defined as trapping sets [15]. In this paper, first, a graph based search method with an importance sampling (IS) simulation technique is used to identify the prevailing trapping sets of the given LDPC code. Then, the identified trapping sets are used in the decoding process to avoid the pre-known failures and to converge to the transmitted codeword. With a minimal additional decoding complexity, the proposed technique is able to provide performance improvements for short-length LDPC codes and push or avoid error-floor behaviors of longer codes.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section II reviews decoder failures, cycles and their relation to trapping sets. Then, Section III presents the proposed algorithm for the BP decoding. Performance improvement of two short length LDPC codes and a longer LDPC code are illustrated in Section IV. Finally, Section V concludes the paper.
II. DECODER FAELURES, CYCLES AND TRAPPING SETS

errors are observed only for short length LDPC codes (few hundreds of bits), where the minimum distance of the code is small. On the other hand, the detected errors, or decoder failures, happen when the decoder is unable to converge to any codeword after the maximum decoding iterations and there are still unsatisfied parity checks. In general, for LDPC codes with lengths longer than few hundred bits, the error space consists of trapping regions or states, once captured, the decoding algorithm is never able to recover from these states resulting in a decoder failure. Thus, BP decoder for longer LDPC codes almost exclusively makes detected errors.

Unlike the BP decoder, the ML decoder only makes undetected errors, and the BP to ML performance gap is introduced due to the decoder failures. It is well known that
BP decoding achieves the ML performance only if the LDPC decoder tree is cycle free. A cycle (or loop) of length v in a bipartite graph of an LDPC code is defined as a path containing v edges that closes back on itself. The graphs of finite-length LDPC codes contain a large number of cycles and cycles as well as the connectivity of cycles with the rest of the graph are closely related to decoder failures and error floor behavior of

As a consequence of having multiple cycles in trapping sets, multiple "mis-satisfied" parity check nodes are formed when the participating cycle bits are all in error simultaneously. A check node is called a "mis-satisfied check" if the parity check contains even number of error bits. In a mis-satisfied check node, all extrinsic outputs are unreliable, which makes it difficult for the decoder to recover all the error bits. When specific mis-satisfied checks occur at the same time, a trapping set is formed and the decoder reinforces the error bits, resulting in a decoder failure. In BP decoding of LDPC codes, dominant decoding failures are, in general, caused by combination of multiple cycles [4]. In [15], the combination of error bits that leads to a decoder failure are defined as trapping sets. These are the sets with a relatively small number of variable nodes such that the induced subgraph has a small number of odd degree (unsatisfied) check nodes. In [19], it is shown that the dominant trapping sets are formed by combination of short cycles present in bipartite graph. Following the notation in [15] and [19], we label the trapping sets as (x, y) trapping sets,

Note that the strength of the trapping set becomes stronger, as
x is increased and y is decreased. For instance, given a regular
LDPC code with degree-3 variable nodes (i.e each variable node has 3 edge connections), (5,1) trapping sets are more powerful traps than (4,2) sets. In a (5, 1) trapping set, 1 parity check strives to correct the error bits against 7 mis-satisfied check nodes, whereas 2 parity checks attempt to improve reliability of the messages against 5 mis-satisfied checks in a (4,2) trap.
III. THE PROPOSED ALGORrrHM
As discussed in Section II, BP to ML performance gap is determined by the number of detected errors or decoder failures occurring in the decoding process. In this section, we describe a unique technique that improves the performance of BP decoding by successfully reducing the number of detected errors. The proposed technique involves two steps: identification of trapping sets, and removal of detected errors.
A. Identification of Trapping Sets

The look-up table is implemented in the BP decoder and the conventional BP algorithm is run with a practical maximum iteration limit. After the maximum number of iterations, if there are still remaining unsatisfied parity checks, the decoder uses the look-up table to determine if a pre-known failure is detected based on the unsatisfied parity checks. Once a match occurs, the decoder simply flips the hard decision values of trapping bits. At this point, all the parities are satisfied and the decoder converges to the valid codeword. The only additional complexity introduced by the proposed algorithm is the logic block used for look-up table and the inverse operation of the trapping set bits.

(96, 48) and (204, 102) LDPC codes can be found in [16] and (2000, 1000) LDPC code is generated using the software provided in [17]. Both of these codes are random constructions with a girth of 6. We have implemented a 6 bit (1 sign bit and 5 quantization bits) approximate-min decoder [18]. A quantized decoder is preferred in the simulations as the error-floor behaviors of LDPC codes are also related to quantization method [20]. The maximum iteration limit is set to 200. All-zero codeword transmission is assumed and BPSK modulation is employed.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we have presented a simple post processing algorithm to improve the performance and eliminate error floor behavior of BP decoding LDPC codes with a negligible additional decoding complexity. Based on the existing cycles of the bipartite graph, the trapping sets are located in a decoding process facilitated using IS method. If a pre-known decoder failure is encountered during the BP decoding, the decoder is able to flip all the trapping set bits -in order to converge to the true codeword. Performance improvements and error floor avoidance capability of the algorithm is illustrated using the (96, 48), (204, 102) and (2000, 1000)
LDPC codes.
