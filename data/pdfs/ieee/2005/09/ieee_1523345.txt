AbstractвҖ” Time-invariant hybrid (HTI) decoding of irregular low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes is studied. Focusing on HTI algorithms with majority-based (MB) binary messagepassing constituents, we use density evolution and finite-length simulation to analyze the performance and the convergence properties of these algorithms. Tight upper bounds on the threshold of MB HTI algorithms are derived, and it is proven that the asymptotic error probability for these algorithms tends to zero at least exponentially with the number of iterations. We devise optimal MB HTI algorithms for irregular LDPC codes, and show that these algorithms outperform GallagerвҖҷs algorithm A applied to optimized irregular LDPC codes. We also show that compared to switch-type algorithms, such as GallagerвҖҷs algorithm B, where a comparable improvement is obtained by switching between different MB algorithms, MB HTI algorithms are more robust, and can better cope with unknown channel conditions, and thus can be practically more attractive.
I. INTRODUCTION
Hybrid algorithms combine existing iterative algorithms to achieve a better error-rate performance or a faster convergence. There are two important categories of hybrid algorithms: switch-type and time-invariant. Switch-type hybrid (HST) algorithms switch between different algorithms throughout the iteration process. A well-known example of HST algorithms is GallagerвҖҷs algorithm B (GB) [3, p. 49]. Time-invariant hybrid (HTI) algorithms use a specific blend of different algorithms in each iteration. The blend, however, does not change by iteration, hence the name time-invariant. In [6] and [8], restricting our attention to binary message-passing majoritybased (MB) algorithms [7], we showed that for regular LDPC codes, MB HTI algorithms outperform their constituents, and are more robust, compared to MB HST algorithms, against changes in channel conditions.
Due to the fact that irregular LDPC codes outperform regular ones [4], [5], it is desirable to study the performance of MB HTI algorithms in decoding irregular LDPC codes. In this paper, we first extend the application of MB HTI algorithms to irregular codes. We then present appropriate formulation for finding optimal degree distributions for irregular LDPC codes decoded by MB HTI algorithms. Finally, we compare MB HTI algorithms with GallagerвҖҷs algorithm A (GA) [2] and GB, applied to optimized irregular codes. Our results demonstrate the superiority of MB HTI algorithms over GA in terms of noise threshold, and over GB in terms of decoding time, in the absence of the perfect knowledge of the channel. Moreover, our study indicates that, unlike the case for GB,

In this section, using density evolution, we first analyze MB HTI algorithms applied to irregular LDPC code ensembles. We then provide characterizations for, and give tight upper bounds on, the thresholds of MB HTI algorithms. At the end, we investigate the speed of convergence of (irregular code ensemblesвҖҷ) error probability to zero, under MB HTI algorithms.

IV. COMPARISONS WITH OPTIMIZED GA AND GB
In this section, we show that for irregular codes, MB HTI algorithms can outperform GA (applied to optimally designed irregular LDPC codes), and can outpace GB (optimally designed for irregular LDPC codes) when no perfect a priori
knowledge of the channel condition is in hand. We demonstrate the strength of MB HTI algorithms by providing two examples. In the first example, we obtain a pair of degree distributions whose threshold under MB HTI algorithms is greater than the best achievable threshold under GA for the same rate. In the second example, which is more elaborate, we
investigate how the lack of perfect knowledge of the channel affects the performance of MB HTI algorithms compared to MB HST algorithms. In both examples, the codes are of rate 1/2. Results and trends similar to those reported in this section
are observed for other code rates as well.
Example 1: It is shown in [2] that amongst rateвҖ“1/2 LDPC codes, the best performance under GA belongs to CвҲһ(О»GA , ПҒGA ), where О»GA = (0, 0, 0.1115, 0.8885) and ПҒGA = (0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0.2600, 0.7400). For this ensemble, we have ОөвҲ—(О»GA , ПҒGA ) = 0.0513, dv вүҲ 3.85, and dc вүҲ 7.7. Using MB HTI algorithms, however, better rates with asymptotical zero error probability can be achieved for Оө = 0.0513. (We can, e.g., attain a rate of r = 0.5076 > 1/2 with the ensemble specified by Оӣ4,1 = 0.8363, Оӣ7,1 = 0.1637, ПҒ8 = 0.2400, and ПҒ9 = 0.7600.) This is a clear indication of the possibility of improving the threshold for rateвҖ“1/2 codes under MB HTI algorithms. To find the optimal threshold based on our optimization tool for the rate, we do as follows. We choose a channel parameter Оө slightly greater than Оө = 0.0513, for
which we maximize the rate. If the new rate is still greater than 1/2, we (incrementally) increase Оө and repeat the rate
maximization step. Otherwise, Оө is (incrementally) decreased
and rate is maximized. By iterating this rather naВЁДұve procedure, we will finally end up with a rateвҖ“1/2 pair of distributions whose threshold is greater than Оө = 0.0513.

Under these algorithms, as can be seen in Fig. 1(a) and Fig. 1(b), the probability of error for both ensembles converges to zero, which is not unexpected. In fact, by setting an MB HTI algorithm to achieve its maximum threshold, its convergence for all channel parameters below the maximum threshold is secured. This is also true for GB. Having said these, it is

observed in the figures that the convergence behavior in the two cases are very different. On the one hand, the MB HTI algorithm exhibits a smooth and fast convergence. One can
see in Fig. 1(a) that the convergence tunnel between the curve y = f (x) and the line y = x is wide open over, roughly speaking, the entire interval (0, Оөact], and therefore the probability of error approaches zero rapidly. For the GB, on the other hand, the convergence changes pace abruptly, and
is generally slower. As portrayed in Fig. 1(b), the convergence tunnel for the GB is wide open over some subintervals of (0, Оөact], and almost closed over some others. This results in a slow convergence, on average, compared to the MB HT I algorithm. This is, of course, for the simple reason that the strategy optimized for Оө = ОөвҲ—(О»GB , ПҒGB) is highly suboptimal for Оө = Оөact, and switching to the right constituent algorithms fails to happen at the right iterations: even-degree (odd-degree) variable-nodes switch their order from 2 to 1 to 0 (1 to 0) in iterations 4 and 102 (iteration 43), instead of iterations 2 and 10 (iteration 4). V. CONCLUSION
Majority-based time-invariant hybrid (MB HTI) algorithms for decoding irregular low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes were introduced. We analyzed the performance and the convergence speed of these algorithms using density evolution. Tight upper bounds on threshold were derived, and it was shown that the asymptotic convergence speed of MB HTI algorithms is at least exponential in the number of iterations. We jointly optimized the degree distributions of LDPC codes and the time-invariant assignment for MB HTI algorithms, and showed the improvement in threshold that MB HTI algorithms can provide compared to the optimized GallagerвҖҷs algorithm A.
