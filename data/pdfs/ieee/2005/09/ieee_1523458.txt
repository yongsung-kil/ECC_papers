I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [6] are one of the most promising error-correcting codes and recently in some communication systems LDPC codes were adopted as standard error-control coding techniques.
LDPC codes are usually classified into two classes, i.e., regular LDPC codes and irregular ones. Algebraically constructed regular LDPC codes have mathematical structure suitable for hardware implementation, but the performance of the codes is inferior to that of irregular LDPC codes, although they need much higher complexity for hardware implementation. Hence we want to construct powerful irregular LDPC codes as algebraically as possible. Recently, several algebraic methods for constructing irregular LDPC codes have been investigated [1], [2], [7], [12]. In [12] Tanner et al. have proposed irregular LDPC codes based on circulant matrices that outperform regular LDPC codes with message-passing iterative decoding. Their irregular LDPC codes have algebraic structure suitable for VLSI implementation and can be encoded efficiently by erasure decoding, but it seems that their LDPC codes have restricted degree distributions.
In this paper we propose an algebraic construction of efficiently encodable irregular LDPC codes. The LDPC codes constructed in this paper have not only an efficient encoding algorithm but also guaranteed minimum dsitances. Furthermore, by applying the mask method introduced by Hocevar [7] and Chen et al. [1], [2], we can construct irregular LDPC codes whose Tanner graphs have arbitrary degree distributions.

Theorem 2.5: The minimum distance of C is at least j + 1. Proof: Let u ∈ F2N be a nonzero codeword of C, where
F2 denotes the binary field. Then u has at least one nonzero information bit. In the Tanner graph of C, the information bit node is connected to j check nodes, each of which is connected to at least one other information or parity bit node whose
value is nonzero. Since the girth of the Tanner graph is 6 by Theorem 2.4, these nonzero bits are distinct from each other. Therefore the weight of the codeword u is at least j +1. Since C is linear and u is arbitrary, the statement follows.

III. THE MODIFIED CONSTRUCTION

In this section we construct irregular LDPC codes by using
the basic construction described in the previous section and
the mask method of Chen et al. [1], [2]. For the sake of
completeness, we review the mask method. Let m, n1 and n2 be positive integers and A = (As,t) an n1 × n2 block matrix such that each As,t, 0 ≤ s ≤ n1 − 1, 0 ≤ t ≤ n2 − 1, is an m × m matrix over F2. For W = (ws,t), an n1 × n2 matrix over F2, we define a matrix product as follows: W ◦ A := (ws,tAs,t), where ws,tAs,t equals the m × m zero matrix for ws,t = 0 and ws,tAs,t equals As,t for ws,t = 1. For brevity we denote the resulting matrix W ◦ A by AW . We call W the masking matrix, AW the masked matrix, and A the base matrix.
Now we can give the detail of our method for constructing
irregular LDPC codes. Let p be a prime number larger than 3, and let 3 ≤ j ≤ k ≤ p−1. We set M := pj and N := p(j+k).

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper we presented an algebraic construction of efficiently encodable irregular LDPC codes, which uses many useful ideas, RS-type quasi-cyclic LDPC codes, the design method of Yang et al., and the mask method. The LDPC codes constructed in this paper have not only algebraic structure and an efficient encoding algorithm but also girth at least 6 and guaranteed minimum distances. They also perform well compared to randomly constructed irregular LDPC codes. Finally, we remark that the algebraic structure of the proposed codes is also useful in designing message-passing decoders [7].
