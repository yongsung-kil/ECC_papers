Improved Reversible LDPC Codes

Abstract— We provide a thorough analysis for a class of iteratively encodable low-density parity-check codes. The iterative encoding technique is based upon the graphical representation of the code and allows the decoder circuit to also be used for encoding, thus saving circuit area. The analysis identifies a weakness in the structure of the code, which arises due to a repetitive pattern in its factor graph. We show that the graph supports pseudo-codewords of low pseudo-weight, and relate this to the empirical observation of some near-codewords. We then propose a new recursive technique for constructing iteratively encodable codes which have improved expansion. The new codes offer a large amount of flexibility in the choice of code length and rate, and performance that compares well to both randomly generated and extended Euclidean-geometry codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
Since the rediscovery of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1], some effort has been directed into finding computationally efficient encoders [2–6]. This has been motivated by the fact that in general, matrix-vector multiplication has complexity O n2 for block length n.
In previous work [7] we introduced an encoding technique based upon the Jacobi method for iterative matrix inversion, which allows the decoder architecture to be reused for encoding. By reusing the decoder architecture for encoding, both operations can be performed by the same circuit on a time switched basis. The utilization of area within practical LDPC decoder implementations to date has been limited by routing congestion [8]. The area required for wire routing in a dedicated LDPC encoder implementation is also significant. By eliminating the need for a separate dedicated encoder circuit we aim to reduce the overall size of the codec. We have recently developed an area efficient reversible LDPC codec architecture which switches between analog decode and digital encode modes. The time taken by this circuit for the encode operation is negligible in comparison to the decode operation, and hence it allows high speed full-duplex communication [9].
It is the task of a decoder circuit to correct errors, thus making its behaviour difficult to verify. However, encoding is a deterministic process in which errors are exposed quickly. Since we are reusing the decoder for encoding, verification of the encode operation also provides implicit verification for components of the decoder circuit.

While there are several methods known for encoding LDPC codes in linear time, most are based upon either back substitution or the exploitation of quasi-cyclic structures. Thus they require serial implementation with complexity that scales with n. In contrast, the encoding method of [7] is a parallel iterative algorithm with complexity that is independent of n.

II. REVERSIBLE LDPC CODES
In [7] we introduced a class of reversible LDPC codes.
These codes allow the decoder to be reused to perform iterative
encoding via the Jacobi method for iterative matrix inversion,
as follows. Consider a binary systematic (n, k) code with codewords
arranged as row vectors x = [xp | xu], where xu are the information bits and xp are the parity bits. Likewise partition the parity check matrix, H = [Hp | Hu]. Defining b = Huxu , encoding becomes equivalent to solving Hpxp = b. For m×m non-singular Hp, we have xp = H− p1b. The Jacobi method for iterative matrix inversion is applied over F2 by performing the iteration

xp:κ+1 = (Hp ⊕ I)xp:κ + b

(1)

In [7] we showed that the above iteration allows the
message-passing decoder to be reused for encoding. We also proved that, for an arbitrary initial guess of the parity bits xp:0, the iteration (1) will converge to the correct solution in κ ≥ κ iterations if and only if

(Hp + I)κ = 0

(2)

We ensure that this constraint on Hp is met by using careful code design. The selection of Hu is then arbitrary with respect to the iterative encodability of H.
In [7, 10] we presented techniques for constructing reversible LDPC codes that used a circulant matrix for Hp. We label such codes type-I reversible LDPC codes. We have discovered that some realisations of these codes exhibit an error floor which is higher than that observed for randomly generated codes. In this paper we provide a thorough analysis of the type-I codes and relate the error floor to the circulant structure of Hp. We note that is not a requirement that Hp be circulant in order for it to satisfy (1). We propose a new class of reversible codes which offer improved performance by incorporating randomness into the code structure.

III. ANALYSIS OF TYPE-I REVERSIBLE LDPC CODES
As an example we analyse two (3,6)-regular type-I reversible LDPC codes, which are encodable using four iterations of the Jacobi algorithm. The parity-check matrix of each code has the form [Hp|Hu], where Hp is a m × m matrix with the first row polynomial h(x) = 1 + x + xm/4+1. Hu is a (3,3)-regular randomly generated matrix chosen such that the graph of H is 4-cycle free. The codes will be referred to as Rev512 and Rev4096, and have length n = 512 and n = 4096 respectively. We compare their performance to that of (3,6)regular random codes c.f. [11] with approximately the same length, n = 504 and n = 4000 respectively, labelled Rand504 and Rand4000. All codes examined are 4-cycle free.
Figure 1 compares the type-I reversible codes to their random benchmarks on the AWGN channel, using a soft decision sum-product decoder [12] for a maximum of 50 iterations. In Figure 2 the comparison is shown for the BEC case using the binary erasure decoder [2]. For the random codes, the performance measured across the information bits matches that measured across the full codeword, and hence it has not been plotted.
Figure 1 shows the performance of the Rev512 code matching that of the Rand504 code, until flooring begins at a BER of around 10−5. As the SNR is increased the BER measured across the full codeword becomes higher than that measured across the information bits alone. This behaviour is again exhibited by the Rev4096 code, which also begins to floor at around 10−5. Moreover, the BER measured across the full codeword is significantly higher, beginning to floor at around 10−3. We observe a much higher error rate for the parity bits of the codeword than for the information bits. This indicates a potential weakness for message-passing in the code subgraph corresponding to Hp. The shorter reversible code fairs better against its respective benchmark than the longer code does. We did not observe undetected errors during any of the experiments.
For the BEC case, Figure 2 shows that the bit erasure performance of the Rev512 code closely matches that of the Rand504 code, above the channel bit erasure probability of

≈ 0.31. Below this point, the bit erasure rate measured across the full codeword becomes higher than that measured across the information bits alone. The bit erasure rate for the Rev4096 code measured across the information bits alone, begins to floor at around 10−8. The bit erasure rate measured across the full codeword becomes significantly higher than this at ≈ 0.39, beginning to floor at around 10−7. As observed for the case of the AWGN channel, the information bits appear to be more heavily protected than the parity bits. This inequality begins to surface as we decrease the channel erasure probability and is more visible for the longer code.
We now employ recently developed theoretical tools to analyse the behaviour of the reversible LDPC codes presented above. We recognise that we have an error control system that represents the pairing of a code to a sub-optimal decoder. It is therefore important not only to analyse the code but to also consider how the structure of the code is suited to the operation of the decoding algorithm.
We can prove that any (3,6)-regular type-I reversible LDPC code that is encodable in 4 iterations has minimum distance dmin ≤ 28, independently of the block length. While this highlights a potential problem for the type-I codes it does not fully explain our experimental observations. We expect a poor minimum distance to cause the decoder to make undetected errors, yet none were observed. Instead, the observed performance floor appears to be related to problems with decoder convergence. Minimum distance is a property of the code which is independent of the chosen decoding algorithm. In the following analysis we consider the structure of the reversible codes in relation to the operation of the decoder.
Stopping set analysis is an appropriate tool for investigating the behaviour of message-passing decoding of LDPC codes over the binary erasure channel [13]. By considering the complete graph of H and connecting information bits, stopping sets are easily formed. The repetitive pattern in the graph causes the undesirable highlighted structure, and others like it with low EMD, to be replicated throughout the graph, thus increasing the probability of stopping set creation. However, as these patterns have a nonzero EMD they require the inclusion of one or more information bits for stopping set closure. At high channel erasure rates the maximal stopping set for a given set of erased bits consists of smaller component stopping sets. The overlapping of such sets accounts for the observed symmetry across the number of information and parity bits in the maximal stopping set. However, at low channel erasure rates, the maximal stopping set is often also the minimal stopping set (ignoring the empty set). In these cases, the heavy weighting of parity bits within the set causes the observed information bit erasure rate to be less than that measured across the full codeword.
We now turn our attention to the experiments performed in presence of AWGN noise. In this case we are interested in the behaviour of the message-passing sum-product decoder, in particular at high SNR where the performance of the reversible codes begins to diverge from that of the random codes.
In contrast to BEC case, we have observed that the distribu-

tion of low weight error vectors is dominated by those which corrupt only parity bits. In particular we have observed a large number of errors vectors, e, with weight w (e) = 4. A closer inspection of these vectors shows that they all have the pattern
e(x) = xs + xβ+s + x2β+s + x3β+s mod (xm + 1), (3)
where β = m/4 and s ∈ {0 . . . m − 1}. Here, the algebraic representation e(x) of e has the same form as that used to represent the first row vector of Hp. This error pattern corresponds to a row of variables shown in Figure 3. Although the subgraph for Hp is 4-cycle free, it contains many intersecting 6-cycles. Near-codewords provide a qualitative account for convergence problems when using the message-passing decoder [15]. Near-codewords can arise from the connection of short cycles in the graph of H, and hence it is not surprising that vectors e defined by e(x) correspond to (4,4) nearcodewords. The pattern of parity bits with an EMD of one, shown in Figure 3, represents a (9, 1) near-codeword.
We may extend our analysis using the finite graph-cover techniques presented in [16]. In order to simplify the discussion we assume the transmission of the all-zero codeword. As suggested in [16], we may build a pseudo-codeword ωe using a procedure similar to that of canonical completion, rooted at the (4, 4) near-codeword e defined by (3). At this point we ignore Hu and operate only on the graph corresponding to Hp. We consider each row of variables in Figure 3 to represent a tier, where each tier is labelled by its leftmost variable index. We start at the root tier, t = 1, and let this correspond to the nonzero elements of e. The graph is constructed to a depth t = β, such that all parity variables appear exactly once. Each variable vs on tier t is then assigned the value ωs = 1/2t−1. This assignment ensures that all local indicator functions [16] are satisfied based upon the parity variables alone. Thus, appending an all-zero information sequence generates a valid pseudo-codeword.
It is straightforward to show that the pseudo-codeword constructed above has pseudo-weight < 12. Moreover, this property is independent of the code length. A similar process can be used to find other pseudo-codewords, e.g. by rooting the canonical completion at the (9, 1) near-codeword discussed above. Furthermore, these low weight pseudo-codewords are replicated through the structure of the graph. Finite graphcover analysis therefore provides a quantitative account for the near-codewords observed through empirical investigation. We have identified that the structure of Hp admits pseudocodewords with low pseudo-weight, thus leading to convergence problems for iterative sum-product decoding in the case of the AWGN channel.

V. CONCLUSIONS
The iterative encoder allows an area efficient codec architecture to be realised and reduces the number of components to be tested during implementation. Circuit routing errors that may be difficult to trace for the decoder are quickly exposed by the encoder. The encoder operates in parallel for a small fixed number of iterations and hence its latency does not increase as we increase block length.
The type-I reversible codes exhibit different characteristics depending upon the channel, and corresponding decoder being employed. They therefore provide an interesting case study and highlight the need for quantitative analytical tools, such as that provided by finite graph-cover analysis. The analysis of the type-I codes also demonstrates the importance of the particular parity-check matrix structure chosen for iterative decoding. The absence of undetected errors suggests that their potentially poor minimum distance properties are much less significant than their poor minimum pseudo-weight. By removing the iterative encodability constraint, a similar analysis may be useful for selecting candidate polynomials to build cyclic or quasi-cyclic LDPC codes.

The type-II reversible codes constructed using the pro-
posed recursive algorithm provide improved performance. The
method employs random components, and provides improved
expansion to the graph. Simulation results for the example
type-II reversible codes compare well to random and extended
Euclidean-geometry LDPC benchmarks. The recursive con-
struction allows type-II reversible codes to be designed with
a high degree of freedom for rate and block length.
