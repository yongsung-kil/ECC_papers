A High-Speed Analog Min-Sum Iterative Decoder

Saied Hemati, Amir H. Banihashemi, and Calvin Plett
Carleton University, 1125 Colonel By Dr., Ottawa, Canada {hemati, ahashemi}@sce.carleton.ca and cp@doe.carleton.ca

Abstract- Current-mode circuits are presented for implementing analog min-sum (MS) iterative decoders. Proposed circuits are devised based on current mirrors. Therefore, in any fabrication technology that accurate current mirrors can be designed, analog MS decoders can be implemented. The functionality of the proposed modules was verified by implementing an analog MS decoder for a (32,8,10) regular LDPC code in 0.18-µm CMOS technology. In low signal to noise ratios when the circuit imperfections are dominated by the noise of the channel, the measured error correcting performance of this chip in steadystate condition surpasses that of the conventional MS decoder, and is close to the performance predicted by the earlier work on the dynamics of the continuous-time analog decoding by Hemati and Banihashemi, ISIT2004. At a throughput of 24Mb/s, loss in the coding gain compared to the conventional MS decoder at BER of 10-3 is about 0.3dB. To the best of our knowledge, this decoder has the highest throughput and the lowest power/speed ratio among the reported analog CMOS iterative decoders.
I. INTRODUCTION
Analog continuous-time iterative decoding was proposed a few years ago to improve the power/speed ratio of the decoder chips that decode capacity achieving codes. It has been shown that belief propagation (BP), which is the best known iterative decoding algorithm, can be implemented by analog BiCMOS or subthresh-
old CMOS circuits [1]-[5]. BiCMOS circuits are costly and subthreshold circuits are rather slow. To make high-
speed analog iterative decoders more affordable, in [6]-
[7] the authors use CMOS circuits in strong inversion biasing condition for implementing high-speed analog
min-sum (MS) iterative decoders. Furthermore, in [8] the dynamics of the continuous-time iterative decoding was studied and it was shown that analog continuoustime decoding has a different dynamics than that of conventional discrete-time decoding. It was also shown that if enough time is given to an analog decoder, its steady-state performance surpasses that of a conventional iterative decoder. In this paper, we generalize the
circuits that were presented earlier in [7] and show that all the major blocks in an analog min-sum iterative decoder can be implemented by using current-mirrors. This implies that our proposed circuits can be implemented in state-of-the-art BiCMOS or CMOS fabrication technologies.
The functionality of the proposed modules was verified by implementing an analog MS decoder for a (n,k,dmin)=(32,8,10) LDPC code in 0.18-µm CMOS technology. The error-correcting performance of this decoder in the low signal to noise ratio (SNR) region, in steady-state is close to the simulation results based on

the ideal analog continuous-time model for iterative
decoding [8]. However, in the high SNR region the error-correcting performance of the chip deteriorates due to mismatch and other implementation imperfections. Also, effects of increasing throughput and limiting decoding time were studied. At speeds of about 250kb/s the decoder has enough time to converge to steady-state behavior and as the speed increases the performance degrades such that at 24Mb/s the performance degradation is about 1dB at BER of 10-3.

The sign of the outgoing message to each variable node is obtained by multiplying the sign of the messages that have been received from other variable nodes. In hardware implementation, it is simpler to work with a sign bit that represents the polarity of the message. For negative numbers the sign bit is set to be one and for non-negative numbers it is set to be zero. In this way, instead of multiplying the signs, we can XOR the sign bits. Finally, four ASTR (absolute value and sign to real converter) modules combine the sign bits and absolute values and generate the outgoing messages.
A. Implementation of RTAS Modules
Fig. 6 illustrates the RTAS module. Fig. 6(a) shows a current rectifier, the output of which is always a positive current, regardless of the polarity of its incoming current. This circuit exploits the fact that n-type current mirrors can only be used for positive currents and ptype current mirrors can only be used for negative currents. Fig. 6(b) shows a sign extractor. In this circuit if Iin is positive, n-type current mirror will be active and it will discharge the input capacitance (Cin) of the NOT gate and the output will be zero. Similarly if Iin is negative, the output will be one.

V. MEASUREMENT RESULTS
The proposed modules were used for implementing an analog MS decoder for a (32,8,10) regular LDPC code in 0.18-µm CMOS technology. In this code, degrees of the variable nodes and the check nodes are three and four, respectively. Low-swing and high-swing cascode current mirrors were used for implementing the
variable nodes and the check nodes, respectively [10]. The general architecture of the implemented analog decoder chip is shown in Fig. 9.
This chip has digital inputs, which simplifies the testing of the chip. Each input signal can be represented by up to six bits, five bits for the magnitude and one bit for the sign. Quantization error in this case is negligible
[13]. Digital inputs are fetched in an on-chip 32×6-bit memory. We have used thirty two 5-bit current steering digital-to-analog converters (DAC) to translate the input digital signals into thirty two different current levels. Gain of the DAC modules can be controlled by an offchip biasing voltage. The DACs’ output currents and the stored sign bits are conducted to ASTR modules to generate bipolar currents for applying to the corresponding variable nodes. The processing core of this analog decoder chip is 630µm × 910µm and the variable nodes and the check nodes roughly consume 60% of the core area. This relatively low utilization factor is due to brute-force manual connection of the processing nodes. An automatic router could significantly increase the utilization factor. The average length of the interconnections in this chip is about 690µm. Before starting the decoding, it is necessary to reset the decoder and clear up any unwanted state in the analog decoder. Otherwise, the decoder might stick to its initial state regardless of the input information. This could eventually degrade the overall decoding performance. This phenomenon was
observed in both [14] and [7]. To avoid an unwanted initial state, the outgoing interconnections of the check

In [15] measurement results for the fabricated analog MS decoder chip with 6-bit quantization for the inputs have been presented. However, by reducing the number of quantization bits to 5 and doubling the DAC gain, the error correcting performance of the decoder was improved. This improvement can be explained by considering the implementation imperfections that deteriorate accuracy of the processing modules for small currents. When the quantization steps are too small compared to the minimum detectable currents in the circuit, the least significant bit may be treated as zero currents. This can degrade the decoding performance.
Fig. 10 shows error rate curves for the decoder chip corresponding with the 5-bit quantization at the input at 250kb/s and 24Mb/s together with simulation results for MS iterative decoding based on conventional discretetime model that uses successive substitution method for updating the messages (SS-MS) [8]. Also in the figure, we have the simulation results for relaxed MS [8] that use successive relaxation method with quantization and clipping similar to the chip with the optimized relaxation factor (SR-MS). SR-MS curves are based on the model in [8], which was introduced for ideal continuous-time analog iterative decoding. To obtain reliable measurement results, in the presence of possible asymmetry in the decoding process, the error correcting performance of the chip was tested by using randomly generated codewords (not always all-zero codeword). The average power consumption of this chip is about 5mW in the tested throughputs. Compared to the other
reported analog CMOS iterative decoders [3]-[5], this chip has the highest throughput and the lowest consumed energy per bit. Also, it is the first analog minsum decoder and the first analog decoder for an LDPC code.
VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, processing modules for implementing analog min-sum iterative decoders were presented. In the proposed approach the main modules can be designed based on current mirrors. Therefore, in any fabrication technology, CMOS or BiCMOS that accurate current mirrors can be implemented, analog MS decoders can also be implemented. A proof-of-concept analog min-sum decoder chip was designed and fabricated for a (32,8,10) regular LDPC code in 0.18-µm CMOS technology. Error correcting performance of this chip in steady-state and in the low signal to noise ratio region is close to simulation results based on ideal continuous-
time iterative decoding [8] and surpasses that of conventional discrete-time decoding.
