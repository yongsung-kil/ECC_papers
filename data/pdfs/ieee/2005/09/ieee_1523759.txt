Extension of Quasi-Cyclic LDPC Codes by Lifting
Seho Myung and Kyeongcheol Yang Dept. of Electronics and Electrical Engineering Pohang University of Science and Technology (POSTECH)
Pohang, Kyungbuk 790-784, Korea Email: {bighead, kcyang}@postech.ac.kr

Abstract— In this paper, we analyze some cycle properties of quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes and show that the girth of a QC-LDPC code is upper bounded by a certain number introduced by the structure of its mother matrix. We also propose a simple method to extend QC-LDPC codes of large length by lifting QC-LDPC codes of smaller length. In particular, it is possible to generate them from a single exponent matrix by a proper modulo-operation. Simulation results show that the more we apply the lifting to QC-LDPC codes, the more memory efficiency becomes better, but it may induce a little performance degradation.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes were first discovered by Gallager [5] and rediscovered by MacKay et al. [7], [8] and Sipser et al. [10]. They have created much interest recently since they are shown to have a remarkable performance with iterative decoding that is very close to the Shannon limit over additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels [1], [8], [11]. Also, LDPC codes possess many advantages including parallelizable decoding, self-error-detection capability by syndrome-check, and an asymptotically better performance than turbo codes, etc.
The performance of LDPC codes of finite length may be strongly affected by their cycle property such as girth and stopping sets [2], etc. Here the girth is the minimum length of cycles in the Tanner graph of a given parity-check matrix. In most cases, it is difficult to analyze explicitly these factors of randomly constructed LDPC codes and predict their performance. One advantage of quasi-cyclic LDPC (QCLDPC) codes based on circulant permutation matrices is that it is easier to analyze their code properties than in the case of random LDPC codes. Recently, several coding theorists proposed some classes of QC-LDPC codes with algebraically strong restriction on the structure and analyzed their properties more explicitly [3], [4], [6], [13].
Another advantage of QC-LDPC codes is memory efficiency. In the case of general LDPC codes, a significant amount of memory is needed to store their parity-check matrices. QC-LDPC codes may be a good candidate to solve the memory problem due to their algebraic structure. The required memory for storing QC-LDPC codes can be reduced by a factor 1/L, when L × L circulant permutation matrices or the zero matrix are employed. Because of these advantages, many engineers expect that QC-LDPC codes will be widespread in commercial applications.

The main contribution of this paper is to analyze some cycle properties of QC-LDPC codes and propose a simple method to extend them by lifting QC-LDPC codes of small length. First, we show that their girth is upper bounded by a number related to the structure of their mother matrices. Second, we propose an extension method, called the lifting. The QC-LDPC codes obtained by the lifting have little performance degradation, while they have a good memory efficiency.
The outline of the paper is as follows. In Section II, we review QC-LDPC codes and introduce some definitions for our presentation. In Section III, we analyze their cycle properties and discuss a simple condition under which the QC-LDPC codes have cycles. We also derive some upper bounds on the girth of QC-LDPC codes. In Section IV, we present a simple method to extend them by lifting QC-LDPC codes of small length. The performance of QC-LDPC codes extended by the lifting is investigated by simulations in Section V and we give concluding remarks in Section VI.

It may be possible to express the cycles of QC-LDPC codes into simple equations by Proposition 1 and therefore avoid short cycles by choosing the exponents of circulant permutation matrices appropriately. However, it is well-known that the cycles of QC-LDPC codes are heavily dependent on the structure of the mother matrix [4], [9]. The following theorem gives an upper bound on the girth in the QC-LDPC codes under the existence of an overlap between two blockcycles.

Theorem 2: ([9]) If there are p overlaps between a blockcycle of length 2l and a block-cycle of length 2k in a QCLDPC code, then there exists a cycle of length 2(2l + 2k − p) which is twice the number of distinct blocks in these block-
cycles. In particular, the girth of the corresponding QC-LDPC code is at most 2(2l + 2k − p).

Corollary 3: ([4], [9]) Assume that there are no zero matrices in (2). Then the girth of the corresponding regular QCLDPC code is at most 12 for any m ≥ 2 and n ≥ 3.
Proof. In a regular QC-LDPC code having no zero matrices there are always two block-cycles of length 4 with an overlap consisting of two blocks. Therefore, the girth is at most 12 by Theorem 2.

Corollary 3 is a well-known fact derived by Fossorier [4], however, the approach in [9] gives another simple proof on it. Moreover, Theorem 2 tells us that there may exist a cycle induced by the structure of the mother matrix, regardless of the size and the exponents of circulant permutation matrices. Therefore, if we reduce as many overlaps between blockcycles in the mother matrix as possible, then we can avoid such cycles in H. However, the girth is still upper bounded by two connected block-cycles although there are no overlaps between block-cycles.

IV. LIFTING OF QUASI-CYCLIC LDPC CODES
For many applications, it is desirable to derive the exponent
matrix for a good QC-LDPC code of large length from QC-
LDPC codes of smaller length. Note that the exponent matrix
conveys the information for the parity-check matrix of a QC-
LDPC code. In this section we introduce a simple method
to obtain the exponent matrix for a QC-LDPC code of large
length by lifting those for given QC-LDPC codes of short
length. The lifting method can be applied to the case that Lk+1 = qkLk for an integer qk and k = 1, 2, . . . , s − 1, where Lk is the size of circulant permutation matrices for the paritycheck matrix of the kth QC-LDPC code Ck as a target code.
Assume that H1, E(H1) and M (H1) for C1 are given such that the cycle property of Tanner graph for C1 is optimized. For simple construction of large-length QC-LDPC codes, we also assume that all Ck’s have the same mother matrix, i.e., M (Hk) = M (H1) for all 2 ≤ k ≤ s. Remark: Note that the lifting is carried out from the columns with lowest degree to the columns with higher degree at each step. It has been believed that the nodes of high degree are robust to errors since they have more paths for updating the messages, whereas the nodes of low degree are vulnerable to the errors because they have fewer paths for updating the messages [1], [11]. Furthermore, it may be guessed that the nodes of low degree are affected too frequently by the nodes with lower reliability if they are linked to a short cycle with nodes of low degree. Therefore, we always make efforts to avoid the cycles between the columns with low degrees when constructing any LDPC code.

This implies that it suffices to store E(Hs), not all E(Hk). In addition, Theorem 6 shows that the girth can not be decreased in the lifting procedure. However, once the mother matrix M is fixed, the cycles induced by connected block-cycles and/or an overlap between blockcycles cannot be eliminated. Because of the restriction on choosing the exponents in Step 2) for each k ≥ 2, E(Hk) may not be optimal from the viewpoint of cycles. Nevertheless, simulation results show that the lifting does not cause a serious performance degradation. However, the more we apply the lifting method to QC-LDPC codes, the more it may induce a performance degradation due to its suboptimality.
V. SIMULATION RESULTS
As shown in previous sections, QC-LDPC codes of large length can be obtained by lifting QC-LDPC codes of smaller length. Their performance over an AWGN channel was analyzed by computer simulations.
Figure 4 shows the bit error rate (BER) and frame error rate (FER) performance of irregular QC-LDPC codes obtained by lifting. All codes have the same mother matrix whose size is 24 × 48. The lifting procedure was carried out with L1 = 1, L2 = 12, L3 = 24, and L4 = 48. In Figure 4, (N, K, L) denotes (code length, information length, the size of the permutation matrices) and the codes with ‘Opt’ were obtained by lifting procedure with L1 = 1, L2 = 24 and L1 = 1, L2 = 48, respectively. Simulation results show that there is a little performance degradation in the former lifting as compared with those in the latter, even though it is not serious. It may be induced by the suboptimality of the exponents introduced in the lifting. As we apply the lifting to QC-LDPC codes more frequently, their performance may get

Simulation results show that there are no serious error floors at FER=10−4. As the block length increases, the performance curve becomes steeper. Note that both (8000, 4000, 200) and (80000, 40000, 2000) codes also have good performance, even though the cases of L = 200 and L = 2000 are excluded from the lifting procedure. In particular, the latter performs by only 0.39 dB away from the Shannon limit at the FER = 10−4.
VI. CONCLUDING REMARKS
We discussed some cycle properties of QC-LDPC codes and how to construct a structured QC-LDPC code with good memory efficiency. First, we showed that the girth of the QC-LDPC codes based on circulant permutation matrices is restricted by the structure of their mother matrices. If a mother matrix has some connected block-cycles and/or an overlap between block-cycles, the girth of the QC-LDPC codes derived from it is upper bounded. Second, we introduced the lifting method to extend a good QC-LDPC code of large length from QC-LDPC codes of small length. The QC-LDPC codes obtained by lifting can also be more efficiently stored in an exponent matrix. The more we apply the lifting to QC-LDPC codes, the more memory efficiency becomes better, but it may induce a little performance degradation.
