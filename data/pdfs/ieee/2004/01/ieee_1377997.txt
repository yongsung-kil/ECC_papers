Low-Density Generator Matrix Codes for Indoor and Markov Channels

Hanqing Lou ECE Department University of Delaware Newark, Delaware 19716 Email: lou@ee.udel.edu

Javier Garcia-Frias ECE Department
University of Delaware Newark, Delaware 19716 Email: jgarcia@ee.udel.edu

Abstract— We propose a modified algorithm for decoding of linear codes with low-density generator matrix (LDGM codes) over indoor and finite-state binary Markov channels. In order to avoid error floors, a concatenation of two LDGM codes is utilized. The hidden Markov model representing the channel is incorporated into the graph corresponding to the LDGM codes, and the message passing algorithm is modified accordingly. The proposed scheme clearly outperforms systems in which the channel statistics are not exploited in the decoding process, allowing reliable communication at rates which are above the capacity of a memoryless channel with the same stationary bit error probability as the Markov channel. Moreover, the proposed method can be successfully applied for real wireless channels that can be modeled with hidden Markov models, such as indoor channels.
I. INTRODUCTION
Many practical digital communications channels exhibit statistical dependencies among errors. The error pattern of the discrete channel (modulator–real channel–demodulator) can be modeled using finite-state binary Markov channels [1]1. It is intuitive that the presence of memory in Markov channels leads to increased capacity relative to memoryless channels with the same stationary bit error probability. Markov channel capacity was treated for the special case of Gilbert-Elliot (GE) channels by Mushkin and Bar-David in [2] and more generally by Goldsmith and Varaiya in [3]. In terms of the notation introduced in [2], the “capacities” that can be associated with a Markov channel include the true capacity Cµ and the capacity CNM (“no memory”) of a memoryless channel with the same stationary bit error probability as the Markov channel. Clearly, CNM ≤ Cµ.
In practice, many communications systems make use of a channel interleaver to distribute the errors so that codes designed for a memoryless channel can be used. While the application of interleaving does not change the capacity of the channel, the achievable performance of a decoder which assumes that the channel is memoryless is limited by CNM . Both [2] and [3] utilize decision feedback decoders which perform recursive state estimations that are used in the decoding process. However, the recursions are vulnerable to error propagation, and the decision feedback decoder can not be reliably used when the channel quality degrades.
In [5], [6] we proposed modifications for the decoding of turbo codes that exploit the structure of the Markov channel and allow communications at rates greater than CNM . The methods proposed in [5], [6] utilize supertrellises jointly describing the constituent codes and the Markov channel, and therefore the decoding complexity is high. Decoding modifications to exploit the structure of binary Markov channels in low-density parity check (LDPC) codes were proposed in [7], [8]2, and also allow communications beyond CNM and close to Cµ.
In this paper we first propose a system to transmit information over Markov channels that makes use of linear codes with low density generator matrix (LDGM codes). Then, we apply the proposed scheme to real indoor channels. The basic idea is to build a hidden Markov model (HMM) capable of statistically characterizing the indoor channel, which will be exploited and re-estimated at each iteration of the decoding process. The advantage of using LDGM codes is the reduction in encoding/decoding complexity with respect to standard LDPC codes and turbo codes: LDGM codes are a special class of LDPC codes, and therefore they can be decoded with the same complexity as standard LDPC codes, which is much less than that of turbo codes. Moreover, the lowdensity nature of the generator matrix makes the encoding trivial. The most important result is that the proposed system can be successfully applied for real wireless channels that can be modeled with hidden Markov models.

Then, the decoding algorithm for LDGM codes can be derived by applying belief propagation [10] (or factor graph decoding [11]) over the graph associated with the equation above.
It is well known that LDGM codes are asymptotically bad. This was corroborated in [12], [13], where it was shown that the use of single LDGM codes always leads to error floors. However, a considerable error floor reduction can be achieved by using a simple serial concatenation of two LDGM codes, such as the one shown in Figure 1. The resulting performance for concatenated LDGM codes over BSC and AWGN channels is, as shown in [12], [13], comparable to that of irregular LDPC and turbo codes, with a very low encoding/decoding complexity.
III. LDGM DECODER MODIFICATIONS FOR FINITE-STATE BINARY MARKOV CHANNELS
The possibility of incorporating Markov channels in the decoding of codes defined over general graphs schemes is suggested in [9], although no results are presented there. The basic idea is to “link” the codeword nodes with the hidden Markov model. Then, the whole structure can be considered as a Bayesian network, and decoding can be performed by proceeding with the message passing algorithm (belief propagation). Notice that due to the structure of LDGM codes, the use of a channel interleaver is unnecessary.

Decoding in Figure 2 is performed by applying the standard message passing algorithm (belief propagation). The introduction of the hidden Markov model produces an increase in the number of cycles in the network. However, as we will see later, these additional cycles do not seem to strongly affect the performance of the decoding algorithm. We assume that message passing is performed according to a global clock. At each clock cycle, a group of nodes is activated. The activation of a node is defined as the process in which the node reads all its incoming messages from all its neighboring nodes, performs computation, and outputs all its outgoing messages to its neighbors. We follow the convention that once a message is produced, it remains available to the corresponding nodes until the message gets updated. Although different activation schedules are possible, in this paper we proceed in the following order:
1) Activate the parity bit nodes from the inner decoder 2) Activate the parity bit nodes from the outer decoder 3) Activate the nodes corresponding to the Markov channel 4) Activate the information bit nodes 5) Go back to step 1 until decoding ends
As explained before, the specific equations for each one of the types of nodes have to be carefully derived applying the belief propagation principles. The resulting equations that implement the belief propagation algorithm over the hidden Markov model are in fact the forward/backward recursions with the difference that the “observation sequence” is now a random vector defined by the probabilities {gnx}, x ∈ {0, 1}, 1 ≤ n ≤ N = L + M . gnx denotes the (input) probability for the error pattern in position n to be x, and is obtained by comparing the probabilities of the information and parity bits obtained in the previous steps with the received observation.

IV. MODELING INDOOR CHANNELS WITH FINITE-STATE BINARY MARKOV CHANNELS
In this paper we focus on DECT channels, which are characterized by well defined bursts separated by long error free intervals [15], [16]. The type of bursts existing in these channels follow a bell shaped error density (i.e., the error density in each burst increases and then decreases).
It was shown in [15], [16] that this type of channels can be modeled utilizing a very specific type of HMMs. If the error pattern is known, the starting point to build the HMM corresponding to a given channel is to extract the bursts from the original error pattern sequence. Once the bursts have been isolated, each one is partitioned into blocks of length L bits and the error density of each block is calculated. Then, different states in the HMM are associated to different density ranges. As shown in Figure 3, the resulting model consists of an error free state D0 and error burst states Uk and Dk, k ∈ {1 . . . K}. For every burst, state Uk (Dk) generates the burst sections where the error density is increasing (decreasing). The probability of error in state Uk (Dk) is denoted as P Uk (P Dk). The HMM parameters can be calculated from the statistics of the error pattern profile as indicated in [15], [16].

V. SIMULATION RESULTS
In order to assess the performance of the proposed system, we consider two types of scenarios. First, in order to compare the performance of this work with previous schemes developed in [6], [8] for turbo and LDPC codes, we assume GilbertElliot channels (which are Markov channels with two states)4. Second, we investigate realistic indoor DECT channels. In this case, the idea is to create an HMM (of the type described in section IV) at the decoder site, and exploit such a model in the decoding process. Provided that this HMM is able to closely characterize the DECT channel, we can expect a performance improvement with respect to the standard decoding case (where the channel statistics are not exploited at the decoder site).
A. Gilbert-Elliot channels
A Gilbert-Elliot (GE) channel contains a “good” state, G, in which the bit error probability is PG and a “bad” state, B, with bit error probability PB. The transition probability of going from the good state to the bad state is denoted by b, and g represents the probability of going from the bad state to the good state.
In order to assess the performance of the proposed system, we consider the two GE channels studied in [6]. For the first channel, the transition probabilities are g = .1092 and b = .0308 (µ = 1 − b − g = .86). The transition probabilities of the second channel are chosen so that the ratio b/g is the same as in the channel above but with µ = .98 (i.e., longer error burst lengths than for the first channel). In both cases, the bit error probability in the bad state is fixed to PB = 0.5. The performance of the system is studied as a function of the value of PG, the bit error probability in the good state. Notice that, since all the other parameters are fixed, there is a one to one correspondence between PG and the stationary bit error probability, ρ, which is the parameter utilized in this section.
For both channels, we use a rate 1/2 concatenated scheme with a (3, 50) LDGM outer code and a (5.1, 5.7) LDGM inner code5. The number of information bits is fixed to 17, 500. For rate 1/2 codes, the bit error probability corresponding to the capacity of a BSC channel is ρ = .11. Therefore, if the memory of the channel is ignored, it is impossible to send reliable information through any of these channels when the stationary bit error probability is higher than .11.
For the first channel (µ = .86) and the proposed LDGM system, we found that after simulating 2, 000 blocks, BER < 10−6 for ρ < 0.118, which surpasses the memoryless limit (ρ = .11) and is close to the theoretical limit for this channel (ρ = .137). This occurs even when the parameters of the GE channel are not known at the decoder site. In this case, when the initial HMM is fixed to [g, b, PG, PB] =

B. Indoor channels
In this section, we consider a DECT channel with error rate 2.1 × 10−2 [15], [16]. For this channel, we study the performance of the proposed system when HMMs of 3 and 5 states of the type described in section IV, as well as a GE model, are utilized to characterize the channel and exploited at the decoder site.
In the first type of simulations presented in this section, we assume knowledge of the error pattern from the original DECT channel, and obtain the corresponding HMMs with 3 and 5 states following the approach developed in [15], [16]. Therefore, under the structural constraints defined in section IV, these HMMs are (locally) optimized to statistically characterize the original error pattern. The resulting 3-states and 5-states HMMs are defined by the vectors [f00, a11, f11, P U1, P D1] = [.9999, .9994, .9995, .0730, .0771] and [f00, a11, u12, a22, f22, f11, P U1, P U2, P D1, P D2] = [.9999, .9994, .0002, .9974, .9977, .9994, .0490, .3959, .3929, .0515], respectively. The GE model is obtained by merging the states U1 and D1 of the 3-state HMM into state B of the GE model. Merging is performed by fixing Ps(B) = Ps(U1) + Ps(D1), where Ps(S) represents the probability of state S, and keeping the value of b and ρ fixed. The resulting GE model is given by [g, b, PG, PB] = [0.0002727, .0001, 0, .0752].

VI. CONCLUSION
We propose a system consisting of the concatenation of two LDGM codes for improved communications over finitestate Markov channels and over indoor channels that can be approximated in this way. As advantages with respect to turbo

and standard LDPC codes, the complexity of the decoding
and encoding procedures is much lower, and the use of a
fix code for different channel environments leads to good
performance. Simulation results show that the proposed system
achieves a performance close to the theoretical limits for the
GE channel, outperforms previous LDPC-based schemes, and
is comparable to the best systems for Markov channels based
on turbo codes. Moreover, the performance is remarkable even
for real indoor wireless channels, such as DECT, that can be
approximated by Markov channels.
