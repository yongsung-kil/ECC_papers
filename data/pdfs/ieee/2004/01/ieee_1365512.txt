Abstract — We propose a family of randomized approximate algorithms, called nearest nonzero codewords search (NNCS), for computing the minimum distance of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, including Gallager-type and finite-geometrytype codes.
I. The NNCS Approach
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes in their broader-sense definition are linear codes whose parity-check matrices have fewer 1s than 0s. It is well known that the determination of the minimum distance of a linear code is an NP-hard problem, and thus the minimum distance of most LDPC codes remains largely unknown.
A closely related work is the error impulse method proposed by Berrou et al. [1], originally intended for computing the minimum distance of turbo codes. However the error impulse method failed to provide the correct answer for nontrivial LDPC codes, largely because of the vast existence of (near) pseudo-codewords.
In [2], we proposed a randomized algorithm called nearest nonzero codeword search (NNCS) approach to tackle this problem for Gallager-type LDPC codes. The fundamental principle is to perturb the transmit vector corresponding to the all-zero codeword with controlled noise, and to use a form of list decoding to obtain nonzero codewords that are closest to the all-zero codeword in terms of Hamming distance. If the perturbing noise is minimized so that the noise-corrupted vector received is decoded just barely away from the all-zero codeword, the hope is that then there is a high probability that the Hamming weight of the nearest nonzero codeword will reveal information on the minimum distance. The efficiency of the NNCS approach primarily depends on the quality of the perturbing noise and on the capability of the list decoder to trap minimum-weight nonzero codewords.
We investigated two classes of perturbing noise: the errorimpulse noise borrowed from [1] and bit-reversing noise. The reliability-based list decoder can be viewed as a modified version of Fossorier’s IRB near-ML decoder [3] in which the reprocessing phase has been modified based on the noise patterns used. The estimated minimum distance and multiplicity of some well-known LDPC codes using the NNCS approach are shown in Table 1. For details of the algorithm and more numerical examples, refer to [2].
II. Application to Finite-Geometry-Type Codes
We found empirically that the algorithm of [2] does not always work well for finite-geometry-type codes, whose parity-check matrices are aboundant in redundant parity-check equations. This algorithm selection is motivated by the following observation: for DSC codes, the BP decoding often converges to a wrong codeword when in error, especially for the BSC (this behavior differs from that of Gallager-type LDPC codes, for which most errors are detectable).
We applied this new version of the NNCS approach to the DSC(1057, 813) code, and found 396 codewords of weight 34 (the true minimum distance). We also applied it to a new class of finite geometry codes, i.e. quasi-cycle codes from a finite affine plane [4], with (238, 158), (990, 748), (238, 174) and (990, 780) codes. From their structural property, the minimum distances of the first two codes are known to be 17 and 33, respectively, whereas those of the latter two codes are only bounded in the range of 8 < dmin < 16 and 16 < dmin < 32, respectively. The algorithm yields numerous codewords of weight 17 for the (238, 158) code and codewords of weight 33 for the (990, 748) code with reasonable time and computing power. For the latter two codes with unknown minimum distance, the algorithm finds 921 codewords of weight 10 for the (238, 174) code and 28 codewords of weight 24 for the (990, 780) code, respectively.
