An Improved Quasi-Cyclic Low-Density ParityCheck Code for Memory Channels

Muruganandam Jayabalan and Hyuck M. Kwon
Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, Wichita State University, Wichita, Kansas, USA.
Email:{mxjayabalan,hyuck.kwon}@wichita.edu

Abstract—This paper presents an improved construction of circulant sub-matrices based Quasi-Cyclic Low Density Parity Check codes (QC-LDPC) under fading channels. The proposed construction yields a performance gain of about 2 to 5 dB at a 10-4 bit error rate (BER). This paper also proposes a decoding method to reduce the implementation complexity in the Belief Propagation algorithm. Furthermore, this paper studies the performance of circulant sub-matrices based QC-LDPC codes at higher rates under AWGN and Fading channels.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low Density Parity Check codes (LDPC) have attracted much research attention because of their performance improvements over the well-known Turbo code [1, 2]. Recently, a Quasi-Cyclic (QC)-LDPC has been proposed to reduce the complexity of the LDPC further while obtaining the similar performance [3]. LDPC codes can be decoded by using the Belief Propagation algorithm. The same soft decision iterative decoding algorithm can be applied to the QC-LDPC codes. The significant benefit of the QC-LDPC lies in the code construction where the rows of the generator matrix are just cyclic shifts of the first row. These structured QC-LDPC codes having a relatively simple algebraic construction that can be implemented with an inexpensive shift register generator which greatly simplifies the encoder design. The generator G or Parity check matrix H of an LDPC has been generated randomly, which requires large power and storage space because of its larger size. The previous work on the circulant sub-matrices based QC-LDPC has been tested only under an additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel environment [3]. These QC LDPC codes should also avoid cycles of length 4 as defined in [3, 4].
This paper proposes an improved QC-LDPC which shows a significant gain over the QC-LDPC [3] under a mobile fading environment. The key idea is to place the ‘ones’ in the sparse circulant sub-matrices of H with a distance larger than the fading depth. We show that the BER performance of closely placed 'ones' is worse than that of the widely placed 'ones' in a circulant sub-matrix. The proposed method can improve the performance of a QC-LDPC significantly because a general LDPC or QC-LDPC does not have an interleaver and deinterleaver. The performance of our proposed QC-LDPC is close to that of a computer generated random LDPC with a

code rate greater than, or equal to, 2/3. In addition, this paper proposes a decoding approach to reduce the hardware complexity involved in the Belief Propagation.

The paper is organized as follows. In Section II, we briefly discuss the code construction of a QC-LDPC code and its improved structure for fading channels. Section III presents an overview about an LDPC code and its decoding algorithm. The proposed low complexity decoding method is also presented in this section. All details about the simulation parameters and results are explained in Section IV. Finally conclusions are drawn and discussed in Section V.

II. QC-LDPC ENCODER

This paper discusses an algebraic construction for the

regular and irregular QC-LDPC codes as in [3]. The regular

LDPC codes have the same number of ones in every row and

column. The irregular LDPC codes have a different number of

ones in columns and rows. The QC-LDPC codes consist of

horizontally concatenated circulant sub-matrices. Each

circulant sub-matrix is a square matrix for which every row is

the cyclic shift of the previous row, and the first row is

obtained by the cyclic shift of the last row. In this way, every

column of each circulant sub-matrix is automatically the

cyclic shift of the previous column, and the first column is

obtained by the cyclic shift of the last column. The H matrix

of dimension (m×Lm) for the QC-LDPC can be written as

H = [H1 H2 H3 ... H L ]

(1)

where Hi is the i-th circulant sub-matrix of dimension

(m×m), i=1, …, L. For the circulant matrices, the row weight

and column weight are the same and fixed. Once the parity

check matrix H is defined, the generator matrix is obtained.

The matrices are created such that they should satisfy the

constraint GH T = 0 . All the bits to be encoded are run through

the generator matrix, and, therefore, all valid code words obey

the property CH T = 0 .

B. Proposed QC-LDPC under Fading Channel
In general the LDPC and QC-LDPC do not employ any interleaver and de-interleaver. Under a fading environment, the performance of the LDPC/QC-LDPC may be degraded due to memory introduced by the fading channel. If the nonzero positions in each row of each circulant sub-matrix in H are widely placed, then the effects of memory introduced by fading channel on the performance can be minimized. This is because the connections between the bit nodes and check nodes in the Tanner graph exist only for the nonzero positions in the H matrix [4]. So we can expect that the QC-LDPC codes tend to perform better with widely placed ‘ones’ when compared to the closely placed ‘ones’ under fading. It is observed that the ‘ones’ should be widely placed not only in the circulant sub-matrix, but also between the adjacent submatrices. Experimental results confirm this conjecture: A significant improvement in the signal-bit-energy-to-noise density ratio (SNR=Eb/N0) is observed over the previous QCLDPC under fading channel. Therefore, this paper proposes to make the separation between the nonzero positions in H, i.e., |ab| in (5), as large as possible. The previous work [3] just specifies the conditions to avoid the cycle length 4 in (5) without discussing the fading channel effects.
III. LDPC/QC-LDPC DECODER
The LDPC and QC-LDPC codes can use the same decoding algorithm using the appropriate parity check matrix H. The LDPC is a linear block code defined by a parity check matrix H with m rows and n columns that mostly contain zeros and very few ones. LDPC codes can be represented by a Tanner graph that contains two different nodes: Bit nodes and Check nodes [4]. Each bit node corresponds to a code bit, and the check node corresponds to one parity check constraint on the bits defining a codeword. In other words, these bit nodes and the check nodes relate to the columns and rows of H, respectively. An edge between a bit node and a check node exits if, and only if, the bits participate in the parity check equation represented by the check node. The decoder can be represented as a soft decision iterative algorithm called a message passing or Belief propagation algorithm based on a Tanner graph made up of check and bit nodes. Messages which are extrinsic information based on a-posteriori probability (APP) are passed along edges. A full iteration is defined as a cycle of message passing from bit nodes to check nodes and check nodes to bit nodes. The decoder is initialized by a soft-decision information received codeword. A full iteration is completed by a hard decision syndrome calculation. If a syndrome is detected, iteration continues. If

IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
The simulation parameters used are shown in TABLE I. Two different channel environments are considered. The first case is with the AWGN channel, and the other with a fading channel. Simulations for both LDPC and QC-LDPC with different code rates are performed. A significant performance gain of about 5 dB was observed at 10−4 for an improved QCLDPC under fading channels as shown in Fig. 3. A careful inspection of Fig. 3 reveals that at very high SNRs, the performance of QC-LDPC and LDPC codes tend to saturate. This is primarily due to the Doppler shift under fading conditions. Furthermore at very high SNRs, the likelihood functions, which are typically real values, tend towards ‘ones’ and ‘zeros’ thus degrading the decoder performance. Fig. 4 shows the simulation results for higher rate QC-LDPC and LDPC codes. The performance of circulant sub-matrices based QC-LDPC codes is comparable to the computer generated random LDPC codes at higher rates. Proposed low complexity decoding method for LDPC yields a similar performance with iterative decoding both in an AWGN and a fading channel. The appropriate results are shown in Fig. 5 and Fig. 6. It is observed that the low complexity decoding approach can be extended for the QC-LDPC codes.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, an improved QC-LDPC construction scheme for fading channels and a low complexity decoding scheme for the LDPC and QC-LDPC have been proposed. The results presented show that the QC-LDPC with widely placed ‘ones’ performs better than the QC-LDPC with closely placed ‘ones’ under fading channels. The proposed low complexity decoding method significantly simplifies the hardware complexity. The structured QC-LDPC codes have a relatively simple algebraic construction, which greatly simplifies the encoder design. Simulation studies also show that the performance of QC-LDPC codes is comparable to the computer generated random LDPC codes at higher rates. This code construction can also be easily extended to the irregular QC-LDPC codes.
