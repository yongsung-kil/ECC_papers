Construction of Low-Density Parity-Check Codes Based on Balanced Incomplete Block Designs
Bassem Ammar, Bahram Honary, Senior Member, IEEE, Yu Kou, Jun Xu, Member, IEEE, and Shu Lin, Life Fellow, IEEE
Abstract—This correspondence presents a method for constructing structured regular low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes based on a special type of combinatoric designs, known as balance incomplete block
designs. Codes constructed by this method have girths at least 6 and they
perform well with iterative decoding. Furthermore, several classes of these codes are quasi-cyclic and hence their encoding can be implemented with simple feedback shift registers.
Index Terms—Balanced incomplete block design, quasic-cyclic code, symmetrically repeated difference.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, discovered by Gallager in early 1960s [1], were recently rediscovered and shown to form a class of Shannon-limit-approaching codes [2]–[8]. These codes decoded with an iterative soft-input and soft-output decoding algorithm, such as the sum–product algorithm (SPA) [1], [4], [5], [8]–[13], achieves amazingly good performance. Long LDPC codes have been constructed and they perform only a few tenths of a decibel (or even a fewer thousands of a decibel [7]) from the Shannon limit. Since their discovery, design, construction, decoding, analysis, and applications of LDPC codes have become focal points of research.
Based on methods of construction, LDPC codes can be divided into two categories: 1) random codes [1], [3]–[7], [14]–[17] and 2) structured codes [8], [13], [18]–[37]. Random LDPC codes are constructed by computer search based on certain design rules or graph structures, such as girth and degree distributions of nodes, while structured LDPC codes are constructed based on algebraic and combinatoric methods. Long random LDPC codes in general perform closer to the Shannon limit than their equivalent structrued LDPC codes, however, they usually do not have sufficient structure to allow simple encoding. In construct, structured codes in general have encoding advantage over random codes, especially cyclic or quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes [8], [18], [30], [32]–[37] which can be encoded with simple shift registers with complexity linearly proportional to the number of parity-check bits for serial coding and to the code length for parallel encoding [38]. This correspondence is concerned with construction of structured LDPC codes based on a branch in combinatorial mathematics, known as balanced incomplete block designs (BIBDs). Construction based on a special family of BIBDs gives a class of structured regular LDPC codes with girths at least 6. The correspondence is organized as follows. In Section II, we first give a brief introduction of BIBDs and then present a simple construction of regular LDPC codes based on BIBDs. In Section III, we describe several special classes of BIBDs with cyclic structure constructed by Bose in the late 1930s [39]. In Section IV, we give constructions of six classes of LDPC codes constructed based on the classes of BIBDs presented in Section III. Four classes of these codes are quasi-cyclic. All codes in these six classes have girth at least 6. Example codes are given and experimental results show that they perform close to the Shannon limit and equally well as their equivalent computer-generated random MacKay codes [4]. In Section V, we present a method for constructing a class of QC-LDPC codes based on a special decomposition of incidence matrices of Bose-BIBDs. In Section IV, we concludes the correspondence with some remarks.
II. DEFINITIONS AND GENERAL CONSTRUCTION OF LDPC CODES BASED ON BIBDS
A binary regular LDPC code is defined as the null space of a sparse parity-check matrix H over GF (2) with the following structural properties: 1) each row has weight ; 2) each column has weight 
; 3) no two rows (or two columns) have more than one 1-component in common; 4) both  and 
 are small compared to the code length. Property 3) ensures that the Tanner graph of the code is free of cycles of length 4 and hence has girth at least 6 [9]. Properties 2) and 3) ensure that there are 
 parity check sums orthogonal on every code bit and hence the minimum distance of the code is at least 
 + 1 [8]. Property 4) implies that the parity-check matrix H of the code is a sparse matrix. The LDPC code defined above is said to be (
; )-regular. If not all the rows or columns of H have constant weights, then the null space of H gives an irregular LDPC code.
An important subject in combinatorial mathematics is the design of experiments, known as combinatoric design. The objective of this subject is to design experiments systematically and with a view to their statistical analysis. One such design is called the BIBD [40]–[43]. This type of design was used for constructing block codes in the early 1970s [43], [44] and later was used for designing interleaving rules for turbo product codes [45], [46].

In this correspondence, we have presented a construction of LDPC codes based on a special type of BIBDs. Six classes of structured LDPC codes have been constructed based on three special classes of BIBDs constructed by Bose. Four of these classes of LDPC codes are quasicyclic.
