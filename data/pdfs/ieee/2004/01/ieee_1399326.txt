A Hybrid Decoding Approach for LDPC Coded MIMO-OFDM Systems
Yan Xin1 and Syed Aon Mujtaba2
1 Dept. of Electrical and Computer Engr., National University of Singapore, Singapore 2 Wireless Systems Research Dept., Agere Systems Inc., Allentown, PA 18109, USA

Abstract—In this paper, we propose a novel decoding approach for coded multiple input multiple output (MIMO) orthogonal frequency division multiplexing (OFDM) systems that employ low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. In the proposed decoding approach, a code word is decoded in two steps, hard decoding and iterative decoding, based on the log-likelihood ratios of coded bits. We show that compared with conventional decoding approaches for LDPC coded MIMO-OFDM systems, the proposed decoding approach not only delivers a considerable reduction in decoding complexity but also maintains comparable performance.
I. INTRODUCTION
Employing multiple transmit and receive antennas (MIMO) in an orthogonal frequency division multiplexing (OFDM) system can considerably increase the spectral efficiency and the reliability of wireless transmissions over frequency-selective channels while minimizing equalization and symbol decoding complexity [1, 2, 9]. Therefore, MIMO-OFDM is envisioned as a key technology in many developing wireless communication systems such as wireless local area networks (WLAN) and 4G mobile wireless systems. The choice of error correcting codes such as convolutional codes, turbo codes, or low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes plays an important role in improving robustness as well as feasibility of MIMO-OFDM. Among these choices, it has been shown in [3,6,7] that LDPC coded MIMO-OFDM systems not only achieve superior performance but also provide design flexibility to tradeoff performance again complexity. However, an LDPC decoder using iterative decoding suffers from increased complexity compared to a convolutional (Viterbi) decoder. It is therefore desirable to design a reduced complexity decoder for LDPC coded MIMO-OFDM systems.
In this paper, we propose a hybrid decoding approach called bypass decoding for LDPC coded MIMO-OFDM systems. In the proposed decoding approach, a code word is decoded in two steps based on the outputs (log-likelihood ratios (LLRs) of coded bits) of the soft demapper. The hard decisions are first made on the coded bits whose LLRs have magnitudes above a certain threshold, and the rest of coded bits will be next decoded by using an iterative LDPC decoder. Because of randomness of the LLRs, a large fraction of coded bits could be decoded by using hard decisions without passing through the iterative LDPC decoder. In other words, the iterative LDPC decoder will be used to decode a code word with much smaller code length on the average, thereby reducing the complexity considerably. Compared with the conventional decoding approach, this decoding approach not only delivers a considerable reduction in decoding complexity but also maintains comparable performance as long as an appropriate threshold is selected. We also provide a heuristic method for selecting a suitable threshold, and show

its effectiveness by simulations.

III. CONVENTIONAL DECODING APPROACHES
The optimum decoding of c requires joint demapping and decoding, in which the likelihood value of each bit is computed using all the received signals y1,1,. . . ,yNc,Nb . Even for a code word with moderate length, the decoding complexity is prohibitive. Hence, as shown in Fig. 1, a conventional receiver uses either iterative decoding or iterative demapping-decoding. In the iterative decoding approach, the LLRs generated by the soft demapper are served as initial inputs of the decoder, within which a code word is decoded in an iterative manner [5, 6]. In the iterative demapping-decoding approach, the soft demapper and the decoder exchange extrinsic information of coded bits iteratively until desirable performance is achieved; within every iteration between the demapper and the decoder, a number of iterations are performed in the decoder to generate reliable soft information [5]. In this paper, we mainly focus on the iterative decoding approach because of its relatively low complexity. We next provide a brief description of this approach.
Consider a coded bit cn in c. Suppose that cn is mapped into a constellation symbol in sk,p for some k and p. We use M(·) to denote the image of such a map, i.e., sk,p ∈ M(cn). For notational simplicity, we henceforth drop the subscripts k and p from yk,p, sk,p, and wk,p, and rewrite (1) as y = Γks + w. We represent a logical one by +1, and a logical zero by −1. we further assume equal a priori probabilities, i.e., P (cn = 1) = P (cn = −1). IV. A HYBRID DECODING APPROACH: BYPASS DECODING
Unlike transmissions over additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels, |L(cn|y)| in a MIMO-OFDM transmission is a random variable that depends on the average (long-term) signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and the instantaneous SNR (instantaneous channel gain) of the underlying sub-channels. Note that when a coded bit is transmitted over a sub-channel with high instantaneous SNR, a hard decision based on its LLR can be made without incurring much performance degradation. In other words, depending on the instantaneous SNRs of the underlying sub-channels certain coded bits can bypass the iterative LDPC decoder, which involves the most decoding complexity. Based on this idea, we propose the following reduced-complexity decoding approach consisting of two steps as hard decision decoding and iterative decoding using SPA. A. Selection of the Lth threshold
In principle, the selection of Lth will be affected by two factors, the acceptable probability of decoding error in step S1 and
the affordable decoding complexity in step S2. Intuitively, the larger Lth the lower the probability of decoding error in step S1 the higher the decoding complexity in step S2. Our design objective here is to select such a threshold Lth that the bypassed decoding technique will offer a similar performance as the con-
ventional approach at a reduced complexity. Thus we will focus
on examining the relationship between the probability of decoding error in step S1 and Lth.
Let P (H0) and P (H1) denote the prior probabilities of the respective hypotheses. Assuming that P (H0) = P (H1), the probability of decoding error on bit cn in step S1 can be written Pe(cn) = (P (H0|H1) + P (H1|H0))/2, where P (Hi|Hj) denotes the conditional probability of deciding Hi given that Hj is true. We further assume that P (H0|H1) = P (H1|H0). Therefore, Pe(cn) = P (H0|H1), which is the probability of the event that the decoder in (3) decides cn = +1 given that cn = −1 is actually transmitted. B. Modification of the LDPC Decoder
Based on the decision rules in (3), the hard decisions are made on certain bits of a code word. The locations and number of bits decoded in step S1 will depend on two parameters, the average SNR and the instantaneous SNR. In other words, the LDPC decoder is used to decoder a code word, which varies according to these two parameters. Correspondingly, we need modify the LDPC decoder such that it can be adaptive to such changes. Suppose that a code word c needs to be decoded. In step S1, some bits from c have already been decoded. Let I denote a set of the indices of coded bits which were decoded from step S1. The LDPC decoder is modified as follows: M1. Remove the columns from H whose indices belong to I.
The resulting matrix is denoted by H¯ . M2. Sum up the removed columns of H (binary addition) to
generate a new column vector, namely h, and insert h into H¯ to form a new LDPC matrix denoted by Hˆ . This step is used to make all parity check equations valid after the removal of the columns in step M1. M3. The SPA is applied to decode the coded bits which remain un-decoded from step S1, by using Hˆ and the corresponding soft information obtained by (2). Because the SPA is used to decode a code word which has a much smaller code length than N on the average, the complexity reduction in this step is significant. Note that step M2 adds extra complexity to compute h. Because H has very low density and the operations are carried out in the binary field. Extra complexity added in step M2 is much smaller compared with the complexity saved in step M3, which reduces a lot of operations in the logarithmic domain.
