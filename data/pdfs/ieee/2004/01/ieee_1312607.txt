Analysis of the Cycle-Structure of LDPC Codes Based on Latin Squares

University of Colorado, Boulder, USA milenkov@colorado.edu

Stefan Laendner Electrical and Computer Engineering Department
University of Colorado, Boulder Stefan.Laendner@colorado.edu

Abstract— In this paper, we introduce a family of structured low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes based on a class of idempotent, symmetric Latin and modified Latin squares. The parity-check matrices of the codes have a block structure with permutation blocks which insures that both their corresponding girth and minimum distance are at least equal to six. The storage requirement for codes from this class is reduced to only one parameter. We also propose a structured method for shortening the codes by removing columns that break a large number of cycles of length six. The storage requirements for the codes obtained using the described procedure consist in memorizing only two integers, while their performance under iterative decoding matches that of random-like codes of comparable length.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes were first introduced by Gallager [6], but their potential for achieving performance close to the Shannon limit was not recognized until the work of MacKay [11]. The revived interest in this graph-based coding technique led to the design of random-like, irregular codes with performance only 0.0045dB away from optimal [13]. Despite their excellent error-correcting properties, LDPC codes with random-like structures have time consuming encoding algorithms and a prohibitively large storage complexity. These drawbacks can be overcome by using structured LDPC codes. Several classes of structured LDPC codes are known, including those based on combinatorial designs [7], [15], finite geometries [8] and mutually orthogonal Latin squares [14]. In this paper, we propose a new design for LDPC codes based on idempotent, symmetric Latin and modified Latin squares. LDPC codes based on properly chosen Latin squares have girth six, they are Pasch-free and their cycle-length distribution can be estimated easily. Modified Latin squares described in this paper represent a new combinatorial class of objects that can be used for constructing both regular and irregular codes.
In order to obtain a large family of Latin square codes, the initially devised codes can be shortened by erasing specific block-columns from their parity-check matrices. This shortening should be performed in such a way that the resulting codes still have a very simple structure and a small set of description parameters. Furthermore, it is desirable to remove block-columns that create the largest possible number of cycles of short length. It will be shown in the paper that both these goals can be achieved for the proposed LDPC codes in a very simple and efficient manner.

The outline of the paper is as follows. In Section 2 we introduce some elementary definitions regarding Latin squares and combinatorial designs, as well as a construction method for Steiner triple systems (STSs) due to Bose [9]. Furthermore, we describe some good choices for Latin squares that result in a Pasch-free design (i.e. a corresponding LDPC code with minimum distance at least six). Additionally, we describe a new combinatorial object, named a modified generalized Latin square, and explain its application for constructing irregular LDPC codes. In Section 3 we establish simple bounds on the number of six-cycles in the codes and classify sub-blocks of the parity check matrix H according to the number of sixcycles they generate. In Section 4 we describe how to shorten the codes by removing block-columns of the parity-check matrix in a structured manner, so as to significantly reduce the number of six-cycles. Simulation results and concluding remarks are presented in Section 5.
II. MATHEMATICAL PRELIMINARIES
A Steiner triple system (STS) is an ordered pair (V, B), where V is a v-element set and B is a collection of b subsets of size three over V , called blocks. Every element of V is contained in exactly r blocks and every two-subset of V is contained in exactly one block. STSs are extensively studied combinatorial, and methods for constructing these designs are described in [1] and [2].
The construction of STSs described below, due to Bose [9], utilizes idempotent, symmetric Latin squares. A Latin square of order m is an m × m array such that each row and column contains the symbols in {1, . . . , m} exactly once. A Latin square is idempotent if cell (i, i) contains symbol i, 1 ≤ i ≤ m, and symmetric (commutative) if cells (i, j) and (j, i) for 1 ≤ i < j ≤ m, contain the same symbol.

• All triples of the form
{(i, 1), (i, 2), (i, 3)},
where 1 ≤ i ≤ 2m + 1 (we refer to these blocks as Type 1 triples);

Let SQ be a Latin square of dimension 2m + 1 ≡ 0 mod 7, with an entry in row i and column j chosen according to the rule i ◦ j = (i + j)/2 mod (2m + 1). A square obtained in this way is clearly symmetric and idempotent. Furthermore, the STS resulting from this construction is Paschfree, i.e. it does not contain a Pasch configuration. A Pasch configuration of a STS is a set of four blocks with exactly six points, and in the context of LDPC coding such configurations introduce codewords of weight six [15]. In order to prove the afore mentioned result, one has to observe the following two properties of the square SQ described above: there are no Latin sub-squares of dimension two contained within SQ (Property 1) and there do not exist two different integers x, y such that x ◦ (x ◦ (x ◦ y)) = y (Property 2). The presence of a sub-square of dimension two necessarily implies that there exist integers i = l and j = k such that i ◦ k = l ◦ j and i ◦ j = l ◦ k. Since the dimension of the square is odd, this is impossible for the given construction since it implies that i = l and k = j. The second property can be easily shown to be true by observing that for the given construction x◦(x◦(x◦y)) = y is equivalent to

The point-block incidence matrix of a STS constructed based on a Latin square can be used as the parity-check matrix of a structured LDPC code. For a Latin square of order 2m+1, the number of parity checks is v = 6m + 3, while the number of blocks is (3m+1)(2m+1) = (v(v −1))/6. The girth of the code-graph is guaranteed to be six, since by the very definition of a STS system every pair of points belongs to exactly one block. In the following section, we will estimate the number of six-cycles in the code and propose a structured approach for shortening the codes by removing columns of the parity check matrix that participate in the largest number of sixcycles. Additionally, we will evaluate the number of six-cycles in the code after the described column removal procedure.

III. STRUCTURE OF THE PARITY-CHECK MATRIX

Throughout the rest of the paper, we will refer to a column

of blocks (matrices) as to a block-column, and to a regular

column of a matrix as to a column. Similarly, we will refer to

a structure corresponding to a six-cycle formed by blocks as a

block-cycle and to a regular six-cycle in the bipartite graph as

to a cycle. The notation for a block-six-cycle will be B6. The

same structures, but with respect to rows, are named similarly.

Due to the cyclic nature of the Bose construction, the parity-

check matrix of the generated code has a block structure, with

blocks that are permutation matrices. Let us label the points

(i, a), 1 ≤ i ≤ 2n+1, a ∈ {1, 2, 3} of the STS by the integers

3·(i−1)+a, respectively. Additionally, let us index the blocks

in such a way that Type 1 triples are numbered consecutively

using the smallest values, followed by Type 2 triples with all

their cyclic shifts in consecutive order. In this manner, Type 1

triples produce 2m + 1 columns with three consecutive ones

in the vertical direction.

Since a set of three consecutive Type 2 triples differs only

in a cyclic shift of their a values, the parity-check matrix H

has a regular structure with 2m + 1 blocks per column and

m(2m + 1) blocks per row. By using the standard notation

The matrix H2 is regular in the sense that there are exactly two I matrices and one P 2 matrix per column; the number of P 2 and I matrices per block-row is m and 2m, respectively. Note that the positions of P 2 matrices within the blocks Mi also define a permutation matrix.
IV. NUMBER OF SIX-CYCLES
It can be shown that a Type 1 triple can be part of only one type of configuration of a B6-cycle, obtained from one P 2 and three I matrices. Once the choices for two ones in 1 and for one I matrix are made, the whole configuration becomes uniquely determined. Hence, each Type 1 triple produces 3 · 2 · 2 m = 12 m six-cycles. Therefore, removing columns corresponding to all Type 1 triples from the paritycheck matrix breaks a number of cycles proportional to the order of the Latin square, and also reduces the code length by 2m + 1; from this point on we will only analyze the part of the parity-check matrix corresponding to Type 2 triples.
A B6-cycle can occur for the following patterns of I and P 2 matrices listed below. Note that whenever allowed by the structure, block-rows and block-columns can be arbitrarily permuted.
• Case (a): Only I matrices participate in the formation of a cycle;
• Case (b): Two P 2 matrices within the same row participate in the formation of a six-cycle;
• Case (c): Two P 2 matrices in two different rows, connected through a pair of I matrices, participate in the cycle. Note that in order to close the cycle, one must also “pass” through two I matrices;
• Case (d): Exactly one P 2 matrix per row and per column participates in the cycle;
One can describe a general pattern for the appearance of blockcycles based on all the described configurations. Let us define the following three mappings:
f0 : I → I, P2 → P2, f1 : P2 → I, f2 : I → P2.
The mappings f i, i ∈ {0, 1, 2} correspond to multiplication with P i; so, for example, f 1(P 2) = P · P 2 = I and f 2(I) = P 2 · I = P 2. In order to construct a valid B6-cycle, one needs to satisfy the property that the composition of the three mappings within the rows as well as the columns produces the identity mappings. The mapping is uniquely specified by taking the first non-zero block in the block-row (column) and mapping it to the second non-zero block, for all except for the last block-row (column) where the direction is reversed. V. STRUCTURED REMOVAL OF SIX-CYCLES
It is straightforward to see that removing the first column in the leftmost block M1 breaks at least as many cycles of each type as described below:
• (Case a) 2m − 1 B6-cycles that contain the first blockcolumn;
• (Case b) 4 · (m − 1) B6-cycles formed by the P 2 matrix in the removed column and another P 2 matrix within the same row;
• (Case c) 2 · (2m − 1) B6-cycles formed by the P 2 matrix in the removed column and and another P 2, connected through a pair of I matrices;
• (Case d) 4 · m B6-cycles formed by the P 2 matrix within the removed column and two other P 2 matrices within different columns.
