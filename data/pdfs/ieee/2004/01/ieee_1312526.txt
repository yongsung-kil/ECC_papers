Hybrid Hard-Decision Iterative Decoding of Regular Low-Density Parity-Check Codes
Pirouz Zarrinkhat and Amir H. Banihashemi Broadband Communications and Wireless Systems (BCWS) Centre Department of Systems and Computer Engineering, Carleton University
1125 Colonel By Drive, Ottawa, Ontario, Canada K1S 5B6 Emails: {pirouzz, ahashemi}@sce.carleton.ca

Abstract— Hybrid decoding is to combine different iterative decoding algorithms with the aim of improving error performance or decoding complexity. In this work, we introduce “time-invariant” hybrid (HT I ) algorithms, and show that for regular low-density parity-check codes and binary messagepassing algorithms, HT I algorithms perform remarkably better than their constituent algorithms. We also show that compared to “switch-type” hybrid algorithms, such as Gallager’s algorithm B, where a comparable improvement is obtained by switching between different iterative decoding algorithms, HT I algorithms are far less sensitive to channel conditions and thus can be practically more attractive.
I. INTRODUCTION
In this paper, we discuss the idea of combining existing iterative decoding algorithms in order to devise more effective algorithms. This is referred to as “hybrid algorithms.” Hybrid algorithms were first introduced by Gallager in the construction of his well-known algorithm B [3, p. 50], and have been since revisited in literature such as [1], [2], [4]. We, more specifically, refer to Gallager’s algorithm B (GB) and algorithms in [1] as “switch-type” hybrid (HST), since they switch between different algorithms through the iteration process. In this work, we propose a new family of hybrid algorithms, called “time-invariant” hybrid (HT I ). These algorithms use a specific blend of different algorithms in each iteration. The blend, however, does not change by iteration, hence, the name “time-invariant.” In Section II, restricting our attention to regular low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [3], we will provide a formal definition for hybrid algorithms. Focusing on an important category of binary message-passing decoding algorithms, “majority-based” (MB) algorithms [6], we will then show the superiority of MB HT I algorithms over their constituents. Finally, in Section III, we compare MB HT I algorithms with MB HST algorithms, both asymptotically, using “density-evolution” [5], and at finite block lengths, using simulations, and demonstrate that in the absence of the knowledge of the channel, MB HT I algorithms can handily beat MB HST algorithms in terms of error performance and decoding complexity (convergence speed). Our study also indicates that, unlike the case for MB HST algorithms, the optimal design of MB HT I algorithms is universal in the sense that it is not very sensitive to the knowledge of the channel parameter. Throughout the paper we follow [5] for basic definitions.

A. Unknown Channel Parameter
Let us consider the case where the channel parameter is unknown and our main concern is to ensure the convergence of MB HTI or HST algorithms for as wide a range of channel parameters as possible. It is shown in Appendix II that the convergence of error probability of an MB hybrid algorithm to zero for a parameter implies its convergence to zero for every parameter < . Therefore, the best strategy we can choose in this case is to set these algorithms for their maximum thresholds. The following two examples show that for such a scenario, although the convergence of MB HST algorithms is faster than that of MB HTI algorithms when the channel parameter is close to the maximum threshold, the latter can outpace the former for a wide range of parameters less than the maximum threshold.

IV. CONCLUSION

A general framework for hybrid decoding algorithms is introduced. Using density-evolution and by focusing on hybrid algorithms which are based on majority-based (MB) algorithms, we show the great improvement in threshold and speed of convergence that time-invariant hybrid (HTI) and switchtype hybrid (HST) algorithms can provide compared to their constituents. We also investigate convergence properties of these algorithms in detail and provide simulation results at a finite block length. Our results indicate that although MB HST algorithms outperform MB HTI algorithms asymptotically (at very large block lengths) and when the channel parameter is perfectly known, the trend changes at finite block lengths or when a perfect knowledge of the channel is not available. This suggests that MB HTI algorithms could be practically more attractive than MB HST algorithms, such as the well-known Gallager’s algorithm B. Although in this paper we have only considered regular low-density parity-check codes, many of our results can be extended to irregular codes, as well.
