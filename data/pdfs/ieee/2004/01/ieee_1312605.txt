Abstract— Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes in their broader-sense definition are linear codes whose parity-check matrices have fewer 1s than 0s. Finding their minimum distance is therefore in general an NP-hard problem. We propose a randomized algorithm called nearest nonzero codeword search (NNCS) approach to tackle this problem for iteratively decodable LDPC codes. The principle of the NNCS approach is to search codewords locally around the all-zero codeword perturbed by minimal noise, anticipating that the resultant nearest nonzero codewords will most likely contain the minimum-Hammingweight codeword whose Hamming weight is equal to the minimum distance of the linear code. This approach has its roots in Berrou et al.’s error-impulse method and a form of Fossorier’s list decoding for LDPC codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes were originally introduced by Gallager [1]. Interest in them has recently been revived because of the success of iterative decoding for turbo codes [2]. LDPC codes are competitors of these codes, as their performance with iterative decoding closely approaches the Shannon limit [3]–[5].
A binary (n, k) LDPC code, in its broader definition, is a linear block code described by a sparse m × n parity-check matrix H, i.e., H has much fewer 1s than 0s. Each symbol is checked by a small number of parity checks, and each parity check includes a small number of symbols. An LDPC code is called (ds, dc)-regular if in the corresponding bipartite graph, every symbol node is connected to ds check nodes and every check node to dc symbol nodes; otherwise it is called an irregular LDPC code.
There are many methods to construct good Tanner graphs or LDPC codes, such as random constructions [4], [6], geometry/graph-theoretic constructions [7]–[13], and the progressive-edge-growth (PEG) construction [14]–[16]. All these codes exhibit excellent empirical performance with the widespread practice of simulating performance only down to a block (bit) error probability of 10−5 (10−7). To obtain lower error probabilities that would exceed current computing capability, one has to rely on the theoretically asymptotic performance, namely, the asymptote of the union bound, which only depends on the minimum distance dmin and the multiplicity a(dmin). Unfortunately, the problem of computing dmin and/or a(dmin) of a particular LDPC code remains largely

unsolved. Our goal is to provide a solution to this difficult problem.
The union bound may not be the only way to estimate the performance under iterative decoding at low error rates, see also the discussion of (near) pseudo-codewords and of trapping sets in [17]–[19]. Nevertheless it still is a useful tool as it may also suggest error floors, as shown by specific examples in what follows.
II. THE NNCS APPROACH
The minimum-distance problem associated with a linear code is to find the minimum distance dmin of the corresponding code defined by a parity-check matrix H (or generator matrix). The minimum distance of a linear code clearly is related to its error-correction capability (dmin − 1)/2 and, therefore, finding dmin is a fundamental computational problem in coding theory. A polynomial time algorithm to compute the distance would be the ideal solution to the problem, as it could be used to construct good error-correcting codes by choosing a parity-check matrix at random and checking whether the associated code has a large minimum distance. Unfortunately, no such algorithm is known. The complexity of this problem (can it be solved in polynomial time?) was first explicitly raised by Berlekamp et al. in 1978 [20], who conjectured it to be nondeterministic polynomial time (NP)hard. This conjecture was finally confirmed by Vardy [21] in 1997, who proved that the minimum distance cannot be computed in polynomial time unless P = NP.
Finding the minimum distance of an LDPC code is therefore in general an NP-hard problem; in other words, no known polynomial deterministic algorithm to compute the minimum distance of a particular, nontrivial LDPC code exactly exists.
A concrete local search approach to approximate the minimum distance of an iteratively decodable LDPC code, called nearest nonzero codeword search (NNCS) approach, is proposed in this section. The fundamental principle is to perturb the transmit vector corresponding to the all-zero codeword with random and/or deterministic noise, and to use a form of list decoding to obtain nonzero codewords that are closest to the all-zero codeword in terms of Hamming distance. The hope is that, if the perturbing noise is minimized so that the noisecorrupted received vector is decoded just barely away from the all-zero codeword and to the nearest nonzero codeword,

B. List Decoder
The NNCS approach for computing the minimum distance of a particular LDPC code relies on the heuristic that the minimum perturbing noise for the decoder to deviate marginally from the all-zero codeword probably leads to the minimum-weight codeword. An exact nearest-codeword decoder under certain criteria, e.g. ML, is computationally infeasible. We present a form of list decoder that is a variant of Fossorier’s iterative reliability-based (IRB) decoding algorithm [24]. The IRB algorithm is a near-ML decoding method combining the sum-product algorithm (SPA) and order-statistic-decoding (OSD) algorithm. At each iteration, the reliability information, i.e. the a posteriori probabilities (APPs) for each bit delivered by the SPA, is sorted to find the most reliable basis (MRB). Then a systematic reprocessing of candidate codewords expressed in the MRB is performed. The difference between the list decoder and the IRB decoder is that the former only has to select the least-weight nonzero codeword from the candidate list as its output, whereas the latter has to select the most likely codeword. Also, owing to the different nature of the noise patterns, another reprocessing strategy is used in the list decoder. The list decoder per each iteration is summarized as follows.
Step 0: Perform the SPA, delivering reliability informations for each bit. Step 1: Determine and permute the positions of N e least reliable bits (marked as erasures) based on the APPs delivered by the SPA to the left of the parity-check matrix. The remaining positions (non-erasures) are preloaded with hard decisions 0 or 1 based on the SPA outputs.
Step 2: Perform semi-Gaussian elimination on the permuted parity-check matrix from left to right, yielding an approximate upper diagonal matrix (see Fig. 1). The dependent columns encountered during the Gaussian elimination are permuted next to the last independent column. Denote the number of dependent columns by N d. Step 3: Try all combinations on the N d erasures corresponding to the dependent columns. For each combination, check the set of parity-check equations N e − N d + 1, . . . , m. If any equation is violated, abort this combination. If all equations are satisfied, then the remaining N e − N d erasures can be determined uniquely by a simple recursive procedure, first the bit position N e − N d and last the left-most bit position, by satisfying the check equations N e − N d, N e − N d − 1, . . . , 1 in sequence. In this way a valid codeword is obtained. At most 2Nd codewords can be found in each iteration.
If the input to the list decoder is the bit-reversing noise pattern specified by (6), the SPA does not update the symbol node at the reversed bit position so that the hard decision of that bit is always 1.
The above procedure runs until a valid codeword has been reached by the SPA, or a maximum number of iterations has been fulfilled. The complexity of the list decoder per iteration depends on the complexity of SPA, and the values of N e and

The conceptual difference between the NNCS approach and the error-impulse method of Berrou et al. is that NNCS does not rely on the correctness of (2), but on the nonzero codewords found around minimally perturbed corrupted vectors. In other words, the minimum distance in the NNCS approach is estimated on the basis of all codewords found (thus an upper bound), rather than the integer values of Ai. As a byproduct, the NNCS approach also yields a lower bound on the multiplicity based on the collected distinct codewords.

IV. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper we have proposed the NNCS approach to solve the minimum-distance problem for iteratively decodable LDPC codes. The principle of the NNCS approach is to search codewords locally around the all-zero codeword perturbed by a minimal level of noise. The resultant nearest codewords most likely contain the minimum Hamming weight codeword whose Hamming weight is equal to the minimum distance of the linear code.
We have considered two classes of perturbing noises: errorimpulse noise from Berrou et al., and bit-reversing noise. The reliability-based list decoder can be viewed as a modified version of Fossorier’s IRB near-ML decoder in which the reprocessing phase has been modified based on the noise patterns used. The effectiveness of this approach has been verified by numerous examples. It is worth mentioning that the notion of reliability-based list decoder can also be combined with Berrou et al.’s error-impulse method to tackle the minimum-distance problem of turbo codes.
The reliability-based list decoder can be computationally intensive, owing to the semi-Gaussian elimination procedure involved. In view of the NP-hardness of the original minimumdistance problem, the polynomial complexity of the semiGaussian elimination procedure represents a significant improvement. Furthermore, this portion of computation should not be a serious problem if care has been taken to exploit the sparseness of the parity-check matrix. Above all, the minimum distance or weight spectrum has to be computed only once.
The NNCS approach has its limitations. For linear block codes, which are not amenable to iterative decoding, the iterative NNCS decoder generally fails to find the nearest nonzero codewords because the SPA does not work well unless a sparse graph representation with a good degree distribution is used. Nevertheless, it seems that in this area not much can be done from an algorithmic point of view, because the approximation of the minimum-distance problem for generic linear codes, as shown in [28], remains unfortunately NP-hard.
