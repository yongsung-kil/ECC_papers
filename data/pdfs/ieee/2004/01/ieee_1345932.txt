Absrracf-Density evolution is a new method for analyzing the asymptotic performance of network capability approaching error-correcting codes. For irregular LDPC codes with Message-Passing decoding, the density evolution method can track the messages to find out the threshold, enabling optimization of the degree distribution. In this paper, the principle of density evolution combined with the decoding process is firstly explored. Then, two algorithms for programming the evolution proceeding will he discussed: the discretized density evolution and the Gaussian approximation, as well as their application conditions. Finally, simulation results are presented. '
Keywords: irregular LDPC codes, density evolution, threshold, Gaussian approximation.
I. INTRODUCTION The error correcting ability of codes ensemble with hard
decision is estimated by analyzing its minimum distance in the classical channel coding theory. However, the performance of some codes appears asymptotic when soft decision and iterative decoding are used. Density evolution is the very method for analyzing such asymptotic performance.
Irregular LDPC codes are excellent error-correcting codes, the best simulation result of which shows that they
can operate within 0.0045dB of the Shannon limit for BIAWGN channelsf2].In this regard, degree distribution is
essential for producing good LDPC code ensemble, for which asymptotic performance can be described by threshold. The greater the threshold, the closer to the Shannon limit the code can operate. Density evolution provides an efficient way to determine the thresholds of LDPC code ensemble, so that one can search good degree distribution pairs. Since there is no theoretical guideline. for the design of irregular LDPC codes, it is meaningful to optimize the codes by density evolution.
Section I1 explores the principle of density evolution for irregular LDPC codes in accordance with the Message-Passing decoding process. Section 111 explains' two algorithms for programming the evolution proceeding,

and compares their differences. Section IV presents some siniulations showing good distribution pairs and their thresholds.
