Methodologies for Designing LDPC Codes Using Protographs and Circulants
Jeremy Thorpe, Kenneth Andrews, and Sam Dolinar1 Jet Propulsion Laboratory, California Institute of Technology, Pasadena, CA, USA
e-mail: {jeremy, andrews, sam}@shannon.jpl.nasa.gov

Abstract — A method is presented for constructing LDPC codes with excellent performance, simple hardware implementation, low encoder complexity, and which can be concisely documented. The simple code structure is achieved by using a base graph, expanded with circulants. The base graph is chosen by computer search using simulated annealing, driven by density evolution’s decoding threshold as determined by the reciprocal channel approximation. To build a full parity check matrix, each edge of the base graph is replaced by a circulant permutation, chosen to maximize loop length by using a Viterbi-like algorithm.

I. Introduction
To design a Tanner graph with some structural regularity, many researchers interconnect T copies of a small (n, k) graph, called a protograph here following [1], analogous to the base graph of Lin, the seed graph of Tanner, and similar to the Multi-Edge-Type construction of Richardson [2]. The endpoints of the T copies of each edge e in the protograph are connected according to a permutation πe of length T .

II. Design of the Protograph
Because the neighborhoods of each node in the code’s graph are equivalent to those in the protograph, they share the same decoding threshold determined by density evolution. An (n, u, r) protograph is constructed from n transmitted variable nodes, u untransmitted variable nodes, and r check nodes, yielding a code of rate k/n ≥ (n + u − r)/n. The nodes are interconnected by a simulated annealing algorithm that inserts, deletes, and interchanges edges. This algorithm attempts to minimize the threshold as determined by the reciprocal channel approximation [3] to density evolution. By appropriately constraining the protograph, and deleting one or a few edges in the final graph, these become Irregular Repeat Accumulate (IRA) codes or a close relative thereof, permitting low complexity encoding.
One particularly good (4, 1, 3) protograph with a 0.5 dB Eb/N0 threshold is shown in Figure 1, and is closely related to the ARA code of [4]. The (6, 0, 3) protograph built from a degree-9, two degree-3, and three degree-2 variable nodes, with their connections evenly distributed among three check nodes, has a threshold of 0.65 dB and has proven particularly robust to changes in graph expansion method, decoding algorithm, and channel characteristics.

The graph resulting from protograph expansion, denoted

(n, u, r) × T , can itself be used as a protograph for further

expansion.

IV. Performance

Simulation results for two protograph codes with k ≈ 4000 are

encoders can operate on multi-symbol words because symbol

ordering is largely preserved by the circulant permutations.

erations corresponding to a single copy of the protograph.
