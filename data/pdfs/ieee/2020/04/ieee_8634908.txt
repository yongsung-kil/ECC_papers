Using Error Modes Aware LDPC to Improve Decoding Performance of 3-D TLC NAND Flash
Fei Wu , Member, IEEE, Meng Zhang , Yajuan Du, Weihua Liu, Zuo Lu, Jiguang Wan, Zhihu Tan, and Changsheng Xie, Member, IEEE

Abstract—3-D triple-level cell (3-D TLC) NAND flash has high storage density and capacity, but degrading data reliability due to high raw bit error rates induced by a certain number of program/erase cycles. To guarantee data reliability, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are selected as the error correction codes in modern flash memories because of strong error correction capability. However, directly adopting LDPC codes induces high decoding latency due to iterative updating of log-likelihood ratio (LLR) information in the decoding process. Increasing LLR information accuracy can greatly improve decoding performance. In this paper, we propose EMAL: using error modes aware LDPC codes for further enhancing the decoding performance of 3-D TLC NAND flash. We first obtain 3-D TLC error modes based on an FPGA testing platform, and then exploit the error modes to optimize LLR information and enable the decoding to converge at a high speed. The simulation results show that the decoding performance is significantly improved, resulting in reduced bit error rates and decoding latency.
Index Terms—3-D triple-level cell (3-D TLC) NAND flash, error modes, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes.

II. BACKGROUND
In this section, we introduce the basics of 3-D TLC NAND flash, describe the LDPC decoding process and explain the performance and factors of affecting decoding.

A. Basics of 3-D TLC NAND Flash
3-D NAND flash blocks with the vertical structure consist of many layers shown in Fig. 1, each of which includes a lot of cells. Bit and word lines (WLs) connect cells to form storage arrays. The source selective line and drain selective line control data programming and reading. 3-D TLC NAND flash stores three bits in each cell with eight states. When programming, three bits per cell are mapped to three different logical pages, the least significant bit (LSB) page, the central significant bit (CSB) page, and the most significant bit (MSB) page. The LSB page is formed by collecting all left bits in the three bits. Similarly, the CSB and MSB pages are formed by collecting all center and right bits, respectively, [23]. Each WL consists of such three pages. The page numbers are successive, such as WL0 with page 0, page 1, and page 2 and WLi with page 3i, page 3i+1, and page 3i+2. Currently, there are two types of 3-D NAND flash cells in industry, floating gate (FG) and CT. CT-based 3-D TLC NAND flash technologies are widely adopted by Samsung, Toshiba, and Hynix, due to better scalability and performance than FG-based 3-D TLC NAND flash [44]. The main difference between them is that an FG cell uses an FG made of doped polycrystalline silicon to store electrons rather than a silicon nitride film used in the CT-based cell. This difference leads to distinct program methods for FG and CT NAND flash [36]. Since the CT uses the insulator, there is less coupling effect between cells that may result in low program disturb. Therefore, the program method for 3-D CT NAND flash is to program three pages (i.e., LSB, CSB, and MSB pages) only undergoing one round as shown in Fig. 2. It means that they are completed to program at the same time, called one-shot-program. On the contrary, the FG has strong program disturb between cells. To program three pages in 3-D and 2-D FG NAND flash requires three rounds to complete, which is called multistep-program [44].
We introduce the read operations on different bit pages as follows.
LSB Read: The LSB read requires to apply two reference thresholds VL1 and VL2 between S0 and S1, between S4 and S5, respectively. If the cell threshold voltage VCT < VL1 or VCT > VL2, the read bit data is “1,” otherwise, “0.”
CSB Read: Three reference voltages VC1, VC2, and VC3 are applied between S1 and S2, between S3 and S4, and between S5 and S6, respectively. If VC2 > VCT > VC1 or VCT > VC3, the read bit data is 0, otherwise, 1.
MSB Read: The MSB is read by using two reference voltages, VM1 and VM2 between S2 and S3 and between S6 and S7. If VM2 > VCT > VM1, the read bit data is 0, otherwise, 1.

A. Obtaining Error Modes
We study error characteristics of 3-D CT TLC NAND flash memory on the real FPGA [45] hardware testing platform. Windows-based applications communicate with the hardware platform by using the PCIe interface and NVMe protocol. The read, program, and erase commands are sent to the testing chip. We test the 3-D CT TLC chip under different retention periods, i.e., 0-week, 1-week, 2-week, 3-week, and 4-week. The error characteristics are studied at the P/E interval as 500 cycles. We obtain several error modes, including cell state shift characteristics and error percentages of LSB, CSB, and MSB pages under different P/E cycles. We first count the state shift percentages shown in Fig. 4. The experimental results show that it is a relatively high percentage for cell states to shift to adjacent directions when suffering from noise interferences, such as state S0 to state S1. There are low percentages for crossing the middle state to shift to other states, such as state S0 to state S2 and state S3 to state S6 with less 1%.
Then, we study the RBER of different 3-D CT TLC NAND flash pages under different P/E cycles and retention time. The experimental results in Fig. 5 shows that P/E cycles and retention time can severely affect the RBER of 3-D-TLC NAND flash. The overall RBER increases as the P/E cycles and retention time increase. The RBER in LSB pages with P/E cycles

presents approximate exponential growth trend, as shown in Fig. 5(a). The RBER in LSB pages is much higher than that of the CSB and MSB pages. The RBER with retention 0-week gets to 0.011 at the P/E cycles of 6000, with retention 1-week getting to 0.0125, with retention 4-week getting to 0.015. If the P/E cycles and retention time continue to increase, the bit error rate will be higher. There is a larger probability to approach 2.0×10−2. It requires to design much stronger error correction code (ECC) to improve data reliability of LSB pages. Once the bit error rates exceed the error correction capability of ECC, the LSB pages cannot be exploited, leading to unreliable storage. Therefore, the LDPC codes with stronger error correction capability are considered.
Finally, error percentages of LSB, CSB, and MSB pages are counted. Experiment results in Fig. 6 show that there are unbalanced bit errors in these three pages. When experiencing more P/E cycles, bit error percentages of LSB pages are much higher than that of the CSB and MSB pages. Because there are much higher state shift percentages of S0 to S1, leading to higher bit errors in the LSB pages.

C. Exploiting Error Modes to Optimize LLR Information
More accurate LLR information can significantly reduce the decoding iterations and latency. We exploit the observed error modes to optimize the LLR information. The specific optimization method is described as follows.
Method 1: Decoding results of MSB and CSB are denoted as DEmsb and DEcsb. Initial LLR information of MSB, CSB, and LSB is denoted as LLRmsb, LLRcsb, and LLRlsb, respectively. We define the optimized LSB LLR information as MMllsb. The decoding result DE corresponds to the LLR information when making the decoding decision, which is given by using the formulas (11) and (12) in Algorithm 1. If the decoding result DE > 0, the corresponding bit is made decision as 1, otherwise, 0. The purpose of using DE and initial LLR is to judge the storage state of MSB and CSB for assisting the decoding decision for LSB. If DEmsb < 0 and LLRmsb < 0, LLRcsb < 0, and DEcsb < 0, it means that MSB and CSB are the bit 0, according to Error Mode 1, LSB is correct with the large probability. Thus, it sets LSB LLR information as MMllsb = 2.5 × LLRlsb. If DEmsb < 0 and LLRmsb < 0 and DEcsb × LLRcsb < 0, it means that MSB is the bit 0 and CSB is the fuzzy bit, LSB is correct with the large probability. Therefore, it sets LSB LLR information as MMllsb = 2.5 × LLRlsb to speed up LSB convergence to the correct bit.
Method 2: If DEmsb > 0 and LLRmsb > 0, LLRcsb < 0 and DEcsb < 0, it means that MSB is the bit 1 and CSB is the bit 0. According to Error Mode 2, LSB is correct with the large probability. Thus, it sets LSB LLR information as MMllsb = 2.5×LLRlsb to expedite LSB convergence to the correct bit. If DEmsb > 0 and LLRmsb > 0, DEcsb × LLRcsb < 0, it means that MSB is the bit 1 and CSB is the fuzzy bit, LSB is the bit 0 with the large probability. It sets MMllsb = −2 to speed up the LSB convergence to the correct bit 0. If DEmsb × LLRmsb < 0 and DEcsb × LLRcsb < 0, it means that MSB and CSB are the fuzzy bits. Therefore, LSB is correct with the large probability. It sets LSB LLR information as MMllsb = 2.5 × LLRlsb.
Method 3: If DEmsb × LLRmsb < 0, LLRcsb < 0 and DEcsb < 0, it means that MSB is the fuzzy bit and CSB is the bit 0. According to Error Mode 3, LSB is correct with the large probability. Thus, it sets LSB LLR information as

MMllsb = 2.5 × LLRlsb to accelerate LSB convergence to the correct bit. If DEmsb × LLRmsb < 0, LLRcsb > 0 and DEcsb > 0, it means that MSB is the fuzzy bit and CSB is the bit 1. LSB is correct with the large probability. Thus, it sets LSB LLR information as MMllsb = 2.5 × LLRlsb.
To adjust the LSB LLR values for gaining more accurate
decoding soft information, we conduct multiple simulation
experiments. Finally, we choose the optimal LLR information as MMllsb = 2.5 × LLRlsb explained in Methods 1–3 as well as MMllsb = −2 explained in Method 2, which enables the decoding to converge with high speed. The simulation results
validate their effectiveness of reducing the decoding latency
and improving the decoding performance.

B. Results and Analysis
We count average decoding iterations when implementing LDPC decoding operations. The simulation results in Fig. 10 show that there are unbalanced decoding iterations between LSB, CSB, and MSB pages due to unbalanced bit errors. Decoding iterations in LSB pages are abnormally higher than that of the other two pages, regardless of the page sizes of 2 KB and 4 KB. In the context, 2 KB and 4 KB are the subpage size of 3-D TLC NAND flash with large page capacity and commonly considered as ECC units. The decoding iterations of LDPC codes are directly affected by the RBER (i.e., the bit error rate without the decoding), and higher RBER leads to more decoding iterations. Therefore, more decoding iterations happen in LSB pages with higher RBER. The gap of RBERs between the MSB and CSB pages is small.

D. Discussion
EMAL is able to improve the LDPC decoding performance and reduce the decoding latency at high RBER. It is more suitable for sequential read operations because obtaining the decoding results of MSB and CSB pages is necessary to decode the LSB page. Fortunately, the prior work [28] has shown that performing sequential read operations is beneficial for improving system performance.

VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we propose the EMAL scheme, which exploits error modes of 3-D TLC NAND flash for improving the decoding performance and decreasing the decoding latency. EMAL incorporates the error modes into the LDPC decoding process. Specifically, the decoding results of both the MSB and CSB pages are combined with the error modes to reasonably deduce the correct bit value of LSB pages, leading to optimized LLR information of decoding the LSB page and, hence, speeding up the decoding convergence. The simulation results show that the proposed scheme significantly improves the decoding performance and reduces the decoding latency.
