Spatially Coupled LDPC Codes With Small Constraint Length and Low Error Floor

Sima Naseri and Amir H. Banihashemi , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract— In this letter, we design time-invariant spatially coupled low-density parity-check (SC-LDPC) codes with small constraint length and low error floor. For this, we modify the codes in the literature that have (close to) minimal constraint length for a given degree distribution and girth, to improve their error floor. This is performed by eliminating (or minimizing the multiplicity of) some of the dominant trapping sets (TSs) of the codes and/or increasing the minimum distance. To reduce the multiplicity of the TSs effectively and efficiently, we devise a technique based on the parent/child relationship between TSs that aims at successively minimizing the multiplicity of TSs depending on their harmfulness.
Index Terms— Spatially coupled low-density parity-check (SC-LDPC) codes, constraint length, trapping sets, error floor.
I. INTRODUCTION
S PATIALLY-COUPLED low-density parity-check (SC-LDPC) codes can asymptotically achieve the capacity [1]. In finite-length (practical) scenarios, however, SC-LDPC codes suffer from error floor [2], as a result of some small substructures of the code’s Tanner graph, referred to as trapping sets (TSs) [3].
In practice, SC-LDPC codes are encoded using shift registers and are decoded using a sliding window decoder based on message-passing algorithms [4]. In this case, both the encoding and the decoding complexity increase linearly with syndrome former constraint length. The decoding latency also depends on the constraint length linearly. In [5], and subsequently in [6], thus, the authors focused on the problem of minimizing the constraint length for a given degree distribution and girth. In [5], lower bounds were derived on the constraint length as a function of girth, and codes were designed that achieved/approached the bounds. Although the error floor of the designed codes was a concern in [5] and [6], the authors only addressed it indirectly through the girth. Our examination of the codes designed in [5] and [6] revealed that many of them suffer from rather high error floors due to small TSs or small minimum distance values. In this work, following the same approach as in [5] and [6], we directly design the symbolic matrix of SC-LDPC codes, and in each case maintain the same degree distribution, girth and constraint length as the codes designed in [5], [6]. This guarantees the same advantages, as the codes of [5], [6], in terms of complexity and latency. In addition, by careful design of the exponent matrix, we significantly reduce the error floor. The main distinguishing factor however is that while the goal in [7] is to exhaustively characterize all the (a, b) LETS structures within a rectangular region of a ≤ amax and b ≤ bmax, in this work, we have no such limitation. In addition, we tailor our parent/child characterization of the target LETS structures to lend itself to a successive search/minimization
of the structures in accordance to their harmfulness. Notations dotm, pam and lom c are used for the expansions
dot, path and lollipop, respectively. For dotm, a variable node is connected to the parent structure through m degree-1 check nodes, where m ≥ 2. For pam, an open or closed path of length m + 1 (m ≥ 2) connects two degree-1 check nodes of the parent structure to form the child structure. Finally, in lom c , a lollipop walk of length m + 1 consisting of a path of length m + 1 − c followed by a cycle of length c is attached to the parent structure through a degree-1 check node. When a parent-child relationship exists between two structures S ⊂ S through an expansion E, we can avoid having the structure S in the Tanner graph G if we make sure that all the instances of structure S in G cannot be expanded through E. In this case, we say that the instances of S are non-expandable (nE) with respect to E, or E-nE. As an example, we say a LETS S is dotm-nE, if there is no variable node outside S that is connected to m degree-1 check nodes of S.

For this, we organize the TS classes in a forest (collection of rooted trees), where the classes in L are the top nodes of the trees and the roots are either short simple cycles or structures that can be easily expanded from short simple cycles. Any branch of the tree represents a parent-child relationship between the end nodes of the branch, where the child (parent) is the head (tail) of the branch.
In this work, since our focus is on LETSs and since each LETS structure has at least one cycle, our goal is to characterize each LETS structure of interest as a nested sequence of LETSs which starts from short simple cycles (cycles of length g or at most g + 2) and then is expanded successively through the three expansions dot, path and lollipop to reach the target LETS. With regards to the short cycles, to minimize the search complexity, we always use cycles of length g as the starting point, unless the target LETS structure cannot be generated by successive expansions of the g-cycles, i.e., the girth g of the subgraph of the target LETS is larger than g. In that case, we choose g as the length of the initial cycle structure. For the expansions, since dot has the lowest complexity, followed by path and lollipop, respectively [7], we use them in the same order of priority, i.e., we use as many dotm expansions as possible, and only if an structure cannot be generated only by dot expansions, then we use the other two expansions with the first priority given to pam before any locm is used.

V. CONCLUSION
In this letter, we designed SC-LDPC codes with constraint lengths as small as those in [5], [6], but with considerably lower error floor. The improvement in the error floor was obtained by careful examination of dominant TSs and their successive minimization according to their relative harmfulness.
