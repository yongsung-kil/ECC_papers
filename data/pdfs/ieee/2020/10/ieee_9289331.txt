Analysis of Bit-Flipping Algorithm of Irregular Low-Density Parity-Check Codes

Myungin Kim School of Electrical Engineering
KAIST Daejeon, Korea kmi9212@kaist.ac.kr

Jeongseok Ha School of Electrical Engineering
KAIST Daejeon, Korea jsha@kaist.edu

Abstract—In this work, we analytically derive the evolution of single-bit messages in the bit-flipping algorithm (BFA) for irregular low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes on the binary symmetric channel (BSC). In particular, the analysis tracks the probability distributions of the flipping functions and the bitdecision error at each iteration, which is possible by introducing a state variable at each variable node. The probability distributions are indirectly obtained by taking advantage of the state and state transition probabilities. The analysis will be confirmed by comparing analytic evaluations with simulation results. For the comparison, we implement LDPC codes at a finite length.
Index Terms—LDPC codes, bit-flipping algorithm, irregular degree distribution, state probability, state transition probability
I. INTRODUCTION
LDPC codes, which were introduced by R. Gallager [1], have been known as a class of error-correcting codes with capacity approaching performances [2]. Thus, they have been adopted in a broad range of areas such as wireless communication standards and storage systems. LDPC codes are usually decoded with a message-passing decoder, e.g. the sum-product algorithm (SPA) [3] and the min-sum algorithm (MSA) [4], which is performed by exchanging reliability messages between variable nodes (VNs) and check nodes (CNs). The messages are expressed in various forms, e.g. multi-bit messages representing log-likelihood ratio values, depending on required decoding performance and complexity.
For low-complexity decoding, a few BFAs [5]–[21] have been proposed, and the BFAs drastically reduce their decoder complexity by exchanging single-bit messages. In return, the single-bit messages cannot convey reliability information, which leads to a certain loss of error-rate performance. In [5], the authors proposed a BFA in which the reliability of the bit-decision at a VN is measured by a function called flipping function defined as the number of unsatisfied CNs adjacent to the VN. It was demonstrated in [5] that the introduction of the flipping function effectively compensates for the performance loss due to single-bit messages.
To analyze the dynamics of message-passing decoders during iterations, the density evolution technique was proposed in [22]. The density evolution provides not only evaluations of threshold performances for LDPC code ensembles but also can be utilized for designing LDPC code ensembles [23]. For

the SPA and MSA, the density evolution was developed in [22] and [24], respectively. However, in contrast to the SPA and MSA, the messages in the BFAs are not statistically independent, which makes it especially difficult to develop the density evolution for the BFAs. Motivated by the technical challenge, in [25], we develop a way to model the messages in the BFA by introducing a state variable which consists of the bit decision value and the flipping function value at a VN. In addition, we derive the probability distribution function of the state variable, which in turn provides the probability distribution function for the messages. However, the result in [25] is limited to regular LDPC codes. In this work, we extend the result in [25] to the BFA for irregular LDPC codes. In particular, this work considers the BFA in [5] but the results developed in this work can be generalized to other BFAs.
This paper is organized as follows. In Section II, the BFA in [5] will be introduced. In Section III, a state variable will be defined in the BFA for irregular LDPC codes, and the evolutions of the state and state transition probabilities will be described during iterations. In Section IV, we will empirically evaluate the state probability and bit error rate (BER) which will be compared with the analytic results derived in Section III. The comparisons confirm that the analytic derivations in Section III fairly predict the behaviors of the BFA. Finally, we will conclude this work in Section V.
