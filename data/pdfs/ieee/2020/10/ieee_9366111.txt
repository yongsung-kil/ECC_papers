An Effective Learning Scheme for Weighted-BP with Parallel Permutation Decoding

Ryota Yoshizawa, Kenichiro Furuta, Yuma Yoshinaga, Osamu Torii and Tomoya Kodama
System Technology R&D Center, Institute of Memory Technology R&D, Kioxia Corporation, 2–5–1 Kasama, Sakae-ku, Yokohama, Kanagawa, 247–8585, JAPAN
Email: {ryota2.yoshizawa ¦ kenichiro.furuta ¦ yuma.yoshinaga ¦ osamu.torii ¦ tomoya.kodama}@kioxia.com

Abstract—Weighted-BP is a method to improve the decoding performance of belief-propagation (BP) by learning its appropriate message weights using deep learning technique. For binary primitive BCH codes, it has been reported in literature that their performance can be enhanced more through parallel decoding which is achieved by exploiting the automorphism property of the codes. In this paper, an effective learning scheme for the parallel weighted-BP is proposed. While in the conventional work the weights preliminarily learned for a single weighted-BP were utilized as is even in the scenario of parallel decoding, we show that some gain can be further obtained if we take the parallel structure into account in the learning phase.
I. INTRODUCTION
Bose-Chaudhuri-Hockenghem (BCH) codes [1] are widely used for error correction in practical applications such as satellite systems, quick response (QR) codes, disk drives, and solid-state drives (SSDs). The popularity of BCH codes mainly stems from their relatively simple algebraic structure, enabling fast and low-energy encoding and hard-decision decoding [2– 4] with small circuit size. While e.g., Berlekamp-Massey algorithm [3] and Euclidean algorithm [4] provide bounded distance decoding based on hard-decision bits, when we aim to improve the decoding performance, the use of soft-decision based decoding is required.
Belief-propagation (BP) is a major soft-decision based decoding scheme for low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [5] since it can achieve near Shannon limit performance with relatively low complexity. However, it is also well known that the application of BP to high-density parity-check (HDPC) codes, including BCH codes, results in considerably poor decoding performance since their Tanner graph generally contains many short cycles and thus BP no longer operates as a nice approximation of bit-wise maximum a posteriori probability (MAP) decoding. For short HDPC codes, one may use the direct MAP decoding or ordered statistics decoding (OSD) [6, 7], but their complexity becomes dominant as their codeword length becomes long. Therefore, direct MAP decoding and OSD are not necessarily suitable for the applications of BCH codes which are generally required to operate with low-power consumption.
To improve the BP performance for HDPC codes, Nachmani et al introduced the weighted-BP [8]. In weighted-BP, appropriate message weights for alleviating the performance degradation due to short cycles are learned using deep learning

technique. Since the message-propagation in BP can be equivalently expressed with neural networks (NNs), the weights can be learned through an efficient gradient descent algorithm, i.e., back-propagation [9]. While the weighted-BP proposed in [8] was based on sum-product algorithm, it was extended to the off-set min-sum algorithm (OMSA) and the normalized minsum algorithm (NMSA) [10] in [11] and [12], respectively.
For primitive BCH codes, their automorphism property can be used for improving the BP performance. The automorphism group Aut(C) of a primitive BCH code C is generated by the permutations of its codeword, and the codes in Aut(C) can be decoded with the same decoder as that of C itself. In [13], the random redundant decoding (RRD), which is a decoding scheme that several permutations are inserted to the BP iterations, is introduced. In [14], the modified version of RRD, called mRRD, is proposed, where it utilizes RRD decoders with different permutations in parallel. Indeed, these permutation-based decoding methods can be applied to weighted-BP as well, and such combination is considered in [12].
However, in [12], the weights preliminarily learned with a single weighted-BP was applied as is even to the parallel decoding scenario. In parallel decoding, such as mRRD, the final estimated codeword is selected from the multiple decoded codewords based on some metric that indicates the reliability. In other words, the decoding path corresponding to the most reliable codeword is chosen and the other paths are dismissed. If we utilize the parallel decoding structure in the learning phase as well and focus on learning the weights corresponding to the final selected decoding path, some performance gain might be obtained compared to the conventional case that learns the weights with a single weighted-BP. This aspect is studied in this work.
This paper is organized as follows: In Section II, we detail the NMSA-based weighted-BP considered in this work. A computationally efficient calculation of the values required in feed-forward process in the weighted-BP is also given here. Section III introduces our learning approach specialized for parallel permutation decoding and its effect is investigated in Section IV in comparison with the conventional learning method. Section V finally summarizes this work.

A. Automorphism Group Let us define an index permutation

B. Parallel Permutation Decoding
The codes in Aut(C) can be decoded with the same decoder as that of C. This means that, even though the decoding is failed for an input LLR L, its permuted version has a chance to be successfully decoded. Therefore, parallel decoding for several permuted LLRs based on (8) can improve the decoding performance. In this work, we consider the parallel decoding structure utilizing M -weighted-BPs where each of their LLR inputs are differently permuted, and the best estimated codeword is finally selected based on a metric which indicates the reliability of the estimation. C. Learning Weights for Parallel Permutation Decoding
In [12], the parallel weighted-BP whose weights are preliminarily learned with a single decoder is investigated. However, the optimum weights for a single decoder do not necessarily match those for the most reliable path selected in parallel decoding. Since the decoding paths other than the most reliable one are dismissed, we actually do not have to care about their weights. Therefore, focusing on optimizing the weights for the most reliable path may lead to decoding performance improvement.
To achieve this, we utilize the parallel decoding structure in the learning phase. In our learning method, we conduct feedforward propagation at all of the decoders and detect the most reliable path based on the selection metric (9). Subsequently, we conduct back-propagation and update gradients using only the selected path. We assume in this work that all weightedBPs in the parallel decoder share the weights as with the conventional work [12].
Figure 5 illustrates the path selection flow in our learning method for an example parallel weighted-BP with M = 4. According to the figure, the second path from the left, which has the least metric value of 0.5, is chosen as the most reliable path. V. CONCLUSION
This paper proposes an effective learning scheme for the parallel permutation decoding with weighted-BP. Through computer simulation, it was shown that the parallel weightedBP using the weights learned by our approach is able to improve the BER performance of the conventional learning method where the weights optimized with a single decoder is used. Moreover, it was observed that our method becomes more effective as the number of parallel paths is larger.
While our work assumes that the weights are shared by the weighted-BP components in the permutation decoder, further performance gain might be obtained in the scenario that each weighted-BP has different weights and the channel is not uniform over the codeword. Moreover, we confirmed previously that the training data filtering with MAP decoding improves the performance for the single weighted-BP decoder, and its application to the technique provided in this paper can be considered as an extension. These topics are left for our future work.
