An Optimized VLSI Implementation of an IEEE 802.11n/ac/ax LDPC Decoder
Saleh Usman and Mohammad M. Mansour Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering
American University of Beirut {sau00, mmansour}@aub.edu.lb

Abstract—This paper proposes optimization techniques for multi-Gbps low-power VLSI implementation of IEEE 802.11n/ac/ax (WiFi) LDPC decoders. The IEEE 802.11n/ac/ax standard features Quasi-Cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes with modular decoder structure composed of arrays of memory blocks, barrel shifter networks, adders, and check-node units (CNUs). To achieve multi-Gbps throughput performance and high energy-efficiency while maintaining a small decoder footprint, careful implementation of these modules along with effective fixed-point analysis is required. This paper proposes techniques for optimized implementation and proper bit-width selection of these modules. These techniques are then employed to design a fully-pipelined IEEE 802.11n/ac/ax standard compliant LDPC decoder. The design is synthesized in a 40 nm standard CMOS process. The synthesized decoder occupies an area of 0.71 mm2, runs at a frequency of 562 MHz, attains a peak throughput of 11.4 Gbps, and achieves an energy-efficiency of 12.5 pJ/bit. The presented decoder outperforms the bestreported decoders in the literature in terms of throughput/area and energy-efficiency, for IEEE 802.11n/ac/ax LDPC codes.
Index Terms—LDPC Codes, layered decoding of LDPC codes, Gallager’s algorithm, IEEE 802.11ac/ax
I. INTRODUCTION LDPC codes featured in IEEE 802.11n/ac/ax (WiFi) [1] standard are QC-LDPC codes [2]. For high-throughput applications, these codes are decoded iteratively using the turbo (layered) decoding schedule [3], [4] to reduce the number of iterations compared with Gallager’s original flooding schedule [5]. Several layered decoder architectures have emerged in the literature, targeting QC-LDPC codes compliant with various standards [6], [7]. VLSI implementation of QC-LDPC decoders involves implementing memory modules, barrel shifter networks, adders/subtractors, and CNUs. An energy-efficient multi-Gbps VLSI implementation of the decoder architecture with a small area-footprint requires careful optimizations of all these modules. For IEEE 802.11 ac/ax LDPC codes, three frame lengths and corresponding sub-block sizes Z are defined. The subblock sizes of long frames are integer multiples of the smaller ones. For high-throughput decoding, it is desirable to process one layer of size Z in a single clock cycle, which requires processing Z number of messages in one cycle, and the size of barrel shifters scales accordingly. However, this increases the decoder area, and the design becomes under-utilized for smaller frame lengths. Processing of one layer per cycle also requires that the CNU be able to process the layer that has the maximum node degree, in a single clock cycle. The maximum

degree is usually close, but not exactly equal, to the number of columns of the Sparse-Parity-Check-Matrix (SPCM), and therefore, the CNUs are generally designed to support the processing of a number of inputs that equals the number of columns of the SPCM, in one cycle; this points towards an opportunity for optimization. In [8], for example, CNUs with a number of inputs equal to 24 are implemented for IEEE 802.11n/ac LDPC codes, but the maximum degree of any layer is no more than 22 .
Furthermore, high signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) accelerates the growth of log-likelihood (LLR) values, and proper selection of quantization-bits for the LLR values becomes critical as it affects the decoding performance along with the overall complexity of the decoder architecture. Several works have emerged in the literature to address the fast-growth. For example, normalization of LLR values by a factor, after each decoding iteration, is proposed in [9], [10], but this requires additional processing elements, in the decoder architecture. In [11], received soft values are directly used instead of the LLR values; however, this approach changes the dynamicrange of the LLR values, with changing channel-noise variance. Proper selection of the quantization-bits also allows to replace at least one set of saturated subtractors of the decoder, with unsaturated ones, and thus avoids corresponding costly operations of comparison and clipping.
Based on the above observations, this paper proposes an efficient memory-organization and barrel-shifters design to rotate the longer frames, in multiple cycles, by using the barrel shifter of the smallest frame. Multiplexers are then designed to forward a number of inputs equal to the maximum row-degree, instead of the number of columns of the SPCM. Finally, a simple modification in the layered decoding algorithm is proposed to reduce the dynamic range of the LLR values, which allowed a reduction in bit-width of several modules of the decoder along with replacement of 22 saturated subtractors, involved in one stage of the decoder, with non-saturated ones. The proposed optimizations are then employed to implement a fully pipelined IEEE 802.11n/ac/ax LDPC decoder, and the decoder is synthesized in a 40 nm standard CMOS process. The synthesized decoder attains a throughput of 3.8-11.4 Gbps, occupies an area of 0.71 mm2 while achieving 12.5 pJ/bit of energy-efficiency.

C. QC-LDPC Codes for IEEE 802.11n/ac/ax Standard QC-LDPC codes have been adopted in recent commu-
nications standards like 5G New-Radio [15], [16], IEEE 802.11n/ac/ax (WiFi) [1], and IEEE 802.11ad (WiGig) [17], among others. The SPCM of a QC-LDPC code is represented by a base-matrix and a sub-block size parameter Z. For IEEE 802.11n/ax/ax LDPC codes, 12 base-matrices are defined; corresponding to 3 frame-lengths, 648, 1296, 1944, and 4 coderates, 1/2, 2/3, 3/4, 5/6. Sub-block sizes of 27, 54, and 84 are defined corresponding to frame-lengths of 648, 1296, and 1944, respectively. The base-matrix for rate R = 5/6 and frame length, FL = 648 is shown in Fig. 1. The integers in the base-matrix represent the circular shift of the Z × Z identity matrix, while dashes (-) correspond to the Z × Z all-zeros matrices. The actual SPCM of the LDPC code is formed by replacing each entry in the base-matrix with a shifted Z × Z identity matrix or an all-zeros matrix.

B. Multiplexers for Reduced Number of Inputs The memory locations of the decoder’s LLRMemory directly

correspond to the columns of the base-matrix of a QC-LDPC code. To read messages from the LLRMemory, usage of small

multiplexers is proposed to reduce the number of output lines

to the rest of the modules to “maximum-row-degree”, instead of the number of columns of the base-matrix. For the IEEE 802.11n/ac/ax case, the maximum row-degree is 22 while the number of columns is 24 , so the multiplexers are used to select the 22 messages from 24 memory locations. From Fig. 1, it could be observed that the messages corresponding to the first 12 columns can be directly passed from memory, while the messages corresponding to the rest of the 12 columns be passed via multiplexers on 10 output lines, as shown in Fig. 5. This reduces the required number of adders, subtractors, and BarrelShifters/Rev.BarrelShifters of Fig. 2, from 24 to 22 , as well as simplifies the CNUs to process 22 inputs rather than 24 . Select-lines of the multiplexers are given different names, m0,m1,m2,m3, and are asserted/de-asserted, based on the connections which are derived from the base-matrix.

V. CONCLUSION

Optimization techniques for efficient VLSI implementation of high-throughput IEEE 802.11n/ac/ax LDPC decoders have been presented. These optimizations target decoder area and power reduction while still maintaining Gbps processing throughput. The proposed optimizations are applied to design a fully-pipelined IEEE 802.11n/ac/ax LDPC decoder, which is synthesized using 40 nm CMOS technology. The decoder attains a peak throughput of 11.4 Gbps while occupying an area of 0.71 mm2. The decoder achieves a troughput/area efficiency of 13.4 Gbps/mm2 while consuming 12.5 pJ/bit of energy, and thus surpasses the best-reported corresponding efficiencies of 11.4 Gbps/mm2 and 15.8 pJ/bit, respectively, for IEEE 802.11n/ac/ax LDPC decoders.
