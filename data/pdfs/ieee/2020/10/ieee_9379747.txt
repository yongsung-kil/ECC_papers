On the Non-concentrated Check-node Degree Distributions in QC-IRA Raptor-like LDPC Codes
Kang Xu, Na Gao, Yin Xu, Dazhi He, Yang Cai, Boyuan Xu, Wenjun Zhang Cooperative Media Network Innovation Center (CMIC), School of Electronics Information and Electrical Engineering,
Shanghai Jiao Tong University, China Academy of Broadcasting Science, NRTA, China

Abstract—The quasi-cyclic irregular-repeat-accumulate (QCIRA) raptor-like LDPC codes in ATSC 3.0 have concentrated check-node degree distributions in the core matrix and extended matrix as well. Actually, the low-rate raptor-like LDPC codes with non-concentrated check-node degree distributions show a promising performance improvement. In this paper, the benefits of non-concentrated check-node degree distributions are first analyzed. After that, a genetic-like algorithm is proposed to search good pairs of non-concentrated check and variable-node degree distributions. Simulation results show that the proposed low-rate LDPC codes with QC-IRA raptor-like structure have at most 0.22 dB gain over those in ATSC 3.0 when bit-error rate (BER) is at 10−4 level over additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels using QPSK modulation.
Index Terms—Low-density parity-check code, QC-IRA raptorlike, Gaussian approximation density evolution, ATSC 3.0.
I. INTRODUCTION
As an excellent forward error correction code, low-density parity-check code (LDPC) has been studied due to its superior performance in capacity approaching, and has already been widely deployed in broadcast communication standards: Digital Video Broadcasting (DVB) series standards [1] , Advanced Television Systems Committee 3.0 (ATSC 3.0) standard [2] , etc. With the continuous evolution of communication standards [3] , the study on the design and performance analysis of LDPC code is still on the way.
In the design process of LDPC codes, degree distribution is a very important factor. The concept was first proposed by Luby et al. in [4]. After that, many papers also proposed several LDPC codes with good performance [5] . All of those findings suggest that LDPC codes with irregular degree distributions have much better waterfall performance than those with regular degree distributions originally proposed by Gallager.

III. OPTIMIZATION SCHEME
In [6], it is expounded that for parity check matrices whose minimum variable node degree is 2, the error probability will decrease by a factor at each decoding iteration when the error probability is small. Actually concentrated check-node degree distributions tend to maximize the factor under the fixed variable-node degree distribution. However, the percent of degree-2 variable nodes is inversely proportional to the factor, which means the percent of degree-2 variable nodes should not be too large.
Meanwhile, according to the analysis of (3), we find that with non-concentrated variable-node degree distributions, the smallest fixed point m∗ (let m(inl)ner = m∗ then m(inl+n1e)r = m∗), where the LLR iteration stops, tends to be relatively large. While concentrated check-node degree distributions help to lower the SNR threshold with non-concentrated variable-node degree distributions.

The arguments above imply that the combinations of concentrated check-node degree distributions and nonconcentrated variable-node degree distributions are good choices for the degree distributions of a high-rate LDPC code. Naturally, the QC-IRA codes of medium and high rates in ATSC 3.0 just behave in that manner.
However, for QC-IRA raptor-like LDPC codes, the analysis above is not suitable. The extended matrices of the parity check matrices of low-rate LDPC codes in ATSC 3.0 have both concentrated check-node degree distributions and concentrated variable-node degree distributions (except the degree1 variable nodes). According to the analysis of (4), there tends to be a relative small fixed point caused by the concentrated variable-node degree distributions, which mismatch the concentrated check-node degree distributions. To mitigate the mismatch, we propose a algorithm to search for better degree distributions as shown below.

This heuristic genetic-like algorithm limits the number of ’population’ to 1, which means only the best candidate is reserved in each iteration. Actually, we can scale the number of ’population’ up to more than 1 in practice. In the algorithm, we first initialize the degree vectors C and V , then their ’descendants’ (neighbors) are generated, where the best performer is reserved for the next ’population evolution’, and once the survival is considered good enough (no descendants outperform the parent), then we finally finish the algorithm and return the result. In each iteration, based on the SNR threshold of the parent, we choose the one who maximize the value at the minimum point t∗ of the convergence gain function (step 5). Meanwhile, in this algorithm, the improvement of SNR threshold in each iteration is guaranteed (step 7).
The generation of new degrees (the subfunction neighbor) is described in Algorithm 2, where the subfunction length returns the dimension of the input vector and the subfunction sort returns a descending sorted array from the input array. The degree vectors newly generated are very similar to the original: either add or remove (step 3 to 5), or add and then remove (step 7 to 11) an edge from the original Tanner graph. So there are both inheritance and variability in the method to generate ’descendants’.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we first analyze the applicable situations of the non-concentrated check-node degree distributions. To further design better codes, a new genetic-like algorithm for efficient search of degree distribution in practice is also proposed. Finally, our analysis is validated by redesigning the codes at several low rates in ATSC 3.0.
In our analysis, for raptor-like structure, non-concentrated check-node degree distributions may perform better than usual concentrated check-node degree distributions. So we propose a joint search algorithm of variable-node degree distributions and check-node degree distributions. Using this algorithm, we redesign the codes at rate 3/15, 4/15 and 5/15 in ATSC 3.0. From the simulation results, a improvement of BER is acquired
