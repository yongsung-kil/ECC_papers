In this work, we investigate the relationship between the two approaches of Theorems 1 and 2. In particular, our goal is to find the relationship between the two spectra {ő∑i} and {őĽi} for bi-regular bipartite graphs. This allows one to compute Nk using Theorem 1, but through the calculation of the graph spectrum {őĽi} rather than the direct calculation of {ő∑i}. II. DEFINITIONS AND NOTATIONS
A graph G = (V, E) is a set V (G) of nodes and a multiset E(G) of unordered pairs of nodes, called edges. If {v, u} ‚ąą E, we say that there is an edge between v and u (i.e., v and u are adjacent). We may also use notations uv or vu for the edge {v, u}. We say that a graph G is simple, if it does not have any loop (i.e., no edge of the form {v, v}) or parallel edges (i.e., no two edges between the same two nodes). A directed graph (digraph) D = (V, E) is a set V of nodes and a multiset E of ordered pairs of nodes called arcs. For an arc e = (u, w), we define the origin of e to be o(e) = u, and the terminus of e to be t(e) = w. The inverse arc of e, denoted by e, is the arc formed by switching the origin and terminus of e. A digraph D is called symmetric if whenever (u, w) is an arc of D, its inverse arc (w, u) is as well. For each graph G, its symmetric digraph D(G) is defined by replacing each edge of G with two arcs in opposite directions. See Fig. 1. Thus, there is a simple correspondence between G and D(G).

In a graph G, the number of edges incident to a node v is called the degree of v, and is denoted by d(v). Also, őĒ(G) and őī(G) are used to denote the maximum and minimum degree of G. For every node v ‚ąą V (G), the set N (v) denotes the set of neighbors of v in G.
For a graph G, a walk of length c is a sequence of nodes v1, v2, . . . , vc+1 in V such that {vi, vi+1} ‚ąą E, for all i ‚ąą {1, . . . , c}. A walk can alternatively be represented by its sequence of edges. A walk v1, v2, . . . , vk+1 is a path if all the nodes v1, v2, . . . , vk are distinct. A walk is called a closed walk if the two end nodes are the same, i.e., if v1 = vk+1. Under the same condition, a path is called a cycle. We denote cycles of length k, also referred to as k-cycles, by Ck. We use Nk for |Ck|. The length of the shortest cycle(s) in a graph is called girth and is denoted by g.
Consider a walk W of length k represented by the sequence of edges ei1 , ei2, . . . , eik . The walk W is backtrackless if eis = eis+1, for any s ‚ąą {1, . . . , k ‚ąí 1}. Also, the walk W is tailless if ei1 = eik . In this paper, we use the term TBC walk to refer to a tailless backtrackless closed walk.
A graph G is connected, if there is a path between any two nodes of G. A graph G = (V, E) is called bipartite, if the node set V can be partitioned into two disjoint subsets U and W , i.e., V = U ‚ą™ W and U ‚ą© W = ‚ąÖ, such that every edge in E connects a node from U to a node from W . A graph is bipartite if and only if the lengths of all its cycles are even. Tanner graphs of LDPC codes are bipartite graphs, in which U and W are referred to as variable nodes and check nodes, respectively. Parameters n and m in this case are used to denote |U | and |W |, respectively. Parameter n is the code‚Äôs block length and the code rate R satisfies R ‚Č• 1 ‚ąí (m/n).
A bipartite graph G = (U ‚ą™ W, E) is called bi-regular, if all the nodes on the same side of the bipartition have the same degree, i.e., if all the nodes in U have the same degree du and all the nodes in W have the same degree dw. In the rest of the paper, we sometimes use notations dv and dc as a replacement for du and dw, respectively, to follow the notations commonly used in coding to denote variable and check node degrees, respectively. It is clear that, for a bi-regular graph, |U |du = |W |dw = |E(G)|. A bipartite graph that is not bi-regular is called irregular. A bipartite graph G(U ‚ą™ W, E) is called complete, and is denoted by K|U|,|W |, if every node in U is connected to every node in W . The degree sequences of a bipartite graph G are defined as the two monotonic non-increasing sequences of the node degrees on the two sides of the graph. For instance, the complete bipartite graph K3,4 has degree sequences (4, 4, 4) and (3, 3, 3, 3).

IV. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we investigated the relationship between the spectra of the adjacency matrix A and the directed edge matrix Ae of a bi-regular bipartite graph. We proved that the latter spectrum can be derived from the former through simple quadratic equations. Through this relationship, we established a connection between two existing computational methods for counting short cycles (of length less than or equal to 2g ‚ąí 2, where g is the girth of the graph) in bi-regular bipartite graphs. The first method performs such computations using the spectrum of Ae and, for a fixed cycle length, has complexity O(|E|3), where |E| is the number of edges in the graph (see, Theorem 1). The second method uses the graph spectrum and degree sequences of the graph for computations, and, for a fixed cycle length, has complexity O(|V |3), where |V | is the number of nodes in the graph (see, Theorem 2). The latter complexity can be significantly lower than the former for graphs with large node degrees. The downside of the latter approach, however, is that the equations involved in the computations are very complex and tedious, especially for the calculation of multiplicity of k-cycles, Nk, with k ‚Č• g + 6. In particular, we note that the extra computational complexity required for the calculation of Nk (in addition to the computation of the graph spectrum) in Theorem 2 increases exponentially with k.
Using the results of this work, one can compute the multiplicity of short cycles in a bi-regular bipartite graph using the first approach but with complexity O(|V |3) (and without any need for the tedious and complex equations of the second approach). In fact, the extra computations of the proposed approach, in addition to the calculation of the graph spectrum, are only to solve some constant number (in terms of the cycle length) of quadratic equations. Compared to the approach of Theorem 2, the proposed approach is advantageous in counting long cycles (say, with length k ‚Č• g + 6), or short cycles (with length k < g + 6) in cases where g or the node degrees are large.

From 1991 to 1994, he was with the Electrical Engineering Research Center, Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, Isfahan University of Technology. In 1997, he joined the Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, University of Toronto, Toronto, where he was a Post-Doctoral Fellow of the Natural Sciences and Engineering Research Council of Canada. He joined the Faculty of Engineering, Carleton University, in 1998, where he is currently a Professor with the Department of Systems and Computer Engineering. His research interests include coding and information theory, digital and wireless communications, network coding, theory and implementation of communication algorithms, and compressed sensing and sampling. He was a recipient of the NSERC Discovery Accelerator Supplement in 2008 and 2013. He served as the Co-Chair of the Communication Theory Symposium of Globecom07, the TPC Co-Chair of the Information Theory Workshop 2007, a member of the organizing committee for the International Symposium on Information Theory 2008, and the Co-Chair of the Canadian Workshop on Information Theory 2009.
