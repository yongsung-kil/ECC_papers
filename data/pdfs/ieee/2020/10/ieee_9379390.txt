5G New Radio LDPC Extensions for Multimedia Broadcast Multicast Service

Abstract—The 3rd Generation Partnership Project (3GPP) introduced the Further evolved Multimedia Broadcast Multicast Service (FeMBMS) transmission mode as an extension of the Long-Term Evolution (LTE) standard in 3GPP Release 14. FeMBMS enables the broadcast of services on a dedicated carrier and is currently being tested in numerous field trials worldwide.
The New Radio (NR) air interface, standardized in 3GPP Release 15, introduced Low-Density Parity Check (LDPC) codes as a new forward error correction scheme, but does not yet support broadcast transmissions. The 5G NR standard is designed for unicast transmissions and only supports short codeword lengths.
In contrast, the digital terrestrial television broadcast standards DVB-T2 and ATSC 3.0 both utilize LDPC codes with long codeword lengths as these have proven to increase the error correction performance of transmission systems.
Similarly to FeMBMS, broadcast services based on 5G NR may be introduced in a future 3GPP release and would benefit from longer LDPC codeword lengths.
The introduction of new codeword lengths into the 5G NR standard requires careful consideration of the existing coding procedures. After detailing the 5G NR LDPC coding chain, this paper presents a set of minimal extensions to the 5G NR standard required in order to realize longer LDPC codes. These extensions are evaluated and the achievable performance is compared to that of the LDPC codes standardized in the ATSC 3.0 specification.
Index Terms—5G New Radio, LDPC, MBMS, FeMBMS, NRMBMS, Mobile-Broadcast Convergence, Terrestrial Delivery.
I. INTRODUCTION
The continuous increase of worldwide mobile data traffic is threatening to stress the existing cellular networks. Ericsson identified video services as the main cause for this increase, generating 76 % of the total mobile data traffic worldwide by 2025, corresponding to 121 ExaByte per month [1]. According to Cisco, video usage tends to occur especially during evening hours and has a prime time [2].
In 2017, 3rd Generation Partnership Project (3GPP) Release 14 introduced the Further evolved Multimedia Broadcast Multicast Service (FeMBMS) transmission mode as an extension of the Long-Term Evolution (LTE) standard. The FeMBMS dedicated mode allows for provisioning of Multimedia Broadcast Multicast Services (MBMS) on a dedicated carrier that can be broadcasted via a High Tower, High Power (HTHP) infrastructure. The FeMBMS signal can be decoded by multiple devices simultaneously, optionally even without requiring a cellular network subscription.

The New Radio (NR) air interface, initially standardized in 3GPP Release 15, currently only supports user-specific unicast transmissions. While LTE relies on Turbo codes for the Forward Error Correction (FEC) of traffic channels, 5G NR adopted Low-Density Parity Check (LDPC) codes for this purpose. [3]
LDPC codes were previously used for the 2nd generation of Digital Video Broadcasting standards (DVB) starting in 2003 [4] [5] and have since proven their performance for mobile broadcast applications. The Advanced Television Systems Committee (ATSC) adopted LDPC codes in the ATSC 3.0 standard in 2016 [6].
Long codewords increase the spectral efficiency and the throughput performance of broadcast transmission systems [7]. DVB-T2 and ATSC 3.0 both support LDPC codeword lengths of up to 64800 bits, whereas the maximum codeword length in 5G NR is only 25344 bits. A possible broadcast extension to the 5G NR standard in a future 3GPP release is expected to benefit from increasing the set of available LDPC codeword lengths as already suggested in [7].
This paper presents and analyzes extensions to the 5G NR standard required in order to realize longer LDPC codewords. These extensions not only facilitate the transmission of longer codewords on the Physical Downlink Shared Channel (PDSCH), but can also be adopted for the definition of a 5G NR based Physical Multicast Channel (PMCH) in an upcoming 3GPP release.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Relevant coding procedures of the 5G NR downlink transport channel coding are detailed in Section II. Section III describes extensions to these procedures in order to realize longer LDPC codeword lengths. The simulation environments used to analyze the performance of the extensions are presented in Section IV. Simulation results are evaluated in Section V, and Section VI summarizes the contents of the paper.

C. LDPC Encoding

In order to compute the LDPC parity check matrix H, e.g. of size 46Zc × 68Zc for base graph 1, the following steps are performed:
1) Subject to the chosen base graph and the set associated with the selected lifting size Zc as shown in Table II, the corresponding predefined shift coefficient matrix Pset is selected. Each Pset consists of entries −1 ≤ Pset,ij < Zsmetax, where Zsmetax is the maximum lifting size in the respective set.
2) For each entry Pset,ij with value −1, Pset,ij is replaced by a Zc × Zc zero matrix.
3) All other entries are replaced by a Zc×Zc identity matrix, cyclically shifted to the right by Pset,ij mod Zc columns.
The systematic LDPC codeword C can then be obtained by solving the equation H · CT = 0. The encoding of 5G NR
LDPC codes can be simplified due to the specific properties
of the parity check matrix as described in [11]. The first 2Zc columns of the parity check ma-
trix are always punctured, resulting in an LDPC codeword of size NCW,BG1 = 66Zc bits for base graph 1 and NCW,BG2 = 50Zc bits for base graph 2. The longest achievable LDPC codeword length is

NCmWax = 66 · Zmax = 66 · 384 bits = 25344 bits. (5)

The resulting base code rate of the LDPC code Rbase = KCB/NCW is in line with Table I. [9]

D. Rate Matching
The LDPC codewords of lengths NCW need to be shortened and further punctured to match the predefined code rate R. The 5G NR LDPC shift coefficient matrices are specifically designed to still provide a good overall performance after rate adaptation is applied [12]. This distinguishes the 5G NR LDPC codes from previous designs, e.g. for ATSC 3.0, whose performance deteriorates significantly due to puncturing [13].
Shortening is performed by removing the KCB − M filler bits inserted during code block segmentation as described in Section II-B.
For further puncturing, the output sequence length of each LDPC codeword in a transport block E is computed and the codeword is stored in a circular buffer. The 5G NR PDSCH utilizes Limited-Buffer Rate Matching (LBRM), where the size of the circular buffer is limited for large transport blocks, setting a lower bound of the code rate. [9]
For Hybrid Automatic Repeat Request (HARQ), 5G NR supports incremental redundancy by defining four different starting points for codeword segments in the circular buffer. An output sequence of E bits is then read from the circular buffer, beginning at the defined starting point. [9]
5G NR utilizes row-column interleaving, with row size depending on the preconfigured modulation order. Additional interleaving of OFDM REs is not in the scope of this paper.
All B rate-matched codewords of a transport block are concatenated and scrambled, before Quadrature Amplitude Modulation (QAM) mapping is applied.
Decoding procedures are not described in the standard, but can be separated into the same steps as described above and as shown in Figure 1.
III. EXTENSIONS FOR MBMS
This section details extensions to the 3GPP Release 15 standard needed for longer 5G NR downlink transport channel LDPC codes. As these extensions are designed with the idea of minimizing the required modifications with respect to 3GPP Release 15, the set of base graphs and the set of shift coefficient matrices are not extended. Only base graph 1 is taken into account in the following considerations, as it targets larger block lengths and code rates in the same range as DVB-T2 and ATSC 3.0. Base graph 2 is designed for short codeword lengths and should not be extended for this purpose.
The lifting size set is extended by the values highlighted in Table II. Consistent with Equation (1), the maximal code block size for base graph 1 is increased to
KCmBa,xBG1 = Kb,BG1 · Zmax = 22 · 1920 bits = 42240 bits. (6)
Lifting sizes up to 896 were discussed within 3GPP during standardization, even though they were not included in the final specification [14]. Extensions to the relevant computational steps are described in the following.

and memory requirements in the transmitter and receiver are increased by up to the factor ζ = Zmax/Zmax = 5.
D. Rate Matching
In general, the rate matching process remains the same, but the length of the circular buffer needs to be increased by the factor ζ. With the utilization of LBRM techniques, a smaller buffer size can be employed. As memory requirements are still low with respect to memory in x86-based deployments, LBRM is not considered in this paper.
The starting point on the circular buffer is always the beginning of the systematic bits, because HARQ cannot be used in broadcast transmissions not supported by an uplink.
IV. SIMULATION ENVIRONMENT
The 5G NR LDPC encoding and decoding chain was realized using the IfN Generic Software Defined Radio (SDR) Toolkit (IGST), a C++ framework for implementing and testing transmission standards developed at Technische Universita¨t Braunschweig [15]. An earlier version of this toolkit already served as the basis for the world’s first DVB-C2 and mobile DVB-T2 measurement receivers, respectively [16]. Since then, the toolkit has been redesigned in order to realize the world’s first dedicated FeMBMS measurement receiver [17].
The separate processing blocks depicted in Figure 1 were implemented in accordance with the 5G NR standard and as

detailed in Section II. The LDPC encoder was implemented following the optimized design presented in [11].
Extensions introduced in Section III can be activated optionally, allowing for a direct performance comparison between the conventional 5G NR and the modified version.
In order to find a benchmark against which both versions can be evaluated, the LDPC codes utilized in ATSC 3.0 are used. Relevant steps of the ATSC 3.0 encoding and decoding chain, similar to Figure 1, were implemented by extending the AFF3CT toolbox [18].
The utilized LDPC decoders are based on the offset minsum decoding algorithm [19] with horizontal-layered scheduling, which has proven to be well suited for x86 based SDR implementations [20]. Large lifting sizes enable an even higher degree of parallelism in the decoder, as typical hardware implementations use Zmax parallel processors [12]. In x86 based implementations, a higher decoding throughput can be achieved by applying Single Instruction, Multiple Data (SIMD) accelerations [21].
V. SIMULATION RESULTS
The evaluations in this section show the performance gain obtained by the proposed extensions. First, the achievable spectral efficiencies of the conventional 5G NR LDPC codes, the extended 5G NR LDPC codes, and the ATSC 3.0 LDPC codes are compared to each other. Then, the performance gain for the complete 5G NR downlink transport channel coding is evaluated by comparing the obtained Bit Error Rates (BER).
A. Spectral Efficiency of Conventional and Extended 5G NR LDPC Codes Compared to ATSC 3.0 LDPC Codes
For a direct comparison of the LDPC code performance, the coding of 5G NR and ATSC 3.0 was simplified to only include the key computational steps as shown in Figure 1.
For ATSC 3.0, only the bit interleaved and coded modulation stage without an outer code was implemented. The longest ATSC 3.0 LDPC codeword length of NCW,ATSC3.0 = 64800 bits was chosen and a set of code rates R with their respective associated parity check matrices as specified in the ATSC 3.0 standard was simulated.
For the simulation of the 5G NR LDPC codes and the proposed extensions, the information bit size was chosen in compliance with equations (1) and (6), respectively. Thus, code block segmentation could be omitted. Rate matching was applied as described in Section II-D, matching the 5G NR codewords to the code rates specified by ATSC 3.0. Simulations were repeated for all modulation options supported in both 5G NR and ATSC 3.0.
The BER for all depicted systems and code rates was simulated with 0.1dB energy per symbol to noise ratio (Es/N0) steps, until a Quasi Error Free (QEF) BERQEF < 10−7 was achieved. For these data points, the spectral efficiency S = R · Qm was calculated.

For the same spectral efficiency, the ATSC 3.0 LDPC codes provide QEF reception with an on average 0.48 dB lower Es/N0 compared to the conventional 5G NR LDPC codes. The results for QPSK modulation are comparable to those presented in [13], where the 5G NR LDPC codes are ratematched to the shorter ATSC 3.0 LDPC codeword length.
B. Bit Error Rate of Complete Downlink Transport Channel with Conventional and Extended 5G NR LDPC Coding
For the evaluation of the performance gain created by the proposed extensions in the context of all 5G NR downlink transport channel coding processing steps, the BER of the system according to Figure 1 with and without the extensions proposed in Section III was analyzed.
For these simulations, the NRE in Equation (2) was chosen corresponding to all available resources of a 10 MHz channel. Due to the magnitude of NRE, the largest available lifting size was chosen by the code block segmentation and a high number of code blocks was used for each transport block during all simulations. Although the extended Zmax enables the usage of longer LDPC code words, it can also lead to a higher degree of shortening and puncturing. The resulting BER curves for MCS index 3 (QPSK, R ≈ 0.438) to MCS index 27 (256-QAM, R ≈ 0.926) are plotted over the energy per bit to noise ratio (Eb/N0) in Figure 3.
Simulation results show that with the proposed extensions one obtains more rapidly decreasing curves of the BER. Consequently, the BER for QEF reception is on average 0.3 dB lower. The gain is especially prominent for MCS indices 9-19, which are the most relevant ones for terrestrial broadcasting.

These simulation results prove the gain of the proposed minimal extensions to the 5G NR transport channel coding. A future 5G NR PMCH could be designed based on the existing PDSCH procedures and would benefit from these extensions, enabling an LDPC code performance closer to ATSC 3.0.
VI. CONCLUSION AND OUTLOOK
This paper presents extensions to the downlink transport channel coding of the current 5G NR standard, allowing for the utilization of larger LDPC codeword lengths. These extensions are designed with a minimum change of the standard in mind and target a possible introduction of a 5G NR broadcast transmission mode in a future 3GPP release.
Simulation results obtained with the conventional and extended 5G NR LDPC codes were compared to results obtained with the longest available LDPC codes specified by the ATSC 3.0 standard in terms of the achieved spectral efficiency. Resulting from the proposed extensions, the performance gap between ATSC 3.0 LDPC codes and 5G NR LDPC codes could be closed by more than half.
In addition to that, the performance gain of the proposed extensions was evaluated in context of the complete downlink transport channel coding procedure, where the extensions provided considerable improvements in terms of BER.
Future research could investigate the influence of LBRM on large codewords. Optimizing the predefined LDPC shift coefficient matrices for larger lifting sizes as described in Section III-C would introduce more substantial extensions to the existing standard, but might further improve the performance. Further research could evaluate the suitability of longer CRC sizes, e.g. of CRC32 used in DVB-T2 and ATSC3.0.
