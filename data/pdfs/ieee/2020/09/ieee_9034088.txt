Min-Sum Algorithm Using Multi-Edge-Type Normalized Scheme for ATSC 3.0 LDPC Decoders
Na Gao , Yin Xu , Dazhi He , Sung-Ik Park , Senior Member, IEEE, Hanjiang Hong , Hao Ju , Changwen Chen , Fellow, IEEE, and Wenjun Zhang , Fellow, IEEE

Abstract—To offer commercial LDPC decoders with better performance, in this paper, multi-edge-type (MET) normalized scheme is proposed to improve the performance of traditional min-sum algorithm (MSA) and its modified versions. With respect to sum-product algorithm (SPA), we firstly analyze and find out that the degradations of convergence in different edge types are distinct in MSA theoretically. To compensate the different degradations above, proposed MET-normalized scheme is used in normalized min-sum algorithm (MET-NMSA). In addition, MET-based density evolution (MET-DE) is also presented to search optimized MET-scaling factors for MET-NMSA. To further verify the validity of MET-normalized scheme, Advanced Television Systems Committee (ATSC) 3.0 LDPC codes are used to evaluate MET-NMSA in comparison with the conventional algorithms, i.e., normalized min-sum algorithm (NMSA), offset min-sum algorithm (OMSA) and variable-scaling normalized min-sum algorithm (VS-NMSA). Extensive simulation results show that MET-NMSA for ATSC 3.0 LDPC decoders has at most 1.53dB, 0.21dB and 0.35dB gain over NMSA, OMSA and VS-NMSA respectively when frame error rate (FER) is at 10−4 level over additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels using QPSK modulation.
Index Terms—ATSC 3.0 LDPC codes, MET-normalized scheme, decoding algorithms, density evolution.
I. INTRODUCTION
L OW-DENSITY parity-check (LDPC) code [1], which was firstly introduced by Gallager, has drawn wide attention consistently due to its near Shannon limit performance and low-complexity implementation. So far, a few classes of structured LDPC codes have already been adopted by many wireless standards and systems, such as 802 standards [2], Digital Video Broadcasting (DVB) [3], [4], ATSC 3.0 [5]–[7] and 5G New Radio (NR) [8], [9].
As is known to all, SPA is one of the most prominent decoding methods for LDPC codes. However, its computational complexity for check-node updates is too high to be commonly adopted in practical hardware implementation. MSA [10] greatly reduces SPA’s high complexity by introducing approximation in check-node updates, but this

They both use a one-type correction (scaling or offset) factor to compensate the overestimation in MSA. In [12], authors provided the optimized scaling and offset factors for ATSC 3.0 LDPC decoders. Their extensive simulation results show that the performance of NMSA and OMSA with the obtained factors is much closer to that of SPA, but some NMSA-based decoders suffer from serious error floor and OMSA-based decoders are overly reliant on perfect channel estimation [12].
To further improve the decoding performance, paper [13] firstly explained that scaling factors are closely related to the reliability of Log likelihood Ratio (LLR) values in the iterative decoding process. Thus, the scaling factors in NMSA should change with iterations since the LLR values actually vary with iterations as well. Recently, different methods (Monte Carlo [14], Machine Learning [15]) were proposed to optimize the scaling factors in VS-NMSA. In [16], authors compared VS-NMSA, NMSA and OMSA for their corresponding ATSC 3.0 LDPC decoders. The simulation results show that VS-NMSA provides better performance in comparison with NMSA in waterfall as well as error floor region. Besides, for ATSC 3.0 LDPC decoders, VS-NMSA performs better than OMSA in 9 cases and worse in 15 cases. However, VS-NMSA has more practical value since OMSA is sensitive to the imperfect channel estimation which is normal in practical systems.
In real situations, different structures of parity check matrices (PCMs) influence the convergence properties differently. For example, for low-rate codes, raptor-like [17], [18] structure is better than irregular-repeat-accumulate (IRA) [19] structure in terms of convergence speed [5]. Basically, the approximation of MSA-based algorithms degrades the convergence speed differently in different structures. That means, in theory, the selection of correction factors (e.g., variable-scaling factors) should take this difference into consideration. However, the selection of correction factors in algorithms mentioned above neglects the different structures in PCMs.
In this paper, we analyze the convergence characteristics of different structures in PCMs and find out that these different convergence characteristics are degraded differently for different structures because of the approximation operation in MSA. In order to describe the different structures within one PCM straightforward, MET is introduced in this paper. MET was proposed in [20]–[22] and it describes the structures within one PCM using several predeterminededge types. To compensate the different degradations in different predetermined-edge types, the correction factors for modified MSA should also change with different predetermined-correction types. In this paper, MET-normalized scheme is proposed to improve NMSA for ATSC 3.0 LDPC decoders. Besides, different with one-edge-type density evolution (OET-DE) in [10], [11], [23], MET-DE is also proposed to search optimized MET-scaling factors for different edge types in MET-NMSA.

II. CHARACTERISTICS OF LDPC CODES IN ATSC 3.0
In the physical layer standard of ATSC 3.0, there are 12 LDPC codes of length 16200 (short codes) and 12 LDPC codes of length 64800 (long codes). Both short and long LDPC codes have a wide range of code rates, varying from 2/15 to 13/15 as shown in Table I. Furthermore, they have two different structures, so-called Type-A (raptor-like) and Type-B (IRA).
The PCMs of Type-A and Type-B LDPC codes are respectively shown in Fig. 1 and 2. In Fig. 1, [A, B] is called the core submatrix and it has the IRA structure. The M1 × M2 submatrix Z is a zero matrix and E is an identity matrix with size M2 × M2. The PCM in Fig. 2 is also IRA-structured. Both M1 × M1 submatrix B in Fig. 1 and M × M submatrix B in Fig. 2 are dual-diagonal lower triangular matrices.
Note that quasi-cyclic (QC) structured LDPC codes are used in ATSC 3.0 because this structure is imposed to facilitate parallelism. Different with the variable lifting size q of PCMs in 5G NR [6], the PCMs of ATSC 3.0 LDPC codes consist of square submatrices with a fixed size q × q, with q equal to 360. These square submatrices can be zero matrices or quasi-cyclic permuted identity matrices. Because of the QC structure, the ATSC 3.0 PCM can be captured by a smaller matrix, called base graph (or protograph) [8], [22]. In base graphs, the notations M1,M2 and M in Fig. 1 and 2 can be converted to m1 = M1/q, m2 = M2/q and m = M/q.
The main characteristic of ATSC 3.0 codes is that the column or row weights in different submatrices are semi-regular. For TypeA codes, in specific, the column weight of submatrix A is always designed to be 3. The column weights in submatrix C are also similar. More intuitively, the column weights in C can be formulized by i∈I nixdv(C,i), where ni is the numbers of columns with column weight dv(C, i) in C and i is the order of different column weights. After that, MET-normalized scheme and its corresponding decoding algorithm (min-sum algorithm using multi-edge-type normalization, MET-NMSA) are proposed according to characteristics of ATSC 3.0 LDPC codes. At last, the modified density evolution is proposed to search scaling factors in MET-NMSA-based decoders.

IV. NUMERICAL RESULTS
In this section, the optimized MET-scaling factors and complexity of MET-NMSA for ATSC 3.0 LDPC codes are first presented. Besides, the comparison between MET-NMSA and other decoding algorithms is analyzed.
Note that actual performance of different decoding algorithms is simulated over additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels using QPSK modulation. In our simulations, the maximum iterations are set as 50. At least 20 erroneous blocks are attained to ensure the reliability of simulation results at each Es/N0 point.

A. Optimized MET-Scaling Factors and Complexity of MET-NMSA for ATSC 3.0 LDPC Codes
In MET-NMSA, the scaling factors are distinct in different edge types. The optimal values are searched for ATSC 3.0 LDPC decoders based on modified DE described in Section III-C. The search space of each factor is (0, 1] and 0.01 is used as the search step. Table I shows the optimized MET-scaling factors of MET-NMSA for ATSC 3.0 LDPC decoders. For Type-A LDPC codes, ST1, ST2 and ST3 in Table I represent α1,1, α2,1 and α2,2, respectively. For Type-B LDPC codes, ST1, ST2 and ST3 in Table I denote α1,1, α1,2 and α1,3, respectively. Besides, m1 and m2 for Type-B codes denote the column numbers of scaling type (1, 1) and (1, 2) in base graph, respectively.
The additional complexity of MET-NMSA, NMSA, OMSA and VS-NMSA compared with MSA is shown in Table II, where M, S and NH denote the multiplication operation, the subtraction operation and the number of edges in each PCM in ATSC 3.0, respectively.

It can be observed that the additional storage complexity of METNMSA is 3 while those of OMSA, NMSA and VS-NMSA are 1, 1 and 50, respectively. For additional computational complexity, OMSA is slightly more complex compared with MET-NMSA, NMSA and VS-NMSA.
B. Performance of MET-NMSA for ATSC 3.0 LDPC Codes For ATSC 3.0 codes, extensive simulation results are presented
in Table III ∼ V. In specific, Table III shows Shannon capacity limits, theoretical thresholds calculated by DE for decoding algorithms, i.e., SPA, MSA, OMSA, NMSA and MET-NMSA, and the corresponding theoretical gaps to Shannon capacity for ATSC 3.0 LDPC codes with short and long lengths under QPSK modulation, respectively. Table IV shows Shannon capacity limits, required Es/N0 to achieve FER = 10−4 in decoding algorithms, i.e., SPA, MSA, OMSA, NMSA, VS-NMSA and MET-NMSA, and the corresponding actual gaps to Shannon capacity for ATSC 3.0 LDPC codes with short and long lengths under QPSK modulation, respectively. In addition, Table V shows the actual gaps to SPA for different algorithms in both short and long codes.
According to Table III and IV, for short codes, the theoretical gaps of MET-NMSA from Shannon capacity are 0.26 ∼ 0.77dB while the actual simulation gaps are 0.66 ∼ 1.56dB. Besides, it can be seen that, for long codes in Table IV and V, the theoretical gaps from Shannon limits are 0.23 ∼ 0.61dB while the actual simulation gaps are 0.52 ∼ 1.16dB in MET-NMSA. These results show that long codes perform better than short codes in decoding algorithms both theoretically and actually. In addition, this phenomenon also implies that the theoretical results based on modified DE are well-matched with actual simulation results within 0.79 dB difference.
In the following, the comparison between MET-NMSA and other decoding algorithms (SPA, MSA, NMSA, OMSA, VS-NMSA) will be analyzed for ATSC 3.0 LDPC codes.
As shown in Table IV, for short ATSC 3.0 LDPC codes, METNMSA performs 0.42 ∼ 1.13dB better than MSA and 0.07 ∼ 1.16dB better than NMSA. Besides, MET-NMSA for long LDPC codes have 0.39 ∼ 1.46dB better performance than MSA and 0.02 ∼ 1.53dB better performance than NMSA. Furthermore, it can be found out that the error-floor performance of MET-NMSA is better than that of NMSA.
In addition, compared with OMSA which greatly relies on perfect channel estimation, MET-NMSA has at most 0.21dB better performance for most ATSC 3.0 codes over AWGN channels except for 6/15 codes. Therefore, OMSA is less advantageous in time-variant channels since the perfect channel estimation is needed to support its optimal performance while MET-NMSA is not sensitive to imperfect channel estimation.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, the MET-normalized scheme was proposed for NMSA-based LDPC decoders in ATSC 3.0. More detailedly, the degenerations of convergence for MSA in different edge types were

firstly demonstrated to be distinct for ATSC 3.0 LDPC codes. Therefore, a fixed scaling factor for all edge types neglects this variation and may cause performance loss in decoders. To reduce the loss, MET-NMSA which uses different correction factors for different types was proposed in this work. In addition, MET-DE was introduced to search the MET-scaling factors in MET-NMSAbased decoders. At last, extensive simulation results were presented to further show the superiority of MET-NMSA for ATSC 3.0 decoders.
As described in Section IV, MET-NMSA with obtained factors for ATSC 3.0 LDPC decoders not only has at most 1.53dB, 0.21dB and 0.35dB better performance than NMSA, OMSA and VS-NMSA, respectively, but also has low complexity. Besides, MET-NMSA is not sensitive to imperfect channel estimation and it can be easily applied to the time-variant channel in actual systems in comparison with OMSA. In summary, MET-NMSA is a superior option for commercial LDPC decoders in ATSC 3.0 receivers.
