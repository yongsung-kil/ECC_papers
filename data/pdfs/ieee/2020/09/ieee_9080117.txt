Minimizing the Number of Detrimental Objects in Multi-Dimensional Graph-Based Codes
Ahmed Hareedy , Member, IEEE, Rohith Kuditipudi, and Robert Calderbank , Fellow, IEEE

Abstract— The increasing demand for access to data has led to dramatic increases in data storage densities, and as densities increase, new sources of error appear. Multi-dimensional (MD) graph-based codes are capable of mitigating error sources like interference and channel non-uniformity in dense storage devices. A recent innovation improves the performance of MD spatially-coupled codes that are based on circulants by carefully relocating some circulants to minimize the number of short cycles. However, cycles become more detrimental when they combine together to form more advanced objects, e.g., absorbing sets, including low-weight codewords. In this paper, we show how MD relocations can be exploited to minimize the number of detrimental objects in the graph of an MD code. Moreover, we demonstrate the savings in the number of relocation arrangements earned by focusing on objects rather than their constituent cycles. Our technique is applicable to a wide variety of one-dimensional (OD) codes. Simulation results demonstrate significant lifetime gains achieved by the proposed MD codes on an industry-recommended model for Flash systems, and signalto-noise ratio gains on an industry-recommended model for magnetic recording systems, both with respect to OD codes with similar parameters. The second order analysis of MD relocations relies on conditions and options for an object, called a pattern, to form a bigger cycle after MD relocations, which are discussed in this paper.
Index Terms— Graph-based codes, multi-dimensional codes, LDPC codes, spatially-coupled codes, absorbing sets, relocations, data storage, lifetime, Flash memory, magnetic recording.
I. INTRODUCTION
T HE continuous and rapid growth in the density of modern storage devices brings many challenges. One of these challenges is an increase in the number of sources of data corruption in the system, which requires advanced error-correcting codes to be applied. Because of their capacityapproaching performance and the degrees of freedom they offer in the code construction, graph-based codes, i.e., variants of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, are applied in many data storage systems. Binary and non-binary graphbased codes are used in both Flash [2], [3] and magnetic recording (MR) [4] systems to significantly improve the performance.

Multi-dimensional (MD) graph-based codes are constructed by coupling different copies of a one-dimensional (OD) code to enhance the code properties. Because of the additional design flexibility offered by MD coupling, MD codes are capable of alleviating different types of interference and channel non-uniformity in modern storage systems, e.g., two-dimensional magnetic recording (TDMR) and three-dimensional Flash systems. One example is mitigating inter-track interference in TDMR systems [4] through specific non-binary LDPC code constructions as in [5]. Various MD spatially-coupled (MD-SC) codes were presented in the literature [6]–[9]. While these MD-SC codes demonstrated performance gains, they have limitations in the underlying OD codes and the topologies of the resulting MD codes. An MDSC code construction that improves the iterative decoding threshold was recently introduced in [10].
Recently, the authors of [11] proposed a technique for a systematic construction of MD-SC codes that are based on circulants. Through carefully-chosen relocations of circulants from the copies of the OD code to certain auxiliary matrices, they managed to significantly reduce the number of short cycles in the graph of the MD-SC code. While cycles are not preferred in graph-based codes, they become a lot more detrimental when they combine together to form absorbing sets (ASs), including low-weight codewords. ASs, not cycles, are the objects that dominate the error profile of graph-based codes in the error floor region [3], [12].
In this paper, we demonstrate how to use MD coupling to eliminate as many detrimental objects as possible from the graph of an MD code. The underlying OD codes we use can be structured or random, and can be block or SC codes. By deriving the fraction of relocation arrangements for different cases, we manifest the savings in relocation options achieved by operating on objects rather than cycles. Numerical results emphasizing the reduction in the multiplicity of detrimental objects are shown. Simulation results demonstrating ≈ 1200 (resp., 1800) program/erase cycles gain in the waterfall (resp., error floor) region over practical Flash channels and ≈ 0.4 (resp., 1.1) dB signal-to-noise ratio gain in the waterfall (resp., error floor) region over practical MR channels compared with OD codes having similar length and rate are presented. We start the second order analysis of MD relocations via studying the conditions and bounding the fraction of relocation arrangements under which smaller objects, which we call patterns, in the OD code copies result in bigger cycles. This study justifies excluding patterns-to-bigger-objects conversion in our MD code construction algorithm.

Under iterative quantized decoding, the detrimental (errorprone) objects in the graph of a code are typically ASs, including low-weight codewords. This was shown to be the case for additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) [12], [15], Flash [3], [16], and MR [3], [17] channels. Thus, recall:
Definition 1. Let V be a subset of VNs in the unlabeled graph of a code. Consider the subgraph induced by V. Let O (resp., T and H) be the set of degree-1 (resp., 2 and > 2) neighboring CNs of V. This graphical configuration is an (a, d1) unlabeled elementary trapping set (UTS) if |V| = a, |O| = d1, and |H| = 0. An (a, d1) UTS is an (a, d1) unlabeled elementary absorbing set (UAS) if each VN in V is connected to strictly more neighbors in T than in O.
While the analysis in the rest of the paper is perfectly applicable to UTSs as well as UASs, we focus on UASs because they are typically the detrimental objects of interest. Having said that, there are situations in which we are interested in UTSs: when a specific UTS is a common substructure in multiple dominant absorbing sets, i.e., a parent of multiple dominant absorbing-set children (see Section V).
Remark 2. Many non-elementary absorbing sets appearing in the error profile of non-binary graph-based codes over practical Flash and MR channels have underlying unlabeled elementary configurations [3], [17].

of a cycle. These savings are reflected in performance gains.

V. ALGORITHM AND EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
We are now ready to introduce the algorithm using which, we design our high performance MD codes. Guided by the previously illustrated theoretical results, Algorithm 1 minimizes the number of instances of a specific (a, d1) UAS, a < M amin, in the graph of the MD code via relocations. This specific (a, d1) UAS (binary) or its labeled AS (non-binary) can either be the most dominant AS in the error profile of the OD code or a common substructure that exists in the most dominant ASs in the OD code.7 Determining this (a, d1) UAS depends on the channel of interest (see [3] and [16]), the OD code being used, and also the decoder.
7Here, the bigger the size of the common substructure is, the better.

Because of their faster encoding and decoding, we focus on graph-based codes that are circulant-based in this section. Since operating on circulants is significantly faster than operating on entries, Algorithm 1 relocates NZ circulants, not NZ entries (see also [11]). The algorithm can be easily changed to relocate NZ entries for codes that are not structured. We say that an (a, d1) UAS instance involves a circulant if the instance has at least one NZ entry corresponding to an edge adjacent to a degree-2 CN inside the circulant. Moreover, the set of relocation decisions is X = {0, 1, . . . , M − 1}. The value ξ ∈ X , ξ > 0 (resp., ξ = 0), refers to the decision “relocate to Xξ” (resp., “no relocation”).
Note that in Step 3 of Algorithm 1, if the OD code is SC, the repetitive nature of the code should be exploited to reduce the processing time. Note also that Step 16 of Algorithm 1 aims to balance the number of NZ circulants across all auxiliary matrices (similar sparsity levels) in addition to its main objective, which is removing (a, d1) UAS instances. Here, we assume that if the UAS entered at Step 1 of the algorithm is an (a, d1) UAS, then all possible (a, d−1 ) UASs do not exist in the OD code. Thus, Algorithm 1 works for regenerable as well as non-regenerable UASs.
Extending Algorithm 1 to operate on multiple detrimental configurations is possible. In this case, different UASs should be ordered according to the values of a and d1 from the smallest to the largest, and the algorithm should operate on them successively. Given a specific (a, d1) UAS of interest, the current algorithm can only operate on its (a, d1) instances having the same configuration in the OD code to eliminate as many instances as possible in the MD code.
In general, objects (or patterns) having a− VNs or/and d2− degree-2 CNs in the OD code may result in (a, d1) UASs with d2 degree-2 CNs in the MD code after relocations. Here, a− (resp., d2−) is slightly less than a (resp., d2). In the next section, we discuss how such smaller objects in the OD code can result in cycles of length 2a in the MD code after relocations. We also show that the fraction of relocation arrangements under which a smaller object is converted into a bigger cycle is significantly smaller than the fraction under which a cycle is converted into a cycle of the same length for practical values of M , justifying our choice mentioned in the previous paragraph for Algorithm 1. Observe that in OD codes with no cycles of length 4, and if a < 6, (a, d1) UASs cannot be generated from smaller objects, i.e., objects having a− VNs or/and d2− degree-2 CNs.
Remark 4. As we shall see in the next section, the concept
of basic cycles can be used to determine the conditions under
which a specific pattern in the OD code results in a bigger cycle in the MD code after relocations. Thus, this concept can also be used to determine whether an (a, d1) UAS can be generated from a smaller object under certain relocations.
The main bottleneck facing Algorithm 1 is that it operates on one object at a time. This is well matched to cases where there is a single dominant detrimental object, or a single substructure common to nearly all of the most significant detrimental objects. Next, we discuss the experimental results. The Flash channel used in this section is a practical, asymmetric Flash channel, which is the normal-Laplace mixture (NLM) Flash channel [2]. In the NLM channel, the threshold voltage distribution of sub-20nm multi-level cell (MLC) Flash memories is carefully modeled. The four levels are modeled as different NLM distributions, incorporating several sources of error due to wear-out effects, e.g., programming/erasing problems, thereby resulting in significant asymmetry. Furthermore, the authors

of [2] provided accurate fitting results of their model for program/erase (P/E) cycles up to 10 times the manufacturer’s endurance specification (up to 30000 P/E cycles). We implemented the NLM channel based on the parameters described in [2]. Here, we use 3 reads, and the sector size is 512 bytes. For decoding, we use a finite-precision (FP) fast Fourier transform based q-ary sum-product algorithm (FFT-QSPA) LDPC decoder [19]. The decoder performs a maximum of 50 iterations, and it stops if a codeword is reached sooner.
The MR system used in this section adopts the partialresponse (PR) channel presented in [17] and sequence detection. This PR channel incorporates the MR channel effects: inter-symbol interference (intrinsic memory), jitter, and electronic noise. The normalized channel density [4], [17] is 1.4, and the PR equalization target is [8 14 2]. The receiver consists of filtering units followed by a Bahl-Cocke-Jelinek-Raviv (BCJR) detector [20], which is based on pattern-dependent noise prediction (PDNP) [21], and again an FP FFT-QSPA (q = 2) LDPC decoder [19]. The number of global (detectordecoder) iterations is 10, and the number of local (decoder only) iterations is 20. Unless a codeword is reached, the decoder performs its prescribed number of local iterations for each global iteration. More details can be found in [17].
We use five OD codes in this section. The SC codes are designed according to [16] and [22], which provide methods to design high performance SC codes for Flash and MR systems, respectively. These methods are based on the optimal overlap, circulant power optimizer (OO-CPO) approach. The block code is designed according to [3, Section VI]. OD Code 1 (resp., OD Code 2) is an SC code defined over GF(4) (resp., GF(2)), which has γ = 3 (resp., = 4), maximum row weight = 19 (resp., = 17), circulant size = 19 (resp., = 17), memory = 1, and coupling length = 7 (resp., = 6). Thus, OD Code 1 (resp., OD Code 2) has block length = 5054 (resp., = 1734) bits and rate ≈ 0.82 (resp., ≈ 0.73). OD Code 3 is a block code defined over GF(2), which has γ = 4, row weight = 40, and circulant size = 53. OD Code 3 has block length = 4240 bits and rate ≈ 0.90. OD Code 4 (resp., OD Code 5) is an SC code that is designed exactly as OD Code 1 (resp., OD Code 2), except for that OD Code 4 (resp., OD Code 5) has coupling length = 21 (resp., = 18) instead of 7 (resp., 6) (three times as long as OD Code 1 (resp., OD Code 2)). Thus, OD Code 4 (resp., OD Code 5) has block length = 15162 (resp., = 5202) bits and rate ≈ 0.83 (resp., ≈ 0.75).

In fact, this is a general AS of type two (GAST) according to [3], but we abbreviate the notation here for simplicity. Additionally, the error profile in the error floor region of OD Code 2 when simulated over the practical MR (PR) channel is dominated by the (4, 4), (7, 4), (8, 4), and (9, 4) UASs. VI. FROM PATTERNS TO BIGGER CYCLES
In this section, we discuss how a small pattern can result in a bigger cycle after relocations. This new cycle can be a substructure in another object of interest. Via bounding the fraction of relocation arrangements under which this bigger cycle generation happens, we demonstrate why ignoring this second order MD phenomenon in Algorithm 1 is acceptable.
Define a β × α pattern as a configuration characterized by a β × α submatrix in the parity-check matrix of the OD code, with the property that it can generate a cycle of length 2a in the MD code after relocations. The M copies of this pattern in the OD code copies can generate M cycles. We ignore degree-1 CNs, and note that this cycle has a VNs and d2 = a degree-2 CNs. Here, degrees of CNs and VNs are with respect to the pattern graph. Moreover, a < M amin, with 2amin being the length of the shortest cycle in the OD code. The graph of the pattern has α VNs, β CNs having degree ≥ 2, and 2a or less edges, where edges that do not contribute to the cycle of length 2a are excluded. This pattern has α ≤ a, β ≤ d2 = a, and at most one of α and β equals a by definition.

Remark 8. In [22], protograph patterns that are not themselves cycles of length 8 were taken into consideration in the optimization problem aiming at minimizing the number of configurations that can result in cycles of length 8 after lifting. The reason is that practical values of the circulant size in the context of lifting are typically much bigger than practical values of M (which are ≤ 7) in the context of relocations.

VII. CONCLUSION
We developed a systematic approach that starts from fundamental understanding of how damaging configurations (objects) are built out of small components (cycles) to design an MD code. We introduced necessary and sufficient conditions for a UAS to stay active or become inactive, i.e., be removed, after a relocation arrangement. We derived the savings in relocation options achieved by focusing on UASs instead of cycles in the MD code design procedure. Examples demonstrating the significance of these savings were introduced for famous UAS configurations. We presented an algorithm to design high performance MD codes by removing detrimental UASs via relocations. Using this algorithm, codes free of specific UASs were designed and simulated. Gains of up to about 1800 in P/E cycles and 1.1 dB in SNR were achieved via our MD codes compared with OD codes of similar parameters over practical Flash and MR channels, respectively. We presented an analysis of how small patterns can result in bigger cycles, which is an important step in the second order analysis of MD relocations, and it supports the idea of the proposed MD code design algorithm. Future work includes extending our MD code design algorithm to operate on multiple objects simultaneously, and combining MD graph-based codes with MD constrained codes to offer more gains.
