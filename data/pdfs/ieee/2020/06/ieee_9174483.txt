Abstract‚ÄĒAbsorbing sets (ASs) cause the error floor phenomenon in many low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes by entrapping iterative decoders. A recent simplified system model for practical min-sum (MS) LDPC decoding predicts that if all variable nodes in an AS have channel messages above a certain threshold, the AS cannot entrap the decoder. The threshold is an AS parameter that depends on its Tanner graph, and is the result of a nonlinear optimization. In this paper, we analyze the messages exchanged in the directed graph (digraph) of the AS during MS decoding while evaluating the AS threshold. By doing this, we unveil the meaning of the threshold value, which is the minimum channel message for which positive feedback loops in the digraph involve all the messages exchanged.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are linear block codes that can be decoded by various low complexity iterative algorithms, such as sum-product algorithm (SPA) [1] or minsum (MS) and its variants [2]. Unluckily, many LDPC codes suffer from the error floor phenomenon, which is a significant change of slope in the bit error rate (BER) curve versus the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). This performance degradation is generally due to the suboptimality of iterative decoding in Tanner graphs with loops, which can fail due to subsets of variable nodes (VN) able to lock the decoder to incorrect decisions. These subsets are named trapping sets (TSs) in [3].
Absorbing sets (ASs) are a class of TSs defined in [4] as subsets D of VNs where every VN has a strict minority of neighboring check nodes (CNs) with odd degree with respect to D. The ASs have been shown to be responsible for the dominant decoding failures of various soft message passing decoders [4]‚Äď[6]. In particular, elementary TSs (ETSs), and their subclass of elementary ASs (EASs), i.e. those whose induced subgraph contains CNs with degree at most 2 with respect to D, have catalyzed most of the attention in the last decade [7]‚Äď[11], since they are recognized as the main cause of errors on Gaussian channels [3], [12], [13], unlike for non-Gaussian channels [14]. The EASs enable a local analysis of iterative decoding inside their Tanner graph through a linear dynamical system or state-space model, under some simplifying hypotheses. The method is introduced in [7] and it is extended in [8] and [9] to SPA decoding. In [9], the effect of messages saturation due to finite precision, is also

investigated, and the saturation level is recognized to strongly impact on the decoder behavior.
In our works, we consider LDPC decoders based on MS decoding with fixed point representation of all messages, as in most practical implementations. Since messages are clipped to a maximal value, the fixed point representation introduces a saturation level that depends on the quantization choices. In [15], we introduce the saturation in the linear state-space model of the MS decoder, and we define the AS threshold, that is an AS parameter that discriminates the existence/nonexistence of misleading equilibria, i.e., equilibria that are not guaranteed to correspond to correct decoding. Though the decoder can generally be trapped by an AS and reach an equilibrium that does not lead to the correct decisions, in [15] it is shown that if all VNs in the AS have channel messages above the AS threshold, the only equilibrium of the system corresponds to maximal extrinsic messages and leads to correct decisions. In [16], we show that for each set of channel messages there exists a minimum equilibrium and that the AS threshold can be found by tracking the minimum equilibrium versus the minimum channel message, up to a point where the minimum equilibrium has the maximal value for extrinsic messages. In [17] the analysis is generalized to scaled-MS decoding.
In this paper we analyze the messages exchanged in the directed graph (or digraph [9]) of the AS when computing its threshold using the algorithm described in [16]. We show that once all the channel messages reach a value greater than the threshold value, the graph becomes fully cyclic and the positive feedback forces all messages of the graph to the upper limit, deactivating the AS. This interpretation of the AS threshold is the main original contribution of this paper.

V. CONCLUSIONS

In this paper, we analyzed the behavior of MS decoders with message saturation, on the message digraph of elementary ASs of an LDPC code Tanner graph. Following Algorithm 1 [16] for the AS threshold computation, we unveiled the real meaning of the threshold value, which is the minimum channel LLR for which correct messages incoming into the AS from the outside graph, are strong enough to ignite loops of messages with positive feedback for each node, so that the internal (wrong) messages that tend to reinforce themselves, are defeated and the AS is deactivated.
