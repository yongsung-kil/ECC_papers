Decoding of LDPC Codes for 5G Standard Using Source Distribution

A. A. Ovchinnikov, Member, IEEE
Saint-Petersburg State University of Aerospace Instrumentation
Saint Petersburg, Russia mldoc@ieee.org

Saint-Petersburg State University of Aerospace Instrumentation

Abstract—The paper examines the decoding of low-density parity-check codes taking into account the source distribution. More specifically, we consider the alteration of LLR calculation on the input of belief propagation decoder. The experimental results for binary input additive white Gaussian noise channel are presented using various source distributions.
Keywords—low-density parity-check codes; 5G; non-uniform source; joint decoding
I. INTRODUCTION Low-density parity-check code (LDPC code) is an error correcting code that is used to reduce the errors that appear during the transmission over the noisy channel. LDPC codes were firstly proposed by Robert G. Gallager in 1962 [1], but due to the computing limitations in implementing the encoder and decoder for such codes, LDPC codes were ignored for almost 30 years. LDPC codes were rediscovered by David Mackay in the 1990s and subsequent development of computing power arose a new wave of interest to LDPC codes, which deserve attention due to the near-Shannon-Limit error-correcting capability, low error-floor [2], and effective procedures of encoding and decoding.
Most recently, quasi-cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes have been approved by 3GPP as the channel coding scheme for the transmission system of 5G communication [3], since QCLDPC codes are able to reach the same decoding performance as previously prevailing turbo codes, but at the same time allowing rate-compatible property, can support multiple lifting sizes and easy design with parallel decoding. These properties make such codes easily adapt various information lengths and rate matching [4].
Construction of error correcting codes in many standards is based on an assumption that information bits are independent and uniformly distributed, which in many cases doesn’t hold due to the properties of the transmission data (for example, multimedia), such that the source contains redundancy that can be exploited at the decoder side. A lot of research has been made on joint source and channel coding which used the source redundancy to both alter the encoding and the decoding operations. But some standards for the transmission systems fix the transmitter part that consists of source and channel encoders, so the changes can be applied only to the receiver

(decoder). In such case, the problem of considering the source statistics in the decoding process appears.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section II defines the main properties of LDPC codes and its subclass – QC-LDPC codes. Also, description of 5G LDPC codes construction is given in the same section. Section III shortly observes the source and channel coding problem and considers the calculating of LLR for additive white Gaussian noise channel using source probability distribution. Section IV contains simulation results in Gaussian channel with belief propagation decoder using various source distributions. Finally, section V concludes the paper.

C. 5G LDPC codes LDPC codes, Turbo codes and Polar codes were
considered as promising candidates for the channel coding in new 5G communication standard, but the comprehensive comparison of the candidates for their ability to be effectively encoded and decoded, maintain low decoding latency and support various length and rates showed that only carefully constructed LDPC codes are able to satisfy all of the

mentioned requirements. Here are some of 5G LDPC codes distinctive features that allow to overcome other candidate codes: multi-edge construction (the generalization of irregular LDPC codes that yields performance improvements and adaptability), high rate sub-matrix code that enables efficient encoding, sparseness leads to low complexity decoding.
As mentioned above, 5G LDPC codes belong to QCLDPC codes and are constructed from protographs. Fig. 2 shows the structure of the base matrix of these standard codes. As shown in the figure, the base matrix consists of five submatrices, where A corresponds to the systematic bits, D corresponds to parity bits, O is a zero matrix, I is an identity matrix [4]. A and D always participate in coding process while O, I and A are used to adjust the matrix for a given length and rate. Two base matrices, denoted by BG1 and BG2 are fixed for 5G LDPC codes. Matrices have similar structure, but are designed for different lengths and rates. BG1 is designed for information lengths up to 8448 and code rates from 1/3 to 8/9, while BG2 for smaller information lengths no more than 3840 and code rates from 1/5 to 2/3.

D. Decoding of LDPC codes The decoding algorithms for LDPC codes are iterative
procedures that operates with each symbol separately and are usually described as exchanging messages over the edges of the Tanner graph between the check and variable nodes. Algorithms are iterative in nature since decoding proceeds until the codeword has zero syndrome or a predefined number of iterations is reached. Since the iterative algorithms operates on each symbol separately, even when the large number of errors in the channel occurs and wrong decision about the codeword is made, the bit error probability may remain low. The most common message passing algorithm with soft decision is the belief propagation algorithm or as it also called sum-product algorithm (SPA). This algorithm is presented in Gallager’s work [1] and is effective when the girth of the Tanner graph is large, where the girth is the length of the shortest cycle in the Tanner graph. That requirement is made so the information corresponding to the node doesn’t loop back to itself very fast, hence providing a little information about neighboring nodes [10]. The messages passed along the edges in this algorithm are probabilities, or beliefs. But in order to reduce the decoding complexity it is sometimes advantageous to work with loglikelihoods instead of probabilities. IV. MODELING RESULTS
We consider the information transmission from nonuniform memoryless source over the BI-AWGN channel, using SPA decoder with 30 iterations for 5G LDPC codes based on the BG1 for information lengths K = 528, K = 1984 and rates R equals to 1/2, 3/4 and 8/9. The considered source model produces zero with probability p0 and one with probability p1 = 1 − p0. Source with uniform distribution p1 = 0.5 is compared to sources with non-uniform distribution p1 = 0.1 (p1 = 0.9) and p1 = 0.3 (p1 = 0.7), since the decoding for the first source cannot be improved by changing LLR calculation, whereas the latter sources are supposed to yield better decoding performance by using the addition information about the source.
It may be concluded from curves showed on Fig. 3 that the additional information about the source leads to even worse performance for codes with rate 1/2. But with the rate increasing the gap between the curves with modified LLR and standard LLR calculation is seem to be more distinguishable.

V. CONCLUSION In this paper we considered a problem of decoding LDPC codes with the redundancy source. Joint source and channel coding, that alters both the encoding and the decoding operations, is well known solution for this problem, which appears to be not suitable for 5G standard which fixes the transmitter part, so the source and channel encoders cannot be changed to improve the decoding performance. To overcome this limitation, we examined introducing changes exclusively on the part of the decoder by changing LLR calculation using source probability distribution. Experiments showed the improvement of the decoding is achieved for high rate codes, whereas applying modified LLR calculation to low rate codes led to decoding performance deterioration. Summarizing the above, the gain over uniform source was shown to increase significantly at high rates codes and independent on the code length. Additionally, a brief review of low-density paritycheck codes, quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check codes and the sum-product algorithm is given. Also, the structure of lowdensity parity-check codes from 5G standard is discussed.
The future work will be pointed on developing the joint decoder for codes from the 5G standard so that the source statistics is not only taken into account during LLR calculation step, but is also directly included in the structure of the decoder.
