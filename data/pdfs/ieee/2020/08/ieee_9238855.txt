Abstract—In this paper, we propose a parallel and memoryefficient decoding for spatially-coupled low-density parity-check (SC LDPC) codes. The new decoding was obtained by applying parallel architecture and efficient memory management to the windowed decoding. Simulation results show that the new decoding greatly reduces decoding latency and requires less memory, and there is no performance degradation. The advantage of the proposed decoding make it appealing in practical applications, especially in low latency scenario.
Index Terms—spatially-coupled LDPC codes, windowed decoding, layered decoding, parallel architecture
I. INTRODUCTION
Spatially-coupled low-density parity-check (SC LDPC) codes were first introduced as low-density parity-check (LDPC) convolutional codes by Jimenez Felstrom and Zigangirov in [1]. Owing to the spatially coupling code struture, the belief propagation (BP) decoding of these codes has a phenomenon called threshold saturation, i.e., the BP threshold of SC LDPC codes is close to the maximum a posteriori (MAP) threshold of an underlying LDPC block codes [2]. Due to excellent performance, SC LDPC codes are considered as attractive candidates for future communication systems.
Unlike LDPC block codes, it is unrealistic to straightly applying the BP decoding to SC LDPC codes, because the codeword length of SC LDPC codes is large or infinite. However, thanks to the special structure, the constraint length of SC LDPC codes is much smaller than the codeword length, which make it possible to decode SC LDPC codes by pipeline decoding as parallelizing the iterations of the BP decoding through several processors [1]. Besides, Pusane et al. have proposed an improved pipeline decoding in [3].
In [4], Iyengar et al. introduced windowed decoding as another decoding scheme for SC LDPC codes. This scheme operates the BP decoding inside a limited length window and then slides the window to next position to keep working. Compared with the pipeline decoding, the windowed decoding requires less memory and have negligible performance degradation. Due to the memory-saving characteristic, the windowed decoding was more effective at the aspect of implement. It has been shown in [5] that, by employing the windowed decoding, SC LDPC codes can outperform their corresponding LDPC block codes even under the same constraints of complexity

and memory. Moreover, several effective windowed decoding schedules are proposed to reduce decoding complexity by avoiding unnecessary message passing or iterations, and we refer the interested reader to [6], [7].
In fact, there are two schedules to operate the BP decoding, these schedules are known as flooding decoding and layered decoding. The flooding decoding can operate fully-parallel while the layered decoding is serial, however, the converge speed of the latter is faster than that of the former, and the layered decoding requires only about half numbers of iterations [8] to achieve same performance. To reduce the decoding complexity, the layered decoding is commonly used in the LDPC block codes, and it can also be applied to the SC LDPC codes [9].
By applying two modifications to the windowed decoding with layered decoding schedule (referred as layered windowed decoding), we obtain a new decoding which has low decoding latency and requires less memory while maintaining the advantage of the original decoding (i.e., low memory requirement and low complexity). These modifications are achieved by paralleling the computation at different position and reducing the unnecessary memory. As a result, the decoding speed is proportional to the numbers of parallel processors, and the required memory can be reduced to 67% in the best case. Note that another parallel architecture was proposed in [10] for the windowed decoding with flooding decoding schedule (referred as flooding windowed decoding) at the cost of performance loss.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. In Section II, the structure of SC LDPC codes is described. The conventional windowed decoding and layered decoding schedule are also detailed. In Section III, we introduce the two modifications of the windowed decoding to improve parallelism and storage requirement without performance loss. In addition, the new decoding algorithm is given and analyzed at the aspect of implementation complexity. In Section IV, simulation results of the new decoding with different parameter are given. Besides, we show the comparison result between different decoding.

III. A PARALLEL AND MEMORY-EFFICIENT DECODING
In the following, we introduce two modifications to improve the layered windowed decoding at the aspect of decoding parallelism and memory requirement. Then we analyze the implementational complexity of the new decoding algorithm.

A. Parallel Computation
Considering the windowed decoding with size w = 8 and iteration I = 1 for SC LDPC code with µ = 2, the partial consecutive process of this decoding is represented in Fig. 4. The processor updates messages layer by layer. At first, it updates the sub-blocks of R0, LLR−2, LLR−1 and LLR0, then the sub-blocks of R1, LLR−1, LLR0 and LLR1, ... , till the end. After the whole memory in decoder have been shifted by one sub-block, the updating in the next stage begins with the sub-blocks of R1, LLR−1, LLR0 and LLR1. Note that the index of LLR or R is the received time instant of each sub-block.
Due to the spatially-coupled structure of the code, the processor operates on only µ+1 sub-blocks of LLR by one layer, without accessing anyother sub-block of LLR in memory. Thus, it is possible to impove the windowed decoding to compute parallelly like pipeline decoding.

C. New Decoding Algorithm
Inspired by the above modifications, we propose a parallel and memory-efficient decoding. The new decoding have two parameter, the parameter P denotes the number of parallel processors, and the parameter I denotes the times of inner iteration. The algorithm of the new decoding is given below.
Algorithm 1 New Decoding Algorithm Init decoder’s LLR memory with a large positive value Init decoder’s R memory with zero-value repeat Put one sub-block of LLR into LLR memory and one sub-block of zero-value into R memory for each p ∈ [1, P ] do for i = 1 → I do for j = 0 → µ do Update one sub-block of R and µ + 1 sub-blocks of LLR, they all start from the (p − 1)(µ + 1) + j + 1th sub-block end for end for Update one sub-block of R and µ + 1 sub-blocks of LLR, these sub-blocks start from the (p−1)(µ+1)+1th sub-block end for Shift LLR memory and R memory by one sub-block until the decoding is end

In the repeat statement, the new decoding have three steps, the first step and the third step are the same as windowed decoding, but the second step is different. In the second step, P processors work parallelly, and each processor executes I(µ + 1) + 1 times of updation between different sub-blocks of R and LLR with layered algorithm. Notably, the top-most (left-most) sub-block of R (LLR) is the first sub-block.

In addition, we analyze the implementation complexity of the new decoding on computational complexity, storage complexity and decoding latency.
1) computational complexity: Let Ccheck denote the computational complexity required for updating a check node with degree dc. In the second step of the new decoding, there are P processors, each processor need to update mS check nodes I(µ + 1) + 1 times. Note that a block of LLR has nS decoded bit, thus the computational complexity per decoded bit is calculated as

Cbit = P mS(I(µ + 1) + 1)Ccheck/(nS)

(6)

= P (1 − R)(I(µ + 1) + 1)Ccheck ,

where R is the rate of the code and R = (n − m)/n. 2) storage complexity: In the memory of decoder, there are
P (µ+1) sub-blocks of R and P (µ+1)+µ sub-blocks of LLR, the elements of these sub-blocks is mSdc and nS separately. Therefore, the size of required memory is given by

Storage = P (µ + 1)mSdc + (P (µ + 1) + µ)nS . (7)
3) decoding latency: Let Tcheck denote the time required for updating a check node with degree dc. In fact, decoding a sub-block of LLR needs to carry out the three step P (µ+1)+µ times. In the three step, the second step take up major time, and it requires mS(I(µ + 1) + 1)Tcheck. Thus, the decoding latency is given as

∆delay = (P (µ + 1) + µ)mS(I(µ + 1) + 1)Tcheck . (8)

We simulate the bit-error rate (BER) of new coding with different parameters for Code1, and the result is shown in Fig. 7 and Fig. 8.
