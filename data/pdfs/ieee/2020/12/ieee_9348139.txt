A Reconstruction-Computation-Quantization (RCQ) Approach to Node Operations in LDPC Decoding

Linfang Wang, Richard D. Wesel University of California, Los Angeles Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering
{lfwang,wesel}@ucla.edu

Maximilian Stark, Gerhard Bauch Hamburg University of Technology
Institute of Communications {maximilian.stark,bauch}@tuhh.de

Abstract—This paper proposes a finite-precision decoding method for low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes that features the three steps of Reconstruction, Computation, and Quantization (RCQ). Unlike Mutual-Information-Maximization Quantized Belief Propagation (MIM-QBP), RCQ can approximate either belief propagation or Min-Sum decoding. MIM-QBP decoders do not work well when the fraction of degree-2 variable nodes is large. However, sometimes a large fraction of degree-2 variable nodes is used to facilitate a fast encoding structure, as seen in the IEEE 802.11 standard and the DVB-S2 standard. In contrast to MIM-QBP, the proposed RCQ decoder may be applied to any off-the-shelf LDPC code, including those with a large fraction of degree-2 variable nodes. Simulations show that a 4-bit MinSum RCQ decoder delivers frame error rate (FER) performance within 0.1 dB of floating point belief propagation (BP) for the IEEE 802.11 standard LDPC code in the low SNR region. The RCQ decoder actually outperforms floating point BP and MinSum in the high SNR region were FER less than 10−5. This paper also introduces Hierarchical Dynamic Quantization (HDQ) to design the time-varying non-uniform quantizers required by RCQ decoders. HDQ is a low-complexity design technique that is slightly sub-optimal. Simulation results comparing HDQ and optimal quantization on the symmetric binary-input memoryless additive white Gaussian noise channel show a mutual information loss of less than 10−6 bits, which is negligible in practice.
Index Terms—Low Precision LDPC decoder, Information Maximization Quantizer, Finite-Precision Decoding.
I. INTRODUCTION
Non-uniform quantization of messages [1]–[12] in LDPC decoders provides excellent performance with low precision, i.e. a coarse quantization of message values. In [1], Planjery et al. replace computations with optimized look-up tables (LUTs) producing a Finite Alphabet Iterative Decoder (FAID) that can overcome the error floor of certain LDPC codes under binary symmetric channel (BSC). Aiming to minimize the performance degradation in the waterfall region, Romero and Kurkoski design LUTS in [2] using Mutual Information Maximization (MIM) creating an MIM-LUT decoder. MIMLUTs decompose the actual node operation into a series of cascaded two-input, single-output LUTs. In [3], Lewandowsky et al. proposed the Information-Optimum decoder, also called

the Information Bottleneck (IB) decoder. In [4]–[6], Stark et al. extended the ideas from [2] and [3] to develop message alignment (MA) such that IB decoders can also work on irregular LDPC codes with arbitrary degree distributions. In [7]–[9], Min-LUT replace the LUTs in the check node with a discrete, cluster-based Min-Sum operation. One problem faced by Min-LUT is it can’t work well when the fraction of degree2 variable node is large [8].
Both the IB decoder and Min-LUT decoder improve decoder throughput via low-bit-width messages and by replacing computations with LUTs. However, one problem faced by LUTbased decoders is the large memory requirement for the LUTs when either maximum node degree or maximum iteration time is high. In [10], Lee and Thorpe proposed a non-uniform BP decoder, which is implemented based only on simple mappings and fixed-point additions. This eliminates the high memory requirement of IB and Min-LUT decoders.
In the non-uniform BP decoder, the messages for computation have high bit width, and the messages for routing have low bit width. Special reconstruction mappings and non-uniform quantizers were found for conversion between high and low bit width messages. He et al. in [11] provided a systematic way to design mappings and proposed MIM-QBP. However, similar to Min-LUT, MIM-QBP also suffers performance degradation when the fraction of degree-2 variable nodes in the LDPC code is large [11]. Sometimes, this limitation can be overcome during code design by optimizing the edge distribution to lower the fraction of degree 2 variable nodes. However, it is sometimes necessary to decode LDPC codes that have a large fraction of degree 2 variable nodes. As an example, the rate-1/2 LDPC code in the IEEE 802.11 standard has 45.8% degree-2 variable nodes. [12].
In this work, we generalize the mapping structure in [10] and propose a finite-precision LDPC decoding method that features the three steps of Reconstruction, Computation, and Quantization (RCQ). Unlike MIM-QBP and Min-LUT, RCQ can be applied on any off-the-shelf LDPC code, including those with a large fraction of degree-2 variable nodes, such as the IEEE 802.11 code. The main contributions in this paper are as follows:
• We propose a general and flexible RCQ decoder paradigm. Unlike the work in [10], [11], RCQ decoders can approximate BP decoders (bp-RCQ), Min-Sum decoders (ms-RCQ), or other decoding approaches.

II. THE RCQ DECODING PARADIGM
Message passing algorithms update messages between variable nodes and check nodes in an iterative manner either until a valid codeword is found, or for a predefined maximum number, IT , of iterations. Each update includes two steps: 1) computation of the output message and 2) communication of the output message to a neighboring node. We call messages used during the computation step internal messages, and messages communicated to neighbors over the edges of the Tanner graph external messages. In [10], the authors proposed a LDPC decoder structure where internal messages have a higher precision than external messages. In this work, we propose a generalization of their structure that features three steps of Reconstruction, Computation and Quantization (RCQ).
As illustrated in Fig.1, the RCQ decoding paradigm consists the following three parts:
1) Reconstruction: Reconstruction R(·) : F2m → Fn2 (m < n) maps external message ui to internal message ri. We denote channel reconstruction by Rch. Additionally, we denote variable node reconstruction and check node reconstruction at iteration t by Rtc and Rtv, respectively.
2) Computation: Computation function F (·) : Fn2 → Fn2 is used to calculate the outgoing message rout. We denote the variable node function and check node function by F v and F c, respectively. F v sums up all incoming messages. F c can have different implementations. We denote the check node operation in BP (i.e. the hyperbolic-tangent operation) and standard MinSum decoder by Fbcp and Fmc s.
3) Quantization: A quantizer Q(·) : Fn2 → F2m quantizes rout to a m bit external message uout. A m bit Quantizer Q

IV. RCQ DESIGN VIA MIM-DDE
The quantization thresholds used by the RCQ paradigm vary with each iteration. In the tth iteration, Qct , Rtv, Qvt , Rtv+1 are constructed by quantizing the joint p.m.f. between code bits and external messages. To find the RCQ thresholds we use DDE to determine the joint p.m.f at each iteration and then use HDQ to find the MIM quantization thresholds. Our DDE is distinct from [17] because our DDE produces p.m.f.’s that arise from non-uniform quantizations. To differentiate our DDE from that of [17], which uses uniform quantization, we refer to our DDE as MIM-DDE.

VI. CONCLUSION
This paper presents RCQ for external message quantization
in LDPC decoders and HDQ as a way to design effective
quantizaion with low complexity. HDQ is used with MIM-
DDE to construct the RCQ decoder. Unlike MIM-QBP, RCQ
can approximate either belief propagation or Min-Sum decod-
ing. We use an IEEE 802.11 standard LDPC code to illustrate
that the RCQ decoder works well even when the fraction of
degree-2 variable nodes is large. Simulations show that an msRCQ decoder with 4-bit external messages delivers frame error rate (FER) performance within 0.1 dB of floating point belief
propagation (BP) in the low SNR region. The RCQ decoder
actually outperforms floating point BP in the high SNR region
because it overcomes elementary trapping sets that decrease
