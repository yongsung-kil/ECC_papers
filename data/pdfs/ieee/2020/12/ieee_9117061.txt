A Flexible and High Parallel Permutation Network for 5G LDPC Decoders
Zhiwei Zhong , Graduate Student Member, IEEE, Yongming Huang , Senior Member, IEEE, Zaichen Zhang , Senior Member, IEEE, Xiaohu You , Fellow, IEEE, and Chuan Zhang , Member, IEEE

Abstract—Cyclic shifting is usually applied in quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) decoders for permutation operations. In this brief, we propose a flexible and high parallel permutation network for QC-LDPC decoders in the 5th generation (5G) communication systems, which supports LDPC codes with arbitrary code lengths and all lifting sizes in 5G standards. The proposed permutation network can cyclically shift at most 128 frames of data in parallel, which greatly improves the hardware efficiency and decoding throughput. Based on the TSMC 90-nm CMOS technology, synthesis results illustrate the superiority of the proposed design.
Index Terms—5G LDPC codes, cyclic shifting, permutation networks, Banyan networks.
I. INTRODUCTION
Q UASI-CYCLIC low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes have been widely applied in modern communication systems due to their good error-correction performance and hardware-friendly implementation [1]. Recently, QCLDPC codes have been deployed in the 5th generation (5G) enhanced mobile broadband data channel.
Permutation networks in QC-LDPC decoders perform cyclic shifting operations based on shift values of submatrices in parity check matrices (PCM). One straightforward implementation of cyclic shifting is barrel shifters. However, a predefined barrel shifter can not process data with different sizes or multiple frames of data in parallel unless extra stages are introduced at the cost of higher complexity [2]. Another attempt to increase the parallelism of barrel shifters is presented in [3], which introduces an extra stage after a barrel shifter to make it suitable for data with multiple sizes and

Nevertheless, as mentioned in [3], its drawbacks are complex input/output operation and complex control signal generating scheme, requiring extra ROMs. Consider that shift values of PCM are not all consecutive integers, hard-wired permutation network [4] and the combination of some fixed cyclic shifters [5] can achieve complexity reduction compared with normal barrel shifters, but can not support parallel processing.
One alternative is Benes network [6], which is an optimal nonblocking network that can achieve arbitrary permutation. The size PM of a regular Benes network is a power of two. Given that sizes of submatrices in most communication standards are not powers of two (e.g., the maximal size Z of submatrices in 5G LDPC codes is 384), using Benes networks as permutation modules leads to the reduction of hardware efficiency. To solve this problem, variation Benes networks are proposed in [7], [8], where the size PM of the Benes network is modified to 3 × 2n or 5 × 2n by introducing 3 × 3 switches or 5 × 5 switches. Though the hardware complexity of Benes networks is reduced by modifying PM, the variation Benes network [7], [8] still has a relatively long critical path: 2 × log2PM − 1 stages. In contrast, a shift network proposed in [9] has a shorter critical path: log2PM + 1 stages. It uses two shifting modules to shift one frame of data differently, and one merge network to obtain the final results. Though a shorter critical path usually indicates higher throughput, the aforementioned shift network is not able to permute multiple frames of data in parallel [9], which limits the throughput from another perspective.
A variation Banyan network-based permutation network is proposed by [10], which can process at most 4 frames of data in parallel and has relatively short latency: log2PM + 1 stages. Theoretically, the permutation network in [10] can be expanded to permute multiple frames of data in parallel as long as the input size Z ≥ 6. Here we regard 6 as the lower bound Zl. If the input size is smaller than Zl, input data are padded with zeros without affecting the permutation results.
In this brief, we propose a flexible and high parallel permutation network for 5G LDPC decoders based on the variation Banyan networks in [10]. The size of the proposed permutation network is 384×384. It supports all lifting sizes Z specified in 5G LDPC codes and parallel processing. Major contributions of this brief are as follows.

II. PRELIMINARIES
A. 5G Standards for LDPC Codes
In 5G LDPC codes, two base graphs (BG1 and BG2) and fifty-one lifting sizes Z are employed to support multiple information lengths and various code rates. BG1 and BG2 are two binary matrices of which the sizes are 46 × 68 and 42 × 52, respectively. Each element 1 or 0 in the base graphs is replaced by a shifted identity matrix or an all-zero matrix of size Z × Z to construct relevant PCM. In 5G standards, lifting sizes Z of submatrices cover a wide range of values from 2 to 384 (Z = i × 2n; i = 2, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, 13, 15; n are non-negative integers). To make 5G LDPC decoders adaptable to various code rates and block lengths, permutation modules are expected to be configurable and flexible to fit all lifting sizes. To further improve hardware efficiency, the permutation modules are required to process multiple frames of data in parallel when facing smaller lifting sizes.
B. Banyan Networks
The Banyan network, which is proposed in [11], can be used to perform cyclic shifting operations. It consists of 2 × 2 switches and can be constructed recursively, as shown in Fig. 1(a). In particular, a 2n × 2n Banyan network can be constructed by combining two 2n−1 × 2n−1 Banyan networks vertically and introducing one extra stage in the front as well as relevant shuffle modules.

Fig. 2. A 12 × 12 permutation network with Z = 10 and SV = 2 [10].
The size of Banyan networks can be modified to (3 × 2n) × (3 × 2n) by utilizing 3 × 3 switches [10]. One example is given in Fig. 1(b), where the size of the network is 12 × 12. To solve the blocking problems when input size Z is smaller than 3 × 2n, a bypass network is introduced [10]. Specifically, suppose the expected shift value is SV, the original network shifts the data by SV while the bypass network shifts the data by 3 × 2n − Z + SV. In addition, a multiplexer (MUX) stage is required to select from the two groups of shifted data to obtain the final results. An example is given in Fig. 2, where a 12 × 12 network is used to shift ten input data by two [10].
III. PERMUTATION NETWORK FOR 5G LDPC DECODERS
A. Architecture
The permutation network (PM × PM) in the form of Fig. 2 [10] can perform arbitrary cyclic shift SV on input data of any size Z as long as 0 ≤ SV < Z ≤ PM, which means the network with PM = 384 can be well deployed in 5G LDPC decoders since values of lifting sizes Z range from 2 to 384. However, it can only shift one frame of data at one time, which is inefficient when Z is much smaller than the maximal lifting size. As pointed out by [10], to support the parallel shifting of multiple frames of data, all 2 × 2 switches in the first stage of a PM × PM Banyan network can be set to bar states to split into two subnetworks of size PM/2×PM/2, so that two frames of data (Z < PM/2) can be processed in parallel. The subnetworks can be recursively split in the same manner to obtain higher parallelism. Generally, a (3 × 2n) × (3 × 2n) Banyan network in [10] can be split into at most 2n−1 6 × 6 Banyan networks to shift submatrices with Z ≤ 6. For example, by setting all 2 × 2 switches in the first stage in a 12 × 12 Banyan network in Fig. 1(b) into bar states, the network is equal to two 6 × 6 Banyan networks, as illustrated in Fig. 3(a).

The proposed PM × PM permutation network (PM = 3 × 2n) consists of N = n + 2 stages, including N − 1 stages in two PM ×PM Banyan networks and one MUX stage. For 2 × 2 switches, 0 or 1 represents bar state or cross state. For 3 × 3 switches, 00, 01, and 10 refers to states that shift data by 0, 1, and 2, respectively. The MUX stage consists of PM 2-to-1 MUX’s that are controlled by CN. The value 0 or 1 of CN[k] indicates the k-th MUX selects the message from the original Banyan network or from the bypass one. C. The Proposed Mapping Scheme
As aforementioned, the control signal Ck for the k-th stage of the proposed permutation network (k = 2, . . . , N − 1) is determined by SV%(3 × 2N−k−1) (Alg. 1), which introduces N − 2 modulo operations in control modules, resulting in a
longer critical path as PM increases. To solve this problem, we propose a mapping scheme between the shift value SV
and its binary representation, indicated by SVb, to remove all the modulo operations in the control modules since the binary
representation SVb is well designed so that it can be used as the result of SV%(3 × 2N−k−1) without computation.
Let ri refer to a set of all possible results of SV%(3 × 2i) and bi be the proposed binary representation of ri (i = 0, 1, 2, . . . , N − 3). For SV%3, we have r0 = {0, 1, 2}. Let b0 refer to {00, 01, 10} which are normal binary values of r0. We show that any bk (k > 0) can be constructed recursively

based on b0: bk is generated by adding 0 or 1 to bk−1 as the most significant bit. Thus, the mapping between ri and bi is bijective. For example, r1 = {0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5} and b1 = {000, 001, 010, 100, 101, 110}. Note that b1 is equal to b0 if the most significant bit of each element in b1 is removed. Each element of r1 is mapped to exactly one element of b1. Therefore, bi can be used as the result of SV%(3 × 2i) though its binary values may not be equal to ri.
Generally, for the permutation network with PM = 3×2n, let SVb = bn so that bn−j can be obtained by removing the most significant j bits of bn. In addition, the mapping between SV and SVb is also bijective, thus no modulo operation is required. As an example, Table II illustrates the proposed mapping
between SV (0-23) and SVb for the permutation network with PM = 24. Suppose SV = 21, without the mapping scheme, the control module needs to calculate 21%12 = 9, 21%6 = 3, and 21%3 = 0. Fortunately, according to the mapping scheme, the shift value 21 can be represented by SVb = 11100, and the results of modulo operations can be obtained by removing
the most significant bits of SVb, which are 1100, 100, and 00, respectively.
For the permutation network for 5G LDPC decoder (PM = 384 = 3 × 27), the new binary representation of SV (0-383) is SVb = b7 which has 9 bits. Consider that the normal binary representation of SV also has 9 bits, the employment
of SVb requires no extra bits and removes all 7 modulo operations. Then the control modules only need regular decoders to
transform sel and SV into corresponding Ci (i = 1, 2, . . . , N).

IV. IMPLEMENTATION AND COMPARISON As in Table III, we synthesize the proposed 384 × 384 permutation network for 5G LDPC decoders with TSMC 90-nm 1.0 V technology. It achieves 660 MHz frequency and shifts at most 128 frames of data in parallel (Z = 2, 3). To our best knowledge, the existing permutation network for 5G LDPC codes is the extended barrel shifter in [3], which also supports all lifting sizes in 5G LDPC codes. But its control logic requires four ROMs (127.4k bytes) to store control signals. As no ASIC result is given in [3] and memory dominates hardware cost, we estimate its area by synthesizing the aforementioned ROMs with SMIC memory compiler, which shows that our design achieves 45.3% area reduction compared with [3]. We synthesize a 96 × 96 permutation network to compare with other works with equal size. Among them, the proposed one achieves the highest clock frequency of 770 MHz due to the mapping scheme which shortens its critical path. The area complexity in term of gate count, however, has 22.4% and 40% increment compared with [10] and [12], respectively. The increased area complexity mainly comes from the control module of the MUX stage as a trade-off for 6 parallelism options. To show the high throughput of the proposed 96 × 96 permutation network, we compare it with state-of-the-art one [10], as shown in Table IV. The proposed permutation network achieves 28.3% higher throughput than [10] when Z = 96, 48, and 24 due to the superiority in clock frequency which is introduced by the mapping scheme. When Z ≤ 12, the throughput of [10] degrades due to its limited parallelism. The increment of throughput of the proposed one rises to 156%, 413%, and 926%, respectively, due to the higher parallelism introduced by the improved control scheme.

V. CONCLUSION
Based on variation Banyan networks in [10], a 384 × 384 permutation network for 5G LDPC decoders is implemented, which supports all the lifting sizes Z in 5G standards for LDPC codes. The proposed permutation network has eight parallelism options and can process at most 128 frames of data in parallel, which is promising for higher throughput and hardware efficiency of 5G LDPC decoders.
