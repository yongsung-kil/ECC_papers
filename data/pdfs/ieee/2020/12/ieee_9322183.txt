Abstract—Mismatch between the true channel and the channel assumed in the design of a communication system can degrade system performance. Joint channel estimation and data recovery via the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm can overcome this problem. Though stable, the EM algorithm can be complex to implement and can exhibit slow convergence. We present approximations of the EM algorithm that reduce computational complexity and accelerate convergence for LDPC-coded discrete memoryless channels. In place of the exact bit-wise maximum a posteriori probability decoder in the E step of the EM algorithm, we use the sum-product decoder and min-sum decoder. In the M step, we use hard information instead of soft information to estimate the channel parameter. In order to evaluate the approximations in a practical channel mismatch scenario, we use a flash memory channel described by a quantized NormalLaplace mixture model. The simulation results demonstrate that the approximations in the E step can estimate the true channel with low-complexity decoding and those in the M step can accelerate the convergence with reduced measurement precision.
I. INTRODUCTION
Reliable communication under unknown channel conditions has been studied from both theoretical and practical perspectives. For a discrete memoryless channel (DMC), the capacity can be attained using a maximum empirical mutual information (MMI) decoder [1]. The MMI decoder does not depend on the probability law of the channel, hence it can, in principle, realize reliable communication on unknown DMCs. However, the complexity of the MMI decoder makes it infeasible for practical applications.
As mentioned in [1], channel uncertainty is an obstacle for commercial applications such as underwater acoustics and mobile wireless communications. In order to estimate an unknown channel without transmission of known symbols, the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm has been used [2], [3]. The EM algorithm is an iterative method to obtain maximum likelihood estimates of channel parameters [4], [5]. In these applications, the parameters to be estimated are often the impulse response of an inter-symbol interference (ISI) channel and the variance of Gaussian noise.
Flash memory is another application in which channel uncertainty degrades reliable communication. In over-stressed situations, the distribution of the channel outputs can be modeled using the Normal-Laplace mixture model, proposed in [6], which reflects the non-Gaussian characteristics of the

flash memory channel. Although ISI causes errors in flash memories, there are effective ISI cancellation techniques, as in [7]. For this reason, it is common to model flash memory as a memoryless channel characterized by the Normal-Laplace mixture model.
Methods to estimate the channel parameter in the context of coded flash memories have been studied recently. The potential of the EM algorithm for joint estimation and decoding of low-density parity-check (LDPC)-coded flash memory has been studied in [8]. Another method to estimate the channel parameter based on syndrome information of LDPC codes has been studied in [9], [10].
This paper focuses on approximations of the EM algorithm in terms of code structures, complexity reduction, and convergence acceleration. The exact EM algorithm requires a bit-wise maximum a posteriori probability (MAP) decoder, which can be complex to implement. The sum-product (SP) decoding of LDPC codes is a good approximation of the bitwise MAP decoding, however the quality of the approximation depends on code structures. For example, if the Tanner graph of an LDPC code contains short cycles in order to increase the minimum distance for a low error floor, the SP decoding of the LDPC code does not approximate the bit-wise MAP decoding as well as for an LDPC code with long cycles. Therefore, two LDPC codes with different girths are compared. Simulation results indicate that the SP decoder for LDPC codes with short cycles still works well for the EM algorithm, but performance can be affected when other types of approximations, as described next, are introduced.
For low complexity and fast convergence, first, the min-sum (MS) decoder is used in place of the SP decoder to reduce complexity. The MS decoder is an approximation of the SP decoder, thus we evaluate how the approximation degrades performance of estimation by the EM algorithm in this paper. Second, hard information is used instead of soft information to estimate the channel parameter to accelerate convergence. Since hard information is obtained by approximating soft information, we investigate whether the approximation accelerates the slow convergence of the EM algorithm and how the approximation affects the performance of the estimation. The results of simulations demonstrate that the approximated EM algorithms even with the MS decoder can estimate the true channel far from a pre-estimated channel with practi-

E. Approximations of the EM Algorithm
The exact bit-wise MAP decoding required in the E step can be implemented with polynomial complexity by the SP decoder when the code has a cycle-free Tanner graph. However, Tanner graphs of linear codes for practical applications have cycles, hence the exact bit-wise MAP decoding is infeasible. Fortunately, the SP decoder can be a good approximation of the exact bit-wise MAP decoder even for Tanner graphs with cycles. The quality of the approximation depends on the length and number of cycles. Therefore, we evaluate how the quality affects the performance of the EM algorithm for coded DMCs by comparing the SP decoders of LDPC codes with girth 6 and girth 8.
To reduce decoding complexity, the MS decoder is often used instead of the SP decoder. The box-plus operation for check nodes in the SP decoder is approximated as

In summary, we evaluate the performance of the approximated E step of the EM algorithm in terms of LDPC code structures and reduced-complexity calculations. This provides new insights regarding the application of the EM algorithm to unknown coded DMCs.

A. Characterization of Channels
Flash memory is composed of cells which store electric charge. The charge is measured by threshold voltage, denoted by v. The flash memory cells are characterized by the distribution of v given that the value written into the cell is w, denoted by p(v|w). In this paper, we consider so-called MLC cells which can store one of 4 values, denoted w ∈ {E, A, B, C}. The cells are damaged every time charge is injected into and ejected from them, operations called program and erase, respectively. After repeated program and erase (P/E) cycles, p(v|w) becomes distorted. For example, Fig. 2 shows the distortions in the Normal-Laplace mixture model [6] due to extreme P/E cycling. Next, let us consider two metrics for distance. The first metric is the Hamming distance dH(xˆs, x) between hard decisions of the log-APP ratios xˆs = (θ(¯l1s), θ(¯l2s), . . . , θ(¯lns )) and the correct codeword x. Two decoding trials at 9.6L P/E cycles are evaluated in Fig. 7: one is for the SP decoder and the other is for the MS decoder. Since all Hamming distances finally reach 0, both SP and MS decodings with both soft and hard EM algorithms succeed. As s increases, the Hamming distances decrease but sometimes remain flat and oscillate up and down. Fig. 8 shows the development of DKL(ϵs, ϵNL(cL)) in the same trials as those in Fig. 7. The KL divergences decrease almost monotonically as s increases. Both metrics show that the hard EM algorithm decreases the distance between an estimated channel parameter and the true one faster than the soft EM algorithm does.
IV. CONCLUSION
We have shown that approximated EM algorithms can successfully estimate unknown LDPC-coded DMCs. In the E step, the bit-wise MAP decoder is replaced with the SP or the MS decoder. These approximations reduce decoder complexity. In the M step, the logistic sigmoid function is replaced with the Heaviside step function. This approximation, called hard EM algorithm, accelerates the convergence. Simulation results on a practical flash memory channel model confirm that, with practical computational complexity, the approximated EM algorithms can estimate the true channel fast and well enough to provide almost the same decoding performance as the optimal LLR table.
In simulations, two restricted situations are considered to study channel mismatches. One is to fix the initial read voltages for read operations, and the other is to use only the default LLR table calculated from the initial channel parameter. Even with such extreme restrictions, the approximated EM algorithms can estimate the true channel, and hence they could eliminate the need to perform measurements of stressed flash memories in order to make optimal LLR tables for various stressed conditions.
Our results show that the approximated EM algorithms can work very well even for LDPC codes with short cycles. However, the hard EM algorithm does not achieve optimal

performance in certain channel mismatch situations. Hence,
one direction for future research is to find improved M step
approximations. Studying approximated EM algorithms using
other classes of codes could further clarify the source of
the performance degradation. We leave these topics and the
investigation of other factors contributing the performance to
future work.
