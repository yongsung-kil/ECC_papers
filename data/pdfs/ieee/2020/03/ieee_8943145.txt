Counting Short Cycles in Bipartite Graphs: A Fast Technique/Algorithm and a Hardness Result
Ali Dehghan and Amir H. Banihashemi , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract— In this paper, we propose a new technique, based

on the so-called breadth-first search algorithm, to count the short

cycles of a bipartite graph. For a general bipartite graph with

|V | nodes and girth g, our technique has a time complexity of O(|V |2Δ) to count g-cycles and (g + 2)-cycles, and a time complexity of O(|V |2Δ2) to count (g + 4)-cycles, where Δ is

has a lower complexity for counting g-cycles and (g + 2)-cycles.

It also has a lower complexity for counting (g + 4)-cycles in

bi-regular graphs and in scenarios where g is increased with the

size of the graph. Related to the problem of counting short cycles,

I. INTRODUCTION
B IPARTITE graphs are often used in science and engineering to represent systems, where the nodes on one side of the bipartition represent variables and the nodes on the other side are representative of local constraints involving their adjacent variables. One example of such representation is the Tanner graph of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, where the variable nodes represent code symbols and the constraint nodes represent the parity-check equations. In graphical representation of systems and codes, the cycle distribution of the graph often plays an important role. For example, the performance of iterative decoding algorithms, commonly used to decode LDPC codes, depends highly on the cycle distribution of the Tanner graph [1]–[10]. In this context, shorter cycles whose lengths are equal to or close to the girth g of the graph are the most influential. As an

Due to the close relationship between the performance of LDPC codes and the cycle distribution of Tanner graphs, there has been a significant amount of research in the coding community on understanding this relationship [1], [3], [4], [6]–[9], on the girth and cycle distribution of Tanner graphs [11], [12], and on devising fast algorithms that can enumerate/count the short cycles of a Tanner graph [3], [13], [14].
Generally, counting cycles of a given length in a Tanner graph is known to be NP-hard [15]. The fastest known algorithm for counting short cycles (of length k in the range g ≤ k ≤ 2g − 2) in a general bipartite graph is the message-passing algorithm of [14].1 This algorithm has a time complexity of O(g|E|2) or O(g|V |2Δ2), where |V |, |E| and Δ are the number of nodes, the number of edges and the maximum degree of the nodes in the graph, respectively. (For a comprehensive review of previous works in this area, the reader is referred to [14].) In related work, Blake and Lin computed the multiplicity of cycles of length g in bi-regular bipartite graphs as a function of the spectrum of the graph plus the extra information about the degree sequences of the graph [17]. The computation of the cycle multiplicity as a function of graph spectrum and degree sequences was then extended to cycles of larger size (up to 2g − 2) in [18], while it was demonstrated in [19] that a similar extension to cycles of size larger than 2g − 2 or to irregular graphs does not exist, in general. The complexity of the computations of [17], [18], through finding the graph spectrum, is O(|V |3).
Quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes are a popular sub-category of LDPC codes, that are particularly important due to their simple implementations in practice. The fastest algorithm for counting short cycles of Tanner graphs with QC structure is that of Karimi and Banihashemi [13], which has a running time of O(N |Eb|3), where |Eb| is the number of edges in the base graph and N is the lifting degree. The algorithm of [13] is based on the relationship between the number of short cycles and the eigenvalues of the directed edge matrix of the graph, and compared to the message-passing algorithm of [14] with complexity O(gN 2|Eb|2), is less complex if gN grows faster than |Eb|. In this paper, we present a new algorithm/technique to

count the number of short cycles (with length k in the range

g ≤ k ≤ 2g − 2) in a general bipartite graph. To develop

the proposed technique, we modify the so-called breadth-first

search (BFS) algorithm, commonly used to find the distances

between a given node (root or source node) and the rest of

the nodes in a graph. The modification is to keep track of the

parents and the children of all the nodes at different depth

levels of the graph (corresponding to different distances to

the root). We then use this information to calculate the cycle

multiplicities through the combinatorial analysis of different

paths between the root and the nodes at different distances

from the root. The complexity of the proposed algorithm for
general bipartite graphs is O(|V |2Δ) to count g-cycles and (g + 2)-cycles, and O(|V |2Δ2) to count (g + 4)-cycles. For

the special category of bi-regular bipartite graphs, the latter complexity is reduced to O(|V |2Δ). Furthermore, for bipartite

graphs with QC structure, the complexity of counting g-cycles and (g + 2)-cycles is reduced to O(N |Eb|2/Δ), and that of counting (g + 4)-cycles is reduced to O(N |Eb|2).
Compared to the message-passing algorithm of [14], which has a complexity O(g|V |2Δ2), the proposed method

always has a lower complexity for counting g-cycles and

(g + 2)-cycles. It also has a lower complexity for counting

(g + 4)-cycles in the case of bi-regular bipartite graphs, and

in the case of general bipartite graphs where g increases with

the size of the graph. Note that the increase of g with the

size of the graph is common in the context of LDPC codes,

where one is often interested in cases for which the degree

distribution of the graph (code rate) is fixed, and the block

length n is increased. Under such circumstances, the girth of

the graph often increases with n. For example, if one uses the

so-called progressive-edge-growth (PEG) algorithm of [2] for

code construction, the girth increases logarithmically with n.

II. DEFINITIONS AND NOTATIONS
For a given graph G, we denote the node set and the edge set of G by V (G) and E(G), or V and E in brief, respectively. For a graph G with node set V and edge set E, we use the notation G = (V, E). We say that a graph G is simple if it has no loop or parallel edges. Throughout this paper, all graphs are assumed to be simple. The number of edges connected to a node v is called the degree of v, and is denoted by deg(v). Also, the maximum degree and the minimum degree of a graph G, denoted by Δ(G) and δ(G), are defined to be the maximum and the minimum degree of its nodes, respectively. A walk of length  in the graph G is a sequence of nodes v1, v2, . . . , v+1 in V (G) such that {vi, vi+1} ∈ E(G), for all i ∈ {1, . . . , }. A walk is called a closed walk if the two end nodes are identical. A path of length  in G, denoted by P, is a sequence of  + 1 distinct nodes v1, v2, . . . , v+1 in V (G), such that {vi, vi+1} ∈ E(G), for 1 ≤ i ≤ . A cycle of length , denoted by C, is a sequence of distinct nodes v1, v2, . . . , v in V (G) such that v1, v2, . . . , v form a path of length −1, and {v, v1} ∈ E(G). The length of the shortest cycle in a graph is called girth and is denoted by g.
A lollipop L(Ci, Pj) is a graph obtained by joining a cycle Ci with i ≥ 3 nodes to a pendant node of a path Pj, j ≥ 1, with j + 1 nodes. The lollipop L(C4, P2) is shown in Fig. 1. In any lollipop, there is a unique node of degree one, which we call the tail of the lollipop. Also, there is a unique node of degree three, which we call the head of the lollipop. Furthermore, the tail has a unique neighbor, which we refer to as the backbone. Note that in the lollipop L(Ci, P1), the backbone is also the head of the lollipop.
Let G = (V, E) be any graph, and let S ⊂ V be any subset of V . Then, the induced subgraph on the set S is the graph whose node set is S and whose edge set consists of all of the edges in E that have both endpoints in S.
A graph G = (V, E) is called bipartite, if the node set V can be partitioned into two disjoint subsets U and W , i.e., V = U ∪ W and U ∩ W = ∅, such that every edge in E connects a node from U to a node from W . Tanner graphs of LDPC codes are bipartite graphs, in which U and W are referred to as variable nodes and check nodes, respectively. Parameters n and m in this case are used to denote |U | and |W |, respectively.

Parameter n is the code’s block length and the code rate r satisfies r ≥ 1 − m/n. A bipartite graph G = (U ∪ W, E) is called bi-regular, if all the nodes on the same side of the bi-partition have the same degree, i.e., if all the nodes in U have the same degree du and all the nodes in W have the same degree dw. In this case, the graph is called (du, dw) bi-regular.
In a graph G, the distance between any two nodes v and u is the number of edges in a shortest path between them. Also, for every v ∈ V (G), notation N (v) is used to denote the neighbor set of v in G. Similarly, the set of nodes of G which has a neighbor in S, for some S ⊆ V (G), is denoted by N (S).
Protograph LDPC codes are constructed by lifting a smaller Tanner graph, called the base graph. We denote the base graph by Gb = (Vb, Eb). The lifted graph will then be assigned as the Tanner graph of the lifted protograph code. The lifting process consists of the base graph being copied N times, and a permutation being applied to each edge cluster associated with every edge of the base graph. Parameter N is called the lifting degree. Assume that the Tanner graph of the lifted code is G = (V, E). In this case, the parity-check matrix H of the lifted code is the bi-adjacency matrix of G. It is clear that we have |E| = N |Eb| and |V | = N |Vb|. When the permutations are cyclic, then the lifted code is a quasicyclic (QC) protograph code. In other words, QC-LDPC codes have parity-check matrices that consist of square blocks, where each square block has size N × N and is either a circulant permutation matrix (CPM), or a zero matrix. In particular, when the square block [H]ij =
 0, then [H]ij = Ipij , pij ∈ ZN = {0, . . . , N − 1}, where Ipij is a CPM whose rows are obtained by cyclically shifting the rows of the identity matrix to the left by pij.
Finally, for computational complexity discussions, we use the notation f (x) = O(g(x)), if for sufficiently large values of x, |f (x)| ≤ a|g(x)|, for some positive value a.
III. PROPOSED ALGORITHM/TECHNIQUE TO COUNT SHORT CYCLES
Our proposed technique for counting short cycles is based on a modification of the breadth-first search (BFS) algorithm. The BFS algorithm, described in Algorithm 1, is often used to find the distance of every node in a graph from a specific node in the graph, referred to as the source node or root. In Algorithm 1, the queue Q is a first-in first-out queue, and is responsible for sorting the nodes according to their distance to the root. The command “Q.enqueue(·)” (“Q.dequeue”) inserts (removes) a node to (from) the queue. The distance of the root (from itself) is set at zero. The distance of all the other nodes in the graph (from the root) is initially set at infinity, and then updated properly as the algorithm progresses. The algorithm first visits the neighbors of the root and then continues, in a recursive fashion, to visit the neighbors of the previously visited nodes until all the nodes in the graph (that are connected to the root) are visited. At each step of the algorithm, the distance of the nodes that are being visited for the first time is updated by the distance of their parent plus one. IV. COMPUTATIONAL COMPLEXITY OF THE PROPOSED TECHNIQUE/ALGORITHM
To calculate the multiplicity of short cycles for a given bipartite graph G based on the technique presented in previous subsections, we first need to find the girth of G. This can be done using the algorithm of Yuster and Zwick [20], which has a computational complexity of O(|V |2). The next step is to run the modification of BFS algorithm (Algorithm 2) on G starting from every node v of G as the source node. We can then find the multiplicities of g-cycles and (g +2)-cycles using Theorems 1 and 3, respectively. If we are also interested in calculating the multiplicity of (g + 4)-cycles using Theorem 5, for each edge (v, u) in G, we need to run Algorithm 2 on the graph G \ {v} with the source node u.
It is known that the time complexity of the original BFS algorithm (Algorithm 1) is O(|V | + |E|) [21]. In Algorithm 2, we run BFS algorithm twice. On the other hand, considering that the extra complexity of Algorithm 2 compared to Algorithm 1 (Lines 13–22 and 32–34) is O(1), we conclude

that the modified BFS algorithm also has a time complexity of

O(|V | + |E|). Moreover, for each visited node, the modified

BFS algorithm stores information about parents, children,

the number of parents, the distance from the source node,

as well as the two parameters a(·) and b(·). The number of

visited nodes in the modified BFS algorithm is upper bounded

by |V |. For each visited node, the number of memory elements

that are required to store the children and the parents is upper

bounded by 2Δ, where the size of each memory element is in

turn O(log(|V |)). Moreover, for each visited node, two mem-

ory elements with sizes O(log(Δ)) and O(log(g)) are required

to store the number of parents and the distance from the source

node, respectively. Finally, two more memory elements per

visited node, each with size O(g log(Δ)), are needed to store

the variables a(·) and b(·). Assuming that Δ log(|V |) grows

faster than g log(Δ), then the total memory requirement of the

modified BFS algorithm is O(Δ|V | log(|V |)).

The time complexity of calculating the multiplicity of g-cycles using Theorem 1 is O(|V |2Δ). This can be seen

by noticing that to use Theorem 1, one needs to run the

modified BFS algorithm at most |V | times, where each run

has a complexity O(|V | + |E|) or O(|V |Δ). The calculations of (4) are also of complexity O(|V |2).

The time complexity of calculating the multiplicity of

(g + 2)-cycles using Theorem 3 is dominated by the calcu-

lation of R1(G, v) and R2(G, v) using (8) and (10). These computations have complexity O(|V |Δ), and considering that

they will have to be performed for all nodes v of G, in total, the computational complexity is O(|V |2Δ).

The hardness of deciding whether for a given general graph G and integer k, the graph G has a cycle of length 2k, has

VII. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we proposed a new technique/algorithm to count the short cycles of a bipartite graph. The proposed technique is based on a modified breadth-first search (BFS) algorithm which establishes parent/child relationships between the nodes in the graph depending on their distance from the source node. Combinatorial equations were then established to derive the multiplicity of cycles using the information supplied by the algorithm. For a graph with |V | nodes and girth g, our technique has a time complexity of O(|V |2Δ) to count g-cycles and (g + 2)-cycles, and a time complexity of O(|V |2Δ2) to count (g + 4)-cycles, where Δ is the maximum node degree in the graph. The latter complexity is reduced to O(|V |2Δ) for the special category of bi-regular bipartite graphs. Compared to the fastest known algorithm, which has a complexity O(g|V |2Δ2), the proposed method always has a lower complexity for counting g-cycles and (g + 2)-cycles. It also has a lower complexity for counting (g + 4)-cycles in scenarios where g grows with the size of the graph and in the case of bi-regular bipartite graphs.

An important application of the results presented here is

to count the short cycles of Tanner graphs of low-density

parity-check (LDPC) codes. Numerical results were presented

to compare the speed of the proposed algorithm with that of

the fastest known message-passing algorithms for counting

the short cycles of finite-length LDPC codes. These results

showed that the proposed algorithm is significantly faster than

the message-passing algorithms, in some cases by more than

an order of magnitude.
