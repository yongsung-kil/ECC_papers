Layered Construction of Quasi-Cyclic LDPC Codes
Xiongfei Tao , Yue Xin, Bifang Wang, and Li Chang

Abstract— A layered construction of LDPC codes on 3-dimensional rectangular lattices is explored in this letter. By analyzing the governing conditions of in-layer polygons and inter-layer polygons, and elaborately selecting the slopes of inlayer and inter-layer lines, we remove all the 6-cycles as well as inter-layer 8-cycles which results in a class of LDPC codes without small trapping sets. We further remove the in-layer 8-cycles to design a class of LDPC codes with girth 10.

Index Terms— Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, layered construction, girth, trapping sets.

Fig. 1. Illustration of the construction based on a cubical lattice [4].

I. INTRODUCTION

L OW-DENSITY parity-check (LDPC) codes are linear block codes, their performances are close to the Shannon limit when they are decoded by the belief propagation (BP) algorithm. Tanner et al. studied the iterative algorithm on graphs, and pointed out that if there are small cycles in the Tanner graph of LDPC codes, it is not conducive to the convergence of the iterative algorithm. Therefore, the girth (length of the shortest cycle) is an important factor when constructing the LDPC codes.
The construction methods of LDPC codes can be divided into random structures and structured structures. For the random construction methods, Amirzade and Sadeghi [1] presented a method to obtain QC-LDPC codes with a given girth and the minimum lifting degree. This approach reduces the size of the search space and the complexity of the search algorithm. In terms of the structured construction of LDPC based on graph, Vasic et al. [2] presented a design of QCLDPC codes with girth 8 on 2-D rectangular lattice, where the lines and points correspond to variable nodes (VNs) and check nodes (CNs). The girth-8 property is achieved by elaborately selecting sets of parallel lines in 2-D lattice. Tao et al. [3] extended this method to construct QC-LDPC codes without small trapping sets (TSs). In [4], a design based on cuboid was proposed to construct (3, k) LDPC codes with girth 8, and this method can be considered as a construction on 3-D lattices. Besides this method, Zhang et al. proposed a new method based on combinatorial designs to construct high-girth

An (a, b) TS is a configuration of a VNs, and the subgraph induced by these VNs contains b CNs with odd-degrees. Moreover, if all the CNs in the induced subgraph have either degree one or two, the TS will be regarded as an elementary trapping set (ETS). Nguyen et al. [6] did researches on the topological relations between different types of ETSs of QCLDPC codes and established a database called trapping set ontology (TSO). In order to distinguish between heterogeneous (a, b) ETSs, Nguyen et al. indexed each representation using (a, b){i}. In [7], Amirzade et al. presented analytical lower bounds on the size of ETSs of variable-regular LDPC codes with any girth, g, and irregular ones with girth 8. And they presented an edge-coloring technique to characterize and avoid ETSs in the Tanner graph in [8].
In this letter, we present a layered construction of (3, k) LDPC codes. By analyzing the relationship between short cycles and small trapping sets of layered LDPC codes, two classes of (3, k) LDPC codes are constructed on 3-D rectangular lattices. The first class of the codes has girths of 8 and all the ETSs in the range of a ≤ 8 and b ≤ 3 are removed, and the second class of the codes is free of 8-cycles.
II. INSPIRATION OF LAYERED CONSTRUCTION ON LDPC CODES
A. Layered Construction Method
Tao et al. proposed a design of (3, k) LDPC codes based on cuboid [4]. Fig.1 shows an example of such geometry with k = 3. There are k3 points and 3k2 lines in the cubical lattice, which represent k3 VNs and 3k2 CNs. Since each VN is a common point of 3 lines and each line passes through k VNs, the graph corresponds to a 3k2×k3 parity-check matrix, whose column weight is 3 and row weight is k, and it results in a (k3, 3, k) LDPC code.

Inspired by the cube-based construction method, we explore a layered construction method of LDPC codes on 3-D rectangular lattices. We first consider the LDPC code constructed by the planar finite geometry method. There are 9 points and 6 lines on the top layer of the cubical lattice illustrated in Fig.1, and the 2-D lattice results in a (9, 2, 3) LDPC code. It can be intuitively observed that there are no triangles, but a number of quadrangles in the figure. Therefore, the LDPC code corresponding to this graphical structure has a girth of 8. Generally, if the size of the 2-D lattice is k by k, a (2, k) LDPC code with a length of k2 can be constructed.
Next, we extend the 2-D finite geometry into a 3-D space. Suppose that we have k parallel layers, we construct a (2, k) LDPC code with a length of k2, by a 2-D lattice with a size of k by k in the first layer as the method mentioned above, then we copy the geometric structure to the other k − 1 layers, and finally we draw inter-layer lines to connect the points on each layer. Every inter-layer line connects only one point on each layer and every two inter-layer lines have no common points. In other words, every point only exists on one interlayer line. The most convenient and straight way to construct the inter-layer lines is to construct k2 lines perpendicular to each layer passing through the k2 points on the first layer. Thus we obtain a 3-D lattice structure with k3 point and 3k2 lines illustrated in Fig. 1. The characteristic that every two interlayer lines do not share common points leads to the generated LDPC codes on 3-D lattices free of 4-cycles and 6-cycles. Therefore, a (3, k) girth-8 LDPC code with a length of k3 can be generated by this method.
In summary, the layered construction method has the following steps:
• Construct a geometric graph corresponding to a (2, k) LDPC code on the first layer;
• Copy the geometric graph to the other k − 1 layers; • Construct inter-layer lines to connect the points on each
layer, and make sure that every point only exists on one inter-layer line.
With this construction method, a (3, k) LDPC code can be easily constructed.
B. Characteristic of Layered Construction Codes
Based on this construction method, there are two types of cycles in the generated codes: in-layer cycles and inter-layer cycles. Due to every two inter-layer lines sharing no common points, there are no inter-layer 4-cycles and 6-cycles.
Lemma 1: Any inter-layer 8-cycle only exists between two different layers.
Proof: Any 8-cycle corresponds to a quadrangle which is formed by 4 points and 4 lines. Suppose an inter-layer 8-cycle with 4 points existing in more than 2 layers, there must be at least 1 layer which has only 1 point of these 4 points. Since this point is in an inter-layer 8-cycle, it must connect with another two points on different layers via two lines, which means that this point exists on two different inter-layer lines. It is contrary to the condition of the construction method.

In order to construct the LDPC codes with girth 8 without (a ≤ 8, b ≤ 3) ETSs by layered construction method, an effective method is to eliminate in-layer 4-cycles, in-layer 6-cycles, and inter-layer 8-cycles.

III. LAYERED CONSTRUCTION OF LDPC CODE WITH GIRTH 8 WITHOUT SMALL ETSS
A. Construction of (2, k) LDPC Code With Girth 8
The first step of the layered construction method is to construct a geometric graph corresponding to a (2, k) LDPC code in the first layer. In order to construct the LDPC codes with girth 8 by layered construction method, a (2, k) girth-8 LDPC code has to be designed in the first place. The method shown in Fig. 1 is not flexible in constructing (2, k) girth-8 LDPC codes with variable code length, thus the design method in [2] is employed in this letter.
Vasic et al. constructed Tanner graphs based on 2-D lattices [2]. A 2-D rectangular lattice is defined as a set of points, L = {(x, y) : 0 ≤ x ≤ k − 1, 0 ≤ y ≤ q − 1}. Starting at the point (0, a), a line with slope s contains k points, {(x, a + s · x mod q) : 0 ≤ x ≤ k − 1}. If q is limited, the rectangular lattice is cyclic which means that the lattice can be regarded as a cylinder. An example of a rectangular lattice with k = 5 and q = 7 is shown in Fig. 4.
In [2], the lines and points correspond to VNs and CNs, in contrast, the lines and the points are regarded as CNs and VNs respectively. All the lines starting at the q points of x = 0 with the same slope is a class of parallel lines, since we are going to construct LDPC codes with column weight 2, there are 2 classes of parallel lines corresponding to 2 different slopes, which are s = 0, and 1 in this letter. V. CONCLUSION
In this letter, we propose a layered construction of LDPC codes on 3-D rectangular lattices. Vasic’s method is employed to construct in-layer geometries corresponding to a (2, k) code with girth 8 and the slopes of inter-layer lines are carefully selected to remove inter-layer quadrangles, which leads to a class of QC-LDPC codes without small TSs. We then adopt the Golomb ruler to further eliminate in-layer quadrangle to design a class of LDPC codes with girth 10. Both classes of the codes can be generated in 3-D rectangular lattices with deterministic width q.
