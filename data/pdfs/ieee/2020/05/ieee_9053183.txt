BIPARTITE BELIEF PROPAGATION POLAR DECODING WITH BIT-FLIPPING
Zihao Gong, Yifei Shen, Houren Ji, Wenqing Song, Zaichen Zhang, Xiaohu You, and Chuan Zhang∗
Lab of Efficient Architectures for Digital-communication and Signal-processing (LEADS) National Mobile Communications Research Laboratory, Southeast University Chien-Shiung Wu College, Nanjing, China Purple Mountain Laboratories, Nanjing, China Email: {zhgong, yfshen, chzhang}@seu.edu.cn

ABSTRACT
For the scenarios with high throughput requirements, the belief propagation (BP) decoding is one of the most promising decoding strategies for polar codes. By pruning the redundant variable nodes (VNs) and check nodes (CNs) in the original factor graph, the graph is condensed to a sparse bipartite graph which is similar to the graph for low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. In this paper, we introduce the bit-flipping scheme into the LDPC-like BP (L-BP) decoding and propose two methods to identify the error-prone VNs. By additional decoding attempts, the L-BP flip (L-BPF) decoding improves the error-rate performance with a similar average complexity for high Eb/N0 values. The simulation results show that the L-BPF decoding achieves 0.25 dB gain compared with the L-BP decoding.
Index Terms— Polar codes, belief propagation, bitflipping, factor graph
1 Introduction
Polar codes, proposed by Arıkan, are proved to be the first error-correcting codes that can achieve the Shannon capacity theoretically under the successive cancellation (SC) decoding [1]. Unfortunately, the SC decoding suffers from poor error-rate performance and high latency for finite code length. Afterwards, the successive cancellation list (SCL) decoding is proposed to enhance the performance [2]. As the list size increases, the SCL decoding can approach the maximum likelihood (ML) performance.
However, the SC-based decoding procedures render poor throughput due to their serial nature. For the practical scenarios with high throughput requirements, the belief propagation (BP) decoding [3] is more promising owing to the inherently parallel procedure. Moreover, compared with the SCbased decoding, the BP decoding could provide soft information iteratively, which is applicable for iterative decoding and detection in practice.

For the conventional BP decoding proposed in [3], messages are propagated over the factor graph based on the generator matrix1. Even though the BP decoding can outperform the SC decoding regarding both error-correction performance [4] and throughput [5], its performance cannot compete with the SCL decoding. To overcome this issue, a series of methods is proposed to optimize the BP decoding by processing the log-likelihood ratios [6–9] and selecting the optimal permutated factor graphs [10–12]. On the other hand, several works have tried to convert the original factor graph to a bipartite graph inspired by the low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, so that the research on the LDPC BP decoding can be explored to enhance the polar BP performance. Following the description in [13], we call this method as the LDPC-like BP (L-BP) decoding. In [14], the bipartite graph is mapped from the parity matrix directly, which degrades the performance significantly compared to the BP decoding due to the dense connection. Exploring the features of the message propagation, the authors of [13] invented the L-BP procedure by pruning redundant variable nodes (VNs) and check nodes (CNs). They successfully condensed the original BP factor graph to a sparse bipartite graph with slight performance loss. Furthermore, the polar construction is discussed for the L-BP decoding [15].
The L-BP decoding makes it possible to apply advanced methods in LDPC to polar codes and design a uniform decoder which supports both LDPC and polar codes. However, due to the slight changes of scheduling, the L-BP decoding in [13] is still inferior to the BP decoding and cannot be satisfactory to real application. Inspired by the bit-flipping [9,16,17], we propose the L-BP flip (L-BPF) decoding algorithm in this paper, which can be applicable to arbitrary construction methods (e.g., [18, 19]). The L-BPF decoding tries extra multiple attempts of L-BP decoding by flipping unreliable VNs if the cyclic redundancy check (CRC) is not satisfied. Two schemes are proposed to identify the error-prone VNs, based on the knowledge of the unsatisfied CNs and the output LLRs. Experimental results show that the proposed algorithm can ob-

3 LDPC-LIKE BP DECODING WITH BIT-FLIPPING

In this section, the L-BPF decoding is proposed. The framework is plotted to illustrate the procedure. Also, the CN-based identification (CNI) and the hybrid identification (HI) methods are proposed to select unreliable VNs.
3.1 L-BPF Decoding
For polar codes, the strategy of the bit-flipping is first applied to the SC decoding [21], which is called the SC flip (SCF) decoding. Also, the BP flip (BPF) is proposed in [9], which enhances the performance by assigning both “+∞” and “−∞” as the initial LLR for the identified error-prone bit.
In this paper, we employ the bit-flipping onto the L-BP decoding by flipping unreliable VNs. Fig. 2 illustrates the procedure of the L-BPF decoding. After the first-round L-BP decoding, the CRC detection is performed to evaluate the estimation. If the CRC check is satisfied, it is considered as “correctness”, and the decoding is terminated. 3.2 CNI Method for VN Identification
During the decoding, if a CN fails to satisfy its corresponding check-sum constraint, it is labeled as an unsatisfied CN. Similar to the trapping set in LDPC decoding [17, 22], the error VNs can also construct trapping set in L-BP polar decoding, where the set of the error VNs is denoted as Ve. In the trapping set, if there exist unsatisfied CNs, they can provide the information to help the VN identification. According to the parity check constraint, for an unsatis∩fied CN c, there must be at least one error VN in Vc, i.e., Ve Vc ̸= ∅. To this end, the CNI method is illuminated by this fact.
After the first-round L-BP decoding, all unsatisfied CNs can be recognized and form a set Cu. All VNs associated with the CNs in Cu generate a set named ΓCNI, which is the flip set Γ. If the parity check fails, the CNI method can ensure that the set Γ must cover at least one erroneous VN . A case is illustrated in the bipartite graph module in Fig. 2, where the red CNs are unsatisfied, and the black VNs are identified to be flipped. For the CNI method, the size of the flip set is changing for different input LLRs.

5 Conclusion
In this work, the L-BPF decoding is proposed to enhance the performance of the L-BP decoding by introducing bitflipping. Also, the CNI and HI methods are proposed to identify the error-prone VNs. Experimental results show that the L-BPF decoding outperforms both BP and L-BP decoding with the similar average complexity at high SNRs.
