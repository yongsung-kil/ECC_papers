Stopping Criterion for LDPC Decoder based on PEXIT Chart Analysis

Taehyun Kim, JooSung Park Samsung Electronics Co., Ltd. Suwon-si, Gyeonggi-do, 16677, Korea Email: {th8978.kim, jsjoo.park}@samsung.com

Jun Heo Korea University Seoul, 02841, Korea Email: junheo@korea.ac.kr

Abstract—In this paper, an early stopping scheme is proposed for low-density parity-check (LDPC) decoders. The proposed scheme predicts that the LDPC decoder will not be able to correct errors and then stops the iterative decoding process before reaching the maximum number of iterations. When the number of parity checks unsatisfied at both the l-th and the (l − T )-th decoding iterations is larger than a threshold, the decoder with the proposed scheme predicts that the decoding of the codeword is failed at the last iteration. Protograph-based extrinsic information transfer (PEXIT) chart analysis is used to determine the threshold. Simulation results show that the LDPC decoder can correct errors with the lower average number of iterations by using both the conventional syndrome test and proposed scheme.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes were proposed by Gallager in 1962 [1]. Ever since Mackay rediscovered LDPC codes [2], LDPC codes have been used in various communication standards and are adopted for enhanced mobile broadband (eMBB) scenario in 5G new radio (NR) [3]. Generally, LDPC decoding is represented as an iterative processing between variable nodes and check nodes. The messages exchanged between variable nodes and check nodes are computed as repeatedly as the predetermined number of times which is called the maximum number of iterations. The updated messages at each iteration are used to determine the transmitted message. However, depending on the channel state, the error correction may fail even though the maximum number of updating processes has been performed. In conventional communication systems, the cyclic redundancy check (CRC) is used to confirm the existence of errors in the decoded message. If an error correction failure is predicted during the LDPC decoding, the unnecessary computation can be reduced by terminating the iterative processing in advance.
Depending on the kind of information used for criterion, the stopping schemes for undecodable case can be divided into two groups. The schemes involved in the first group use a posteriori probability (APP). The scheme proposed in [4] observed the change in the magnitude of the APP-log-likelihood ratio (APPLLR) in consecutive iterations. The stopping criterion stops the decoding process if the sum of magnitude does not change or decreases. In [5], if the number of cases that the change of mean magnitude is less than a threshold is more than a certain number, the decoder determines decoding failure. Similarly,

the scheme in [6] observed the mean of APP, and determined decoding failure when the mean is less than that at the previous iteration. The drawback of the schemes using mean value is that the process for calculating mean value is complicated. Therefore, the scheme based on the variation of the sign and magnitude of each APP-LLR [7] were introduced.
The schemes in the second group predict decoding failure based on the parity check. Since the number of parity checks is less than the number of variable nodes, the schemes based on the parity check are less complicated than APP-based schemes. The scheme proposed in [8] focused on the number of satisfied parity checks. When the number of satisfied parity checks converges, the decoder stops decoding process. Similarly, the schemes in [9] and [10] used the number of unsatisfied parity checks. They stop the decoding process when the number keeps the same as that at the previous iteration [9] or higher than thresholds [10]. Recently, a simple early stopping scheme using the LLR test on the single parity check was proposed in [11].
In this paper, we propose an early stopping scheme based on protograph-based extrinsic information transfer (PEXIT) chart [12] analysis. When the number of parity checks unsatisfied at both the l-th and the (l − T )-th iterations is higher than a threshold, we consider that the corresponding codeword is undecodable. The major cost of the scheme is the additional memory required for storing the syndrome vectors. In order to reduce the cost, the scheme with partial parity checks is also presented. The threshold for the proposed scheme is determined from the information-theoretic analysis which is called PEXIT chart, while most of the previous schemes have experimentally determined thresholds.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. In Section II, we briefly review the decoding algorithm of LDPC codes. Section III introduces the proposed early stopping and threshold decision scheme. The error correction performance and complexity caused by early stopping schemes are discussed in Section IV. Finally, Section V concludes this paper.

III. EARLY STOPPING CRITERION BASED ON PEXIT CHART ANALYSIS
In this section, a new early stopping scheme is introduced. First, by showing the comparison of parity check results obtained in the l-th iteration and the (l − T )-th iteration, we show that the decoding failure can be predicted through the proposed scheme. The threshold for the stopping criterion should be determined appropriately so as not to cause performance degradation while reducing the number of iterations as much as possible. We propose a threshold decision scheme based on information-theoretic analysis for LDPC codes. Also, we present a method to reduce the complexity and memory requirements of the proposed stopping scheme at the last of this section.

In contrast to previous schemes, the most significant cost of VUPC-T is the additional memory required to store the previous syndrome vectors. Because the syndrome vector is a binary vector of length M , additional M bit memory is required to store it. Since the stored syndrome vector at the l-th iteration is used after T iterations, it should be stored in the memory until the end of the (l + T )-th iteration and then erased from the memory after decoding failure determination. Therefore, the total amount of the additional memory required for VUPC-T is M · T . Since the number of parity checks M depends on the code rate R and code length N , the LDPC codes with lower code rate or longer code length require more memory. We present a solution to reduce the memory requirement for practical usage at the end of this section.

C. Threshold Decision Method
Threshold is determined using a PEXIT chart, which is an analysis tool for protograph-based and multi-edge type LDPC codes. Since the NR LDPC codes have several degree-one variable nodes and punctured nodes, the PEXIT chart is very suitable for analyzing the NR LDPC codes. Assume that there

is an LDPC code that is defined by a Mp × Np base matrix B. Hereafter, ”type-i check nodes” means the check nodes positioned at the i-th row of the base matrix, and ”type-j variable nodes” means the variable nodes positioned at the jth column of the base matrix. D. Early Stopping with Partial Parity Checks
The additional memory requirement, which is the primary cost of the VUPC-T scheme, is proportional to the number of observed parity checks and the observation interval. To reduce the memory requirements of the VUPC-T scheme, we observe a fraction of parity checks for decoding failure prediction instead of observing full parity checks. We denote the scheme based on the variation of unsatisfied partial parity check as VUPPC-T . The reason for selecting the observed parity check rather than the observation interval to reduce memory requirement is that it also reduces the computational complexity.
Because the parity checks observed for VUPPC-T are different from that in the VUPC-T , the new threshold deter-

mination formula is required. The threshold for VUPPC-T is calculated as follows:

IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
Simulations are performed over an additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel with binary phase-shift keying (BPSK) modulation. For the comparison with the VUPPC-T , we simulate previously proposed early stopping schemes. The scheme based on the convergence of mean magnitude (CMM) [5] is chosen among the schemes using information related to the variable node and the schemes based on the checksum variation pattern (CSVP) [9] is selected among the schemes related to the parity check. The observation interval T in proposed VUPPC-T is set as 3. In the simulations, all schemes are used with the syndrome test represented in Section II. In order to show the effect of the stopping criterion for the undecodable case, we also simulate the performance of the conventional LDPC decoding algorithm which only has the syndrome test (ST) for early stopping.
We simulate several practical LDPC codes with different lengths and rates. NR LDPC codes with various information bit length (260 and 520) and code rates (1/2, 1/3 and 1/4) are used to evaluate the performance. The decoding algorithm is the sum-product algorithm, and the maximum number of iterations is 30.

B. Computational Complexity Analysis
In order to verify the practicality, the additional complexity caused by the operations added to perform early stopping should be considered. To compare the computational complexity, the number of operations for each scheme is first identified. The operations commonly used in all schemes are addition and comparison.
Let Nadd denotes the number of additive operations, and Ncomp denotes the number of comparative operations. Because of the different complexity of each operation, it is not fair to compare the total number of operations. Therefore, the computational complexity of each scheme considering the complexity of each operation is
CESC = Wadd · Nadd + Wcomp · Ncomp + Cetc, (13)
where Wadd and Wcomp are the weight of operations. The complexity caused by the other operations is represented as Cetc which is a different value for each scheme, and Cetc is also obtained by the product of the weight and the number of operations. In this paper, the weights are determined by using the execution time of operation. To reduce measurement errors, a sufficient number of operations are performed to obtain an average execution time. From the simulation, we confirm that the execution time for the comparative operation is similar to

that for additive operation. For simplicity, we set Wadd and Wcomp as 1, and the weight for the other operations is the normalized execution time.
Figure 4 shows the computational complexity of each stopping scheme. As we mentioned before, CMM, which is an APP-based scheme, shows higher complexity than parity check based schemes. In comparison to CSVP, the proposed VUPPC-T shows much lower complexity due to partial observation. The difference in complexity between CMM and VUPPC-T increases as the code rate of LDPC codes decreases, and the difference between CSVP and VUPPC-T increases as the number of parity checks increases.
V. CONCLUSION
An early stopping scheme for the prediction of LDPC decoding failure, which is called the VUPC-T scheme, is proposed in this paper. In the LDPC decoder with the VUPC-T scheme, the decoding is terminated when the number of parity checks unsatisfied at the both iterations in T interval is greater than a threshold. To reduce the cost of the scheme, VUPCT with partial observation, which is called the VUPPC-T , is also presented. The threshold for the proposed scheme is determined using the PEXIT chart which is a performance analysis tool for the LDPC codes. Simulation results show that the VUPPC-T and threshold decision scheme performed properly.
