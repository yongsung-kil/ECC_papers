Fine-Grained Bit-Flipping Decoding for LDPC Codes
Yuxing Chen , Hangxuan Cui , Jun Lin, Senior Member, IEEE, and Zhongfeng Wang , Fellow, IEEE

Abstract—This brief presents a novel class of hard-decision algorithms for decoding low density parity check codes. The new algorithms, named fine-grained bit-flipping (FBF) algorithms, employ a detailed classification of each bit, by introducing the XOR value of its estimated and received value as a subdividing criterion. The fine-grained classification allows the algorithms to strengthen the information utilization during each iteration. Simulation results show that the FBF algorithms can achieve up to 5 times better decoding performance than the stateof-the-art bit-flipping algorithms over the binary symmetric channel. Additionally, a well-optimized hardware architecture is developed for implementing FBF algorithms. Compared to other decoders, implementation results demonstrate that the FBF decoders achieve higher throughput and area efficiency.
Index Terms—Low density parity check codes, fine-grained classification, bit-flipping, high throughput, low-complexity implementation.
I. INTRODUCTION
L OW DENSITY parity check (LDPC) codes [1] have gained increasing attention in recent years. Among all decoding methods, the soft-decision algorithms, such as the sum product (SP) [2], min-sum (MS) [3], and offset min-sum (OMS) [4] algorithms, have good error correction capability at the cost of high computation complexity. On the contrary, the hard-decision algorithms significantly reduce the computation comlexity, but lead to a non-negligible performance loss. The decoding of hard-decision algorithms is governed by the flipping rule and the inversion (energy) function. The hard-decision algorithms can be divided into two categories: global-searching bit-flipping (GB) algorithms and non-globalsearching bit-flipping (NB) algorithms. GB algorithms require a global search to identify the least reliable bits at each iteration and only these bits have a possibility to be flipped. In contrast, NB algorithms omit the global search, and more bits have chance to be flipped. GB algorithms generally perform

better than NB algorithms at the expense of a much longer critical path caused by the global-searching operation.
To improve the performance of bit-flipping (BF) algorithms, the gradient descent BF (GDBF) algorithms have been proposed by Wadayama et al. [5], which add a term representing the similarity between the estimated and the received bit into the inversion function. Then, the probabilistic GDBF (PGDBF) [6] further improves the performance of GB algorithms by flipping the least reliable bits with a fixed probability. More recently, modified versions of PGDBF, tabulist aided PGDBF (TPGDBF) [7] and tabu-list random-penalty GDBF (TRGDBF) [8] have been proposed, in both of which a tabu-list is added to avoid the repeated bit flipping. These two algorithms exhibit the best error correction performance in all BF-based algorithms. The probabilistic parallel BF (PPBF) [9] is the state-of-the-art NB algorithm. The authors of [9] introduce a flipping probability function, which assigns different flipping probabilities to bits according to their reliabilities.
In this brief, we first propose a fine-grained classification method for LDPC decoding, which enhances information utilization during each iteration. Based on the proposed method, two algorithms, global-searching fine-grained BF (G-FBF) and non-global-searching fine-grained BF (N-FBF), are introduced. G-FBF offers the best performance among all hard-decision algorithms. Although N-FBF does not have as much error correction gain as G-FBF, it operates at a higher frequency. Moreover, it outperforms existing NB algorithms.
The rest of this brief is organized as follows. Section II introduces the notations and two existing hard-decision algorithms. In Section III, the fine-grained classification is described in detail. Section IV presents the simulation results including performance comparisons. The hardware architecture is presented in Section V. At last, the conclusion is drawn in Section VI.

III. THE FINE-GRAINED CLASSIFICATION METHOD
The energy value in Eq. (1) is composed of two terms. The first term represents the correlation between the received bit yn and its estimated value xn, denoted as Cn(t) = xn(t) ⊕ yn. The second term is the number of unsatisfied neighboring CNs, denoted as Sn(t) = m∈Mn s(mt). The inversion function puts the same weight on the two terms as shown in Eq. (1). However, the local maximum likelihood (LML) interpretation described in [10] shows the importance of these terms differs as iterations change. Therefore, the decoding performance can be further improved by introducing a weight factor to the second term. However, the weight factor leads to extra computation.

V. THE HARDWARE ARCHITECTURE
A. Top-Level Architecture
The proposed architecture of N-FBF and G-FBF is presented in Fig. 5. The difference between them mainly lies in the maximum indicator in the dashed box which G-FBF includes but N-FBF excludes. The critical path of G-FBF (the blue line) is longer than that of N-FBF (the red line) due to its maximum indicator. Thus, N-FBF operates at a higher frequency. The decoding process is described in the following. First, the estimated codeword x in VN units (VNUs) is initialized as the received codeword y. Second, CN units (CNUs) calculate the syndrome vector s. Every bit of s is sent to the

Fig. 5. The top-level architecture for N-FBF and G-FBF decoders.
syndrome check unit to judge the validation of the estimated codeword. Next, the energy calculation units (ECUs) calculate the energy values. In G-FBF, the maximum indicator is added to produce the indicator value In of each VN, for which with the maximum energy value, In = 1, otherwise In = 0. The random sequence is pre-generated and loaded into the random number generator during initialization. The sequence is cyclically shifted at each iteration. Finally, bits that satisfy the flipping condition described in Alg. 4 or Alg. 3 will be flipped. The decoding terminates when the valid codeword is obtained or the maximum iteration is reached.
Similar to the non-syndrome PPBF (NS-PPBF) decoder introduced in [9], we could implement the non-syndrome NFBF (NS-N-FBF) decoder to remove the syndrome check unit shown in Fig. 5. However, the average values of throughput and area efficency for the NS-PPBF decoder do not really have practical relevance as the authors of [9] have already pointed out. We do not further discuss the NS-N-FBF decoder in this brief for the lack of practical value.

VI. CONCLUSION
This brief introduces a class of novel fine-grained BF algorithms to resolve the weight issue in the energy function. The VNs are classified into two classes according to the difference between the received bit and the estimated bit. Different classes of VNs are flipped with unequal probabilities. The proposed classification is applied in both categories of GB and NB as G-FBF and N-FBF, respectively, which achieve a significant performance gain in comparison to the best known BF-based algorithms. The synthesis results of the proposed architecture confirm that the proposed G-FBF and N-FBF are promising decoders with high decoding throughput and high area efficiency.
