Sub-Nyquist Spectrum Sensing of Sparse Wideband Signals Using Low-Density Measurement Matrices
Yash Vasavada , Member, IEEE, and Chandra Prakash

Abstract—The problem of wideband spectrum sensing/sampling in the sub-Nyquist domain is solved in this paper using sparse (low-density) binary-valued measurement matrices. Key objectives are (i) to achieve an efficient compression ratio, and (ii) improve the signal reconstruction performance. We propose a novel RF front-end with parallel branches that we have called Low-Density Wideband Converter (LDWC). We show that the LDWC implements a binary Low-Density Parity Check (LDPC) matrix as the compressive sensing (CS) measurement matrix. We evaluate, using an Information-Theoretic approach, the asymptotic bound on the required number of LDWC parallel branches for sparsity detection. We develop two new belief propagation (BP) algorithms that operate on the Tanner graph of the CS measurements. We have derived the first algorithm by assuming independence among the variable nodes (VNs) of the Tanner graph. For the second method, we have accounted for the joint probability distribution of the VNs. Analytical and simulated performance results prove the concepts of the LDWC and the proposed BP algorithms and quantify the attainment of objectives (i) and (ii) stated above.
Index Terms—Analog to Information Converter, Belief Propagation, Modulated Wideband Converter (MWC), Random Demodulator (RD), Sub-Nyquist Spectrum Sensing (SNSS).
I. INTRODUCTION
I N SEVERAL signal processing problems of current interest, such as detection of Primary Users at Cognitive Radios [1], electronic warfare using radar systems [2], and interference/jammer detection at a communication system employing Frequency Division Multiple Access (FDMA) [3], the bandwidth W = fmax − fmin of the wideband signal received at the antenna is large compared to the width B of a few narrow occupied subbands. The objective of the spectrum sensing schemes is to detect these active subband signals.

Furthermore, this rate may far exceed the theoretical lower bound when the signal comprises only a few occupied subbands. When the center frequencies of the occupied subbands are known, this bound is the Landau rate [6] — the sum of the widths B of these subbands. When they are unknown, this bound is twice the Landau rate [7].
Practical schemes (called sub-Nyquist spectrum sensing (SNSS) schemes) that attempt to achieve the theoretical rate bound fall in two categories. The schemes in the first category reduce the sample rate without introducing any aliasing [8] — they scan the full spectrum either sequentially in time [9] or using L parallel hardware branches [10]. Although the sample rate is reduced, the former exhibits a latency and may not be agile in responding to varying spectral occupancy when the ratio M = W/B is large, whereas the latter requires a large L (ratio L/M , called the compression ratio, can approach 1).
A. Random Demodulator and Modulated Wideband Converter
The above factors have motivated the development of Compressive Sensing (CS) schemes that fall in the second category, where the required L of the parallel spectrum scanning methods is reduced by deliberately introducing aliasing [7]. The two pioneering CS architectures — the random demodulator (RD) [11] and the modulated wideband converter or MWC [12] — have the same design concept shown in Fig. 1 (the detailed assumptions and implementation are different [12, Section VI.A]). The wideband signal is multiplied on the th parallel branch (0 ≤ < L) by a periodic signal p (t) with rectangularly-shaped pulses (or chips). This mixing process is represented by a matrix A = S F D, where S is a matrix of pseudo-random (PR) ±1 chip amplitudes [12] (this matrix is denoted as M in [11, Section IV.B]), F is the DFT (Discrete Fourier Transformation) matrix, and D is a diagonal matrix defined by the sinc function [12, Eq. 20], [11, Eq. 3].

The RD and the MWC (that we hereafter refer to as the RD-MWC) have two stages: (S1) a stage for detection of indices of active subbands (support recovery), and (S2) a signal reconstruction stage that uses the detected indices in Stage (S1) as the input. The Stage (S2) of these schemes suffers from noise enhancement at the band edges [12, Section III.B (last paragraph)], [11, Section IV.C]. The sinc function values (the elements of D) corresponding to the subbands close to the band edge are near zero. While recovering the signals at these subbands, the Least-Squares solution used in Stage (S2) amplifies the noise at these subbands (this is analogous to the noise amplification drawback of the Zero Forcing Equalizer [13]). Furthermore, the Stage (S1) of the RD-MWC also has a limitation due to which the required L/M increases above the Landau bound. The MWC relies upon the Orthogonal Matching Pursuit (OMP) algorithm [14], which (similar to the basis pursuit algorithm [15] and Low-Density Parity Check (LDPC) based CS methods [16]– [19]) is designed to estimate not only the support but also the analog-valued nonzero elements. However, the latter are not used by the RD-MWC.
Several extensions of the RD-MWC, and in general the Analog to Information Converters (AIC) [20], aim to (i) improve the estimation of locations and number of active subbands, and reduce the false alarm rate [21]–[23], (ii) improve the energy requirement and the sensitivity [20], [24], (iii) reduce L or the bandwidth of the PR mixing signals [2], [25]–[27], or (iv) provide joint SNSS and DoA (Direction of Arrival) estimation [28]. While these studies extend the RD-MWC and the AIC in important ways, none have considered the above-mentioned limitations of Stages (S1) and (S2) that our paper addresses.
B. Our Contributions: Low-Density Wideband Converter
The major contribution of this paper is the development and analysis of a novel SNSS scheme that we have called LowDensity Wideband Converter (LDWC). The LDWC Stage (S2) does not have the noise enhancement problem, and its Stage (S1) allows a compression ratio L/M which — for a given occupancy rate κ/M , where κ is the number of occupied subbands — is substantially smaller than that required for the RD-MWC. These benefits are realized because of two reasons. One, the front-end architecture of the LDWC is based on a new set of mixing signals, each of which is a sum of a few sinusoids oscillating at randomly-defined frequencies. With this, the LDWC sensing matrix A becomes a sparse binary matrix whose nonzero elements are not affected by sinc-shaped diagonal elements of the RD-MWC matrix D. An example result with M = 18 subbands shows that the mean squared error (MSE) of the RD-MWC at the edge of the band (i.e., for the 18th subband) increases by a factor of 25 compared to the first subband, whereas the MSE for the LDWC does not exhibit this growth. Two, unlike the RD-MWC, the LDWC Stage (S1) is focused only on the estimation of the binary support vector of interest. Furthermore, it brings to bear the performance advantages of the LDPC codes [29]) in solving this problem. We show that, under certain ideal noiseless conditions (the received powers are equal, and each signal is contained

For example, [14, Table 1] shows that the required L/M for estimating κ = 12 and 20 nonzero elements (with M = 256 and a success rate > 99%) are 136/256 = 0.53 and 228/256 = 0.89, respectively. In contrast, the LDWC Stage (S1) for the same success and occupancy rates requires L/M ∼ 0.25 and 0.35. The ideal conditions mentioned above facilitate a theoretical development but are otherwise restrictive for practical applications. As shown in Fig. 2, our proposal generalizes to the operational conditions (unequal received powers and a signal not entirely contained in a subband) by accounting for the measurement and the model noise. A contribution of our paper is the development of two new Belief Propagation (BP) algorithms for detecting the binary support vector in the presence of this noise. These are the enablers of the superior performance of LDWC Stage (S1) — not only in the ideal conditions but, as the results show, also in the practical scenarios.
Several other contributions of this paper are as follows: (i) a new information-theoretic analysis on the lower bound of the compression ratio L/M , (ii) a statistical analysis of the MSE in the signal reconstructed in Stage (S2) and an analysis of the impact on this MSE when the support estimated in Stage (S1) is erroneous, (iii) the development of an analog, a digital and a hybrid design for the synthesis of the LDWC mixing signals, and (iv) the development of an end-to-end communication test-bench that uses the quadrature phase-shift keying (QPSK) modulation. Our paper is the first to provide closed-form expressions (which are verified by simulation) of the probability of symbol errors when the QPSK signal received over the Additive White Gaussian Noise (AWGN) is processed using the SNSS architectures studied in this paper.

C. Synthesis of Analog Mixing Waveforms

Analog Domain Synthesis: A reference frequency of B Hz is applied to a comb generator to produce a signal comprising of M harmonics of B [35]. This signal, for the synthesis of p (t), is fed to a bank of dc digitally tunable bandpass filters (BPFs) centered at {f (k) = m (k)}dkc=1 [36]. Their outputs are combined to obtain p (t). Another approach uses a bank of phase-locked loops (PLLs) that track a common reference at B Hz, and act as its multipliers {m (k)}dkc=1. The mixing signal p (t) is obtained by combining the PLL outputs. This approach can use commer-
cially available (PLL-based) RF synthesizer chips with multiple
RF output ports [37], [38]. These provide multiple synthesized
sinusoidal outputs at configurable frequencies disciplined by a
single clock reference.

ital synthesizers with multiple RF output ports (e.g., [39]) can

Our main contribution is in defining two new methods for the second step. This is explained using an example CN = 3 with a measured value w3 = 2 (the three VNs connected to this CN are m = 2, 5, and 7). In the first method called Independent Probability Evaluation or IPE, the VNs m = 5 and m = 7 are taken as independent RVs. Since w3 = 2, the VN 2 takes the value of 1 in one of two events: (i) Event A (see Fig. 6) in which VN 5 is 0 and VN 7 is 1. The probability of this event equals (1 − q5→3) q7→3; (ii) Event B — VN 5 is 1 and VN 7 is 0 —

with a probability of q5→3 (1 − q7→3). The event in which VN 2 equals one is a union of these two mutually-exclusive events. Thus, the required probability is

p3→2 ∝ (1 − q5→3)q7→3 + p5→3(1 − p7→3). (17)

The IPE method does not enforce that the probabilities of the impossible events given a measured w are zero. In the above example, the event {c5 = 0, c7 = 0} cannot occur given w3 = 2 (the sum of the three VNs connected to CN 3 in this event ≤ 1). However, under the IPE assumption, the probability of this event, i.e., (1 − q5→3)(1 − q7→3), is nonzero. This anomaly occurs since, given w3 = 2, the VNs c5 and c7 do not remain independent. This observation motivates the evaluation of the joint probabilities of the VNs. This is in general a difficult problem, but a computationally-efficient solution — the Joint Probability Evaluation (JPE) — is proposed below. The simulated threshold values p∗ of p (p∗A and p∗B — the largest p ∈ [0, 0.5], and the smallest p ∈ [0.5, 1], respectively, at which different algorithms succeed in detecting the support) are shown in Fig. 11 for different L/M . An observation from Fig. 11 is that the IPE and JPE exhibit improved performance compared to the OMP/SC. A future extension is to bridge the gap in Fig. 11 between the theory and the observed performance of the IPE / JPE.
In agreement with the analytical result in Fig. 9, the required normalized rates L/M for the JPE, the IPE and the SC in Fig. 11 are observed to be symmetric around p = 0.5 (i.e., pA∗ ≈ 1 − p∗B for a given L/M ). A brief explanation follows. Consider two values p = 0 and 1 symmetric around p = 0.5. When p = 0, all the VNs are zeros (see (1)); similarly, they all are ones when p = 1. A CN with degree dc observes w = 0 in the first case and declares with certainty that each connected VN is 0. Similarly, when p = 1, the CN, which now measures w = dc, declares with certainty that each connected VN is 1. Thus, the CN is successful in detecting the VNs, and its detection certainty is identical, in these two symmetrical cases of p around p = 0.5. Generalizing, it can be shown that, for an arbitrary value of 0 < p < 0.5, if a CN with a value of 0 ≤ w < dc/2 assigns an LLR of λ to a VN, a CN with a value of dc − w assigns an LLR of −λ in the symmetric case, i.e., when the occupancy probability is 1 − p.
This leads to the following observations. One, the required L/M for the proposed algorithms decreases as p increases beyond p = 0.5 and it becomes near zero as p → 1 (the OMP, in contrast, requires rate L/M → 1 as p → 1 even in this ideal noiseless case). Two, unlike the OMP, the proposed algorithms correctly detect the sparsity pattern when L is less than the Landau limit of 2κ (as p → 1, κ → M , but the rate L/M required by these algorithms drops, i.e., L 2κ. The result in Fig. 12 for the deterministic occupancy model confirms this performance trend). This is a significant improvement over the MWC requirement of L ≈ 4κ ln(M/2κ)).

VI. SUMMARY
This paper presents Low-Density Wideband Converter (LDWC) — a new architecture for sub-Nyquist spectrum sensing (SNSS) based on the LDPC measurement matrix. The LDWC provides two benefits over the prior SNSS schemes: (i) a robust sparsity detection with improved compression ratio L/M for a given occupancy rate κ/M , or improved κ/M for a given L/M , and (ii) mitigation of the problem of noise enhancement in signal reconstruction. In contrast to the prior CS-based SNSS methods that rely on the existing algorithms (such as basis/matching pursuit) for sparsity detection, two new BP algorithms are developed and studied in this paper. An Information-Theoretic analytic bound on L/M for successful sparsity detection in the absence of noise is derived. It is shown

that the proposed algorithms outperform the alternative algorithms studied in this paper and perform closer to the analytical bound. An evaluation of support detection in the presence of noise shows that our proposal outperforms the existing methods in this case also. Finally, the paper has simulation and analytical results that show that the noise enhancement problem that affects the RD-MWC is mitigated in the LDWC. Furthermore, with an increase in either κ or M , the LDWC performance is observed to improve, whereas the RD-MWC performance worsens.
The future extensions of this work include the development of a hardware prototype, a generalization of the BP algorithms and a study of their convergence, a method of normalization of the measured energy in (14), and an extension of the MSE analysis conducted in this paper.
