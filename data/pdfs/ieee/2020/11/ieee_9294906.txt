Fair comparison of hardware and software LDPC decoder implementations for SDR space links

Vincent Pignoly, Bertrand Le Gal, Christophe Jego CNRS IMS Laboratory, University of Bordeaux, France
Email: firstname.lastname@ims-bordeaux.fr

Benjamin Gadat, Lyonel Barthe Airbus Defense and Space, Toulouse, France

Abstract—LDPC codes are a family of error correcting codes used in most wireless and space communication standards such as CCSDS [1] and DVB [2] ones. Thanks to their high error correction features and their parallelism capabilities, different technical solutions nowadays enable their real-time implementations in Software Defined Radio (SDR) contexts. Indeed, even if Error Correction Code (ECC) decoders were previously implemented on dedicated hardware targets (ASIC/FPGA), their real-time implementations on multi-core devices [3]–[5], is now possible. Software implementations currently provide high-throughput and low-latency features, scalability and also flexibility that makes them attractive for SDR systems. However, multi-core power consumption, throughput performance level and prices could be worse compared to ASIC/FPGA solutions for very highthroughput SDR systems. In this paper, a fair comparison of multi-core and FPGA solutions for the development of a 10 Gbps communication system for space communications is detailed.
Index Terms—LDPC codes, FPGA, multi-core, SDR, optical space communication.
I. INTRODUCTION
Introduced by R. Gallager in 1962 [6], Low-Density ParityCheck codes are a popular class of linear block codes. They were introduced in 1962 but were too complex for workable implementations. LDPC codes were then rediscovered by Mackay [7] in 1996 whereas the increasing computing capacity made their usage realistic. Due to their good error correction performance close to the channel capacity [8], LDPC codes were adopted in many wireless standards such as WiFi [9] and WiMax [10]. Space standards including DVB-S2 [2], DVB-S2 [11] and CCSDS [1] also selected LDPC codes.
Nowadays, optical wireless communications for satellites is a topic of interest to improve communication links up to 10 Gbps. However, optical wireless communications suffer from atmospheric phenomenons that strongly affect the quality of the link. To overcome these perturbations, the use of error correcting codes is essential. These codes must have good properties in terms of convergence speed and error floor because the communication link is unidirectional and do not allow frame retransmissions.
To date, FPGA or ASIC technologies were the single solutions to perform real-time LDPC decoding for mobile and/or base station systems. Indeed, LDPC decoding algorithms are characterized by a costly computational complexity discarding other kinds of implementations. The software implementations of LDPC decoders were limited to simulation purposes. However, the increasing processing performance of software pro-

grammable devices, associated to efficient decoding algorithm descriptions, [3]–[5], [12] made the implementation of highthroughput software decoders possible. In fact, during the last five years, a large set of research works focused on the implementations of various ECC decoders (LDPC [3]–[5], [12], turbo codes [13], etc.) on multi-core and many-core targets. These software decoders reach Mbps or even tens of Gbps throughput performance. Their advantage comes from the fact that they offer a high flexibility level required by Software Defined Radio systems. These high performance levels stem from the massive computation parallelism levels provided by GPUs and CPUs. However, they also have drawback effects on scalability, power consumption and device prices compared to FPGA ones.
Previous studies targeting mobile communications with throughput below 1 Gbps consider that software implementations provide greater flexibility at the cost of inefficiencies in terms of throughput and energy consumption [12]. In this article, we propose to accurately evaluate the advantages and drawbacks of both approaches. This study is based on specifications derived from the design of a base station for the implementation of down-link optical space communication system constrained by a 10 Gbps throughput.
The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 introduces LDPC codes and the state of the art solution available to hardware and software implementations. Then, the study context concerning the optical wireless communication system is provided in Section 3. Section 4 summarizes the experimental results obtained and a fair comparison of both solutions is provided.
II. LDPC CODES
A. LDPC code structure and decoding
An LDPC code is a linear block code defined by a binary sparse M × N parity-check matrix called H. In the H matrix, M = N − K rows are associated to parity-check information (CN) and N columns represent received bit-node information (VN) with N the codeword length and K the number of information bits in the received sequence. The number of one elements in the yth row of H is defined as the row degree dc(y). Similarly, the number of one elements in the xth column is defined as the column degree dv(x). The decoding can be performed thanks to a message passing (MP) approach where VNs and CNs exchange soft information. The

In their general form, H matrix are un-structured and irregular. It means that dc and dv are not constant in the H matrix. To enable the design of efficient LDPC decoders, a special class of LDPC codes was proposed (Quasi-Cyclic LDPC codes). QC-LDPC codes are LDPC codes that are composed of an array of Z ×Z circulant identity sub-matrices. Z is the order that defines the parallelism level. Figure 1 shows the H matrix of a QC-LDPC code used in [15]. The QCLDPC form eases data storage and insures that Z parallel CNs computations can be done in parallel without memory access conflicts. Consequently, QC-LDPC codes were adopted in most digital communication standards such as [1].
B. LDPC decoder implementations
1) Hardware decoder implementations: Since about 10 years, the design of efficient LDPC decoders for QC-LDPC codes is mastered. Indeed, QC H matrix structure associated with horizontal layered scheduling enables hardware architectures to allocate and efficiently use at least Z processing units for a parallel processing [15]–[17]. In order to provide Gbps feature for space communications, the QC LDPC codes are designed with high Z values. For example, in DVB-S2 and DVB-S2x standards, the QC LDPC codes are constructed with Z = 360 for both short frames (N = 16200) and for long frames (N = 64800).
The coarse grain architectural model for hardware LDPC decoders proposed in [15], [17] is detailed in Figure 2. It is composed of two main sets of memory, one to store the VN elements and the other one to store the exchanged messages. Associated to memories, an interleaver drives the Z VN elements to the Z processing units accordingly to the H matrix constraints. A controller module drives the control signals to the overall elements. This semi-parallel architectural model is dedicated to QC-LDPC codes because it is scalable according to Z value. It offers an efficient trade-off solution between throughput performance and hardware complexity [15]–[18].
2) Software decoder implementations: Hardware decoder implementations on ASIC or FPGA technologies provides, at the same time, high throughput and energy efficiency. However, development times are long and flexibility level is quite low. Within the last five years, a large set of research works focused on LDPC decoder implementations on multi-

core and many-core targets [3]–[5]. These software decoders reach Mbps or even up to Gbps throughput performances. They also offer the flexibility level required by Software Defined Radio systems. High-performance levels are obtained using the massive computation parallelism provided by GPUs and CPUs. However, these implementations, based on massive frame decoding parallelism, failed to scale with large decoding pool set when N > 1024 due to memory bandwidth and L1/L2 memory cache limitations [3].
To overcome these limitations, an implementation processing one frame at a time was detailed in [4]. This method relies on the behavioral mimicry of hardware LDPC decoders. However, the main difference comes from the maximum exploitable parallelism factor, which is limited by the targeted multicore architecture. For instance, for AXV2 architectures, the computational parallelism is limited to 32 8-bit values whereas for AVX512 devices, the parallelism can reach 64 8-bit values. However, this limitation is partly mitigated by the difference in operating frequency between the multi-core programmable architectures and the FPGA-based architectures. C. Work motivations
Currently, the solutions proposed in [15], [17] allow hardware decoders on FPGA targets to reach data rates up to 10 Gbps for LDPC codes of sizes greater than or equal to N = 16k. Similarly, the software decoders presented in [4] should provide throughput performance of the same order of magnitude for such kind of LDPC codes. However, even if software decoders are attractive because of the flexibility and development time gains, they also suffer from shortcomings such as power consumption, lack of scalability or financial costs. In previous studies, no reliable data were provided on these aspects. This was the reason for this work whose context is the design of a base station for satellite communications.

IV. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
In order to compare fairly both LDPC decoder implementation approaches, two distinct platforms were used:
Platform 1 - It is an hardware system composed of an Xilinx Zynq xczu9eg-3ffvb1156e FPGA. This FPGA includes 274080 LUTs, 548160 flip flops and 912 RAM blocs. These hardware features enables to instantiate many LDPC decoder architectures to achieve high-throughput performances [15]. This mid-range FPGA device has been chosen in this work because it is currently used for space applications by industrial partner.
Platform 2 - The multicore system is composed of a dual socket INTEL Xeon Gold 6148 CPU with 256 GB of RAM memory. Each Xeon processor is composed of 20 physical processor cores. The overall processor core share a 28160K L3 memory cache. Even if the turbo boost feature enables a working frequency of the cores up to 3, 70 GHz, the average

working frequency when all cores are activated is around 2,60 GHz due to power dissipation constraint. The system runs a CentOS 7 Linux distribution and source codes where compiled with LLVM 7.0.
To consolidate the experimental results and to confirm the conclusions, two LDPC codes were added. The first one comes the DVB-S2x standard (N=64800 and R=11/20) whereas the second one is CCSDS code (N=22528 and R=4/5). Similar LDPC decoding configuration were applied on both platforms.
The results of the decoder implementation on FPGA are reported in Table I. Results are given after place and route step. Xilinx Power Estimator (XPE) was used to estimate decoder power consumption. The decoder functionality has been verified on chip thank to hardware in the loop technique. For the overall experiments, FPGA maximum usage is limited to 80 % because when the FPGA is nearly full, routing tools have difficulties to find a routing solution and to maintain high working frequency. Results with the (16384, 4096) LDPC code match with the constraints of the base station context whereas the other codes provide also interesting throughput performances. These performances are lower due to the code construction technique applied to generate them e.g. their H matrix densities that is higher.
The values measured1 for the software LDPC decoder implementations on P2 platform are reported in Table II. The throughput and latency measurements first demonstrate that the target throughput of 10 Gbps for custom LDPC code is reach on the Xeon multicore. A decoding latency of 60 µs is required to decode the information. High performance levels are also observed for CCSDS and DBV-S2x LDPC codes. Some performance variations appear according to the LDPC code property such as for hardware decoder implementations. As expected, the working frequency of the multicore system falls down when the overall processor cores are activated to limit the power consumption and ease the heat dissipation of the system. From a power consumption point of view, the multicore platform consumes up to 345 Watts when the overall processor cores are activated to reach 10 Gbps.
Experimental results demonstrates that both implementations enable to reach very high-throughput performances. However, as summarized in Figure 4, the obtained features are not the same. Indeed, software decoders ease system development but suffer from a 30× higher power consumption and a processing latency increases from 7× up to 20×. When the device cost is also considered, the FPGA solution looks better. Indeed, the P1 platform currently costs is about $5200 whereas the P2 platform costs about $7500.
