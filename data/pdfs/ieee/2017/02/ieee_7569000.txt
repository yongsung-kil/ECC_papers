Spatially Coupled Codes Optimized for Magnetic Recording Applications
Homa Esfahanizadeh, Ahmed Hareedy, and Lara Dolecek
Department of Electrical Engineering, University of California, Los Angeles (UCLA), Los Angeles, CA 90095 USA

Spatially coupled (SC) codes are a class of sparse graph-based codes known to have capacity-approaching performance. SC codes are constructed based on an underlying low-density parity-check (LDPC) code, by first partitioning the underlying block code and then putting replicas of the components together. Significant recent research efforts have been devoted to the asymptotic, ensemble-averaged study of SC codes, as these coupled variants of the existing LDPC codes offer excellent properties. While the asymptotic analysis is important, due to simplifying assumptions and averaging effects, results from the asymptotic domain are not readily translatable to the practical, finite-length setting. Despite this chasm, the finite-length analysis of SC codes is still largely unexplored. In this paper, we tackle the problem of optimized design of SC codes in the context of magnetic-recording (MR) applications. In particular, we identify combinatorial structures in the graphical representation of the code that are detrimental in the MR setting. An intriguing observation is that for the same SC code, the problematic objects for the MR channels are combinatorially different from the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) setting, thus necessitating a careful code design approach for the MR applications. We first demonstrate that the choice of the so-called cutting vector, which guides the code partitioning in the SC code design, directly affects the cardinality of these problematic objects. In particular, we show that the number of problematic objects is the highest—and consequently that the performance is the worst—in the case of the degenerate cutting vector, which precisely corresponds to uncoupled LDPC block codes. We, therefore, show that coupling always improves the performance and that the degree of improvement is dependent on the choice of the cutting vector. We then extend our analysis to different column weights and present SC codes that outperform block codes with unoptimized edge weights by more than 3.5 orders of magnitude and that also outperform both optimized block codes and unoptimized SC codes by more than two orders of magnitude. Through presented examples, we demonstrate high performance of the proposed code design methodology.
Index Terms— Absorbing sets (ASs), balanced ASs (BASs), error floor, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, magnetic recording (MR), non-binary (NB) codes, spatially coupled (SC) codes, trapping sets (TSs).

I. INTRODUCTION

M ODERN magnetic-recording (MR) applications require channel codes with outstanding error correction capabilities. Currently, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are a preferred choice in MR technologies [1]–[3] due to their good performance. In this paper, we investigate the practical utility of a new class of graph-based codes, the so-called spatially coupled (SC) codes. We demonstrate that, with appropriate MR-channel aware code optimization techniques developed here, SC codes can offer substantial performance advantage over popular LDPC codes.
SC codes are a class of graph-based codes, introduced in [4], that have capacity-approaching performance under iterative decoding in the limit of long block lengths. SC codes are constructed by coupling several identical LDPC codes together to make a chain of coupled block codes. We represent a block LDPC code by its parity-check matrix H . Construction of an SC code is akin to partitioning H into H0 and H1 via the so-called cutting vector and repeatedly piecing replicas of H0 and H1 together to yield the parity-check matrix HSC, as shown in Fig. 1.

In this paper, we offer the first comprehensive study of SC codes for MR applications. In particular, we identify combinatorial structures in the graphical representation of the code that are detrimental in the MR setting. An intriguing observation made in our previous work [15] is that for the same LDPC code, the problematic objects for MR channels are combinatorially different from those encountered in the AWGN setting, thus necessitating a careful code design methodology for the MR applications. Here, we show that the same property holds for SC codes.
We demonstrate that the choice of the cutting vector, which guides the code partitioning in the SC code design, directly affects the cardinality of these problematic objects, which are shown to be certain absorbing sets (ASs) [16]. In particular, we show that the count of detrimental ASs is the highest—and consequently that the performance is the worst—in the case of the degenerate cutting vector, which precisely corresponds to uncoupled LDPC block codes. We, therefore, demonstrate that coupling always improves the performance, and that the degree of improvement is dependent on the choice of the cutting vector. We consider circulant-based (CB) LDPC codes, where all circulants are non-zero, as the underlying block codes. CB codes are a broad class of structured LDPC codes, including separable-CB (SCB) codes [17], array-based (AB) codes [16], and among others.
First, we present a general approach for the enumeration of detrimental objects that can exist in the Tanner graph representation of SC codes. Some methods have been presented in [18] and [19] for the enumeration of ASs within SC codes. The major limitation of these works is that they are too complex to be used for longer codes and for column weights larger than 3. In contrast to [18] and [19], our approach is neither brute force nor case-based, and thus offers the ability to study a variety of code lengths and column weights.
We then build upon results in [15], where we focused on block codes, and we identify dominant errors of SC codes in the MR setting. We show that decoding errors can be viewed as the so-called balanced ASs (BASs). BASs are a sub-class of ASs, and were previously introduced in [15] to characterize the performance of LDPC (block) codes over MR channels. What is particularly interesting is that the type of problematic objects that are dominant in the error floor performance of SC codes over MR channels is different from AWGN channels. These observations, therefore, necessitate a careful SC code design approach for the MR applications. By a careful analysis of the error profile in the error floor region, we demonstrate that several dominant BASs in the MR setting share a “common denominator structure”. It is then this sub-structure that we focus on: we analytically compute the cutting vector that corresponds to the minimal number of these sub-structures, which in turn yields our first step in the code optimization. For non-binary (NB) SC codes, we then perform the existing edge weight optimization techniques (based on [15] and [20])

that further reduce the number of remaining detrimental structures.
Our code design methodology produces codes that have more than 3.5 orders of magnitude performance improvement compared with block codes with unoptimized edge weights and more than two orders of magnitude compared with both optimized block codes and unoptimized SC codes.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. In Section II, we briefly review the structure of CB and SC codes, the system model for MR channels, and the definitions of problematic combinatorial objects. In Section III, we propose our novel approach for the enumeration of combinatorial objects within SC codes. In Section IV, we focus our attention on the AB-SC code family, as an illustrative exemplar of CB-SC codes. We investigate the dominant BASs of AB-SC codes and establish common sub-structures of the dominant BASs. Section V discusses the proposed methodology for the SC code optimization over MR channels. Our simulation results are given in Section VI. Finally, Section VII delivers conclusions and offers suggestions for further research.

B. Overview of the MR System
Here, we briefly overview the key components of our 1-D MR system. We use the same MR setup as in our previous work [15]. We describe the system model for the 1-D MR system which will be used in this paper. Consider an NB LDPC code over GF(q), q = 2ν, with code length m and message length μ. As shown in Fig. 4, the system model has the following components.
1) Encoding: The message sequence u ∈ GF(q)μ is encoded into a codeword c ∈ GF(q)m . In this paper, we focus on NB CB-SC codes for which m = Lκ p.
2) Transmission: Binary conversion, modulation, and interleaving are applied sequentially to the codeword c in order to generate the sequence of data d, which is then written onto the MR channel.
3) Channel: The MR channel incorporates inter-symbol interference in addition to jitter and electronic noise. The channel oversampled output sequence is xov. As in [15], the channel density is set to 1.4.
4) Filtering: To generate the sequence y, xov is passed through a continuous-time filter (CTF), a downsampler, and a digital finite impulse response (DFIR) filter. The partial response channel consists of the MR channel, CTF, and DFIR units. As in [15], the equalization target used is [8 14 2].

C. Combinatorial Objects of Interest
In this section, we quickly summarize the key combinatorial objects of interest. First, it is well known that under iterative decoding algorithms, graph-based codes suffer from the error floor phenomenon caused by the existence of certain combinatorial structures in the Tanner graph of the code. These combinatorial structures correspond to fixed points of the decoder, and need not be just codewords. Previous works studied these problematic configurations in depth. We now revisit the key definitions.
Definition 1 [28], [29]: Consider a sub-graph induced by a subset V of VNs in the Tanner graph of an NB LDPC code. Set all the VNs in V to values in GF(q)\{0} and set all other VNs to 0. The set V is said to be an (a, b) trapping set (TS) if the size of V is a and the number of neighboring unsatisfied CNs is b (for the given assignment of VN values).
Definition 2 [16], [30]: Consider a sub-graph induced by a subset V of VNs in the Tanner graph of an NB LDPC code. Set all the VNs in V to values in GF(q)\{0} and set all other VNs to 0. The set V is said to be an (a, b) AS if the size of V is a, the number of neighboring unsatisfied CNs is b, and each VN in V is connected to strictly more satisfied than unsatisfied neighboring CNs (for the given assignment of VN values).
Clearly, the class of TSs subsumes the class of ASs. While TSs that are not ASs are usually harmless (as for the AWGN channel, these configurations are typically unstable under iterative decoding), we purposely recall the definition here. The reason is, as we shall see later in this paper, a systematic elimination of multiple problematic ASs can be deftly achieved by focusing on the elimination of the common sub-structure that these ASs share. In some cases, these common structures will be certain TSs that on their own do not appear as decoding errors.

We recently observed that in the context of the MR applications, only certain sub-classes of ASs matter [15]. The reason is that in the MR setting, the global iterations between the detector and the decoder can often (with a sufficient number of outer iterations) overcome the errors due to ASs that are on the verge of instability, and what remains then are the errors due to a certain sub-class of ASs, which we call BASs. We thus classify ASs as either BASs or unbalanced ASs (UBASs). Both are defined as follows.
Definition 3 [15]: Let g = (γ − 1)/2 for a given column weight γ . An (a, b) AS with 0 ≤ b ≤ (ag)/2 is defined as a BAS, while an (a, b) AS with (ag)/2 < b ≤ ag is defined as a UBAS.
For instance, for the binary case (q = 2), Fig. 6(a) shows a (3, 3) AS, Fig. 8(d) shows a (4, 8) TS which cannot be categorized as an AS, and Fig. 7(a) shows two non-isomorphic configurations for (6, 0) BAS. We remark that BASs need not be codewords.
For the NB setup, the BASs/ASs/TSs of interest are described in terms of both the underlying topology (which is the unlabeled configuration) and the edge weights. Eliminating structures with a given topology thus eliminates all NB configurations that have that topology.
III. EFFICIENT APPROACH FOR THE ENUMERATION OF ASs/TSs FOR SC CODES
In this section, we propose a method to efficiently enumerate (a, b) configurations of interest inside the Tanner graph of SC codes. Specifically, we work on the unlabeled version of the graph (and the parity-check matrix). Using the repetitive structure of SC codes, we reduce the search zone for finding combinatorial objects of interest to a few small areas. The features of our enumeration method are as follows.
1) We take the advantage of the repetitive structure of SC codes to search over few smaller sub-graphs rather than the whole Tanner graph (alternatively, to search over certain sub-matrices of the parity-check matrix). We then carefully combine these intermediate results to compute the total number of objects of interest.
2) This method can be used for any SC code with CB underlying block code, including SCB codes, AB codes, and others.
3) This method can be used for the enumeration of any (a, b) TSs, ASs, cycles, or other combinatorial objects of interest.

IV. PROBLEMATIC OBJECTS OF AB-SC CODES FOR MR APPLICATIONS
In this section, we explicitly characterize the combinatorial objects of interest in the MR setting. We consider the class of AB-SC codes as an exemplar of CB-SC code, and determine the dominant BASs for AB-SC codes with column weights γ = 3 and γ = 4 in Sections IV-A and IV-B. In each case, we establish a “common denominator structure” that the unlabeled configurations of dominant BASs share. Here and elsewhere, dominant BASs refer to BASs that cause most of the decoding errors in the error floor region. Characterization of the common sub-structure is useful for two reasons: 1) by developing code design approaches that minimize the number of the common sub-structures, we can simultaneously target a number of BASs and notably decrease their collective occurrence and 2) this structure has a simpler combinatorial description than the relevant super-structures, and consequently, can be identified easier in the search step.
Our code optimization methodology improves upon the existing works [18] and [19], as we seek to eliminate common denominators that are shared by several dominant AS/BASs in an attempt to remove as many as possible of them at once. In contrast, [18] and [19] work exclusively on the elimination of the single most dominant configuration for AB-SC codes.
A. AB-SC Codes With γ = 3
For AB-SC codes with γ = 3, we recognized that the dominant BAS (unlabeled) configurations are (6, 0), (8, 0), and (10, 0) in the MR setting. For example, consider AB-SC Code 1, defined over GF(4), with p = 23, γ = 3, and L = 5 (code length of 5290 bits). The error profile for AB-SC Code 1 over the MR channel is shown in Table I(a). This error profile corresponds to the FER curve of AB-SC Code 1 at SNR = 17.25 dB in Fig. 9. We note that this is in contrast to the AWGN setting, where (3, 3) and (4, 2) ASs are deemed most problematic [18]. Moreover, unlabeled configurations of (6, 1) and (8, 2) BASs that appear in Table I(a) are in fact the same as the unlabeled configurations of (6, 0) and (8, 0) BASs, respectively.
An interesting property is that there are exactly two (6, 0) BASs that are topologically non-isomorphic, as shown in Fig. 7(a). As it is apparent from Fig. 7(a), both of these configurations have (4, 4) ASs as sub-structures, while for

example, (3, 3) AS is just found in one of the configurations. In addition, (4, 4) AS also exists as a sub-structure in other relevant BASs, such as (8, 0) and (10, 0). As a result, we identify (4, 4) AS, shown in Fig. 7(b), as the common denominator structure that we seek to minimize.
We thus focus our attention on the (4, 4) sub-structure, as the new object of interest in the cutting vector optimization and argue that minimizing the population of the (4, 4) AS, as the parent configuration (sub-structure), reduces the number of all children configurations (super-structures), and improves the code performance. We also remark that for this column weight, (4, 4) AS is not an object of interest in canonical AWGN channels. As a result, using AWGN-optimized codes is an inferior strategy for MR channels.
B. AB-SC Codes With γ = 4
For AB-SC codes with γ = 4 in the MR setting, the dominant (unlabeled) BASs are (8, 2), (8, 4), (10, 0), and (12, 0), which is in contrast to the AWGN setting where (6, 4) ASs are the most problematic [16]. This observation again demonstrates that using AWGN-optimized codes is not the best strategy for MR channels.
For example, consider AB-SC Code 5, defined over GF(4), with p = 29, γ = 4, and L = 5 (code length of 8410 bits). The error profile for AB-SC Code 5 over the MR channel is shown in Table I(b). This error profile corresponds to the FER curve of AB-SC Code 5 at SNR = 16.25 dB in Fig. 11.
As an example, Fig. 8 shows two non-isomorphic configurations for (8, 2) and one configuration for (10, 0) BASs. Both depicted configurations for the (8, 2) BAS share (4, 8) and (3, 6) TSs as common sub-structures [as do other (8, 2) BAS configurations not pictured here], but the (10, 0) configuration, shown in Fig. 8(c), does not include any (3, 6) TS [neither do some other (10, 0) BAS configurations not pictured here]. As a result, we are interested in minimizing the number of instances of (4, 8) TS shown in Fig. 8(d) as the common denominator structure. It is interesting to note that (4, 8) TS does not appear as a decoding error by itself. This is the first time that we would consider a TS as an object of interest, since TSs that are not ASs are usually harmless (as for the AWGN channel, these configurations are typically unstable under iterative decoding).

V. AB-SC CODE DESIGN FOR MR APPLICATIONS
In this section, we present our methodology to design SC codes optimized for MR applications. Spatial coupling effectively “rewires” the nodes in the Tanner graph of the code. As a result, some of the necessary existence conditions for BASs/ASs/TSs are violated, so that the number of detrimental objects is always fewer for SC codes compared with the uncoupled block codes. The amount of reduction of the number of these detrimental objects is dictated by the choice of the cutting vector.
Our procedure for the optimization of SC codes for MR channels includes two main steps. The first step applies to both binary and non-binary designs, and the second step applies to non-binary designs where one has an additional choice of selecting edge weights. First, given an underlying block code and a desired coupling length, we find the cutting vector that results in the minimum number of instances of the common denominator structure. Which structure one seeks to eliminate depends on the underlying code parameters (e.g., column weight), as we discussed in Section IV. For the NB codes, we then apply the technique from [15] for the edge weight optimization to eliminate as many as possible of the remaining dominant BASs.
According to Section IV, the common denominator structures for AB-SC codes with γ = 3 and γ = 4 are (4, 4) AS and (4, 8) TS, respectively. Both of these objects can be categorized as a cycle-8 with no interconnections. For finding the optimal cutting vector, we consider all possible cutting vectors and find the one that results in the least number of the objects of interest in the Tanner graph. As the final step of our design procedure, we replace all 1’s of HSC, that is constructed by the optimal cutting vector, with non-zero elements in GF(q) and apply the edge weight optimization to remove as many as possible of remaining dominant BASs. For the NB setting, both the topology and edge weights can be manipulated to eliminate a problematic AS. In other words, by choosing the optimal cutting vector that minimizes the number of common denominators, we minimize the number of problematic objects that have that common denominator as parts of their configurations. Then, using edge weight optimization proposed in [15] and [20], we attempt to eliminate as many as possible of remaining BASs. We remark that for NB SC codes, selection of the edge weights alone is typically not sufficient for the MR applications due to a variety of BASs that appear in the error floor. We comment on this property in the following simulation section.
VI. SIMULATION RESULTS
In this section, we compare the number of “common denominator” structures described in Section V for SC codes constructed with the optimal, randomly selected, and worst cutting vectors. We explicitly show that the degenerate case, where H0 = H and H1 = 0 or vice versa, is the most inferior, since it results in the highest number of these detrimental structures. Our simulation results also confirm that this degenerate case leads to the worst error floor compared with other cutting vectors. We compare the error floor performance of SC codes with random and optimal cutting vectors, and show that using the optimal cutting vector has almost 0.6 of an order of magnitude performance improvement over a randomly selected cutting vector.

VII. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we presented the first comprehensive study of SC codes for MR applications. We presented a code design methodology that in a channel-aware way exploits the structure of dominant errors to systematically set the values of the cutting vector and edge weights. We demonstrated significant performance advantage of our proposed optimized SC codes relative to their block counterparts as well as relative to unoptimized SC codes. A fruitful future direction is to extend the proposed code design methodology to multidimensional SC codes for 2-D MR applications.
