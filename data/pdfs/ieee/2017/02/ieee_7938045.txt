Design of Protograph-based LDPC Code Ensembles
with Fast Convergence Properties

Abstract—The design of protograph-based low-density paritycheck (LDPC) code ensembles optimized for a finite number of decoder iterations is investigated. We show by example that protograph-based EXIT (PEXIT) analysis is not sufficiently reliable in the finite-iteration case, and develop a new technique which, similarly to PEXIT analysis, uses the mutual information (MI) as a tool in order to predict the performance of ensembles based on protographs. In contrast to PEXIT analysis, in which a critical value of the MI is used to define the decoding threshold, in our technique we examine the behavior of the MI over a range of channel parameters in order to define the threshold. Using this new definition of the iteration-constrained threshold, we perform a search over a large family of protographs in order to find the protograph with the highest iteration-constrained threshold for a finite number of iterations over the BEC.
I. INTRODUCTION
Due to the increasing demand to achieve reliable communications with a limited latency in processing the received signal, fast convergence is emerging as one of the most important features for modern iteratively-decodable channel codes.
In the case of an iterative decoder for a low-density parity check (LDPC) code, latency is affected by several parameters, including both algorithmic and hardware aspects. From a purely algorithmic point of view, the two main factors governing latency, i.e., the average number of iterations and the complexity per iteration, are strongly affected by the scheduling of message passing between the variable nodes (VNs) and the check nodes (CNs) of the Tanner graph during decoding. Works such as [1]–[4] (and references therein) have shown that the original parallel (or flooding) schedule is not the most efficient one from a decoding convergence point of view, and that more efficient schedules exist, such as sequential schedules and informed dynamic schedules. Although most of them are characterized by a higher complexity per iteration than the traditional flooding schedule, their capability to reduce the average number of decoding iterations turns out to be the prominent effect. This not only yields an overall latency reduction, but also a better error correction capability whenever the maximum number of decoding iterations is constrained to be small.
Most LDPC codes are designed following a well-known procedure that consists of design of an ensemble, followed by a finite-length code construction. Ensemble design is performed using asymptotic analysis tools, such as density evolution [5]

or extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) analysis [6], [7]. The considered schedule is the flooding one and the parameter being optimized is usually the asymptotic decoding threshold. The optimization may possibly impose a constraint on the typical minimum distance, stopping distance, or trapping distance [8]. Once an ensemble of codes with a satisfactory threshold has been designed, a finite-length code is drawn from the ensemble using, for example, algorithms targeting the girth or the extrinsic message degree of cycles [9]. The design of capacity-approaching protograph LDPC codes was explored in detail by Divsalar et al. in [10]. An instructive example is given in [10, Figs. 7, 8], where a rate-1/2 protograph was designed having a very good asymptotic threshold. We shall refer to the designed protograph ( [10, Fig. 8]) as Protograph A throughout this paper. We note that the final design stage of this protograph, as reported in [10], is the result of “a few hand-selected searches”.
There remain open questions with regard to design of codes (and ensembles) for a fast convergence context. In addition to the adopted decoder, the LDPC code structure is also expected to have a large impact on the error rate when the maximum number of iterations is constrained to be small. Since in the traditional code design approaches described above, the asymptotic decoding threshold is defined from the perspective of the decoder’s behavior over an infinite number of iterations, the design procedure does not necessarily yield LDPC codes suitable for applications where low latency is required.
In this paper, we show that the protograph EXIT (PEXIT) analysis approach proposed in [7] cannot be used to accurately predict the BER waterfall of a code from a protograph ensemble in the finite-iteration case. We then show that application of the method proposed in [11] does not solve this problem for the protograph context. We then propose a new approach to defining and computing the finite-iteration threshold of a protograph ensemble, based on the behavior of the average MI and its derivatives as a function of the channel parameter. It is shown that the proposed approach can successfully discriminate between protograph ensembles exhibiting good and bad BER waterfall behaviors. The developed threshold evaluation tool is also used to optimize the finite-iteration threshold over a family of rate-1/2 protographs including Protograph A, and the resulting code is found to have a BER performance very close to that produced by Protograph A.

II. DEFINITIONS AND NOTATION
In this section, we define a protograph LDPC code ensemble. The protograph is a graph containing a set of variable nodes (VNs), indexed by j ∈ J , and a set of check nodes (CNs), indexed by i ∈ I. Each VN j ∈ J is connected to CN i ∈ Ij ⊂ I by bij ≥ 1 parallel edges. Equivalently, each CN i ∈ I is connected to VN j ∈ Ji ⊂ J by bij parallel edges. To obtain a specific LDPC code from the protograph ensemble, a copy-and-permute (or lifting) operation with copy factor Q is performed on the protograph as follows. Each VN j ∈ J is copied Q times, and each CN i ∈ I is copied Q times. Then, each of the bij edges connecting VN j ∈ J and CN i ∈ Ij is copied Q times, and each resulting set of Q edges are made to connect the copies of VN j and the copies of CN i in a one-to-one fashion (in this step, parallel edges are usually avoided). The resulting graph is the Tanner graph for an LDPC code. A subset of VNs in the protograph may be punctured, in the sense that all of their copies are punctured (i.e., not transmitted). The protograph LDPC code ensemble is defined with respect to a uniform distribution over all local edge permutations between VN and CN copies.
III. PEXIT ANALYSIS
In order to overcome the limitations of EXIT analysis in the case of protograph-based ensembles, protograph EXIT (PEXIT) analysis was introduced in [7]. Before describing PEXIT analysis in detail, we first define some notation, following the definitions in [7].
Denote by Ich(j) the MI between codeword bit j ∈ J and its associated channel observation. IEv (i, j) is the MI between the jth codeword bit and the extrinsic message sent from the jth VN to the ith CN along one of the bij edges connecting them. Similarly, IEc (i, j) is the MI between the jth codeword bit and the extrinsic message sent from the ith CN to the jth VN along one of the bij edges connecting them. Finally, we represent the MI between the a posteriori probability evaluated by VN j and the jth codeword bit by IAPP(j). Algorithm 1 gives the steps involved in PEXIT analysis for the binary erasure channel (BEC) with channel parameter ε. The PEXIT threshold for a particular protograph (ensemble), denoted ε∗, is defined to be the lowest value of ε for which Algorithm 1 converges (i.e., stops).
The efficacy of PEXIT analysis was demonstrated in [7] by applying the analysis to a series of protographs for accumulaterepeat-accumulate (ARA) codes which were previously examined by Abbasfar et al. in [12]. The decoding thresholds obtained using PEXIT analysis correspond closely to those obtained using density evolution for both the BEC and the BI-AWGN channel where the maximum number of decoder iterations is not constrained.

We can clearly see that while Code B has better BER performance than Code C, the iteration-constrained thresholds suggest that codes which are lifted from Protograph C should exhibit superior performance, as ε∗C > εB∗ (note that for a given protograph, repeated generation of random codes via the lifting process yielded codes with consistently similar BER performance to those shown).
We note that in the case where the maximum number of decoder iterations is not constrained, the decoding thresholds obtained using PEXIT analysis give a highly accurate estimation of the location of the waterfall. This is shown in Fig. 3, where modified PEXIT analysis with imax = 200 is used to approximate the infinite-iteration case. The poor correlation between the decoding threshold and the BER waterfall seen in the 10-iteration case thus appears to be a consequence of the tightly constrained number of iterations.
By reducing the value of ξ, it is possible to obtain a value for ε∗ which coincides with the waterfalls of codes lifted from a particular protograph. However, this value of ξ is particular to the protograph in question, and applying it to PEXIT analysis of another protograph does not, in general, yield a value for ε∗ that lies on the waterfall of codes lifted from that protograph. We conclude that PEXIT analysis does not allow us to reliably predict or compare the performance of ensembles when the maximum number of decoder iterations is constrained.

We observe that in Fig. 5, the peak of the first derivative lies within the waterfall region of the BER curve. Motivated by this observation, we hypothesize that, in general, this peak may be used to approximate the location of the waterfall. Figs. 5 to 7 show the first derivatives of the Iavg curves for several protographs. We note that for certain protographs there is more than one peak in the first derivative. In Fig. 6, for example, we see two sharp drops in the first derivative of the Iavg curve. It is noteworthy that each of these peaks identifies the location of a sharp drop in the BER of a code lifted from the protograph. This observed behavior corroborates our proposal that peaks in the first derivative may be used to locate regions where the BER falls suddenly. As the channel erasure probability ε increases from zero, the first peak in the derivative of Iavg correlates reasonably well with the location of the waterfall. We define the value of the channel parameter at this peak as the iteration-constrained threshold for the protograph. In the discussion that follows, we shall continue to refer to the “first” and “second” peaks of derivatives, where a peak may be either a positive or negative peak, and the peaks are named in the order in which they are seen as ε increases, i.e., the “first” and “second” peaks are the first and second encountered peaks moving from right to left in the figures.
Algorithm 2 outlines the steps involved in finding this peak. While this method produces a good approximation of the location of the waterfall, it is clear from Figs. 5 to 7 that there is some variation in the location of the peak in relation to the BER waterfall. In Figs. 5 and 6, the peak is located in the center of the waterfall, while the peak in Fig. 7 occurs at the onset of the waterfall. We observe, however, the different nature of the the first derivative in these cases. Noting the presence of a pair of peaks in Fig. 6, we conjecture that the occurrence of two peaks in the first derivative of the MI curve may be a general phenomenon (where these peaks may be either distinct or overlapping). This motivates us to investigate higher derivatives of the mean MI.

V. PROTOGRAPH OPTIMIZATION
In this section we apply the methods proposed in Section IV to search over a family of protographs in order to determine which of the protographs have favorable thresholds for a constrained number of iterations.
The family which we consider is the one from which the protographs in Example 1 were drawn, i.e., the family in which all of the protographs contain one degree-16 VN and seven degree-3 VNs, and four CNs whose degrees are not constrained (note that this family was considered in the design phase of Protograph A [10, Fig. 8]). There are 1.28 × 109 protographs within this family. Careful construction of these protographs allows for many isomorphic protographs to be eliminated by avoiding the creation of protographs whose corresponding base matrices are simple column-permutations of one another, reducing the number of protographs to 657, 800.
