On the Relationship Between the KL Means Algorithm and the Information Bottleneck Method
Brian M. Kurkoski School of Information Science Japan Advanced Institute of Science and Technology Nomi, Ishikawa, Japan 923-1292

Abstract—The problem of finding the mutual informationmaximizing quantizer of a discrete memoryless channel is important in the implementation of communication receivers, LDPC code decoders, and in the design of polar codes. Two algorithms algorithms that provide suboptimal solutions in polynomial time are the information bottleneck method and the KL means algorithm. The contribution of this paper is show that the information bottleneck method with β → ∞ is algorithmically equivalent to the KL means algorithm. This is done by showing that both the DMC channel outputs, and the quantizer outputs, are in the same J-dimensional space, where J is the cardinality of the input alphabet.
I. INTRODUCTION
A. Overview
Consider the quantization of discrete memoryless channels (DMC) as follows. Let X be the input to an arbitrary discrete memoryless channel, and let Y be its output. The channel output Y is quantized to a new variable Z using a quantizer Q. The problem of finding the channel quantizer Q∗ which is optimal in the sense of maximizing mutual information is:

KL divergence will maximize the mutual information as in (1).
This paper shows that these two algorithms, the information bottleneck method with β → ∞, and the KL means algorithm are equivalent. This is done by showing that both the DMC channel outputs, and the quantizer outputs, are in the same Euclidean space. The algorithms superficially differ in their initialization, but it is shown that both initializations are random clusterings.
Both algorithms are iterative and suboptimal, but have modest complexity compared to optimal quantization, which is exponentially complex in general. The KL means algorithm works with “hard clustering,” that is the algorithm works with candidate quantizers where each channel output belongs to exactly one quantizer output. In general the information bottleneck method works with “soft clustering,” where each channel output is probabilistically assigned to various quantizer outputs. However, as β → ∞ these soft clusters tend towards hard clusters.

Recently this problem has received substantial attention, because it has connections to the design of polar codes, the implementation of LDPC decoders, as well as receivers with factor graph representations. But there is no known polynomial-time algorithm to find the optimal quantizer Q∗ in general. Unless the problem has some special structure which can be exploited, we must use an algorithm with exponential complexity, or accept a possibly suboptimal solution.
Two suboptimal algorithms are of interest in this paper. The information bottleneck method solves a kind of rate-distortion problem for the Markov chain above, using a fixed parameter β [1]. When β → ∞, then the information bottleneck method solves a problem that coincides with (1). B. Related Work
Both the information bottleneck method and the KL means algorithm have foundations in the area of machine learning and stochastic learning theory.
The K-means algorithm, an unsupervised learning approach to perform clustering or quantization using a given data set, has a long history, and has been quite successful [2]. Kmeans clustering is usually presented using Euclidean distance as an distortion or distance metric. Concentrating on the classification and regression trees, Chou described in 1991 generalizations with various metrics, including Kullback– Leibler divergence [3]. Burshtein, Della Pietra, Kanevsky, and Nádas gave a rigorous proof for conditions under which the preimage for an optimal classifier must be a convex set [4], and this was further explored by Coppersmith, Hong and Hosking [5]. From the clustering perspective, the algorithm has also been generalized to a broad class of distortion functions, including Itakura-Saito distance and of course, the Kullback– Leiber divergence [6]. Thus, the KL Means algorithm [20] is a special case of a more general class of algorithms, while its name suggests the important connection with maximizing mutual information.

The information bottleneck method was presented by Tishby, Pereira and Bialek in 1999 [1]. The information bottleneck method is applied to the Markov chain X → Y → Z. Given a joint distribution on X, Y it provides a mapping from Y to Z such that Z can be used to approximate X. There is a tradeoff between the rate and distortion, and as such it is a generalization of rate-distortion theory in information theory. The information bottleneck method has been successful in machine learning, being used in applications such as text processing, document classification, image classification and astronomy.
In particular, the Lewandowsky, Stark and Bauch applied the information bottleneck method to the design of communication receivers [7]. Lewandowsky et al. also applied the information bottleneck method to the implementation of quantization in LDPC decoders [8], although dynamic programing approaches [21] can also be applied for the same goal [22]. Thus, it is natural to ask the question what is the connection between the information bottleneck method and the KL means algorithm, which is the question addressed in this paper.
Note that optimal quantization has recently received attention in the context of designing polar codes, but these approaches are considered beyond the scope of this paper. One suboptimal but efficient approach was described by Tal, Sharov and Vardy for quantizing discrete distributions when designing polar codes for the multiple-access channel [9], this has complexity M log M . Gulcu, Ye and Barg also quantize discrete distributions, for channel degradation, in the context of polar codes, with complexity which is quartic in an algorithm parameter [10]. Another suboptimal approach called greedy combining or greedy merging has been re-discovered several times [23]. And when X is binary, the structure of the problem can be exploited to reduce complexity. The maximum of mutual information may be found by a dynamic programming algorithm [21]. Iwata and Ozawa gave an efficient, linear-time complexity algorithm to find Q∗ [11].

The KL means algorithm is a quantizer design algorithm, which as mentioned earlier, belongs to a class of K-means algorithm by replacing the Euclidean distance function by KullbackLeibler divergence. When applied to clustering of probability distributions, the KL means algorithm yields a local minimum of the KL divergence [13]. IV. INFORMATION BOTTLENECK METHOD
The information bottleneck regards how much information that Y provides about X through a “bottleneck” Z. The goal is to push as much information through this bottleneck. In particular, the problem is stated as:

min I(Y; Z) − βI(X; Z)

(23)

pZ|Y (z|y)

for a fixed parameter β ≥ 0. The optimization is over possible pZ|Y(z|y), including both deterministic and probabilistic solutions. The alphabet sizes |X |, |Y| and |Z| are fixed.
The information bottleneck problem can be regarded as a
generalization of the Arimoto-Blahut method for finding the
rate-distortion function for a discrete source [18]. Whereas the rate-distortion function minimizes I(Y; Z) over possible pZ|Y(z|y) subject to a distortion constraint, in the information bottleneck method, I(X; Y) is minimized while I(X; Z) forms a kind of constraint. Varying β has the effect of sweeping a kind of rate-distortion curve. Both the Arimoto-Blahut
algorithm and the information bottleneck methods are iterative
algorithms, and are convex optimization methods (at least for finite β).
As β → ∞, then objective function (23) above and the objective function (1) coincide. This can be seen by observing that the contribution of I(Y; Z) becomes increasingly small compared to βI(X; Z), but β is a constant with respect to the minimization, and minimization of the negative function
is maximization.

V. COMPARISONS BETWEEN KL MEANS AND IB METHOD
This section argues the main contribution of this paper: The information bottleneck method with β → ∞ and the KL means algorithm algorithmically perform the same steps. There are differences with respect to initialization, but these are not relevant if the initialization is chosen to be the same. It is important to recognize that with β → ∞, the output of the information bottleneck method will be a deterministic quantizer.

A. Optimality of Deterministic Quantizers
The information bottleneck method is initialized with a probabilistic quantizer pZ|Y(z|y), and can produce a probabilistic quantizer as its output. However, as β → ∞, the output of the information bottleneck method tends to be a deterministic quantizer, that is pZ|Y(z|y) = 0 or 1.
This is not surprising, because for the optimization problem (1) there exists an optimal quantizer which is deterministic. For a DMC pY|X(y|x), a deterministic quantizer Q∗ maximizes mutual information, that is Qz|y ∈ {0, 1} for all y, z [21, Lemma 1].
If the mutual information objective function is strictly concave, this lemma can be strengthened to say that all optimal quantizers are deterministic. While the information bottleneck method searches over all quantizers including probabilistic ones, if it finds an optimal quantizer, it should be deterministic, when β → ∞.

C. Algorithm Initialization
The information bottleneck method is initialized with a random quantizer pZ|Y(z|y), which includes probabilistic quantizers, however it is clear from the previous subsection that if β → ∞ is used, then by the end of Step 3 on the first iteration, pZ|Y(z|y) has become a deterministic quantizer.
On the other hand, KL means algorithm is initialized by selecting K of the M data points as the means. On the next Step 1 of the first iteration, clusters are formed using these means. Since each channel output is in exactly one cluster, this is equivalent to pZ|Y(z|y) being a deterministic quantizer. Thus, while the two initialization methods are not identical, one can see that they both amount to random selection of a deterministic channel quantizer.
It is well known that the performance of these algorithms are sensitive to the selection of the initialization, and can be improved by careful selection of the initialization, or seed. For example, Arthur and Vassilvitskii described the “K-means++” algorithm, which replaces uniformly random means with a seeding algorithm that makes closely-spaced means less likely [19]. Such ideas could be extended to the KL means algorithm and the information bottleneck method.
D. Algorithm Termination
The algorithm termination also differs. For the KL means Algorithm 1 in Step 3, the termination is usually very simple: if the quantizer has not changed since the previous iteration, then stop. This is possible because the quantizer is discrete. Note that it is possible for the algorithm to iteratively alternate between two (or more) quantizers, in which case the algorithm should stop when the maximum number of iterations has been reached.
On the other hand, the the information bottleneck method terminates in Step 4 when the Jensen-Shannon divergence is sufficiently small. This is needed because the the algorithm produces “soft clusters.” In practice β is not infinity, but some large value, and thus the soft clusters do change as the iterations progress.
The KL means algorithm may lend us some insight on improving the termination of the information bottleneck method: if hard decisions (suitably defined) of the soft clusters are made, and they do not change from the following iteration, then the algorithm may be terminated.
VI. DISCUSSION
This paper has shown that the information bottleneck method with β → ∞ is equivalent to the KL means algorithm. Both are candidates to solve the information maximization problem given in (1), but there is not a significant distinction between these two algorithms. These algorithms are important because even though the solutions they provide are suboptimal, they do so in polynomial time, in contrast to general optimal algorithms.
