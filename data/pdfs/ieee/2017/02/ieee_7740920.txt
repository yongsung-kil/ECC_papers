Improved Penalty Functions of ADMM Penalized Decoder for LDPC Codes
Biao Wang, Jianjun Mu, Xiaopeng Jiao, and Zhongfei Wang

Abstract— By making the pseudocodewords more costly than codewords, the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) penalized decoder can improve the error rate performances significantly for low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes at low signal-to-noise ratios. In this letter, we design two improved piecewise penalty functions for ADMM penalized decoder by increasing the slope of the penalty function at the points near x = 0 and x = 1. The improved penalty functions can punish pseudocodewords more quickly and thus increase the decoding speed. Simulation results over two LDPC codes show that the proposed method improves the error rate performances and reduces the decoding time considerably.
Index Terms— Alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM), LDPC codes, penalty function, pseudocodewords.

III. IMPROVED PENALTY FUNCTIONS
A. Motivation
In [8], Liu et al. show that the FER performances of ADMM decoder with the “log” penalty function g3(x) = −log(|x − 0.5|) is inferior to the ADMM decoders with the l1 and l2 penalty functions. From the curves of the three functions, we can see that the slope of g1(x) is 1 when x ∈ (0, 1/2), the slope of g2(x) decreases from 1 to 0 when x ∈ (0, 1/2), and the slope of g3(x) increases from 2loge to +∞ when x ∈ (0, 1/2). The inferior performances of g3(x) implies that the slope of the penalty function at the points near x = 0.5 should not be large. This is because that the large slope may prevent the ADMM decoder from transforming the variables with values near 0.5 to 0 or 1. On the other hand, the slope of the penalty function at the points near x = 0 and x = 1 should not be small. For the reason we consider an extreme case such that g(x) = l where l is a constant and the slope of g(x) is 0 when x ∈ (0, 1/2), then the ADMM penalized decoder will be degenerated into the original ADMM decoder without using penalty functions.
Based on the above analysis, we know that the slope of the penalty function is a sensitive factor for the ADMM penalized decoder. Therefore, it is interesting to consider the penalty functions with large slopes at the points near x = 0 and x = 1 and small slopes at the points near x = 0.5.

A. Choice of Parameters
For all the decoders, the error tolerance and the overrelaxation parameter are set to 10−5 and 1.9, respectively. The early termination scheme such as H xT = 0 is used for all simulations. For the original ADMM penalized decoders, the parameters are optimized by the method proposed in [1]. For the ADMM decoders with I-PFs, We use the differential evolution (DE) to optimize the parameters α, β and μ such as the parameter generator used in [9]. We set the maximum generation Gmax = 500, the population size L = 30 and the scale factor δ = 0.5 as in [9] and the SNR values we used for C1 and C2 are 2.2dB and 3.0dB, respectively. It should be noted that though the optimized parameters for the ADMM penalized decoder with I-PFs are carried out at specific SNRs, they can still perform fairly well at other SNRs.

C. Comments on the Improved Penalty Functions
(1) In [3], the authors list four properties for the penalty functions. They proved that if a penalty function g(x) has the properties (i) and (ii), then the Ideal ML test, Weak ML test and All-zero assumption hold for the ADMM penalized decoder [3]. Since I-PFs have the properties (i) and (ii), the Ideal ML test, Weak ML test and All-zero assumption also hold for the ADMM decoder with I-PFs. It should also be noted that the proposed I-PFs do not have the property (iii). However, this is not a concern since we can still determine the ADMM updates with explicit form easily.
(2) For the I-PFs, we only consider to divide (0, 1/2) into two pieces for simplicity, though it is possible to use more pieces to get better performances.
(3) Note that the method in [9] can only be applied to irregular LDPC codes. However, the proposed method in this letter is valid for both regular and irregular LDPC codes. Moreover, the proposed method can be combined with [9] to obtain better FER performances (see Section IV-B).

B. FER Performances of the Proposed Method
The FER performances of C1 and C2 are obtained by generating 200 error frames. The maximum number of iterations is set to 1000. Fig. 2 shows the FER performances of C1 and C2 for the ADMM decoders with the original l1 and l2 penalty functions and the proposed I-PFs. It can be seen that the ADMM decoders with I-PFs perform better than that with the original l1 and l2 penalty functions. For example, for C1 at FER=10−5, the performance gains for the I-l1-PF and I-l2-PF are 0.13dB and 0.14dB when compared to the original l1 and l2 penalty functions, respectively. Fig. 2 also provides the FER performances of our method combined with the method proposed in [9]. As expected, the combination method provides better error rate performances than our method for both C1 and C2. The symbol ‘&’ appeared in the legend of Fig. 2 represents the combination of the two methods noted at the left and the right side of ‘&’. For example, the “ADMM & I-l1 & [9]" means that the ADMM penalized decoder combined with the I-l1-PF and the method in [9] is used.

V. CONCLUSION
To increase the level of punishment for the pseudocodewords, we design improved penalty functions by enlarging the slope at the points near x = 0 and x = 1 in this letter. Simulation results show that the proposed method improves the FER performances, and reduces the average number of iterations and the average decoding time considerably. In addition, the proposed I-PFs can be combined with the methods proposed in [6], [7], and [9] to obtain better error rate performances and faster decoding speeds.
