Discrete Channel Estimation by Integer Passing in Information Bottleneck Graphs
Jan Lewandowsky, Maximilian Stark, Rico Mendrzik, Gerhard Bauch Hamburg University of Technology Institute of Communications 21073 Hamburg, Germany
Email: {jan.lewandowsky,maximilian.stark,rico.mendrzik,bauch}@tuhh.de

AbstractвҖ”The aim of this work is to develop low complexity receivers with close to optimum performance that circumvent high precision signal processing in the hardware. In particular, we consider receivers that perform all signal processing only on output indices of a quantizer. For such completely integer based receivers this paper introduces a new discrete channel estimation based on integer passing in an Information Bottleneck Graph. Its design principle is the maximization of relevant information with the Information Bottleneck method. The proposed discrete channel estimation reveals new insights into the theory of integer passing in Information Bottleneck Graphs and links integer passing in Information Bottleneck Graphs to the passing of real valued functions in the sum-product algorithm. The performance of the discrete channel estimation is investigated in an earlier introduced discrete receiver chain for low density parity check encoded transmission over flat Rayleigh fading channels.
I. INTRODUCTION
Message passing schemes implementing the sum-product algorithm on factor graphs are powerful tools to efficiently solve a huge variety of modern engineering problems e.g. cooperative localization in wireless networks [1] and decoding of low density parity check (LDPC) codes [2]вҖ“[4]. The common mathematical problem solved by the sum-product algorithm on a factor graph is calculation of (approximate) marginal probability distributions. For decoding of LDPC codes the sum-product algorithm is typically implemented in the domain of log-likelihood-ratios (LLRs). Sum-product based soft-decision algorithms processing LLRs have already become standard for many channel codes, including LDPC codes. A natural issue of LLRs is that LLRs in general can take any real value. Thus soft-decision algorithms require quasi continuous signal processing to yield the best results. Quasi continuous signal processing of real values in digital hardware requires many bits to represent information on a single code bit. Furthermore, the required floating point arithmetical units are costly in terms of chip area and power consumption. This is why in practice often limited precision has to be used at the expense of a performance degradation. Also other signal processing steps e.g. channel estimation with the minimum mean squared error (MMSE) approach require processing of real values with sufficient precision in inphase and quadrature path. In contrast to real values, unsigned integers map to digital hardware very efficiently and can be processed by simple processors. Thus receivers with coarse quantization and

signal processing algorithms only working on output indices of a quantizer are of interest. Systematic construction methods for discrete LDPC decoders only working on such unsigned integers have been presented recently [5]вҖ“[9]. These decoders are based on information theory: Lookup tables replace node operations and they generate integer messages maximizing the mutual information with the bits the messages represent. These integers are the passed messages in the discrete decoders. Construction of the lookup tables is performed by clustering algorithms that maximize mutual information in a discrete density evolution scheme [5]вҖ“[9]. The Information Bottleneck method [10]вҖ“[12] provides algorithms that solve the related information optimum clustering problem. In [13] information optimum lookup tables were illustrated in Information Bottleneck Graphs and applied to develop a receiver chain including channel estimation, detection and LDPC decoding which only processes unsigned 4 bit integers. An Information Bottleneck Graph is a tool introduced in [13] which aims to compactly describe the flow of relevant information in a factor graph. This paper improves the integer based receiver from [13] and makes the following contributions: A new discrete channel estimation is proposed and described in an Information Bottleneck Graph. The discrete channel estimation in [13] has exponential space complexity in the number of pilot symbols and only allows very short training sequences at reasonable complexity. The newly proposed channel estimation has linear complexity in the number of pilot symbols and allows training sequences of arbitrary length. As an additional contribution, similarities between the sum-product algorithm and integer passing in the corresponding Information Bottleneck Graph are discussed. The paper is structured as follows: The next section summarizes prerequisites on the Information Bottleneck method and Information Bottleneck Graphs. Section III describes the transmitter and the channel model. Section IV investigates the applied quantizer and the new discrete channel estimation. Section V illustrates the practical contribution of this work by providing and discussing simulation results for an integer based receiver including the proposed channel estimation. Section VI concludes the paper. II. PREREQUISITES
A. Information Bottleneck method
The Information Bottleneck method [10]вҖ“[12] aims to compress an observed random variable Y (realization y вҲҲ Y) to a compact compression variable T (t вҲҲ T ) while trying to preserve as much mutual information I(T ; X) вүӨ I(X; Y ) with a relevant variable X (x вҲҲ X ) as possible. For this purpose a compression mapping p(t|y) is introduced which is target of an optimization. In the scope of this paper the compression mapping p(t|y) characterizes a deterministic mapping (i.e. p(t|y) вҲҲ {0, 1} вҲҖ(t, y)). An equivalent description of deterministic mapping p(t|y) is given by function t = f (y) which can be implemented in a lookup table, if Y has a finite number of elements. We casually refer to deterministic mappings p(t|y) as lookup tables in this paper. The mathematical problem setup of the Information Bottleneck method is described by

All occuring Information Bottleneck algorithms in this paper use an identical output cardinality |T |.

B. Information Bottleneck Graphs
Information Bottleneck Graphs were introduced in [13] and extend factor graphs [2] by a compact description of the flow of relevant information through the factor nodes. For this purpose a modified node symbol is used for all factor nodes representing compression mappings p(t|y) that were designed using the Information Bottleneck method. In an Information Bottleneck Graph the typical square notation of factor nodes is replaced by a trapezoid symbol for all compression mappings. An example is shown in Figure 1. The relevant variable is written in the center of the trapezoid symbol to illustrate the Information Bottleneck with respect to this variable. The compression variable is connected to the shortest side of the trapezoid. All other connected variables correspond to a (possibly multivariate) observed variable. The example in Figure 1 illustrates the flow of relevant information on x from random vector y = [y0, . . . , yNвҲ’1]T to t, i.e. t = f (y) compresses y, while preserving relevant information on x.

V. DISCUSSION AND RESULTS
We analyze the bit error rate of an LDPC encoded transmission scheme with a discrete receiver chain which uses the proposed channel estimation as a function of Eb/N0 in a numerical simulation. The performance is compared to a receiver with double precision arithmetic. The reference receiver uses MMSE channel estimation, calculates LLRs matched to this estimation and uses sum-product LDPC decoding. More details on the reference receiver can be found in [13]. Our aim is to approach the performance of this reference receiver as closely as possible with the discrete receiver chain.
The memory amount required for the lookup tables of the discrete receiver determines its implementation complexity. Table I summarizes expressions for the memory amount of all lookup tables in the receiver. |T q| controls a trade off between lookup table size and performance. In this paper we used |T q| = 32 to obtain a receiver that works on log2 32 = 5 bit integers, because we found that it has close to optimum performance. In our simulations the longest training sequence had length P = 6. The memory amount required to implement the proposed channel estimation in this configuration is 3.13 kilobyte. The original channel estimation from [13] would require impractical 640 megabyte (!) with these parameters. The lookup tables for detection in Table 1 have a total size of 1.25 kilobyte. A discrete receiver mismatched to the Eb/N0 is of practical interest. We found that matching the quantizer, the channel estimation and the lookup tables for detection in Figure 6 to the actual channel quality is crucial. In practice thresholds for the quantizer (cf. Figure 3) and lookup tables for channel estimation and detection can be generated offline for the expected range of Eb/N0 in small step size due to their low required memory amount. The discrete LDPC decoder was constructed only for one particular Eb/N0 and used mismatched in our simulations. The LDPC decoder performed imax = 50 iterations. The static lookup tables of the LDPC decoder occupy 218.8 kilobyte.

In both investigated scenarios the discrete receiver that works exclusively on unsigned 5 bit integers and performs lookups in tables of such integers only looses a fraction of a decibel over Eb/N0 in comparison to the competitor system with double precision signal processing. The loss of the discrete receiver typically lies between 0.1вҲ’0.2 dB and thus almost vanishes completely. The very slight remaining loss brings the advantage of a very simple receiver only performing lookup operations of unsigned integers for channel estimation, detection and LDPC decoding. Due to compact message representation and application of lookup tables for costly signal processing steps, we think that the

presented receiver components might enable low complexity implementations. A complexity analysis of the proposed receiver components on signal processing hardware is part of our current research activities.
VI. CONCLUSION
A new discrete channel estimation for purely integer based receiver chains was proposed and investigated. The channel estimation is based on passing integers in an Information Bottleneck Graph and has highly reduced complexity in comparison to our originally proposed discrete channel estimation from [13]. In the investigated scenarios a discrete receiver with the proposed channel estimation and discrete LDPC decoding achieves almost identical performance as a high precision receiver with MMSE channel estimation and sumproduct LDPC decoding. The discussed connections between integer passing and the sum-product algorithm might lead to a generic construction method for integer based message passing algorithms from Information Bottleneck Graphs in the future.
