Symmetrical Constructions for Regular Girth-8 QC-LDPC Codes
Alireza Tasdighi, Amir H. Banihashemi, Senior Member, IEEE, and Mohammad-Reza Sadeghi

Abstract— In this paper, we propose new constructions for regular girth-8 quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes based on circulant permutation matrices (CPM). The constructions assume symmetries in the structure of the paritycheck matrix and employ a greedy exhaustive search algorithm to find the permutation shifts of the CPMs. As a result of symmetries, the new codes have a more compact representation compared with their counterparts. In majority of cases, also, they achieve the girth 8 at a shorter block length for the same degree distribution (code rate). Deterministic (explicit) constructions are also presented to expand the proposed parity-check matrices to larger block lengths and higher rates. The proposed long highrate codes are often substantially shorter than regular girth-8 QC-LDPC codes of similar rate in the literature. Simulation results demonstrate that the proposed symmetric codes have competitive performance in comparison with similar existing QC-LDPC codes that lack symmetry.
Index Terms— QC-LDPC codes, regular LDPC codes, QC-LDPC code construction, protograph-based LDPC codes, girth, QC-LDPC codes of girth 8, symmetrical QC-LDPC codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
L OW-DENSITY parity-check (LDPC) codes are capable of achieving the capacity of many communication channels with low-complexity iterative decoding. Among LDPC codes, the category of quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes is of particular interest, since the codes in this category lend themselves to efficient implementations. As a result, QC-LDPC codes have been the primary choice for practical applications, and have been adopted in a number of standards [1]–[3]. A popular group of QC-LDPC codes is those constructed based on circulant permutation matrices (CPM) [4]–[19]. In particular, regular QC-LDPC codes whose parity-check matrix is an m × n array of CPMs of the same size N × N, have been the subject of much research [5], [6], [11]–[14], [16], [17], [19]. Such codes have block length L = n N and rate R ≥ 1 − m/n, and their Tanner graph can be viewed as a cyclic N-lifting of a fully-connected bipartite protograph,

In the Tanner graph of the QC-LDPC codes just described, the variable and check node degrees are dv = m and dc = n, respectively. We refer to LDPC codes in which all the variable nodes have the same degree dv, and all the check nodes have the same degree dc, (dv, dc) regular. One main advantage of regularity in the degree distribution of LDPC codes is to attain a better error floor performance compared to similar irregular codes. In this paper, our focus is on QC-LDPC codes that are cyclic liftings of the fully-connected base graph.
As a simple criterion for the performance of QC-LDPC codes, researchers have focused on the girth g of the code’s Tanner graph [4]–[19]. For a given base graph, the problem of interest has often been to find the smallest lifting degree N that achieves a certain girth. This corresponds to the shortest block length for the resulting QC-LDPC code. The efforts in this direction can be, in general, classified into two categories: (i) search-based techniques [5], [6], [8], [12], [19], and (ii) explicit constructions [11], [13]–[16]. For the case of girth 8, regular CPM-based QC-LDPC codes were found in [5], [6], [8], [11]–[14], [16], and [19], for different values of m. For m = 3 and g = 8, the best results, depending on the value of n, are those of [5], [6], [8], [12], [13], and [19]. For m = 4 and g = 8, the shortest CPM-based QC-LDPC codes are those of [5], [6], [11], and [14], for different values of n. Most recently, in [16], QC-LDPC codes with g = 8, for larger variable degrees of m = 5 and m = 6, were designed that were shorter than those constructed in [14].
Similar to the above literature, we also focus on the construction of QC-LDPC codes of girth 8 for m = 3, 4, 5, 6, and different n values. Our method of construction is search-based, and we look for codes of girth 8 with shortest block length. To perform the search efficiently, we introduce symmetries in the structure of the parity-check matrix, and perform the search exhaustively in a greedy fashion. The introduced symmetries have the additional advantage of a more compact representation for the code, which can consequently translate to a more efficient implementation. The constructed codes, in many cases, are significantly shorter than the existing QC codes with the same girth (g = 8) constructed from the same base graphs. This is particularly the case for larger values of n, which correspond to higher rate codes. As an example, for m = 3 and n = 20, we find a QC-LDPC code of length n N = 2100 (N = 105) and rate 0.85 with g = 8. This is in comparison with the shortest similar code in [13], which has a block length of 4020 (almost twice as long). As another

B. Search Algorithm
Suppose that the values m, n and N are given, and that we are interested in finding a QC-LDPC code of girth 8 with the structure of the exponent matrix as described in (2) (or its counterpart for n odd). To search for the non-zero elements of the exponent matrix, we only focus on the first half. The second half is simply obtained through the symmetry. The search is performed column-wise and in a greedy fashion. For each column, starting from the left-most one, the elements are selected from the top to the bottom. Each element is chosen from the set {1, . . . , N − 1} by testing different values from 1 all the way to N − 1. The test includes checking all the inequalities described in Proposition 1 (or its counterpart for odd values of n) that involve the new element and all the previously selected elements of the matrix. As soon as one value satisfies all the inequalities, that value is assigned to the new element, and we move on to select the next element of the matrix. Consider a case, where the element of the matrix whose value was just assigned is π, and thus the algorithm has moved on to assign a value to the next element π of the permutation matrix. If for π , all the values in the set {1, . . . , N − 1} are tested and none can satisfy all the required inequalities, we back track to the previous element π and change its value to the next feasible solution that satisfies all the inequalities. If such a value is found, we then move forward and search for a feasible solution for π . If not, then we back track one more step and reassign the value of the element that was chosen immediately prior to π. This process of forward/backward search will continue until (i) the process is completed by assigning values to all the elements in the first half of the exponent matrix, or (ii) no feasible solution

that satisfies all the inequalities for all the elements of the exponent matrix is found. In Case (i), the search process is declared successful. (In this case, the elements of the second half are determined through symmetry.) In Case (ii), a failure is declared.

IV. NUMERICAL RESULTS
To present our results in comparison with the state-of-theart, we have performed the following experiment: For each size m × n of the base graph, we start by the smallest value of N reported in the literature as providing girth 8 for a QC-LDPC code obtained from a cyclic lifting of degree N of the fully-connected m × n base graph. For this value of N, we apply the proposed search algorithm to see if we can find an exponent matrix of the form (2) or (3) (depending on n being even or odd, respectively) for a QC-LDPC code of girth 8. If we succeed, we then reduce the value of N by one, and repeat the same experiment. We continue until the proposed algorithm fails to provide an answer. At that point, we report the previous value of N along with the corresponding exponent matrix found by the algorithm. These results are presented in Tables I-IV, for values of m = 3, 4, 5 and 6, respectively. To present the exponent matrices, we have only provided rows 1 to m − 1 for the column-wise first half of the matrix. V. CONCLUDING REMARKS
In this paper, we proposed a symmetric structure for girth-8 QC-LDPC codes constructed by cyclically lifting fullyconnected base graphs. The symmetry made it possible to extend the reach of the search process, and find codes with large check node degrees (rates) that would be normally out of the reach of search algorithms. In almost all cases, the constructed codes had either the same block length or a shorter length, for a similar rate, compared to the best known existing codes of girth 8.
