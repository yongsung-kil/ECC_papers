Abstract—NAND flash is rapidly becoming the media of choice for data storage, due in part to its speed and low power consumption. However, flash wears out through repeated program-erase (P-E) cycling, causing the raw bit error rate (RBER) to increase. Error correction codes (ECCs) are used to detect and correct errors in a sector of data called a codeword. An uncorrectable error occurs when the number of bit errors in a codeword exceeds a certain level, meaning the data cannot be recovered.
This research uses machine learning to predict how far each sector of a NAND device can be cycled before it becomes uncorrectable by ECC, thereby enabling the controller to better manage the flash. We characterise a number of flash metrics to investigate their suitability for use as inputs to the prediction model, and determine the impact of each metric on the accuracy of the model. We show for the first time that it is possible to reliably make such predictions, achieving an overall accuracy of greater than 99% when the model was tested on unseen data.
I. INTRODUCTION
NAND flash devices have a number of error sources, including retention errors, read errors and program disturb errors. The RBER from all of these error sources increases with P-E cycling [1]. These raw bit errors must be corrected by ECC, which associates parity bits with a sector of data bits (typically 1KB or 2KB) to form a codeword. To ensure the number of errors in a codeword does not exceed the ECC limit, NAND manufacturers specify a P-E cycling limit which should not be exceeded. This is called the endurance specification. However, there is a large variation in quality of physical sectors from device-to-device and within individual devices (block-to-block, page-to-page). NAND manufacturers specify their endurance ratings very conservatively to account for this, even though most sectors are capable of far exceeding this rating.
As process geometries have scaled down, flash cells have become smaller and more densely packed, resulting in an increase in cell-to-cell interference. This has led to current NAND Flash devices having significantly higher RBER than previous-generation devices. Traditional algebraic ECCs such as Bose-Chaudhuri-Hocquenghem (BCH) codes require increased parity overhead to handle this higher RBER [2], so there has been a shift in the industry towards more advanced error correction codes such as low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes [3].

If the first read (known as the hard read) fails, LDPC performs extra soft reads, by offsetting the read reference point, to determine the probability that a bit has been read correctly. This greatly improves the error correction capability, but the additional soft reads can dramatically slow down the performance of the system [3]. The proportion of total reads that require additional soft reads is termed the trigger rate, and it is desirable to minimise this for performance purposes.
The motivation for this research is to pre-emptively identify these worst-case sectors and to predict how far each sector can be P-E cycled before uncorrectable errors occur. This will reduce the uncorrectable error rate of traditional BCH engines, and the trigger rate of LDPC engines. It may also allow for reduced-complexity ECC engines for latest-generation flash devices, and will enable the controller to optimise its NAND management and usage.
For example, NAND Flash controllers use wear leveling to reduce the uncorrectable error rate by spreading P-E cycles evenly across all blocks within a device. This prevents individual blocks from being excessively cycled and wearing out prematurely. Using our proposed model, rather than spreading the wear evenly among blocks, the wear leveling algorithm could be modified such that blocks predicted to reach higher cycling levels are cycled more frequently than blocks predicted to reach lower cycling levels. We believe that the data required to make the prediction for each block could be obtained as a background task when the controller is idle, thereby incurring no latency overhead.
This research focuses on devices from a sub-20nm multilevel cell (MLC) flash family, with an endurance rating of 5K P-E cycles. More than 27 million sectors from a population of devices were grouped into a number of predefined P-E cycling levels beyond the endurance rating, for the purpose of answering the following three specific research questions:
1) Which metrics, to be determined through characterisation, are most suitable as inputs to the prediction model?
2) What is the impact of each identified metric on the accuracy of the model?
3) When all identified inputs are used in conjunction with each other, what is the achievable accuracy of the model?

III. EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN
A custom-designed test system was developed, capable of P-E cycling NAND devices and reading back data at various cycling points. Bit error rates were determined by comparing data read back to previously programmed random data. The test system was also capable of measuring the time taken for program and erase operations.
In total, 6,675 blocks were tested across 45 devices. Blocks were split into ten cycling levels: from 6K to 15K in 1K steps. P-E cycling was performed over 500 hours at 81◦C, as per the JEDEC specification for testing the endurance of solid state drives [4].

Blocks consist of a number of rows of flash cells called wordlines (WLs), and a number of columns called bitlines (BLs). For MLC devices, each Flash cell stores two bits, a Least Significant Bit (LSB) and a Most Significant Bit (MSB). A collection of LSBs on a wordline forms an LSB page, and a collection of MSBs forms an MSB page. The devices used in this research contain four pages per wordline: LSB and MSB even for cells connected to even-numbered bitlines; LSB and MSB odd for cells connected to odd-numbered bitlines. Each page is 16,384 bytes long, and there are 512 pages per block.
NAND devices are programmed on a page basis but can only be erased on a block basis. Pages must be programmed in sequential order, meaning that even pages on a wordline are programmed before odd pages. When an even page is programmed, the subsequent programming of the odd page on the same wordline disturbs the even page data. This means that even and odd pages have different error characteristics [1].
Pages are subdivided into eight sectors of 2,048 bytes each. The user data stored in each sector, plus parity bits stored associated with that data, make up an ECC codeword. The parity bits are stored in the spare area at the end of each page.
For the remainder of this paper, the term codeword applies to the physical 2,048-byte sector used to store user data. We do not generate or store ECC parity data.

Each cycling level was sub-divided into 1,000 intervals, such that the interval for the 6K level was 6 cycles, for the 7K level was 7 cycles etc. At the end of each interval, the following data was recorded for each codeword:
1) Number of errors in that codeword. 2) Program time for the page that codeword belongs to. 3) Erase time for the block that codeword belongs to.
At the end of cycling, the number of codeword errors was recorded. Greater than 100 errors was deemed a fail; otherwise the codeword was deemed to have passed. This level was chosen arbitrarily and is within the typical range of error correction capability achievable with BCH or ’hard-decode’ LDPC for a 2,048-byte codeword.

B. Cycling Effects
BER, program time and erase time are known to change with P-E cycling. To investigate whether this change could have predictive value we have characterised the impact of cycling on these metrics.

2) Program Time vs Cycling: Fig. 4 shows the average page program time for each page type as a function of cycling. It shows that MSB pages take approximately ten times longer to program than LSB pages. The program time for LSB pages is approximately 200us, with LSB odd pages taking slightly longer to program than LSB even. MSB odd pages take approximately 1,800us, with MSB even pages taking approximately 2,100us.
The difference is because LSB pages use a coarse programming algorithm, whereas MSB pages use a much more fine grained programming algorithm for the precise placement of charge. MSB even pages require even more precise placement of charge than MSB odd pages due to the disturb mechanism they experience from the programming of odd pages on the same wordline [1], as outlined in Section II.
Such differences in programming time for different page types have been noted in the literature [5], which also noted a 10-15% reduction in program time as the device wears out with cycling. While our results show a 12% reduction in program time over the first 5,000 cycles for LSB pages, MSB pages actually increase by 2% and 4% respectively for even and odd pages. The devices studied in [5] were relatively largegeometry devices (50nm+) from 2009. This program time increase for current sub-20nm planar MLC devices has not previously been reported in the literature.

A. Machine Learning Method
Previous research compared multiple machine learning methods for building a classification model to predict the point of destruction of Flash blocks [10]. This study was an extension of [7], as discussed in Section IV-B4, which used program and erase times to predict the number of cycles at which pages or blocks became non-functional. It found that Support Vector Machines (SVMs) gave the most accurate predictions. For this reason, SVMs were chosen as the machine learning method used in this research.
SVMs are one of the most popular machine learning classification techniques [11]. They work by trying to find a separating hyperplane between the two classes of data. If the data is not linearly separable (as is usually the case for complex classification problems), it is mapped to a higher dimensional space using kernel techniques.
This research used the machine learning library e1071 [12] as part of the R programming language [13] to build and test the SVM models. The SVM was first tuned on the data to find the best values of the internal parameters cost and gamma, using the svm.tune function in R.

B. Model Data
1) Data Sampling: Due to the extemely imbalanced dataset (99.97% of all codewords tested passed) the model could simply predict that all codewords pass and achieve 99.97% accuracy. In cases like this, additional criteria are used to evaluate the model in the form of sensitivity and specificity.
Sensitivity is a measure of the true positive rate (TPR). For this research, a positive is taken to be a passing codeword, so TPR is the proportion of passing codewords that are predicted to pass. Specificity is a measure of the true negative rate (TNR), or the proportion of failing codewords that are predicted to fail. Accuracy is the total proportion of correct predictions and as such is the average of sensitivity and specificity.
It is important that the model performs well on both sensitivity and specificity. Precicting that all codewords pass will results in very high accuracy and sensitivity (99.97%), but very poor specificity. In order to balance the sensitivity versus specificity the majority class of passing codewords needs to be subsampled so that both classes are similar sizes. There are 7,857 failing codewords in the dataset, so 7,857 passing codewords (out of more than 27 million) were randomly chosen to make up the dataset.
2) Model Inputs and Outputs: For each of the 15,714 data points in the dataset (7,857 failing codewords; 7,857 randomly-chosen passing codewords), a number of metrics were chosen for investigation as suitable inputs to the model. Based on the data analysis discussed in Section IV, the following metrics were selected: page number, program time, erase time and codeword errors.
Some or all of these metrics, along with cycling level, were used as inputs to train the model. In practice, these metrics would be measured for a codeword and supplied to the model, along with the cycling level of interest. The model will then predict if the codeword will pass or fail at that cycling level. For example, the input metrics would first be passed to the model together with a cycling level of 6K. If the model predicted a pass, the same inputs would be passed with a cyling level of 7K, and so on until a cycling level is reached for which the model predicts a fail. This is the predicted endurance level for that codeword.
The output from the model is the classification result: 1 if it predicts the codeword will have more than 100 errors; otherwise 0.
3) Model Training and Testing: Typically when building machine learning models, a subset of data is removed from the data set and reserved to test the model, and the remaining data is used to train the model. A common approach is to use k-fold cross validation, in which the data is evenly split into k groups. Following this, k machine learning runs are performed. In each run, 1 group is used to test the model and the remaining k-1 groups are used to train the model. A different group is used for testing for each of the k runs. Results are then averaged across the k runs to give a representation of how well the system can generalise to solve the problem.

This research divided the 15,714 data points into 9 groups of 1,746 data points each. Eight of these groups were used for 8-fold cross validation and average results were calculated in terms of accuracy, sensitivity and specificity, as defined in Section V-B1. The ninth group was used a hold-out test set, to test a model trained on the other eight groups.

1pt and 2pt results are presented. 1pt means that a single measurement, after 5K cycles, was used as an input to the model. 2pt means that two measurements - one after 10 cycles and one after 5K cycles - were used as inputs to the model.
Results show that all the metrics selected improve the accuracy of the model. All metrics that used 2pt measurements showed an improvement in accuracy when the second measurement point was added. This proves that the rate of change of BER and program/erase timings with cycling is a valuable metric for predicting post-cycling errors.
The metric that provided the most value was codeword errors, improving the accuracy from 75.56% to 91.09% when the errors at 5K cycles were used as an input. This increased to 95% when the errors at 10 cycles were added.
The next most valuable metric was erase time, improving the accuracy from 75.56% to 87.71% for a 1pt measurement and to 89.45% for a 2pt measurement. This is a surprising result. Since erase time applies to a whole block and there are 4,096 codewords per block, it was not expected to have enough granularity to provide value at the codeword level.
Closer examination of the data showed that 90% of the codewords that failed at 10K cycles came from a single defective block, and 90% of codewords that failed at 12K cycles also came from a single defective block. The model was able to use those blocks’ erase time to identify that they were defective and predict that the majority of codewords would fail, leading to substantial improvements in the prediction accuracy.
Page number and 2pt program time provide similar improvements in overall accuracy, up to approximately 86% from the baseline of 75.56%. However, page number gives superior results for specificity at the expense of sensitivity, indicating that it is better at predicting failing codewords. Conversely, program time has higher sensitivity and lower specificity, indicating that it is more valuable for predicting passing codewords.

It can be seen that the overall accuracy for both methods of validation (cross-validation and hold-out test) exceeds 99%. Furthermore, the sensitivity and specificity are well balanced, indicating that the model can predict passing codewords and failing codewords equally well.
VI. CONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE WORK
This research gathered large-volume data from currentgeneration NAND flash devices. A number of performance metrics were characterised, such as raw bit error rate as a function of page number and P-E cycles, and program/erase timings as a function of P-E cycles. The purpose was to find flash metrics that could be measured while the device was P-E cycling, and use them to predict the true endurance of individual flash codewords.
Having identified suitable metrics, machine learning models were built which used them to predict whether a codeword would pass or fail at different cycling levels. The impact of each metric on the accuracy of the model was assessed. It was found that each of the selected metrics improved the accuracy of the model. Furthermore, it was shown that when using error rate and timing data measured from the flash device while cycling, better results are achieved with measurements taken at more than one cycling point.
Finally, prediction accuracy was shown to be greater than 99% when a model was built that used all of the selected metrics. This is a remarkable result and highlights the potential of this technique for real-world applications.
The models built in this research were based on data taken immediately after cycling. This work will be extended to incorporate other error sources such as retention and read disturb. We propose to develop individual models for each of the dominant NAND error mechanisms.
