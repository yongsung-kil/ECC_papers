A Novel Combinatorial Framework to Construct Spatially-Coupled Codes: Minimum Overlap Partitioning
Homa Esfahanizadeh, Ahmed Hareedy, and Lara Dolecek Department of Electrical Engineering, University of California, Los Angeles, USA
hesfahanizadeh@ucla.edu, ahareedy@ucla.edu, and dolecek@ee.ucla.edu

Abstract—Spatially-coupled (SC) codes are a family of graphbased codes that have attracted significant attention thanks to their capacity approaching performance. An SC code is constructed by partitioning an underlying block code into a number of components, and coupling their copies together. The number of components is determined by the memory parameter. In this paper, we study a finite length construction for the circulantbased SC codes. We introduce a new partitioning scheme, which we call minimum overlap partitioning, that outperforms previous methods. We also present a general approach for the enumeration of problematic objects in the error-floor regime that can be applied to any circulant-based SC code and to a variety of partitioning schemes. Compared to the uncoupled block codes, an SC code constructed by the new approach has more than 1.5 and 3 orders of magnitude performance improvement for the memory 1 and 2, respectively. Additionally, it outperforms the existing method of partitioning via cutting vectors by at least half an order of magnitude; this performance advantage becomes more pronounced for SC codes with higher memories.
I. INTRODUCTION
Spatially-coupled (SC) codes are a class of graph-based codes that are constructed by coupling together a series of disjoint block codes into a single coupled chain [1]. This operation can be viewed as partitioning the parity-check matrix H of a block code into component matrices Hi, where i ∈ {0, 1, · · · , m} and m is the memory, and piecing L copies of component matrices together to obtain the parity-check matrix HSC of an SC code, as shown in Fig. 1. Here, we consider circulant-based (CB) LDPC codes, where all circulants are non-zero, as the underlying block codes [2].
A majority of recent research on SC codes has been devoted to the asymptotic analysis, i.e., where the code length approaches infinity, e.g., [3], [4], among others. While the asymptotic analysis is important, the results cannot be immediately translated to the finite length case, because the underlying assumptions on the code structure, e.g., cycles, are different. There are a few works that have studied the finite length analysis and design of SC codes, [5]–[9], among others. These approaches, while promising, have some limitations: they just consider memory m = 1, focus on a restricted partitioning technique, or they are too complex to be used for practical code lengths and column weights.
In this paper, we propose a new combinatorial framework for the finite length analysis and design of circulant-based SC codes. We present a systematic scheme, that we call Minimum Overlap (MO) partitioning, for partitioning the underlying block code and constructing SC codes with a superior performance

Fig. 1: The parity-check matrix of an SC code with parameters L and m.
in the error-floor regime. The approach aims to minimize the non-zero overlap amongst the rows of the component matrices in order to structurally prevent certain detrimental structures in the graph representation of the code. As such, the approach is in particular suitable for code optimizations in the regime outside the reach of brute force methods. We demonstrate that the new scheme shows much better performance compared to the existing approach of partitioning by cutting vectors, which was first introduced in [6] and extended in [7]. We also demonstrate that by increasing memory, we can construct SC codes that can achieve a very low error rate even with a moderate code length and column weight thanks to this new partitioning scheme.
Next, we seek to find an MO partitioning that results in the minimum number of problematic objects in the errorfloor regime. These problematic objects can be trapping sets (TS), absorbing sets (AS), or cycles, depending on the code specifications and the channel model [10]. We introduce a new enumeration approach that exploits the structure of circulantbased SC codes in order to efficiently enumerate combinatorial objects of interest. Our new approach is more general than our previous work [9], since it can not only be applied to a variety of partitioning schemes including random partitioning, MO partitioning, and partitioning by cutting vectors, but it also incorporates the structure of SC codes with memories m > 1.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. In Section II, we briefly review the structure of SC codes and the definitions of problematic objects in the AWGN setting. In Section III, we propose our general approach for the enumeration of combinatorial objects within SC codes. In Section IV, we propose our novel minimum overlap partitioning scheme to construct SC codes with column weight 3. Our simulation results are given in Section V. Finally, conclusions appear in Section VI.

Under iterative decoding algorithms, certain structures in the Tanner graph of graph-based codes cause the error-floor phenomenon. These structures are error-prone, and the errors resulting from them are not necessarily codeword errors. As an example of such structures, we recall absorbing sets.

Definition 1. (cf. [10]) Consider a sub-graph induced by a subset V of VNs in the Tanner graph of a binary LDPC code. Let E be the set of even degree CNs connected to V, and similarly, let O be the set of odd degree CNs connected to V. The set V is said to be an (a, b) absorbing set (AS) if the size of V is a, the size of O is b, and each VN in V is connected to strictly more CNs in E rather than O.

In this section, we propose a novel combinatorial approach, called Minimum Overlap (MO), for partitioning the underlying block code and construction of SC codes. In MO partitioning, the resulting components do not necessarily each have a contiguous share in the underlying block code. This property is in contrast with the scheme of partitioning with cutting vectors [6], [7], in which components – by design – have large overlaps, which is, as we show later, an undesirable feature for the finite-length design. We first describe MO partitioning for SC codes with memory m = 1. Then, we consider higher memories to construct SC codes with even better error-floor performance.
For constructing SC codes, we partition κγ circulants of an underlying CB code into (m + 1) components, and piece copies of them together to construct an SC code. The ultimate goal in the partitioning step is constructing SC codes that have the minimum number of problematic objects. In this section, we focus on CB codes with γ = 3 as underlying block codes, and we consider AWGN channels. As a result, the problematic objects are (3, 3), (4, 2), and (5, 3) ASs shown in Fig. 2, as verified by comprehensive Monte Carlo simulations. In order to optimize SC codes, we seek to find a partitioning that results in a minimum number of (3, 3) ASs, since (3, 3) AS is not only one of the dominant error types but also appears as a subgraph in (4, 2) AS and one of the two (5, 3) AS topologies (cf. Fig. 2). Additionally, and as we show later, optimizing SC codes to reduce the number of (3, 3) ASs leads to a reduction of (4, 4) ASs as well, which themselves appear as sub-graphs of the other (5, 3) AS topology.
A. MO Partitioning with Memory 1
For m = 1, an underlying code is partitioned into two matrices H0 and H1, such that H = H0 + H1. Consider an underlying code with parameters κ and γ. Each circulant of H can belong to either of the two components, leading to 2κγ possible partitionings. We want to choose a partitioning that results in the least number of (3, 3) ASs. Considering all possible ways of partitioning in a brute force way to find the optimal one is not practical. We narrow down the search to a subset of partitioning options that still contains at least nearoptimal solutions. This restricted search results in SC codes that possess a significantly lower population of problematic objects, compared to existing methods [6], [7]. We first specify the circulants that belong to H0. Then, the remaining circulants belong to H1. Our new partitioning scheme is described below:
1) Each row group of H has κ circulants. We define the set S = {1, · · · , κ} which contains one element for each circulant.

V. SIMULATION RESULTS
In this section, we compare SC codes constructed by the partitioning with cutting vectors and by the MO partitioning. First, we show that both of these partitioning schemes result in SC codes that outperform the uncoupled case in which all circulants of the underlying block code are assigned to one of Hi’s. Then, we demonstrate that the MO partitioning enables a dramatic reduction in the population of problematic ASs. For both schemes, we consider memories m = 1, and 2.
For the simulation, we consider the AWGN channel and AB-SC codes, which are SC codes with AB codes as the underlying block codes. We consider γ = 3, so the object of interest is (3, 3) AS, since this configuration appears as a sub-graph of several error prone configurations, see Fig 2.
For enumerating the number of (3, 3) ASs, we use Theorem 1 that exploits the structure of SC codes to lower the complexity of the enumeration. For practical code lengths, without such an approach, it would not be possible to find the best partitioning among the collection of available choices. VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we presented a new combinatorial approach
for the finite length analysis and design of circulant-based
SC codes. Our framework encompasses a novel partitioning
scheme for the construction of SC codes, along with a general
enumeration approach that works for a variety of SC codes
with different partitioning schemes and memories. A promising
research direction is to investigate SC codes constructed by
the new scheme in modern data storage applications, such as
multilevel Flash and multidimensional magnetic recording.
