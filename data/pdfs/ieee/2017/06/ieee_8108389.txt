Iterative Decoding for the Concatenated Code to Correct Nonbinary Insertions/Deletions
Yuan Liu and Weigang Chen School of Electronic Information Engineering, Tianjin University, Tianjin, 300072, China
Email: {liuyuantju,chenwg}@tju.edu.cn

Abstract—The lack of knowledge of the transmitted codeword limits the synchronization capability of the concatenated code, consisting of a low-density parity-check (LDPC) code and a watermark code, which is used to correct nonbinary insertion/deletion errors. In order to improve the synchronizationerror-locating capability of the watermark decoder and further decrease the error probability of the system, an iterative decoding method is presented in which the extrinsic soft information from the LDPC decoder is fed back into the watermark decoder. Simulation results show that, compared with the non-iterative counterpart, significant performance gain is achieved by employing the proposed decoding scheme.
I. INTRODUCTION
In the communication systems, it is difficult that the receiver is perfectly synchronized with the transmitter [1]. Insertions and deletions [2] resulted from the loss of synchronization may occur. Since insertions/deletions can cause catastrophic bursts of substitutions, the information cannot be recovered by using traditional error-correcting coding schemes designed for random errors or short burst errors.
Codes that can correct binary insertions/deletions include algebraic block codes [3], convolutional codes [4] and concatenated codes [5]-[9]. In terms of the nonbinary insertion/deletion channel, a concatenated scheme was proposed in [10]. In this scheme, by executing the forward-backward decoding, the watermark decoder infers the position of the symbol-level insertions/deletions and generates the loglikelihood ratios (LLRs) for the outer decoder. A LDPC code is selected as the outer code. By using the soft information output of the inner decoder, the LDPC decoder corrects substitutions caused by the channel and residual errors caused by misidentified insertions/deletions.
However, in [10], the specific transmitted LDPC codeword is not considered in the computation of the forward/backward quantities, and the priori probability for the LDPC information bit is set to equal probability. It reduces the accuracy of the forward/backward quantities, which further affects the reliability of the LLRs; and then leads to a large number of residual errors in the output, which is produced by the inner decoder with weak synchronization capability. In brief, the lack of knowledge of the LDPC codeword limits the capability of the inner decoder to correct insertion/deletion errors and reduces the system performance.
In this paper, we focus on an efficient scheme for correcting nonbinary insertions/deletions, and present an iterative decoding scheme to solve the problem encountered in [10]. In this

scheme, the inner decoder and outer decoder exchange the extrinsic soft information. As the iteration number increases, the ability of the inner decoder to locate insertion/deletion errors is improved accordingly, and the number of residual errors in the sequence output by the inner decoder decreases, and the LLRs from the inner decoder are more accurate. When receiving the accurate LLRs, the outer decoder updates the codeword. Finally, by iteratively updating the codeword information outputted by the decoder, the proposed scheme outperform the non-iterative approach.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section II describes the system model of the traditional concatenated code. Section III proposes the iterative decoding scheme and gives the computation complexity. Section IV indicates that significant performance gain is achieved by employing the proposed scheme. Section V concludes the research.
II. THE NON-ITERATIVE SYSTEM
In this section, the non-iterative concatenated scheme proposed in [10] for correcting nonbinary insertions or deletions is reviewed.
A. The System Model and the Encoder
The non-iterative system model is shown in Fig. 1. The information sequence b is encoded into d by a powerful traditional channel coding. d is divided into N subsequences each having m bits. A single bit from the known watermark w of length N bits is inserted between each subsequence. Then, the codeword is mapped to the transmitted signal x by employing the mapping µ : {0, 1}m+1 → χ given in Fig. 2 for m = 2. Every m+1 bits of the concatenated codeword is mapped to a transmitted symbol. The symbol set χ includes two disjoint subsets χ0 and χ1 each having M = 2m signal points corresponding to watermark bit w = 0 and w = 1, respectively. x is then transmitted over a channel with symbollevel insertions/deletions and additive noise.

III. PROPOSED ITERATIVE DECODING SCHEME
Prior to the initial run, all the watermark decoder knows is the watermark code itself. In this case, the watermark decoder may fail to locate errors precisely. The concatenation of the watermark and LDPC decoders gives us the possibility of improving the error identification capability with iteration. Since the iterative LDPC decoding scheme is also used, there are two iterations in the proposed scheme, which are the internal iterative LDPC decoder and the external iteration with information exchange between the watermark and LDPC decoders. The structure and updating procedure of the iterative decoder are shown in Fig. 3 and Fig. 4, respectively. In Fig. 3, the extrinsic information from the LDPC decoder is fed back into the watermark decoder to update l(δ+1) in the (δ + 1)th iteration, which is also in the form of (1). Specifically, as shown in Fig. 4, the watermark and the LDPC decoding algorithms can be seen as forward-backward passing on a symbol-level trellis and belief propagation on a factor graph, respectively. The watermark decoder compares the received symbols with the modulation symbol sets and generates the conditional probability P (y|xi, w) for each symbol. Since a watermark bit and two LDPC bits are grouped as a transmitted symbol, the symbol conditional probability need to be transformed to bit-by-bit LLRs l(δ) for the binary LDPC decoder. After performing the binary LDPC decoder, the extrinsic soft information is fed back into the watermark decoder to update β(δ+1) in the next iteration. The forward/backward quantities are then refined and the LLRs are updated in the next iteration. Finally, the LDPC decoder generates a new estimate of the LDPC code. As the iteration number increases, the estimation of the LDPC codeword converges to the correct codeword.

IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
An example is used to evaluate the performance of the proposed scheme. A (3,6)-regular binary LDPC code with the code length of 4000 bits and the code rate of 1/2 is constructed using the progressive edge growth algorithm [12] as the outer code. Since m=2, the number of transmitted symbols is N=2000. I=5. Pi = Pd. The signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) is set to 20 dB. 8-ary phase-shift keying (8-PSK) modulation is used. The maximum allowed synchronization drift at the symbol-level is tmax = 5 N Pd/(1 − Pd). Forward passes are initialized as follows. F0(t) = 1 if t = 0 and F0(t) = 0 otherwise. Since block boundaries are not known to the receiver, we initialize the backward pass as follows. BN+5tmax (t) = 1/(2tmax + 1) if |t| ≤ tmax and BN+5tmax (t) = 0 otherwise. The belief-propagation (BP) algorithm in logdomain is used by the LDPC decoder. The proposed external iteration stops when δ = itermax or the decoded codeword is correct, where itermax is the maximum number of external iterations.

The proposed scheme performs significantly better than the non-iterative version as one would expect in exchange for higher decoding complexity. It is also shown that the performance gain obtained by the iterative decoding approach do not increase monotonically with increasing itermax. Thus, in order to achieve significant performance gain at the expense of a small increase in decoding complexity, it is not necessary to set large itermax when using the iterative decoding approach in practice. The optimal choice for the iterative decoding scheme is itermax = 10.
Fig. 7 shows curves of the average number of iterations for the proposed iterative decoding scheme under different maximum number of BP iterations at itermax = 30. As shown in Fig. 7, the average number of iterations for the proposed scheme is small. Thus, the convergence speed of the iterative decoding algorithm is fast. For small insertion/deletion probabilities, the performance improvement afforded by the iterative decoding come at a price of only a small increase in decoding complexity compared with the non-iterative scheme.
V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, in order to improve the capability of locating the nonbinary insertions/deletions and decrease the error probability of the concatenating system with the LDPC code and the watermark code, we propose an iterative decoding scheme. In this scheme, the decoded codeword are used to aid the identification of insertions or deletions. By exploiting the more accurate information of the transmitted codeword iteratively, this approach improves the overall performance of the non-iterative system. In the future, we can straightforwardly extend the proposed scheme to other applications using the synchronization error-correcting concatenated codes.
