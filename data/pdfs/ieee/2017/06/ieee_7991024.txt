2-Dimensional Minimum Fast-Searching Design Approach of LDPC Decoder Architecture for IEEE 802.11n/ac/ax Applications

Xin-Yu Shih and Yu-Chun Chen Department of Electrical Engineering, National Sun Yat-sen University
Kaohsiung, Taiwan, 80424, R.O.C xyshih@mail.ee.nsysu.edu.tw

Abstract—This work proposes a systematic design approach to perform minimum and second minimum fast-searching in 2dimensional xy-planes. This fast-searching approach can extremely improve the calculating timing in LDPC decoder architecture, especially for high row weights defined in IEEE 802.11n/ac/ax SPEC. In a design example with row weight of 22, our developed approach only requires about 42.9% of critical-path timing under 12.2% hardware overhead with respect to often-used binary-tree approach. This approach also extends to other various high row weights for enhancing critical-path timing performance.
Keywords—LDPC, IEEE 802.11ax, Minimum and Second Minimum Searching, Critical-Path Timing
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes were first introduced by Gallager in 1962 [1]. In the past two decades, it has been widely discussed as the modern VLSI technology makes a huge progress. In order to enhance target operating frequency or system throughput for the advanced communication requirement, the critical-path timing should be improved accordingly. The calculating timing is basically dominated by minimum and second minimum searching (min-sum algorithm [2] or layered decoding [3]), especially for high-row-weight conditions in the high-code-rate LDPC decoder systems. Therefore, we develop a systematic approach to improve critical-path timing performance.
II. PROPOSED DESIGN APPROACH
As for LDPC defined in most of current communication standards, such as IEEE 802.11n/ac/ax, the maximal row weight of higher code rate case (R=5/6) is 22. Accordingly, it’s needed to search for minimum (min) and second minimum (min2) among 22 inputs no matter what the decoding algorithm [2][3] is actually adopted. In order to clearly demonstrate proposed systematic design methodology, the overall developed procedure in 2dimensional xy-planes is divided into 4 steps (also see Fig. 1).
Step 1: All of 22 data inputs are arbitrarily placed into 3 2dimensional xy-planes, such as Plane A, B, and C. Plane A and B are both of 3x3 squares whereas Plane C is of 2x2 squares. In x direction, we make the ascending sorting for 8 data streams in

parallel (3, 3, and 2 for Plane A, B, and C, respectively). Take something for examples. 3 data inputs located in (A1,1, A2,1, and A3,1) are formed as 1 data stream in x-direction. We use 3 subtractors and 3 muxes to perform the data comparison and relocate the data as the relationship: A1,1 ≤ A2,1 ≤ A3,1. The other 7 data streams behave in the similar manner.
Step 2: In y direction, we make the ascending sorting for 6 data streams in parallel (2 for each of Plane A, B, and C). 3 data located in (A1,1, A1,2, and A1,3) are formed as 1 data stream in y direction. We also use 3 subtractors and 3 muxes to realize the data-comparing and re-locate the data as the relationship: A1,1 ≤ A1,2 ≤ A1,3. So do the other 5 data streams. The gray-colored zone indicates the data activated for required sorting comparison. In this step, there are only 16 data to be sorted, instead of 22.
Step 3: First, we pick up 9 data placed in (A1,1, A1,2, A2,1, B1,1, B1,2, B2,1, C1,1, C1,2, and C2,1) in Step 2. Second, we re-locate these 9 data into a new xy-plane with 3x3 squares (Plane K). Finally, we can do the x-direction ascending sorting for 3 data streams in parallel.
Step 4: The data located in square D is the minimum among 22 data inputs. But there needs one more step to searching for second minimum, which is the minimum value of {E, F, G}.
By using 4 steps specified, we not only handle row weight of 22, but also extend the similar design methodology to other various high row weights in a systematic way.
III. DESIGN ANALYSIS AND COMPARISON
As for 4-step systematic design methodology regarding dealing with row weight of 22, the numbers of subtractors in Step 1~4 are 20 (3*6 + 1*2), 14 (3*4 + 1*2), 9 (3*3), and 3, respectively. Totally, it needs 46 subtractors in hardware resource. In the critical-path timing point-of-view, each step is mainly dominated by only 1 subtractor while ignoring the muxes used. Thus, our critical path is just calculated as 4 subtractors in series.
In the minimum and second minimum searching approach currently applied for most of LDPC works, the common way is using binary-tree sorting/comparison [4][5]. While facing with row weight of 22, the critical path needs 8 subtractors in series.

IV. CONCLUSION
We propose a systematic approach for minimum and second minimum fast-searching in 2D xy-planes. It features shorter calculating timing under small/acceptable hardware overhead.
