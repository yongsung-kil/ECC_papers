Design of a Capacity-Approaching Chaos-Based Multiaccess Transmission System
Pingping Chen , Member, IEEE, Yi Fang , Member, IEEE, Kaixiong Su, and Guanrong Chen, Fellow, IEEE

Abstract—As an enhanced version of Walsh-code-based multiaccess differential chaos shift keying (DCSK-WC), the differentially DCSK-WC (DDCSK-WC) benefits from better error performance and stronger robustness against inter-symbol interference in multipath fading channels. In this paper, the Bahl– Cocke–Jelinek–Raviv (BCJR) decoding algorithm is applied in the detection of DDCSK-WC to form DDCSK-WC-BCJR. Theoretical analyses and simulation results demonstrate that the BCJR detector achieves a significant performance gain with respect to the conventional generalized-maximum-likelihood detector, while retaining the hardware complexity almost unchanged. To further improve the performance, protograph-based low-density-paritycheck channel codes are incorporated into the DDCSK-WC-BCJR system so as to construct a serial concatenated coding scheme. However, the capacity-approaching protograph code in additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels performs poorly in the DDCSK-WC-BCJR system over multipath fading channels. To tackle this problem, a new protograph code is designed with the help of a modified extrinsic information transfer analysis. The proposed code has decoding thresholds gap within 1.0 dB way from to the system capacity limits. Besides, the performance superiority of the protograph-coded DDCSK-WC-BCJR architecture is illustrated over a practical UWB channel. More importantly, the proposed scheme does not require channel state information as in uncoded-DCSK schemes, which makes it extremely promising for low-cost and low-complexity wireless communication applications.
Index Terms—Bahl–Cocke–Jelinek–Raviv (BCJR) detector, differentially differential chaos shift keying (DDCSK), low-densityparity-check (LDPC) codes, multi-access, Walsh code.

The receiver of DCSK requires only an auto-coherent demodulator (i.e., non-coherent demodulator) and does not need synchronization of chaotic carrier, which are of great benefit to low hardware implementation complexity. More importantly, DCSK has shown high resistance to jamming and excellent immunity against multipath fading effects [10]–[12]. In addition, some theoretical results on capacity limit of DCSK modulation for additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel and fading channels have been derived and then used as benchmarks for the DCSK-based systems [13], [14]. Also, high-efficiency DCSK [15]–[17] and M -ary DCSK (M -DCSK) [18], [19] have been proposed to improve the data rates of the DCSK systems. Recently, DCSK-based systems have been considered as good candidates in a significant amount of designs for simple wireless personal area networks (WPANs) and wireless local networks (WLANs) [20]–[22]. Besides, by combining this emerging technology with ultra-wideband (UWB) radio communications, several chaotic-based UWB modulation schemes have been proposed as possible solutions for WPANs by the IEEE 802.15.4a Task group and for wireless body area networks (WBANs) by the latest international standard IEEE 802.15.6 [23], [24].
However, a major drawback of DCSK is its relatively low spectral efficiency because the spectrum of the original signal is spread over a large bandwidth. Thus, to address this issue, we attempt to combine DCSK with multi-access communications to achieve better spectral efficiency. By combining Walsh codes and chaotic signals, a multi-access DCSK system, referred to as the Walsh-code-based DCSK (DCSK-WC) system, has been proposed [2]. The Walsh codes is exploited to ensure the orthogonal multi-access channels such that multi-access interference can be avoided. In addition, for the existing UWB multi-access schemes, a new DCSK solution based on chaotic pulse cluster signals has been proposed to enhance the user capability limited by the order of the Walsh codes [25]. It also has been shown that the performance of cooperative DCSK-WC can be greatly improved, benefiting from the cooperative diversity provided by independent multipath fading channels [26], [27]. Moreover, gaining insight into the properties of the Walsh codes and chaotic signals, the transmitting data frame of DCSK-WC was redesigned, forming the differentially DCSK-WC (DDCSK-WC) transmission scheme [28]. Exploiting the inherent relationship between the transmitted sequences, it was recently proposed to adopt the Bahl-CockeJelinek-Raviv (BCJR) decoding algorithm for the demodulation of DDCSK-WC [30]. The simulation results have shown that the resultant DDCSK-WC-BCJR system achieves a significant 2 dB performance gain over the conventional DDCSK-WC (i.e., DDCSK-WC-Conv) system, almost without increasing hardware implementation complexity.
The DDCSK-WC-BCJR is a general technique that can be seamlessly combined with channel coding. Consequently, protograph-based low-density-parity-check (LDPC) codes are incorporated into the proposed DDCSK-WC-BCJR system with an aim to further boosting the error performance. Indeed, protograph LDPC codes have been taken into account to improve the performance of chaos-based systems in [31], [33]. However, due to the fact that a BCJR detector is induced, it is found that the protograph code that is good for AWGN channels no longer performs well in the DDCSK-WC-BCJR framework. Therefore, a simple method is conceived to design a good protograph code for DDCSK-WC-BCJR with the help of the extrinsic-information-transfer (EXIT) analysis. The proposed rate-1/2 protograph code has an asymptotic decoding threshold of 11.25 dB, which is 0.9 dB away from the capacity limit. Simulations also exhibit that the protograph codeword of 4096 bits has a gap of 1.8 dB to the capacity limit of DDCSK-WC-BCJR at a bit-error-rate (BER) of 10−6. To validate the feasibility of this coding scheme in practical applications, it will be shown that the performance superiority of protograph-coded frequencymodulated (FM)-DDCSK-WC-BCJR is prominent over a typical UWB channel. Note that this chaos-based protograph-coded system does not require any complicated channel estimation, carrier synchronization or Rake reception, as in the original DCSK-WC. Therefore, it possesses great potential to become a good alternative for chaos-based multi-access communications over multipath channels, such as WLAN/WPAN channels.

IV. CHANNEL-CODED DDCSK-WC-BCJR
This section investigates the application of LDPC codes to DDCSK-WC-BCJR for enhancing the error performance. One can view the LDPC-coded DDCSK-WC-BCJR as a concatenated code of an inner BCJR detector and an outer LDPC channel code. Therefore, the receiver can apply a turbo decoder that iteratively exchanges messages between the demodulator and the decoder, as shown in Fig. 2. Given the received messages, the decoding of such a concatenated code system is described as follows:
(1) The channel message is first decoded by the BCJR channel detector, as described in Section III-C.
(2) The output extrinsic messages from the BCJR detector are fed into the LDPC decoder as a priori messages. Then, MS decoding is used, which does not require CSI for the channel decoder. If the decoding is successful or the maximum turbo iteration number is reached, then the decoding algorithm stops. Otherwise, proceed to the next step.
(3) The output extrinsic messages from the channel decoder are fed into the BCJR detector as a priori messages. After BCJR decoding, go to Step 2.
Remark: In UWB-based low-complexity and low-cost applications, the complex channel estimation should be avoided. Indeed, it is difficult to obtain reliable channel estimation for a typical UWB channel of a large number of resolvable multipaths. The proposed decoding algorithm suggests that the chaotic demodulation and channel decoding are jointly accomplished without the need of any CSI as in the case of demodulating the conventional DCSK signals. Therefore, such a chaos-based coded scheme is hardware-efficient for practical UWB applications because it only incorporates extra additions in the MS operations as compared to the uncoded DCSK systems.

A. Background of Protograph-Based LDPC Codes

A protograph code [32], [36] is a structured LDPC code,

which can be constructed from a small protograph. Proto-

graph codes over AWGN channels have demonstrated very good

thresholds with low encoder complexity as well as fast decod-

ing. A protograph consists of the variable nodes set V , the

check nodes set C and the edges set. In a protograph, parallel

edges are allowed. A protograph with N variable nodes and M

check nodes can be represented by a base matrix B of dimension

M × N . The (i, j)-th element of B, denoted by bi,j , represents

the number of edges connecting the variable node Vj to the

check node Ci. One can obtain a larger protograph graph from

a given protograph by a “copy-and-permute” operation.

The EXIT analysis can evaluate a decoding threshold of the

binary protograph codes [37], where the mutual information be-

tween the check nodes and the variable nodes is tracked. By

modifying the traditional protograph EXIT analysis for AWGN

channels, one can evaluate the performance of the chaos-based

DDCSK-WC-BCJR system over multipath fading channels.

D. Analysis and Design of Protograph LDPC Codes

This section proposes a protograph-based LDPC code for

DDCSK-WC-BCJR over multipath Rayleigh fading channels

with the help of EXIT analysis. For an AWGN channel, the

decoder is a simple LDPC decoder. Channel LLR messages

are passed into the decoder and then the LLR messages are ex-

changed iteratively between the variable-node set and the check-

node set in the decoder. However, the decoder in DDCSK-WC-

BCJR over a multipath channel consists of an inner detector

(BCJR detector) and an outer decoder (LDPC decoder). The

channel messages distorted by multipath fading and AWGN

noise are first passed to the inner detector. Subsequently, the

extrinsic LLR values are exchanged between the inner detector

and the outer decoder in each turbo iteration. While it is known

that punctured variable nodes with a higher degree (as in the

AR4JA code) can be recovered more easily for an AWGN chan-

nel, such punctured nodes induce inferior error performance in

this iterative decoding system with an inner detector.

VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, a joint chaos-based detection and decoding algorithm for the coded DDCSK-WC-BCJR has been proposed, where the messages are iteratively exchanged between the BCJR detector and the LDPC decoder. Indeed, the demodulation of DDCSK-WC-BCJR takes into consideration both characteristics of chaotic modulation and DDCSK-WC transmission to enhance the received SNR. Theoretical analyses and simulated results have shown that the DDCSK-WC-BCJR enables performance gains of 2 dB and 4 dB over the DDCSK-WC-Conv and DCSK-WC, respectively, in the overall SNR region under study.
To further improve the performance of DDCSK-WC-BCJR, protograph-based channel codes have been exploited to form a serial concatenated coding system. Unfortunately, the conventional protograph code having excellent error performance in AWGN channels is not able to perform well in DDCSK-WCBCJR systems over multipath fading channels. To overcome this weakness, a new protograph code has been designed, which significantly outperforms the conventional protograph code in both multipath fading and UWB channels. In particular, the proposed protograph code is only 1.9 dB from the capacity limit at a BER of 10−5 over a two-ray multipath fading channel. It is worth noting that the protograph-coded DDCSK-WC-BCJR system requires neither complicated channel estimation nor Rake reception by using min-sum decoding. Therefore, the proposed chaos-based framework is of great usefulness to enhance the quality of signal transmission in low-cost and low-complexity wireless communication networks.

He has been a Chair Professor and the Founding Director of the Centre for Chaos and Complex Networks, City University of Hong Kong, Hong Kong, since 2000, prior to that he was a tenured Full Professor at the University of Houston, Houston, TX, USA. He received the 2011 Euler Gold Medal, Russia, and was conferred Honorary Doctorate by the Saint Petersburg State University, Russia, in 2011 and by the University of Le Havre, Romandie, France in 2014. He is a member of the Academia of Europe and a Fellow of The World Academy of Sciences, and is a Highly Cited Researcher in Engineering as well as in Mathematics according to Thomson Reuters.
