Concatenated Finite Geometry and Finite Field LDPC Codes

Mona Nasseri1, Xin Xiao2, Shaoliang Zhang3, Ting Wang3, Shu Lin1
1University of California, Davis, USA, 95616, 2University of Arizona, Tucson, AZ 85721
3NEC Laboratories America, Inc Princeton, NH 08540 mnasser@ucdavis.edu

Abstract— This paper presents two types of concatenated finite-geometry and finite-field LDPC codes which have the distinct features of both finite geometry and finite field LDPC codes, such as large minimum distances, no small trapping sets, fast decoding convergence, capable of correcting both random errors and bursts of erasures, flexibility in code construction, cyclic and quasi-cyclic structures. It is shown that these concatenated codes are globally coupled LDPC codes and they perform well over the AWGN and binary erasure channels with a two-phase iterative decoding scheme.
Keywords—Concatenated LDPC code; Trapping set; Low error floor; Finite Geometry; Globally coupled LDPC code.
I. INTRODUCTION
Concatenated coding scheme is prevalent and has been used for error control in many communication systems over past 50 years since its introduction in 1966 [1]. In this paper, we use this coding scheme to concatenate finite geometry (FG) and finite field (FF) LDPC codes to form a class of concatenated LDPC codes which have the distinct features of both FG and FF-LDPC codes such as large minimum distances, no small trapping sets, fast decoding convergence, capable of correcting both random errors and bursts of erasures, cyclic and quasi-cyclic structures, and flexibility in code construction. These codes are referred to as concatenated FGFF-LDPC codes whose parity-check matrices can be formulated. Based on the structure of their parity-check matrices, we find that concatenated FG-FF-LDPC codes are globally coupled LDPC codes. Concatenated FG-FF-LDPC codes perform well over the AWGN channel and the binary erasure channel (BEC).
LDPC codes [2] perform amazingly well with iterative decoding algorithms, such as the sum-product algorithm (SPA) [3] or the min-sum algorithm (MSA) [4]. However, with iterative decoding, most LDPC codes have a common severe weakness, known as error-floor [5]. For the AWGN channel, the error-floor of an LDPC code is mostly caused by an undesirable structure, known as a trapping set [6], in the Tanner graph of the code based on which the decoding is carried out.
Let G be the Tanner graph of a binary LDPC code C given by the null space of an m × n matrix H over GF(2). For 1  ț  n and 0  Ĳ  m, a (ț, Ĳ) trapping set is a set T(ț, Ĳ) of ț variable nodes (VNs) in G which induce a subgraph of G with

exactly Ĳ odd-degree check nodes (CN) and an arbitrary number of even-degree CNs. The parameter ț is called the size of the trapping set T(ț, Ĳ). A trapping set simply corresponds to an error pattern which prevents an iterative LDPC-decoder to converge. For the AWGN channel, error patterns with small number of errors are more probable than error patterns with larger number of errors. Consequently, in iterative decoding algorithms, the most harmful trapping sets are the trapping sets of small sizes which generally result in high error-floors. If an LDPC code has a reasonable large minimum distance and its Tanner graph contains no trapping sets with sizes much smaller than its minimum distance, the code can achieve a low error rate without an error-floor.
Since FG-LDPC codes have large minimum distances [7] and their Tanner graphs contain no trapping sets of sizes smaller than their distances minus 3 [8], [9], they can achieve very low error rates without error-floors. Unfortunately, FGLDPC codes are not very flexible in construction, especially in the choice of their lengths. There are many other classes of structured LDPC codes constructed based on finite fields. These codes, called finite field (FF) LDPC codes, have quasicyclic (QC) structure and their Tanner graphs have girth at least 6 [10]. QC-structure simplifies both encoding and decoding implementations. FF-LDPC codes are very flexible in construction in terms of lengths and rates. They, in general, perform very well over the AWGN channel in the waterfall region and can achieve relatively low error-floor. However, their exact minimum distances and trapping structures are generally unknown and their Tanner graphs do contain small trapping sets if the column weights of their parity-check matrices are small. The objective of this paper is to concatenate these two types of codes to obtain concatenated LDPC codes which achieve most distinct features of these two types of codes.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section II gives a brief review of FG-LDPC codes and their distinct features. Section III presents a very flexible method for constructing FF-LDPC codes. Section IV presents two types of concatenated FG-FF-LDPC codes and their decoding methods. Section V uses an example to demonstrate the construction and performance of a specific concatenated FGFF-LDPC code. Section VI concludes the paper.

II. CYCLIC FG-LDPC CODES
FG-LDPC codes were introduced in 2001 [7]. These codes are constructed based on finite Euclidean and projective geometries (EGs and PGs). Among the FG-LDPC codes, the most impressive subclass codes are the codes constructed based on the lines in two-dimensional EGs or PGs over finite fields of characteristic 2. A two-dimensional projective geometry over the field GF(2s), denoted by PG(2, 2s) consists of 4s + 2s + 1points and 4s + 2s +1 lines, each line consisting of 2s + 1 points. The geometry has no origin. For each point in PG(2, 2s), there are 2s + 1 lines intersecting it. The paritycheck matrix HPG of a PG-LDPC code constructed based on the incidence vectors of the lines in PG(2, 2s) is a (4s +2s +1) × (4s +2s +1) circulant matrix over GF(2). It has constant column and row weights 2s + 1. The rank of HPG is 3s + 1. The null space over GF(2) of HPG gives a (2s + 1, 2s + 1)-regular (4s + 2s + 1, 4s – 3s + 2s) cyclic PG-LDPC code of length 4s + 2s + 1, dimension 4s – 3s + 2s, and minimum distance 2s + 2. Its Tanner graph contains no trapping sets with sizes smaller than 2s – 1 [8], [9]. An EG-LDPC code constructed based on the lines of a two-dimensional Euclidean geometry EG(2, 2s) has almost the same structural properties as that of a PGLDPC code. In this paper, we only use PG-LDPC codes to illustrate the constructions of concatenated FG-FF-LDPC codes. A two-dimensional FG-LDPC code decoded with an iterative decoding algorithm, such as the SPA or the MSA, performs very well over the AWGN channel and can achieve a very low error rate without an error-floor.
The parity-check matrix HPG of PG-LDPC code has a very large row redundancy (large number of redundant rows). This large row redundancy of the parity-check matrix of the code together with no small harmful trapping sets in its Tanner graph result in a fast rate of decoding convergence and a very low error-floor. Large row redundancy results in a large column weight which increases the complexity of an iterative decoder. However, based on the cyclic structure of the paritycheck matrix HPG of the code, the reduced-complexity revolving iterative decoding (RID) scheme proposed in [15] can be used to reduce the decoder complexity and memory size significantly without (or with a little) performance degradation. The decoding is based on a submatrix of HPG that consists of any l consecutive rows of HPG, even l = 1.
Example 1: Consider the projective geometry PG(2, 25) which contains 1057 points and 1057 lines. Based on 1057 lines of PG(2, 25), we construct a 1057 × 1057 circulant matrix HPG of rank 244 with both column and row weights 33. The null space over GF(2) of HPG gives a binary (33, 33)regular (1057, 813) cyclic PG-LDPC code with rate 0.7691 and minimum distance 34 whose Tanner graph GPG has girth 6 and contains no trapping sets of sizes smaller than 31. Each VN in GPG is connected to other 1056 VNs in GPG by paths of length 2 which is called the connection number. Therefore, each VN in GPG has a very large connectivity. This large VN-connectivity results in fast information exchange in each decoding iteration and hence makes the iterative decoding of the code converge quickly,

i.e., the decoding requires a small number of iterations to terminate successfully. With all the above distinct structural properties, large minimum distance, no small harmful trapping sets, large row redundancy and large VN-connectivity, the code decoded with either the SPA [3] or the MSA [4], can achieve a very low error-rate without an error-floor and a fast rate of decoding convergence. The bit error rate (BER) and block error rate (BLER) of the code over the AWGN channel using BPSK signaling decoded with 5, 10 and 50 iterations of the MSA are shown in Fig. 1. We see that the decoding converges very fast. The code’s waterfall error performance drops fast and smoothly. At the BER of 10-8, the code decoded with 50 iterations of the MSA performs about 2.2 dB from the Shannon limit (1.784 dB). At the BER of 10-7, the performance gap between 5 and 50 decoding iterations is about 0.2 dB and performance gap between 10 and 50 decoding iterations is about 0.1 dB.
Since the parity-check matrix of the (1057, 813) cyclic PG-LDPC code has a large column weight, the hardware implementation of an iterative decoder for this code based on its entire parity-check matrix may be very complex. As pointed out earlier, a cyclic LDPC code can be decoded with the RID scheme proposed in [15] using a small submatrix of its parity-check matrix for decoding in a revolving manner to reduce the decoder hardware complexity and memory size significantly without (or a very little) performance degradation. To demonstrate this, we decode the (1057, 813) cyclic PG-LDPC code using the RID scheme in conjunction with the MSA, denoted by RID/MSA. Let l = 244, which is the rank of HPG. Suppose we decode the code with the RID/MSA using a decoding matrix HPG,dec which consists of the top 244 rows of HPG and is an irregular parity check matrix with column weights from 5 to 13 and the average column weight of 7.6. The BER and BLER performances of the code over the AWGN channel using BPSK signaling decoded based on HPG,dec with 5, 10 and 50 iterations of the RID/MSA are shown in Fig. 2. We see that the RID/MSA decoding of the (1057, 813) PG-LDPC code also converges very fast. From Figures 1 and 2, we see that the MSA and RID/MSA perform almost the same. At the BER of 10-10, the code decoded with 50 iterations of the RID/MSA performs about 2.4 dB from the Shannon limit. The performance gap between 5 and 50 is less than 0.2 dB. With the above decoding matrix, the RID/MSA reduces the decoder hardware complexity by a factor almost 5.
An FG-LDPC code is also one-step majority-logic decodable and it can correct 2(s-1) or fewer random errors with a simple one-step majority-logic-decoding (OS-MLGD) algorithm (a hard-decision decoding algorithm) [7], [10], [11]. The decoding is based on the structure that for each code symbol, 2s + 1 (a PG-LDPC code) check-sums orthogonal on the code symbol can be formed. Based on these orthogonal check-sums, a PG-LDPC code can also correct up to 2s + 1 random erasures for the BEC.
Since an FG-LDPC code is cyclic, it can be shortened by various degrees to obtain shortened FG-LDPC codes of various lengths and rates [11]. The encoder and decoder designed for the un-shortened FG-LDPC code can be used for encoding and decoding of a shortened FG-LDPC code [11]. A shortened FG-

The matrix B(m, n) has the following structural properties: (1) All the entries in a row (or a column) are distinct elements of GF(q); (2) Each row (or each column) contains at most one zero element; (3) No two rows (or two columns) have identical entries in any position and (4) Each 2×2 submatrix of B(m, n) is non-singular. The 4-th structural property is referred to as the 2×2 submatrix (SM) constraint [12], [13].
For 0  i < q-1, we represent the element Įi in GF(q) by a circulant permutation matrix (CPM) of size (q-1)×(q–1) (with columns and rows labeled from 0 to q–2) whose generator (or the top row) has a single 1-component at the position i. We call the above matrix representation of a field element as the CPM-dispersion [10], [12] - [14]. The CPM-dispersion of a field element in GF(q) is unique. The inverse of the CPM-dispersion A(Įi) gives the field element Įi. Hence, the mapping defined by the CPMdispersion is one-to-one.

This array H(m, n) is called the CPM-dispersion of B(m, n) and is an m(q– 1)×n(q–1) matrix over GF(2). The 2×2 SM-constraint structure of B(m, n) ensures that the array H(m, n) as a m(q– 1)×n(q–1) matrix satisfies the RC-constraint [10], [12] - [14] i.e., no two rows in H(m, n) have more than one position where they both have 1-components. The RC-constraint structure of H(m, n) ensures that the Tanner graph associated with H(m, n) has girth at least 6 [10], [12] - [14].

The matrix B(m, n) is called the base matrix for constructing the code CFF(m, n) (or its parity-check matrix H(m, n)). Each VN in G(6, 80) is connected to other 6×79 = 474 VNs by paths of length 2. Hence, each VN in G(6, 80) has a large connectivity. In each decoding iteration of a chosen iterative decoding algorithm, a VN receives extrinsic information from 474 other VN’s. This results in a large amount of information exchanges between VNs in each decoding iteration and hence a very fast rate of decoding convergence.

At the BER of 10-8, the code performs about 1.4 dB from the Shannon limit (3.6 dB) with 50 iterations of the MSA. This code may find applications in high-speed optical communication systems and flash memory.
IV. CONCATENATED FG-FF-LDPC LDPC CODES
In this section, we present constructions of two types of concatenated FG-FF-LDPC codes and show that these concatenated LDPC codes possess the distinct features of both the FG- and the FF- LDPC codes.

B. Decoding
Based on the structure of a type-1 concatenated FG-FF-LDPC code CFG,FF,casc, two decoding methods can be devised to decode the code. Let r be the received word of cn0 + r symbols. We decode r in two phases. The two-phase decoding can be carried out in two different manners: (1) inner/outer decoding; and (2) outer/inner decoding. Both decoding schemes may be considered as a form of serial turbo decoding [10], [11]. Each decoding phase is carried out iteratively using an iterative decoding algorithm.
With the inner/outer decoding scheme, we first decode r based on the parity-check matrix HFF of the FFLDPC inner code CFF using a chosen iterative decoding algorithm, say the SPA or the MSA. Set the maximum number of inner decoding iterations to Iin,max. Decode r based on the chosen inner iterative algorithm. At the end of each inner decoding iteration, we check the syndrome SFF of the decoded word x based on HFF. If SFF = 0, then x is codeword in CFF. If SFF  0, we continue the inner iterative decoding until either the decoded word x is a codeword in CFF or the

preset maximum number Iin,max of inner decoding iterations is reached.
If a codeword w in CFF is obtained during the inner decoding phase, we remove all the r inner parity-check symbols in w. This gives c decoded words v0, v1,…, vc-1 for the c transmitted outer codewords in CFG. For each decoded word vi, 0 i < c, we compute its syndrome SFG,i based on the parity-check matrix HFG of the outer FG-LDPC code CFG. If the syndromes of c decoded words are all zeroes, then v0, v1,…, vc-1 are codewords in the outer code CPG. In this case, we stop the entire decoding process. We then remove all the parity symbols from the c decoded outer codewords and deliver the ck0 decoded information bits to the user.
If the syndrome of any of the c decoded words is not zero, the outer code decoder is activated to perform decoding on the decoded words whose syndromes are not zero. These words are referred as failed words. The decoding algorithm used for decoding the outer code can be the same or different from the decoding algorithm used for decoding the inner code.
For decoding of each decoded word at the output of inner decoder whose syndrome is not zero, we decode it based on the chosen decoding algorithm. Set the maximum number of outer decoding iterations to Iout,max. If the outer code decoding of all the failed words outputted by the inner decoder is successful, we remove all the parity bits from all the decoded outer codewords and deliver the ck0 decoded information bits to the user. If the decoding of any failed word at the output of the inner decoder is unsuccessful after Iout,max iterations, we switch back to the inner code decoding with the decoded information and the channel information as input to decode the received word r again with the chosen inner decoding algorithm. We perform the inner/outer decoding process iteratively until either the entire decoding is successful or a present maximum number Iin/out,max of inner/outer decoding iterations is reached. With the inner/outer decoding of the concatenated FG-FFLDPC code, the outer decoding is used to correct the local errors that the inner decoder fails to correct. The inner/outer iterative decoding process is a serial turbo/iterative decoding process.
With the above two-phase decoding, no trapping set of size d0 - 3 or smaller will trap the outer decoder, where d0 is the minimum distance of the outer FG-LDPC code which is very large as stated in Section II. In fact, no trapping set of size d0 - 3 or smaller, regardless of the distribution of its VNs, will trap the two-phase decoder of the concatenated FG-FFLDPC code CFG,FF,casc. If a trapping set of size d0 - 3 or less than that traps the inner decoder, it will be un-trapped by the outer decoder. This is a unique feature of the concatenated FG-FF-LDPC code CFG,FF,casc. This is to say that with the twophase inner/outer iterative decoding, the Tanner graph of the concatenated FG-FF-LDPC code CFG,FF,casc contains no harmful trapping set with size smaller than d0 - 3.
Since every codeword in CFG,FF,casc consists of c outer codewords in the outer code CFG, the inner decoding is actually performed on a collection of c received outer codewords, jointly. During the decoding process, the

reliability information of each decoded outer codeword is shared by the others to enhance the overall reliability of all decoded outer codewords. This joint-decoding and information sharing at the inner decoding phase may reduce the probability of performing the outer decoding phase and cause the outer decoding of the received outer codewords to converge more quickly. This is another important feature of the concatenated FG-FF-LDPC code CFG,FF,casc.
Opposite to inner/outer decoding, we can decode CFG,FF,casc in a reverse order. Recall that each concatenated codeword in systematic form consists of c outer codewords in cascade followed by r parity-check symbols of the inner code CFF. In outer/inner decoding of CFG,FF,casc, outer decoding is carried out as soon as a word of n0 symbols corresponding to a transmitted outer codeword is received. If errors spread out among the c received outer codewords and errors in each received outer codeword do not create a harmful trapping set with size greater than d0 - 3, each outer decoding will be successful. In this case, inner decoding is avoided. If outer decoding fails to give c correctly decoded outer codewords after Iout,max decoding iterations, inner iterative decoding is then activated. The rest of the decoding process is the same as the inner/outer decoding. The outer/inner decoding is effective for correcting local random errors and erasures.
C. Interleaving Concatenation
At the end of first stage of concatenated encoding, instead of cascading the c outer codewords v0, v1,…, vc-1 at the output of the outer code encoder, we interleave the c outer codewords to form an interleaved word y = (y0, y1,…, yn0 -1) which consists of n0 sections, each containing of c code symbols. For 0 j < n0, the j-th section yj consists of the j-th symbols from v0, v1,…, vc-1. The interleaving operation is known as the bit interleaving. There are 2ck0 such interleaved words which form an (cn0, ck0) linear code, denoted CFG,interl(c) and called an interleaved code of the outer FG-LDPC code CFG. The interleaved codeword y is then used as the input sequence to the inner encoder and then encoded into a codeword in the inner FF-LDPC code CFF for transmission. This interleaved concatenation of the outer FG-LDPC code CFG and inner FFLDPC code CFF results in a concatenated FG-FF-LDPC of type-2, denoted CFG,FF,interl. The parameter c is now called the interleaving degree.
As pointed out in section II, the outer FG-LDPC code CFG is capable of correcting d0 - 1 random erasures where d0 is the minimum distance of CFG (d0 = 2s + 2 for a PG-LDPC codes). Then, the interleaved code CFG,interl(c) is capable of correcting any erasure pattern with erasures distributed in d0-1 or fewer sections in a received interleaved word y'=(y'0, y'1,…, y'n0-1) of cn0 symbols. After deinterleaving y', each received outer codeword v'i, 0  i < c, contains at most d0 - 1 erasures which can be corrected by the outer FG-LDPC code CFG. This implies that a single burst of erasures of length c(d0 - 1) or shorter can be corrected after deinterleaving. This also implies that during transmission of an interleaved codeword y = (y0, y1,…, yn0-1), if d0 - 1 or fewer sections of y are erased, they can be recovered by the outer

FG-LDPC code CFG after de-interleaving. Based on the erasure correction feature of the interleaved code CFG,interl(c) of the outer FG-code, we see that the type-2 concatenated FGFF-LDPC code CFG,FF,interl is effective in correcting erasures, random or bursts.

The two schemes proposed for decoding a type-1 concatenated FG-FF-LDPC code can be applied to decode a type-2 concatenated FG-FF-LDPC code. However, in decoding a type-2 concatenated FG-FF-LDPC code, deinterleaving and interleaving of the decoded words at the outputs of the inner and outer decoders must be performed before switching from one decoding phase to the other. At the end of the inner decoding phase, the decoded interleaved word must be de-interleaved into c decoded outer codewords before the outer decoding phase begins, and at the end of the outer decoding phase, the c decoded outer codewords must be interleaved before the inner decoding begins. Since the type-1 and type-2 concatenated FG-FF-LDPC codes are combinatorially equivalent, they perform the same over the AWGN channel if the same decoding scheme is used. The two types of codes have the same trapping set structure, minimum distances and fast rate of decoding convergence. For correcting erasures, the outer/inner decoding scheme should be used. In this case, the outer decoder should be implemented to perform both iterative decoding and erasure correction.
V. AN EXAMPLE
In this section, we use an example to illustrate the design of an (8000, 5600) concatenated FG-FF-LDPC code of length 8000 bits and rate 0.7. In designing such a code, we use the (1057, 813) cyclic PG-LDPC code CPG given in example 1 as the outer code and the (6, 80)-regular (8000, 7400) QC-FFLDPC code CFF(6, 80) given in Example 2 as the inner code.

In constructing a type-1 concatenated FG-FF-LDPC code, we set the cascading degree c = 7. An information sequence of 5600 bits is first divided into 7 messages, each consisting of 800 information bits. Then, 13 zero bits (fill-in bits) are added to each message at the front end to obtain an augmented message of 813 bits. Then, each of the 7 augmented messages is encoded into a codeword in the (1057, 813) outer PG-LDPC code CPG. Outer encoding of 7 augmented messages results in 7 codewords v0, v1, v2, v3, v4, v5,v6 in CPG. Since the code CFG is in systematic form, the first 13 code bits (information bits) of each of these 7 codewords are zeroes. Next, we cascade these 7 codewords to form a cascaded codeword v = (v0, v1, v2, v3, v4, v5,v6 ) of 7399 code bits. There are 25600 such cascaded codewords which form a (7399, 5600) cascaded code CFG,casc(7). Now, we add a fill-in zero bit to each cascaded codeword in CFG,casc(7). This results in a (7400, 5600) extended cascaded code of length 7400, denoted by CFG,casc,ext(7).
Next, each codeword in CFG,casc,ext(7) is used as an information sequence and encoded into a codeword w in the (8000, 7400) FF-LDPC code CFF. The encoding of the 25600 codewords in CFG,casc,ext(7) results in 25600 codewords in the inner FF-LDPC code CFF(6,8) which form a type-1 concatenated FG-FF-LDPC code CPG,FF,casc of length 8000. Since each codeword in CPG,FF,casc contains 5600 information bits, it is a (8000, 5600) code with rate 0.7. The code is a globally coupled LDPC code. Its Tanner graph contains 7 disjoint copies of the Tanner graph GPG of CPG connected by 600 global CNs which correspond to the 600 parity-check constraints on the inner code CFF(6, 80). Since GPG contains no harmful trapping sets of size smaller than 31, no trapping set of size 31 or less will trap the inner/outer two-phase decoder of the code CPG,FF,casc.
To carry out the decoding, we first decode the received word r based on the (8000, 7400) FF-LDPC inner code CFF(6, 80) using the MSA. At the end of each inner decoding iteration, we check the syndrome SFF of the decoded word x based on parity-check matrix H(6, 80) of the inner code CFF(6, 80). If SFF = 0, then x is codeword in CFF(6, 80). If SFF  0, we continue the inner iterative decoding until either the decoded word x is a codeword in CFF(6, 80) or the preset maximum number Iin,max of inner decoding iterations is reached. If a codeword w in CFF(6, 80) is obtained during the inner decoding phase, we remove all the inner parity-check bits in w and the fill-in zero bit. This gives 7 decoded words v'0, v'1, v'2, v'3, v'4, v'5, v'6 for the 7 transmitted outer codewords in CPG. For each decoded word v'i, we compute its syndrome SPG,i, 1  i  7, based on the parity-check matrix HPG of CPG. If the syndromes of 7 decoded words are zero, then v'0, v'1, v'2, v'3, v'4, v'5, v'6 are codewords in the outer code CPG. In this case, we stop the entire decoding process. We then remove all the parity bits and the 91 fill-in zero bits from the 7 decoded outer codewords and deliver the 5600 decoded information bits to the user.

VI. CONCLUSION AND REMARKS
In this paper, we presented two types of concatenated FGFF-LDPC codes which have the distinct feature of both FGand FF-LDPC codes, large minimum distances, no small trapping sets, fast decoding convergence, capable of correcting both random errors and bursts of erasures and flexibility in code construction. Parity-check matrices of both types of concatenated codes were given. Based on the structures of these parity-check matrices, we showed that both types of concatenated codes are CN-based globally coupled LDPC codes. The Tanner graph of such a code is composed of multiple disjoint copies of the Tanner graph of the outer FG-LDPC code which are connected by a set of global CNs. Two two-phase iterative decoding schemes for the two types of concatenated FG-FF-LDPC codes were presented. These two-phase iterative decoding schemes are serial turbo-iterative decoding schemes. We showed that, decoding of a concatenated FG-FF-LDPC code with either of the two-phase decoding schemes, the error-floor performance of the code is dominated by the trapping set structure of the outer FG-LDPC code. Since the outer FG-LDPC code has a very large minimum distance d0 and its Tanner graph contains no trapping sets with sizes smaller than d0 – 3. It is expected that a concatenated FG-FF-LDPC code can achieve a very low error rate without an error-floor and also a large coding gain. In the paper, we also showed that the type-2 concatenated FGFF-LDPC codes are also very effective in correction of

random bursts of erasures or a single long burst of erasures. To reduce the decoder complexity, the revolving iterative decoding (RID) schemes proposed in [14] and [15] can be used, which reduce the decoding complexity significantly without degrading the error performance of a code.
