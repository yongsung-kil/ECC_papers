Yannick Saouter, Member, IEEE

Abstract‚Äî In this letter, a new framework for the construction of low density parity check codes is exposed. Parameters of the new codes are estimated, performance simulations are reported and compared with some classical constructions.

I. INTRODUCTION
I N THE scientific literature, a large number of algebraic constructions for low density parity check codes (LDPCs) have been given. Various structures have been used for that purpose including finite geometries [1], finite fields [2], combinatorial designs [3]. In this letter, new families of LDPCs based on elliptic curves are described.

D. Girth 6 Condition
The matrix MK(i, j ) will be used as a parity matrix to define error correcting codes. In this section, we prove that the
incidence structure has girth at least 6, i.e. that two elements of P are incident to at most one row of MK(i, j ). This condition is important to obtain codes with good decoding
performance under belief propagation decoding. Let then P0 = (x0, y0) and P1 = (x1, y1) be representatives
of two distinct elements of P . By construction of P , we have P0 = P1 and P0 = ‚àíP1. If the two corresponding elements of P are incident to a row of MK(i, j ) parametrized by (A, B), then as pairs of K2, P0 and P1 belong to the reduced elliptic curve defined by A and B.

IV. APPLICATION TO CODING THEORY
Parity check matrices of the new LDPC codes are built from matrices MK. In order to obtain efficient codes of various encoding rates, some rows of parity check matrices might be deleted. The used strategy is to eliminate groups of rows with the same Hamming weight. Moreover columns were possibly deleted, either if they intersect with less than three other columns or on the contrary if they intersect with too much other columns. This criterion allows the code to have a decent convergence threshold.
For instance, when K = GF(32), the obtained incidence structure has 992 rows and 527 columns. In this structure, we preserved only rows with Hamming weight 14. The new structure has then 155 rows and 527 columns. Then columns with weight smaller than 2 or larger than 8 were deleted. The obtained incidence matrix is the parity check matrix of the code. This matrix has 155 rows and 351 columns, turns out to be full row rank and defines a [351, 196] binary LDPC code.
Performance was obtained after at most 50 iterations of floating point belief propagation decoding of frames modulated by BPSK modulation over an AWGN channel. Figures 1-3 depict the bit error rate performance of various codes versus the signal to noise ratio of the channel. Most of these codes behave well under belief propagation decoding. However, the case of the [336, 192] code in Figure 3 proves that strong flattening can also occurs.

The decoding cost of a LDPC code is proportional to the number of non-null entries in its decoding matrix. For projective geometry codes, this number is close to the Moore bound, giving then an almost maximal decoding cost. For instance, the [4161, 3431] parity check matrix has 65 non null entries per column. On the contrary, Kirkman triple systems codes have a constant column weight of 3 and their decoding cost is minimal. Elliptic curve codes lie in an intermediate position. For the [3240, 2704] code, column weight is constant and equal to 5. The decoding cost of this code is then less than twice that of the [3258, 2715] code and 13 times smaller than that of the projective geometry code.

V. CONCLUSION
In this letter, a new framework for the construction of LDPC codes is presented. The new proposition makes use of elliptic curves and obtained performance is satisfactory, when compared to classical constructions. The possible generalization to arbitrary hyperelliptic varieties will be a future axis of research. Finally, the author wishes to thank the anonymous referees for their helpful comments.
