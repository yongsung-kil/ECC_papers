Low-Complexity Decoding Algorithms for the LDM Core Layer at Fixed Receivers in ATSC 3.0
Sunghye Cho, Youngjun Hwang, Seho Myung, and Kyeongcheol Yang, Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—Gallager’s decoding algorithms B (GDB) and E (GDE) for low-density parity-check codes have much lower computational complexity and much less required memory size than the sum-product algorithm (SPA). This is because GDB and GDE only use binary or integer operations, while the SPA requires real operations and a look-up table. However, they are hardly used in commercial communication systems since they have a worse performance than the SPA. Layered-division multiplexing (LDM) is considered in ATSC 3.0 in order to deliver multiple broadcasting streams with distinct robustness in a single radio frequency channel. Due to the unique characteristic of the LDM, we propose to use GDB or GDE rather than the SPA for decoding the core layer signal at fixed receivers. Numerical results show that the computational complexity and the required memory size can be reduced without any performance loss by about 50 percent and 80 percent, respectively, when GDB and GDE are employed.
Index Terms—ATSC 3.0, Gallager’s decoding algorithm, layered-division multiplexing (LDM), low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, sum-product algorithm (SPA).

II. BIT-INTERLEAVED CODED MODULATION (BICM)
We briefly review the BICM module described in ATSC A/322. The BICM module are divided into three parts: a forward error correction (FEC) part, a bit interleaver, and a constellation mapper, as shown in Fig. 1. The FEC part is formed by an inner encoder and an outer encoder. The bit interleaver consists of a parity interleaver, a groupwise interleaver, and a block interleaver. For the constellation mapper, complex-valued QAM constellations are adopted.
A. Forward Error Correction (FEC)
The structure of the FEC part is a cascade of an inner code and an outer code. An LDPC code is used as the inner code, whereas the outer code may be a Bose-ChaudhuriHocquenghem (BCH) code, a cyclic redundancy check (CRC) code, or none. LDPC codes, BCH codes, and CRC codes are chosen to be systematic such that information bits are directly placed at a part of a codeword. Therefore, a codeword of the FEC part is a concatenation of information bits, BCH or CRC parity-bits, and LDPC parity-bits.
LDPC codes as a mandatory inner code have two distinct structures called type A and type B. Type A is a protograph-based Raptor-like LDPC code which provides excellent performance at very low rates (in general, less than or equal to 5/15) [19], [20]. Richardson and Urbanke [21]

LDPC codes of type B are similar to those used in DVB-T2 and provide excellent performance at medium and high rates. They have length 16,200 or 64,800 bits, as in DVB-T2. The performance of a longer LDPC code is better, but a shorter LDPC code may be employed in applications where latency is critical, or a simpler encoder and decoder structure is preferred.
BCH codes as the outer code can correct 12 or less errors so that they can lower the error floor of an LDPC code when they are combined. They have 192 and 168 parity bits for LDPC codes of length 64,800 and 16,200, respectively, and improve error correction and detection. The CRC code as the outer code has 32 parity bits and provides only error detection. Finally, if the error-correcting capability of the inner code is sufficient for an application, the outer code may be omitted to achieve a higher throughput.

III. LAYERED DIVISION MULTIPLEXING (LDM)
LDM is a constellation superposition technology that delivers multiple broadcasting streams with distinct robustness in a single RF channel and uses full frequency and time resources. In current ATSC 3.0, only two layers are used for LDM, and will be considered in this paper.
A. Transmitter
The transmitter structure for LDM in ATSC 3.0 is depicted in Fig. 4. The two layers are named the core layer and the enhanced layer, respectively. The core layer is designed to deliver a robust mobile broadcasting service to mobile receivers. For this purpose, the core layer is designed to have high transmission power and use an LDPC code of type A with low-order modulation. On the other hand, the enhanced layer is designed to deliver HDTV or ultra HDTV services to fixed receivers. Fixed receivers usually have large antennas at high locations (e.g., rooftop) and sometimes directional antennas capable of providing significantly higher receive antenna gain than mobile receivers. Therefore, the enhanced layer signal is usually designed to have lower transmission power than the core layer signal. Furthermore, an LDPC code of type B with high order modulation is used for the transmission of enhanced layer signals.

yk = hkxk(c) + ghkxk(e) + nk, k = 0, 1, . . . , Nsub − 1 (1)
where hk is the fading channel coefficient, and nk represents the effect of additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) and is modeled as an independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.), circularly symmetric, zero-mean complex Gaussian random variable.
The core layer signal xk(c) can be decoded by treating the enhanced layer signal as an interference. The impact of the interference can be controlled through the injection level of the LDM system. The injection level may be chosen either to satisfy the service requirements of the two layers or to optimize the channel capacity.
The enhanced layer signal can be decoded by cancelling the core layer signal in the received LDM signal and then applying a proper decoding algorithm. The received signal of the enhanced layer can be computed as

IV. LOW-COMPLEXITY DECODING ALGORITHMS FOR LDM
Since the enhanced layer is designed to deliver high datarate services to fixed receivers at high SNRs, the core layer signals of the LDM system usually have high SNRs. As a result, they may be safely decoded even when some parity bits are not used in order to reduce the decoding complexity. In the case that all the parity bits are involved in decoding, it may also be possible to reduce the computational decoding complexity by applying low-complexity decoding algorithms such as GDB or GDE, instead of the SPA or the min-sum algorithm (MSA). Generally speaking, the SPA and the MSA are mostly used for decoding LDPC codes due to their excellent performance, although they have much higher computational complexity than GDB or GDE. However, the unique characteristic of the LDM system allows us to use GDB or GDE rather than the SPA or the MSA, for decoding the core layer signal without any performance loss at fixed receivers. This can significantly reduce the decoding complexity of the LDM system, since GDB or GDE has much lower complexity than the SPA and the MSA.

C. Analysis of Complexity and Required Memory
For our complexity analysis of the considered algorithms,
let us first consider a (dv, dc)-regular LDPC code with a paritycheck matrix H having M rows and N columns where dv and dc are the degrees of variable and check nodes, respectively. In order to calculate pm→n at Step 2 of GDB, we firstly calculate the product of all dc inputs with −1 and +1, and then multiply it with qn→m. Therefore, Step 2 involves M(2dc − 1) binary multiplications. The complexity of {+1, −1}-multiplication is the same as that of {0, 1}-addition. To calculate tn→m at Step 3, we calculate the sum of all dv inputs and then subtract pm→n from the sum. Therefore, Step 3 requires N(2dv − 1) integer additions and Ndv integer comparisons for calculating qn→m. At Step 4, we need dv − 1 integer addition and one integer comparison to calculate ˆtn and cˆn. We also need M(dc − 1) binary additions to calculate Hcˆ. Therefore, Step 4 requires N(dv − 1) integer additions, N integer comparisons, and M(dc − 1) binary additions.
Since GDE has three symbols for a message, it is possi-
ble to implement GDE using their binary expressions, where the binary expressions of −1, 0, and +1 are 11, 00, and 01, respectively. At Step 2, we firstly find the number of 0’s, η0, in the second bit positions of all incoming massages. There
are three cases. • Case η0 = 0 : We consider only the first bit of the output because the second bit should be 1. The

V. NUMERICAL RESULTS
For the performance evaluation, we consider two scenarios: • LDM-1: (16200, 4320) LDPC code with QPSK for the
core layer; and (64800, 43200) LDPC code with 64-NUC for the enhanced layer • LDM-2: (64800, 17280) LDPC code with QPSK for the core layer; and (64800, 38880) LDPC code with 256-NUC for the enhanced layer. These LDPC codes are listed in Annex A of [3], and NUCs are listed in Annex C of [3]. For our simulations, the maximum iteration number is set to 50. Fig. 6 shows the frame error rate (FER) performance of LDM-1 with SPA when the injection levels (ILs) are 4, 5, and 6 dB. As the IL increases, the power of the core layer signal increases while that of the enhanced layer signal decreases under the assumption that the total power of a transmit signal is constant. Therefore, as the IL increases, the performance of the core layer is upgraded, while that of the enhanced layer is degraded. In order to make the enhanced layer reliable, the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) should be much larger than 17.5 dB. On the other hand, if the SNR is greater than 1 dB, the FER of the core layer is much lower than 10−4. The FER performance of LDM-2 with SPA is shown in Fig. 7 when the ILs are 4, 5, and 6 dB. LDM-2 has a similar performance behavior to the LDM-1. In order to make the enhanced layer reliable, the SNR should be much larger than 21.0 dB. On the other hand, if the SNR is greater than 0 dB, the FER of the core layer is much lower than 10−4. Because the error probability of the core layer signal is extremely low at high SNRs (higher than 17.5 dB for LDM-1 or 21.0 dB for LDM-2), low-complexity decoding algorithms such as GDB or GDE can be used instead of the SPA. The FER performance of the core layer with GDB is shown Fig. 8 when the ILs are 4, 5, and 6 dB. If the SNR is greater than 11 dB, the FER of LDM-1 is much lower than 10−4. Similarly, if the SNR is greater than 13 dB, the FER of LDM-2 is much lower than 10−4. In order to evaluate the robustness of the considered decoding algorithms against the ILs, numerical results are additionally shown in Tables VIII–XV. When the IL is smaller than 3 dB, the SPA with ωR = 5 or the MSA with ωR = 7 is recommendable with respect to their required complexity and memory size. Interestingly, GDE is desirable for the IL between 3 dB and 5 dB while GDB is recommendable for the IL larger than or equal to 6 dB.
VI. CONCLUSION
Based on the special characteristic of the LDM system for ATSC 3.0, we proposed to use GDB or GDE for decoding the core layer signal at fixed receivers without any performance degradation, instead of the SPA. They have much lower complexity and much less required memory size, since they require only binary or integer operations while the SPA needs real operations and an LUT. When the injection level is very small, GDB and GDE cannot be adopted due to the degradation of decoding performance. However, GDE is very recommendable for the IL between 3 dB and 5 dB, and GDB is the most efficient algorithm among the considered decoding algorithms for the IL larger than or equal to 6 dB.
