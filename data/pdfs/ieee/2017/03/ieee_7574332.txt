Efficient Decoding Schemes of LDPC Codes for the Layered-Division Multiplexing Systems in ATSC 3.0
Youngjun Hwang, Sunghye Cho, Seho Myung, and Kyeongcheol Yang, Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—A low-complexity decoding scheme of low-density parity-check codes for the layered-division multiplexing (LDM) systems in ATSC 3.0 is proposed. Based on the unique characteristic of the LDM and the special structure of a parity-check matrix (PCM) for the core layer, the proposed scheme applies the sum-product algorithm (SPA) to only a part of the PCM. For this reason, it is referred to as “the partial SPA,” compared with the conventional scheme, denoted by “the full SPA.” Clearly, it has a significantly lower complexity than the full SPA. Furthermore, we propose hybrid decoders employing both the full SPA and the partial SPA to achieve a better performance, while maintaining their complexity low. Numerical results show that the proposed schemes have a good performance enough to perform successive interference cancellation for the enhanced layer of the LDM systems in ATSC 3.0 as well as they have a low-complexity merit depending on system parameters. Since they only schedule the decoding procedure without modifying the decoder structure, they also have a benefit in the implementation of decoders.
Index Terms—Layered-division multiplexing (LDM), successive interference cancellation (SIC), low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes, sum-product algorithm (SPA), decoding schedules.

In order to satisfy them, efficient and flexible technologies for broadcasting systems have been investigated. Since the frequency resource for broadcasting systems is highly limited, other attributes have also been studied to achieve a high spectral efficiency [1].

For an efficient utilization of time and frequency resources, hierarchical structures with multiple layers for broadcasting systems have been proposed. The Integrated Service Digital Broadcasting-Terrestrial (ISDB-T) [2] simultaneously supports mobile and indoor services based on frequency-division multiplexing (FDM), while the Digital Video BroadcastingTerrestrial 2 (DVB-T2) [3] transmits data streams in a part of the allocated time (i.e., time-division multiplexing, TDM) and in a part of the allocated frequency band (i.e., FDM). Recently, the Advanced Television Systems Committee (ATSC) has been developing a new international broadcasting standard for the next-generation digital TV (NG-DTV), called ATSC 3.0 [4], based on the technology with high spectral efficiency.
As a promising candidate technology for the physical layer (PHY) in ATSC 3.0 [5], the cloud transmission (Cloud-Txn) system [6], [7], which employs a spectrum overlay technology such as layered-division multiplexing (LDM) [8], was proposed. Unlike traditional multiplexing systems such as TDM and FDM, LDM supports multiple digital broadcast/multicast services by transmitting distinct data streams with a hierarchy in one radio frequency (RF) channel. LDM can fully utilizes time-frequency resources to transmit multiple program streams over layers with distinct characteristics and robustness. It was shown in [9] that LDM may have a higher spectrum efficiency than TDM or FDM in a range of signal-to-noise ratios (SNRs). For this reason, there have been extensive studies on LDM systems [20]–[22].
For a PHY specification of ATSC 3.0, Michael and Gómez-Barquero [12] introduced bit-interleaved coded modulation (BICM) combining channel coding, bit-interleaving, and modulation, and showed it outperforms the pre-existing digital terrestrial broadcasting standards in the aspects of the channel capacity and coverage. Kim et al. [13] studied low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [15], [16] for ATSC 3.0 and Loghin et al. [14] considered non-uniform constellation (NUC) for ATSC 3.0. The LDPC codes adopted for ATSC 3.0 have irregular repeat-accumulate (IRA) and multi-edge type (MET) structures. The NUC for ATSC 3.0 is optimized depending on a given code rate, and gives a performance gain, compared with quadrature-amplitude modulation (QAM). In [17]–[19], the signaling methods and framing technologies were presented for the PHY in ATSC 3.0. As the main proposals for ATSC 3.0, the performance of LDM systems were presented in [20]–[22]. The layers of LDM systems are protected by channel codes such as LDPC codes. The coded bits are properly grouped and mapped into constellation symbols. The symbols in each layer are adjusted by a given power level and are combined one by one to form an LDM symbol. Using the distinct power levels, each layer can be efficiently decoded in turn by SIC. However, this decoding procedure results in high latency, in addition to high computational complexity due to multiple decoders. In [21], a low-complexity LDM system for ATSC 3.0 is proposed. Based on the sum-product algorithm (SPA) [26] for decoding LDPC codes, it was shown in [21] that decoding the core layer for SIC requires only a few iterations with little performance degradation, while achieving a complexity reduction.
In this paper, we propose efficient decoding schemes for the two-layered LDM systems in ATSC 3.0. We focus on the inborn characteristics of raptor-like LDPC codes [27] in the core layer of the LDM system in ATSC 3.0. Since they have a cascaded structure with degree-one parity-bit nodes, it is possible to decode a part of coded bits by first using only a submatrix of the parity-check matrix (PCM), which do not have degree-one bit nodes (and the corresponding check nodes). When the coded bits are successfully decoded at the first trial, a codeword can be generated by encoding them. This scheme will be referred to as the partial SPA, compared with the original SPA applied to the whole PCM, which is referred to as ‘the full SPA’. We also propose hybrid decoders which employ the full SPA or the partial SPA depending on the state of iterations. Finally, we evaluate the performance of the proposed decoding schemes by numerical simulations and analyze their average numbers of iterations and their computational complexities. Under a range of system parameters, the proposed schemes may have lower computation complexity than the original SPA. Furthermore, they have a benefit in implementing decoders, since they do not require to modify the decoder structure but to schedule the decoding procedure.

In Section IV, we analyze the computational complexities of the proposed decoding schemes. Finally, we give concluding remarks in Section V.

II. LAYERED DIVISION MULTIPLEXING IN ATSC 3.0
A. Review of Layered Division Multiplexing
For an LDM system with L layers, we assume that the information stream at the lth layer is independently encoded by the encoder of the lth LDPC code, and then coded bits are properly grouped and mapped into an symbol xl,j in an Ml-ary signal constellation at time j. For the lth layer, a layer with index < l is called its upper layer, while a layer with index > l is called its lower layer. The LDM symbol sj at time j is the superposition of the mapped symbols x1,j, . . . , xL,j at time j, that is, sj = d1x1,j + d2x2,j + · · · + dLxL,j, where dl is a real-valued scaling factor at the lth layer for 1 ≤ l ≤ L such that |d1| > |d2| > · · · > |dL|. It is transmitted over an additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel and the received signal at time j is represented by
yj = sj + wj = d1x1,j + d2x2,j + · · · + dLxL,j + wj,
where wj is a sample of a complex white Gaussian noise random process at time j.
The symbol xl,j of the lth layer at time j can be estimated from yj by regarding the signals at the lower layers as an additive noise and successively cancelling the signals at the upper layers. The output of the lth interference canceller, yj(l) is calculated by
yj(l) = yj(l−1) − dl−1xˆl−1,j,
where xˆl,j is the hard-decision estimate of xl,j and y(j0) = yj. In this paper, we consider two-layered LDM systems for
ATSC 3.0 [5], as shown in Fig. 1. The upper and lower layers are called the core layer (CL) and enhanced layer (EL), respectively. For simplicity, we set two LDM coefficients d1 and d2 to 1 and g, respectively. Then, the received signal of a two-layered LDM system is represented by
yj = x1,j + gx2,j + wj,
where 0 ≤ g ≤ 1. The injection level is defined as the power ratio of CL signals to EL signals and is measured by α −20 log10 g [dB]. The block diagram of a two-layered LDM encoder is described in Fig. 2.

B. Bit-Interleaved Coded Modulation Scheme for ATSC 3.0
The bit-interleaved coded modulation (BICM) scheme considered in ATSC 3.0 consists of three parts: a forward error-correction (FEC) code concatenating LDPC codes with BCH codes, a bit interleaver, and a bit-to-symbol mapper, as shown in Fig. 3. An information block is input to the FEC encoder and a valid parity block is added. Conventionally, the employed BCH code is called an outer code, and the employed LDPC code is called an inner code.
Throughout the paper, we consider two types of LDPCcoded LDM systems for ATSC 3.0, denoted by LDM-1 and LDM-2, respectively.
• LDM-1: (16200, 4320) LDPC code with quadriphaseshift keying (QPSK) for the CL; and (64800, 43200) LDPC code with 64-QAM or 64-NUC for the EL
• LDM-2: (64800, 17280) LDPC code with QPSK for the CL; and (64800, 38880) LDPC code with 256-QAM or 256-NUC for the EL.
The PCMs of raptor-like LDPC codes [27] for the CL consist of five submatrices, as shown in Fig. 4. The P × K submatrix A corresponds to the information part, while the P × P submatrix B with dual-diagonal structure generates P parity bits sequentially by accumulating the previous parity bit with the information bits prescribed by A. The submatrix Z denotes the P×(N −K −P) all-zero matrix. In order to provide low-rate codes, degree-one parity-bit nodes are added by the (N−K−P)×(N−K−P) identity matrix D. III. NOVEL DECODING SCHEMES AT A FIXED RECEIVER IN ATSC 3.0
A. Computational Complexity and Latency Problems at a Fixed Receiver
A mobile receiver recovering a CL signal in ATSC 3.0 performs an ordinary decoding procedure by regarding an EL signal as a white Gaussian noise. On the other hand, in order to recover an EL signal, a fixed receiver should perform SIC to eliminate the CL signal from the received signal, which needs two distinct decoding procedures. This makes the computational complexity of the fixed receiver increase drastically. Moreover, the decoding procedures should be serially performed, which incurs high latency.
In the fixed receiver, the CL signal has much higher SNR than the EL signal. Hence, the CL signal can be successfully decoded with high probability at the first decoding procedure, and then the EL signal may be recovered without any significant loss by SIC. C. Partial SPA and Re-Encoding Scheme for Raptor-Like LDPC Codes
In a raptor-like LDPC code with the PCM in Fig. 4, the check nodes associated with submatrices A and B are not connected to degree-one parity-bit nodes. CL signals can be decoded in two steps: 1) Estimate the information and parity bits by applying the SPA only to A and B; and 2) re-encode the estimated bits and generate a codeword. In the first step, the SPA is partially applied to a subgraph of the original factor graph and this decoding scheme will be referred to as the partial SPA, compared with the full SPA applied to the whole PCM. Since degree-one parity-bit nodes are not connected

to the parity-bit nodes associated with A and B, they can be separately updated. In particular, the parity bits associated with degree-one parity-bit nodes can be encoded linearly with parity-bit length. If the information and parity bits associated with A and B are successfully decoded, a correct codeword may be obtained.
The FER performances of the CL of LDM-1 and LDM-2 over AWGN channels is shown in Fig. 7 according to the injection level, when the partial SPA is employed. As the injection level decreases, the partial SPA has a significant performance degradation in both QAM and NUC, unlike the full SPA. For example, see the case that α = 4 dB for LDM-1 with 64-QAM and the cases that α = 5, 6 dB for LDM-2 with 256-NUC. For these cases, re-encoding may not give a correct codeword at the operating SNR of the EL because the CL has a poorer performance than the EL. In particular, unlike the full SPA, the partial SPA with NUC has a more degraded performance, compared with that with QAM. D. Hybrid Decoders
In order to enhance the performance of the partial SPA, we propose two hybrid decoders.
• Hybrid-A decoder: First perform the full SPA for a fixed number of iterations, Iinit. And then perform the partial SPA.
• Hybrid-B decoder: First perform the SPA not only to A and B, but also a part of columns and rows of the PCM, which corresponds to degree-one parity bits and their associated check nodes, respectively, for Iinit iterations. And then perform the partial SPA.
In this paper, (N −K −P)/2 parity bits are additionally considered for the hybrid-B decoder, compared with the partial SPA. Based on Table III, we may set Iinit to one for the hybrid-A decoder, and to 1, 2, or 5 for the hybrid-B decoder.

The FER performance of the CL of LDM-1 and LDM-2 employing QAM or NUC for the EL over AWGN channels is shown in Fig. 9 according to the injection level when the hybrid-A decoder is employed with Iinit = 1 and Imax = 50. Although the full SPA is performed only for the first iteration of the hybrid-A decoder, the performance gets significantly improved, compared with the decoder employing only the partial SPA. In particular, the cases that cannot be decoded successfully with the partial SPA may be decoded successfully with the hybrid-A decoder. V. CONCLUSION
We proposed novel decoding schemes for raptor-like LDPC codes of the two-layered LDM systems in ATSC 3.0. Based on the distinct characteristics and robustness of each layer, we consider SIC for decoding superpositioned signals. Since the fixed receiver should perform two different decoding procedures to obtain EL signals, its decoding complexity and latency

become significantly high. In order to alleviate them, we devise a modification of the SPA, called the partial SPA, which is performed on only a part of the original PCM of a raptor-like LDPC code. Furthermore, we proposed two hybrid decoders which have a significant performance improvement. Numerical results show that the proposed schemes have a good performance enough to achieve a decoding success for SIC, while reducing the decoding complexity and the average number of iterations.
