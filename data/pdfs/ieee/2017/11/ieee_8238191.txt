Abstract— The Bit-Flipping (BF) algorithm is considered as a hard decoding method for LDPC codes. It is much simpler than the probabilistic methods like Sum Product Algorithm (SPA), and can be efficiently implemented by electronic circuits. In this paper, we propose a new Bit Flipping algorithm for Low-Density ParityCheck codes (LDPC) called Single Bit-Flipping (SBF). Compared to the Gallager Bit-Flipping algorithm, the proposed algorithm employs criteria of flipping single bit chosen carefully. This method eliminates the risk for flipping more than one bit at a time that can induce additional error bits and propagate the errors to the later iterations. We present some results obtained by applying this new method that provides a gain in performance in comparison to the standard Gallager Bit-Flipping with low complexity.
Keywords— Low-Density Parity-Check Codes (LDPC), Bit-
Flipping decoding, Hard decision decoding, Single bit, Iterations, complexity.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-Density Parity-Check codes (LDPC) were first introduced by Gallager in 1962 in his PhD Thesis at MIT[1][10]. At the time, their incredible potential remained undiscovered due to the computational demands of simulation in an era when vacuum tubes were only just being replaced by the first transistors. Later, Tanner [2] extended Gallager’s probabilistic decoding algorithm to the more general case where the paritychecks are defined by subcodes, instead of simple single paritycheck equations.
The LDPC codes remained largely neglected for over 35 years, until their rediscovery by MacKay and Neal [3] in the late nineties due to their performance that fell well short of the theoretically achievable limits set down by Shannon in his seminal 1948 paper [4].
LDPC codes have been adopted by many state-of-the-art communication systems, such as in second-generation satellite broadcasting systems (DVB-S2), in gigabit Ethernet systems (10Gbase-T), and are being considered in high-speed wireless LAN systems (IEEE 802.3an). It is a kind of binary linear block code whose parity check matrix is sparse which has much fewer

1’s than a common matrix. A sparse parity check matrix facilitates simple decoding algorithms and low-complexity decoder designs.
In literature there are many LDPC decoding methods, the Sum-Product Algorithm (SPA) [5][6], a probabilistic soft decision message passing algorithm that has the highest decoding performance closed to Shannon limit. Nevertheless, due to its high complexity, SPA is impractical for applications. In contrast, Gallager proposed [1] a simplified hard decision decoding algorithm, the hard decision bit flipping (BF) method was designed to be less complex than SPA decoding but its performance is much lower than that of SPA.
The majority of Bit Flipping techniques fall into the category of simplified soft decision decoding methods and form the basis of a novel decoding algorithm combining improvement in performance (but less then SPA) and simple complexity as BF like Weighted Bit Flipping (WBF)[7], Modified Weighted Bit Flipping (MWBF)[8] and Gradient Descent Bit Flipping (GDBF) [9].
In this paper, we propose a new decoding algorithm that uses BF with new method of choosing and flipping bits. This paper is organized as follows. Section 2 presents a review of Gallager Bit-Flipping decoding algorithm (BF). Section 3 provides details of the proposed Bit Flipping algorithm called “Single Bit-flipping” (SBF). The simulation results are presented in the section 4. In section 5, we will study the complexity of both decoders and an experimental result will be given. And the paper concludes with a summary of the results in section 5.

B. Gallager’s BF decoding algorithm
The Bit-Flipping algorithm is a hard-decision algorithm for LDPC codes. A binary (hard) decision about each received bit is made by the demodulator and passed to the decoder.
The Bit-Flipping decoder can be immediately terminated whenever a valid codeword has been found by checking if all of the parity-check equations are satisfied.
The Bit-Flipping algorithm is based on the principal that a bit involved in a large number of incorrect check equations is likely to be incorrect itself. The sparseness of H helps spread out the bits into checks so that parity-check equations are unlikely to contain the same set of codeword bits.
The variables used are as follows: I is a count for iteration, Imax is the maximum number of iterations, T is a threshold for the termination condition of the algorithm, w is a temporal code word, and w0 is a code word for the next iteration. A symbol “←” is used to signify a substitution from the right-hand side to the left-hand side.
Let Si be the set of nonzero syndromes checking on bit i, i.e., Si= { Sj | Sj=1,cj ∈C(wi)} where C(wℓ) denotes the set of check nodes that are connected to bit node ℓ , i.e., positions of “1”s in the ℓth column of the parity-check matrix.

III. SINGLE BIT FLIPPING ALGORITHM
A. Motivation The Gallager’s BF decoder algorithm flips more than one bit
in each iteration, which is the most intuitive and simplest way to reduce iteration numbers. However, there is risk for flipping

more than one bit at a time to induce additional error bits and propagate the errors to the later iterations.
Therefore, flipping just one bit each time can be good solution to prevent the flipping of good bits unintentionally.

B. The Proposed BF Algorithm
Our proposed algorithm flips only one bit in each iteration. Hence, it needs high iteration counts to obtain the final decoding results but go to the objective surely. The Single Bit Flipping Algorithm will flip the ℓth bit in the hard decision codeword’s, where the ℓ value is the first value within maximum values of the positions column in parity check matrix that contains the most bit connected node in the unsatisfied check equations. Therefore, if it’s large, then the ℓth bit may be incorrect with high probability.

VI. CONCLUSIONS
The proposed SBF algorithm for decoding LDPC codes can flip a single bit chosen carefully each time. It helps to avoid flipping many bits at a time where probability in wrongly flipping good bits increases. So inducing additional error bits and propagates the errors to the later iterations will no more be a matter for our decoder.
Compared with Gallager BF algorithm, the proposed decoder can achieve better decoding performances and also offer less complexity in term of iterations for decoding.
The obtained results open new horizons for hard decision decoding of the LDPC codes and the proposed algorithm can be developed to a soft decision decoding BF.
