Analysis of Practical LDPC Decoders in Tanner Graphs with Absorbing Sets
Marco Ferrari∗, Alessandro Tomasoni∗ and Sandro Bellini† ∗CNR-IEIIT, Politecnico di Milano, Milan, Italy, Email: firstname.secondname@ieiit.cnr.it
†Politecnico di Milano, Milan, Italy, Email: sandro.bellini@elet.polimi.it

Abstract—Absorbing sets (ASs) cause the error floor phenomenon in many Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes. A recent, simplified system model for Min-Sum (MS) LDPC decoding [1] predicts that ASs exhibit a threshold behavior: if all variable nodes in an AS have channel messages above the threshold, the AS cannot trap the decoder. The threshold is a real-valued parameter that depends on the topology of the AS, and can be evaluated by a nonlinear optimization.
In this paper we describe a simple, fast algorithm for evaluating the AS threshold. Additionally, we show that the algorithm is valid also for scaled-MS decoding. We show with an example that the threshold values under scaled-MS decoding are smaller than under MS decoding. Accordingly, scaling decreases the error floor.
Index Terms—Low-Density Parity-Check codes, Error floor, Absorbing sets, Min-Sum decoding, Threshold, Tanner graph.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes suffer from the error floor phenomenon, i.e., a significant change of slope in the word error rate (WER) curve versus the signal-tonoise ratio (SNR). This performance degradation is due to the suboptimality of message passing decoding in graphs with cycles. There exist low Hamming weight sequences that are competitors of the all-zero codeword, notwithstanding some unsatisfied check node equations.
Absorbing sets (ASs) defined in [2] (or absorption sets in [3]), are combinatorial substructures of the Tanner graph that correspond to these low Hamming weight sequences. ASs, and in particular elementary ASs, describe the dominant decoding failures of various soft message passing decoders over AWGN channels [2], [4], [5].
In [6] and [3] a model for the analysis of iterative decoding inside an elementary AS was proposed. Considering Min-Sum (MS) decoding, and assuming that the messages in the rest of the graph converge towards the correct decisions according to Density Evolution, the updated messages are given by linear combinations of messages at the previous iteration [6]. This linear model was generalized to Sum-Product decoders in [3]. The authors applied their model to the ASs of the IEEE 802.3an LDPC code, to predict the error floor.
In [1] the analysis of iterative decoding inside ASs was modified, introducing a limited dynamic range for the extrinsic messages, which is typical of fixed-point decoder implementations. The decoder can be trapped by an AS and reach an equilibrium that does not lead to the correct decisions. However, in [1] it is shown that ASs exhibit a threshold

behavior: if all Variable Nodes (VNs) in the AS have channel messages above the AS threshold, the only equilibrium has maximum extrinsic messages and leads to correct decisions. The threshold is a real-valued parameter that depends only on the topology of the AS, and is the result of a max min optimization, described in [7].
For ASs with negative threshold, the condition that channel messages are above the threshold can be guaranteed choosing a suitable saturation level of the extrinsic messages. Then, these ASs are deactivated. These results are in agreement with the fact that the error floor level of quantized LDPC decoders is strongly related to the quantization choices [5], [8]. An AS whose threshold is positive cannot be deactivated, and must be eliminated by other means (see, e.g., [9], [10]).
In this paper we delve into the behavior of the decoder model introduced in [1] to describe the evolution of extrinsic messages exchanged by VNs and Check Nodes (CNs) inside an AS. We analyze the equilibria and we show that for each set of channel messages there exists a minimum equilibrium. We also prove that minimum equilibria can be ordered according to the minimum of the channel messages. As a consequence, we show that the threshold of an AS can be found tracking the minimum equilibrium versus the minimum channel message, up to a point where extrinsic messages reach their saturation level. This algorithm is much simpler and faster than [7]. We test it with the well known Margulis code, whose population of ASs is described in [1].
Finally, we show that the same results hold for scaled-MS decoding, a popular means to reduce the SNR loss of MS decoding with respect to the Sum-Product Algorithm (SPA). We show that the threshold values for scaled-MS decoders are smaller than those of the MS decoder. Therefore, beside improving convergence, scaling also decreases the error floor. This fact is confirmed by simulation of the Margulis code.

A. Absorbing Sets and message evolution
Consider a subset of VNs D ⊂ V and the CNs in the neighborhood N (D) {N (vi), vi ∈ D}. Let E(D) and O(D) be the set of neighboring CNs connected to D an even or odd number of times, respectively. Let a and b denote the cardinality of D and O(D) respectively. D is an (a, b) absorbing set if every VN in D has more neighboring CNs in E(D) than in O(D) [2]. Besides, an AS D(a, b) is elementary if the CNs in N (D) are connected to D no more than twice.
Elementary ASs allow a simple model for the evolution
of the extrinsic messages inside the AS [6], [3], [1]. Let the column vector x(k) represent the scaled extrinsic messages u(vki→ ) cj having vi ∈ D and cj ∈ E(D). The length of x is N = i:vi∈D di(v) − b, where d(iv) is the degree of vi.

Let e(k) be the vector (of length a) of messages forwarded by O(D) to D. Unlike [6] and [3], in [1] the analysis of the iterations is started when the messages uvi→cj are already saturated to +1 for all vi ∈/ D . The initial vector x(0) of messages generated by the VNs in the AS is the result of the
message evolution up to that iteration, and is unknown. Any starting configuration x(0) that could result in a convergence
failure must be taken into account. This point of view is chosen
to decouple the dynamical behavior of the decoder inside and outside the AS. If no x(0) can trap the decoder, this is true
independently of the evolution of messages inside the AS. Let D(a, b) be an elementary AS, with the VNs outside D
propagating messages saturated to +1. All the CNs in E(D)
simply forward on each edge the input message from the other edge in D. For two VNs vi and vm belonging to D and connected by the CN cj , uv(ki→ ) cj = u(ckj→ ) vm . CNs in E (D) can be neglected, and messages x(k) can be treated as if they were exchanged between VNs of D. All the CNs in O(D) connect the AS to the rest of the graph. Since the ith entry of e(k) gathers only messages saturated to +1, e(ik) = ei is the number of edges connecting vi to O(D).

IV. EQUILIBRIA FOR SCALED-MS DECODING
Scaling is a practical means to reduce the SNR loss of MS decoding with respect to SPA decoding. VNs compute extrinsic LLRs to be forwarded to CNs as in (1), but this is correct only if these values are true LLRs. CNs implementing (2) overestimate the extrinsic LLRs. Scaling the messages uc(kj→ ) vi by a factor α < 1 improves the convergence of iterative MS decoding [12]. What is less understood is why scaling also improves the error floor.

In Fig. 2 we plot the thresholds of all topologies, computed by the Algorithm 1 for MS, and for scaled-MS decoding with α = 3/4. The computation of all the thresholds took a few seconds, instead of about one hour with the algorithm in [7].
Under scaled-MS decoding all the thresholds are reduced. The two most critical ASs (12,4) and (14,4) (topologies 11 and 12) have τ < 0 instead of τ = 0.
Let, for instance, Λmax = 7 and Umax = 15. Many ASs with topologies 9 to 35 can trap the MS decoder, even though the main contribution to the error floor comes from topologies 11 and 12. Under scaled-MS decoding, almost all of them are deactivated since τ < −7/15. The only ASs able to trap the scaled-MS decoder are those with topologies 10, 11, and 12. Yet, their thresholds and their dangerousness are reduced, compared to MS-decoding.
Fig. 3 shows the performance of MS and scaled-MS decoding for Λmax = 7 and Umax = 2qe−1 − 1 = 7 or 15 for the extrinsic messages (qe = 4 or 5). As predicted by the threshold analysis, the error-floor with qe = 5 is substantially reduced for scaled-MS decoding, compared to MS-decoding.
A final comment on the merits of scaled-MS decoding is the following. In [1] it is shown that left-regular LDPC codes with even CN degree cannot have ASs with negative threshold. As a consequence, no ASs of these LDPC codes can be deactivated choosing a suitable saturation level Umax of extrinsic messages. With scaled-MS decoding, ASs with negative threshold can exist1. These ASs are deactivated by a properly designed scaled MS-decoder.
V. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we analyzed the behavior of MS (and scaled MS) LDPC decoders in Tanner graphs with elementary ASs. Taking into account the effect of the saturation of messages in practical implementations, the evolution of extrinsic messages

inside an elementary AS can be studied using a simplified
linear model (with saturation). ASs exhibit a threshold behavior. If all VNs in D have channel messages above the AS threshold, the only equilibrium of the system leads to
the correct decisions. For ASs with negative threshold, the
condition that channel messages are above the threshold can be
guaranteed choosing a suitable saturation level of the extrinsic
messages. Then, these ASs are deactivated.
We proved that there exists a ranking of the equilibria that
enables a simple algorithm for the computation of the AS
threshold. This algorithm is much simpler and faster that the
one proposed in [7], and works also for scaled-MS decoding.
As an example, we considered the Margulis LDPC code.
Increasing the saturation value of extrinsic messages in the
MS decoder deactivates several ASs, as expected. Simulations
confirm that the error floor is lowered. Since scaled-MS de-
coding reduces the AS thresholds, compared to MS decoding,
the error floor is further reduced.
