Improved Belief Propagation based on the Estimation Backtracking
Jan Broulim, Vjaceslav Georgiev, Michael Holik, Jan Zich

Abstract—We propose a general method to improve the decoding performance of hard-decision and soft-decision belief propagation decoders. The performance of the proposed algorithm is tested on MacKay’s widely known LDPC (504,252) code, CCSDS LDPC (256,128) and our irregular LDPC (128,64) code. The bit error rate indicated by our novel solution is up to five times lower for a particular signal-to-noise ratio of tested codes measured through AWGN simulations. Furthermore, a decoding time required for the same bit error rate as the bit error rate of a traditional BP decoder is up to three times lower when our approach is used in a non-pipelined implementation.
Index Terms—belief propagation, error control, estimation backtracking, LDPC.
I. INTRODUCTION
E RROR correction coding has found many applications in data communication systems. The first error correction codes emerged in Hammings work in 1950 [1]. During years of research and development, Reed-Solomon codes [2], turbo codes [3] and Linear Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes [4] have become popular because of their good correcting capabilities. LDPC codes [4] decoded by the Belief Propagation (BP) algorithm surpassed other codes at correction capabilities through the work of Davey and MacKay [5]. Such codes found significant applications in data communication networks, television broadcasting and deep space transmission (e.g. the New Horizon spacecraft, currently enroute to Pluto).
LDPC codes, introduced by Gallager in 1962 [4], were neglected for over 30 years. In 1995, they were rediscovered by the work of MacKay. Over the past two decades, there has been a dramatic increase in research related to LDPC codes.
LDPC codes are defined in terms of a sparse parity-check matrix. Several construction methods have been proposed in the research work. Regular forms of the parity-check matrix were originally considered by Gallager, but it has been conclusively shown that irregular structures perform better [6].

The Progressive Edge Growth algorithm, generalised in 2005 [7], can be considered as a respected standard for designing effective LDPC codes.
Decoding of LDPC codes is performed by iterative Belief Propagation (BP) algorithms, which work on the Tanner graph representation [8] of the parity-check matrix. The BP includes the Sum-Product and the Min-Sum algorithms [9]. These algorithms work with probabilities in the iteration process (soft-decision). A hard decision can be also used for decoding (the Bit Flipping algorithm). The correction performance of LDPC codes is given by the algorithm used for decoding and by circumstances in the Tanner graph. The presence of short cycles in the Tanner graph negatively affects the convergence of BP algorithms.
The performance of decoding algorithms is usually simulated on the Additive White Gaussian Noise (AWGN) channel. The simulation results provide the dependence of a Bit Error Rate (BER) on a Signal-To-Noise (SNR) ratio. SNR is often recalculated to Eb/N0.
The convergence of BP algorithms is guaranteed solely in graphs with a tree structure. The occurrence of cycles in the Tanner graph causes the convergence failure, which has a negative impact on the decoding performance. Short cycles generally cause the worse convergence. However, there are methods that show how the convergence of the BP can be improved [10, 11, 12, 13]. Our method based on the Estimation Backtracking is described in the following sections.
LDPC codes, defined by the parity-check matrix H, are decoded by hard-decision and soft-decision iterative algorithms [9], including the Sum-Product (SP), the Min-Sum (MS) and Bit Flipping (BF) algorithm. Such algorithms calculate a new estimation of a codeword after every iteration is finished. The decoding is terminated if the estimation belongs to the code space or if the maximum number of iterations is reached. The maximum number of iterations is the parameter of the decoder chosen with regard to computing resources.
In the following section, we propose our improved Belief Propagation algorithm based on the Estimation Backtracking. In section III we provide a complexity and performance analysis of the original BP and our improved algorithm. Comprehensive AWGN we show in Section IV. In Section V we conclude and discuss future work.

B. Memory requirements
The proposed algorithm requires all codeword estimations to be stored in the memory (stack). These vectors are being read during backtracking process starting with the last codeword estimation. The number of bits needed to be stored is:

n × ITERATIONS

(3)

where n is the number of columns of H. The Estimation Backtracking algorithm also requires the vectors Mj and Ni to be stored. Supposing the average variable degree dv, the average check degree dc, the number of integers needed to be stored is:

n × dv + (n − k) × dc

(4)

where (n − k) is the number of rows of H. However, information provided by vectors Mj and Ni can be shared with the BP decoder. Thus, the memory requirements highly depend on the architecture used for the implementation of the decoder.

IV. COMPREHENSIVE AWGN SIMULATIONS
The performance of the proposed decoding algorithms was tested on Mackay’s widely known code (504,252) [14], irregular code (128,64) [15], irregular code (256,128) provided by NASA CCSDS standard [16]. The dependence of the Bit Error Rate on the Signal-to-Noise Ratio was measured while the Additive White Gaussian Noise (AWGN) channel was used for transmission. 150 decoding iterations were performed for each comprehensive simulation. All Bit Error Rates are calculated from 400 codewords (except Fig. 6) for which the iterative decoding algorithm failed at particular Signal-to-Noise Ratios. The Bit Error Rates simulated on LDPC (128,64) with the use of Min-Sum and Bit Flipping algorithms are shown in Fig. 1 and 2. The Bit Error Rates of CCSDS (256,128) and MacKay’s (502,252) code are shown in Fig. 3 and 4. Results confirm an improvement of the Bit Error Rate calculated for particular Eb/N0 ratio. The simulated BER can be decreased several times according to the used code. An interesting improvement can be seen in the simulation of MacKay’s code.

V. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper we proposed an algorithm for improving the performance of hard-decision and soft-decision iterative algorithms used for LDPC code decoding. The proposed algorithm uses the backtracking of codeword estimations in order to find the minimum distance codeword in the code space. This method leads to an improvement of the Bit Error Rate or to decrease the decoding time. Three different codes, irregular LDPC (128,64), CCSDS (256,128) and MacKay’s (504,252), were used for the performance testing.
