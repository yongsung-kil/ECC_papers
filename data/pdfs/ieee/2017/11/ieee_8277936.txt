Lowering the Error Floors of Low-Density Parity-Check Codes with Additional Check Nodes
Dongming Yuan, Lu Li, and Yuanan Liu School of Electronic Engineering
Beijing University of Posts and Telecommunications, P. R. China Email: yuandm@bupt.edu.cn, lilukrystal@outlook.com, yuliu@bupt.edu.cn

Abstract—This paper proposes a novel scheme for bit recovery of Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) Codes. Firstly, the most erroneous and the most reliable bits are located by searching the codeword bits. Subsequently, new check nodes are added to connect them, thus the negative effects of the most erroneous bits along with the corresponding trapping sets are avoided. Eventually, the BER performance is dramatically improved in the error-floor region according to the simulated results. This idea could be a more concise and simple alternative to weaken the effects of trapping sets in LDPC applications.
Index Terms—Low-density parity-check codes, bit recovery, trapping sets, error floor, additional check nodes.
I. INTRODUCTION
Due to its outstanding ability of being able to realize near Shannon limit channel capacity, the Low-density paritycheck (LDPC) code [1] has been extensively researched in recent years. LDPC code has already been adopted in many communication systems, such as IEEE 802 series, DVB, microwave communication, optical fiber and so on. Due to its better flexibility, higher throughput and lower complexity, the LDPC code has been chosen for the channel coding scheme of 5G system. Despite its capacity-approaching merit when applied to iterative decoders in binary-input additive-whiteGaussian-noise (BIAWGN) channel, LDPC codes still suffer from the deleterious error floor [2] as the SNR increases. This performance in error-floor region is mainly resulted from the trapping sets [2] under various channels, including the AWGN, binary symmetric (BSC), and binary erasure channel (BEC) [3].
Because the error-floor effects may cause serious issues in systems requiring extreme-low error rates, extensive attempts have been made to improve the performance of LDPC codes in error-floor region. The key for reducing the error-floor effects is breaking the trapping sets or small stopping sets of the LDPC codes. In [4], to eliminate small stopping sets in irregular LDPC codes, the authors present a scheme that connects small stopping sets under BEC by adding several new check nodes. The authors in [5] propose a modified version of PCM (parity-check matrix) extension system by taking the weight of each stopping sets into consideration. Literature [6] proposes a strategy for breaking trapping sets on AWGN channel through removing certain check nodes. In this algorithm, the bit involved in the trapping sets are firstly located, then some check nodes connected with the chosen

variable nodes are removed. This process for locating the trapping sets may time-consuming and make the algorithm complicated. Rather than analyzing the trapping set (or the stopping sets) directly, reference [7] searches information bits associated with the dominant trapping sets and shorten them by pre-assigning known values before encoding. This datashortening method would disconnect some edges between trapping sets in order to weaken the effect of them.
In this paper, we propose a bit-recovery scheme based on bit-searching and check node-addition algorithms. The error level of each codeword bit is estimated through Monte Carlos simulations. Then we select the most erroneous and reliable variable nodes simultaneously and connect them through the additional check nodes. The negative influence of the most erroneous variable nodes will be recovered by the message transmitted from the most reliable ones through the additional edges. Since most of the bits prone to be incorrectly decoded are always involved in dominant trapping sets, this algorithm will be a more concise and simple way to locate the nodes in trapping sets compared with identifying trapping sets directly. Eventually, the corresponding simulations are carried out, the results of which confirm the robustness and effectiveness of this proposed idea.

C. A k-iteration computation tree Tk(v) and separation
It is well known that the trapping sets are the main cause of
the error floor of LDPC codes, and the definition of trapping
sets can be found in [2]. The trapping set with a set of
a variable and b check nodes of odd degree is written as T S(a, b).
Define G = (V ∪ C, E) as the Tanner graph of a binary LDPC code with VNs V = {v1, ..., vn}, CNs C = {c1, ..., cm}, and edge set E. Then, let S be the subgraph of an trapping set (a, b) belongs to G, which includes VN set VS ⊆ V and CN set CS ⊆ C. Define set C1 ⊆ CS as the set of degree-one CNs in the subgraph S, and set V1 ⊆ VS as the set of adjacent VNs of CNs in C1. For the convenience of analysis and proof, we introduce two concepts, the computation tree and separation, which are first proposed in literature [8].
Definition 1 (computation tree): We define Tk(v) as the computation tree at the k-th decoding iterations. Tk(v) is a tree constructed by the following way. First choose an VN vr ⊆ V1 ⊆ S as the root, and then recursively add the edges and leaf nodes according to the path of the k decoding iterations.
Definition 2 (k-separated): In Tk(v), we say vr is kseparated, if during k iterations, there exists at least one adjacent degree-one CN, all the descendants of which are not
included in VS. An example is shown in Fig. 1. Fig. 1(a) illustrates a (5, 1)
trapping set in (96, 48) Mackay code, where c8 is the degreeone CN and v6, v7 is the neighboring VNs of the trapping set.

III. BIT-RECOVERY SCHEMES
A. Bit-recovery schemes based on bit-searching and check node-addition algorithms
Rather than locating the trapping sets directly, we utilize an indirect method. We firstly locate the most erroneous and reliable variable nodes, then add new check nodes connecting the most erroneous and reliable variable nodes. The bitsearching process is described in Algorithm 1.
Fig. 2(a) and 2(b) show the original and modified Tanner graph, where different gray-scales of the variable nodes express different levels of errors. Fig. 2(b) shows that the additional check nodes connect the most erroneous variable nodes and the most reliable variable nodes through adding new edges to the Tanner graph. In the case of regular codes, for each most erroneous variable node, we select (dc − 1) reliable variable nodes to connect to it, where dc is the check node degree of the code. In addition, for different lengths of codes, the quantities of additional check nodes should be carefully considered, in order to obtain a good error performance while avoid severe rate loss.
For the convenience of implementation, the detailed procedure of the proposed scheme is summarized in Algorithm 2.

1) vr ∈ Vs and vr ∈/ V1: This means the most erroneous VN is in a trapping set but only connected to mis-satisfied CN, which is the most common case. Also, this type of VN is most likely to be incorrectly decoded.
It can be seen from Fig. 4(a) that the new additional check nodes Cadd becomes a new neighboring degree-one CN of vr, when vr is connected to the most reliable variable nodes through Cadd. That means vr ∈ V1. Then through the iterative message-passing decoding, the most reliable VNs will participate in the decoding process, thus they will be the descendants of Cadd in the corresponding computation tree T (vr).
The most reliable variable nodes have the minimal probability of being wrongly decoded during the decoding process, thus it is reasonable to assume that they are away from the trapping sets. That means the number of iterations will be relatively large before a VN v ∈ VS becomes a descendants of Cadd. Therefore, according to definition 1, after adding the additional CNs, the separation of the most erroneous VN vr will be dramatically increased.
2) vr ∈ Vs and vr ∈ V1: This means the most erroneous VN vr is in a trapping set and has a neighboring degree-one CN. As mentioned before, the Cadd will become an alternative neighboring degree-one CN, as indicated in Fig. 4(b). As a result, separation k of vr will be further increased. Moreover, with the number of degree-one CN increasing, the paths to transmit the correct information from the outside are also increased.
In summary, no matter what cases vr belongs to, the separation of vr will be increased after adding the additional CNs.
According to Lemma 1, due to the increase of the separation, the selected erroneous nodes vr can be recovered by applying Algorithm 2. After correcting the selected erroneous VN, the relative (a, b) trapping sets will be transformed to an less detrimental trapping set (a − 1, b + dv) or (a − 1, b + dv − 2), corresponding to the two cases of vr respectively (in proof of theorem 1). This conclusion has already been proved in literature [9]. Hence, the negative influence of the most erroneous variable nodes and the involved trapping sets will be reduced, and further lower the error floor.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, bits recovery schemes based on bit-searching and check node-addition algorithms was proposed. By searching the most erroneous variable nodes and the most reliable variable nodes and adding additional check nodes to connect them, the detrimental effect caused by the most erroneous variable nodes could be recovered by high-reliable extrinsic message transmitted from the most reliable variable nodes.
