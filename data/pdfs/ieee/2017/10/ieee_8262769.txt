Joint Decoding of RAID-ECC Solutions for SSDs

Qing Li ScaleFlux Inc. San Jose, CA, 95112 qing.li@scaleflux.com

Ning Zheng ScaleFlux Inc. San Jose, CA, 95112

Abstract—There are two independent layers of error correction mechanisms, i.e., error-correction codes (ECC) and redundant array of independent disks (RAID), to protect NAND-based Solid State Drives (SSDs) from serious noise/disturb. However, bit error rates of modern SSDs have increased to the point that more powerful ECCs or Maximal Distance Separate codes are only regarded as sufficient solutions.
The theme of this work is to show that instead of designing new error correction mechanisms, it is possible to improve the NANDbased SSD reliability by designing a joint decoder exploring the inherent information between ECCs and RAIDs. We first show that it is possible to recover two-page failures using the inherent information with RAID 4/5; we then show that these ideas can be extended to RAID 6; finally, we conduct information theoretic study to explore how much information can be reliably stored with the help of RAID system in NAND-based SSD.
I. INTRODUCTION
There are two independent layers of error correction mechanisms, i.e., error-correction codes (ECC) and RAID (redundant array of independent disks), to protect NAND-based SSD from serious noise/disturb [4], [18]. However, bit error rates of modern SSDs have increased to the point that more complex ECCs (e.g., Low-Density Parity Check Codes (LDPC) or polar codes [19]) or Maximal Distance Separate (MDS) codes (e.g., RAID 6 or Partial MDS codes [2]) are only regarded as sufficient solutions. The theme of this work is to show that instead of designing new error correction mechanisms, the NAND-based SSD reliability can be improved by designing a joint decoder exploring the inherent information between ECCs and RAIDs.
We first present SSD internals that are essential for understanding the error correction mechanisms. For a thorough investigation, we encourage interested readers to [5]. A basic unit is a page whose size ranges between 2KB and 64KB. A group of pages, usually in the order of hundreds of pages, form a block. A plane contains blocks of pages. A channel connects multiple planes. Several plans (e.g., 1 ∼ 4) can be packaged as a single chip. A page stripe is made up of pages on different planes or, possibly, different chips.
ECCs and RAIDs are two levels of coding techniques to protect SSDs. Pages are protected by ECCs, and stripes are protected by RAIDs [3] when ECCs fail (for which we say a hard error occurs). Therefore, when reading a page, the page goes through a ECC decoder first, then a RAID decoder if the ECC decoder fails, and the page reading fails if both decoders fail.

A linear [m, k, d] code is called a RAID if there are k information pages, r = m − k redundancy pages, and it can tolerate r hard errors. For example, consider an array of m SSD pages within a stripe, m − 1 pages carry information, while the mth is the XOR-summing of the m − 1 information pages. The pattern is repeated for each stripe of pages in planes. Such an architecture is called RAID 4 or RAID 5, and only one hard error is recoverable (see Fig. 1 for an example).
Despite a large body of coding works on SSD, e.g., [6], [11]–[17], [19], [21], [27], complex ECCs/RAIDs are still needed. The theme of this work is to show that instead of designing new error correction mechanisms, the NAND-based SSD reliability can be improved by designing a joint decoder exploring the inherent information between ECCs and RAIDs. Essentially, the RAID-ECC solutions form a product code [20]. Let C1 be an [n1, k1, d1] linear block code and C2 be an [n2, k2, d2] linear block code. The product code C = C1 ⊗ C2 consists of all matrices whose rows are C1 and whose columns are C2, which is an [n1n2, k1k2, d1d2] [20] linear block code. In example 1, the product code of [7, 4, 3] Hamming code and [5, 4, 2] RAID 4 is an [35, 16, 6] linear block code, and it can correct up to 5 erasures.
In this work, we aim at improving the NAND-based SSD reliability by designing a joint decoder of the product code formed by LDPC and RAID (LDPC/RAID). Although the general decoding of a product code is the Chase algorithm [22], a joint decoder of LDPC/RAID adopts a simple and effective message-passing algorithm. We first consider an LDPC/RAID 4 system. Conventionally, only one hard error is recoverable, however, when using the inherent information it is possible to recover two hard errors even with a single parity page. A similar conclusion is generalized to an LDPC/RAID 6 system.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. In Section II.A, we give our joint decoder for LDPC/RAID 4 and show that even with a single parity page it is possible to recover two hard errors. In Section II.B, we generalize the joint decoder scheme to an LDPC/RAID 6 system to accomplish the same task with two parity pages. In Section III, we conduct information theoretic study to explore how much information can be reliably stored with the help of RAID system in NAND-based SSD. Finally, Section IV concludes the paper and suggests problems for future research.

A. Bilayer LDPC for LDPC/RAID 4
1) d = 2 case: y0n and y1n satisfy two sets of parities: k zero parities enforced by the LDPC codebook separately, and n joint extra (presumably known nonzero) parity bits provided by the RAID 4. The two sets of constraints form a

bilayer LDPC code [23] as shown in Fig. 2a: the lower layer corresponds to LDPC codes within two pages (y0n and y1n) and their LDPC parity constraints; the upper layer corresponds to the two pages (y0n and y1n) and the RAID 4 constraint (xn0 + xn1 ), which consists of n parity bits.
The formed bilayer LDPC codes are cosets with n different RAID parity bits and they are identical to each other geometrically. Therefore, we can safely assume n extra parity bits are all zeros to simplify our analysis.
The decoding of the bilayer LDPC/RAID 4 is to decode LDPC codes within pages (i.e., the lower bilayer LDPC codes) and to decode LDPC codes with RAID 4 constraints across pages (i.e., the upper bilayer LDPC code) alternatively. Note that the top bilayer LDPC is (1, 2) regular LDPC code, and there are no cycles in it. Therefore, the conventional sum-product algorithm and density evolution analysis can be applied to the bilayer LDPC as long as the lower bilayer LDPC is assumed to be cycle-free.
To be more concrete, in the following, we assume that the storage channel is the BIAWGN channel with parameter σ. III. SIMULATION RESULTS
Existing RAID 6 codes such as EVENODD, Reed-Solomon code, etc, require an optimal number of parity page columns, namely two, to recover from two catastrophic failures (i.e., a whole colums of pages are hard errors in the RAID system). In addition to the catastrophic failures, the recently proposed

Partial MDS devotes even more extra global parity pages to recover unexpected hard errors. However, one major advantage of RAID-ECC joint decoder is that it achieves the same errorcorrection ability as RAID 6 or Partial MDS code with high probability without extra colums of pages or global pages.
To further verify the advantages of our proposed joint decoding schemes, we conduct experiments to compare the bit error rates (BER) of the conventional individual decoding algorithm, and our proposed joint decoding schemes. The LDPC component of the LDPC/RAID system is (3, 6) LDPCs, of length 800, and are constructed based on [10]. The decoding algorithm of the conventional LDPC algorithm is sum-product decoding, and joint decoding algorithms are based on the proposed algorithms. In all the simulations presented, the maximal decoding iteration is 80.
Fig. 5 shows the performance of the conventional LDPC decoding scheme and our proposed joint decoders over AWGN channels. The results from Fig. 5 show that the performances of the joint decoder are much better than the conventional LDPC decoder, which is consistent with our analytical results. Further, it can be seen that gains of 1.4dB, 1.6dB, and 1.8dB over conventional sum-product decoding are obtained at BER of 10−3 for joint decoders of LDPC/RAID 4 with two hard errors, LDPC/RAID 4 with three hard errors, and case one LDPC/RDP, respectively.
IV. INFORMATION THEORETIC STUDY
In this part, we conduct information theoretic study to explore how much information can be reliably stored with the help of RAID system in NAND-based SSD. Our system model can be abstracted as one special form of the classic relay channel coding model. Information theoretic results are obtained immediately by utilizing known results of relay channel coding.
A. System Model
We model the NAND-based SSD with k RAID parities as a relay channel [9] (k = 1 for RAID 4/5, and k = 2 for RAID 6). Specifically, let X = (X0, X1, · · · , Xm−1) ∈ X m denote the symbol in m NAND flash page cells at the source, and let W = (W0, W1, · · · , Wm−1) ∈ Wm denote the noisy symbol of X received at the relay to calculate a RAID parity Z = (Z0, Z1, · · · , Zk−1) ∈ Zk. In our setting, the noisy symbols of Z and X are accessible at the destination, which can always recover Z. V. CONCLUSION
The main goal of this work is to show how the inherent information within page error-correction code and stripe RAID can be used in order to improve the reliability of SSDs. The main feature which we concerned is the density evolution, where we showed that even with a single parity it is possible to recover two page failures. We believe that the examples and decoders we gave in this paper are only first ideas for better decoders which use similar ideas and properties from NAND flash-based SSDs to improve the reliability. In particular, these ideas can be used in other error-correction codes as well as other families of erasure codes in order to further improve the reliability.
