A Universal Interleaver Design for Bit-Interleaved QC-LDPC Coded Modulation

Xiaojian Liu, and Yuejun Wei Huawei Technology CO., LTD
Shanghai Institution Shanghai, China 201206 Email: {liuxiaojian5, weiyuejun}@huawei.com

Ming Jiang National Mobile Communications Research Laboratory
Southeast University, Nanjing, P. R. China Email: jiang ming@seu.edu.cn

Abstract—A universal design methodology for interleavers in bit-interleaved coded modulation (BICM) systems deploying binary irregular quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes and higher-order modulation is presented. The interleaver is introduced to lower decoding threshold when higher-order modulation is adopted. For this purpose, an optimization procedure with modified numerical analysis as cost function is applied to derive good mapping distributions, which are used to describe the interleaver configuration. Decoding simulation, being consistent with numerical results shows that our method works effectively for LDPC coded modulation.
Index Terms—QC-LDPC; BICM system; PEXIT;
I. INTRODUCTION
The combination of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] and higher-order modulation is adopted by many international standards for wired and wireless communication system. Three current examples are the 5G mobile communication standard, IEEE standard 802.11n [2] and the second generation of digital video broad-casting (DVB-X2) [3].
Bit-interleaved coded modulation systems based on LDPC codes is a power- and bandwidth-efficient technique that combines the optimized error-correcting coding and higher-order modulations. There is a large amount of publications on how to map the coded bits to the signal constellation. Some early studies [4][5] simply design interleavers to put high protected bits to low reliable labeling position, or vice versa. However, these intuitive methods often lead to worse thresholds or higher error floor. After that, based on mapping distributions, later researches [6][7] adopt density evolution (DE) [8] or extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) chart techniques [9] to get more dedicated interleaver design. Of this type, from aspect of LDPC codes, only the row/column weight distribution of parity-check matrixes is considered. It is well known that DE and EXIT analysis can not offer accurate thresholds evaluation for specific LDPC codes, since they ignore the connecting

relationship between variable and check nodes. Although these studies tried to solve the problem through interpreting and modifying the shape of EXIT charts, the numerical results often mismatch with decoding simulation. Therefore, in order to find more practical methods to design interleavers, the accuracy of numerical analysis tools must be improved. In [10], a modified protograph EXIT (PEXIT) analysis is introduced to optimizing mapping for a special class of protograph LDPC codes (AR4A codes) modulated with 16QAM. By taking into account the protograph structure of the code, their numerical analysis results seems consistent with simulation results. The limitation of this approach is the number of reliability levels and variable node types, excepting punctured node, must be equal. For many communication standards, such restriction is too strict.
In this paper, we present a universal design methodology for interleavers in BICM system deploying QCLDPC codes. Motivated by [7] and [10], we develop a modified PEXIT analysis for QC-LDPC coded modulation by taking into account the structure of base matrix and mapping distribution. Our proposed analysis allows an accurate and simple prediction of the threshold. Then, we resort to the differential evolution algorithm [11] to get optimized design. Simulation results show that the optimum mapping derived from our method can guarantee a better or equivalent performance in compare with previously proposed methods.
The paper is organized as follows. Section II describes the system model and mapping distribution. In section III, a modified PEXIT analysis for QC-LDPC coded modulation and a procedure of optimization are presented. Section IV provides the simulation results before concluding remarks in Section V.

II. BIT-INTERLEAVED QC-LDPC CODED MODULATION
A. System model
The model of a BICM block-by-block transmission is depicted in Fig. 1. At the transmitter, a sequence of binary input is encoded by the QC-LDPC encoder. The QC-LDPC code is defined by its parity-check matrix H, which can be reduced to the base matrix with a relatively small size. Fig. 2 illustrate the base graph of rate-2/3 QC-LDPC codes in IEEE 802.11n. In the base matrix, there are a few entries with each 1 and 0 representing a cyclic permutation matrix and a zero matrix, respectively. The tanner graph G(H) of a base matrix reveals not only the weight distribution of variable and check nodes, but also their connection relationship, which makes precise numerical analysis possible.

III. OPTIMIZATION OF MAPPING DISTRIBUTION
A. PEXIT Analysis for QC-LDPC Coded Modulation
EXIT charts are often used to design and optimize the iterative receiver with soft information. An EXIT function provides a method to quantify the exchange of mutual information (MI) between the QC-LDPC decoder and demodulator. The base graph of QC-LDPC codes can be viewed as a special class of protograph with only 1 time copy. We propose a modified EXIT analysis that combines EXIT analysis [9] for demodulator with PEXIT analysis for QC-LDPC codes [14]. In this way, we then derive the extrinsic MI at the output of maximum a posteriori (MAP) demodulator and calculate the asymptotic thresholds of QC-LDPC decoder using different mapping distributions. The distribution with the lowest threshold corresponds to the optimum mapping scheme.

For an AWGN channel, the modified EXIT analysis will only converge when the selected Eb/N0 is greater than the threshold. To find the exact threshold (Eb/N0)∗, we can resort to the method of bisection which is a simple but powerful numerical tool. With only a few iterations, we can get (Eb/N0)∗ with enough precision. As to the short or moderate length codes, the maximum iteration number should be set in the order of 10, since decoders requires less iterations to converge.
The proposed EXIT analysis offers a more precise numerical approach to evaluate the threshold of QCLDPC coded modulation system than traditional EXIT analysis, and requires much less amount of calculation than the DE approach.

IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
Based on the BICM system model in Fig. 1, we simulate the performance over the AWGN channel using QC-LDPC codes according to IEEE 802.11n with code rate 1/2, 2/3, 3/4 and block length N = 1944. We apply Gray-labeled 256-QAM, which is widely used in wireless communication systems. For each case, we generate individual optimized mapping distribution as described in Section III-B. In the decoding simulations, the QAM signals are detected by the log maximum

a posteriori (Log-MAP) algorithm with symbol-to-bit metric. With the soft output of the demodulator, the QC-LDPC codes are decoded by the standard sumproduct algorithm (SPA), where the maximum number of iteration is set to 50.
First, in Fig. 3 we compare our scheme with even and the optimized mapping distribution proposed in [7]. A 256-QAM transmission over the AWGN channel and the LDPC code with R = 1/2 and N = 1944 are considered. In Table I the optimized mapping distribution is provided. With our modified EXIT analysis with maximum iteration set to 50, The estimated thresholds of ours and [7] are both 8.22dB. While in [7], the estimated threshold is 7.62dB. In Fig. 3, we observe that the two mapping distributions show equivalent performance, and our numerical result is much closer to the simulation results. Around 0.5dB gain is achieved compared with even mapping distribution. It is worth noting that two mapping distributions with very different patterns exhibit almost the same performance. Thus, there is no oneto-one relationship between mapping distributions and good performance. It explains why the intuitive methods, like [7] or its transformed versions, can not guarantee good performance. Besides, our scheme is only designed to optimize thresholds. The error floor performance of QC-LDPC codes in higher order modulation is affected by many other aspects. A detailed discussion can be found in [15].
In Fig. 4 results for R = 2/3 and R = 3/4 in terms of BER and FER are given. Their mapping distributions are provided in Table II and Table III. The thresholds get by our PEXIT analysis are 11.08dB and 12.48dB for R = 2/3 and R = 3/4, respectively. We observe that our numerical results are still consistent with simulation results.
