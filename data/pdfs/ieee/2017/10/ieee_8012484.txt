UEP Constructions of Quasi-Cyclic Low-Density Parity-Check Codes via Masking
Chi-Jen Wu, Chung-Hsuan Wang, Member, IEEE, and Chi-chao Chao, Member, IEEE

Abstract‚ÄĒ In this paper, the algebraic constructions of quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes with the unequal error protection (UEP) property are considered. A criterion for constructing such codes via the masking technique is proposed, based on which explicit conditions on the base paritycheck matrices and masking matrices to achieve UEP are provided. We also give three specific constructions of UEP QC-LDPC codes. Furthermore, a sufficient condition to ensure strict UEP is presented. Simulation results demonstrate the superiority of our constructed codes over time-sharing schemes. The constructed codes also have competitive error performance against randomly constructed equal-error-protection LDPC codes and irregular UEP LDPC codes designed based on the degree distribution.
Index Terms‚ÄĒ Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, quasicyclic (QC) codes, unequal error protection (UEP), masking.
I. INTRODUCTION
I N many practical systems, such as broadcast channels, packet-switching networks, visual communication systems, storage systems, etc., error-correcting codes for unequal error protection (UEP) [1], [2] are more desirable than those for equal error protection (EEP). For example, color and depth are two major factors in the efficient representation of threedimension video over mobile devices. The packet loss of color causes more image distortion than that of depth. Therefore, color should be protected more strongly than depth. If traditional EEP error-correcting codes are applied, color and depth are both provided with the same error-correcting capability. Such a channel coding scheme seems inefficient because too much protection may be put on depth. In contrast, for UEP codes, the coded bits are divided into several parts and each part has a different error-correcting capability. If UEP codes are employed in such scenario, then we can more efficiently protect the source data with different importance.

However, quite a number of good LDPC codes were based on random constructions, which result in large unstructured parity-check matrices, causing high wiring complexity for the very-large-scale-integration (VLSI) implementation. The processes of storing and accessing messages based on the corresponding Tanner graphs of the unstructured parity-check matrices in encoding/decoding become very complicated. Quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes [5]‚Äď[11] are LDPC codes with parity-check matrices usually composed of blocks of circulant matrices. Owing to their structured parity-check matrices, QC-LDPC codes have the advantages of efficient encoding/decoding and allowing for high-speed VLSI implementation. Moreover, good QC-LDPC codes have similar error performance as randomly constructed LDPC codes [6]‚Äď[11].
In recent years, a number of research efforts were made to construct LDPC codes with good UEP capability. In [12] and [13], families of UEP codes were constructed by puncturing. Given an LDPC mother code, the higher-rate child codes are obtained by periodically deleting certain bits of codewords of the mother code, and all child codes share the same decoder of their mother code. In addition, the selection of punctured bits plays an important role on the puncturing performance. However, in the conventional puncturing process, the allocation of punctured bits and the design of mother codes are considered separately to reduce the design complexity; the resulting high-rate child codes may have degraded error performance compared with good codes of the same rate. In [14]‚Äď[19], irregular LDPC codes were designed for UEP by analyzing the thresholds of LDPC codes according to the degree distributions of variable nodes. However, besides design of the degree distribution, it still needs to carefully take other critical factors which also influence the error performance of LDPC codes into consideration to obtain a desirable parity-check matrix. For example, a short-length LDPC code which satisfies the designed degree distribution with many short cycles in the corresponding Tanner graph should be avoided. In [20], a lower-triangular form of the parity-check matrices was introduced for UEP, but only two levels of protection could be provided. In [21], a modified Plotkin-type construction for multi-level UEP was proposed; however, such a construction introduces a lot of length-4 cycles in the Tanner graphs of the constructed parity-check matrices, which could cause critical performance loss by conventional message-passing decoding. In addition, the error-correcting capability of those codes may not be fully exploited because decoding is done for each component code individually. In [22], a general framework to obtain distributed joint source-channel codes by using irregular UEP LDPC codes with unequal thresholds for different groups of variable nodes was proposed. In [23] and [24], several UEP schemes of LDPC codes for applications to radiography image transmission were provided; different LDPC codes with some overlapped nodes are needed in those schemes. For block codes, the separation [1] is defined as the minimum Hamming weight of the codewords associated with a certain coded bit, and it is an useful tool to evaluate the UEP performance based on the viewpoint of the minimum distance. The blockwise separation considered in this paper is an extension of the separation. For a codeword, the coded bits can be divided into several blocks, and the blockwise separation is the minimum weight of the codewords with the coded bits in a certain block not all equal to zero, which describes the error-correcting capability of the coded bits in that block. We adopt the blockwise separation for describing the UEP property under the premise that the constructed codes do not have too small column weights and length-4 cycles in the corresponding Tanner graphs. Although the error performance of QC-LDPC codes is influenced by many factors, such as the girth, minimum distance, trapping sets [26], etc., the work in [27] has shown that if a code has no harmful trapping sets of size smaller than its minimum distance, the error performance is dominated by the minimum distance rather than the trapping sets. Therefore, we study how to construct good UEP QC-LDPC codes with rich algebraic structures and suitable for decoding by conventional messagepassing algorithms based on the viewpoint of the minimum distance in this work. Furthermore, originally the masking technique [8] was proposed to construct EEP QC-LDPC codes, where a parity-check matrix of a QC-LDPC code is first chosen as the base matrix and then some nonzero submatrices in the base matrix are set to zero matrices according to a specially designed masking matrix which does not decrease the minimum distance; the resulting masked matrix hence has a reduced number of short cycles to improve the error performance. We observe that the number and positions of the zero matrices in the base matrix, which can be changed according to the masking matrix, have a huge influence on the separations of the QC-LDPC code. In this paper, we make a brand new attempt to design the masking strategy for construction of good UEP QC-LDPC codes.
In the following, we summarize the main results of this paper. First of all, a shift criterion for LDPC codes constructed via masking to achieve UEP is proposed. This criterion states that for each blockwise separation, if there exists a codeword

with weight equal to the blockwise separation such that it is also a legitimate codeword after a left shift of the corresponding blocks of columns, then the blockwise separations of the LDPC code must be in the increasing order. We further give explicit conditions to choose the base matrix and masking matrix to satisfy the proposed shift criterion. The first condition is that the base matrix is of the modified blockwise circulant form and the second condition is that the masking matrix is of the upper triangular form with equal entries on every diagonal, which can easily be achieved. Three explicit forms of the masking matrix: the triangular form, terminal triangular form, and removal triangular form are also presented for construction of UEP codes. Our previous constructions of UEP QC-LDPC codes in [28] and [29] could be considered as special cases of the general construction in this paper. Furthermore, by adding one extra condition that the codewords satisfying the shift criterion have at least one bit equal to 1 in the positions corresponding to the first block of columns of the parity-check matrix, the modified shift criterion guarantees that the blockwise separations are in the strictly increasing order, which ensures the strict UEP property. Since it is too costly to conduct a direct search for codewords satisfying the modified shift criterion, we further give a sufficient condition which can easily be checked. Our work has the advantage of flexible choices of the code rate, code length, and number of UEP levels. Furthermore, no matter how many levels of UEP are desired, only a single message-passing decoder is needed and the coded bits corresponding to different UEP levels are decoded simultaneously. Computer simulations are also conducted to verify the UEP capabilities of constructed codes. The results show that if the component matrices of the modified blockwise circulant base matrices correspond to the parity-check matrices of QC-LDPC codes with good error performance, the constructed codes also have good error performance.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. The basic concepts of QC-LDPC codes and the masking technique are reviewed in Section II. Section III presents the generalized constructions of UEP QC-LDPC codes based on masking. Then in Section IV, a sufficient condition which ensures the blockwise separations in the strictly increasing order is described. Simulation results are provided in Section V for performance verification. Finally, the work is concluded in Section VI.

Based on this property, a QC-LDPC code can be described by a parity-check matrix consisting of blocks of circulant matrices after some row and column permutations. IV. SUFFICIENT CONDITION TO ENSURE STRICT UEP
In Section III, we have presented the constructions of UEP QC-LDPC codes with blockwise separations in the nondecreasing order, i.e., d1 ‚Č§ d2 ‚Č§ ¬∑ ¬∑ ¬∑ ‚Č§ dl. In this section, we give a sufficient condition to ensure the blockwise separations in the strictly increasing order, i.e., d1 < d2 < ¬∑ ¬∑ ¬∑ < dl. We call this property strict UEP. With strict UEP, coded bits corresponding to the higher level experience strictly better protection than those corresponding to the lower level, and hence the UEP capability is guaranteed.

V. SIMULATION RESULTS
Traditional time-sharing (TS) approaches for UEP are to design several codes with different error-correcting capabilities. Here we consider two types of time-sharing UEP schemes for comparison with the two-level UEP code based on our construction. Assume that almost the same number of information bits are transmitted by the constructed UEP QC-LDPC code and the type I TS scheme within a fixed time slot. For the type I scheme, the information bits are divided into two parts. One part is protected by a QC-LDPC code of lower-rate with better error-correcting capability for level-2 protection. The other part is protected by another code of higher-rate with worse error performance for level-1 protection. The total number of parity-bits of these two QC-LDPC codes of different rates is almost the same as that of the constructed code for fair comparison. This is equivalent to keeping almost the same overall code rates within a time slot. We further consider an extreme case for the type II TS scheme. VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we propose a shift criterion to achieve UEP for the QC-LDPC codes constructed via masking. We further give explicit conditions on the base matrices and masking matrices to satisfy the shift criterion. Then three specific constructions for UEP QC-LDPC codes are presented. Furthermore, a sufficient condition to ensure the strict UEP property is also given. Simulation results have demonstrated the superiority of the constructed codes over time-sharing schemes. The constructed codes also have competitive error performance against randomly constructed EEP LDPC codes and irregular UEP LDPC codes designed based on the degree distribution.
Although our constructions provide UEP codes of flexible choices of rates, lengths, and levels of protection, unfortunately, direct application of existing techniques for performance analysis of LDPC codes such as density evolution [31]
