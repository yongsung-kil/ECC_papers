Stochastic Resonance in Iterative Decoding: Message Passing and Gradient Descent Bit Flipping
Predrag Ivanis, Srdan Brkic and Bane Vasic´

Abstract—This paper contains a survey on iterative decoders of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes made of unreliable logic gates that are capable to provide lower probability of error, when compared to their perfectly reliable counterparts. We have recently shown that the error-floor performance of messagepassing decoders can be improved, if randomness that exists in unreliable logic gates is incorporated into decoding deliberately, without any complexity cost. Furthermore, we have shown that controlling the level of unreliability enable us to exploit the stochastic resonance phenomenon, previously observed in theoretical physics, electronic and magnetic systems. In contrary to common belief, we have shown that for a narrow range of gate failure probability the overall decoding performance is dramatically increased. In this paper, we show that the effect of stochastic resonance is even more noticeable for the case of the gradient descent bit-flipping (GDBF) algorithm. This decoder combines the simplest iterative decoding algorithm with gradient descent optimization, making it an attractive solution for a variety of low complexity storage systems, or code-based cryptosystems. In addition, we show that getting the most of the stochastic resonance is essentially a deep learning problem, since setting the levels of unreliability for individual parts of the decoder by a training process is a step toward incorporating the machine learning techniques into design and analysis of iterative decoders of LDPC codes.
Keywords—Bit-flipping, Gallager B, gradient-descent, faulttolerance, Low-Density Parity-Check codes, stochastic resonance
I. INTRODUCTION
With the steady increase of integration factor density offered by nano-technologies, the main technological obstacle to overcome is to develop a non-determinisitic computing paradigm beyond the legacy of von Neumann, Turing, and Boole that will embrace the inherent stochastic behavior of nano devices and circuit fabrics [1], [2].
Classical approach is to handle such stochastic behavior of logic gates in combinatorial logic and finite automata is due John von Neumann [3] at Princeton and Shmuel Winograd [4] at IBM. The increased reliability of a circuit is obtained by redundancy, i.e., replication of logic gates and majority voting. Another way of adding redundancy to increase reliability is through error correction coding. Coding can be viewed as a generalization of replication and majority voting, but unlike

A foundation of this approach was established by Michael Taylor [5] at MIT and Alexander Kuznetsov [6] at the Russian Academy of Sciences, who proved information can be stored in an unreliable memory even if a decoder is noisy. Their method relies on low-density parity check (LDPC) codes, which is the only class of codes known today capable of tolerating noise in a decoder.
The past decade has witnessed surge in work in fault tolerant error correction decoders. It was initiated by our results [7]– [13], in which we have established a connection between the Taylor and Kuznetsov fault-tolerant memory architectures on one hand and iterative decoders such as Gallager and bit-flipping decoders, on the other. This has allowed us to improve classical results, and enable the analysis of faulttolerant decoders using tools developed in modern coding theory, such as density evolution [14]–[18]. It has also enabled the analysis of realistic gate failure models such as correlated gate failures [19]–[23]. The main theme of these results is an assumption that all logic gates in a decoder are noisy. In this setting, the best one can hope for is to approach the performance noiseless decoder.
While searching for decoders that can tolerate noise in logic gates, we discovered that under certain conditions, noise can actually improve decoding performance [24]. This effect occurs when we allow a tiny fraction of reliable logic gates.
This positive effect of noise, known as stochastic resonance is a well established phenomenon in neural systems, lasers, semiconductor and quantum interference devices. It has been exploited computationally in image processing, detection, parameter estimation and search algorithms, but until our work [24], [25] and the work by Sundararajan et al. [26], there has been little effort in applying these methods in error-correction coding. In the past two years, we have made a progress in this direction, and showed that stochastic resonance decoders have exceptional performance and very low resource cost, and may prove superior to the existing techniques in computer memory and data storage applications [27]–[29]. We have also developed a methodology based on absorbing Markov chains to analyze the decoder dynamics, including convergence and codeword probability of error [25], [30], [31].
The paper provides an overview of the current state of our understanding of the the mechanisms beneath stochastic resonance in iterative decoders, and methods their analysis, optimizations, and harnessing for their full benefit. The decision rule of the γi-input MAJ gate is slightly changed. If all inputs of the MAJ gate are the same, the output is equal to the values of the inputs. If this condition is not satisfied, the threshold is decremented and it is allowed that one input can be different than the other to make the decision. If more than one input is different than the others, the threshold is further decremented until the decision is made. As the threshold does not depend on the column weight, in the case of irregular codes this decision rule favors the inverting of the variable nodes with the larger column weight, that can improve the decoder performance. It is also assumed that the corresponding syndrome is calculated by using a perfect hardware.
In the variable nodes, the MAJ gates with γi − 1 inputs generate massages νv(1i→ ) cj which are sent to the check node neighbors. The initial estimate from the channel appear at the output of a MAJ gate if number of zeros and ones at its inputs is equal. In the next iteration, massages μ(c2j)→vi are send from the check nodes to the variable nodes, the corresponding state is μ(1) = (000, 00, 00, 00, 111), and the codeword estimate after the second iteration is obtained as xˆ(2) = (0, 0, 0, 0, 1) .
In the subsequent iterations, the outputs of the (γi − 1) XOR gates define inputs of the (γi − 1) MAJ gates and vice versa. In further iterations, the codeword estimates will be xˆ(3) = (0, 0, 0, 0, 0), xˆ(4) = (1, 0, 0, 0, 0), etc. In other words, in even iterations the codeword estimate will be incorrect and correspond to the sent codeword, while in odd iterations the codeword estimate will result in a zero-syndrome and will be correct. This illustrates that increasing the maximum number of iterations L does not necessarily result in a successful decoding if the syndrome is checked in only in one (last) iteration. In the above case, successful decoding is possible only if the syndrome is checked (by the perfect checker) in no less than two successive iterations. In general, the minimal required number of consecutive syndrome checks may vary for different error patterns.

The previous analysis is given for the perfect decoder. In the case of the faulty decoder, failures in logic gates make it possible to reach an absorbing state provided large enough L. As shown in [25] the F ER and M ER of the faulty decoder can be calculated by using absorbing Markov chains. It can be proved that the conditional F ER is lower bounded by the corresponding conditional M ER, and if tends to infinity we obtain F ERe(∞) = M ERe(∞), for any error pattern e.
IV. NUMERICAL RESULTS
A. The Noisy Gallager B Decoder
It is clear that some patterns correctable by the perfect decoder may become uncorrectable by the noisy decoder, but that some uncorrectable patterns may become correctable by a noisy decoder with some probability. We will now illustrate this is a few examples, where the numerical results for conditional F ER and M ER are obtained both by using Monte Carlo simulations and by using the analytical approach based on Markov chains. As it is previously shown that errors and XOR and MAJ gates have the similar effect to the decoder performance, for the reason of simplicity, we will assume in all examples that only XOR gates are faulty with probability α⊕.
First, we will consider weight-two error pattern e=(11000), uncorrectable by using the perfect Gallager B algorithm. It is evident that increase in number of iterations results in better performance for the certain values of the failure rates. Probability that the pattern is not decoded converge to the

corresponding miscorrection probability, for the infinite number of decoding iterations. This bound for the M ER depends on the failure rate, but for low failure rates usually have constant value. Also, it can be noticed that the convergence speed depend on the failure rate and convergence is faster for larger failure rates α⊕. Numerical results obtained by using the presented analytical approach based on absorbing Markov chains perfectly corresponds to the simulation results.
We also consider weight-three error pattern e = (00111), uncorrectable by using the perfect Gallager B algorithm. It is interesting that this pattern is uncorrectable also (with probability one) by using perfect maximum likelihood decoder but the numerical results presented in Fig. 5(b) indicate that there is non-zero probability to correct it by using noisy Gallager B decoder. Although the probability of miscorrection is larger than probability of correct decoding, this effect is not negligible. If the failure rate in the logic gates is lower, more decoding iterations are required for successive decoding but probability that the pattern is not corrected always converge to the miscorrection probability.
The conditional F ER for the correctable low-weight error pattern e=(10000) is presented in Fig. 6(a), as a function of number of decoding iterations. If the failure rate is low, the convergence speed is slower but also the upper bound for M ER (lower bound for F ER) bound is reduced. Performance for an another correctable error pattern (e=(10000)) is presented in Fig. 6(b), and the smaller failure rate also reduce the upper bound for M ER in this case. In contrary to the other considered error patterns, in this case the conditional F ER can not be decreased by prolonging of the decoding process but the relation F ERe(∞) = M ERe(∞) is still satisfied. B. Comparison of Different Noisy Decoders
In the previous section we have shown that described Markov chain model can adequately predict error-rates of noisy decoders applied on short codes. Furthermore, in [25] we have verified its applicability in predicting error-floor performance of larger codes also. Here we use Monte Carlo simulations in order to illustrate the full potential of the stochastic resonance notable in message-passing and gradient-descent noisy decoders. Decoders are run on reference quasi-cyclic (155,64) code, called Tanner code [33], for different levels of gate unreliability and number of iterations L, which is depicted in Fig. 7. Presented results illustrate surprising effect attributed to

stochastic resonance - hardware induced errors help decoder to overcome the structural deficiencies that exist in finite-length codes, otherwise known as trapping sets.
In order to take advantage of stochastic resonance the level of noise added into decoder should be carefully chosen: too much noise forces the decoder into miscorrection, while to little do not break trapping sets. The optimal noise level which produces stochastic resonance depends on the chosen decoder, minimum distance of the code, as well as communication channel unreliability. Since computational errors are built-in within the existing hardware, there is no additional hardware cost paid to reduce decoding error rate. However, as shown in Fig. 7 the performance gain is strongly dependent on the number of iterations allowed for the decoding. It can be noted that latency increase for the Gallager B decoder is higher, compared to GDBF decoder, despite of its superiority when noiseless hardware is used. For example, targeted error rate of F ER = 10−5, at BSC cross-over probability αM = 10−2, noisy Gallager B decoder achieves for L = 1000 iterations, while GDBF decoder requires only L = 100 iterations. As we have shown in [30], noisy GDBF decoding can be further improved by periodical rewinding, which assumes that after a short round (usually no more that 20 iterations) the decoding process restarts. Rewinding enables full exploitation of noiseinserted randomness, and minimizes possible miscorrections. We have reported in [30] that noisy decoders not only achieve sphere-packing bound, but are able to approach ML performance also.
V. CONCLUSION
The paper explains stochastic resonance phenomenon, recently observed in the context of decoding LDPC codes by unreliable logic gates. We mainly focused on Gallager B decoder due to its simplicity and ability to contract analytical model that can predict performance if update node operations are prone to hardware-induced errors. We showed, by tracking

the dynamic of decoding by Markov process, that after sufficiently large number of iterations every trapping set breaks and decoder converges to a valid codeword. Only constraint regarding the improvement provided by stochastic resonance is related to decoding latency, due to possible applications of stochastic decoders in high-speed communication systems.
It should be emphasized that the decoder constructed from large groups of logic gates (all XOR gate, or MAJ gates) with the same level of unreliability is not necessary the best choice in terms of decoding latency. Adding more noise to nodes that are involved in trapping sets, while simultaneously reducing level of unreliability for other parts of the decoder can speedup the convergence. Finding optimal noise insertion method in essentially a deep-learning problem and solving it is left for our future work.
