IMWBF Decoding Algorithm with Dynamic Magnitude Update for LDPC Codes
Hui Zhao, Jianping Zheng State Key Laboratory of Integrated Services Networks, Xidian University, Xi’an, P.R. China
zhaohui0203@163.com, jpzheng@xidian.edu.cn

Abstract—This paper proposes a modified IMWBF decoding algorithm for low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. In the proposed modification, the magnitude of the received signal is updated when the number of the unsatisfied parity-check equations participated by this received signal is larger than a predetermined threshold. Through this modification, the outlier, defined as the undesired large-magnitude received signal, can achieve a larger probability to be flipped, compared with the conventional IMWBF. Simulation results show that the proposed method has better performance than the conventional IMWBF, especially for the LDPC code with low column-degree distribution.
Index Terms—LDPC; decoding; IMWBF; dynamic magnitude update
I. INTRODUCTION
The bit-flipping (BF) algorithm of low-density parity check (LDPC) codes was first proposed by Gallager [1]. In recent years, the BF algorithm and its variants have been investigated extensively. One of the most popular variant, called weighted BF (WBF) [2], incorporates the channel magnitude information and shows better performance than BF. Following [2], modified weighted BF (MWBF) [3] and improved MWBF (IMWBF) [4] were further proposed to improve the performance, which consider both the check-based and bit-based reliabilities. In [5], the reliability ratio WBF (RRWBF) algorithm was further proposed, which utilizes a normalized reliability ratio as reliability metric of check node. In [6], parallel WBF was proposed, where the maximum metric is searched only in the subset of symbols associated with one parity-check. Thus, it overcomes the drawback of high search complexity resulted by the global search of the maximum metric in the other WBF algorithms [2]-[5]. In [7], a reliability-based scheduling of BP decoding was proposed, where unreliable bits are identified and then prevented from propagating their messages in the successive iterations.
Another variant of the BF algorithm is the gradient descent BF (GDBF) [8]. In GDBF, the metric is calculated by a maximum-likelihood-based gradient descent optimization method. In [9], the adaptive threshold technique was applied for GDBF, and the proposed adaptive threshold bit flipping (ATBF) provides lower latency and lower power consumption with a cost of performance degradation compared with the multiple-bit flipping GDBF. In [10], the ATBF was further improved by employing a modified reliability ratio. The resulted reliability ratio weighted GDBF (RRWGDBF) achieves a better trade-off between performance and latency. In [11],

the noisy GDBF algorithm was proposed, which introduces a random perturbation in the metric and allows the decoding to escape from undesirable local maxima. Recently, the dynamic WBF algorithm based on WBF and GDBF was studied [12], where the checksum weight is updated dynamically based on the flipping function.
For the WBF algorithms, the received signal magnitude is used as the reliability information. In general, a signal with larger magnitude means higher reliability and smaller probability to be flipped. However, those signals with large magnitude resulted by the deconstructive large noise are undesired, since their hard decisions are incorrect and have a small probability to be flipped in the WBF iteration. Here, the deconstructive large noise is defined as the noise with large magnitude and an opposite sign to the transmit signal. For notation simplicity, these received signals are termed as outliers.
To address the above problem partly, this paper proposes a modified IMWBF algorithm. In the proposed method, the magnitude of the received signal is scaled down when the number of unsatisfied parity-check equations participated by this received signal is larger than a predetermined threshold. By this modification, the outlier acquires an opportunity to decrease its magnitude (reliability) and achieves a larger probability to be flipped in the WBF iteration. Simulation results show that the proposed method can achieve a better performance with a cost of small complexity increasing.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. A brief introduction of LDPC codes and IMWBF algorithm is given in Section II. The proposed modified IMWBF is presented in Section III. The simulation results and conclusions are given in Section IV and Section V, respectively.
II. LDPC CODES AND IMWBF
A. LDPC Codes
A regular binary LDPC code is usually denoted as (N , K, dv, dc) LDPC. Here, N is the codeword length, K is the information length, dv and dc are the column weight and row weight, respectively of the parity check matrix H = [hij], i = 1, 2, ..., M , j = 1, 2, ..., N with M =N −K. Moreover, the set of bits that participate in check i is denoted by N (i) = {j|hij = 1}, and the set of checks participated by bit j by M (j) = {i|hij = 1}.

C. Complexity
In this section, the additional complexity introduced by the proposed modified IMWBF is analyzed. From Section II-B and Section III-B, it is easy to see that the additional complexity is resulted by Step 4 and Step 5. Denote Na as the average time of the received signal magnitude update in Step 4. Then, the check-based reliability update of (1) in Step 5 is performed at most Nadcdv times, and each of the checkbased reliability update requires dc − 1 magnitude comparison operations. Note that the magnitude update is performed once per Q iterations, the complexity introduced by Step 4 is Na/Q real multiplications per iteration and that by Step 5 is Nadcdv/Q minimum-value search (from a set of size dc − 1) operations per iteration.

IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
This section studies the performance of the proposed modified IMWBF through computer simulations. Three codes, Code 1, Code 2 and Code 3, are used here. Code 1 is the PEG-constructed (1008, 504, 3, 6) LDPC code. Code 2 is the FG-constructed (273, 191, 17, 17) LDPC code. Code 3 is the FG-constructed (1023, 781, 32, 32) LDPC code. In the simulation, the maximum iteration number is 100 for Code 1 and Code 2. The parameter α is 0.4 for Code 1 and 1.3 for Code 2. For Code 3, the maximum iteration number is 50, and the parameter α is 1.8.
A. Impact of Correction Factor
The impact of the correction factor ρ on the proposed modified IMWBF is studied. Fig. 3 gives the bit error rate (BER) curves of Code 1 for varying SNR and ρ. Meanwhile, the impact of ρ on the number of outliers being flipped is shown in Table III. From both Fig. 3 and Table III, ρ=0.8 is a good choice in this scenario. Too small or too large ρ will lead to performance degradation.

Finally, the performance comparison with DWBF [12] is presented. Fig. 8 shows the BER performance of Code 3 in both the modified IMWBF and DWBF. From Fig. 8, the proposed modified IMWBF achieves about 0.1dB gains over IMWBF at BER=10−5. Compared with DWBF algorithm, the modified IMWBF has a loss of performance about 0.25dB at BER=10−5. Note that the dynamic strategy is realized by simple scaling of the received signal in the modified IMWBF while that is by the clipping of the flipping functions which are varied each iteration. Thus, compared with DWBF, the proposed algorithm has a lower complexity and a little worse performance.

D. Perfomance Comparison with IMWBF
The performance of the modified IMWBF is compared with other WBF algorithms. Figs. 6 and 7 give the performance comparison of the modified IMWBF with IMWBF for Code 1 and Code 2, respectively. From this figure, the performance gain of the modified IMWBF is only about 0.1 dB at BER=10−5. V. CONCLUSION
This paper proposed a modified IMWBF algorithm for LDPC codes. In this modification, the magnitude of the received signal is updated dynamically. This increases the probability of the outlier to be flipped in the WBF iterations. Therefore, the proposed modification achieves a better performance than IMWBF.
