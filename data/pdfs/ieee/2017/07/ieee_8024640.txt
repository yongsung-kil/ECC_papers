A Survey on Decoding Schedules of LDPC Convolutional Codes and Associated Hardware
Architectures

Hayfa BEN THAMEUR1, Bertrand LE GAL2, Nadia KHOUJA1, Fethi TLILI1, Christophe JEGO2 1 SUPCOM, GRESCOM Lab, University of Carthage, Tunisia.
2 Bordeaux INP, CNRS IMS, UMR 5218, Bordeaux University, France.

Abstract—Low-density parity-check convolutional codes (LDPC-CC) have interesting error correction features. They have a great potential to become a key error-correcting codes for enhancing reliability of modern digital communication systems, optical systems and storage devices. On the implementation side, however, the design of low-cost low-power and high-throughput LDPC-CC decoders remains challenging. This survey paper provides an overview of the state-of-the-art of different algorithmic optimizations proposed to ease and improve the LDPC-CC decoder implementations. To this end, a summary of the available decoding scheduling approaches is provided. Besides, a complexity analysis, performance comparison and an architectural analysis of LDPC-CC decoders based on different scheduling techniques is detailed.

I. INTRODUCTION

Introduced in [1], LDPC convolutional codes (LDPC-CC)

are gaining great attention due to their capacity to achieve the

maximum a-posterior probability (MAP) decoding threshold

when iteratively decoded using the belief propagation (BP)

message passing algorithm (MP) [2] [3]. It has been proven

that this excellent BP threshold is reached when transmission

takes place over general binary-input memoryless symmetric-

output (BMS) channels, like the binary erasure channel (BEC)

[3] [2] and the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) [3],

as well as general multi-user channels, such as the multiple

access channel (MAC) with erasures [4]. Interestingly, such

a university is not afforded by polar codes [5] and irregular

LDPC block codes (LDPC-BC) [6].

Notably, this key feature of LDPC-CCs is, successfully,

achieved under two crucial assumptions. The first one demands

that large termination length L must be considered. The second

one imposes that the BP algorithm must be performed.

The conventional decoding technique of LDPC-CCs is

based on the block wise approach [1] [7] [8], meaning that

the decoder waits until L code blocks are entirely received to

start the BP algorithm. This approach provides high memory

complexity and high processing latency. Referring to the first

assumption, it seems that such decoder is depreciated. Alter-

natively, a window based decoding approach was proposed

in [9]–[13]. It operates on window of size W that slides

along the Tanner graph sequentially. As W L, the storage

requirement is reduced by a factor of

L W

as well as the

decoder initial latency. Moreover, a fully parallel window

based decoder has been recently introduced in [13]. It reveals

a fixed overall latency, for each received code block, that is equal to the time needed to execute one iteration.
Despite its good error correction performances [14]–[18], the BP algorithm has low convergence speed and high hardware complexity due to the costly tanh operations. In order to comply with the practical hardware circuit constraints, two main strategies were considered. The first one aims to simplify message computation rules. Thereby, sub-optimal versions of the BP algorithm have been, extensively, experimented [16] [18]–[30]. The second one relies on message computations reordering using efficient MP schedules. An MP schedule defines the order of passing the messages along the Tanner graph edges. It determines the number of messages needed to be processed so that the propagation rate of information over the LDPC-CC’s graph. Consequently, it affects the convergence speed and the computational complexity of the decoder and thus its hardware implementation performances.
In this survey, we present the different iterative MP scheduling techniques proposed in literature for decoding LDPCCCs. Related hardware architectures are also reported in order to point out the impact of schedules on the hardware cost (throughput, area, memory) and the decoding latency of LDPC-CC decoders.
The remainder of this survey is organized as follows: Section II describes LDPC-CC codes. Section III introduces possible approaches for iteratively decoding LDPC-CCs. Through section IV and V, existing scheduling procedures are discussed from an algorithmic and computational/memory complexity standpoints. Finally, LDPC-CC decoders hardware architectures and performances are provided in section VI.

III. ITERATIVE DECODING OF LDPC-CC CODES
A. Message Update Rules
Similar to LDPC-BCs, LDPC-CCs can be decoded with message passing algorithms. The BP algorithm, also known as the Sum-Product algorithm (SPA), is the most efficient one in terms of error correction performance [14]–[18]. However, it suffers from high hardware complexity due to the costly tanh operations which makes it ill-suited for VLSI implementation. A straightforward alternative to the SPA is its Min-Sum (MS) approximation [16] [18]–[22]. Yet, despite the resulting complexity reduction, a significant loss in terms of error-ratio is observed for the MS [16] [18]. It can be recovered by considering several MS variants namely the Normalized MS (NMS) [23] [25]–[28] and the Offset MS (OMS) [24] [29].

B. Possible Approaches For Decoding LDPC-CCs

decoder (BW) starts the decoding process once the entire

codeword v[0,L] is available [8] [21] [31] [32] [16]. Fig. 2.a illustrates the decoding of three codewords. Obviously, the

in [26] reaches 3.66 Gbps at frequency 305 Mhz. Nevertheless,

both decoding techniques demand huge ressource consumption

IV. ITERATIVE MESSAGE UPDATE SCHEDULES FOR DECODING LDPC-CC CODES
A. Flooding-based Schedule
The classical MP schedule for CN and VN processing for a LDPC-CC is called the flooding scheduling (FS) [1] [19]–[22] [14]–[16] [32].
The iterative processing is mainly composed of four intensive computation kernels: Initialization, Check Node Activation (CNA), Variable Node Activation (VNA) and Decision. During each iteration, throughout kernel 2, all CNs computations are performed based on VN LLR messages obtained in the previous iteration. Moreover, all CNs statistics are computed simultaneously (kernel 2) and so it is the case for VNs (kernel 3). Theses properties make the FS an interesting approach used for massive computation parallelization. Consequently, high throughput pipeline hardware decoders based on the FS algorithm was proposed in [1] [7] [22] [16]. For instance, the ASIC decoder presented in [16] achieves up to 2 Gbps throughput at frequency 250 Mhz (see Table II).
Inherently, Kernel 2 and 3 are executed M × Imax times and N × Imax times, respectively. Hitherto, the maximum computation complexity of the flooding decoding algorithm, denoted as Cflooding, can be approximated by:

C. Layered-based Schedule
As mentioned above, at the ith iteration, CNs processing is based only on VN LLRs computed in the previous iteration. Hence, updated CN messages can’t profit from newly updated VN messages during the same iteration. To overcome such inefficiency, a first scheduling technique was proposed in [36] [23] [27] called On-Demand Variable Node Activation (OVA). It was improved as the OVA-CC (OVA with Concealing Channel values) algorithm [23]. Then, it was optimized as a Layered based scheduling approach [25] [26] [28] [18]. Although these techniques are ”CN centric”, ”VN centric” schedules have been also investigated for LDPC-CCs [3].
1) OVA-based Schedule: Firstly introduced in [36], the main idea of the OVA scheme is to change the order of VN and CN updates. As a matter of fact, every CN is considered as one layer. For each layer cj , j ∈ J , only VNs that are involved in computing CN outgoing LLRs are activated. The activation of a variable node vk, k ∈ Nv(cj), consists in calculating the LLR message sent from vk to cj based on the channel LLR Lch(vk) and the previously received LLRs from its connected CNs excluding cj. Note that layers are executed sequentially.
This modification of nodes’ updating order impacts the maximum computation complexity of the OVA decoding algorithm. Denoted as Cova, it can be approximated by:

Cova = Imax × M × (O(CN ) + degc × O(V N )), (6)

where degc is the degree of a CN.

The memory complexity of the OVA is the same as for

the FS schedule. Nonetheless, it has been observed that the

convergence speed of the OVA is twice faster then the FS [36]

[23] [27]. Indeed, to reach similar error bit ratio value to the

FS decoding approach, only half of the number of decoding

iterations is needed. Therefore, the hardware complexity of

LDPC-CC decoder is divided by two.

2) OVA-CC-based Schedule: In order to lessen memory

storage needed by the FS, TBS and OVA approaches, an

alternative scheduling algorithm has been proposed in [23]

termed as OVA-CC. As indicated by its appelation, memory

savings are reached by removing channel LLRs. Indeed,

compared with the OVA technique, computation operations

executed when activating involved VNs are modified in such

a way that channel LLRs are concealed inside the updated VN

to CN messages. This trick relies on two facts. The first one is

that channel LLRs are the same during the decoding process.

The second one is the successive layers’ processing during

each iteration [23]. The memory saving percentage per VN is

In the jth layer, j ∈ J , outgoing messages from the check node cj is computed based on its immediately updated VN neighbors. Incoming messages to cj are said to be calculated on the fly because, unlike previous schedulings, they are not supposed to be kept in memory during the decoding process. D. Windowed Schedule
The previously explored schedules are prevailingly used for BW LDPC-CC decoders. However, they result in fairly great latency compared with WD LDPC-CC decoders. Thereby, a windowed schedule was introduced for windowed decoding of LDPC-CCs [9]–[11] [13] [33]–[35].
Algorithm 1 depicts a formalization of the windowed schedule. The decoding process is carried over windows of size W that slide from position p = 1 to position p = L (Algorithm 1, line 2). In fact, during the pth window decoding (Algorithm 1, Step 2), VNs and CNs updating operations (Algorithm 1, Step 2.1 and 2.2) are performed in the same way as it is done in kernels 2 and 3 of the FS algorithm. The major difference is that only Nw VNs and their connected Mw CNs that are covered by the current window are activated. After executing Imax iterations, decision on the target symbols is made (Algorithm 1, Step 3). Contrary to the former schedules,

The memory complexity of the windowed scheduling can be deduced from analysing memory requirements of one window. In fact, only memory units used for storing messages related to Nw VNs and Mw CNs are needed. This property has motivated intensive works to determine the optimum window size W [3] [9] [12] [33]–[35].
As the windowed schedule has attractive memory and computational complexity improvement, it has been combined with other efficient updating schedule like TBS [34] [9] [10] and OVA schedules [9]. Notably, in [9], it has been shown that such combinations permit to decrease the average number of node updates required per symbol by almost 45% and so the LDPC-CC decoder complexity.
V. UNIFORM AND NON-UNIFORM SCHEDULES
A. Uniform Schedule
An uniform schedule consists on considering the entire set of VNs, Sv, and CNs, Sc, that have to be updated in each iteration. Uniform schedules can be parallel or serial. In one hand, in a uniform parallel schedule, all CNs are simultaneously updated followed by simultaneous VN updates. For instance, the flooding algorithm is a uniform parallel schedule [9] [13]. Thus, Sv and Sc are equal to N and M , respectively. In the other hand, in a uniform serial schedule, all CN updates are performed during each decoding iteration. Within every CN processing, all its connected VNs are updated. The OVA, OVA-CC and the HL decoding techniques are uniform serial schedules [9].
B. Non-Uniform Schedule
In a non-uniform schedule, the ensemble of CNs and VNs that have to be updated vary from one iteration to the next one, i.e, Sv and Sc are re-sized throughout the decoding process. The principal purpose is to avoid unnecessary updates to particular nodes that meet certain assumptions.
In literature, two main assumptions have been reported. The first one relies on comparing the error bit probability (Pe(t)) of VNs involved at each time instant (t) to a pre-determined maximal value Pemax [35] [9] [11]. Indeed, at the end of the ith

iteration, the Pei(t) is calculated for all time instants t. Then, if the value of Pe(t) is less than the one of Pemax, related VNs and CNs are excluded from Sv and Sc, respectively. The second updating constraint relies on the importance of Pe(t) improvements between two successive iterations. As a matter of fact, at the end of the ith iteration, the ratio Pe(t) of the difference between Pei−1(t) and Pei(t) by Pei−1(t) is compared to a minimum improvement θ, 0 ≤ θ ≤ 1, [35] [9] [11]. Hence, if Pe(t) is less then θ, exchanged messages with involved VNs are omitted from the list of nodes to be updated in the (i + 1)th iteration.
C. Discussion
The major difference between uniform and non-uniform schedules is the elimination of superfluous node updates that yields insignificant improvement. This fact leads to an important reduction in complexity for non-uniform schedules compared with uniform ones. Indeed, experiments in [9] [11] have shown that the average number of node updates per symbol of both parallel and serial based uniform schedules is considerably greater than parallel and serial based non-uniform schedules. The gains are further important if improved serial schedules are applied [37] [11]. As a matter a fact, it has been observed that non-uniform parallel windowed schedule provides a 35% - 40% reduction in complexity compared with the uniform ones and it can be increased up to 50% when using a serial non-uniform windowed schedule [11].
VI. HARDWARE ARCHITECTURAL ASPECTS OF LDPC-CC DECODERS
A. Basic Processor Prototype Architecture
From a high-level perspective, similar to LDPC-BCs [38], the implementation of LDPC-CC decoders starts from an isomorphic architecture which is a straightforward mapping of the Tanner graph to related hardware components of an elementary unit, termed as processor. As illustrated in Fig. 4, a processor is a physically independent processing unit that consists of VN units (VNUs) and CN units (CNUs) to perform LLR updates, storage elements for LLRs and a routing network representing the flow of LLRs along the graph’s edges.
In most hardware implementations, throughout the decoding process, a processor executes one decoding iteration including CN and VN computations. B. Possible LLR Messages Storage Approaches
The LLR and message storage elements can be either register-based or memory-based. Register-based processor design passes LLRs along FIFO delay lines. Although, they are suited for high speed ASIC implementations [19], it has been shown in [14] that they are ill-suited for FPGA targets. Besides, for LDPC-CCs of medium to large ms, this approach is undesirable as using large number of registers exhibits high cost and power consumption. Alternatively, memory-based processor design was introduced in [14] and then adopted in almost LDPC-CC decoders implementations (see Table II). LLRs are stored in a RAM based circular buffer and their position addresses, ofently stored in a ROM [15] [32] [16], are tracked using a pointer. They suited for ASIC as well as FPGA targets (see Table II). Depending on the storage choice, the processing parallelism exploitation can becomes harder. Indeed, in RAM based design a single RAM element can be read by clock cycles but register based implementations provides a wider element access parallelism. In [16], an hybrid-partitioned storage element was proposed. It combines registers and memories to trade-off bandwidth and power. Indeed, using 3 memory banks, a 54% clock buffers reduction is achieved as compared with the fully register-based decoder.

Basically, memory is made up using either single-port or multi-port RAMs that can be arranged in single block or in multiple banks. For a parallel CNU, accessing memory simultaneously assumes that there is no memory collision/congestion. In practice, this assumption can not be easily satisfied. Hence, various techniques were proposed to address this challenge. The first one considers one block of single-port memory [8] [22]. In the second one, multi-port memory are developed [16]. The third one relies on splitting the memory into memory banks as illustrated in [14] [32] [8] [39] [22] [16]. In [14], it is has been demonstrated that this method can be inefficient due to the unbalanced code structure. Indeed, it can be found within a Tanner graph more than one LLR message, that are stored in the same memory bank, are connected to the current CNU. This memory access conflict can be avoided by overcloking the memory [14] [15] [32] or optimizing the number of memory banks using the graph coloring algorithm [22] so that the highest throughput is reached with the minimum number of banks and the smaller routing area. In addition, in [17], the switch network was completely replaced by dedicated processing units that provide simpler LLRs routing and higher throughput. Nevertheless, these approaches remains inefficient as code structure optimization is not considered. In [39] [16] [18] [26], a jointly code construction and decoder design have been developed under implementation and performance oriented constraints.
D. Discussion
LDPC-CC decoders’ architectures are summarized in Table II. For fair evaluation, for ASIC based designs, fc and area are scaled to 90 nm technology [22].
Both FPGA-based and ASIC-based LDPC-CC pipelined architectures were intensively studied in literature [1] [14] [20] [22] [16] [29] [24] [17] [26] [28]. The main reason is that they are able to reach throughputs range Gbps at moderate values of fc. For example, a 2.37 Gbps (resp. 2.4 Gbps) is achieved by the ASIC (resp. FPGA)-based LDPCCC decoder, designed in [23](resp. [24]), using only 5 (resp. 9) processors with fc = 128 (resp. 75) MHz . However, such attractive performance comes at the cost of a great latency. Indeed, based on the latency expression (2), stated in subsection III-B3, it is obvious that the corresponding latency depends on the number of processors as well as the ms parameter. Thus, pipelined architectures are particularly unsuitable for LDPC-CCs with large code memory and mostly undesirable for real time applications. Interestingly, to mitigate such limitations, LDPC-CC decoders with single processor were considered in literature [8] [21] [32] [39] [16] [18], known as serial decoders. Unlike pipelined architectures, only few hardware resources have to be consumed. Nevertheless, the resulted low-cost design poses a significant limitation on the achievable decoding throughput. Indeed, in terms of area, it is clear that serial ASIC-based LDPC-CC decoders are smaller than pipelined ones. For instance, the area of the pipelined decoder in [29] is 2.7 times larger than the serial one in [21] but its throughput is 2.5 times higher than

Therefore, serial decoders are mostly recommended for low-cost, moderate throughput applications such as wireless mobile telephone networks and space satellite communications [32]. Several initiatives were proposed to alleviate the latency and enhance the decoding throughput of both LDPC-CC decoder architectures. In this survey, a node based and a scheduling based optimizations are discussed.
Firstly, a node based level of parallelism was introduced in literature. Let ρ be the factor of node parallelization. The main idea is to execute ρ CNUs simultaneously. Table I resumes possible combination of parallelism levels for LDPC-CC decoders. Importantly, this approach has led to a substantial improvement in throughput. With the same value of ρ (=8), the serial decoder in [15] reaches 2 Gbps throughput (fc=250 MHz), however, the pipelined one in [29] only achieves 1.025 Gbps (fc=256.4 Mhz). This gap is due to the latency caused by the pipelined structure which is theoretically 11 times higher than the serial one. In addition, in [23], it has been shown that the decoding latency per processor can be reduced by 62.5% in terms of cycles with only ρ=3. Nonetheless, parallel node operations poses a significant limitation on the occupied area of related decoders as well as complex switching network. Using the same number of processors (=5), the area of the proposed ASIC-based decoder in [23] (ρ=12) is 4.8 times larger than the one synthesized in [27] (ρ=1). Note that the achieved throughput of the former decoder is 23 times higher than the latter one. It was attained at an operating frequency that is 2.2 times slower.
Secondly, the scheduling based optimization relies on the layered schedule. In fact, as explained in the subsection IV-C, the major feature of the layered schedule is its capability to double the convergence speed of related LDPC-CC decoders. Consequently, high throughput low latency decoders can be designed with relatively two times less processors than flooding based ones. Compared with the 0.175 Gbpsflooding based decoder in [20], a 3.66 Gbps throughput was obtained by the layered based decoder in [26] with only 2.5 less processors. Both decoders operate at close frequencies and ρ=1. Ultimately, we can note that important throughputs are achieved by layered based LDPC-CC decoders.
Importantly, for both serial and pipelined LDPC-CC decoders, these alternative strategies are implementable within the same architecture if few control logic is appended. Thereby, it is possible to design LDPC-CC decoders that can dynamically trade throughput for latency, area and memory consumption constraints. Essentially, the best choice in this tradeoff is application dependent and also code dependent.

VII. CONCLUSION
State-of-the-art schedules for decoding LDPC-CC decoders are investigated in this survey from an algorithmic and computational/memory complexity perspectives. On the architectural side, both ASIC and FPGA implementations were assessed. Interestingly, schedule and node level optimizations are considered as key factors to achieve Gpbs of throughput and reduce decoding latency as well as decoder area and memory occupation, at the same time.
In one hand, the throughput and the latency constraints are critical for 5G and future standards. In fact, to meet the 10 Gbps throughput that is recommended by the 5G standard, further algorithmic and implementation aspects should be investigated. In the other hand, works on software implementation of LDPC-CC decoders for SDR or Cloud-RAN systems should also be performed to reach efficient implementation like for LDPC-BC decoders.
