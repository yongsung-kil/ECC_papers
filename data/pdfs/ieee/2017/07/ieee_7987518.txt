A Multi-Gbps Fully Pipelined Layered Decoder for IEEE 802.11n/ac/ax LDPC Codes
Saleh Usman, Mohammad M. Mansour, Ali Chehab Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering American University of Beirut Beirut 1107 2020, Lebanon {sau00, mmansour, chehab}@aub.edu.lb

Abstract—This paper presents a fully pipelined layered decoder architecture for IEEE 802.11n/ac/ax LDPC codes, free of idle cycles. Several decoder architectures for such codes have emerged in the literature featuring throughputs in the multiGbps range. The proposed architecture surpasses the highest reported throughput for IEEE 802.11n/ac/ax LDPC codes. This is achieved 1) algorithmically, by implementing the layered LDPC decoding schedule, and 2) architecturally, by optimizing registerbased memories for IEEE 802.11n/ac LDPC codes and implementing an idle-cycle-free pipelined single-codeword datapath decoder. Register-based memories provide full bandwidth access to read and write all messages of a layer in one clock cycle. Single-codeword processing in the datapath significantly reduces memory overhead compared to other architectures that process multiple codewords to boost throughput at the expense of a larger footprint. The proposed architecture is synthesized in 40 nm CMOS process for IEEE 802.11n/ac, rate 1/2 LDPC codes. The decoder occupies an area of 0.38 mm2, runs at a frequency of 780 MHz, and achieves a throughput of 4.2 Gbps.
Index Terms—LDPC Codes, turbo-decoding of LDPC codes, layered decoding architectures, IEEE 802.11n/ac/ax LDPC codes
I. INTRODUCTION
LDPC codes have become the coding scheme of choice for high performance error correction codes in many IEEE standards like 802.11n/ac/ax (WiFi), 802.11ad (WiGig) [1], [2], owing to their near-optimal performance [3]. For LDPC decoding, mainly two types of schedules are employed. One is Gallager’s standard message-passing (SMP) technique, known as the flooding schedule [4], and which consists of two distinct phases. In one phase, all variable nodes are updated, and then in the second, all the check nodes are updated. This technique was originally proposed by Gallager [4], and later used by Mackay [5] in his rediscovery of LDPC codes.
In layered decoding [7], on the other hand, message computations carried out in one phase (say variable nodes) are immediately propagated to the other phase (connected check nodes in this case). This immediate information exchange between the two phases improves the convergence speed of layered decoders by roughly a factor of two compared to SMP decoders at high signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) [6]. Layered decoders are further divided into two types, known as row MP (RMP) decoders [7] and column MP (CMP) decoders [8], based on whether the row or column order of the parity-check matrix is followed in the decoding process. A recently pro-

posed layered decoder has combined the effect of both RMP and CMP decoders and thus has improved the convergence speed of the LDPC decoder by a factor of four compared to SMP decoders [9].
Existing architectures for high throughput LDPC decoders implement one of the three aforementioned schedules. SMP is preferred in several architectures despite its slower convergence speed to avoid the complexity of passing interdependent messages between the two types of nodes [10], [11], [12]. On the other hand, layered decoder implementations are logical option for high throughput applications like 802.11 ac/ax (WiFi), to cut the number of iterations required for decoding. To this end, several layered decoder architectures emerged which implement either the RMP or CMP schedules [14], [16]. There are two clear benefits of implementing RMP over CMP. Firstly, RMP allows memory efficient implementation of the decoder [13] and secondly it requires less number of cycles than CMP to complete one decoding iteration [18]. This is because of the property of SPCM which always has less number of rows than number of columns.
To achieve a throughput of LDPC decoders in the Gbps range, memories-based designs become inefficient because a large number of small memory banks is required for the targeted throughput [11]. Registers-based memories are more convenient to get full bandwidth access as well as to be used as pipeline registers for one pipeline stage. For IEEE 802.11n/ac LDPC codes, not all the memory locations are needed to be accessed by each read and write port, because of SPCM structure, so the reading and writing circuitry can also be optimized for the registers-based memory designs to be used in LDPC decoders.
Pipelining of layered decoders involves interdependent message passing between the two types of processing nodes of the decoder. The interdependency either introduces idle cycles or slows down the convergence speed. Idle cycles are introduced because of the wait time required to get updated messages from the previous layer, before starting the processing of a new layer [19]. Decoding more than one frame simultaneously is a solution to remove the idle cycles but this approach introduces memory overhead which is multiplied by the number of frames being processed simultaneously [20].

To fully remove the idle cycles, a tradeoff on convergence speed is necessary but the effect could be minimized by selecting a shorter pipeline depth. There are few fully pipelined idle-cycle-free RMP based decoder architectures for IEEE 802.11n/ac LDPC codes but their throughputs are in Mbps range, especially for small block lengths [18], [21]. Like in [21], a pipelined RMP decoder for IEEE 802.11n/ac LDPC codes achieves a throughput of 541 Mbps for the block length of 648. The highest reported throughput for IEEE 802.11n/ac, rate 1/2 LDPC codes, is 1.76 Gbps and is presented in [20]. However, this architecture processes multiple frames and therefore introduces a memory overhead which is proportional to the number of frames being processed.
This paper proposes fully pipelined, idle-cycle-free, RMP based decoder architecture for IEEE 802.11n/ac, rate 1/2 LDPC codes with throughput in the Gbps range. The architecture implements registers-based memories, optimized for IEEE 802.11n/ac, rate 1/2 LDPC codes to achieve targeted throughput while processing one frame at a time to avoid any memory overhead and thus getting smaller footprint. Reduction in the convergence speed of the proposed architecture is minimized by balancing it carefully with the pipeline depth. To this end, the impact of the pipeline depth on the convergence speed of IEEE 802.11n/ac LDPC code is assessed via simulations and presented in the paper. Based on this observation, an architecture with pipeline depth of 3 is developed for IEEE 802.11n/ac, rate 1/2 LDPC codes. The proposed decoder is synthesized using 40 nm CMOS process and achieves a throughput of 4.2 Gbps while occupying a chip area of 0.38 mm2. The performance of both parameters is several order better than the reported work with highest throughput in [20].
The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Background material related to SMP, CMP, and RMP schedules, IEEE 802.11n/ac LDPC codes, and check node computations is presented in Section II. Analysis of the effect of pipeline depth on convergence speed of IEEE 802.11n/ac LDPC codes is presented Section III. The proposed architecture is described in IV. Simulation and synthesis results are given in Section V. Finally, Section VI concludes the paper.
II. BACKGROUND
LDPC codes are either represented by a sparse parity-check matrix, or defined by a sparse bipartite Tanner graph consisting of variable nodes and check nodes. Variable nodes in the Tanner graph represent columns of the SPCM, and check nodes are associated with rows of the SPCM. Consider an LDPC code having N variable nodes and M check nodes in its associated Tanner graph with dm as degree of variable nodes and the degree of check nodes is dc. Let Pji denote the message computed at the jth variable node corresponding to the ith check node, and Lij be the message computed at ith check node for the jth variable node. Message computations based on the standard message passing scheduling algorithm is described next.

B. Column Message-Passing (CMP)
This scheduling starts from the first variable node and computes its message using equation (2). Then, instead of proceeding to the second variable node, all check nodes connected to this variable node are updated first using equation (1) without waiting for the next iteration. This process of evaluating each variable node and immediately updating connected check nodes is repeated for all variable nodes in one iteration. Decision making process in the final iteration is based on evaluating (3) as in SMP.

C. Row Message Passing (RMP)
In row message-passing, decoding proceeds row-wise instead of column-wise, i.e., check node messages are computed using (1), and then all of its connected variable nodes are updated immediately using equation (2). This process is repeated for all check nodes, in one iteration. Compared to CMP, this scheme has the advantage that it involves additional computations at variable nodes that are much less expensive than check node computations. Moreover, this scheme can also be implemented in a memory efficient way [15].

III. DECODING PERFORMANCE EVALUATION
In idle-cycle-free pipelined implementations of the layered LDPC decoder, the convergence speed of the decoder becomes slower because the availability of new check node values gets delayed at variable nodes. This delay is directly related to the pipeline depth of the decoder. To evaluate the loss in convergence speed as a function of the pipeline depth, floatingpoint pipelined implementations of IEEE 802.11n LDPC codes for block length 648 bits and rate 1/2 are simulated and compared against a non-pipelined (standard RMP) version. As shown in Figs. 2 and 3, the design is simulated for up to three pipeline stages. The figures show that, a pipeline depth of three is an adequate compromise between the convergence speed and pipeline depth of the proposed architecture.
Three other design parameters that impact the decoding performance of the architecture are also investigated: word length (WL) and fraction length (FL) of quantization bits, check node correction factor β, and maximum number of iterations (Imax). B. Reading and Preprocessing Block
The Reading and Preprocessing block involves an Address Generator, a Circular Shift Generator, a Subtractor module, and a Barrel Shifter module. The Address Generator, evidently, generates addresses for memory read operations based on input from the ROM block where the base matrix is saved. The Subtractor module is required for memory efficient implementation purposes since it removes the extrinsic information before passing values for further processing. The module incorporates z × dc saturated subtractors to complete the subtraction operation within one clock cycle. The Barrel Shifter and Circular Shift Generator modules implement the required circular shift operations after reading the numbers from the ROM block corresponding to each layer of the SPCM.
Going deeper into the design of Barrel Shifter module, it consists of 6 barrel shifters that work in parallel. Based on the maximum degree of check nodes, 8 barrel shifters would have been required but after considering the base matrix, 6 barrel shifters are incorporated because in each row there are two which implement a 0 shift. Each barrel shifter has z inputs, so it circularly shifts a complete sub-block in one shot. Six such

C. Postprocessing and Writing Block
This block reverses the operations of the Reading and Preprocessing block and consists of an Address Generator, a Circular Shift Generator, Subtractor and Adder modules, and a Barrel Shifter module. The Subtractor and Adder modules replace the old messages in LLR Mem with newly computed messages by subtracting the old message and adding in new messages. The Barrel Shifter module incorporates the same six barrel shifters as in the Reading and Preprocessing block and relies on the Circular Shift Generator to implement the reverse shifting operation. The Circular Shift Generator generates numbers according to the formula (z − f s), where f s is the forward shift given to the sub-block in the Reading and Preprocessing block, and z is the sub-block size.
D. Check Node Units (CNUs)
The Check Node Unit implements (4), which is equivalent to finding the first and second minimum among the check node inputs, the index of the first minimum value, and as well as the signs of the check node output values. The second minimum is then assigned to the index of the first minimum and all other indices are assigned the first minimum value. This process is done by the Outputs Magnitudes module in the block diagram of the CNU shown in Fig. 10. Since the check node has to process magnitudes and signs separately, a 2’s complement-to-signed-magnitude converter and its inverse are implemented after the input and before the output ports of the check node, respectively. Each CNU further consists of a Magnitude Comparator that finds first and second minimum, the index of the first minimum value, a Sign Determination unit and a β Corr. module. By using the outputs of the Outputs Magnitudes module, and the Sign Determination unit, each CNU completes implementation of (4) and produces outputs in one cycle. VI. CONCLUSIONS
A fully pipelined LDPC decoder architecture, free of idle cycles based on the RMP LDPC schedule has been presented. The decoder optimized register-based memories for IEEE 802.11n/ac, rate 1/2 LDPC codes to maintain Gbps throughput operation. The datapath of the decoder is pipelined and processes a single frame only, a simplification that achieves smaller footprint compared to existing multi-frame architectures. The impact of the pipeline depth on convergence speed of the decoder due to idle-cycle-free pipelining is analyzed through simulations. It is observed that an RMP decoder architecture with pipeline depth of 3 is an adequate compromise when RMP pipelined decoding is employed on a single frame. The proposed decoder achieves a throughput of 4.2 Gbps and this throughput surpasses the highest reported throughput for IEEE 802.11n/ac, rate 1/2 LDPC codes.
