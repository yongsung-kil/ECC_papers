Efficient ADMM Decoding of LDPC Codes Using Lookup Tables
Xiaopeng Jiao, Jianjun Mu, Yu-Cheng He, and Chao Chen

Abstract— Linear programming decoding with the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) is a promising decoding technique for low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, where the computational complexity of Euclidean projections onto check polytopes becomes a prominent problem. In this paper, the problem is circumvented by building lookup tables (LUTs) and quantizing the inputs to approach approximate Euclidean projections at low computational complexities. To challenge the huge memory cost of LUTs, we first propose two commutative compositions of Euclidean projection and self-map, and show the existence of a small quantization range which does not alter the Euclidean projection. Then, we investigate the design and simplification of the LUTs by exploiting the commutative compositions and check node decomposition techniques. An efficient algorithm for the LUT-based projection is demonstrated by using one simplification method. Simulation results show that for both the regular and irregular LDPC codes, the ADMM decoding using LUT-based projection can substantially reduce the decoding time while maintaining the error rate performance at a comparatively large memory cost.
Index Terms— Alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM), check polytope, Euclidean projection, look-up table, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
L INEAR programming (LP) decoding for error-correcting codes has been studied extensively in coding theory
since Feldman et al. published their seminal paper [1]. The maximum-likelihood (ML) decoding for binary linear codes
is relaxed to be an LP problem by converting the convex
hull of all codewords into a fundamental polytope [2]. For low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, the fundamental poly-
tope of LP decoding is established as the intersection of a
group of small polytopes, called check polytopes, which can easily be described by linear inequalities [1].

The decoding complexity is the main concern for practical applications of LP decoding. Recently, Barman et al. proposed an efficient LP decoding algorithm for LDPC codes based on alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) [8]. Roughly stated, ADMM presents a distributed framework of convex optimization problems by a decomposition-coordination procedure, in which a large-scale optimization problem is decomposed into small local subproblems, and a solution to the primal problem is found by coordinating the subproblems [13]. In virtue of the distributed nature, a multicore implementation of ADMM decoding is proposed in [14].
In the ADMM decoding for LDPC codes, Euclidean projections onto check polytopes constitute a key component which dominates the computational complexity of decoding [14]. For a check node of degree d, the check polytope is the convex hull of the binary vectors with length d and any even number of 1’s. In [8], a “two-slice” lemma is developed for describing the check polytopes, and thus an efficient Euclidean projection algorithm is proposed. Later, in [15], a more efficient Euclidean projection algorithm is presented based on the cut search algorithm of [11]. In [16], the Euclidean projection is further simplified based on an efficient simplex projection algorithm. By integrating the ideas in [15] and [16], a hardware compatible projection algorithm is proposed in [17]. In addition to the work on projection simplification, Wei et al. considered in [18] projection reduction technique to reduce the number of Euclidean projections in ADMM decoding. Then, Debbabi et al. investigated in [19] a layered scheduling for ADMM decoding and showed a significant decoding speedup by combining the layered scheduling with the projection reduction method of [18].
It has been shown that the ADMM decoding for LDPC codes performs faster than the ordinary LP decoding and is comparable to belief propagation (BP) decoding [15]. Unfortunately, it suffers from a significant error rate performance loss over the waterfall region compared with BP decoding [8]. To recover the loss, Liu et al. proposed an ADMM penalized decoding method by introducing a penalty term into the objective function in the LP decoding problem [20]–[22]. The penalty term makes the pseudo-codewords more costly than true codewords so that the ADMM penalized decoding can achieve comparable error rate performances to that of BP decoding. Efficient hardware implementation is often indispensable to practical applications of LDPC codes. In this paper, we investigate the implementation of Euclidean projections using look-up tables (LUTs) to challenge the computational complexity problem for the ADMM decoding of LDPC codes. The contributions of the work lie mainly in the following aspects:
1) To facilitate the simplification of LUTs, two commutative compositions of projection and self-map are developed on the analysis of Euclidean projections onto check polytopes.
2) The concept of approximate Euclidean projection based on LUTs is defined as a cascade of vector quantization and table lookup. We show that a) there exists a small quantization range that covers mostly the real-valued inputs and allows the LUT-based projection to approach exact Euclidean projection by using a few discrete values; and b) a symmetric quantization condition can satisfy the all-zero assumption for the LUT-based ADMM decoding.
3) To reduce memory requirements, we start the design of LUTs in a linear addressing manner. Then, we explore the simplification of LUTs in two directions: a) the number of table items is reduced exponentially by exploiting the two commutative compositions; and b) the number of components in table items is reduced by incorporating check node decompositions.
4) Since the LUTs become nonlinearly addressed due to the removal of table items, an efficient algorithm for table lookup in LUT-based projection is proposed.
5) In support of simulation results, we analyze the impacts of quantization parameters on the LUT-based ADMM penalized decoding for both regular and irregular LDPC codes.
6) By simulations, we show that the LUT-based ADMM decoding can be improved in time efficiency by incorporating the complexity reduction techniques in [18] and [19].

In Section IV, we investigate the design and simplification of LUTs. In Section V, we present the LUTbased projection algorithm together with the computational complexity analysis. In Section VI, we provide the simulation results. Finally, we conclude the paper by Section VII.

IV. LUT DESIGN FOR APPROXIMATE EUCLIDEAN PROJECTION

Then, we simplify the LUTs by exploiting the properties proved above to achieve an efficient implementation. We also discuss the potential of check node decomposition techniques in further reducing the memory requirements of LUTs.

C. Symmetry Condition for the All-Zero Assumption
The all-zero assumption for the ADMM penalized decoding was proved, i.e., the probability of decoding failure is independent of the codeword transmitted in a symmetric channel [22]. We need to show when the all-zero assumption holds under quantization of inputs to the projection.
As shown in Observation 1, the quantization range [a, b] is determined with a < 0 and b > 0. Consider a condition a + b = 1 such that the quantization range is centered at (a + b)/2 = 0.5. It is easy to derive qL−k+1 = 1 − qk for all k ∈ QI D, which we refer to as the odd symmetry with respect to the centroid 0.5 in the form of qL−k+1 − 0.5 = −(qk − 0.5).

The odd symmetry can be expressed as Q(1−v) = 1− Q(v) for v ∈ R, which states the symmetry of Q(v) around the centroid (0.5, 0.5) in the input-output square plane. If L is even, v = 0.5 locates the boundary between intervals L/2 and L/2 + 1, where Q(0.5) = 1 − Q(0.5). If L is odd, v = 0.5 points to the middle of interval (L + 1)/2 with Q(0.5) = 1 − Q(0.5) = 0.5.

E. Simplification of LUTs for High Dimensional Polytopes
For large d values, the simplified LUTs still require a huge amount of memory. To circumvent this problem, we can consider the use of check node decomposition techniques in [6], [32], and [33]. It was proved that the descriptive complexity of LP in [1] can be reduced by decomposing each high-degree check node into several check nodes with degree 3 or smaller. In [6], a cascaded LP (C-LP) was thus proposed. In [33], it was shown that the descriptive complexity of C-LP can further be reduced by decomposition with degrees larger than 3.
Without loss of generality, we use a degree-12 check node to illustrate three types of decomposition in Fig. 2. In the figure, the circles represent original variable nodes, the double circles represent auxiliary variable nodes, and the squares represent check nodes. By the degree-3 decomposition in Fig. 2(a), the degree-12 check node is decomposed into 10 check nodes of degree 3 by adding 9 auxiliary variable nodes. This implies a way to approach the projection onto PP12 by performing 10 times the projection onto PP3 in three layers. Similarly, the degree-6 decomposition in Fig. 2(b) shows the way of using twice the projection onto PP6 plus one projection onto PP4. Instead, the degree-7 decomposition in Fig. 2(c) gives a way of twice the projection onto PP7 plus one projection onto PP2.
The decomposition of a high-degree check node essentially converts a high dimensional projection into a serial of layered lower dimensional projections. By choosing proper decompositions for LDPC codes with high-degree check nodes, we can implement the ADMM decoding with LUTs designed for only a few small d values.

As proved theoretically and supported by simulation results, it is promising to choose a small quantization range so as to reduce the memory cost of LUTs while maintaining the FER performance of the ADMM decoding of LDPC codes. The efficiency advantage of the LUT-based ADMM decoding may be viewed as a tradeoff against the memory cost. For LDPC codes with high-degree check nodes, the huge memory cost of the LUT-based ADMM decoding can be reduced by incorporating check node decomposition techniques in the way of regulating the dimensions of polytopes. Simulation results have shown that the use of check node decomposition techniques yields various performance losses in order to reduce the memory cost of the LUT-based decoding. Furthermore, simulation results have shown that the LUT-based decoding of LDPC codes can also be improved to different extents by combining with other complexity reduction techniques, including early termination [8], projection reduction [18], and layered scheduling [19].
Finally, the work on the LUT-based ADMM decoding may be extended to: a) global fixed-point implementation, b) check node decomposition combined with parallel implementation, and c) low-complexity LUT-based projection algorithm based on the simplification of LUTs by both Propositions 1 and 2. The results on these directions may make the LUT-based ADMM decoding technique more competitive in high speed communications at low to moderate SNR values.
