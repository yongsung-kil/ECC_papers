A Link between Consensus Building and Low Density Parity Check Decoding

Kamesh Namuduri and Murali Varanasi Department of Electrical Engineering University of North Texas Denton, Texas

Abstract—In this paper, we highlight a connection between consensus building and Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) decoding. This connection serves as the foundation for estimating the convergence time for LDPC decoding and for designing good LDPC codes. Preliminary results are presented to demonstrate the significance of this key relationship in estimating the convergence time for the decoder, and in identifying short cycles that may slow down the process of convergence.
I. INTRODUCTION
Convergence characteristics of Low-Density Parity Check (LDPC) decoding have been investigated for more than three decades in the literature. In this paper, we provide a new perspective to estimate the convergence time. The key to this new perspective is the discovery of a key relationship between consensus building and LDPC decoding. This relationship serves as a foundation for designing good LDPC codes as well.
In our previous research, we used LDPC-like belief propagation to develop a strategy for consensus building in wireless sensor networks [25]. The results demonstrated that an LDPC-like bipartite structure achieves faster convergence of consensus building algorithms in wireless sensor networks or decentralized systems, in general. In the current research, we turn to the opposite direction and make use of the fundamental concepts of consensus building to estimate the convergence time in LDPC decoding. The discovery of this important two-way link between consensus building and LDPC decoding is expected to lead to promising results in both consensus building as well as LDPC decoding.
In order to analyze the convergence behavior of LPDC decoding, one needs to draw the bipartite graph in LDPC decoding as two unipartite graphs, each from the perspective of one of the partitions while keeping the connections among the nodes intact. The graph drawn from the perspective of the top layer shows short cycles that may be present in the bipartite graph as multiple links between nodes. The spectral properties of the Laplacian matrix corresponding to the graph drawn from the perspective of the bottom layer inform us the

convergence properties of the LDPC decoder. Preliminary results on a typical small graphs are presented to demonstrate the validity of the the results.
A. Belief Propagation
Belief propagation refers to a class of iterative algorithms originally used for inference in graphical models, i.e. for computing marginal and conditional distributions of random variables whose independence structures are specified by a factor graph. The foundations of BP lie in seminal work by Gallager in channel coding in the 1960s [7], and in Pearl’s developments in artificial intelligence in the 1980s. Since then, BP and its variants have been used in many areas of communication and signal processing. In recent years, there has been resurgent interest in BP in signal processing (e.g., for source localization [8], distributed pose estimation [28], distributed compression [24], parameter estimation [9], [18], [23], [26], [30]) and communications (e.g., iterative decoding [19]). Recently, the message-passingbased architecture that underlies BP has also been adapted for various distributed computations [5], [31].
B. Gossiping
In belief propagation, each node shares its information with all of its neighbors. Gossiping [2], [3], [22] is a variation of belief-propagation in which a node shares information with only one of its neighbors chosen at random at a given instance. Thus, gossiping follows a one-to-one (peer-to-peer) communication model and hence is slower compared to belief propagation which follows a one-to-many (broadcast) communication model.

Consensus algorithms, in particular, appear promising for decisionmaking in modern networks because they can permit nodes to reach a common opinion in a fully distributed way, for several network topologies. A primary focus of our proposed research is to overcome some of these challenges. To this end, we propose development of consensus-building algorithms that exploit the message-passing paradigm of BP.
D. LDPC Codes
LDPC codes were originally invented by Gallager [6] in 1960, bud did not receive enough attention until 1996 when Turbo codes, which used the same concept of iterative decoding, were invented. The excellent error correction capabilities of these codes as well as their performance approaching Shannon‘s limit have resulted in renewed interest among researchers in designing codes suitable for many applications. LDPC codes were reinvented by Mackay [12], and Luby [10], and since that time have been used in many modern applications such as 10GBase-T Ethernet, WiFi, WiMAX, and Digital Video Broadcasting (DVB). The performance of LPDC codes is determined by the throughput and complexity of decoding which in turn depend on code length, information rate, complexity of computation at the nodes, the parallelism, and number of iterations needed.
LDPC codes are characterized by a (n − k) × n sparse matrix in which n − k redundant digits are added to k information digits to form a codeword of length n digits. LDPC codes are best described by a bipartite graph called a Tanner graph that contain variable nodes (corresponding to a coded digit), and check nodes corresponding to the parity check equations.
Encoding and decoding of LDPC codes has attracted considerable attention among the research community. Richardson and Urbanke [21] have proposed a rather efficient encoding method that works well even for large matrices. Decoding of the LDPC codes is performed by message passing algorithms, among which the belief propagation algorithm is an important subclass. This algorithm is present in Gallager‘s work [6]. In this method, the messages passed along the edges of the Tanner graph are probabilities or beliefs. The messages

Similarly, the message passed from c to v is the probability that v has a certain value given all the messages passed to c previously from message nodes other than v. Each iteration of the algorithm has two steps: Check nodes processing the incoming information and passing new messages to the symbol nodes, and symbol nodes processing the incoming messages sent by the check nodes and sending updated information about their value and the associated reliability to the check nodes. At each iteration, a hard decision on the value of each bit is performed at the symbol nodes. The algorithm is terminated when all the parity check equations are satisfied or an upper bound for the number of iterations is reached.
II. LINK BETWEEN CONSENSUS BUILDING AND LDPC DECODING
We highlight a strong relationship between consensus-building and Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) decoding. This connection will be exploited to investigate, analyze and design good LDPC codes, estimate bounds for convergence in LDPC decoding, and to expedite the decoding process.
To begin with, let us observe the common ground between the consensus-building and LDPC decoding, i.e., convergence. Convergence in LDPC decoding is similar to convergence in consensus-building. In LDPC decoding, the check nodes help correct the errors in variable nodes. Convergence implies that all the check nodes satisfy the parity check equations, i.e., consensus is achieved among the check nodes. In consensusbuilding, consensus among the sensor nodes is achieved by exchanging information among themselves. Convergence implies that all the sensor nodes achieved consensus.

III. CONVERGENCE CHARACTERISTICS OF LDPC
CODES
Convergence time for LDPC coding has been investigated over the past three decades. Here, we provide a new perspective for estimating this convergence time using the new found link between consensus building and LDPC decoding discussed above. Note that convergence is achieved when all the check nodes satisfy the parity check conditions, i.e., reach a consensus value.
Literature suggests that cycles in Tanner graph may slow down the convergence process of LDPC decoding. The rationale behind this observation is that in a loop the value of an incorrect bit will propagate back to itself, effectively reinforcing itself. Thus, cycles have the effect of resisting the error correction. With longer loops this effect is diluted, and hence their impact on error correction performance is not critical [13]. However, it has also been suggested that not all short loops are harmful [27]. Some short cycles with good graph connectivity may be allowed, whereas, longer cycles with poor graph connectivity may need to be discarded.
Short loops appear as multiple links in C-graph as shown in Fig. 1. There are two duplicate links, one between c1 and c4 and the other between c2 and c3. Removing loops is equivalent to removing duplicate links from the C-graph. This needs to be reflected back in the Tanner graph as well. While removing loops is optional, this process may improve the convergence behavior of the LDPC decoder.
