Abstract— Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes are a class of forward error correction (FEC) linear block codes which provide near-capacity performance for power-efficient communications. Optimum decoding requires accurate combining ratio estimation to scale the input signal in an additive white Gaussian noise channel (AWGN). Test data and analysis show that the performance of the optimal LDPC decoding algorithm is severely degraded when encountering pulsed radio frequency interference (RFI) from sources such as ground based radars. This research effort first reveals that the LDPC performance degradation under pulsed RFI is not due to the burst of errors but to the inaccurate combining ratio estimation. Although an ideal combining ratio estimator (symbol-wise) could mitigate the degradation caused by pulsed RFI, it is not practical for implementation. Some near optimal LDPC decoding algorithms which do not require combining ratio estimation are investigated. One near optimum decoding algorithm "min-scale" is proposed in the robust decoder design to mitigate pulsed RFI. Analysis shows that "min-scale" only loses about 0.2 dB performance compared to the optimal decoding algorithm under nominal condition; in a pulsed RFI environment, the designed performance can be achieved if the system design has adequate margin. Without combining ratio estimation, the decoder design complexity is reduced and the risk of performance degradation due to combining ratio estimation error is eliminated. This robust decoder design enables reliable communications to space vehicles which may encounter strong pulsed RFI signals from high power radar systems.

1. INTRODUCTION
Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes are a class of forward error correction (FEC) linear block codes which provide near-capacity performance on a variety of communication and storage channels.

Due to some advantages of LDPC codes over Turbo codes such as higher code rates, lower error-rate floor and a parallelizable decoder, the Consultative Committee for Space Data Systems (CCSDS) adopted two sets of LDPC codes in the CCSDS standards for space communication applications [1]. One set called the AR4JA (Accumulate, Repeat-by-4, and Jagged Accumulate) family is optimized for very low Signal to Noise Ratio (SNR) for Deep Space applications and the other set known as C2, is a quasi-cyclic high-rate LDPC code for Near Earth applications. The National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA)’s next exploration spacecraft Orion chose the LDPC AR4JA (2048, 1024) code from the AR4JA family as the FEC code in its C&T system design. The ground systems of the Space Communication and Navigation (SCaN) are also upgraded to use this code to provide services to Orion.
The LDPC AR4JA (2048, 1024) code is a powerful FEC code which can achieve the codeword error rate (CWER) 10-7 at Es/No -0.8 dB in a nominal additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel. However, test results showed that when it encountered the pulsed radio frequency interference (RFI) specified in Orion S-band transponder requirements, the decoding performance is severely degraded when conventional pre-scaling techniques are implemented. This problem is of considerable interest in the context of providing reliable communications to the space vehicle which might suffer severe degradation due to pulsed RFI sources such as ground based radars [2-4]. An analysis in this research effort leads to a finding that the root cause of this severe performance degradation is inaccurate combining ratio estimation (CRE) required for optimal LDPC decoding. Thus, some near optimal LDPC decoding algorithms which don’t require CRE are explored to design a robust decoder to mitigate the pulsed RFI.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. In Section II, the test results of an LDPC decoder under pulsed RFI conditions are briefly discussed. The simulation approach of pulsed RFI and results are discussed in Section III. An ideal combining ratio estimator is evaluated to reveal the root cause of performance degradation in Section IV. In section V, near optimal decoding algorithms without CRE are evaluated to demonstrate certain robustness under pulsed RFI. Some concluding remarks are presented in Section VI.

The pulse train function g(t) is assumed to be a periodic gating function with pulse repetition rate 1/T and duty cycle τ/T where T is pulse period and τ is pulse width.
In order to find out the overlap pattern of pulsed RFI on the desired signal, we take an approach to define the data frame and interfering pulse in terms of symbols. For example, with 1 Msps symbol rate, the pulse width of 10 µs is equal to 10 symbols and the pulse period of 300 µs is equal to 300 symbols. The data frame is defined as a 2112-symbol frame which is a 64-symbol attached synchronization marker (ASM) plus a 2048-symbol LDPC codeword. No matter whether data frames and pulses are synchronized or asynchronous, the overlap pattern will repeat at the period of the Least Common Multiple (LCM) of data frame and pulse period.

4. IDEAL COMBINING RATIO ESTIMATOR

Decoders for the modern LDPC codes are based on probabilistic inference. The optimal LDPC decoding algorithm is an iterative belief-propagation (BP) algorithm based on probabilistic inference. The input to the decoder is not just the received code symbols; instead, the log likelihood ratio (LLR) of the probability information is requested as decoder input. study also shows that error in ‘CR’ estimation results in degradation of decoding performance. It degrades rapidly with an underestimate, and more slowly with an overestimate. An underestimate of 2 dB can severely degrade the decoding performance. Two CREs are modeled and evaluated in an analysis work in [12], one is a pilot guided estimator that uses several 64-bit ASMs while the other is a blind estimator that uses an entire codeword of 2048 symbols. Both CREs can achieve estimation accuracy within 0.5 dB of the true CR value in an AWGN channel. It is worthy of note that the estimated CR is used to scale all symbols inside one codeword assuming all symbols have same average level of SNR. With exposure to pulsed RFI, the estimated CR from data is lower than the CR of unaffected symbols but higher than the CR of affected symbols. Therefore, inside one codeword, the estimated CR is an overestimate for affected symbols and (more significantly) an underestimate for unaffected symbols. This inaccurate CR estimation results in the distorted LLR as the input to the decoder, thus severely degrading the decoding performance.
Let us assume an ideal CRE exists, which responds immediately to the pulsed RFI and scales each symbol appropriately for its true CR. This symbol-wise CRE is modeled and evaluated with same pulsed RFI characteristics in Section II except the pulse power level. The pulse power is set 100 dB stronger than the signal power in order to represent an overpowering ratio. The simulation results in Figure 6 show that with this ideal symbol-wise CRE, the decoding performance under pulsed RFI is improved significantly. There is only a loss of about 0.4 dB at CWER level of 10-6.

The near optimal decoding algorithm “min-offset” achieves the desired performance under pulsed RFI by setting the offset to a constant. Since this constant is an offset to an absolute value of LLR, it is dependent on the signal amplitude A, which is assumed to be one in the simulation. For a practical system, the received signal amplitude (A + noise) is regulated by the automatic gain control (AGC) component. At the output of AGC, the signal amplitude is varying with noise so that it is very difficult to keep it constant.

The near optimal LDPC decoding algorithm “min-scale” does not require CR estimation too. The choice of constant scaler is independent of signal amplitude, therefore it can be implemented in a practical system.

The simulation results in Figure 9 show that this decoding algorithm demonstrates certain robustness under pulsed RFI compared to the optimal algorithm “min*” which requires CR estimation. It is estimated that the desired performance CWER=10-7 can be regained at about Es/No 4.8 dB using this near optimal decoding algorithm “min-scale” under the pulsed RFI interference.
Without CR estimation, the decoder design complexity is reduced and the risk of performance degradation due to CR estimation error is eliminated. Only about 0.2 dB of performance is lost in the nominal AWGN condition compared to the optimal algorithm “min*”; while exposed to pulsed RFI, unlike the “min*” decoder which loses performance even with increased SNR, the required performance can be obtained by “min-scale” decoder if SNR margin allows.

6. CONCLUSION
Test data and analysis showed that the performance of the optimal LDPC decoding algorithm used in Orion C&T system baseband processor is severely degraded when

encountering pulsed RFI interference. This research effort reveals that the LDPC performance degradation under pulsed RFI is not due to the burst of the errors but to the inaccurate combining ratio estimation. Although an ideal combining ratio estimator (symbol-wise) could mitigate the degradation caused by pulsed RFI, it is not practical for implementation. Some near optimal LDPC decoding algorithms which do not require combining ratio estimation were investigated. One near optimum decoding algorithm "min-scale" is proposed in the robust decoder design to mitigate pulsed RFI. It shows that "min-scale" only loses about 0.2 dB performance compared to the optimal decoding algorithm under nominal AWGN condition. In a pulsed RFI environment, the required performance can still be achieved if the system design has adequate margin. By eliminating combining ratio estimation, the decoder design complexity is reduced and the risk of performance degradation due to combining ratio estimation error is also eliminated.
Even though the study focuses on the LDPC AR4JA (2048, 1024) code chosen by the NASA Orion project, the general principle of using a robust near optimal decoder without CRE to mitigate the pulsed RFI can be applied to other LDPC codes. Future work includes modifying the LDPC decoder FPGA core with “min-scale” algorithm and testing it with pulsed RFI to verify the robustness of the decoder.
