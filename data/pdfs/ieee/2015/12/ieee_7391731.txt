Trellis based Node Operations for LDPC Decoders from the Information Bottleneck Method
Jan Lewandowsky, Gerhard Bauch Hamburg University of Technology Institute of Communications 21073 Hamburg, Germany
Email: {jan.lewandowsky,bauch}@tuhh.de

Abstract—We utilize the Information Bottleneck method in a discrete density evolution scheme that was introduced by Brian M. Kurkoski et al. in order to find message mappings for the node operations of discrete LDPC decoders. The resulting decoders have performance close to belief propagation decoding but require much lower implementation efforts because they only process unsigned integers instead of log-likelihood-ratios. An efficient trellis implementation of the resulting discrete node operations is presented. In the trellis implementation the passed messages are calculated using recursive formulas that are fully characterized by two static vectors of integers. Bit error rate simulations for exemplary (3, 6)-regular LDPC codes with a 4 bit message representation prove the performance of the proposed decoders that even outperform Min-Sum decoders with quasi continuous message representation.
I. INTRODUCTION
Binary Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) codes offer very good error correction performance what made them very popular in the communications community. There exist very sophisticated code optimization techniques [1] as well as decoding algorithms based on message passing [2] between the distinct decoding components of an LDPC decoder: the variable and the check nodes. Parity check equations of an LDPC code are commonly visualized in a bipartite Tanner graph. Iterative belief propagation is a widely known decoding algorithm for LDPC codes that works on such a graph. In belief propagation decoding messages are exchanged along the edges of the Tanner graph and these messages are loglikelihood-ratios (LLRs). LLRs require a quasi continuous message representation to yield the best decoding result. Furthermore the nodes which calculate the messages propagated into the graph need to evaluate functions also using quasi continuous arithmetics. In contrast the hardware complexity that determines the required chip area and the energy consumption of the decoder is limited and often forms a bottleneck. This is why the task of finding suboptimal decoding algorithms that require low implementation efforts but perform close to belief propagation has been adressed in the past [2], [3] and still is a hot topic [4], [5]. A popular method to investigate the ensemble performance of LDPC codes under message passing decoding is density evolution. In density evolution the messages iteratively exchanged between the nodes are interpreted as random variables and their probability densities are tracked under the assumption of a cycle free graph. In

[6] a density evolution technique that adresses decoders that just use messages from a discrete alphabet (i.e. a limited set of integers) is presented. This technique is used to construct message mappings that map the inputs of a node operation to an output message. The idea is trying to pick the node output message which maximizes the mutual information shared between the node output message and the correct bit it represents. Unfortunately [6] does not include investigations of the performance of the introduced message mapping decoding strategy for finite length codes and does not include an efficient implementation strategy of the resulting node operations. The Information Bottleneck method is a generic framework which yields a compression of a random variable Y in form of a compressed representation T of Y . The random variable T on the one hand is as compact as possible, but on the other hand shares the maximum possible relevant information I(T, X) with another random variable of interest X [7]. Available Information Bottleneck algorithms yield generally stochastical, locally optimal mappings from each element of the event space of Y to the elements of the event space of variable T , that are optimal in the sense that they maximize the mutual information I(T, X) under the constraint of a desired small I(T, Y ) [7]. So far, the method has only rarely been utilized in communications. Some important works are [8], [9], [10] and [11]. The existing works mostly focus on network coding schemes that are designed using the Information Bottleneck method. In this paper we show that the Information Bottleneck method also offers great opportunities for LDPC decoding. It already has been observed in very unrelated work [12] that applying the Information Bottleneck method to random vectors can find approximations of functions of several inputs which are optimal in the sense that they minimize the loss of mutual information that is caused by the approximation. This motivates us to pick up the density evolution strategy from [6] but in contrast to [6] utilize the Information Bottleneck framework to design the node operations. We show that the message mappings for variable and check nodes that result from an application of the Information Bottleneck algorithm can be implemented very efficiently in a trellis structure. Additionally two new Information Bottleneck algorithms tailored to the problems of finding optimum quantizers for the output of binary input discrete memoryless channels and the construction of discrete

II. TRANSMISSION SYSTEM

The transmitter uses a rate Rc (dv, dc)-regular LDPC code with a sparse binary Nc × Nv parity check matrix H that has column weight dv and row weight dc. For all binary codewords x = [x[0], x[1], . . . , x[Nv − 1]]T the product H · x = [0, 0, . . . , 0]T. The codeword x is transmitted over a symmetric binary input discrete memoryless channel. Its output is modelled as a discrete random variable Y with realization y and event space Y. The transition probabilities are consolidated in the conditional probability density p(y|x), where the time indices are left out, because the channels transition probabilites are modelled to be time invariant. The discrete memoryless channel with a huge number |Y| of possible output levels is concatenated with a quantizer that consolidates several output levels in clusters and maps them to a certain representant t. As a consequence the quantizer output is also modeled as a realization t of a random variable T with event space T . The algorithm to design the quantizer that induces a minimal loss of mutual information between the channel input and the quantizer output is presented in the next section. The concatenation of the channel and the quantizer is characterized by the conditional probability density p(t|x). In information theoretical considerations the actual event space of the variables is irrelevant. For simplicity we define all occuring event spaces to be given by sets of contiguous integers and assign a fixed letter which represents an arbitrary element from these sets:

III. QUANTIZERS FROM THE INFORMATION BOTTLENECK METHOD
This section starts with a brief recapitulation of the Information Bottleneck method that is visualized in Figure 1. For a deeper analysis of the method we recommend the first chapters of [13]. Afterwards a dedicated Information Bottleneck algorithm for quantizer design of binary input discrete memoryless channels is presented. The resulting quantizers form the receivers interface to the channel in the rest of the paper and as a consequence shall be designed to minimize the loss of relevant information that results from quantization. The quantizers output unsigned integers instead of real valued representants such as LLRs. The integers from the quantizer are used as inputs for the discrete LDPC decoders that are described in the remainder of the paper. As a consequence neither quasi continuous number representation nor the respective arithmetical units are required for decoding. This strategy yields a receiver that has very low complexity.

C. Modified sequential Information Bottleneck algorithm
In Algorithm 2 we propose a modified version of the sequential Information Bottleneck algorithm. The first important distinction to the classical sequential Information Bottleneck algorithm is, that the algorithm does start with a random initial clustering, but all clusters are forced to consist just of contiguous members y ∈ Y. Such a clustering can easily be constructed by seperating a vector of contiguous integers from 0 to |Y| − 1 into different subvectors. V. PERFORMANCE AND DISCUSSION
Figure 10 shows the bit error rate of our decoder for a maximum number of imax = 50 decoder iterations for two random (3, 6)-regular LDPC codes of different lengths Nv = 104 and Nv = 105. We used a message alphabet cardinality of |T | = 16, because consistent with the theoretical results in [6], we found out that a 4 bit message representation is sufficient to perform very close to belief propagation decoding also for finite length codes. The bit error rate of exact belief propagation decoding for a quasi continuous channel output and a quantized channel output are shown as references. For these reference simulations the LLRs used for belief propagation decoding were calculated from the distribution

p(x|y) for the quasi continuous channel and from p(x|t) for the quantized channel. In this example we first uniformly split the channel output interval [−3, +3] into 2048 partitions that then were quantized to 16 clusters in order to approximate a 4 bit A/D-conversion of the output signal of a continuous AWGN channel. The reference curves reveal an interesting perspective on the small but inevitable loss of mutual information that follows from the proposed quantizers and has already been shown in the bottom part of Figure 4. The actual influence of optimum quantization on the bit error rate of the transmission scheme can be observed. The reference curves show that the loss of using 16 quantizer levels instead of quasi continuous received samples is just about 0.2 dB over Eb/N0. This is a very strong argument for consideration of minimum loss of mutual information quantization. Interpreting both plots in Figure 10 from left to right then reveals a loss of our decoder of less than 0.4 dB over Eb/N0 in comparison to the exact belief propagation decoder that uses the LLRs from the quantizer in the waterfall regions of the codes. The proposed decoder does not represent any values by LLRs or fractional numbers, but instead by unsigned integers. If the introduced 4 bit quantizer that simply outputs unsigned integers is used at the output of a continuous AWGN channel, the marked loss of less than 0.4 dB over Eb/N0 that is observed for both codes in comparison to the belief propagation decoder is the low price for resigning all higher precision units, signed number representation and all nonlinear functions in the receiver. Instead a decoder that calculates and passes messages also in a 4 bit integer architecture in the proposed static trellis diagrams can be used. Furthermore the proposed decoder just needs to implement the +-operation over integers, which requires much lower implementation efforts than the -operation that is needed for belief propagation decoding. Finally Figure 10 compares our proposed fully discrete and integer based decoder to the well known Min-Sum decoder that is applied for the quantized channel. Our decoder outperforms the MinSum decoder by about 0.3 dB in the waterfall region despite the Min-Sum decoder receives and furtherly processes exact LLRs from the quantizer and requires higher precision units and relational operators to determine the check node outputs.
VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper we used the Information Bottleneck method in a discrete density evolution scheme to construct message mappings for the node operations of discrete LDPC decoders. The major advantage of the resulting decoders in comparison to state of the art message passing decoders is that they only process unsigned integers instead of any signed fractional values and do not need to evaluate any nonlinear functions to calculcate the messages that are propagated into the graph during decoding. As a consequence in practice the presented decoders could improve the adaptation of the decoding to hardware constraints. We presented a method to implement the message mappings that result from discrete density evolution in a trellis structure which degenerates the node operations to the evaluation of simple, recursive, integer valued functions

that are characterized by two static vectors of integers. Despite
their low complexity the proposed decoders perform close to
belief propagation decoders applied to the quantized output of
a symmetric AWGN channel.
