On the LLR Metrics for DPSK Modulations Over Two-Symbol Observation Intervals for the Flat Rician Fading Channel
Haifeng Yuan and Pooi-Yuen Kam, Fellow, IEEE

Abstract—We consider the differential phase-shift keying modulation over the flat Rician fading channel with perfect channel model information (PCMI), and derive the two-symbolobservation-interval log-likelihood ratio (LLR) metric from first principles. The work generalizes the previous LLR derivations in [16]–[19], and demonstrates the necessity of the knowledge of the statistical channel model in the exact LLR computation. Assuming the channel is slowly time-varying, we also propose a simple approximate LLR metric based on the generalized likelihood ratio test (GLRT), which requires only the information of the noise spectral density. The advantage of the newly derived PCMI-LLR metric over the approximate metric and the other LLR metrics in the literature is explained from the information-theoretic perspective via the generalized mutual information. Computer simulations are also provided to demonstrate the superiority of the PCMI-LLR metric in both error rate performance and convergence speed for iterative decoding of turbo and low-density parity-check codes in practical systems. This paper aims to present the fundamental principles of LLR computation, emphasizing the importance of selecting the appropriate metric for iterative decoding over fading channels.
Index Terms—Iterative decoding, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, log-likelihood ratio, Rician fading channel, statistical channel model, turbo codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
T O PERFORM coherent detection over wireless links, the channel state information (CSI) has to be acquired at the receiver. The conventional CSI acquisition via pilotsymbol-assisted modulation [1] usually requires a large pilot overhead, which will significantly reduce the power- and bandwidth-efficiency. Decision-directed/decision-aided estimation schemes can utilize the energy of received data signals and effectively reduce the pilot overhead, at the expense of increased receiver complexity [2]. The differential modulation scheme offers a simple alternative to detect the transmitted information without requiring explicit CSI acquisition, especially when the channel varies with time. As a robust demodulation method, the two-symbol-observation-interval

(TSOI) differential detection is most commonly used [3]–[20]. The main advantage of the TSOI differential detection scheme is that the code bit/symbol information can be conveniently retrieved via simple computation using only two consecutively received signals. Although differential detection schemes using multiple-symbol observation intervals [21]–[24] can improve the error performance, they yield much higher receiver complexity. In this paper, we will only focus on the TSOI differential detection scheme.
The conventional TSOI hard-decision based differential detection is simple to implement, but it does not fully utilize the information of the received signals and results in significant performance degradation [5]–[7]. In comparison, the soft information, which is the sufficient statistics for the hard decision and also accounts for the reliability of each received data symbol based on the channel output, is commonly preferred nowadays [8]–[20]. Conventionally, the soft information is defined in terms of the a posteriori probability (APP). For robustness and computational efficiency, the log-likelihood ratio (LLR) has been used more frequently in the literature as the soft information metric [25]. References [8]–[10] considered some crude forms of LLR, but the approaches adopted are ad hoc, which lack theoretical justification. In [11]–[13], the LLR values are approximated based on some numerical integration methods, which is cumbersome to use in practice. Reference [14] provided the fundamental form of the LLR, but no explicit LLR metric was derived. Several explicit forms of the TSOI-based LLR metric under the differential phase-shift keying (DPSK) modulation have been proposed/derived in [15]–[20]. However, it is not conceptually clear how the exact LLR should be computed from first principles and what the necessary conditions are that enable this computation at the receiver. Moreover, the LLR metrics in [15]–[20] are difficult to compare because they are obtained using different approaches under different channel models with different assumptions. Without a unified study, it would be difficult for designers to select the most appropriate metric in applications.
This paper presents the fundamental study on the LLR metric for the TSOI-based differential modulation and detection scheme, and provides a unifying view on the LLR computation. As an illustration, we consider M-ary DPSK modulation over the time-correlated, frequency-flat, Rician fading channel model with perfect channel model information (PCMI) and demonstrate the correct theoretical approach of deriving the PCMI-LLR metric. The derivation explains why the complete statistical information of the channel is required for exact LLR

If the channel model is unknown or the statistical information is not fully available, it is not possible to compute the LLR values from the PCMI-LLR metric. Based on some insights from the PCMI-LLR metric and the generalized likelihood ratio test (GLRT) [26], [27], we propose a simple GLRT-LLR metric, which requires only the knowledge of the noise spectral density, and this metric can be applied to any fading channel that is slowly time-varying. The LLR-value mismatches and the amount of information loss for some approximate LLR metrics are also demonstrated. The rest of the paper is organized as follows. The system model is presented in section II. In section III, we first explain how the bit LLR should be computed from the symbol LLR and the bit-to-symbol constellation mapping. In section IV, the PCMI-LLR metric is compared with the existing LLR metric in the literature, and we show in detail how this metric generalizes the previous work in [16]– [19]. Asymptotic analysis and simulation studies of the LLR metrics are given in section V and section VI, respectively. The paper is concluded in section VII.

Several remarks related to the PCMI-LLR metric should be

made here:

1. The derivation above shows that the conditional PDF

p(rk, rk−1| φk) does not depend on a particular real-

ization of sk−1 (or sk). In other words, no additional information of the LLR metric can be obtained from the

prior knowledge of sk−1 alone.

2. The exact computation of the TSOI LLR requires the con-

ditional PDF p(rk, rk−1| φk), which can only be com-

puted if the statistical information of the channel gains

hk and hk−1 is available. (Note that under the assumption

of perfect CSI, hk and hk−1 have statistical distributions which are point mass functions.) This conclusion is not

just limited to the case presented here. In fact, from the

definition, it is clear that the LLR is a statistical quantity

which can only be obtained if the statistical distribution of

the received signal conditioned on the transmitted signal

can be exactly determined. Otherwise, only approximate

LLR metrics can be obtained via some ad hoc approaches,

e.g., the JE-TSOI-LLR in [16], the Gauss-LLR in [20], as

well as the GLRT-LLR which will be discussed in the next

section.

3. For the simplicity of the LLR computation, we only

consider two-symbol observation intervals. However,

our derivation foreshadows how the multiple-symbol-

observation-interval (MSOI) LLR metric should be com-

puted. Note that the symbol information φk is carried

only in rk and rk−1, while the remaining received signals are related to φk through the channel gains hk and hk−1. Thus, the MSOI-LLR metric can be computed using a

similar approach as described above, by replacing the a

priori channel statistics of hk and hk−1 with the a posteriori PDF p(hk , hk−1|r0, . . . , rk−2, rk+1, . . . , rL N/κ ).

However, this PDF is usually difficult to compute, and the

resultant LLR metric would be rather complicated, which

defeats the intended purpose of choosing the differential

modulation scheme for receiver complexity reduction.

assumed that each message bit is independently chosen

A. Study of LLR Metrics Through the GMI
For a typical BICM system as in Fig. 1, the GMI can provide an effective measure of the mutual information between the transmitted bit sequence and the received symbol sequence under a specific LLR metric. Assume that the code bits {ck,i }k,i are independent, with each bit ck,i having the uniform distribution over {0, 1}. Based on [29, Eqn. 18], the GMI for the LLR metric λk,i under the mismatch factor s can be expressed as
Iλk,i (s) = 1 − E sλk,i log(1 + exp(−sgn(ck,i )) , (52)
where the function sgn(·) is given by sgn(0) = 1 and sgn(1) = 0, and mismatch factor s measures the amount of mismatch or imperfection introduced by the particular choice of the bit LLR metric λk,i . Similar to the concept of mutual information [38], the GMI defines the supremum of all the achievable rates for the BICM system with the demodulation LLR metric λk,i [33]. When the exact channel LLR metric is used, the maximum of (52) always occurs at s = 1, and in this case the GMI agrees with the conventional definition of mutual information [38] between the code bit ck,i and the two-symbol observations rk and rk−1 under the differential modulation, , which is given in equation (53). The quantity I (ck,i ; rk, rk−1) has been shown in [38] to be the maximal amount of information on the symbol ck,i that can be retrieved from the received signals rk and rk−1. On the other hand, when an approximate LLR metric is used for demodulation, equation (52) can be maximized by selecting a proper value of s, which is usually not equal to one except for the BDPSK modulation. It is observed that the soft information metrics yield much higher GMI than the conventional hard-decision metric. The PCMI-LLR gives the highest GMI, which is the same as the MI from the direct MC simulation using (53). In terms of GMI, the PCMI-LLR can be well approximated by the A-PCMI-LLR, while the GLRT-LLR and the Gauss-LLR both incur noticeable performance loss and the performance gap becomes wider at lower SNRs, higher Rician K-factors and larger values of σp2. There is a large performance gap between the coherent detection and the differential detection. However, the former requires accurate CSI, which is obtained at the expense of sacrificed the power- and bandwidthefficiency or increased receiver complexity, while the latter offers a simple and robust detection scheme, which leads to low-complexity receivers with no transmission overhead.
