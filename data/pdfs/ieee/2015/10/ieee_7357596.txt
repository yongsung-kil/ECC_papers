A log-likelihood ratio for improved receiver performance for VLF/LF communication in
atmospheric noise
K. Wiklundh, Member, IEEE, K. Fors, and P. Holm, Member, IEEE

Abstract— Receiver-improving techniques are important for submarine communication at the LF/VLF bands, due to very demanding reception conditions. At the surface, the interference environment is dominated by the atmospheric noise, which is highly impulsive in character and may impede the reception of the radio signals. To handle the demanding channel and the impulsive interference environment, error correction and an adapted receiver are necessary.
In this paper, we propose a log-likelihood ratio (LLR) as soft output from the demodulator suitable for atmospheric noise. The radio system is assumed to use minimum shift keying (MSK) and a low parity density check (LDPC) code. It is shown that the proposed interference-adapted LLR improves the performance substantially in atmospheric noise compared to when the LLR is designed for AWGN. The performance is also compared to a solution, where the soft output from the demodulator is simplified to a limiter, and to a solution when a larger system bandwidth is used in combination with a limiter. It is concluded that the proposed interference-adapted LLR achieves the best performance in the comparison, although the performance could probably be further improved, when compared to the obtained Shannon capacity of this particular interference.
Index Terms—LF/VLF band; underwater communication; impulsive noise; atmospheric noise; low parity density check (LDPC) code; log-likelihood ratio (LLR)
I. INTRODUCTION
RADIO communication to submarines is traditionally performed on the low frequency and the very low frequency (LF/VLF) bands. This communication may transport tactical information and the communication ability is of vital importance. The communication range and the possibilities to receive at large depth are important, as well as good coverage and availability. The primary advantage of the LF/VLF bands is its ability to penetrate water. Even though the attenuation in water is lower at the LF/VLF band than other bands, it is still significant and the receiver performance needs to be as good as possible. The attenuation in water is increasing with frequency and with the conductivity of the water and can typically be of 3 dB/m for 30 kHz and a conductivity of 1 S/m. In water with a conductivity of 4 S/m, the attenuation is over 6 dB/m for 30 kHz. The signal above

the surface can propagate long distances by reflection in the ionosphere. However, in the meantime, this reflection creates a large amount of fading due to interference between the reflected sky wave and the ground wave. In addition, the frequency band is associated with a challenging interference environment. At the surface and in this frequency band, the interference environment is dominated by the atmospheric noise, which is highly impulsive in character and may impede the reception of the radio signals. As the submarine enters a submerged position and increases its depth, the interference environment becomes more and more dominated by additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN). At a large depth, the atmospheric noise can in most cases be neglected.
The atmospheric noise is well studied and reported by ITU. The atmospheric noise is described by its impulsiveness and its level, in terms of impulsive ratio and antenna noise factor, respectively, [1]. To deal with the impulsive interference at the surface, interference suppression techniques can be implemented in the receiver. Mitigation techniques are important, since impulsive interference can otherwise result in a much worse degradation of the receiving performance than in AWGN [2]. One solution to handle impulsive interference is to use non-linear functions, such as limiters. Limiters can be placed before or after the detector in the receiver with different impact. Other kinds of improving techniques are forward error correction codes, with soft or hard decisions, suitable for impulsive interference. In [3], we investigated the improvement by using a larger system bandwidth in combination with error correction codes and a limiter. Another way to improve the performance is to adapt the log-likelihood ratio (LLR) of the soft-decision output of the demodulation to the statistical character of the impulsive noise. The LLR can then be utlized by the decoder.
In this work, we are proposing an LLR function suitable for atmospheric noise to improve the performance. Adapted LLR has been proposed for other kinds of interference models in several earlier works [4-8]. The derivation of the LLR for a certain type of interference environment is often straight forward, when the probability density function (pdf) of the signal can be mathematically described with a closed-form expression. In [4], the performance of a communication system using a low parity density check (LDPC) code is studied in Middleton Class A interference. The derivation of

However, for a log-normal distributed envelope, which is a commonly used model for atmospheric noise, the pdf of the quadrature components is not known and an LLR for this kind of interference has not yet been presented. For this purpose, we derive a closed-form solution of the joint distribution of the complex signal. However, for the pdf of the quadrature components, a numerical approach is needed. The performance of the proposed LLR is then compared to the obtained performance when no adaption to the atmospheric noise is made, i.e. the LLR is adapted to AWGN. Furthermore, the performance is also compared to the performance when a larger system bandwidth is used in combination with a limiter and when the LLR is substituted with a limiter at the output of the demodulator. In the evaluation, the receiver is assumed to use minimum shift keying (MSK) modulation and LDPC codes. As a complement in the performance comparison, the constrained Shannon capacity for MSK modulation of the current interference is derived.

II. SYSTEM ASSUMPTIONS
A. System model
The assumed communication system uses MSK modulation, which is a bandwidth efficient modulation scheme with constant envelope. Here, MSK is assumed to be coherent and with differential encoded information. The constant envelope has the advantage that it can be used in combination with a limiter on the front-end of the receiver. Furthermore as error correction, the system is assumed to use an LDPC code. In general, LDPC and turbo codes exhibit remarkable good results close to the Shannon limit, [9]. The LDPC code used in the simulations has a code rate of ½ and a parity-check matrix of size 32400 by 64800, which gives a block length of 64800 bits and corresponds to an irregular LDPC code. The LDPC decoder requires the LLR as soft input. In Fig. 1, the system model is depicted. The communication system is either interfered by atmospheric noise or by AWGN. The thermal reciver noise is neglected in order to be able to analyze the direct impact of the atmospheric noise. If the thermal receiver noise needs to be considered, it can be assumed to be included in the interference signal. The receiver blocks illustrated in the figure includes the different analyzed receiver soulutions:
• The LLR is adapted to AWGN. • The LLR is simplified to a limiter. • A larger system bandwidth and a limiter on the
complex signal before the MSK demodulator. The LLR is adapted to AWGN. • The LLR is adapted to the atmospheric noise. In all of these solutions the MSK has soft output.
B. Interference model
The atmospheric noise is in this frequency band mainly originated from thunderstorms and it is very impulsive in character. A number of different interference models are presented in the literature. In [10], an interference model is presented with a log-normal distributed envelope, with the impulsiveness ratio Vd as parameter. Vd is defined as the ratio between the root-mean-square (RMS) value and the average value of the interference envelope voltage. This model is

IV. RESULTS
A. BEP Performance
In the following, we will analyze the results of a system that uses MSK modulation and a LDPC code. Simulations are conducted based on the system model shown in Fig. 1. In Fig. 4, the BEP is shown as a function of Eb/NI, where Eb is the signal energy per bit and NI is the power spectral density of the interference signal. The performance is determined for either AWGN or atmospheric noise, here denoted as LN interference, with Vd =10 dB. For these interference types, the different receiver solutions described in Section IIA are analyzed and compared. The figure shows that the proposed interference-adapted LLR achieves in best performance. Compared to the performance when the LLR is adapted to a Normal distributed noise, the gain is substantially; in atmospheric noise Eb/NI =6 dB is needed when no adaption of LLR is done, whereas the Eb/NI= -4 dB for adapted LLR, for BEP=10-3. We can also see that the limiter performs rather well, although not as well as the interference-adapted LLR; at BEP=10-3, the difference in Eb/NI is about 3 dB.
As comparison, the performance derived in [1] for a larger system bandwidth in combination with a limiter is shown in Fig. 5. In this case, the limiter is applied on the envelope before the modulation, according to (12). In Fig. 5, we can see the performance for different system bandwidths. The larger system bandwidth is here denoted as NB, where N indicates the multiple of the signal bandwidth B. That is, N=5 means that the system bandwidth is five times the signal bandwidth and N=1 does not imply any extended system bandwidth. The figure shows that the performance is improved by enlarging the system bandwidth for atmospheric noise. An even larger bandwidth, that is, N > 5, will further improve the system performance for atmospheric noise. For AWGN, we have the opposite behavior and the system performance is degraded with a larger bandwidth.

V. CONCLUSION
Atmospheric noise constitutes a demanding interference environment for communication in the LF/VLF band. Since the noise differs severely from Normal distributed noise at the surface, the ordinary AWGN-adapted soft-output LLR is not particularly suitable. Hence, to improve the performance of the receiver, an interference-adapted LLR is proposed. The derived LLR results in a substantial performance improvement compared to when the traditional LLR is used. It also outperforms a receiver, where the LLR is substituted with a limiter or when a larger system bandwidth is used, in combination with a limiter, where the system bandwidth is of five times the signal bandwidth. Nevertheless, the results of the interference-adapted LLR is still some dB from the Shannon limit of required Eb/NI derived for this kind of interference.
