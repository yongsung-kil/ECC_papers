A High-Throughput Multi-rate LDPC Decoder for Error Correction of Solid-State Drives

Yishan Zhang*, Chun Zhang*, Zhiyuan Yan͋, Shuang ChenЪ, Hanjun Jiang*
Tsinghua National Laboratory for Information Science and Technology*Ъ Institute of Microelectronics*, Department of Electronic EngineeringЪ Tsinghua University, Beijing, 100084, China*Ъ Department of ECE, Lehigh University, Bethlehem, PA 18015, USA͋

Abstract—This paper presents a high-throughput multi-rate low density parity check (LDPC) decoder for error correction in solid-state drives (SSDs). In order to meet the high throughput requirement of SSDs, an adaptive normalized min-sum algorithm (NMSA) is implemented by using multiple look-up tables. With the proposed technique, a reduction in average iteration number of around 22.5%~30.3% is achieved when signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) is 6.14dB, and the area overhead is only 0.87%. The LDPC decoder is implemented in TSMC 65nm standard CMOS technology. For a rate-0.889 length-36864 quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC code, the decoder achieves a throughput of 14.9 Gb/s with an area of 5.365 mm2, and compares favorably with previously proposed architectures.
Keywords—low density parity check (LDPC) codes; solid-state drives (SSDs); adaptive normalization factor; decoder
I. INTRODUCTION
NAND Flash memory is widely used in consumer electronic products as it has the characteristics of fast access speed, high capacity and low power consumption. Especially, Solid-state-drives (SSDs) based on NAND Flash memory becomes more and more popular, which has the trend to replace Hard-disk-drives (HDDs) in some applications. Conventionally, Bose-Chaudhuri-Hocquenghem (BCH) codes are used to correct memory cell errors. However, as multi-level cell (MLC) NAND Flash becoming popular and the physical NAND flash memory cell feature size scaling-down, the BCH codes are no longer powerful enough for MLC. Low density parity check (LDPC) codes [1] are one of the promising replacers for the future NAND flash products.
Especially, for SSDs, the challenges for error correcting codes (ECC) also come from other technology evolution. Peripheral Component Interconnect Express (PCIe) is a highspeed serial computer expansion bus standard, which is used as data interface in SSD, replacing the traditional Serial ATA Express (SATAe). In PCIe 3.0, the data transfer rate reaches up to 8 Gb/s per lane [2]. Meanwhile, the data rate of the communication with NAND Flash also increases a lot. Open NAND Flash Interface Working Group (ONFI) is a consortium of technology companies working to develop open standards. The maximum data rate is 800 MB/s per channel in ONFI 4.0 standard [3]. And SSD system usually has more than one PCIe lane or ONFI channel. In this situation, the LDPC decoder should have a high enough throughput, or it would become the

bottleneck in the data flow of SSDs.
For NAND Flash memory, in the early lifetime, the signalto-noise ratio (SNR) of read-data is higher, and hard-decision is mainly used [4], which needs weaker error correcting capability. As the Program/Erase (PE) times of NAND Flash increases, the SNR becomes lower and lower, and stronger correcting capability is needed, especially in the end of lifetime. The needs of correcting capability are changed in different stage of NAND Flash’s lifetime. For LDPC, this leads to different code rates at different stage of lifetime: higher code rate for early lifetime and lower code rate for later lifetime. In this way, more available data space can leave for users. Therefore, supporting multi-rate is necessary for LDPC decoder.
In this paper, we present a high-throughput and areaefficient LDPC decoder for NAND Flash memories, supporting multi-rate quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes [5]. This decoder realizes the normalized min-sum algorithm (NMSA) [6] and layered message passing [7]. To reach higher throughput, the technique of adaptive normalization factor is applied to NMSA. In conventional NMSA, the normalization factor is fixed, such as 0.75 in [14] and 0.25 in [15]. But in adaptive NMSA, the normalization factor is determined based on the number of ungratified parity-check formulas in Η matrix. The implementation results for a (36864, 32768) LDPC code show that, when SNR is 6.14 dB, this improvement can reduces the average iteration number by around 22.5%~30.3%, which means a higher throughput by around 29.2%~43.4%. Moreover, the error performance also is enhanced by 0.2 dB, at the expense of only 0.87% area overhead. To support multirate QC-LDPC codes, the decoder is designed with the idea of single instruction multiple data (SIMD) processor for reference. One instruction corresponds to a packet of data belongs to one sub-matrix of QC-LDPC codes. Thus, different QC-LDPC codes can be achieved by using different instructions, but with the same hardware.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. Section II reviews the QC-LDPC codes and layered NMSA, and presents the improvement about adaptive normalization factor. Section III describes the architecture of LDPC decoder and the realization of adaptive normalization factor. In Section IV, the implementation results are provided. Finally, Section V concludes this paper.

C. Adaptive NMSA
In conventional layered NMSA, the normalization factor D is fixed, which is chosen before decoding or even fixed in hardware [15]. However, the optimal D is actually highly related to the reliability of incoming LLR values [9] [10]. As the LLR values change per iteration, D should be changed adaptively as well. When the reliability of LLR increases with the iterations going, the normalization factor should increase and finally tending to 1 [11]. To quantize the reliability of LLR in hardware, the number E(k) of unsatisfied parity-checks in Η is counted after each iteration, which means counting the non-zero bits of e(k) in (6). This counted number E(k) can present the error situation of the LLR values, and is easy to implement in hardware. With this number, the suitable normalization factor can be chosen for next iteration. A lower normalization factor will be chosen when E(k) is larger, and vice versa. Some formulas should be added in step decision:

A. Hardware Friendly Implementation of Adaptive NMSA
The original function )( ) in (11) is hard to implement in
hardware, so it is simplified into several look-up tables to realize different multiplications with different normalization factors. In hardware, the continuous normalization factor between 0 and 1 is hardly supported, but several discrete normalization factors can be supported easily. Each look-up table corresponds to one discrete normalization factor. By using different look-up tables, the normalization factors are adaptively chosen depending on error situation. When there are more errors, a smaller normalization factor is chosen, and vice versa. The number of the look-up tables is a trade-off: more look-up tables means more flexibility, better performance, but more area. From experiment experience, two look-up tables enhance the performance without excessively increasing the logic complexity. The proposed decoder is implemented with two look-up tables, as shown in Table I, where the values in the second (or third) row is the normalized values of the ones in the first row. The second row corresponds to a smaller normalization factor, while the third row a greater one. Based on our experiments, 256 is chosen as a threshold of E(k) : when E(k) is larger than 256, the second row is used, otherwise the third is used. This threshold number is chosen by experiment experience, and it needs more work to optimize it by theory.

V. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we proposed a high-throughput efficient LDPC decoder to meet the requirement imposed by the technology evolution of SSDs system. With the proposed techniques of adaptive normalization factor, the decoding speed increase by around 22.5%~30.3% when SNR is 6.14 dB, and the area overhead is only 0.87%. Besides, the error performance is also enhanced by 0.2 dB. The implementation results in TSMC 65 nm process show that, using (36864, 32768)
