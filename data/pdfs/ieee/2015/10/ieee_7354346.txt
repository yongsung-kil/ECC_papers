Construction of LDPC Codes Based on Resolvable Group Divisible Designs
Hengzhou Xu, Dan Feng, Cheng Sun, Baoming Bai State Key Laboratory of Integrated Services Networks, Xidian University
Xian 710071, P. R. China Email: bmbai@mail.xidian.edu.cn

Abstract—In this paper, we present a method for constructing LDPC codes from resolvable group divisible designs (RGDDs). According to the incidence matrices (or their submatrices) of RGDDs, a class of regular LDPC codes can be constructed. Since the parity-check matrix of the resulting code satisfies the rowcolumn constraint, the girth of the proposed codes is at least six. Furthermore, based on a resolvable group divisible design, we can obtain a sequence of LDPC codes with various lengths and rates, which perform well over the AWGN channel with iterative decoding and have low error floors.
I. INTRODUCTION
LDPC codes are a class of capacity-approaching channel codes, which were invented by Gallager in the early 1960’s [1] and then were rediscovered in the late 1990’s [2]. Since their rediscovery, the construction, encoding, decoding, performance analysis, and applications of LDPC codes have become focal points of research. On the construction of LDPC codes, many methods have been proposed. They can be roughly classified into two categories: random or randomlike constructions by means of computer search [3], [4] and structured constructions based on algebraic or combinatorial tools [5]−[8]. For LDPC codes with long block length, it has been shown that the codes based on random or random-like constructions could achieve performance extremely close to Shannon capacity [9]. However, they usually require higher complexity encoders and decoders due to the lack of sufficient structure. On the other side, for short to moderate code lengths, the structured LDPC codes can perform better than the random or random-like ones [10]. Besides, the structured LDPC codes have much lower error-floors [7]. This makes structured LDPC codes much attractive for applications requiring very low errorfloors, such as optical and microwave communications, and data storage systems.
Due to the merits of quick and simple encoding, low decoding complexity, and low error-floor of structured LDPC codes, a great deal of work was expended on the constructions of structured LDPC codes. Y. Kou, S. Lin et al. [5] first proposed a class of regular LDPC codes from finite geometries. Subsequently, S. Lin et al. applied the other mathematical tools to the construction of LDPC codes, such as BIBD [6], partial geometry [7] and finite field [8]. S. Johnson and S. Weller constructed the regular LDPC codes based on the Kirkman triple systems [11] and oval designs [12] in 2001. B. Vasic et al. presented some combinatorial constructions of LDPC

codes for iterative decoding in 2004 [13]. On the basis of the singer perfect difference set, C. Chen, B. Bai et al. proposed a method for constructing quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC cycle codes in 2010 [14]. In the above methods for constructing LDPC codes, short cycles, especially 4-cycles, can be avoided by employing the incidence matrices of chosen geometries and combinatorial tools as parity-check matrices. Furthermore, these structured LDPC codes have good performance decoded with the sum-product algorithm (SPA) [10].
X. Shan and T. Li [15] first used a class of group divisible designs, which is equal to strong regular geometries, to construct LDPC codes. They also proved that the incidence matrices of these group divisible designs have full rank. In this paper, we propose to employ another type of group divisible designs known as resolvable group divisible designs (RGDDs) for constructing structured LDPC codes. A method for obtaining a sequence of LDPC codes from a RGDD is presented. Different from the work of [15], we can take various submatrices of a RGDD’s incidence matrix as paritycheck matrices and construct a sequence of LDPC codes with various rates and lengths. For convenience, we will denote the resulting LDPC codes by RGDD-LDPC codes in the following. According to the structure properties of RGDD, it is ensured that these RGDD-LDPC codes are regular. Meanwhile, the Tanner graphs of the RGDD-LDPC codes are free of 4-cycles, then their girth is at least six. From different RGDDs, we can construct lots of RGDD-LDPC codes with a wide range of lengths and rates. Numerical results show that the proposed LDPC codes have performances similar to EGLDPC codes proposed by S. Lin [16], random MacKay LDPC codes [17], and (or better than in some cases) QC LDPC codes from finite fields [16], [18]. In addition, we can give plentiful RGDD-LDPC codes with various rates and lengths, and then greatly extend the class of algebraically structured LDPC codes in theory. The proposed codes also have fast decoding convergence and show no error floor down to the BER of 10−8.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. In Section II, some definitions and concepts on LDPC codes and resolvable group divisible designs are briefly reviewed. In Section III, RGDD-LDPC codes are proposed. In Section IV, design examples and numerical results are provided to demonstrate the performance of RGDD-LDPC codes. At last, we conclude the paper in Section V.

II. PRELIMINARIES
A. LDPC Codes
An LDPC code is a special class of (n, k) linear block codes defined by the null space of an m × n sparse paritycheck matrix H. If the matrix H has the following properties: (1) each column has γ nonzero elements; (2) each row has ρ nonzero elements; (3) γ m and ρ n, then the LDPC code generated by such H is called a (γ, ρ)-regular LDPC code. A simple and common way to describe LDPC codes is to utilize a graphical model called Tanner graph. The Tanner graph of an LDPC code is a bipartite graph in which the two types of nodes are variable nodes (representing coded symbol variables) and check nodes (representing the local check-sum constraints), respectively. An edge connects the variable node i to the check node j if and only if the entry hij is nonzero in H. Usually, there are many cycles in the Tanner graph. The size of the shortest cycle is called the girth of the Tanner graph (or LDPC code). The short cycles affect the decoding performance with iterative algorithm based on belief propagation, especially, the 4-cycles. In order to create an LDPC code free of 4-cycle, a structural property is imposed on H: no two rows (or two columns) have more than one nonzero element in common. This constraint on H is known as the row-column constraint (or RC constraint) [7]. The RC-constraint ensures that the girth of the LDPC codes generated by such H is at least six.
B. Resolvable Group Divisible Design(RGDD)
Let υ and λ be non-negative integers, and let K and M be two positive integer sets. A group divisible design (GDD), denoted by GDD(K, λ, M ; υ), or K-GDD for short, is a triple (X , G, B), where X is a point set, G = {G1, G2, ..., Gt} is a partition of X , and B = {B1, B2, ..., Bl} is a class of subsets of X with the following properties [19]−[22]. (each element of X is called point, each element of G is called group and each element of B is called block)
1) The size of X is υ, i.e. |X | = υ. 2) The size of any group is in M , i.e. for 1 ≤ i ≤ t, |Gi| ∈
M. 3) The size of any block is in K, i.e. for 1 ≤ j ≤ l, |Bj| ∈
K. 4) A group and a block contain at most one point in
common, i.e. for 1 ≤ i ≤ t, 1 ≤ j ≤ l, |Gi ∩ Bj| ≤ 1. 5) Every pair of points from distinct groups is contained in
λ blocks precisely.
The mutiset {|G1|, |G2|, ..., |Gt|} is called the group-type (or type) of K-GDD and for 1 ≤ i ≤ t, |Gi| is called the size of the group Gi. We always use exponential notation to shorten the type of K-GDD: G has a type g1u1 g2u2 · · · gtut if G contains u1 groups of size g1, u2 groups of size g1, ... , ut groups of size gt. A K-GDD is uniform if all groups have the same size; for some non-negative integers g and u, we denote uniform K-GDDs which have a type gu as K-GDD(gu). When K = {k}, such a K-GDD will be denoted by {k}-GDD, or k-GDD for short. When M = {g}, such a k-GDD can be written as GDD(k, λ, g; υ).

A K-GDD is said to be resolvable if its blocks can be

classified into parallel classes, each of which partitions the point set X . Such a K-GDD can be denoted by K-RGDD. If a GDD(k, λ, g; υ) is resolvable, it can be written as k-RGDD or RGDD(k, λ, g; υ). We take 3-RGDD as an example in the

On basis of the above analysis, it is obvious that we can use a RGDD(k, 1, g; υ) to construct a sequence of regular LDPC codes free of 4-cycles. Now we count the number of pairs of points in a RGDD(k, 1, g; υ) from two perspectives. First, the number of all the pairs of points in X is clearly Cυ2 = υ(υ −1)/2. On the other hand, any two points in X is either in a block or in a group. So the pairs of points can be divided into two parts: the pairs of points in groups and the pairs of points in blocks. Since the size of a group in RGDD(k, 1, g; υ) is g, there are υ/g groups. Because each group has Cg2 = g(g−1)/2 pairs of points, so the number of pairs of points in groups is υ(g − 1)/2. Since the cardinality of each block is k, there are υ/k blocks in a parallel class and the number of pairs of points in a block is Ck2 = k(k − 1)/2. Let us suppose that there are p parallel classes in RGDD(k, 1, g; υ), then the number of pairs of points in blocks is p · υ/k · k(k − 1)/2 = pυ(k − 1)/2. So we can get the following equation:

As an example, the incidence matrix I of the RGDD(3,1,4;12) is depicted in Fig. 1, where I1, I2, I3, and I4 represent the incidence matrix of a single parallel class, respectively. Based on the incidence matrix I, we can define an incidence graph of the RGDD, which has two sets of vertices: X and B. There is an edge connecting two vertices α and β in the incidence graph if and only if α ∈ X and β ∈ B or α ∈ B and β ∈ X . A cycle in the graph is a series of vertices which start and end at the same vertex and contain other vertices no more than once. The length of a cycle is the number of vertices (or edges) it contains and the length of its shortest cycle is the girth of the incidence graph. Since the incidence graph is bipartite, the length of a cycle must be even and at least four. In a RGDD(k, 1, g; υ), every pair of points from distinct groups is contained in one block precisely. That is, every pair of points in X is contained in at most one block and every pair of blocks contain at most one point. This results in that no two rows (or columns) in incidence matrix I have more than one position where they both have 1-component in common. Therefore, the incidence matrix I satisfies the RC constraint. The girth of the incidence graph based on such a RGDD is at least six.

By solving the above equation, we have p = (v − g)/(k − 1). In other words, there are (v − g)/(k − 1) (For convenience, let p substitute for (v − g)/(k − 1) in the following) parallel classes in RGDD(k, 1, g; υ). The cardinality of each block is k, so each block contains k points. Since one point in X is contained in one parallel class precisely, the row and column weight of the incidence matrix I corresponding to a RGDD are p and k, respectively.
Suppose we employ the blocks in ρ (no more than p) parallel classes as columns and the points in X as rows to form a υ × (ρυ/k) matrix H (a submatrix of the incidence matrix I). It is clear that the row and column weight of the matrix H are ρ and k, respectively. Numerical results show that no matter how many parallel classes we employ, the matrix H has almost full rank. When the number of columns is greater than the number of rows in H, the rank of matrix H approaches to υ. We take the matrix H as a parity-check matrix of LDPC code and then the null space of the matrix H gives an LDPC code of length ρυ/k with rate about (1 − k/ρ). The structure of parity-check matrix H is illustrated in Fig. 2. By following this method, based on a RGDD, we take different number of parallel classes, and then a sequence of LDPC codes with various lengths and rates can be obtained. The girths of the resulting codes are at least six.

Although the RGDDLDPC codes have performances similar to (or better than in some cases) QC LDPC codes from finite fields [16], [18], they will spend much more encoding and decoding time because of without quasi-cyclic structure. A sequence of LDPC codes can be formed by increasing some parallel classes from a RGDD. The length and rate of the RGDD-LDPC codes vary with the number of parallel classes. In other words, the constructed LDPC codes from a RGDD have the same number of check nodes. Since the existence of 3-RGDDs and 4-RGDDs have been solved [19], [20], we can give the RGDD-LDPC codes that almost cover all rates and lengths, which perform well over AWGN channels, especially at high rates. For short code lengths, the proposed codes preform about 1.2 dB away from the capacity limit of finite lengths [23] at WER = 10−5. That’s to say, there is still a lot of room for us to optimize the RGDDLDPC codes.
V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we propose a method for constructing regular LDPC codes based on a combinatorial tool, resolvable group divisible designs (RGDDs). Numerical results show that the proposed regular LDPC codes perform well over AWGN channel with iterative decoding algorithm and have no error floor down to the BER of 10−8. Meanwhile, based on a RGDD, the parity-check matrix of the resultant LDPC code with rates more than 0.5 has almost full rank. So using this construction method, we can design LDPC codes with the required “rate and length” parameters precisely. Nevertheless, there is a constraint: the rate of LDPC codes obtained from a RGDD varies with the code length. In other words, for a constant code length, the LDPC codes with various rates can’t be obtained from a given RGDD. However, they still can be constructed based on different RGDDs.
