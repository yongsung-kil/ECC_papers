Energy-Adaptive Codes

Haewon Jeong (haewon@cmu.edu) School of Electrical and Computer Engineering
Carnegie Mellon University

Pulkit Grover (pulkit@cmu.edu) School of Electrical and Computer Engineering
Carnegie Mellon University

Abstract— We provide the first constructions of a new family of error-correcting codes called “energy-adaptive codes.” These codes are designed to enable adaptive circuit implementations that minimize the total system-level energy based on varying distances and target error probabilities. Recent work has explored fundamental limits and practical strategies for minimizing total (transmit + circuit) power, considering both power consumed in computational nodes as well as wires in the circuit. It is now established that to minimize total power, code choice and circuit-design should change with communication distances and/or target error probability. Motivated by circuit area constraints, the energy-adaptive codes adapt energy consumption as distances and/or target error probability change. These codes shrink and expand the wiring area they occupy as demands on the system change, adjusting the hardware by turning on and off non-local wires in the circuit. The code constructions are hierarchical, and use QC-LDPC codes as the basis. We estimate the decoding power savings attained by use of these codes through simulation results. Such codes can be of increasing utility as we enter the era of 1000 devices per person where designing the skilled labor for obtaining optimized designs for each system will simply be unavailable. While our first constructions are admittedly simplistic, the goal of the paper is to bring this new problem to the attention of the coding-theory community.
I. INTRODUCTION
Recent work [1]–[6] has explored fundamental limits and practical strategies for communication minimizing total (transmit + circuit) power, considering both power consumed in computational nodes as well as wires in the circuit. Experimental work has also verified that wiring power is a substantial fraction of power consumed in the decoding circuitry [4], and code-design that ignores wiring power can consume more power than is needed [5], [7], [8]. It is now well understood that to minimize total power, code choice should change with communication distances and/or target error probability: the energy-optimal codes for short distance communication, or higher error probability, are “simpler” codes (e.g. lower girth, smaller blocklength) requiring smaller circuit energy even at the cost of increased transmit energy. At long distances, energy-optimal codes operate closer to capacity because circuit power matters less and less in an overall system-level power optimization1.
In modern systems, communication distance can vary from a few meters to a few kilometers, the target error probability can vary, for e.g., from 10−4 to 10−15, and the SNR and the code-rate can also vary depending on the channel
1In some cases, such as downlink in mobile communications, circuit power matters even at long distances because it is constrained by the cellphone’s battery.

conditions and the application requirements. Even though it is demonstrably better to use optimized codes for different situations, with circuit designs that adapt accordingly, circuit area constraints limit us from implementing one decoder for each different situation. As we enter the era of 1000 wireless devices per-person and the Internet of Things, not only will we be constrained by the circuit area, the current method of code choice and circuit design itself will need to be overhauled. Currently, optimized design of decoders for each application requires multiple Ph.D. theses (see, e.g., [9]). This resource of highly skilled personnel will simply be unavailable for tens of thousands of systems that will exist, which motivates design of a single universal code and circuit that adapts its rate and energy to suit the system requirements for as many systems as possible. It is conceivable that such a universal system can then be optimized by the available skilled personnel.
This motivates us to come up with a new family of codes which we call “energy-adaptive” codes. These codes can be implemented using a single decoder, but they enable the implementation to adapt its decoding energy to suit different communication requirements. A similar concept, “rate-adaptive codes” has been used to overcome the circuit area constraints when the communication rate requirements are changing [10]–[12]. However, the questions we ask in energy-adaptive codes and rate-adaptive codes are fundamentally different. Rate-adaptive codes aim to adapt to different communication rates using the same hardware to encode and decode. In the case of energy-adaptive codes, communication rate is fixed but the goal is to design adaptive codes for efficient power usage in decoder circuits. Also, the degree of adaptivity needed for rate-adaptive codes could be much larger than that for rate-adaptive codes since the possible variation is even larger, because communication distances and error-probabilities can together have a significantly larger range of variation than the rate (which is a single parameter).2

While the current code choice, an RS-LDPC code [9], is a fixed code chosen for its performance at the worst-case distance, can design of a flexible code lower the energy requirements? Similarly, in wireless communication, a mobile user has distances that change as the user moves. Can the system adapt its circuit and transmit energy to minimize the total system energy? A flexible code design can enable such adaptation without an increase in hardware area.
The proposed energy-adaptive codes shrink and expand the wiring area they occupy as demands on the system change, adjusting the hardware by turning on and off non-local wires in the circuit. In this paper, we use quasi-cyclic low density parity check (QC-LDPC) codes [15], [16] as our basis and add hierarchical structures to them. The focus on wiring area comes from our prior work [2], [4], [17], which shows that wiring energy-costs are a significant fraction of total energy, and dominate node-energy as target error-probability is lowered to zero. Our hierarchical constructions with the different levels of hierarchy in a code enable adaptively turning off the wires at higher levels when requirements on the system are less stringent. By giving an explicit layout of message-passing decoding circuitry, we will show that how this code construction enables creating local and global structures on the circuit. As the demands on the system increase (in lowering SNR or reducing target error probability), higher levels of hierarchy are used which turn on increasingly longer, global wires.
A general definition of hierarchical QC-LDPC codes was introduced in [18]. While our code construction can be viewed as a special case of these codes, the purpose of our

codes is to minimize the decoding circuit energy adaptively, while the goal in [18] was to build high-girth codes.
It is natural to ask whether rate-adaptive codes be easily modified to achieve energy-adaptive codes. The difficulty with reducing energy consumption is that one needs to reduce not just the number of operations, but also the wiring energy [2], [4] that these codes do not target. As a consequence, the average wire length that is used in computation does not reduce as the requirements on the required performance are lowered (e.g. through higher SNR or relaxed target error probability). While there are code constructions that optimize wiring lengths for a given performance (e.g. [7], [8], [19] etc.) as well as codes that reduce computational overhead for wiring by explicitly making code design such that interconnections are simple to route, these codes are not adaptive.
In Section III, we provide our hierarchical code construction. In Section IV, we provide simulation results for a 2-level hierarchical design that show the advantage of our hierarchical constructions over an LDPC code designed for the worst-case error-probability with the same overall degree distribution. For a fair comparison with this nonadaptive code, we still allow the nonadaptive code to change its number of iterations in response to changing target errorprobability. We show that when the target error-probability happens to be higher than worst-case target error probability, the proposed codes save significant energy (up to a factor of 2). We expect gains as communication distances change, and increased gains as the number of levels of hierarchy are increased (with careful design), and this is a problem currently under investigation. We discuss this and further future directions in Section V.

Does an inefficiency of not using adaptive codes really exist in real-world practical systems? We examine this through the example of data-center communication networks. Let

Comparing with Fig. 1(b), which is a classical Shannon-theoretic representation (the “Shannon-waterfall curves”) makes it clear that these codes were chosen because of their good performance in terms of lowering transmit power. That is, they perform close to the channel capacity. However, as we discussed, this Shannon-theoretic view is insufficient to understand the total system energy since it only focuses on transmit power, while the total system energy gets dominated by encoding/decoding circuit energy at small distances, or as the target error probability approaches 0. Hence, the code chosen merely by this analysis leads to a highly inefficient choice of a code.
Furthermore, this code choice was driven by the worstcase distance of communication, 100 meters, while the average distance in data centers is only 14 meters. If we use simpler codes for shorter distances of communications, we can save a considerable amount of decoding energy. In fact, analysis in [13] suggests that up to 10x power savings in data-center networks are possible through proper code-design and system optimization. Further, data-centers consume 2% of the world’s electricity, and data-center networks consume 20-50% of the entire data-centers’ energy [13]. Thus a proper code-choice and corresponding circuit design for data-center networks will make a nonnegligible reduction to energy levels on a global scale!
Finally, this is just one standard. Data center networks use many different technologies and standards, and there are many networks beyond data-center networks that consume significant energy (e.g. set-top boxes). The energy requirements for Information and Communication Technologies exceed 10% of the world energy, and are quickly increasing. Thus this problem is of increasing importance.
III. CODE DESIGN, AND WIRING AREA AND ENERGY ESTIMATES
A. Code Design
In this section, we describe how we design energy-adaptive codes. Since codes are built based on the QC-LDPC codes, we will briefly review on QC-LDPC codes. Then adaptive codes with a hierarchical structure will be introduced. We will then discuss models and estimates of wiring area and energy consumption of decoder circuits.
QC-LDPC codes are characterized by a parity-check matrix which consists of small submatrices that can be either a circulant permutation matrix or an all-zero matrix. Unlike fully random LDPC codes, QC-LDPC codes are constructed in a more structured way using circulant permutation matrices. This reduces the wiring complexity of encoders and decoders, simplifying the hardware design while still enabling large girth codes [15], [24]–[26]. For these reasons, they are widely adopted as wireless communication standards such as IEEE 802.16e (WiMAX), IEEE 802.11n (Wireless LAN), and IEEE 10-Gbase-T (for wired Ethernet, including data-center networks).
Now, we propose a modified version of conventional QCLDPC codes which has hierarchical structures that make the code circuit-energy-adaptive. A hierarchical structure was devised to easily adjust the code length just by turning off higher levels in the hierarchy to achieve different error rate requirement. This is one possible construction of energyadaptive codes which can adapt its decoding energy, increasing or reducing depending on the application’s requirements.
A simple 2-level hierarchical code is shown in Fig. 2a. Level 1 blocks can be thought as local structures and the Level 2 block as a global structure which connects two local blocks of Level 1. In Level 1, variable nodes are divided into equal halves and variable-to-check edges connect the nodes only within those separated blocks. Check nodes connecting variable nodes from the both Level 1 blocks appear in the Level 2 block.

The key idea is to divide the code into local and global structures so that we can save the decoding energy by using only local structures when the desired accuracy is not too high. This construction makes it possible to save substantial energy when we are only decoding the Level 1 sub-code since we can turn off all the long wires in the global structure. In coding-theory literature, there is a class of codes called “locally decodable codes” [27], which sounds similar to our concept of energy-adaptive codes. However, in their goals, these codes are quite different: locally-decodable codes aim at recovering any bit using any set of neighbors of certain size; whereas energy-adaptive codes proposed here prefer using fixed local neighborhoods that are geographically nearby, thereby reducing the required wire lengths.
We can further extend a simple 2-level structure to a hierarchical code with more levels. Adding more levels allows us to have a more refined control of the flexibility of codes. While there are benefits to increasing the number of hierarchical levels, it comes with its own costs: the code length increases exponentially in the number of levels, and the degrees add up as well. For regularLDPC codes, it is well known that under belief propagation decoding, the performance does not improve beyond a certain degree (for a fixed rate). Hence, we believe we will need to

explore irregular LDPC constructions to allow incorporating more hierarchical levels.
B. Circuit Energy and Wiring Area Estimates
In this subsection, we will explain how we estimate the energy consumed by a decoding circuit. To give an asymptotic bound on the decoding circuit energy, we will follow Thompson’s VLSI theory and use a simple wire-andnode model to calculate circuit area. This is the model used in [17], [28]. In this model, the total energy consumed is given as follows:

Etot = ρwireAwiresT

(5)

where ρwire is a unit area power consumption per unit time, Awire is area occupied by all the wires and T is the total computation time. As we are considering decoding circuits, T here can be thought as the number of message passing iterations. As you can see from the equation, energy consumed by nodes are ignored and only wiring energy is considered. This is a good approximation of circuit energy since it is known that wiring power is dominant in the total circuit power consumption.
Among these parameters in (5), what we can largely improve by a new design of codes is Awires, wiring area. Hence the remaining part of this subsection will focus on how to estimate wiring area of decoding circuits. First we will analyze a decoding circuit of a regular (dv, dc) LDPC code, then we will show how to extend this analysis to our hierarchical code design.

Just as traditional regular LDPC code are denoted as (dv, dc)-regular code, we will denote 2-level adaptive codes as (dv1, dc1)-(dv2, dc2)-adaptive code. Codes we used here are (9,16)-(3,16), (8,16)-(4,16), (7,16)-(5,16) adaptive codes and (12,16)-regular code was used for the comparison. All of them are QC-LDPC codes with the block length 1024, rate 1/4, and girth 6.

V. CONCLUSIONS AND DISCUSSIONS
In this paper, we observed that while it appears necessary to change the code to adapt to changing distances and error-probabilities to achieve energy optimality, implementing multiple decoders on one chip is very impractical. A new class of codes that we call energy-adaptive codes was proposed to solve the problem, which can flexibly adapt its decoding energy to not waste unnecessary energy on decoder circuits.
We suggested one possible construction of such energyadaptive codes by using hierarchical QC-LDPC codes. To examine the effectiveness of our code design, we built three different 2-level hierarchical codes and a non-adaptive code and compared them. By using VLSI models to estimate circuit energy consumption and, Gallager B decoder simulation to obtain BER at each iteration, we provided a tradeoff of BER and circuit energy. Through this analysis, we were able to confirm that using energy-adaptive codes can substantially reduce the circuit energy, for example, 2x less energy at the target bit error rate 10−4. We believe substantially larger savings should be possible using more careful code design.
Our goal is also to bring this problem to the attention of the community, and this work has many shortcomings. Even though a wire model is more indicative of energy consumption than a node-model, it is still insufficient to describe the circuit energy consumption accurately. Further, theoretical analyses need to be accompanied with the simulation results to have a better understanding of code design.
Introducing hierarchical structure on QC-LDPC codes is only one way of designing energy-adaptive codes. We believe other class of codes can be transformed into energy-adaptive codes as well. Our ongoing work explores protograph-based energy-adaptive codes and we aim to examine other classes of codes beyond sparse-graph codes such as polar codes [31] or more classical codes such as Reed-Soloman codes as well.
