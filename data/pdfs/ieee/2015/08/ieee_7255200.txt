Cost-effectively Improving Life Endurance of MLC NAND Flash SSDs via Hierarchical Data
Redundancy and Heterogeneous Flash Memory

Shishi Tan, Ruirong Yu, Shenggang Wan, and Qiang Cao School of Computer Science and Technology, Huazhong University of Science and Technology, Wuhan, China 430074
Data Storage System Laboratory, Wuhan National Laboratory for Optoelectronics, Wuhan, China 430074 Email:{shishi tan, ruirong, sgwan, caoqiang}@hust.edu.cn

Abstract—As an alternative to conventional spinning HDDs, MLC NAND flash memory based SSDs suffer from a low life endurance. To cost-effectively address this problem, we propose integrating both Hierarchical data redundancy and Heterogeneous flash memory techniques into those SSDs, named as H2-SSD. By deploying across-chips data redundancy in addition to the conventional in-page ECCs, the error correction capacity of H2SSD can be dramatically enhanced. As a result, the life endurance can be significantly improved. Furthermore, an extra small sized SLC chip is deployed in H2-SSD to store the across-chips parity. Due to the high Program/Erase performance and life endurance of that SLC chip, the degradation of I/O performance induced by the hierarchical data redundancy will be slight in most cases, even under a strict synchronous parity update strategy.
Both quantitatively analysis and trace-driven simulation are conducted to evaluate the effectiveness of H2-SSD. The results demonstrate that H2-SSD outperforms the conventional SSDs in the maximum Program/Erase cycles by 23% to 178% and suffer from a less than 10% degradation of I/O performance under most cases.
I. INTRODUCTION
NAND flash memory based SSDs (Solid State Drives/Disks), with high I/O performance, energy efficiency, and shock resistance, become a promising alternative to conventional spinning HDDs (Hard Disk Drives). To improve the bit density of those SSDs, MLC (Multi-Level Cell, which stores two or more bits in a memory cell) techniques are widely adopted, while the feature size of the memory cell is constantly being scaled down. However, those efforts to improve the bit density can lead to a lower life endurance of the SSDs, i.e., an SSD would wear out after a less number of P/E (Program/Erase) cycles.
The life endurance of NAND flash memory is mainly limited by its underlying structures. In an NAND flash memory cell, an isolate layer is used to trap electrons. The P/E operations can strain that isolate layer. It leads those electrons become more probable to escape the memory cell during a certain period of retention time. Since states of the memory cell are directly determined by the number of electrons, raw bit errors (incorrect cell states) will increase with the growth of the P/E cycles.
In-page ECCs are deployed in the NAND flash SSD to correct the raw bit errors. To maintain an acceptable uncorrectable

BER (Bit Error Rate), e.g., < 10−15, the error correction capacity of the in-page ECCs should be constantly improved under an increasing raw BER. Typically, it will induce a longer decoding latency and be limited by available in-page spare bytes.
An alternative approach is deploying extra across-chips data redundancy. Typically, only a very small portion of pages have sufficient bit errors that cannot be corrected by the in-page ECC. Since the error detection capacity of the in-page ECC is usually much stronger than its error correction capacity, most of those pages with uncorrected bit errors can be identified by the in-page ECC and reconstructed by the across-chips data redundancy. As a result, a lower uncorrectable BER can be achieved.
Clearly, the across-chips data redundancy will induce extra I/Os to write/update the parity. All existing solutions cannot effectively address this problem. For example, CR5M [1] deploys an extra chip on each channel of an SSD. That chip acts as a write buffer to store a replica of recently written data instead of immediately writing/updating the corresponding parity. Consequentially, it can reduce the aforementioned extra writes/updates sent to the rest chips. However, the remained writes/updates can still significantly affect the write throughput of the SSD and life endurance of the rest chips. EECC [2] uses a small sized HDD to absorb those extra writes/updates on parity, but it will lose the shock resistance due to that additional HDD. Furthermore, the HDD may become the bottleneck of the overall performance under a write intensive workload.
Different from CR5M and EECC, we believe that hierarchical data redundancy built upon heterogeneous NAND flash memory chips can be explored and exploited to costeffectively handle those extra writes/updates, which is rooted by the following two important but usually overlooked facts.
• SLC (Single-Level Cell) flash memory far outperforms MLC flash memory in P/E performance and life endurance, e.g., 0.23ms v.s. 1.6ms (program latency), 0.7ms v.s. 3.0ms (erase latency), and 100, 000 v.s. 3, 000 (maximum P/E cycles) [3], [4]. As a result, we can deploy dedicated SLC chip to store acrosschips parity (which is similar to the parity disk in RAID-4). The SLC chip is able to well service those extra writes/updates on parity due to its high P/E performance and life endurance.

For example, as analyzed in Section III-A, an MLC chip wears out under its peak write throughput after about nine months even under the protection of a strong in-page ECC code. These two observations motivate us to propose a Hierarchical-data-redundancy and Heterogeneous-flashmemory based SSD, named as H2-SSD. H2-SSD leverages across-chips data redundancy to reconstruct the pages with uncorrectable bit errors. Therefore, the bit error correction capacity of H2-SSD can be dramatically improved, thus leads to much higher life endurance. Furthermore, one small sized SLC chip is deployed to store the across-chips parity which is generated by the asymmetric rule. Due to the high P/E performance and life endurance of the SLC flash memory, the extra writes/updates on the across-chips parity can be well serviced and have very slight impact on the I/O performance of H2-SSD.
We make the following contributions in this paper.
• We design a novel SSD architecture, named as H2SSD, which is based on a Hierarchical data redundancy scheme (in-page ECC and across-chips redundancy) and built upon Heterogeneous flash memory chips (SLC and MLC chips). A dedicated parity generation rule is proposed to effectively mine the advantages of SLC chips in P/E performance and life endurance, and reduce the extra cost of deploying the SLC chip.
• We quantitatively compare the error correction capacity of the conventional in-page ECC and hierarchical data redundancy schemes. The comparison results demonstrate the huge benefits of the latter one on improving the life endurance. For example, the life endurance of H2-SSD is 1.23 to 2.78 times of that of conventional SSD in terms of P/E cycles.
• We evaluate the I/O performance of H2-SSD based on a validated SSD simulator Micro-Add-on. The results collected from trace-driven simulation demonstrate that a proper number of the MLC chips can effectively mitigate the degradation of I/O performance induced by using across-chips data redundancy, even under a strict synchronous parity update strategy. For example, in an H2-SSD composed of eight MLC chips and one SLC chip, the degradation of peak throughput is less than 10%, compared to a conventional SSD consisting of eight MLC chips.

The rest of this paper is organized as follows. Section II reviews the necessary background materials and related work. In Section III, we demonstrate the general and detailed design of H2-SSD. Section IV illustrates the benefits on improving the life endurance by deploying hierarchical data redundancy in H2-SSD. Section V evaluates I/O performance of H2SSD through trace-driven simulation and analyzes simulation results. We conclude our paper and summarize the future work in Section VI.
II. BACKGROUND AND RELATED WORK
In this section, we will review the background materials and related work about conventional in-page ECC, across-chips data redundancy, and heterogeneous flash memory techniques in SSDs.
A. Raw Bit Errors and In-page ECCs
Typically, an SSD is worn-out when the raw bit errors cannot be effectively hidden by in-page ECCs, i.e., the uncorrectable bit error rate exceeds a certain threshold, e.g., 10−15.
In essential, raw bit errors in NAND flash memory are incurred by the strained oxide layer. An NAND flash memory cell resembles an MOS transistor having two gates. One is the control gate. The other surrounded by oxide is known as the floating gate. Isolated by the oxide layer, the floating gate can trap electrons, which form some non-overlapped threshold voltage windows. Program operations charge the floating gate to modify the threshold voltage of the memory cell; while erase operations remove electrons from the floating gate. The program/erase operations will significantly strain the oxide layer, which leads the electrons in the floating gate to be instable, i.e., easy to escape. As a result, the threshold voltage of the memory cell may jump into another threshold voltage window after a certain period of retention time, thus results in raw bit error(s). Furthermore, according to the observation [6], the raw bit error rate of the NAND flash memory increases exponentially as the growth of P/E cycles.
As the MLC flash memory stores two or more bits in a cell, it usually contains much more threshold voltage windows than the SLC flash memory, e.g., the SLC flash memory cell only has two threshold windows but the MLC flash cell stores two bits has four threshold windows. Consequentially, both the threshold voltage window and the interval between two adjacent windows of the MLC flash memory become narrow. Therefore, the MLC flash memory is more likely to be affected by the escaping of electrons and suffers from a high raw bit error rate, e.g., two orders of magnitude worse than SLC flash memory under the same feature size [7], [8]. In the meantime, due to the continuously scaling-down feature size of the flash memory, the raw BER of the MLC flash memory increases rapidly [9]. To hide this high and constantly increasing raw bit error rate in MLC flash memory, complicated ECCs (Error Correction Codes) are deployed, e.g., BCH codes [10], [11], [12] and LDPC codes [13].
BCH codes [10], [11], [12] were invented in 1959 and a type of cyclic error correction codes constructed using finite fields. The key features of BCH codes are that they have a precise control over the number of correctable symbol errors

The decoding latency of the BCH codes will increase rapidly with the growth of their error correction/detection capacity, e.g., that latency in a (216 + 102, 216, 6) BCH code ranges from 10us to 90us, while that in a (216 + 204, 216, 12) BCH code ranges from 17us to 180us [14]. In other words, all read requests will suffer from a significantly increasing latency by using more powerful in-page BCH codes. Furthermore, the available spare bytes in the flash chips can also limit the deployment of BCH codes with stronger error correction capacity, since other storage applications, e.g., file systems [15], also use that spare bytes to improve the performance or reliability of the computer system.
In addition, a small sized code word can be helpful to reduce the read latency [16]. As a result, the segment length of BCH codes in real-world SSD is usually smaller than the page size, e.g., 0.5KiB ∼ 4KiB [17], [18] v.s. 2KiB ∼ 16KiB [19], [4]. In other words, to use BCH codes, a page of which the size is larger than 4KiB can be first divided into several small sized segments.
When the raw bit errors exceed the error correction capacity of the in-page ECC, they are treated as uncorrectable bit errors. To reduce those uncorrectable bit errors induced by the inadequate in-page ECC, we propose a hierarchical data redundancy scheme that uses extra across-chips data redundancy to reconstruct the data segments with uncorrectable bit errors.

B. Data Redundancy across Chips
As mentioned in Section I, the across-chips data redundancy in SSDs can further reduce uncorrectable bit errors. However, extra I/Os to write/update the parity can be induced by that across-chips data redundancy. The frequent parity writes/updates will affect the write throughput and life endurance of the SSD. To handle those extra writes/updates, solutions [1], [2] are proposed.
Wang et al. [1] propose CR5M, which deploys an extra chip on each channel in the RAID5-structured SSD. Recently written data can be replicated to the extra chip in the same channel and protected by that replication. Therefore, the parity

generated by that data need not to be updated immediately, thus reduces the write requests sent to the rest chips. A significant drawback of CR5M is the huge extra monetary cost of deploying those additional chips, particularly under a configuration that only one or two chips are actually deployed in one channel. In addition, when that extra chip is busy or full, CR5M need to writes/updates the parity on the rest chips. As a result, those rest chips will still suffer from extra writes.
Subedi et al. [2] present a hybrid erasure-coded SSD architecture named EECC which uses a weaken in-page ECC and deploys an HDD to store parity. The HDD absorbs the parity writes/updates so that both the performance and reliability can be improved. However, the shock resistance of the system will lose by deploying that HDD, particularly in a mobile environment, e.g., laptop. In addition, EECC is mainly designed for read intensive workload, as to write dominant workload, the HDD may become the bottleneck of the whole system. Finally, the asynchronous writes/updates on parity in EECC can cause potential consistence problems.
Similar to above approaches, our H2-SSD also employs a hierarchical data redundancy scheme including both in-page ECC and across-chips data redundancy. However, different from their methods, H2-SSD uses SLC chips with high P/E performance and life endurance to store the across-chips parity. As a result, the MLC chips will not suffer from any extra writes/updates while maintain a good shock resistance. In addition, synchronous writes/updates on parity in H2-SSD will mitigate the consistence problems.
C. Heterogeneous Flash Memory Techniques
Compared to MLC flash memory, SLC flash memory usually demonstrates much higher P/E performance and life endurance. Therefore, heterogeneous flash memory techniques are proposed to improve both the I/O performance and life endurance of the SSD [20], [21]. However, all existing solutions [20], [21] using heterogeneous flash memory techniques focus on exploring hot spots and exploiting the SLC chips to better service those hot spots.
Similar to those solutions, our H2-SSD also aims at building heterogeneous flash memory based SSDs. However, the SLC chip in H2-SSD is used to absorb extra P/E operations induced by writing/updating the across-chips parity. Because of the outstanding performance of the SLC chips compared to the MLC chips, the across-chips data redundancy will have slight impact on both the overall performance and life endurance of H2-SSD in most cases.

B. General Design of H2-SSD
To build SSDs with high raw BER tolerability thus achieving high life endurance, we propose H2-SSD, which employs both hierarchical data redundancy (in-page and across-chips) and heterogeneous flash memory techniques (SLC and MLC). More specifically, the SLC chips in H2-SSD are deployed as the dedicated parity chips. Though any write request may result in an extra write/update on the corresponding parity, the overall I/O performance and life endurance of H2-SSD may not be seriously affected due to the high P/E performance and life endurance of the dedicated SLC parity chips.
The architecture of H2-SSD is illustrated in Figure 1. In additional to its common functions, the controller of H2SSD is also in-charge of generating across-chips parity and reconstructing segments with uncorrectable bit errors. The original data are first divided into pages and then distributed on the MLC chips. And the parity generated from the original data is distributed on the SLC chips. In H2-SSD, the acrosschips data redundancy is organized according to the logical address, due to in-place updates are not supported by NAND flash memory.
The detailed procedures of handling read and write requests are illustrates in Figure 2(a) and 2(b), respectively. When the controller receives a write request, it splits that write request into sub-requests according to page size and mapping strategy. Then, the controller generates ECC checksums for each data segments, and across-chips parity for each stripe (the basic unit to independently recover data through acrosschips data redundancy). More specifically, for a full stripe write (all bits in a stripe are written/updated), the generation of corresponding across-chips parity will not result in extra reads; otherwise, a portion of data in the same stripe should be read from the SLC and MLC chips to conduct a read-modify write or reconstruction write, which may affect the overall I/O performance under a strict synchronous write strategy. Finally, the data/parity and their checksums are written to the MLC/SLC chips.
The procedures of handling a read request is similar to that of a write request. After splitting the read request into subrequests, the controller fetches data and checksums from the MLC chips. However, the conventional across-chips data redundancy cannot be straightforwardly integrated into H2-SSD, particularly under the consideration of the cost-effectiveness of the overall system. In other words, we need to minimize the cost of deploying the SLC flash memory, since the price per GB of the SLC flash memory is usually much higher than that of the MLC flash memory. In the next two subsections, we will illustrate the detailed design of using a small sized SLC chip to service several large sized MLC chips to make H2-SSD cost-effective.
C. Generation of Parity
In this subsection, we demonstrate using only one small sized SLC chip to service the other large sized MLC chips in our H2-SSD. To simplify our discussion, we make the following definitions and assumptions. First, strip is the basic unit to generate parity. Second, a stripe is the basic unit that can independently recover a strip with uncorrectable bit errors by itself through across-chips data redundancy. Third, all MLC chips have the same capacity. In addition, the capacity of an MLC chip is c1 times as large as that of an SLC chip, where c1 is an integer power of 2.
As we mentioned in Section III-A, the SSDs may be destroyed by the uncorrectable bit errors before the chip failures, particularly under a write intensive workload. Therefore, we deploy the across-chips data redundancy in H2-SSD to reconstruct data strips with uncorrectable bit errors, but not all data on a failed chip, which is different from CR5M [1] and EECC [2]. In other words, we can distribute a portion of strips in the same stripe on one chip, under the assumption that chips rarely fail. More specifically, within one stripe, we need to put the c1 × s data strips on each MLC chip at least, and the s parity strips on the SLC chip, in compensation for the low capacity of the SLC chip, as demonstrated in Figure 3.

D. Strip Size and Life Endurance
In this subsection, we demonstrate our choice on strip sizes, which can significantly influence the write throughput and life endurance of H2-SSD. To simplify the design of H2-SSD, any SLC or MLC page will not span two or more stripes to avoid read or write amplification.
If the s parity strips in the same stripe on the SLC chip span multiple SLC pages, it will result in multiple page writes to write/update those parity strips. Consequentially, any SLC page is composed of s parity strips in the same stripe.
We assume that the page size of the MLC chips is c2 times as large as that of the SLC chip, i.e., an MLC page contains c2 × s data strips, where c2 is also an integer power of 2. To avoid write amplifications on the SLC chip, a stripe should contain max(c1, c2) data strips on each MLC chip. In addition, if c2 is larger than c1, only c1/c2 capacity of the SLC chip is used to store parity logically.
Before using across-chips data redundancy to reconstruct a strip, we should first make sure that strip does have uncorrectable bit errors. As a result, the strip should have its own independent ECC checksum(s), i.e., the strip is composed of one or more entire segments, which is the base unit to generate an ECC checksum as we mentioned in Section II-A. Since the segment size is adjustable as we mentioned in Section II-A, we simply assume that a strip consist of only one segment.
Finally, it is worth to note that if we choose a smaller strip size, i.e., increase s, the strip failure tolerability of H2-SSD will increase correspondingly.

V. TRACE-DRIVEN SIMULATION AND RESULT ANALYSIS
In this section, we first describe the trace-driven simulation to evaluate the degradation of I/O performance induced by deploying across-chips data redundancy and heterogeneous flash memory schemes in H2-SSD, under a strict synchronous parity update strategy.
A. Experiment Methodology
Actually, under a strict synchronous parity update strategy, the SLC chip in H2-SSD may affect the overall I/O performance of H2-SSD, although it far outperforms the MLC chips in P/E performance. Consider the worst case that H2-SSD is under a pure random-write workload. Each write request sent to an MLC chip will incur a write on the SLC chip, i.e., the SLC chip will suffer from much more writes than any MLC chip. And this write amplification on the SLC chip tends to be severe with growth of the MLC chips in H2-SSD. In contrast, the relative extra cost of deploying the SLC chip will decrease with the growth of the number of the MLC chips in H2-SSD. As a result, we conduct experiments to find a proper nubmer of the MLC chips in H2-SSD, which can maintain a slight I/O performance degradation as well as a relative low extra cost of deploying the SLC chip. More specifically, we measure the normalized I/O performance of H2-SSD to the conventional SSD with the same number of MLC chips under four typical workloads with a wide range of read-write ratios. And that normalized I/O performance is reported as a function of the number of MLC chips in H2-SSD or the conventional SSD.
Four real-world block I/O traces are used in our experiments. The F1 and F2 traces are collected from OLTP applications running at two financial institutions. The W3 trace is from a popular search engine. All above three traces are from the SPC-1 benchmark [22]. The D1 is published by Microsoft Corporation [23]. It is collected from a file server accessed by more than 3, 000 users to download various daily. The characteristics of those four traces are summarized in Table IV. The F1 trace is write intensive traces while the W3 trace is almost composed of read requests.
Our simulation is built upon a validated SSD simulator Micro-Add-on [24]. The implementation of H2-SSD is basically according to the general and detailed design described in Section III. The modification contains about 1, 400 lines of C codes.

B. Throughput as a Function of Number of MLC Chips
Figure 5 represents the normalized I/O throughput of H2SSD to the conventional SSD with the same number of the MLC chips under four typical workloads as a function of the number of the MLC chips. The number of MLC chips in H2SSD ranges from 2 to 16. We do not consider the probability of degraded reads in this measurement.
The close-loop experimental results demonstrate that the degradation of I/O throughput will increase with the growth of the number of the MLC chips in H2-SSD, which is consistent with our analysis. More specifically, for those two read-dominated workloads W3 and F2, the degradation of I/O throughput is very slight, e.g., nearly zero for the W3 workload and up to 7.3% for the F2 workload. In contrast, the degradation of I/O throughput under the F1 and D1 workloads is a bit significant, e.g., up to 13.0% and 19.5% respectively. Clearly, the I/O throughput degradation is induced by the competition on the SLC chip to write/update the across-chips parity. Due to the low read-write ratio in the F1 and D1 workloads, the competition on the SLC chip will be more severe thus leading to a high degradation of I/O throughput.
It is worth to note that the degradation of I/O throughput under the D1 workload is even larger than that under the F1 workload which has a lower read-write ratio than the D1 workload. The requests in the D1 workload have a much larger average size compared to the F1 workload, which results in a higher load on each chip under the same queue depth and leading to a more severe competition on the SLC chip.
In addition, when the number of the MLC chips ranges from 7 to 9, the maximum degradation of I/O throughput under all those four workloads is below 10%, and the relative extra cost of deploying the SLC chips ranges from 11.1% to 14.3%. As a result, those configurations are recommended to be applied due to the slight degradation of I/O throughput and low relative extra cost.

Since there are almost no write requests in the W3 workload, the average response time will not increase under that workload.
VI. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
In this section, we first draw the conclusion and then discuss the future work of this paper.
A. Conclusion
To cost-effectively improve the life endurance of MLC NAND flash memory based SSDs, we propose H2-SSD, which adopts both a hierarchical data redundancy scheme (in-page ECC and across-chip data redundancy) and a heterogeneous flash memory technique (SLC and MLC flash memory chips). The across-chip data redundancy is used to reconstruct data with raw bit errors which cannot be corrected by the inpage ECC. And one extra small sized SLC chip is deployed to store parity generated by the across-chip data redundancy scheme. Quantitatively analysis and trace-driven simulation results demonstrate that H2-SSD outperforms the conventional SSDs in the maximum Program/Erase cycles by 23% to 178% with a less than 10% degradation of I/O performance in most cases.
B. Future Work
The future work is listed as follows:
• We will try to solve the high degradation of I/O performance under the workloads with large sized requests.
• Except for the monetary cost, we will further study the extra power cost induced by H2-SSD.
• We will use parameters of various flash chips to comprehensively evaluate the effectiveness of H2SSD.
• The I/O performance of H2-SSD at the end of its lifetime will be evaluated.
• We will compare our work with CR5M [1] and EECC [2] in terms of I/O performance and life endurance.
