An Unnoticed Strong Connection Between Algebraic-Based and Protograph-Based LDPC Codes, Part I: Binary Case and Interpretation

Juane Li1, Keke Liu1, Shu Lin1, Khaled Abdel-Ghaffar1, and William E. Ryan2 1Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, University of California, Davis, CA 95616, USA
2Zeta Associates, 10302 Eaton Place #500, Fairfax, VA 22030
Email: {shulin, kkeliu, jueli, kaghaffar}@ucdavis.edu, ryan@ece.arizona.edu

Abstract—This paper unveils a strong connection between two major constructions of LDPC codes, namely the algebraic-based and the protograph-based constructions. It is shown that, from a graph-theoretic point of view, an algebraic LDPC code whose parity-check matrix is an array of submatrices of the same size over a finite field is a protograph LDPC code. Conversely, from a matrix-theoretic point of view, since the parity-check matrix of a protograph code can be arranged as an array of submatrices of the same size over a finite field and its base graph (or base matrix) can be constructed algebraically, a protograph LDPC code is an algebraic LDPC code. These two major approaches have their advantages and disadvantages in code construction. Unification of these two approaches may lead to better designs and constructions of LDPC codes to achieve good overall performance in terms of error performance in waterfall region, error-floor location and rate of decoding convergence.
This paper is the first part of a series of two parts, PartI and Part-II. Part-I investigates only the binary LDPC codes constructed by the superposition and the protograph-based methods. Part-II explores nonbinary LDPC codes from both superposition and protograph points of view. Also included in Part II are specific superposition constructions of both binary and nonbinary quasi-cyclic LDPC codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
Major methods for constructing LDPC codes [1] can be classified into two general categories: graph-theoreticbased and algebraic-based methods. Most well-known graphtheoretic-based construction methods are progressive edgegrowth (PEG) [2], [3] and protograph-based methods. Algebraic constructions of LDPC codes are mainly based on finite fields, finite geometries, and combinatorial designs.
The algebraic-based method for constructing LDPC codes was first introduced in 2000 [4]–[6]. Since its introduction, various algebraic methods for constructing LDPC codes, binary and nonbinary, have been developed over the last 15 years using mathematical tools, such as finite geometries, finite fields and combinatorial designs. Many classes of algebraic LDPC codes have been constructed [7]-[33] (also see references in these papers). Algebraic construction of LDPC codes has several important ingredients including base matrices, matrixdispersion, and masking. By proper choice and combination of these ingredients, algebraic LDPC codes with excellent overall error performance can be constructed. Algebraic LDPC codes in general have much lower error-floors than other types of LDPC codes. Algebraic LDPC codes that achieve a very

low bit-error-rate, say 10−15, without a visible error-floor have been recently reported [31]–[33]. A unique feature of algebraic construction of LDPC codes is that the construction is systematic without relying on the aid of a computer.
Many algebraic constructions of LDPC codes that appear in the literature evolved from the hybrid construction proposed in 2002 [9] which was renamed the superposition construction in 2003 [10], [11]. The superposition method of code construction starts with a small base matrix B and a set R of sparse matrices of the same size. Then, we replace each nonzero entry in B by a matrix in R following a certain replacement rule [9], [16]. The replacement operation expands the base matrix B into an array H of sparse matrices in R. The null space of H gives an LDPC code. We refer to this matrix replacement of a nonzero entry in B as superposition. The matrices in R are called constituent (or member) matrices and the set R is called the replacement set. In [10], it was shown that the Tanner graph associated with the matrix H is a product of the Tanner graph associated with the base matrix B and the Tanner graphs associated with the member matrices in R.
The protograph-based method for constructing LDPC codes was introduced by Thorpe in 2003 [34]. This method was devised to construct Tanner graphs of LDPC codes of large sizes using smaller well-designed protographs as base Tanner graphs. In the construction, the first step is to choose a bipartite graph of small size, called a protograph, with a nearcapacity decoding threshold [35], [36] as a building block (or as a base). The second step is to make copies of the chosen protograph and permute the edges of copies according to certain rules to connect them into a Tanner graph of larger size. The null space of the adjacency matrix of the resultant Tanner graph gives an LDPC code, called a protograph (or protograph-based) LDPC code. Basically, the second step of this construction is a graph expansion process. Since the introduction of this method for LDPC code construction, it has been extensively investigated over the last 12 years. Impressive theoretical results have been developed and many protographbased LDPC codes with good error-rate performance have been constructed. Since the number of publications on this graph-theoretic code construction method is too large for us to include in this paper, we only give a dozen of them as our references, [34]-[46].

If we look at these two construction methods from the same point of view, say the graph point of view, we will find that there is a strong connection between these two construction methods. In fact, the protograph-based method is a special case of the superposition method.
The main objective of this paper is to link the protographbased method of code construction to the superposition method. This linkage unifies these two powerful methods of LDPC code construction. Hopefully, such a unification will lead to much easier design and construction of protographs (or base matrices) for constructing LDPC codes with good overall performance in terms of error rate in the waterfall region, error-floor location and rate of decoding convergence.
This paper is the first part of a series of two parts, Part-I and Part-II. Part-I investigates the link between the protographbased and the superposition constructions of LDPC codes and explores some basic structures of quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes from both the superposition and the protograph points of view. Only binary LDPC codes are considered in this part. Part-II explores nonbinary LDPC codes from both superposition and protograph points of view.
The rest of this paper (Part-I) is organized as follows. Section II gives some definitions and introduces some concepts which will be used in the later sections. In Section III, we first review the protograph-based method for constructing LDPC codes from the conventional graph point of view and then present a simple algebraic method for constructing protographbased QC-LDPC codes. In Section IV, we first give a review of the superposition method for constructing LDPC codes. Then, we give a graph-theoretic interpretation of the superposition construction of LDPC codes and show that the protographbased construction is actually a special case of the superposition construction. In Section V, we give constructions of binary QC-LDPC codes using the superposition method and show that a QC-LDPC code is a protograph QC-LDPC code and vise versa. Section VI presents a special type of the superposition method for constructing binary QC-LDPC codes. This construction method is based on the dispersing (or expanding) the nonzero entries of an algebraically constructed base matrix over a nonbinay field into binary circulants of the same size. This construction method is called the matrixdispersion construction. The same section also gives the necessary and sufficient conditions on a base matrix whose dispersion results in a Tanner graph with girth at least 6 or 8, respectively. Section VII concludes the paper with some remarks.
II. DEFINITIONS AND CONCEPTS
In this section, we give some definitions and introduce some concepts which will be used in the later sections.
A k × k matrix A over GF(2) is called a regular matrix if every row has the same weight, called row-weight, every

column has the same weight, called column weight, and the row-weight is equal to the column-weight. If the row-weight (or column weight) of a regular matrix A is w, we say that A has weight w. It is clear that a permutation matrix (PM) is regular and has weight one. Two different PMs are said to be mutually disjoint if no two corresponding rows (or two corresponding columns) have their 1-components located at the same position. A k×k regular matrix with weight w is then the sum of w mutually disjoint PMs. It is also clear that two different circulant permutation matrices (CPMs) are mutually disjoint. A ciuclant with weight w is the sum of w mutually disjoint CPMs. The top row of a circulant is referred to as the generator of the circulant.
A matrix for which no two columns (or rows) have more than one location in common with 1-entries is said to satisfy the row-column (RC) constraint [8], [16], [19], [26], [30]. Such a matrix is called an RC-constrained matrix. The Tanner graph of an RC-constrained matrix has girth at least 6 [8], [16], [19], [26], [30]. Two RC-constrained matrices A and B of the same size are said to satisfy the pair-wise RC-constraint if the matrix formed by A and B arranged either in a row-block [AB] or in a column-block [AT BT ]T satisfies the RC-constraint.
III. PROTOGRAPH-BASED CONSTRUCTION OF LDPC CODES
In this section, we first give a review of the protographbased construction from the conventional graph-theoretic point of view. Then, we interpret this construction from the matrixtheoretic point of view and give an algebraic construction technique of protograph-based QC-LDPC codes.
A. The Protograph-Based Construction of LDPC Codes
The protograph-based construction of LDPC codes introduced by Thorpe in 2003 [34] starts with a properly chosen bipartite graph, denoted by Gptg = (V, W, E), of a relatively small size, which consists of a set V = {v0, v1, . . . , vn−1} of n variable nodes (VNs), a set W = {w0, w1, . . . , wm−1} of m check nodes (CNs), and a set E = {(j, i)} of edges where (j, i) denotes the edge that connects the j-th VN vj to the i-th CN wi. Let λ denote the number of edges in E. The next construction step is to take k copies of Gptg. We denote these k copies by Gptg,0, Gptg,1, . . . , Gptg,k−1. For 0 ≤ j < n, we group the copies of the j-th VN vj in the k copies of Gptg into a set of k VNs, denoted by Φj = {v0,j, v1,j . . . , vk−1,j}. The k VNs in Φj are identical to the j-th VN vj of Gptg. We refer to the k VNs in Φj as the type-j VNs. For 0 ≤ i < m, we group the copies of the i-th CN wi in the k copies of Gptg into a set of k CNs, denoted by Ωi = {w0,i, w1,i, . . . , wk−1,i}. The k CNs in Ωi are identical to the i-th CN wi of Gptg. We refer to the k CNs in Ωi as the type-i CNs. The kλ edges in the k copies of Gptg are grouped into λ sets, E0, E1, . . . , Eλ−1, each consisting of k copies of an edge in the edge set E of Gptg. For 0 ≤ t < λ, if the edges in Et are the copies of the edge (j, i) in the edge set E of Gptg, then the edges in Et are said to be type-(j, i) edges. The third construction step is to connect the k copies Gptg,0, Gptg,1, . . . , Gptg,k−1 by permuting

the edges of the individual copies of Gptg among the copies of Gptg. The permutation is carried out under the restriction: for 0 ≤ j < n, 0 ≤ i < m, a type-j VN in Φj can only connect to one of the type-i CNs in Ωi by an edge of type-(j, i). The edge permutations among the copies of Gptg result in a connected bipartite graph, denoted by Gptg(k, k), with kn VNs, km CNs and kλ edges, which has the same degree distributions as the original graph Gptg. (The pair (k, k) in Gb(k, k) indicates that each VN in Gptg is expanded into k VNs in Gptg(k, k) and each CN in Gptg is expanded into k CNs in Gptg(k, k).)
The above copy-and-permute operations expand a small bipartite graph Gptg into a larger bipartite graph Gptg(k, k) by a factor of k. The parameter k is called the expansion factor (or lifting degree). The graph Gptg is called the protograph for the code construction whose adjacency matrix, denoted by Bptg, is an m × n matrix with rows corresponding to the m CNs of Gptg and columns corresponding to the n VNs of Gptg. The adjacency matrix Bptg is called the base matrix for the protograph-based code construction. The adjacency matrix of the expanded graph Gptg(k, k), denoted by Hptg, is an mk × nk matrix. If Hptg is sparse, the null space of Hptg gives an LDPC code Cptg, called a protograph LDPC code. The subscript “ptg” of Hptg, Bptg and Cptg stands for “protograph”. In choosing a protograph Gptg for LDPC code construction, parallel edges between a VN and a CN are
permitted in the protograph, but the edge permutations must be performed in such a way that the expanded graph Gptg(k, k) does not contain parallel edges between a VN and a CN.
The connection of the k VNs of type-j in Φj to the k CNs of type-i in Ωi via edges of type-(j, i) can be specified by a k ×k regular matrix, called the connection matrix. Commonly, the connection matrices for constructing a protograph LDPC code Cptg are circulants. In this case, the parity-check matrix Hptg of a protograph code is an m × n array of circulants and zero matrices of size k × k and the code Cptg given by the null space of Hptg is a QC-LDPC code. If the j-th VN of the protograph Gptg is connected to the i-th CN of Gptg by w parallel edges, with 1 ≤ w ≤ k, then the connection matrix for connecting the k VNs of type-j in Φj to the k CNs of type-i in Ωi via the edges of (j, i)-type is a circulant with weight w.
The performance of a protograph LDPC code depends
on the choice of the protograph, its degree distribution and
permutation of the edges among the copies. Protographs are
generally designed with good decoding thresholds [35], [36]
based on a computer-aided density evolution search procedure.
A good decoding threshold, in general, gives a good waterfall
performance, but not necessarily a low error-floor or a fast
rate of decoding convergence.

If the adjacency matrix Bptg of the protograph Gptg does not contain an integer greater than one, then Hptg is an m × n array of CPMs and/or ZMs of size k ×k. With the above construction of a protograph QC-LDPC code, the graphical process of edge permutation and connection of copies of a protograph is replaced by a simple matrix decomposition, an algebraic approach. It is clear that there are many possible decompositions of the base matrix Bptg, each resulting in a different m × n parity-check array Hptg (i.e., a different composition of circulants). To find the decomposition that optimizes the code performance is under investigation.
The general guidelines to decompose the adjacency matrix Bptg of the protograph Gptg are : (1) each matrix De in the decomposition set {D0, D1, . . . , Dk−1} satisfies the RCconstraint; and (2) any two matrices De and Dj, e = j, satisfy the pair-wise RC-constraint. These general guidelines are taken to prevent short cycles in the Tanner graph Gptg(k, k) of the protograph LDPC code constructed based on the protograph Gptg.
With the above algebraic construction of a protograph-based QC-LDPC code, there is no upper limit on the expansion factor k. Consequently, we can construct a parity-check array Hptg of CPMs (or circulants) and/or ZMs of very large size from a relatively small protograph. However, the expansion factor k must be greater than or equal to the largest integer in the adjacency matrix Bptg of the chosen protograph Gptg.
We can label the edge(s) connecting the j-th VN and the i-th CN of the protograph Gptg with the generator vi,j = (vi,j,0, vi,j,1, . . . , vi,j,k−1) of the constituent circulants Ai,j of Hptg. In this case, vi,j = (vi,j,0, vi,j,1, . . . , vi,j,k−1) is called the vector label of the edge (i, j). The number of nonzero components in vi,j gives the number of the parallel edges connecting the j-th VN and the i-th CN of the protograph Gptg. Using the conventional graphical construction of a protographbased LDPC code, the vector labels of the edges in Gptg gives the precise information of how to permute edges of the copies of the protograph Gptg and connect them into the Tanner graph of the protograph-based QC-LDPC code.

IV. SUPERPOSITION CONSTRUCTION OF LDPC CODES
In this subsection, we briefly review the original superposition method for constructing LDPC codes presented in 2002 [9], [11], [16]. We show that the protograph-based method of LDPC code construction is a special case of the superposition construction method.
Let Bb = [bi,j ]0≤i<m,0≤j<n be a small m × n binary matrix. Let R = {A1, A2, . . . , Ar} be a set of properly chosen k × t sparse matrices over GF(2) with k ≤ t. From Bb and

the member matrices of R, we construct an m × n array Hsp of k × t matrices by replacing each 1-entry in Bb by a member matrix in R and each 0-entry by a k × t zero matrix (ZM). This replacement of entries in Bb by the member matrices in R and/or ZMs expands the matrix Bb into an mk × nt sparse matrix Hsp. As an m × n array, Hsp consists of n column-blocks, labeled by H0, H1, . . . , Hn−1, and m row-blocks, denoted by M0, M1, . . . , Mm−1. Each columnblock Hj consists of t consecutive columns of Hsp and each row-block Mi consists of k consecutive rows of Hsp. The above operation of replacing the entries in Bb by the member matrices in R and/or ZMs is called superposition in [11], [16].
The superposition operation is simply an expansion operation.
The null space of Hsp gives an LDPC code Csp. The subscript “sp” of Hsp and Csp stands for “superposition”. The matrix Bb is called the base matrix for superposition construction and R is referred to as the replacement (or superposition) set.
Let Gb be the Tanner graph of Bb which consists of n VNs and m CNs, labeled by v0, v1, . . . , vn−1 and w0, w1, . . . , wm−1, respectively. The Tanner graph associated with the matrix Hsp, denoted by Gsp(k, t), consists of nt VNs and mk CNs. Partition the nt VNs of Gsp(k, t) into n disjoint sets, denoted by Φ0, Φ1, . . . , Φn−1. For 0 ≤ j < n, Φj consists of t VNs of Gsp(k, t) which correspond to the t columns of the j-th column-block Hj. The t VNs in Φj are referred to as the type-j VNs. Partition the mk CNs of Gsp(k, t) into m disjoint sets, denoted by Ω0, Ω1, . . . , Ωm−1. For 0 ≤ i < m, Ωi consists of k CNs of Gsp(k, t) which correspond to the k rows of the i-th row-block Mi of Hsp. The k CNs in Ωi are referred to as the type-i CNs.
The superposition operation simply expands each VN in Gb into t VNs in Gsp(k, t) and each CN in Gb into k CNs in Gsp(k, t). In Gsp(k, t), the VNs in Φ0, Φ1, . . . , Φn−1 are connected to the CNs in Ω0, Ω1, . . . , Ωm−1 based on the 1entries in the member matrices of R that are used to replace the nonzero entries in Bb. The graph Gsp(k, t) may be viewed as a Tanner graph consisting of n super-VNs, Φ0, Φ1, . . . , Φn−1, and m super-CNs, Ω0, Ω1, . . . , Ωm−1. The graph Gsp(k, t) is an expansion of the Tanner graph Gb of the base matrix Bb.
If the member matrices in R are k ×k regular matrices, then
the superposition construction of LDPC codes is simply the
protograph-based construction of LDPC codes. In this case,
from the graph-theoretical point of view, the superposition
operation is equivalent to taking k copies of the Tanner graph Gb of the base matrix Bb and connecting the copies by the member matrices in R that are used to replace the 1-entries
in Bb. Therefore, the protograph-based construction of LDPC codes is a special case of the superposition construction of LDPC codes for t = k. If there are member matrices in R with weight w greater than one, then the Tanner graph Gb (now a protograph) has parallel edges.
From the graph point of view, the superposition method
is a general protograph-based method for constructing LDPC codes. In this case, the Tanner graph Gb of the base matrix Bb is the protograph for code construction.
For the superposition construction, the member matrices of

the replacement set R are chosen to satisfy the pair-wise RCconstraint. In the replacement process, all the 1-entries in a row or in a column of Bb are replaced by distinct member matrices in R. This replacement rule is called the replacement constraint [9], [16]. This replacement constraint ensures that the Tanner graph associated with the array Hsp obtained by expanding the base matrix Bb using superposition has girth at least 6. Many methods have been proposed for constructing pair-wise RC-constrained matrices in the literature [4]–[31].
CPMs of the same size satisfy the pair-wise RC-constraint. They can be used as the member matrices of the replacement set R for the superposition code construction. They can also be used as building blocks to form matrices of larger size which satisfy the pair-wise RC-constraint [16], [27]. These larger matrices can be used as the member matrices of the replacement set R for superposition construction. In this case, the code constructed using superposition construction is a QCLDPC code.
From the above discussion, we see that the superposition method is a very general algebraic method for constructing LDPC codes. It can be interpreted from either the matrixtheoretic point of view or the graph-theoretic point of view. From the perspective of simplicity, the matrix-theoretic approach to the superposition construction of LDPC codes is preferable in terms of designs of base matrices and the replacement sets of matrices, as will be shown in later sections of this paper (Part-I) and its companion paper (Part-II).
V. SUPERPOSITION CONSTRUCTION OF QC-LDPC CODES
In this section, we consider construction of binary QCLDPC codes using the superposition method and investigate some basic structures of these codes from both the matrixtheoretic and the protograph point of view.
Again, let Bb be an m × n base matrix over GF(2) for superposition construction of LDPC codes. Suppose the member matrices of the replacement set R are sparse binary circulants of the same size k × k, not necessarily of the same weight. VI. SUPERPOSITION CONSTRUCTION OF BINARY QC-LDPC CODES THROUGH CPM-DISPERSION
Over the last 12 years, many algebraic methods have been developed for constructing RC-constrained arrays of CPMs, binary or nonbinary [8]–[31]. These methods basically evolved from the superposition method for constructing LDPC codes introduced in 2002 [9]. The main feature of these constructions is to disperse (or expand) each nonzero entry of a base matrix over a finite field into a binary CPM of certain size. The base matrix is constructed using mathematical tools, such as finite fields, finite geometries and combinatorial designs, and it satisfies certain conditions to ensure that the Tanner graph of the QC-LDPC code constructed based on it has girth at least 6 or 8. All these methods are special types of protographbased methods for constructing QC-LDPC codes. Many codes with excellent performance, especially very low error-floors, have been constructed. QC-LDPC codes with bit-error-rate as low as 10−15 without a visible error-floor have been recently reported in [31]–[33], [47].
An interesting example is the NASA standard (8176, 7165) QC-LDPC code for satellite and space communications [27], [49]. This code can achieve a bit error-rate of 10−14 without a visible error-floor and has been used in several NASA missions including the NASAs Landsat Data Continuity Mission and Interface Region Imaging Spectrograph (IRIS) Solar Observatory. If this code is considered as a protograph LDPC code, its protograph Gptg consists of 2 CNs and 16 VNs. Each VN is connected to a CN with 2 parallel edges. Its base matrix is

A. Binary QC-LDPC Code construction by CPM-Dispersion
Let GF(q) be a finite field with q elements where q is either a prime or a power of a prime. Let k be a factor of q − 1 and k = 1 and β be an element in GF(q) of order k. Then βk = 1 and the elements 1, β, β2, . . . , βk−1 form a cyclic subgroup of GF(q), denoted by G. For 0 ≤ j < k, we represent the element βj by a binary CPM, denoted by A(βj), of size k × k with columns and rows labeled from 0 to k − 1 whose generator has the unit-element “1” of GF(q) as its single nonzero component at the position j. It is clear that the representation of the element βj by the binary CPM A(βj) of size k × k is unique and the mapping between βj and A(βj) is one-to-one. This matrix representation of βj is referred to as the binary CPM-dispersion of βj. Notice that the size of the binary CPM A(βj) is the order of β.
Let Bq = [bi,j ]0≤i<m,0≤j<n be an m×n matrix over GF(q) whose nonzero entries are elements in the cyclic subgroup G = {1, β, β2, . . . , βk−1}. We form an m × n array Hb,qc of binary CPMs and/or ZMs of size k × k from Bq as follows: (1) if bi,j = 0 and bi,j = βli,j , we replace bi,j with its binary CPM-dispersion A(βli,j ); and (2) if bi,j = 0, we replace bi,j with a ZM of size k × k, i.e., A(0) is a k × k ZM. The array Hb,qc is called the binary CPMdispersion of Bq with respect to β. We consider the array Hb,qc as an mk × nk matrix over GF(2). The null space of Hb,qc gives a binary QC-LDPC code Cb,qc of length nk, called a binary CPM-QC-LDPC code. The above method for constructing binary CPM-QC-LDPC codes is called the CPMdispersion (CPM-D) construction. The CPM-D construction is basically a superposition construction with a deterministic CPM replacement for each nonzero entry in the base matrix Bq. Notice that the base matrix for superposition construction is now a matrix over a finite field.
Let Gq denote the Tanner graph of Bq. The edges of Gq are labeled by their corresponding nonzero entries in Bq. The binary CPM-dispersion of the nonzero entries in Bq expands the Tanner graph Gq of Bq by a factor of k into a large bipartite graph, denoted by Gb,qc(k, k), with nk VNs and mk CNs. Each VN in Gq is expanded into k VNs in Gb,qc(k, k) and each CN in Gq is expanded into k CNs in Gb,qc(k, k).
In terms of protograph-based code construction, Gq is used as the protograph. Gq is duplicated k times. The edges of the copies of Gq are permuted to connect the copies into a large Tanner graph based on the constituent CPMs in the array Hb,qc

and the deterministic CPM replacement rules. In case we want to disperse each nonzero entry bi,j of the
base matrix Bq into a binary CPM of larger size, say q−1, this can be done as follows. We first express each nonzero entry bi,j of Bq as a power of a primitive element α of GF(q), say bi,j = αli,j with 0 ≤ li,j < q − 1. Then, we form a (q − 1) × (q − 1) binary CPM A(αli,j ), with columns and rows labeled from 0 to q − 2, whose generator has its single 1-entry at the location li,j. A(αli,j ) is the (q − 1) × (q − 1) CPM-dispersion of αli,j . With this dispersion of the nonzero entries in Bq, we obtain an m×n array Hb⋆,qc of CPMs and/or ZMs of size (q − 1) × (q − 1). The null space of Hb⋆,qc gives a CPM-QC-LDPC code of length n(q − 1).
The above CPM-D construction of a binary CPM-QC-LDPC code reduces to the construction of the base matrix Bq over GF(q). For such a CPM-QC-LDPC code, the girth of the Tanner graph of the code is related to the choice of the base matrix Bq. The following two theorems give the necessary and sufficient conditions on the base matrix Bq for the Tanner graph of a CPM-QC-LDPC code to have girth at least 6 or 8, respectively. The two theorems were proved in [29], [30].
Theorem 1. Let Bq be the base matrix of a binary CPMQC-LDPC code Cb,qc whose parity-check matrix Hb,qc is the binary CPM-dispersion of Bq. A necessary and sufficient condition for the Tanner graph of Cb,qc to have girth at least 6 is that every 2 × 2 submatrix in the base matrix Bq contains at least one zero entry or is non-singular.
Theorem 2. A necessary and sufficient condition for the Tanner graph of a binary CPM-QC-LDPC code Cb,qc with base matrix Bq to have girth at least 8 is that no 2×2 or 3×3 submatrix in the base matrix Bq has two identical nonzero terms in its determinant expansion.
For convenience, the conditions given in Theorems 1 and 2 are called the 2×2 and 3×3 submatrices (SM) constraints (the 2 × 2 and 3 × 3 SM-constraints), respectively. In the CPM-D construction of a binary CPM-QC-LDPC code, the 2 × 2 and 3 × 3 SM-constraints are used to construct a base matrix.

B. Masking
There are many algebraic methods for constructing base matrices that satisfy the 2×2 SM-constraint given in Theorem 1. The Tanner graph associated with the arrays obtained by CPM-dispersions of these base matrices have girth at least 6. The masking technique presented in [18], [19], [27], [31] can be performed on a 2×2 SM-constrained base matrix to obtain a new base matrix which also satisfies the 3 × 3 SM-constraint. Then, the Tanner graph associated with the CPM-dispersion of the base matrix after masking has girth at least 8. Properly masking a base matrix not only increases the girth but also significantly reduces the number of short cycles of the Tanner graph associated with the array obtained by CPM-dispersion of the unmasked base matrix.

VII. CONCLUSION AND REMARKS
In this paper, we unveiled a strong connection between an algebraic method of LDPC code construction, namely superposition construction proposed in 2002 [9], and the protographbased method of LDPC code construction proposed in 2003 by Thorpe [34]. We showed that, from the graph point of view, the protograph-based code construction method is a special case of the superposition code construction method. We also showed that many algebraic methods for constructing LDPC codes proposed after 2002 actually evolved from the superposition code construction method. Our finding puts the two powerful categories of LDPC code constructions, namely the algebraic method and the protograph-based method, under a single framework. The unification of these two construction approaches may lead to better designs and constructions of LDPC codes to achieve good overall performance in terms of error performance in waterfall region, error-floor location and rate of decoding convergence.
Also presented in this paper is a simple algebraic method for constructing protograph QC-LDPC codes without going through the graphical process of permuting the edges among the copies of a protograph.

This paper is the first part of a series of two parts, Part-I and Part-II. In Part-II of this series, we explore nonbinary LDPC codes from both the superposition and the protograph points of view. Specific superposition constructions of both binary and nonbinary QC-LDPC codes are presented.
