Approaching the DT Bound Using Linear Codes in the Short Blocklength Regime
Luis Salamanca, Juan José Murillo-Fuentes, Pablo M. Olmos, Fernando Pérez-Cruz, and Sergio Verdú

Abstract—The dependence-testing (DT) bound is one of the strongest achievability bounds for the binary erasure channel (BEC) in the finite block length regime. In this paper, we show that maximum likelihood decoded regular low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes with at least 5 ones per column almost achieve the DT bound. Specifically, using quasi-regular LDPC codes with block length of 256 bits, we achieve a rate that is less than 1% away from the rate predicted by the DT bound for a word error rate below 10−3. The results also indicate that the maximum-likelihood solution is computationally feasible for decoding block codes over the BEC with several hundred bits.
Index Terms—LDPC codes, finite blocklength regime, ML decoding, binary erasure channel, random coding.
I. INTRODUCTION
L OW-DENSITY parity-check (LDPC) codes [1], [2] approach capacity under belief propagation (BP) decoding over binary memoryless symmetric channels when the degree distribution is appropriately optimized [3], [4]. Nevertheless, the BP performance of irregular LDPC codes can be severely degraded in the finite blocklength regime [5]. LDPC convolutional codes [6] also approach channel capacity under BP decoding, but they require very long codewords [7], because their convolutional-like structure imposes a significant rateloss for short blocklengths. Sparse codes that approach channel capacity under maximum likelihood (ML) decoding are structurally much simpler. For instance, under ML decoding over the binary erasure channel (BEC), a rate-1/2 LDPC ensemble with 3 ones per column asymptotically decodes a fraction of up to 0.48815, while for a code with 5 ones per column this

In [8] bounds on the maximum achievable coding rate in the finite-length regime are presented. These bounds can be regarded as the extension of the Shannon’s coding theorem when the number of channel uses is fixed. In this paper, we investigate the performance of block codes in the finite blocklength regime under ML decoding with respect to the bounds in [8]. ML decoding for the BEC can be achieved via Gaussian elimination and there are several efficient implementations for ML decoding of LDPC codes that, by exploiting the sparsity of the code, significantly reduce the multiplicative factor associated to the cubic complexity term, see [9] and the references therein. For code lengths below a few hundred bits, the leading complexity term is quadratic in the blocklength for most of these implementations.
In this paper, we measure the residual word error rate (WER) for quasi-regular LDPC codes, irregular LDPC codes optimized to approach capacity, and BCH codes over the BEC, when we decode using the generalized tree-structured expectation propagation (GTEP) algorithm, which efficiently computes the ML solution [9]. By quasi-regular LDPC codes, we refer to LDPC codes where, after choosing the variable nodes’ degree, we adjust the code rate using parity-check nodes with degree either r or r − 1. We notice that quasi-regular LDPC codes with 5 ones per column lie just below the dependence-testing (DT) achievability bound [8] for the BEC. Also, increasing the number of ones per column does not have a practical impact on the achievable rates, while it significantly increases the computational complexity of the decoder. In addition, BCH codes present a similar behavior to quasi-regular LDPC codes, although their rates are by design less flexible, and irregular LDPC codes present a poor performance for finite blocklengths under ML decoding, as they do for BP decoding [5]. This should not come as a surprise, as irregular LDPC codes need long blocklengths to present their near-capacity properties.
To sum up, this paper shows that quasi-regular LDPC channel coding in conjunction with ML decoding is almost-optimal for the BEC. This result is not only theoretically interesting, but also practically relevant in the field of erasure correcting codes, which are widely used in the link layer of many communication standards, and also to resolve conflicts in random medium access control protocols [10].

We say a variable (or parity-check) has a degree (or r), if there are (or r) ones in its column (or row) of the parity-check matrix. V. DISCUSSION
Efficient ML algorithms for decoding sparse linear block codes over the BEC have been known for some time [9], [22]– [24], but to the best of our knowledge their performances have never been compared to the achievability bounds. In this paper, we have shown that quasi-regular LDPC codes are close to the DT achievability bound for the BEC and we have found one specific coding scheme for which the achieved rate is less than 1% away from the rate predicted by the DT bound and 3–5% away from the converse. Our proposal is based on linear LDPC codes that have polynomial encoding and decoding complexity. While general nonlinear code constructions might yield better performance (even above the DT bound as it is a random coding bound), such a performance improvement would quite likely not be significant since the DT bound and its converse are quite tight.
