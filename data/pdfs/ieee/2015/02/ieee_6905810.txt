Design and Analysis of Root-Protograph LDPC Codes for Non-Ergodic Block-Fading Channels
Yi Fang, Guoan Bi, Senior Member, IEEE, and Yong Liang Guan, Member, IEEE

Abstract—We investigate the performance of the protograph low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes over Nakagami blockfading (BF) channels with multiple receive antennas. Using the modified protograph extrinsic information transfer (PEXIT) algorithm, we observe that the existing protograph LDPC codes, which have been shown to possess excellent error performance over additive white Gaussian noise channels, cannot perform well over non-ergodic BF channels. To address this problem, a simple design scheme is proposed to construct three new root-protograph (RP) LDPC codes, i.e., the regular RP code, the improved RP1 (IRP1) code, and the IRP2 code. The convergence analysis, error-performance analysis, and simulated results show that the three proposed RP codes outperform the existing protograph LDPC codes and regular quasi-cyclic LDPC code. Furthermore, the IRP1 code and the IRP2 code are superior to the regular RP code and the regular root-LDPC code and exhibit outage-limitapproaching error performance. Consequently, these two proposed IRP codes appear to be better alternatives as compared to other error-correction codes for slowly-varying wireless communication systems.
Index Terms—Extrinsic information transfer (EXIT), multiple receive antennas, non-ergodic block-fading (BF) channel, outage region, protograph low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
T HE block-fading (BF) channel has been firstly proposed in [1], [2] so as to model slowly-varying transmission scenarios, where the channel fading gains remain constant on each fading block and take different independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) random variables (RVs) on different fading blocks. This model has attracted much attention in recent years since it is a realistic and simple channel model that captures the fundamental characteristics of a number of practical wireless communication systems, such as orthogonal frequency division multiplexing (OFDM) systems and free-space optical systems [3]. In practical applications, the number of independent fading blocks is always extremely limited. For instance, only a few groups of sub-carriers can be considered as independent in the OFDM systems since there is a high degree of frequency correlation. In summary, the BF channel can be treated as a useful model to develop the design scheme of error-correction codes (ECCs).

As a critical technique in wireless communication systems, ECCs can be exploited to significantly enhance the reliability of signal transmission over BF channels. However, the coding design in such scenarios becomes a challenging problem, which is quite different from that over additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) and ergodic fading (i.e., fast-fading) channels [4]. The Shannon capacity of the BF channel is zero because there is an irreducible probability that the decoder makes an error [1], [2]. As a result, one can only use the outage probability to determine the fundamental limit which cannot be exceeded by the word error rate (WER) of any coding scheme [1], [2]. The capacityapproaching LDPC codes in AWGN [5] and ergodic fading channels [6] cannot possess outstanding performance in a BF channel. Recently, a significant amount of effort has been made to design and analyze the ECCs in BF channels [4], [7]–[17]. In particular, a family of structured low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, namely root-LDPC codes, has been proposed in [11] to not only achieve full diversity but also perform close to the outage limit with iterative decoding algorithm in such channels. Additionally, the ECCs have been optimized for multiple-input–multiple-output (MIMO) BF channels [10], [14].
However, the best-performing root-LDPC code in [11] is irregular and hence suffers from the drawback of nonlinear encoding. To reduce the encoding complexity, the quasicyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes have been proposed [18], [19]. Besides, as one type of multi-edge-type LDPC codes, the protograph LDPC code has emerged as a promising coding scheme because of its excellent error performance and low complexity [20]–[23]. One classical protograph LDPC code, namely the accumulate-repeat-by-4-jagged-accumulate (AR4JA) code, which possesses a simple structure to realize linear encoding and decoding, has been investigated in [21], [22]. Employing the concept of generalized LDPC codes [24], the Hamming constraint nodes can be easily inserted into the protographs to form the powerful Hamming-doped protograph (HD-protograph) LDPC codes [25], [26]. Motivated by the aforementioned advantages, this type of code has been applied to a variety of communication systems, e.g., space communication systems [20]–[22], data storage systems [27], [28] and wireless communication systems [16], [29]–[31]. Moreover, the protograph EXIT (PEXIT) algorithm has been proposed for analyzing the convergence performance of protograph LDPC codes in such scenarios [16], [27], [30], [32].

conduct an insightful study on the performance of protograph LDPC codes over Nakagami single-input–multiple-output BF (SIMO-BF) channels mainly due to the fact that fading statistics in practical wireless communications are more suitably described by a Nakagami distribution [33], [34]. We firstly analyze the performance of three existing protograph codes, i.e., the HD-protograph code, AR4JA code, and regular protograph code, utilizing the modified PEXIT algorithm. The results suggest that these three types of protograph codes possess poor performance in BF channels. To overcome this weakness, we propose a simple design scheme and hence construct three new types of RP codes, including one regular RP code and two improved RP (IRP) codes. Both the theoretical analyses and simulated results illustrate that all three new RP codes outperform the existing protograph codes and the QC-LDPC code in terms of convergence and error performance. Further, the two IRP codes outperform the regular RP code, which produces similar performance as the regular root-LDPC code, and approach the outage limit. Since the two IRP codes not only inherit the advantages of conventional protograph codes, i.e., low encoding and decoding complexities, but also have excellent error performance, they can become very good candidates for use in slowly-varying fading channels.

In Section III, we propose the RP codes and elaborate how to use the modified PEXIT algorithm for this type of code. We also develop the analytical error performance of the RP codes. In Section IV, the performance of the conventional protograph LDPC codes is analyzed and the design of RP codes is proposed. The numerical results are shown in Section V, and the concluding remarks are given in Section VI.

The main difference between our proposed modified PEXIT algorithm and the ones in [16], [30] are described as below: for the protograph of a RP code, the VNs corresponding to the information bits, i.e., vj ∈ Vi, are clearly distinguished from those corresponding to the parity bits, i.e., vj ∈ Vp, Vp = Vp1 Vp2. In the proposed algorithm, we mainly trace the convergence behavior of the VNs corresponding to the information bits so as to attain the SNR outage boundary. It is because only the information bits, which are related to the error performance, are protected with full diversity.

• Given a fixed Eb/N0, NA different fading–threshold pairs (α1,th, α2,th) can be calculated, and hence another version

of the outage boundary (called the fading outage boundary), which describes the relationship between α1,th and α2,th, is further obtained, i.e.,

We now propose a general design scheme for the RP codes with M ≥ 3, i.e., at least three CNs. It has been shown that degree-2 VNs help producing good error performance in the waterfall region but too many degree-2 VNs will lead to an error floor in the high-SNR region over ergodic channels [5], [6]. Accordingly, a proper fraction of degree-2 VNs in a protograph can reduce the ergodic threshold. Additionally, the precoding structure (i.e., degree-1 VN) may bring a precoding gain to a protograph and thus improve the ergodic threshold [21], [22]. Based on the aforementioned discussion and the properties of the outage-limit-approaching protograph codes, the design scheme of the RP codes is proposed as follows.

1) Keep a certain fraction of degree-2 VNs in the RP code. 2) Add a precoding structure, i.e., a degree-1 VN, into the
RP code. 3) Select the base matrix with the lowest outage re-

V. NUMERICAL RESULTS AND DISCUSSIONS
In this section, we compare the performance of the proposed rate-1/2 RP codes with three existing protograph codes (i.e., the HD-protograph code [26], AR4JA code [21], and regular protograph code [21]) and two conventional LDPC codes (i.e., the regular QC-LDPC code2 [18] and regular root-LDPC code [11]). The channels being considered are the Nakagami SISO-BF channel and SIMO-BF channel. Unless otherwise stated, in the simulations, the transmitted codeword length (i.e., the codeword length except the punctured bits) is assumed to N = 2048 (N = N if there is no punctured bit). The standard BP decoder performs a maximum of 100 iterations for each codeword.
A. Convergence Speed
We firstly study the convergence behavior of the codes in a Nakagami SISO-BF channel with parameters NR = 1,

Fig. 6. Average number of BP iterations required to decode each codeword in a Nakagami SISO-BF channel. The parameters used are L = 2, NR = 1, m1,l = 1, R = 1/2, N = 2048, and Eb/N0 = 12 dB.
m1,l = 1, and Eb/N0 = 12 dB. Using Monte Carlo simulations, we evaluate the average number of BP iterations for each decoded codeword and show the results in Fig. 6. As observed, the average numbers of BP iterations of the proposed RP codes are smaller than those of other three protograph codes (i.e., the HD-protograph code,3 the AR4JA code, and the regular protograph code) and the regular QC-LDPC code—the average numbers of BP iterations of the regular RP code, the IRP1 code, and the IRP2 code are all less than 10.5 while those of the HDprotograph code, the AR4JA code, the regular protograph code, and the regular QC-LDPC code are 19.07, 13.25, 12.58 and 12.60, respectively. Also, the regular RP code has almost the same average number of BP iterations as the regular root-LDPC code. Accordingly, the two proposed IRP codes possess faster convergence speeds as compared to all the existing protograph codes and conventional LDPC codes, while the regular RP code possesses similar convergence speed as the regular root-LDPC code. Further, the convergence performance of IRP2 code is the best among the three RP codes. These observations are highly consistent with the outage-region analysis in Section IV.
