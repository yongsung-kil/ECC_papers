Joint Weighted Bit-Flipping Decoder for use in Diversity Network Coding
Heather King and Mark F. Flanagan School of Electrical, Electronic and Communications Engineering
University College Dublin Ireland
Email: heather.king@ucdconnect.ie, mark.flanagan@ieee.org

Abstract—A new joint message-passing decoder is presented for use in a two-user cooperative wireless network. This decoder is designed for use with the diversity network coding scheme of Xiao et al. [1], which uses nonbinary network codes together with channel coding to achieve high diversity gains. The proposed joint decoder, together with appropriate modifications to the cooperative protocol of [1], significantly reduces the required implementation complexity both at the relay nodes and at the receiver. The destination’s joint decoder structure consists of two parallel weighted bit flipping (PWBF) decoders which exchange extrinsic soft/hard information regarding the two users’ packets. It is demonstrated through simulations that the proposed joint decoder achieves not only the same diversity gain as [1], but also approximately 5dB of additional coding gain. It is also notable that a significant improvement in system performance can be achieved after only two joint decoding iterations.
I. INTRODUCTION
Cooperative communications is a widely used method to combat the effect of fading in wireless networks, where users in the network can act as partners to transmit each other’s information. This generates a virtual multiple-antenna transmitter, thus facilitating transmit diversity. Transmit diversity usually requires more than one antenna at the transmitter, however many wireless devices are limited by size, hardware restrictions etc, to one antenna. Cooperative communications enables users in a multi-user network to share their antennas and act as relays nodes to transmit multiple copies of the same information to the receiver [2]-[3].
A network coding approach to achieving diversity in cooperative communications is presented in [4], where channel codes are superimposed over finite fields. This novel approach achieves substantial coding gain over other cooperative diversity techniques, however, the technique is hard to generalize for cooperative networks involving more than two users. Xiao and Skoglund presented an effective new method for cooperative wireless networks using non-binary network coding in [1], which can be readily adapted for the case of multiple users. These so-called “diversity network codes” are designed over finite fields such that the base station (BS) can extract the information of all users from a minimum set of received signals. It is shown that this form of network coding has substantial improvements in error rate performance when compared to schemes based on binary codes or schemes

which do not use network coding. The scheme is shown to achieve a diversity order of 3 for the two-user scenario, and significant improvements in error rate performance, especially when analyzed at medium-to-high signal-to-noise ratios (SNRs). However, the scheme has some drawbacks due to implementation complexity at both the receiver and the relay nodes. For the encoding and transmission process, each user first transmits to the BS and to each other using low density parity check (LDPC) codes [5]. Once the users have received their neighbor’s information, they act as relay nodes and must decode the received codeword to obtain the information bits only. Network coding is then performed over GF(4) and the packet is re-encoded using LDPC codes for transmission. A generalization of the diversity network codes from [1] is presented in [6], where generalized-dynamic network codes are used to increase the diversity order of cooperative wireless communications systems, without sacrificing the system’s rate.
In this paper, we propose a joint decoding scheme at the destination for a protocol similar to that presented in [1]. The joint message-passing decoding system reduces the number of required decoders at the destination, as it requires only one decoder per user. This greatly reduces the required implementation complexity at the receiving BS. For our transmission protocol, the two users broadcast their LDPCcoded information packets to the BS and to each other. Each relay node decodes the received packet and then performs network coding over the entire codeword for transmission. This eliminates the need to re-encode the packet using LDPC codes. In [1], the BS receives information packets as well as network coded packets, and requires an LDPC decoder for each of the received packets. For the two-user case in [1], four packets are received and four separate decoders are needed, hence two more decoders are required at the BS than the proposed joint decoder. We show through simulation results that our new joint decoder additionally achieves a diversity order of 3 as well, and also has significant improvements in error rate performance over [1].

In Fig. 3, we present simulation results for the new joint decoder and compare them with the decoding strategy given in [1]. We use Type-II Euclidean Geometry LDPC codes as channel codes [9], due to their very good performance under PWBF decoding. Independent fading for successive codewords of the same channels is assumed and BPSK modulation is used for transmission. The codes have 781 information bits and 1023 coded bits. For the binary and GF(4) mapping, an extra known bit is added to the codeword length, making 1024 bits. This allows for even pairing of bits for use in the binary to GF(4) symbol mapping. Once the mapping has been computed, the 1024th bit is dropped from any hard decisions zi.
From Fig. 3 it can be seen that the proposed joint decoder achieves the same diversity order of 3, as does the scheme of [1], but with a ~5dB improvement in error rate performance. This gain is due to the fact that in the strategy proposed in [1], the destination’s decoders operate independently and not cooperatively. For the system proposed in [1], if two or more of the received packets are successfully decoded at the destination then the frame error rate (FER) can be considered to be 0, as both of the original information packets U1 and U2 can be obtained. If only one packet is successfully decoded, the FER depends on which packet this is: if it is one of the channel coded information packets then the FER can be considered to be equal to 0.5, as half of the information packets have been recovered. However if the successfully decoded packet is one of the two network coded packets, then neither of the information packets can be retrieved and the FER can be taken to be 1.
In Fig. 4, we show simulation results for different numbers of iterations of the joint decoding scheme (here an iteration is taken to mean the use of one PWBF decoder, followed by probabilistic processing to yield new information for use in the other decoder i.e. each iteration involves message passing from only one decoder to the other). With zero iterations, the information packets are PWBF decoded, and no message passing between the two decoders occurs, hence no diversity gain is achieved. With one iteration, C1 is PWBF decoded and used to obtain new probabilities for use in the decoder for C2, however the decoder for User 1 receives no new extrinsic information. With two iterations, the decoder for User 1 updates its input L1,in using the two new LLR values provided by the probabilistic processing of Cˆ 2 together with L3 and L4. However the decoder for User 2 receives new extrinsic information for a second time, hence for each iteration l, C2 decodes using new information l times, while C1 decodes using new information l − 1 times.
It can be seen from Fig. 3 and Fig. 4 that when the number of iterations is greater than 1, a diversity order of 3 is achieved, and that three iterations yields a coding gain of ~5dB over [1]. IV. CONCLUSIONS
We have presented a new joint message-passing decoding scheme for use in a two-user cooperative network, which is capable of delivering the diversity gains of the network coding scheme presented in [1] with a low implementation complexity. The new scheme uses network coding with LDPC codes for transmission, similar to the transmission protocol in [1], but with reduced complexity at the relay nodes as well as at the destination.
