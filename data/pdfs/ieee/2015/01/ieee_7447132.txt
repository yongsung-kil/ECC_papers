Belief-Propagation Reconstruction for Compressed
Sensing: Quantization vs. Gaussian Approximation

Mengke Lian and Henry D. Pfister Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, Duke University

Abstract—This work considers the compressed sensing (CS) of i.i.d. signals with sparse measurement matrices and beliefpropagation (BP) reconstruction. In general, BP reconstruction for CS requires the passing of messages that are distributions over the real numbers. To implement this in practice, one typically uses either quantized distributions or a Gaussian approximation. In this work, we use density evolution to compare the reconstruction performance of these two methods. Since the reconstruction performance depends on the signal realization, this analysis makes use of a novel change of variables to analyze the performance for a typical signal. Simulation results are provided to support the results.
Index Terms—compressed sensing, density evolution, LDPC codes, message-passing algorithms
I. INTRODUCTION
Compressed sensing (CS) is an area of signal processing and statistics that first emerged in the 1990’s and then became very active in the mid-2000’s [1]–[7]. The main idea is that many signals can be reconstructed and/or processed using many fewer measurements than predicted by a naive application of Nyquist’s theorem. There are now too many papers on CS to list here and, for an overview, we direct readers to the recent textbook [8].
This paper uses the standard CS model where one receives m linear measurements of an n-dimensional i.i.d. random signal vector. The implied reconstruction problem is to estimate the true signal from these measurements. To make this problem well-posed, one generally assumes that the signal is sparse (e.g., the amplitude distribution has a point mass at 0).
In this paper, we focus on belief-propagation (BP) reconstruction of the unknown signal using either quantized densities [9] or a Gaussian approximation [10]. Our main contribution is the introduction of a density-evolution analysis for the reconstruction process that applies to sparse random measurement matrices. Previous work has focused on the state-evolution recursion which can be derived either from the large-sparse-system limit [10] or using the approximate message passing (AMP) framework with dense measurement matrices [11], [12]. By considering the sparse case directly, we can evaluate the effect of the Gaussian approximation for sparse measurement matrices.

II. BACKGROUND
A. Compressed Sensing Problem
Consider the CS problem defined in Problem 2 and assume that the measurement noise W ∼ N (0, σW 2 ) is Gaussian. If fX is positive and m < n, then there are multiple signal vectors that give rise to the same measurements. Thus, additional information (e.g., the prior fX or some sparsity assumption on x) is required to identify a unique solution for x. A common sparsity assumption is that 1−ε fraction of the entries in the true signal are exactly zero. This is similar to assuming that the signal values are drawn from the BernoulliGaussian prior
x fX (x) = (1 − ε)δ0(x) + εφ σ ,
where δ0(x) is the Dirac delta function, φ(x) is the density function of a standard Gaussian and σ2 is the variance of the continuous component. The prior fX (x) implies that every sample drawn from it has probability 1 − ε to be zero, and probability ε to be a Gaussian random variable with zeromean and variance σ2. Hence, for the maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimator in (2), the prior fX (x) provides a partial ordering on the space of possible signals x.
B. Sparse Measurement Matrix
Implementing the exact belief-propagation algorithm for the CS problem with continuous signals is infeasible because the messages are probability measures which cannot be compactly parameterized. There are two natural solutions to this problem: quantization and approximation.
When storing a general probability distribution on a computer, it is natural to use quantization and truncation. The main drawback of this approach is that quantization error decays slowly with the complexity and it can be difficult to deal with the boundary issues caused by truncation.
Alternatively, one can use a Gaussian approximation for all messages. Compared with quantization, this provides a much more compact parameterization of the messages and a continuous approximation of the mean. The drawback is that it often does not capture the general shape of the probability measure and it neglects entirely the possibility of bimodal distributions. . For the CS problem, using a Gaussian approximation for edge messages yields gives the relaxed

belief propagation (RBP) described in [10]. In the largesparse-system limit, this approach can be rigorously analyzed using density evolution. For dense matrices, the approximate message passing (AMP) approach can be used to reduce complexity and allow rigorous analysis [11], [12], [17]. In the proper setting, the performance analysis of RBP and AMP both lead to the same state evolution recursion for variance of the signal estimates.
Using a sparse Φ can reduce the computational complexity of BP reconstruction. In this paper, we choose a sparse random measurement matrix Φ drawn from the (wc, wr)regular Gallager-Rademacher ensemble. The locations of non-zero entries in Φ are determined by a random matrix from Gallager’s (wc, wr)-regular ensemble of LDPC codes and values of the non-zero entries are drawn i.i.d. from the Rademacher distribution (i.e., uniform on {−1, 1}). The Rademacher distribution is used for the values because it works well and it avoids some technical difficulties associated with scaling the support of quantized measures.
C. Factor Graphs and Belief Propagation
A factor graph is a bipartite graph that defines the conditional independence structure of a set of random variables each taking values in X . Such graphs are widely used to represent the setup of statistical inference problems. In practice, they are popular because the BP algorithm can be used to efficiently solve (or approximate) marginal inference problems associated with factor graphs [18], [19].
A factor graph (V ∪ F, E) is defined by a set of variable nodes V, a set of factor nodes F, and a set of edges E ⊆ V × F that connect variables and factors. For a variable node i ∈ V or a factor node a ∈ F, let ∂ denote the neighborhood operator defined by
∂i = {b ∈ F | (i, b) ∈ E}
∂a = {j ∈ V | (j, a) ∈ E} .
BP operates by passing messages, µi→a(x) and µˆa→i(x), between variable and factor nodes. These messages are functions that map X to R and define distributions over X . For the CS problem, the signal values live in the set X = R. Each iteration contains two phases. First the variables nodes pass messages to all adjacent factor nodes, then the factor nodes pass messages to all adjacent variable nodes.
If the factor graph is a tree, then the messages become constant after sufficiently many iterations and the algorithm can compute the true marginals. If the factor graph is loopy, then the the sequence of messages may or may not converge. Even if it does converge, the final results will only be approximations of the true marginals. In practice, a finite number of iterations are performed and damping is used to avoid oscillation and improve convergence. In the final step, the marginal of each variable is computed by combining all messages that enter the variable node. For the CS problem, the signal estimate is chosen either to be the mean or the mode of the marginal.

Extending DE to CS requires that several difficulties be overcome. In particular, one must deal with the continuous alphabet X = R and with the fact that the reconstruction performance depends on the signal. To overcome the first problem, one can represent distributions over messages numerically by using the empirical distribution determined by a large random sample of messages. This results in a sequential Monte Carlo method, called population dynamics in the statistical physics literature, that is similar to particle filtering [19, p. 323]. Part of the challenge still remains though because each message a real function. Thus, it is also necessary to represent functions over R efficiently. The second problem is that the y values depend on the random x values and these are not easy to track directly in DE. In this paper, we adapt DE to analyze compressed sensing with sparse measurement matrices and BP reconstruction.

D. Density Evolution
In channel coding, density evolution (DE) is a method of analyzing the average performance of the BP decoding algorithm in the large-system (or infinite block-length) limit. It is based on recursively tracking the distribution of messages passed during each BP decoding iteration, in the large-system limit. The key point is that, in this limit, the input messages used to compute an output message are all statistically independent.
For example, consider a binary LDPC code on the binary erasure channel (BEC). In this case, a bit is either known to be 0, known to be 1, or it is erased (i.e., it has a equal probability of being 0 or 1). Thus, there are 3 types of messages and the fraction of messages of each type can be represented by 3 real numbers1. In the large-system limit, these numbers concentrate around their expected values and these expected values satisfy a simple DE recursion. This allows one to predict the precise performance of the decoder, in the large system limit, after any fixed number of iterations. If the expected fraction of erasures vanishes as the number of iterations increases, then decoding is successful with high probability.

As mentioned before, exact implementation of beliefpropagation for compressed sensing requires messages are probability distribution over R. In order to implement this in practice, either quantization or Gaussian approximation is typically used [9], [10].
For quantization, there are two key issues: range and resolution. Theoretically, high precision comes requires both a wide range and high resolution. After quantization, the resulting PMF’s can have large support and the complexity can be prohibitive (e.g., an FFT used for convolution at the factor node).
On the other hand, low resolution implies large quantization error and a narrow range can cause problems with time-domain aliasing because the FFT is used to implement circular convolution. Computing the exact linear convolution requires a prohibitive amount zero padding because the support is propo√rtional to wr − 1 but the effective support grows only like wr − 1 due to the central limit theorem.
To simplify implementation, we also assume that the PMF after quantization has a regularly-spaced support set that has odd cardinality and is symmetric around x = 0. This implies that the support set remains fixed after convolution followed by truncation of the tail. Based on these constraints, the support set for our simulations and analysis was chosen to cover the range [−10σ, 10σ] with 243 equally spaced quantization points.

The reconstruction experiments for simulation and density evolution are performed using (3, 6), (5, 10), (7, 14) Gallager-Rademacher ensembles with noise variance σW 2 = 0.25. For the reconstruction simulation, the length of signal is n = 5000. For the density evolution results, the sample size is N = 500. All iterative algorithms are stopped if T = 200 iterations have been conducted or less than a 1% change is made in 20 iterations.
For the Bernoulli-Gaussian priors, the variance of large components is σ2 = 100, while the sparsity density ε ranges from 0.01 to 0.5 with a step-size of 0.01. For each ensemble (wc, wr) and each sparsity density ε, the value on the curve is obtained by average MSE’s of 100 reconstructed signals for decoder and 100 trials for density evolution.
Figures III-F and 2 show that the density evolution curves

match the decoder simulation curves for degree distributions (3, 6) and (5, 10). For (7, 14) case, a small deviation is caused by the fact that the quantization range is not wide enough to approximate linear convolution by FFT (the range is [−10σ, 10σ] but 13 distributions needed to be convoluted).
Hence, CSDE-Q-ML and CSDE-Q-Mean provide good estimates for the performance of CSBP-Q-ML and CSBP-QMean algorithms when (wc, wc) is small. For large (wc, wr), obtaining an accurate approximation is difficult since the quantization range grows linearly with wr.
In contrast, Figure 3 shows CSDE-GA gives good approximation for the performance of CSDE-GA algorithm when (wc, wc) is larger than (3, 6). We believe the reason is that, when (wc, wr) is small, the Gaussian approximation is too coarse.
For almost all the density evolution and BP simulation curves, there is a sharp transition occurred near εBP = 0.28 (except that the transition occurs earlies for CSBP-GA and CSDE-GA). This transition is similar to BP-threshold in LDPC decoding: when ε < εBP, the reconstruction has very small MSE; when ε > εBP, reconstruction fails with high probability with large MSE. The figures show that the MSE’s on different sides of εBP differ by more than 100 times.
Comparing the performance among three reconstruction algorithms, several observation can be obtained:
• For (3, 6) case, CSBP-Q-ML and CSBP-Q-Mean outperform CSBP-GA in ε ∈ [0.2, 0.3], but CSBP-GA has slightly smaller MSE in ε ∈ [0, 0.2].
• For other cases, when ε < εBP, the following relations hold approximately:
mseCSBP-Q-ML ≈ 2 mseCSBP-GA
mseCSBP-Q-Mean ≈ 1.5 mseCSBP-GA
• For ε > εBP, the following relation holds:
mseCSBP-Q-ML > mseCSBP-Q-Mean ≈ mseCSBP-GA.

IV. CONCLUSION
In this paper, both quantization and Gaussian approximation are used for BP-based reconstruction algorithms in compressed sensing. A shift operation is also introduced to allow density evolution analysis of the reconstruction algorithms. The idea behind the shift is discussed both from a computation tree and MMSE perspective. The CSDE-GA algorithm is also derived as an approximation of CSDE algorithm. Simulation results are presented for the (3, 6), (5, 10), and (7, 14) Gallager-Radamacher matrix ensembles. For the chosen parameters, it is shown that the CSDE algorithm approximates closely the performance of corresponding CSBP algorithm. Future work will consider the reasons why CSDEGA is unstable when the node degree is small and how to accurately apply density evolution with damping.
