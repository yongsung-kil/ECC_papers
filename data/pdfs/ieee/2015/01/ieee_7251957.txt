Hardware Implementation of a Real-time Distributed Video Decoder
Hsin-Ping Yang!, Meng-Hsuan HOi, Hsiao-Chi Hsiehl, Po-Hsun Cheng2, and Sao-Jie Chenl IGraduate Institute of Electronics Engineering, National Taiwan University, Taipei, Taiwan

Abstract-Distributed video coding (DYC), based on Slepian-Wolf Theorem and/or Wyner-Ziv Theorem, was proposed to apply for the situation with little encoding and big decoding. We design and implement a distributed video decoder which is majorly composed of low-density parity-check accumulate (LDPCA), correlation noise modeling, soft input computation, and side information creation. Our proposed DYC decoder architecture, implemented in TSMC 90nm GUTM process technology, can meet the requirement of decoding a QCIF video with a speed of 30fps. The maximum operating frequency of the designed chip is lOOMHz, the chip area is 4.67 mm2, and the gate count is 690K.
Keywords-Distributed Video Decoder; LDPCA Decoder; Side Information Creation
1. INTRODUCTION
The Distributed Video Coding (DVC) [1] codec has been proposed, based on two theorems: Slepian-Wolf Theorem [2] and/or Wyner-Ziv (WZ) Theorem [3], where separately encoded and correlated sources could be jointly decoded with a rate of the joint entropy in theory. In a typical WZ based DVC [3], there are two types of coding frames, WZ frames and key frames. The key frames are coded by an H.264/AVC [4] Intra encoder. The WZ frames are coded through a WZ encoder. In such a DVC encoder, WZ frames will be transformed by a Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT). The quantizer transforms the continuous analog data of different frequencies to discrete digital data according to a selected quantization factor. Then, syndromes created by the Low-density Parity-check Accumulate (LDPCA) encoder will be stored in a buffer. In the decoder, periodically inserted key frames will be referred to generate Side Information (SI) using bidirectional motion estimation and motion compensation. Then, frames are reconstructed by the LDPCA decoder from SI and received syndromes.
A more efficient DVC codec, Distributed Coding for Video Services (DISCOVER) [5], a result from the Future and Emerging

Technologies (FET) program of the Information Society Technologies (1ST) in the European Union, was published later. Recently, a new hybrid DVC design with residual coding and frame level coding mode selection [6] was proposed and achieved high-efficiency coding compared to the DISCOVER.
The architecture of a distributed video codec is different from the architecture of conventional video codec since many parts with a high computing complexity like LDPCA decoder and SI creation are used in the distributed video decoder. We evaluate respective computing complexities by running a hybrid DVC decoding software with a test sequence 0[150 frames of Foreman in Quarter Common Intermediate Format (QCIF) on a server with an Intel i7-2600 CPU. Running with a quality parameter of 8, frames can only be decoded in 0.156 fps, obtained from 150 frames divided by 958.9205 seconds. Hence, we implemented a hardware of DVC decoder to decode frames in real time and to meet the requirement of decoding a QCIF video with a speed of 30fps.
This paper proposes a more balanced architecture of DVC. A new idea of our proposed architecture is that the motion vector is predicted in the DVC encoder in a small range such that more precise motion vectors can be input to the DVC decoder. Adding motion estimation in the DVC decoder will result in an increase of time complexity in the encoding process, but could improve the Rate Distortion (RD) performance. And compared to software simulation, it will only increase about 10% of time complexity in the encoding process. Besides, hardware architecture of LDPCA and an algorithm of creating comparing tree for checking nodes in the LDPCA are proposed.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. Our proposed architecture of DVC with balanced computing complexity in the decoder will be illustrated in Section II. The experiment results of our proposed DVC implementation will be discussed in Section III. Finally, we will conclude this paper in Section IV.

II. PROPOSED ARCHITECTURE OF DISTRIBUTED VIDEO DECODER
Lots of architectures of DVe have been proposed, and their goal is to improve the RD performance by adding extra components in the decoder, which also increases the complexity of the decoding process. High complexity of the decoding process causes that the decoded video cannot be played in real time. Hence, we propose an architecture of DVe based on hybrid DVe [6J to improve these situations and implement this architecture in hardware. In the following sections, we will describe in details the proposed architectures of DVe decoder, and two of its components, SI creation, and LDPeA decoder, which are different from the conventional video decoder.

B. Proposed Side Information Creation
The SI creation is used to generate approximate WZ frames. The proposed framework of SI creation is shown in Fig. 3, consisting of three parts: spatial motion smoothing, bidirectional motion compensation, and interpolation. First, spatial motion smoothing adjusts the motion vectors on a frame according to the input motion vectors of previous key frame and next key frame. Next, a motion-compensated key frame is generated by bidirectional motion compensation using adjusted motion vectors. Then, the motion-compensated key frame and a motion­ compensated previous key frame are combined by interpolation to create S1.
C. Proposed LD PCA D ecoder
Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) code, a linear block code, was invented by Gallager [7] in 1962. Then, Tanner's graph [8] transformed from the sparse parity-check matrix was proposed to clearly depict the concept of LDPC. Chung et at. [9] proved that the error-correcting performance of LDPC is only 0.0045 dB far from the Shannon limit. With its outstanding error-correcting performance, LDPC code has recently been applied to many new digital communication systems as channel coding. Besides, LDPC code can be used in fixed-rate distributed source coding [10]. We can use LDPC in a syndrome code form to transmit

different amount of syndromes to achieve the goal of data compression. Though LDPC in syndrome code form can be used to compress data, there is a disadvantage that not every variable node is connected to check nodes in high compression rate. To solve this problem, the LDPCA was proposed [11]. The idea of LDPCA is that the syndrome generated by the encoder will be accumulated such that variable nodes will not be unconnected to check nodes.
We proposed an architecture of LDPCA decoder as shown in Fig. 4. Intrinsic log-likelihood ration (LLR) and accumulated syndrome bits are input data as well as extrinsic LLR will be output when the decoding process is finished. The accumulated syndrome bits stored in the syndrome buffer are required by the LDPCA decoder in different code rates according to a predefined order. Basic check nodes (CNs) merged in the same CN will fetch the same syndrome bits. The syndrome bits fetched by basic check nodes are generated by a de-accumulator. IV. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we showed that our implemented distributed video decoder can decode video sequences in real time, and our designed hardware DVC decoder had a RD performance better than the presentative distributed video codec, DISCOVER [5]. The crucial challenge for a real-time distributed video decoder is that the computing complexities of side information creation and LDPCA are high. Our approach is to design a hardware of distributed video decoder, which is not only focused on improving the architectures of LDPCA and SI creation but also considers the trade-off between hardware cost and performance.
A prototype of our proposed DVC decoder was implemented in TSMC 90nm GUTM process technology and fabricated through National Chip Implementation Center, Taiwan. Our decoder can meet the requirement of decoding a QCIF video with a speed of 30tps. The maximum operation frequency of our design in the post-layout stage is 100MHz. Its chip area is 4.67 mm2, and gate count is 690K.
