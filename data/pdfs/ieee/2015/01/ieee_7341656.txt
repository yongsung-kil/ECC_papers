Experimental Demonstration of Cycle-Slip-Tolerant Turbo Differential Decoding for 100-Gb/s DP-DQPSK Coherent Transmission
Fan Yu(1), Zhiyu Xiao(1), Mo Li(1), Nebojsa Stojanovic(2), Changsong Xie(2), and Xiaozhong Shi(1)
(1) Huawei Technologies Co., Ltd., Network Research Department, Shenzhen, 518129, China irene.yufan@huawei.com (2) Huawei Technologies Duesseldorf GmbH, European Research Center, Riesstrasse 25, D-80992 Munich, Germany
Abstract We propose and experimentally demonstrate the use of dynamic decoding and limiting to achieve high-performance cycle-slip-tolerant turbo differential decoding in soft-decision LDPC, reducing the differential decoding penalty by 2 dB even in the presence of 10-2 probability of cycle slips.

Introduction
In digital coherent receivers for high-speed
optical transmission systems, cycle slips (CS)
occur due to imperfect carrier phase estimation
(CPE), especially in some practical high-
nonlinearity scenarios such as a 100G channel
neighbored by 10G OOK or 40G DP-QPSK ones1. Soft differential decoding (SDD) is often
used to handle the CS problem. However, it also
introduces performance degradation of up to 2.7
dB in back-to-back DQPSK receivers supported by a soft-decision (SD) FEC decoder2. To
mitigate the differential penalty, turbo differential
decoding (TDD) based on SD LDPC codes was
proposed, which can effectively improve the
performance of differential systems while still keeping the SD benefit2.
Some practical problems occur when the TDD is
used in DQPSK 100G coherent systems
suffering from CS. It was reported that the turbo
decoder failed with frequent CS by showing high error floor3. A great deal of effort has been
conducted to mitigate the error floor. It was
shown that the CS induced error floor can be lowered from BER of 10-5 to 10-8 by using hybrid turbo differential decoding (HTDD)4 but a HD
outer code is still required to clear up residual
error bits. By optimizing the trellis diagram of
TDD to take into account additional transitions
due to the CS, the decoder can be designed universally for any frequency of CS5,6. However,

the modified trellis diagram has much more transitions which greatly increase the implementation complexity. Since the MAP (maximum a-posteriori probability) decoder stores all the bits of a LDPC codeword, large storage resource will be required for the long LDPC codeword used in optical systems. Moreover, system throughput will be greatly limited by the sequential decoding architecture of MAP. So it is quite necessary to find a lowcomplexity way to implement the TDD at high throughput up to 100 Gb/s.
In this paper, we propose DSP-efficient implementation operations based on dynamic decoding and limiting to remove the CS-induced high error floor in the TDD decoder. We further discuss another implementation issue on the parallel TDD algorithm to realize high throughput. Our experiment illustrates that the TDD keeps the benefit of about 2dB from SDD with without CS-induced high error floor by employing the proposed implementation operations.

However, the MAP decoder provides bigger wrong extrinsic information to the LDPC decoder in the next iteration, which forces Cn to be further increased until all the Cn values achieve their maximum. Then, the APP turns back to the wrong value as it is affected by Mn.
From the previous analyses, it turns out that one can eliminate the CS induced error floor by using DSP-efficient implementation operations. When using dynamic decoding control in the LDPC decoder for power saving, the APP values are checked after each iteration. Once the decoded bits are correct, the iterative turbo decoding will stop working and the boundary bits have no more chance to be affected by the wrong MAP information. Another method that can achieve the same effect is value limiting. When implementing fixed-point turbo decoding, the value of the extrinsic information Mn are always limited to a value lower than the maximum of Ln. So the limited Mn can no longer draw the APP to the wrong side against bigger Cn values. Therefore, both operations can achieve the same post-FEC performance.
Parallel TDD Decoder The parallel TDD decoder is designed in order to improve the throughput of decoder. The MAP decoder consists of a sequence of short overlapped parallel BCJR (OP-BCJR) blocks shown in Fig.2(a). For example, when the QCLDPC(18360,4,8)2 is used as the FEC code, 18360 bits are divided into 510 blocks, each with 18 symbols. Compared with the conventional serial concatenated MAP decoding, 510 OPBCJR blocks can be processed in parallel.
To guarantee the turbo performance of parallel TDD decoder without high penalty an overlapped BCJR scheme is developed, in which adjacent segments are overlapped by several symbols. As illustrated in Fig.2.(b), the segment length is M symbols and the number of overlapped symbols is D. The initial state of each segment has equal probability in the first iteration, and the initial state of the subsequent iterations is obtained from the memory which stores the state of corresponding symbols after last iteration. M is set as 18, and D is 2 here. According to our simulation results (see Fig.2(c)), the EbN0 degradation of parallel TDD from sequential TDD is less than 0.05dB which is negligible.

A tunable laser operating at 1552.5nm with 100KHz linewidth is used as the optical source. Three transmission scenarios with fixed-point SDD and TDD algorithms are tested to verify the performance, including back-to-back, multi-channel (21 channels of 112Gb/s DP-DQPSK WDM, 1200km link, without DCF, 50GHz channel spacing), and hybrid-channel (a 112G DPDQPSK coherent channel neighbored with 4 channels of 10G OOK WDM, 300km link, DCF, 100GHz channel spacing, 1dBm/2dBm launch power (LP) ).
The received data are processed with offline DSP including clock recovery, residual chromatic dispersion compensation, polarization mode dispersion compensation, carrier recovery, among others. QC-LDPC-CC(18360,4,8)7 is employed instead of QC-LDPC(18360,4,8) for higher FEC performance. The aforementioned implementation operations including dynamic iterative decoding, Mn value limiting and TDD parallel algorithm are used in the decoder. The measured performance is shown in Fig. 4. Compared with SDD algorithm, almost 2dB differential penalty in all transmission scenarios are compensated after maximum 9 TDD iterations. Error floor is not observed even in hybrid-channel scenario with high CS probability. The results indicate that the implemented TDD decoder is robust to CS and suitable for high throughput 100Gb/s DP-DQPSK transmission.

Conclusions
We have studied the CS-induced error floor in turbo differential decoding, and showed that it is caused by the residual error bits from wrong transitions in trellis graph which cannot be decoded due to the APP fluctuation in iterative decoding. Such an error floor can be eliminated by DSP-efficient implementation operations such as dynamic decoding and limiting. We have also proposed a parallel TDD algorithm based on overlapped BCJR segments to support high throughput. Furthermore, we have experimentally shown that the TDD outperforms the SDD by about 2dB in OSNR, and achieves low error floor in 100-Gb/s DP-DQPSK coherent transmission even in the presence of a CS probability as high as 10-2.
