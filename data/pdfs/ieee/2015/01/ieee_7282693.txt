Scaling Rules for the Energy of Decoder Circuits

Christopher G. Blake and Frank R. Kschischang Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, University of Toronto
christopher.blake@mail.utoronto.ca, frank@ece.utoronto.ca

Abstract—A standard VLSI model is used to derive universal lower bounds on the energy of decoder circuits. In the circuit model used, the product of the circuit area and number of clock cycles, or the area-time complexity is proportional to the energy of computation. Lower bounds as a function of block length n are presented for three different circuit paradigms. Firstly, for
1
circuits that compute in parallel, an Ω n (log n) 2 scaling rule is
shown. Secondly, for circuits that compute serially, an Ω (n log n) lower bound is presented. Thirdly, for a sequence of decoding circuits in which the number of output pins grows arbitrarily
1
with block length, the energy is shown to grow as Ω n (log n) 5 .
In addition, it is shown that the energy complexity of almost all LDPC decoders that can get close to capacity and whose Tanner graphs are generated according to a uniform standard configuration model must take Ω n2 area to implement directly.

Keywords—Energy, decoders, circuits, LDPC.

In this paper, in Section II we discuss in detail the circuit model we will use for our results. In Section IV we present our fundamental lower bound results for serial, parallel, and general decoders. In Section V we present a scaling rule for the area of circuit implementations of almost all LDPC decoders that are generated according to a uniform configuration model.
Notation: In our discussion we will use standard Big-Oh and Big-Omega Notation. The statement g (x) = O (f (x)) means that g is asymptotically upper bounded by f , meaning that there exists some constant c ≥ 0 in which for all x sufficiently large, g (x) ≤ cf (x). The statement that g (x) = Ω (f (x)) can be thought of as f providing an asymptotic lower bound on g. Precisely, this statement means that f = O (g (x)).
II. CIRCUIT MODEL
The circuit model that we will use is based on [1] and was used to model energy of decoders in [3]. For the purpose of this paper, a circuit is a mathematical object consistent with the following axioms. For a more complete discussion of this model see [1], and for its applicability to the decoding problem see [3], [7].
• A circuit is a collection of nodes and wires laid out on a planar grid of squares. A grid square can either be empty, contain a node, a wire, or a wire crossing. Nodes are the “computational” part of a circuit and wires the “communication” part, sending information from computational node to computational node. There are two types of special nodes called input nodes and output nodes. In general, a circuit is to compute some specified function g : {0, 1}n → {0, 1}k. Thus, this circuit is said to have n binary inputs and k binary outputs.
• The wires of a circuit have width λw and thus each grid square has area λw2 .
• A computation is carried out in a set number τ of clock cycles.
• Computational nodes can be attached to up to 4 wires. In general, let a computational node have f ≤ 4 wires leading into it. For the purposes of lower bound, a node is able to perform any function g : {0, 1}f → {0, 1}f . Input nodes are able to perform any function {0, 1}f+1 → {0, 1}f , where the extra

Output nodes can perform any preset function f : {0, 1}f → {0, 1}f+1, where the f inputs into the node come from the wires leading to it and f outputs are passed along the wires to which the node is connected, and 1 output bit is considered to be an output of the circuit. For decoding circuits for an erasure channel, we in addition allow an erasure input to be an input to an input node. This assumption is valid for the purposes of forming energy complexity lower bounds.
• A wire, for the purposes of lower bound, is assumed to be bidirectional, so during every clock cycle a circuit can send a bit across its wires in both directions.

• The inputs of the computation are input into the input nodes. In general, we say that there are p input nodes and j output nodes. In a fully parallel implementation of a circuit, there are p = n input nodes and j = k output nodes. However, in a general computation, we allow for serialization of the inputs into the circuit and the outputs. Thus, over the course of the computation it is required that all k outputs of the circuit appear at an output node during some pre-set clock cycle of the computation.

III. DEFINITIONS AND MAIN LEMMAS
Our results rely on the notion of the minimum bisection width of a graph. Intuitively, the minimum bisection width of a graph is the minimum number of edges that need to be removed to result in two unconnected graphs that have roughly

the same number of vertices of interest. This is precisely defined below.
Definition 1. Let G = (V, E) be a graph and V ⊆ V be a subset of the vertices. A set of edges E ⊆ E is called a bisection of G with respect to V if removal of the edges in E from G results in two disconnected graphs, with vertices V1 and V2, in which V1 ∪ V2 = V and in which ||V1 ∩ V | − |V2 ∩ V || ≤ 1. A bisection whose size is minimal over all bisections of a graph is called a minimal bisection. The size of a minimal bisection is called the minimum bisection width.

Any graph can have the edges of a minimum bisection removed, resulting in two disconnected graphs. In turn, these two graphs can each be minimally bisected again, resulting in 4 graphs. This process can be repeated r times resulting in 2r disconnected graphs. The repeated process of minimum bisecting a graph is called performing r-stages of nested minimum bisections.

Often, the minimum bisection width refers to the minimum bisection that divides all the vertices of a graph in half. Note that in our proofs, we will perform r-stages of nested minimum bisections with respect to the vertices corresponding to the output nodes of the circuit. Thus, after one minimum bisection of the graph with respect to the output nodes, the two resulting disconnected graphs must have a close to equal number of output nodes, but may not necessarily have an equal number of total nodes.

Definition 3. A coding scheme is an infinite sequence of decoder circuits C1, C2, . . ..
Since each circuit is associated with a code and decoding algorithm, thus each circuit can be thought of as having an associated block length and rate (the block length and rate of the code that the circuit is to decode). Definition 4. Coding schemes in which the block length of each successive circuit increases are considered to be increasing block length coding schemes.
Definition 5. A coding scheme in which the number of output pins j is equal to the number of outputs of the function for each circuit is considered to be a fully parallel coding scheme.
Definition 6. A coding scheme in which the number of output pins stays constant is considered to be a serial coding scheme.
Definition 7. An output regular decoder is a decoder in which each output pin is responsible for outputting an equal fraction of the output bits. As well, such a decoder has set clock cycles in which all of the output pins are to hold one of the outputs of their computation. A decoding scheme is output regular if each of the circuits in the scheme is output regular.
The properties of an output regular decoder allow us to conveniently divide the output pins of a circuit in space (into subcircuits), and time (into epochs). This assumption is needed for Theorem 2.

V. ANALYSIS OF ENERGY OF LDPC DECODING ALGORITHMS

In this section, we show that if an LDPC code is generated according to a uniform configuration distribution, then asymptotically, with probability 1, a circuit that instantiates such a code must have area that scales as Ω n2 . LDPC codes are a class of linear error control codes first studied by Gallager in [10]. The analyses of LDPC codes often assume a Tanner graph generated from a family of configurations with particular node degrees. One such distribution is known as the uniform configuration and is discussed in [11].

assumptions on the typical wire length in the instantiation of
the LDPC code, the authors show that the area of an LDPC code must scale as Ω n2 . Here we make rigorous this result:

VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper we have presented three lower bounds on the energy complexity of decoders, and showed that in all cases good decoders must have energy that scales super-linearly with block length n. This suggests decoders operating at a rate strictly less than capacity may optimize decoding energy per bit.
LDPC codes are a well studied class where the dependency graph of the decoding algorithm is induced by a Tanner graph of the associated linear code. We showed that, with probability approaching 1 as block length grows, the minimum bisection width of a Tanner graph generated using a configuration model (often used to analyze LDPC codes) scales proportionally to the number of nodes, implying that the area of circuit instantiations of such graphs scales as Ω n2 with probability approaching one. This does not imply, however, that all LDPC decoders are fundamentally inefficient, as it could be that some low-complexity Tanner graphs have good performance. However, the common analysis technique of generating random Tanner graph configurations that is used to understand the performance of LDPC codes does not factor in the energy complexity of decoding as a parameter to be traded-off with other code parameters.
This work suggests that currently there is a “gap” between our fundamental complexity lower bound and corresponding upper bounds. Work remains to be done to analyze whether other low complexity, high performance coding schemes like polar codes and turbo codes have circuit complexities that approach these lower bounds. It may also be that our lower bound is not tight. Future work should consider both directions: finding tighter lower bounds (if they exist) and better upper bounds (if they exist).
Furthermore, as suggested in [15], there is little theoretical understanding of the trade-off between the interconnect complexity of LDPC decoder Tanner graphs and code performance.

This suggests that theoretical tool characterizing this trade-off similar to that suggested in [16] may be useful in applying these theoretical results.
Finally, it should be clear that our results are applicable only to the Thompson model of computation energy. In particular, models described in [17] suggest that circuit energy savings may result from lowering the voltage driving the circuit at the cost of noisy logic gates. In the case of LDPC decoders this has been shown to result in energy savings in practical circuits [18]. We have not considered such models in this paper.
