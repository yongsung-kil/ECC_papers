Spatially Coupled LDPC Codes Constructed by Parallelly Connecting Multiple Chains
Yang Liu, Ying Li, and Yuhao Chi

Abstract—A particular class of spatially coupled low-density parity-check (SC-LDPC) codes are constructed by parallelly connecting multiple different coupled chains. The connection structure is realized by edge exchanges between adjacent chains, which can help these chains support each other and improve the iterative decoding thresholds. By varying the number of the connected chains and the degree of each chain, a family of SC-LDPC codes with wide rate range can be obtained. Density evolution analysis shows that the decoding thresholds of the proposed codes are very close to Shannon limits and are slightly better than that constructed by a single chain over the binary erasure channel.
Index Terms—Spatially coupled LDPC codes, density evolution, iterative decoding thresholds.
I. INTRODUCTION
T HE studies on spatially-coupled low-density parity-check (SC-LDPC) codes date back to convolutional LDPC codes [1]. It was shown that convolutional LDPC codes constructed from a block LDPC code can improve the belief propagation (BP) threshold up to the maximum a posterior (MAP) threshold of the underlying code. Kudekar et al. named this phenomenon threshold saturation and proved it rigorously for the binary erasure channels (BEC) in [2] and the binary-input memoryless symmetric-output (BMS) channels in [3]. After that, several new SC-LDPC codes were constructed to further improve the performance by introducing irregularity [4]–[6].
In [4], Truhachev et al. proposed a method to connect two or more spatially coupled chains by adding extra edges to variable nodes at special positions. They demonstrated that the thresholds of this new ensemble can be improved compared to that of a single chain. Following a different approach, the connection of multiple coupled chains in parallel was discussed in [5], where a spatially coupled code was assigned to each of the parallel channels and then lateral connectivity to connect the graphs of the single chains was introduced. It was shown that the new ensemble outperforms parallel, independent spatially coupled codes and offers an increased resilience against burst errors. In [6], two types of nearly-regular SC-LDPC ensembles were investigated. Both ensembles were built upon the mixture of two favorable regular codes. It was shown that these codes exhibit excellent performances for all rates and have low decoding complexity.
In this letter, we propose a different method to design a class of SC-LDPC codes, which are constructed by parallelly connecting multiple SC-LDPC chains, abbreviated as PC-MSCLDPC codes. Compared with the codes in [4]–[6], the proposed codes have the following properties. Firstly, PC-MSC-LDPC

II. SPATIALLY COUPLED LDPC CODES WITH PARALLEL CONNECTIVITY
A. SC-LDPC Codes
We consider two ensembles: the first is used to construct practical SC-LDPC codes and the other is to derive density evolution equations.
1) The (J, K, L) Ensemble: According to [2], a (J, K, L) coupled chain can be constructed by coupling L uncoupled (J, K)regular LDPC protographs. Let p =gcd(J, K) denote the greatest common divisor of J and K. There are J = J/p check nodes and K = K/p variable nodes at each position of the coupled chain.
To couple these L protographs, we spread p edges of each variable node to its nearby check nodes in the chain. More precisely, these p edges of the variable node located at position i are connected to the check nodes at positions {i − (p − 1)/2 , · · · , i − 1, i, i + 1, · · · , i + (p − 1)/2 }. To terminate the coupled chain, p − 1 extra check node positions should be added. An SC-LDPC code can be obtained by taking an “M-lifting” of the (J, K, L) coupled chain [7].
2) The (J, K, L, ω) Ensemble: The (J, K, L, ω) ensemble can be obtained by adding a “smoothing” parameter ω to randomize the edge connections [2]. To do this, each edge of a variable node at position i is uniformly and independently connected to one of the check nodes in the range {i, · · · , i + ω − 1}. Correspondingly, each edge of a check node at position i is independently connected to one of the variable nodes in the range {i − ω + 1, · · · , i}.

In this section, we evaluate the performance of the proposed PC-MSC-LDPC ensemble over the BEC via density evolution. When calculating the BP thresholds, we set 104 as the maximum iteration number and set 10-6 as the breakout condition of the erasure probability at each coupled position.
1) Rate Higher Than 1/2: We first assume that C chains have the same coupling length Lup and coupling width 4. The thresholds of the PC-MSC-LDPC ensembles for different Lups
are shown in Fig. 4. Clearly, the threshold can be improved with the increase of Lup. Then we investigate the code with C chains having different coupling length and coupling width.
In the simulation, we fix the sum of all the coupling lengths at 100 × C. One possible choice is that the coupling length of C chains is set to be 90, 100 and 110 sequentially and cyclically. For example, when C = 6, the coupling lengths of these chains, from the first chain to the last chain, are 90,100,110,90,100,110. The coupling width of each chain is set to be 4 or 5 randomly.
The result shows that it performs almost the same as the code with each chain having Lup = 100. One point to note is that this simulation just gives one special case to show the PC-MSC-
LDPC ensemble can work when each chain has different coupling length and width. The optimization of the coupling length for each chain is an interesting topic for future. Another interesting observation we find is that when Lup is fixed, the gaps between the thresholds and Shannon limits are fluctuant, i.e., the gaps first decrease as C increases and then become large when C ≥ 5. The reason might be that the fraction of the exchanged edges is becoming smaller, which will weaken the support among these C chains.

V. CONCLUSION
In this letter, we propose a new method to design PC-MSCLDPC codes to cover a wide rate range, which are constructed by parallelly connecting multiple different coupled chains. The connection structure is realized by exchanging the edges of the variable nodes between different chains at the same position. Density evolution analysis shows that the thresholds of the proposed PC-MSC-LDPC ensembles are very close to Shannon limits over the BEC and are slightly better than that of one single chain. Since the proposed PC-MSC-LDPC codes have flexible rates, this method may raise up a new viewpoint for constructing rate-compatible SC-LDPC codes. This is also our future work.
