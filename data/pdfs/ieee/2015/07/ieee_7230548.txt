An efficient structure for terminating spatially coupled LDPC codes

Junyang Ma, Zhongwei Si, Zhiqiang He Key Lab of Universal Wireless Communications, Ministry of Education Beijing University of Posts and Telecommunications(BUPT), Beijing, China

Abstract—A recursive encoding of spatially coupled LDPC codes with low complexity has been proposed in recent works. But it has to solve a system of linear equations to terminate the codes. In this paper we modify the protograph of spatially coupled LDPC codes by adding extra variable nodes, called repeataccumulate(RA) tail, which can simplify the termination without time delays. Performance analysis with density evolution and simulation results over binary erasure channel(BEC) channels are also provided. Simulation results show that, for spatially coupled LDPC codes with RA tail, the complexity of termination can be reduced with a small trade-off in performance.
I. INTRODUCTION
LDPC(Low-density parity-check code)[1] has attracted much attentions in the last decade with its capacityapproaching performance. Its convolutional counterparts, spatially coupled LDPC codes, were proposed in [2] and further researched by, e.g.,[3], [4]. Different structures of spatially coupled LDPC codes were introduced in [5], [6]. In [3], [7], it was observed that belief-propagation(BP) thresholds of the spatially coupled LDPC codes were equal to the maximumlikelihood(ML) thresholds of LDPC block code with same variable and check degree. In [4], it was proved that the BP thresholds of spatially coupled LDPC codes could achieve the maximum a posteriori probability(MAP) thresholds of corresponding LDPC block code over BEC. It was also shown that spatially coupled LDPC codes can approach the capacity of binary memoryless symmetric(BMS) channel in [3], [8].
A recursive encoder of spatially coupled LDPC codes using a shift-register was provided in [5]. It reduces the complexity of the encoder. But the state of the encoder after encoding is non-zero. In order to bring the encoder to the zero state, some tail bits are added to the information sequence. In [5], the tail bits are calculated by solving a system of linear equations. And the computational cost of calculating the tail bits is proportional to the square of the lifting number. Generally speaking, the lifting number is always larger than the coupling number, thus calculating the tail bits needs more computations than encoding. To reduce the computational cost of terminating spatially coupled LDPC codes, a modified spatially coupled LDPC code was proposed In [9]. Based on the modified structure, the computational cost can be decreased by using an accumulator. But some time is sacrificed to achieve this goal, and it will cause lager time delays.
In this paper, we modified structure of spatially coupled LDPC codes in [5] by adding variable nodes, which is similar to the structure of spatially coupled repeat-accumulate codes. These variable nodes are called RA tail in this paper. After

Performance analysis with design rate and density evolution is provided. A. Spatially Coupled LDPC Codes With RA Tail
Without loss of generality, let’s take a (dv, dc, L) = (3, 6, 5) modified spatially coupled LDPC ensemble for example, as shown in Fig.2. The notation are the same as in Fig.1. Compared with Fig.1, extra variable nodes are added to the protograph, corresponding to the RA tail bits. These extra variable nodes are represented by black circles. We can see that the degree of variable nodes at position 0 to 4 equals 3, while the degree of tail variable nodes at position 5 and 6 equals 2, and the degree of check nodes at position 6 and 7 is increased to 5 by adding extra variable nodes. The modified structure is similar to spatially coupled repeat-accumulate codes[10], these extra variable nodes correspond to parity nodes in spatial coupled repeat-accumulate code. Generally speaking, the extra variable nodes at position i, i ∈ [L, L + ms − 2] connect with the check nodes at position i and i+1, while the extra variable node at position L + ms − 2 only connects with the last check node. In Fig.2, zi denotes the tail bits at position i. It can be found that zi can be calculated by the encoded bits at the former positions.
According to the protograph of the (3,6,5) spatially coupled LDPC codes with modified structure, the parity-check matrix can be illustrated as Fig.3. The parity-check matrix is composed of permuted identity sub-matrices with size M ×M . The sub-matrices P(0,0)(5), P(0,1)(5) and P(0,0)(6) correspond to the connections between extra variable nodes and check nodes. C. Complexity
In this part, we will compare the complexity of termination for spatially coupled LDPC codes in [5][9] with spatially coupled LDPC codes with RA tail. In [5], a system of linear equations should be solved to find the tail bits. As this process needs a multiplication of matrix of size M ×M , the complexity of the termination process is O(M 2). In [9], by choosing special submatrix, the tail bits can be calculated using an accumulator with 2M accumulations. And the complexity of termination is reduced to O(M ). But because the bits at current position are calculated depending on the bits at latter positions, some time delays will exist, and the time delays is proportional to M , which can not be ignored.
For the spatially coupled LDPC codes with RA tail, the RA tail bits are computed with equation (4). It can be found that the RA tail bits are got depending on the encoded bits at former positions, so there are no time delays during the process, which is a clear advantage to the algorithm in [9]. This makes spatially coupled LDPC codes with RA tail highly adaptive. Besides, according to equation (4), it is easy to see that the complexity of the termination is O(M ).

Besides, simulation results for spatially coupled LDPC codes with RA tail and spatially coupled LDPC codes in [5] with finite code lengths over BEC channels are provided.
The BP thresholds of spatially coupled LDPC codes with RA tail over BEC is shown in Table I. Here, we set L = 50 for all the spatially coupled LDPC codes, and change the value of dc and dv. As shown in Table I, the BP thresholds decrease as dc increases with fixed dv, while the gaps between Shannon limits and BP thresholds degrade. Besides, the BP thresholds grow with memory increases while the gaps decrease. We also provide the BP thresholds of spatially coupled LDPC codes in [5] in Table II. We compare the gaps between the Shannon limits and simulated BP thresholds in Table I with those in Table II. It can be found that spatially coupled LDPC codes with RA tail suffer a little loss to its corresponding spatially coupled LDPC codes.

VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we propose a modified structure for terminated spatially coupled LDPC codes with RA tail, which can simplify the termination of encoding without extra time consumption. The construction will cause a little rate-loss, but it can be ignored with L increasing. And the spatially coupled LDPC codes with RA tail have nearly the same BP thresholds with its corresponding spatially coupled LDPC codes shown in [5] over BEC channels.
