Low-Complexity and Piecewise Systematic Encoding of Non-Full-Rank QC-LDPC Codes
Chien-Fu Tseng and Jenn-Hwan Tarng, Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—In this letter, we present a low-complexity encoding method for quasi-cyclic (QC) low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes in the case of non-full-rank parity-check matrices. Gaussian elimination can achieve systematic encoding, but it is usually too complex to implement. For QC-LDPC codes, efficient encoding methods have been presented by using shift-registers, but the encoded codewords are not systematic when parity-check matrices are non-full-rank. However, a systematic structure is important in practice. Therefore, we propose an encoding method which allows all information bits appear piecewise in the codeword, called piecewise systematic encoding.
Index Terms—Systematic, encoding, quasi-cyclic (QC) lowdensity parity-check (LDPC) codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
B OSE-Chaudhuri-Hocquenghem (BCH) codes are the default error-correcting code (ECC) for flash controllers. For the high raw bit-error rate (RBER) of the upcoming flash devices, a significantly higher overhead BCH must be taken. For example, BCH code that can protect 1K-bytes user data requires a code rate of less than 0.8 to achieve a user bit error rate (UBER) of 1.0−15 at an average RBER of 1.0−2. This is ineffective for flash controllers; hence, there has been a significant push to employ more powerful ECC such as LDPC codes. LDPC codes with Shannon-limit-approaching performance and reasonable hardware-implementation complexity are strong candidates for the next-generation flash controllers.
For flash storage systems high reliability is essential; hence, the applied LDPC codes with ultra-low error-floor is necessity. A class of algebraic QC-LDPC codes has good correction capability and achieves low error-floor due to their redundant (or called dependent) parity-check rows and related high hamming distance [1]–[3]. In addition, since the parity-check matrix of QC-LDPC codes consist of circulants, which are circularshifted square matrices or zero matrices, the routing complexity and the required memory can be significantly reduced, as compared with randomly constructed LDPC codes.
On the other hand, since parity-check matrices of this kind are usually non-full-rank, which include dependent paritycheck rows, it makes encoding become more complicated. Although the generator matrix can be obtained by Gaussian elimination from the given parity-check matrix of a QC-LDPC

To reduce the encoding complexity, Richardson and Urbanke have presented a method of preprocessing the parity check matrix into an approximate lower triangular (ALT) form to take advantage of the sparsity of the parity-check matrix in encoding [4]. However, for high column degree QC-LDPC codes, the preprocessed ALT matrix is not as large as expected and other sub-matrices are not in circulant form. Consequently, the implementation is still complex. With regard to encoding in circulant form, Li et al. However, when parity-check matrices include a number of dependent rows, the encoded codeword is not systematic, in the sense that message bits do not appear directly in the encoded codeword. In practice, systems expect the successfully decoded codewords as recovered user data. If a encoded codeword is not systematic and transmitted to channel, after receiving and decoding the codeword it still has to be converted to user data, and this converting may not be easy. It may trouble a system design. In light of this issue, we propose an encoding method which allows all information bits appear piecewise in the codeword, and then the user data can be completely recovered after successful decoding.

IV. CONCLUSION
In this letter, we present a low-complexity and piecewise systematic encoding method for QC-LDPC codes in the case of non-full-rank parity-check matrices. The proposed method is a two-phase calculation which is implemented by co-using the same SRAA circuits. By reducing a half of registers of the original SRAA circuits, a efficient encoder has thus been presented.

Here, p(2) is composed of a(2) and parity. Because the message a(2) is at the fixed positions of p(2) in spite of piecewise, the message a(1) and a(2) can be completely recovered by a decoder’s direct output after successful decoding. Therefore, the proposed method is called piecewise systematic encoding.
The phase 2 encoding process, shown in (26), can be implemented by utilizing the l SRAA circuits of phase 1 encoding. After phase 1 encoding done, sequentially load the i-th column of S into the register B and input the i-th bit of (q + a(2)) per clock. After lb − r clock operations, the phase 2 encoding is done, and p(2) is stored in the register A. Besides, we notice that the right-feedback shift-register B can be replaced by operating left-feedback shift on register A; hence, the register B can be directly passed.
