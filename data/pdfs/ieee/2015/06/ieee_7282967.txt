Abstract-Pearl's belief propagation (BP) is an algorithm to 
solve inference problems on probability models defined in terms of 
graphical models. Computational complexity of BP per iteration 
is typically exponential in the degrees of nodes in the graph, 
which makes application of BP impractical in problems repre­
sented by dense graphs. For the aneviation of the computational 
difficulty of BP, an approximation of BP dubbed approximate 
message passing (AMP) is proposed in the area of compressed 
sensing. Since theoretical treatments of AMP are mostly in the 
infinite-dimensional limit, they are not mathematicany justifiable 
when the system size is not large enough and little is known 
about the cases where the system size is sman. We investigate 
poor performance of the AMP algorithm applied to small-sized 
problems, which is frequently observed in numerical experiments, 
by comparing performance of the AMP algorithm and that of the 
original BP algorithm. In this paper, firstly, we show that, under 
several assumptions, one can perform the original BP algorithm 
in time complexity that is polynomial in the degrees of the nodes, 
utilizing a method similar to what is employed in BP decoding 
for LDPC codes. Next, we apply the preceding discussion to the 
CDMA multinser detection problem, to which AMP has been 
successfully applied in existing researches. Finally, we compare 
the performance of the BP and AMP algorithms and discuss 
the effects of the approximation involved in deriving the AMP 
algorithm, when the system size is not large enough. 
Keywords-Approximate message passing, belief propagation, 
message passing. 
I. 
INTRODUCTION 
The approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm is for­
mulated as an approximation of Pearl's belief propagation (BP) 
algorithm applied to loopy graphs [1]. The AMP algorithm 
was first proposed in [2], although essentially the same idea 
can be found before that, for example in [3], [4], [5]. The 
AMP algorithm is generally applied to dense factor graphs 
to alleviate the computational difficulty which would arise if 
one applies the BP algorithm. The computational difficulty has 
been resolved by the approximation introduced in the AMP 
algorithm. 
However, the approximation is based on the central limit 
theorem, and hence is not theoretically justifiable when the 
system size is not large enough. It has also been observed 
empirically that the AMP algorithm does not perform well 
in small-sized problems. Even though there are intensive 
studies in the large-system limit, from which guarantees on the 
performance have been available, there is, to our knowledge,

The main purpose of this paper is to discuss the question 
"Why does the AMP algorithm perform poorly when the 
system size is small?" There are two leaps in deriving the 
AMP algorithm which are not theoretically justified, and 
either of these leaps is therefore suspected to be "the factor" 
causing the poor performance of the AMP algorithm to small­
sized problems. One is the BP algorithm itself applied to 
graphs with cycles. More concretely, there is no guarantee 
of convergence of the BP algorithm and the BP algorithm 
may perform poorly even if it converges. The other leap is 
the approximation applied in deriving the AMP algorithm. 
Since the approximation is based on the central limit theorem, 
it is imprecise when the system size is small, and therefore 
this can also cause poor performance of the AMP algorithm. 
We might be able to isolate "the factor" which causes poor 
performance of the AMP algorithm if we could compare the 
AMP algorithm with the original BP algorithm without the 
approximation. However, performing the original BP algorithm 
applied to the dense graphs can be computationally prohibitive 
in general cases. The computational difficulty prevents us from 
studying the true factor leading to the poor performance. 
In this paper, we show that one can perform the original 
BP instead of the AMP in polynomial time under certain 
conditions, even though the underlying graph is dense. This 
method is mathematically identical on specific conditions to 
those proposed for decoding of Low-Density Parity-Check 
(LDPC) codes [6], [7], [8]. We then apply the AMP and 
BP algorithms to CDMA multi user detection as in [5], as a 
sample problem, and discuss their behaviors. By comparing 
the performance of the BP and AMP algorithms, we expect 
to specify which of the above leaps is the factor causing the 
poor performance of the AMP algorithm in small-sized CDMA 
multi user detection problems. 
This paper is organized as follows. In Section IT, we 
show a method to reformulate the original BP algorithm 
as a polynomial-time algorithm under a certain condition. 
We also mention the relationship between our method and 
those proposed for LDPC codes. In Section rn, we apply the 
proposed method to CDMA multiuser detection problem. In 
Section IV, we conduct numerical experiments and compare 
the BP and AMP algorithms. In Section V, we summarize this 
work.

NUMERICAL EXPERIMENT 
We evaluated bit error rate (BER) of hard-decision tentative 
estimates of bit symbols at each iteration, obtained by each of 
the two algorithms: the BP algorithm, in which the proposed 
method was used, and the AMP algorithm proposed in [5]. 
We also evaluated BERs via density evolution which calculates 
theoretical behavior of the BP and AMP algorithms applied to 
infinitely large systems [5]. 
In the numerical experiments, we fixed K / N to 0.5 and 
set the signal-to-noise ratio, defined as SNR 
= 1/ (Y2, to 4 
and 9. We varied only the system sizes and fixed the other 
parameters. 
Figure 1 shows the time evolution of BERs of the AMP 
algorithm for N = 2000 and that of the BP algorithm for 
N = 500, both averaged over 10 000 trials. The system sizes 
N = 2000 and N = 500 for the AMP and BP algorithms 
were determined so that one can regard them large enough for 
observing large-system behaviors of the respective algorithms 
while they are feasible in terms of computational complexity. 
Figures 2 and 3 show the results for N = 100 and N = 50, 
respectively, where the BERs were averaged over 1 000 000 
trials for the AMP algorithm and 10 000 trials for the BP 
algorithm. The results obtained from density evolution are also 
shown in all of these figures. 
Figure 1 shows that, when the system size is large enough, 
the dynamical behavior of AMP and BP is predicted by 
density evolution quite well. Figures 2 and 3 report that, 
firstly, the BERs of AMP and BP algorithms converge more 
slowly than the predictions of density evolution. In addition 
to this, comparison of Figs. 2 and 3 reveals that the speed 
of convergence of AMP and BP algorithms gets slower as 
the problem size becomes smaller. It is also found that the 
convergence of the AMP algorithm is a little slower than that 
of BP algorithm. 
From these experimental results, firstly, we confirmed that, 
as reported in [5], the AMP algorithm performs as good 
as expected via density evolution when the system size is 
sufficiently large. We observed that this is also the case for BP 
algorithm. However, that does not hold when the system size is 
small. BERs of AMP and BP algorithms converge more slowly 
compared with the case where the system size is sufficiently 
large. We also observed that BP algorithm performs slightly 
better compared with the AMP algorithm in such cases. 
From these results, we can conclude that the poor perfor­
mance of the AMP algorithm in small-sized systems is mainly 
ascribed to the application of BP algorithm to graphs with 
cycles. The approximation based on the central limit theorem 
also causes the performance degradation of AMP, but its effect 
is relatively small.

V. 
CONCLUSION 
In this paper, we have discussed dynamical properties of 
AMP and BP algorithms applied to the CDMA multiuser 
detection problem when the system size is not large. For this 
purpose, we have developed a novel method to reduce compu­
tational cost of BP algorithm. The numerical experiments have 
demonstrated that BP algorithm does not work well when the 
system size is small. On the basis of this observation, we can 
conclude that the poor performance of the AMP algorithm 
in small-sized CDMA multiuser detection problems is mainly 
ascribed to the application of BP algorithm to graphs with 
cycles. 
For the more general discussions, our future work is to 
apply our framework to other problems. In this paper, we 
have so far applied our framework to the CDMA multi user 
detection problem. Future work will include application of our 
framework to other problems in order to study possible factors 
affecting performance of the AMP algorithm.
