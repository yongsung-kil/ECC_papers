Knowledge-Aided Informed Dynamic Scheduling for LDPC Decoding

Cornelius Healy CETUC-PUC-Rio, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
Email: cthealy@cetuc.puc-rio.br
Abstract—Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes offer excellent performance at competitive levels of complexity. Short block length LDPC codes avoid the large processing latency incurred by the large block lengths classically considered for this class of codes, making them a potential candidate for next generation wireless communications systems. In this paper, a novel informed dynamic scheduling (IDS) approach for decoding LDPC codes is developed based on the use of the current message reliabilities and the residuals of the potential updates to select the messages passed in the graph during iterative Sum Product decoding. An alternative measure of the iteration of the IDS schemes is also proposed which highlights the high cost of those algorithms in terms of processing complexity and motivates the development of the proposed approach. The proposed Rel.-RBP decoding algorithm offers very fast convergence at reduced complexity and gains in error rate performance when compared to the previous schemes.
Keywords—Belief propagation, low-density parity-check codes, message-passing schedule, LDPC decoding.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, first discovered by Gallager [1], have seen increasing use in industry standards in recent years including IEEE 802.11n (Wifi) [2], IEEE 802.16e (WiMAX) [3], DVB-S2 [4], IEEE 802.3an (Ethernet) [5] and IEEE 802.11ad (Gigabit Wifi) [6]. In recent standards, shorter block length codes have been proposed, rather than the larger block lengths classically considered for LDPC codes. This is significant for the potential applications of these codes to next generation networks as the block processing time incurred by the use of large block lengths would introduce unacceptable delay to the system attempting to achieve the projected requirements of future wireless communications systems. The sparse nature of the LDPC code graph allows for relatively low complexity decoding, and encoding complexity may be reduced through the use of structured graphs [7], allowing the complexity burden of channel coding to be reduced towards the energy efficiency goals of the future networks.
An outstanding issue for LDPC codes is the delay introduced by the slow convergence of the iterative sum-product (SPA) decoder and its variants [8] [9] [10] [11]. In recent years, the schedule of message updates of the iterative decoder has attracted much interest, as the potential for performance increases were evidenced by the layered belief propagation (LBP) decoder [12] [13], in that case with very little increase in complexity. The residual belief propagation (RBP) decoder [14] [15] [16] uses informed dynamic scheduling, whereby the message update order is not preset but rather is dynam-

This involves precomputation of potential message updates and selection of the update which is projected to have the best impact on overall convergence, as measured by the message residual. The promising results of the IDS schemes has generated a great deal of interest and work [17]- [21].
In this work, the reliability of the messages currently passed in the graph, i.e., the message magnitude or the message belief, is used to reduce the number of potential message updates to two only per node, greatly reducing the required complexity of the scheduling algorithm. We devise a knowledge-aided IDS decoding algorithm that exploits the reliability of the messages to refine the exchange of information in the graph. A procedure to measure the iterations along with their cost in a more effective way is also presented. The results show that the proposed algorithm outperforms the RBP decoding algorithm in several scenarios of practical interest.
The rest of this paper is laid out as follows: Section II presents the notation and model of the LDPC decoding system, along with the message passing rules of the SPA and the residual-based BP algorithms. Section III discusses the measurement of the iterations performed in the IDS schemes and provides some motivation for the work presented in this paper. In Section IV, the proposed reliability-based RBP algorithm is presented. Section V provides the simulation study and discussion of the results. Section VI concludes the paper.

In the RBP scheduled decoder, the algorithm iterates in individual check-to-variable message updates, rather than in updating all nodes of one or both type, as in the LBP or flooding scheduled BP algorithms, respectively. In the binary RBP decoder, the next message to be updated is selected by the message residuals:

Only the message update with the largest residual is assigned, all others are stored for potential future use. Following this, the outgoing messages from the variable node vj receiving the assigned check-to-variable message are updated. New residuals are then computed at all check nodes receiving those updates. This process then iterates. The RBP algorithm is depicted in Fig. 3. III. MEASUREMENT OF ITERATIONS
In order to allow meaningful performance comparisons, it is necessary to define an equivalent iteration, the points in the processing of the IDS decoders can be said to be equivalent to the iterations of the established schemes. The choice made in [15], used in the simulation results of that work to support the RBP and NS-BP algorithms and termed classic iterations in this paper, is to evaluate the performance when the number of check node message updates assigned by the IDS-based algorithm equals that of the standard schemes. This treats all message computations required for residual calculation as a cost of the scheduling scheme. However, these additional update computations are effectively indistinguishable from those carried out for the messages passed, they must be done with up-to-date information and can not be precomputed. This motivates the consideration of an alternative iteration measure.
The modified iteration is taken to be the point in the processing of the IDS-based scheme at which the number of check node message updates computed equals that of the flooding and layered schemes. Under this modified iteration measure a single iteration of each scheme, both established and IDS-based, requires the same number of check node updates and thus approximately the same complexity.

IV. RELIABILITY-RESIDUAL BELIEF PROPAGATION
In order to reduce the significant computational cost of the IDS schemes, as demonstrated by Fig. 4, while retaining the excellent convergence performance of those schemes, efforts were made to identify the edge to update offering most to overall convergence while avoiding precomputation of all messages as required by RBP and NS-BP. It was observed that, in the initial stage of operation when no previous messages have been passed, (5) reduces to |µ(cki→ ) vj | and from the properties of (2) this residual will always be associated with the edge with smallest magnitude incoming LLR, |µ(vkh)→ci |. This magnitude of LLR is termed the reliability of the message, as it conveys information about the level of belief of the message concerning the bit value of the node it is associated with. Upon further investigation of residuals and incoming message reliabilities it was observed that the largest residual message was found at the edges with two smallest incoming reliabilities in the majority of cases. In this paper this fact is exploited to reduce the number of residual computations required for each message assignation.

VII. CONCLUSION
In this paper, in addition to the introduction of a new measure of iteration for the IDS schemes for performance comparison, an improved knowledge-based dynamic scheduling scheme was proposed and demonstrated to achieve improved performance with reduced complexity with respect to the RBP and NS-BP schemes, particularly achieving very fast convergence to low error rates, even at short block lengths. Such a scheme may contribute to the solution of the open question of achieving the latency and energy efficiency requirements of future 5G communications systems.
