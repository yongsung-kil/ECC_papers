Improved Message-Passing Algorithm For Counting Short Cycles in Bipartite Graphs

Juane Li, Shu Lin, and Khaled Abdel-Ghaffar University of California, Davis Davis, CA 95616, USA
E-mail: {jueli, shulin, ghaffar}@ucdavis.edu

Abstract—Recently, Karimi and Banihashemi proposed an algorithm based on message-passing to count cycles in a graph of lengths less than double its girth. The algorithm uses only integer additions and subtractions to compute messages at the nodes of the graph that are passed to adjacent nodes. The complexity of the algorithm, when applied to a bipartite graph of girth g that has E edges, is O(gE2). The algorithm is superior to many other existing algorithms in the literature. In this paper, an improvement of this algorithm is presented that cuts both the complexity and the computing time by a factor of two. The improved algorithm is also applied to Tanner graphs of quasicyclic codes and, in this case, the complexity can be further cut by a factor of p, where p is the size of the circulants in the paritycheck matrix of the quasi-cyclic code. Keywords: Bipartite graph, Counting cycles, LDPC code, Message-passing algorithm, Tanner graph.
I. INTRODUCTION Numerous studies have shown that the performance of lowdensity parity-check (LDPC) codes decoded with iterative decoding techniques, such as the sum-product algorithm, depends on the girth and the number of short cycles in the Tanner graph used for decoding. Short cycles cause messages exchanged in iterative decoding to be correlated after a few iterations, which prohibits the decoding from converging or makes it converge slowly, see e.g., [1] and references therein. The important role that cycles play in the performance of LDPC codes motivates developing algorithms to efficiently count and find short cycles in Tanner graphs of LDPC codes. In the following, we consider a bipartite graph which can be thought of as the Tanner graph of a code that has n variable nodes, m check nodes, and E edges. Let g denote the girth of the graph. Fan and Xiao [2] developed a method to count cycles of length 4, 6, 8, and 10 with complexity O(mk+1), where k is half the cycle’s length. In [3], Halford and Chugg proposed an algorithm for counting cycles of length g, g + 2, and g + 4. The complexity of their algorithm is O(gλ3), where λ = max(m, n). In a recent paper [4], Karimi and Banihashemi proposed a message-passing algorithm to efficiently count short cycles of length g, g +2, . . . , 2g −2 with complexity O(gE2). In [5], they proposed another algorithm to count cycles of the same lengths in the Tanner graphs of quasi-cyclic (QC) protograph LDPC codes by calculating the eigenvalues of p Eb × Eb submatrices, where p is the lifting degree of the protogragh LDPC codes and Eb is the number of edges in the protograph. The complexity is O(pEb3). In [6], Lan et al. presented a method to find cycles of length g based on a trellis representing the graph. Their algorithm allows them to break cycles to obtain a new graph with larger
gg
girth. The complexity of their method is O(ndu2 dw2 ) for a

(du, dw)-regular bipartite graph, i.e., for which each variable node is of degree du and each check node is of degree dw.
In [4], several algorithms were compared for counting short
cycles and it was shown that the iterative message-passing algorithm in [4], which we denote in the following by the MPA, seems to be the most efficient. Our contribution is to present
an improvement on the MPA, which we call the improved message-passing algorithm (IMPA), that cuts the complexity and the computing time of the MPA by a factor of two. We also
present a simpler version of the IMPA that can be applied to the Tanner graphs of QC-LDPC codes that cuts the complexity further by a factor of p, where p is the size of the circulants in the parity-check matrix of the code.
Throughout this paper, G = (U ∪ W, E) denotes a bipratite graph which is the Tanner graph of an LDPC code where U = {u1, u2 . . . , un} is the set of variable nodes (VNs), W = {w1, w2, . . . , wm} is the set of check nodes (CNs), and E is the set of E edges, each connecting a VN to a CN. A path in G of length l is a sequence of nodes v1, v2, . . . , vl+1 in U ∪ W such that there is an edge in E connecting vi and vi+1 for 1 ≤ i ≤ l and v1, v2, . . . , vl are distinct. The path is a cycle if v1 = vl+1. The girth, g, of G is the length of a shortest cycle, which is even for a bipartite graph. By short cycles in a bipartite graph we mean cycles of lengths less than 2g. We only consider short cycles since message-passing algorithms,
such as the MPA and the IMPA, fail to distinguish between cycles and closed walks consisting of more than one cycle. The lengths of these closed walks are at least twice the girth.
Therefore, by restricting the algorithms to counting cycles of length less than 2g, all these closed walks that are not cycles are eliminated.
This paper is organized as follows. Section II gives a very brief review of the message-passing algorithm proposed in [4] which is the basis of our proposed improved algorithm
presented in Section III. In Section IV, we show that our algorithm can be simplified when applied to Tanner graphs of QC-LDPC codes. Section V concludes the paper.

A. The IMPA

In the MPA proposed in [4], to count the number of cycles

of length 2k, g ≤ 2k ≤ 2g −2, the algorithm has to go through

k iterations, i.e., 2k time slots. This is the time required for a

message sent from a node u to propagate through a cycle of

that length and to arrive back at u. We present an improvement

over this algorithm that allows for counting cycles of length 2k

after k time slots. The improvement is based on the following

observation. A cycle of length 2k passing through u and edges j and j′ incident to it can be traced starting from ej to ej′ and from ej′ to ej. Since the node u sends messages through all

edges incident to it, then there is a message propagating from

ej to ej′ and a message propagating from ej′ to ej. These two messages propagate through the same cycle. At time t = k −1,

these two messages are received by a node vt ∈ U ∪W through different edges. Receiving these two messages at time k − 1

proves the existence of a cycle passing through u of length

2(t + 1) = 2k. In particular, there is no need to wait for each

message to go through the complete cycle and arrive back at

node u at time 2k−1 in the k-th iteration to prove its existence.

This simple observation is the basis of our algorithm which

counts short cycles as explained next.

B. Complexity of the IMPA For a graph G = (U ∪W, E), the computational complexity
of the MPA in [4] is O(gE2). The operations required in the IMPA are the same as those in the MPA but it only requires half the number of iterations. Hence, the computational complexity
of the IMPA is about half that of the MPA. More importantly, since we reduce the number of iterations by a factor of two, the IMPA is almost twice faster than the MPA.
Regarding memory usage, the IMPA requires only one set of memory locations to store the messages. When CNs are active, they will first read the messages from the set of memory
locations (at this time, this set of memory locations stores the messages that VNs sent to CNs at the previous iteration), calculate messages which will be sent to VNs at the next iteration and then write back to the corresponding memory locations. It is similar for the VNs when active. Let dU and dW be the maximum degrees of a node in U and W, respectively. If the algorithm is initialized from the VNs, then the total number of memory locations needed is dU E while it is dW E if the algorithm is initialized from the CNs. Hence, the IMPA can be implemented with min(dU , dW )E memory locations. It is stated in [4], that the MPA requires 2duE memory locations for a regular graph.

A. The QC-IMPA

The IMPA described in the previous section can be applied

to any LDPC code. However, if the LDPC code is in QC form,

the algorithm for counting cycles can be further simplified. A

code is QC if it has a parity-check matrix H which is an array

of circulants of equal size, where each row in a circulant is

a cyclic shift of the row above it by one place to the right

and the first row is the cyclic shift of the last row one place

to the right. In most constructions of QC-LDPC codes, each

circulant either has a single 1 in each row, in which case it

is a circulant permutation matrix (CPM), or is a zero matrix

(ZM). In particular, let H = [As,r]0≤s<J,0≤r<L be a J × L array of p × p matrices, As,r’s, which are either CPMs or
ZMs. Then, H is a parity-check matrix of a QC-LDPC code

of length n = Lp.

For 0 ≤ s < J and 0 ≤ r < L, suppose that As,r is a CPM in which the (is, jr) entry is 1, where 0 ≤ is, jr < p. All other entries in the is-th row and in the jr-th column are zeros. We use the pair of two ordered tuples, ((s, r), (is, jr)), to indicate
the location of this 1-entry in the overall array H, where the

first tuple, (s, r), identifies the CPM, As,r, containing this 1entry and the second tuple, (is, jr), identifies the location of the 1-entry within the CPM. Since As,r is a CPM, then its p 1-entries are located at ((s, t), ((is + l)p, (jr + l)p)), for 0 ≤ l < p, where (i)p, for any integer i, denotes the nonnegative integer equal to i modulo p.

QC-LDPC code corresponding to the parity-check matrix

B. Complexity of the QC-IMPA The computational complexity of the QC-IMPA is 1/p of
that of the IMPA, where p is the size of the circulants in the
parity-check matrix of the QC-LDPC code. In particular, it is O(gE2/p). (This can be reduced further to O(gE2/n) if the parity-check matrix used has a row-block cyclic structure.)
The memory usage of the QC-IMPA is the same as that of the
IMPA.
In [5], the authors proposed an algorithm to count short
cycles of QC protograph LDPC codes by calculating the eigenvalues of p matrices, each of size E/p × E/p. (Here, p corresponds to the lifting degree denoted by N in [5] while E/p denotes the number of edges in the protograph denoted by Eb in [5]. For comparison, we only consider (du, dw)-regular QC-LDPC codes. However, the results can also be applied to
irregular QC-LDPC codes.) The computational complexity of
this algorithm which involves addition and multiplication of complex numbers is O(E3/p2). Compared to the complexity O(gE2/p) of the proposed algorithm which involves only
addition and subtraction of integer numbers, the proposed algorithm has lower complexity if E grows faster than gp, which is the case for many classes of QC-LDPC codes. In terms of memory requirement, for a (du, dw)-regular QCLDPC code, the proposed algorithm needs min(du, dw)E memory locations. Compared to the memory requirement E2/p of the algorithm in [5], the proposed QC-IMPA requires less memory locations, corresponding to a reduction by a factor of max(m, n)/p.

V. CONCLUSION In this paper, we presented an improvement over the message-passing algorithm proposed in [4] to count short cycles in bipartite graphs. We also presented a simpler version of our algorithm in case the bipartite graph is the Tanner graph of a QC-LDPC code with parity-check matrix composed of CPMs and ZMs. The proposed algorithms are faster and require less computational complexity and memory usage compared to other algorithms.
