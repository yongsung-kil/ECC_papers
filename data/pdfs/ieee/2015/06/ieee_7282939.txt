Abstract—Alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) decoding is a new decoding framework for low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. It can be used to implement linear programming (LP) decoding or penalized LP decoding. Similar to belief propagation (BP) decoding, ADMM decoding consists of local “check” and “variable updates”. However, ADMM decoding performs better than BP at high signal-to-noise ratios (SNRs). To understand why these two locally operating algorithms result in different error floor behaviors, we study the dynamics of ADMM decoding in this paper. In particular, we focus on trapping sets, which are observed to cause error floors in ADMM decoding. Our results show that the dynamics of ADMM decoding on trapping sets can be characterized as a jump linear system. Furthermore, these results indicate that the Lagrange multipliers involved in ADMM play an important role in correcting trapping set errors. Finally, we present simulation results that support this understanding.
I. INTRODUCTION
It is well known that low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes suffer from an “error-floor” effect when message passing decoding algorithms are used [1], [2]. This behavior limits the use of (pure) LDPC codes in applications such as magnetic recording and fiber-optic transport networks. An alternative to message passing decoding is linear programming (LP) decoding, which is introduced by Feldman et al. in [3]. Many studies reveal a close relationship between LP decoding and message passing decoding (e.g., [4]). However, the two algorithms have different empirical performances. Monte Carlo simulation results from [5]–[8] show that LP decoding performs worse than BP decoding at low SNRs but better at high SNRs. On the other hand, instanton analysis results presented in [9]–[11] indicate that LP decoding achieves a lower error rate than BP decoding at asymptotic high SNRs.
In [8], the authors use the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) to develop a distributed LP decoding algorithm. ADMM decoding is extended in [12] to decode a penalized LP objective. The simulation results in [12] show that ADMM penalized decoding is surprisingly promising, because it can outperform BP decoding at low SNRs and retain LP decoding-like behavior at high SNRs. We now summarize a few key aspects of these three algorithms, and pose an intriguing question. First, results for ADMM LP decoding, ADMM penalized decoding, and BP decoding presented

in [12] are all based on non-quantized and non-saturating (for BP) implementations. This ensures that BP decoding is not suffering from quantization or saturation effects, which are two reasons known to cause error floors [13], [14]. Second, all three decoders are based on “local” update rules. Thus, it is inevitable that decoding messages pass through cycles in the factor graph. Finally, similar to BP decoding, trapping sets cause difficulties for both (ADMM) LP decoding and ADMM penalized decoding, as is observed via an instanton analysis in [10], [12]. However, although at sufficiently high SNRs, trapping sets cause problems for both ADMM LP decoding and ADMM penalized decoding, these effects are realized only at higher SNRs than they are for BP (see, e.g., [12]). These considerations lead to the following question: What is the key aspect of in ADMM decoding that makes it outperform BP decoding at high SNRs?
In this work, we initiate the study of this problem by characterizing the dynamics of ADMM on trapping sets. Although there is no difference between ADMM LP decoding and other LP decoding algorithms in terms of decoding results, we do not follow the convex analysis path taken in the literature originating in [15]. The reason is two-fold. First, we need tools that can also be applied to ADMM penalized decoding, which is a non-convex problem. Second, due to the observation that LP decoding also suffer from trapping sets, we focus on trapping sets specific ADMM updates to try to understand the algorithm’s behavior. We list our contributions in this paper.
• We prove some fundamental properties of a necessary projection onto the parity polytope. We show that, under some conditions suitable for trapping sets, the projection is a linear operation (Section III-B).
• We present a novel jump linear system characterization for ADMM LP decoding on trapping sets. We show a theorem to calculate stationary states (Section III-C).
• We observe from our analysis that the Lagrange multipliers involved in ADMM play an important role in correcting trapping set errors, a claim supported by empirical simulation results (Section IV).

B. Trapping set of LDPC codes
Definition 1: (cf. [16]) A (w, u) trapping set of an LDPC code is a set of w variable nodes that includes a sub-graph with u odd degree check nodes and arbitrary number of even degree check nodes.
In this work, we make the following assumptions regarding trapping sets.
Assumption 1: (Trapping set assumptions)
1.1 The all-zeros codeword is sent. 1.2 We consider trapping sets that are elementary, i.e., check
nodes of the induced subgraph are of degree one or two. 1.3 The messages within the trapping set have little impact
on the rest of the graph. This assumption is used in [13], [17] to derive linear system model for BP decoding.

III. ADMM LP BEHAVIOR ON TRAPPING SETS

In this section, we study ADMM dynamics on trapping sets. We focus on the LP decoding problem, and briefly discuss how to extend the tools to ADMM penalized decoding in Section V.

A. Simplifications of ADMM decoding on trapping sets
The exact ADMM decoding algorithm is quite complex, so we simplify the problem using the following assumptions.
1) Using instanton analysis to simplify the objective function: First, we simplify the problem by letting the loglikelihood ratio γ takes onto two values: γi = 1 or γi = C, where C is a negative real number. Further, we let γi = C for all i in the trapping set of interest. The reason for this assumption originates from the instanton and pseudocodeword analysis results in [10], [12], [18]. First, it is there observed that the most destructive noise configurations often contain only a few non-zero entries. Second, the largest several nonzero entries are often related to trapping sets. Third, these largest non-zeros entries often have values that are close. Combining the above three points, the log-likelihood ratios for the most destructive noise configurations can be simplified to having two values. Because we can divide γ by a constant without affecting the decoding results, we normalize the positive value to 1.
2) ADMM initialization: Since ADMM solves the LP decoding problem, the initialization of ADMM does not affect LP decoding results. The following initializations make the upcoming analysis in Section III-C easier.
1) xi = 1 for all i in the trapping set. 2) zj = (1, 1, 0, . . . , 0) if dc(j) is even, and zj =
(1, 0, . . . , 0) if dc(j) is odd. 3) λj are set to zero.
This initialization is a worst case scenario. It corresponds to the case where all variables inside a trapping set are initially guessed erroneously by the decoder.
3) ADMM updates: Note that due to Assumption 1.3, for each ADMM iteration, the variables and replicas outside the trapping set are always 0. Unfortunately, even with all the above simplifications, the x-update and the z-update are still nonlinear. The x-update is nonlinear due to the projection onto the [0, 1] box, and the z-update is nonlinear due to the projection onto the parity polytope. However, the x-update is linear if the box constraint is inactive. We show in the next section that the projection onto the parity polytope is linear under some conditions. We will show in Section III-C that executing ADMM decoding on elementary trapping sets can satisfy these conditions.

V. DISCUSSIONS
In this paper, we identify key aspects that make ADMM decoding perform better than BP decoding at high SNRs.
First, we conclude from our analysis that the Lagrange multipliers drive a cascading type decoding behavior of ADMM decoding on trapping sets. The multiplier associated with a variable xi and replica zj(i) keeps increasing if the variable does not agree with the replica. Eventually, the multiplier will grow sufficiently large that the equality constraints enforced by the multiplier will overwhelm the incorrect channel evidence internal to the trapping set. At this point, xi is corrected. This intuition matches our empirical simulation results.
Second, Theorem 1 indicates that if all variables are floating, the only possible stationary point is the all-zeros vector. We omit the proof and note that it can be proved by contradiction. This result means that if the simplifications in Section III-A1 are valid, then LP decoding can correct any trapping set (cf. Example 1). We note the key assumption that can be violated in practice is Assumption 1.3. Understanding how ADMM messages inside trapping sets influence messages outside trapping sets is an important direction of research.
Finally, ADMM penalized decoding works with slightly modified x-update rules. Note that the 1 penalized decoder (cf. [12] for details) is piece-wise linear. This means the

dynamics continue to behave like a jump linear system.
However, the analysis would be slightly different. A notable
fact is that, for the 1 penalized decoder, the first transition point is at a larger iteration number than that for LP decod-
ing per Proposition 1. Furthermore, the iteration of the first
transition point increases as the penalty coefficient involved
in penalized decoding (cf. [12] for details) is increased. This
delay of the first transition point indicates that a large penalty
coefficient makes correcting trapping set errors more difficult.
This intuition agrees with an observation made via an instanton
analysis in [12]. There it is observed that a large penalty
coefficient results in worse high SNR performance.
