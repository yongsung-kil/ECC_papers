Design of Finite-Length Rate-Compatible LDPC Codes

Min Xiao
Department of Communication Engineering Xiamen University of Technology Xiamen, China e-mail: xiaomin_xmut@163.com

Abstract—This paper considers rate-compatible puncturing for finite-length low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. Among the existing systematic puncturing methods, the grouping and sorting algorithm (GSA) stands out for its good performance and easy implementation, but as is proven in this paper, there exists a limit on the amount of puncturing that can be done, which restricts the range of the achievable code rates as well as the choice of the mother code. A new puncturing algorithm with better flexibility is developed. The new algorithm can be generally applied to an arbitrary irregular or regular LDPC code to obtain a code rate arbitrarily close to 1, and at the same time to achieve performance that is superior to the GSA.
Keywords-LDPC codes; rate-compatible; puncturing; wireless communication
I. INTRODUCTION
In most wireless communication systems, the channels are time varying. In order to maximize the throughput, a hybrid automatic repeat-request (HARQ) scheme can be used in combination with rate-compatible (RC) errorcorrection codes, codes whose rates can be easily changed from high rates with light error protection but high bandwidth efficiency, to low rates with heavier redundancy and much stronger error protection [1].
A widely-used method to achieve rate compatibility is puncturing. Since the renaissance of low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes [2], a number of papers have investigated issues around the design of good ratecompatible punctured LDPC (RCP-LDPC) codes. The design of finite-length RCP-LDPC codes is much harder than infinite-length ones. This is because with finite-lengths, the underlying Tanner graphs will inevitably have short cycles and possibly many of them. Uniformly random puncturing patterns were used in the early finite-length RCPLDPC codes which had relatively severe performance loss [1] [3]. Subsequently, several non-random algorithms were presented to find good puncturing patterns for RCP-LDPC codes [4]-[6]. There have also been studies about the design of puncturing algorithms for some special classes of LDPC codes [7]-[9].
A useful concept of recoverable step was introduced in [4] to denote how fast a punctured variable node (PVN) can be recovered in the decoding of a punctured LDPC code. This concept has since been adopted in many subsequent papers [6][7]. Based on this concept, [4] proposed an algorithm

The GSA can be applied to all the LDPC codes, but the highest puncturing rate is often limited. Below we will first derive an upper bound that limits the highest puncturing rate achievable by the GSA, and show that this limitation can be severe for some mother codes. In an effort to overcome such rate limitation and to generate good RCP-LDPC codes with a wide rate range, we develop in this paper a new puncturing algorithm called recoverability acceleration algorithm(RAA) that exercises a philosophy exactly the opposite to the GSA. We show that this RAA puncturing algorithm can be applied to arbitrary LDPC codes and achieve code rates arbitrarily close to 1. Further, the resultant RCP-LDPC codes perform on par with or slightly better than those from the GSA. Hence this RAA is more advantageous than the GSA in terms of being more flexible and general while maintaining the same high standard of performance.
II. BACKGROUND OF GSA
The concept of recovery originates from the decoding process of binary erasure channels (BEC), when an erased variable node gets some message in the message-passing decoding. This concept is extended to additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels by [4]. A punctured variable node, one that has no channel observation and hence zero initial message, is said to be recovered when it receives a non-zero message from one of its neighboring check nodes, regardless of how reliable the message actually is; and the number of decoding iterations when this first happens is called the recovery step.
Definition1 [1-step recoverable (1-SR) node]: A PVN is called a one-step recoverable node if it has at least one neighboring check node whose other connected variable nodes are all unpunctured. And this check node is called a survived check node for the PVN.
Definition 2 [k-step recoverable (k-SR) node]: A PVN is called a k-step recoverable node if a PVN has at least one neighboring check node which has at least one (kí1)-SR node and the other neighboring check nodes are m-SR nodes, where 0 d m d k 1 .
From the above definition, it is easily seen that a k-SR node can be recovered after k decoding iterations. The group containing all the k-SR nodes is defined as k-SR group and

The GSA is based on the assumption that the more iterations a PVN needs for its recovery, the less statistically reliable the recovery message is [4]. Therefore its purpose is to maximize the size of G k for small k >0. A systematic procedure is developed in [4] for determining exactly which variable nodes should be punctured and in which order. The algorithm consists of two steps: grouping, followed by sorting. In the grouping step, as many 1-SR nodes as possible are firstly grouped into G1 . When no more 1-SR nodes can be found, which means the size of G1 has been maximized, it proceeds to 2-SR nodes, and so on. The variable nodes in G1 are selected to be punctured first, followed by the variable nodes in G2 , etc. If there are more
than one k-SR nodes in Gk (where k t 2 ), the second step
(sorting) will choose the variable nodes with more survived check nodes and larger degrees to be punctured first.

B. Recoverability acceleration algorithm
Clearly, maximizing the size of 1-SR group, such as what is being done in GSA, is but one attempt to maintain good performance. Fundamentally we wish to identify a puncturing pattern that will result in an “optimal”
arrangement of i-SR groups, for i=1,2,  . The optimality
will ultimately be judged by the code performance (bit error rate or BER, for example), but conceptually, it should include, on one end, maximizing the size of 1-SR group followed by maximizing 2-SR group and so on, and on the
other end, minimizing the highest recovery level K and minimizing the size of K -SR group followed by minimizing (K -1)-SR group and so on. These two ends of objectives
clearly compete and interfere with each other, and hence an overall optimal solution is far from being obvious or tractable. The GSA has essentially provided a feasible means of handling the problem through the greedy approach,

Through a careful and extensive study, we have found that, rather than 1-SR group, the highest-SR group may actually play a more important role in the punctured code performance. This observation motivates us to propose a different puncturing algorithm that is coming from the opposite end, namely, to
minimize the highest recovery level K and the size of K -

SR group. This change of philosophy leads to a new puncturing algorithm, which we call recoverability acceleration algorithm, which not only improves the performance of the GSA, but also overcomes the rate limitation imposed by the GSA.
To start controlling from the low end of recovery levels, such as 1-SR group followed by 2-SR group, is a simple and nature procedure. To proceed from the other direction is less obvious. To gain control on the highest level of recoverability and the size of the highest-SR group requires the prediction of recoverable steps for all the PVNs when a candidate variable node v is punctured. After a candidate variable node v is assumed to be punctured, let l(v), s(v) and r(v) denote the highest level of recoverability of all the PVNs, the size of the highest-SR group and the number of survived check nodes neighboring v, respectively.

Comments:

In the RAA, Step3 and Step4 are inspired by the sorting process of the GSA. Obviously, the more survived check nodes a PVN has, the more reliable its recovery message is [4]. Choosing variable nodes with the smallest degree is equivalent to choosing variable nodes with the smallest number of deed check [4].
The proposed RAA is similar to the GSA in that both are greedy approaches, but they differ in the following ways. First, they come from the two opposite and competing ends of the recovery group. While the GSA aims to maximize the size of Gk for all small k from k=1 to2 and up, the RAA
concentrates only on the highest level of recoverability and the size of the highest-SR group, and works to retard the growth of them. Secondly, in the GSA once a variable node has been selected in a group, it remains in that group and its recoverable step can not be updated throughout the process. In contrast, in the RAA the recoverable step of each PVN can be and is updated during the course of the puncturing process, which provides more flexibility and adaptability.
The complexity of the RAA is O(n3) , which is higher
than that of the GSA, but the RAA is applicable on an arbitrary LDPC mother code and achieve an arbitrary high code rate. The algorithm selects one variable node to be punctured per round, so we can ensure the resultant set of codes are rate-compatible and if so desired, achieves the finest rate granularity possible.
IV. SIMULATIONS
To confirm the efficacy of the proposed algorithm, we present in this section performance simulation results of the punctured regular and irregular LDPC codes resulted from the RAA. For comparison, we also present those of the GSA using the same mother codes. The encoded bits are sent through binary-input AWGN channels. For decoding simulation, we use the sum-product algorithm and set the number of maximum iterations as 100. Each simulation point is collected over the statistics of 100 block errors.
We consider a rate-1/2 (3,6) regular LDPC code as the mother code. The code has 512 information bits, and is constructed randomly and free of 4-cycles. The GSA and the RAA are implemented to puncture the mother code to rate 0.6,0.7,0.8 and 0.9. With the GSA since one can only find 269 1-SR nodes, 50 2-SR nodes and 2 3-SR nodes in the mother code, the highest punctured code rate is limited to 0.728. Beyond this puncturing limit, we apply random puncturing to get higher code rates, in order to compare with the proposed RAA. The performances and the group distributions of the punctured regular LDPC codes with these two algorithms are shown in Figure 1 and Table 1, respectively. Because of the partial use of random puncturing in this GSA example, the highest level of recoverability found in the rate-0.9 punctured code goes up to as high as 49! Hence we have to leave out the group distribution of the 0.9-rate punctured codes to save page space.

As pointed out in [4], the puncturing performances at the intermediate rates (0.6 and 0.7) do not totally depend on the levels of recoverability. When the rates go up very high (0.8 and 0.9), the levels of recoverability and especially the highest level become an increasingly dominating factor for the code performance. Sadly for the GSA, because of its puncturing limit, another worse-performing puncturing method such as random puncturing has to be used in order to achieve the desired high code rate, which may result in uncontrollably high levels of recoverability and cause performance deterioration. In comparison, the RAA can effectively control the worst case (i.e. highest level of recoverability and the size of the highest-SR group) throughout the process, rendering consistently good performance over a wide range of code rates.

I. CONCLUSION
The existing GSA puncturing algorithm results in very good RCP-LDPC codes, except that the highest achievable code rate is rather limited (unless the mother code is carefully designed for the purpose of GSA puncturing rather than targeting the performance). We have developed a new greedy puncturing algorithm that aims to accelerate the recoverability for PVNs. The new algorithm not only

outperforms the GSA, but is also generally applicable to any LDPC code, and can achieve code rates arbitrarily close to 1.
