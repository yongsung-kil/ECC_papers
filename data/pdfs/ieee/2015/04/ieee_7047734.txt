Anytime Reliability of Spatially Coupled Codes
Md. Noor-A-Rahim, Student Member, IEEE, Khoa D. Nguyen, Member, IEEE, and Gottfried Lechner, Member, IEEE

Abstract—Anytime transmission was previously shown to be necessary and sufficient for tracking and controlling an unstable plant over a noisy channel. In this paper, we propose an efficient channel coding scheme for anytime transmission. We design this coding scheme based on spatially coupled codes and investigate its performance over binary erasure channels (BEC) and binary input additive white Gaussian noise (BIAWGN) channels. Through density evolution analysis, we asymptotically show the desired anytime properties of the proposed code. For the BEC, we derive the corresponding delay-exponent and numerically predict the finite-length performance of the proposed anytime code. We also propose adaptive window decoding techniques to ensure low complexity anytime receivers.
Index Terms—Spatially coupled LDPC codes, anytime codes, anytime reliability, BEC, AWGN channel.
I. INTRODUCTION
R ELIABLE streaming of information over a noisy channel in real-time is known to be a relevant problem in communication engineering [1]. Such transmission is particularly desired for wireless automation, where real-time system state information from remote observers is required to determine appropriate control actions. Traditional block and convolutional codings are inappropriate for such applications because block codes attain reliability only at infinite delays; while convolutional codes with finite memory-length result in non-zero error probability. Both approaches result in instability in closed loop controls [2]. Other approaches, including dynamic programming that jointly optimizes control and transmission functions, are introduced for control applications [3]. However, dynamic programming suffers from high computational complexity and is intractable in practical applications. Delay-universal/anytime transmission [4], on the other hand, allows for separating the transmission problem from controller design and therefore is a promising approach.

Anytime transmission feasibility was proved with sequential random codes in [4] and [5], but the codes were not suitable for practical implementation. The existence of anytime linear codes was shown in [6], where the anytime behavior of timeinvariant codes under maximum likelihood (ML) decoding was investigated. A causal anytime encoding scheme using unequal error protection (UEP) rateless codes was studied in [7]. Later, anytime reliability of low-density parity-check (LDPC) convolutional codes over BEC and BIAWGN channels was proved in [8] and [9], respectively. However, these codes suffer from error floors at large decoding delay and have limited practicality due to their high decoding complexity.1 Thus, it is essential to design low complexity anytime codes, which provide better performance than existing anytime codes and permit a tractable finite-length analysis.
In this work, we propose the design of anytime codes based on spatially coupled LDPC codes. Although the anytime code proposed in [8] and our proposed anytime code are both LDPC convolutional codes, they differ in the following manner. For the code in [8], the degrees of variable and check nodes increase linearly with the decoding time instant. On the other hand, we propose a code structure with fixed variable and check nodes degrees. Through density evolution analysis, we show that our proposed anytime codes exhibit the desired anytime transmission properties. For the BEC, we find an analytical bound for the corresponding delay-exponent using mathematical induction technique and we provide an algorithm to estimate the finite-length performance of the proposed anytime code. We also develop low complexity decoders for both BEC and BIAWGN channels.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. In Section II, we describe the background of anytime transmission and spatially coupled codes. The design of anytime codes based on spatially coupled codes is proposed in Section III. In Section IV, the anytime characteristics of the proposed anytime codes are investigated for the BEC. We present the algorithm for finite-length performance estimation and propose a low complexity anytime decoder. We also present simulation results along with the performance comparison between proposed and existing anytime codes. We extend our analysis to BIAWGN channels in Section V, where we show the anytime transmission properties of the proposed codes and develop the adaptive window decoding algorithms under belief propagation decoding.

According to the anytime code construction mentioned in
Section III, the resultant code structure leads to a lower triangular unterminated check matrix H. The lower triangular
property of the check matrix enables us to perform decoding with a portion of H, i.e., a portion which is associated with the
received bits. We denote the active portion of the check matrix H at time t as Ht.
Let at × nt be the size of the active check matrix Ht. We define the parity vector pt = [p1, p2, . . . , pat ] ∈ {0, 1}at , where pi is the parity of coded bits associated to the ith row of the check matrix. We initialize pt with all zeros. At each decoding time instant, the adaptive window decoder removes the recovered coded bits and the corresponding columns of Ht. If the value of a recovered coded bit j is 1, then we flip the value of associated parity components, i.e., pi = pi ⊕[Ht]ijbj, where [Ht]ij denotes the entry of Ht at the ith row and jth column and bj (j ∈ {1, 2, . . . , nt}) denotes the value of the jth coded bit. Then the all-zero rows of the check matrix are removed. Therefore we get a reduced size check matrix Hr and the decoding algorithm runs on the corresponding Tanner graph of Hr. From the conventional erasure decoding algorithm [10], the check node update rule is modified in the following
manner. In the first iteration, we recover all the variable nodes
that are connected with a check node of degree one. Then at each iteration, a check node C (let C corresponds to ith row of Ht) sends to a connected variable node V an erasure if it receives at least one erasure from the connected variable nodes other than V ; otherwise C sends to V the sum (mod 2) of pi and incoming messages from all connected variable nodes other than V .
