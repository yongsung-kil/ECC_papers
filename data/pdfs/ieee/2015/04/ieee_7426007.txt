IMPROVED QUASI-CYCLIC LDPC CODES BASED ON EUCLIDEAN GEOMETRY
LIU Yuan-hua, ZHANG Mei-ling*
*School of communication and information engineering, Xi’an University of Posts and Telecommunications, Xi’an, 710121, China, Email: yuanhliu@163.com

Keywords: Low-density parity-check codes; quasi-cyclic; Euclidean geometry; circulant permutation matrix
Abstract
This paper presents an improved Euclidean geometry (EG) method for constructing parity check matrix of quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes to decrease the performance degradation of short cycles to the iterative decoding. The new method results in a class of QC-LDPC codes with much fewer short cycles than usual EG-LDPC codes with the same parameters. Simulation results show that the designed QC-LDPC codes with fewer short cycles perform better than the existing Euclidean geometry LDPC codes under iterative decoding.
1 Introduction
Due to the ease of implementation and excellent performance, quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes are widely applied in deep space communication, optical fiber communication and Digital Video Broadcasting-Satellite, etc. Random LDPC codes generated by computer search can be designed with flexible parameters, such as code lengths and rates, etc. However, without some algebraic structure, random LDPC codes have high encoding complexity and large hardware storage requirement, which have been the major restrictive factor for the development of practical. Although random LDPC codes with length long enough perform excellently, QC-LDPC codes can lead to much simpler implementations. Furthermore, for practical code lengths, it has been shown [1-10] that well-designed QC-LDPC codes can perform as well as random LDPC codes.
Most Euclidean geometry (EG) LDPC codes are cyclic or quasi-cyclic. They can easily be encoded using simple shiftregisters with linear complexity. QC-LDPC codes can solve the hardware memory problem due to their algebraic structure. The required hardware memory for storing QC-LDPC codes can be reduced by a factor 1/n, when n×n circulant permutation matrices or the zero matrices are employed. Meanwhile, EG-LDPC codes are suitable for various decoding algorithms with different complexity, which provide good trade-off between the decoding complexity, decoding speed and error correction performance.

As known that good LDPC codes tend to have large girth and a small number of short cycles, since the short cycles in the Tanner graph of LDPC code will result in the correlation of the message passed along the edges in the Tanner graph, which will degrade the performance of iterative decoding. The more the short cycles, the more serious correlation of the passed messages and the worse performance of iterative decoding will be. Although the EG-LDPC codes presented in [8] are free of 4-cycles, there exist also a certain number of 6cycles, which will degrade the performance of iterative decoding. To decrease the number of short cycles in the Tanner graph, an improved method for constructing QCLDPC codes is presented in this paper. The improved method can construct QC-LDPC codes with fewer short cycles, and therefore reduce the influence of short cycle to the effectives of iterative decoding. Simulation results show that the new codes with fewer short cycles perform better than the existing Euclidean geometry LDPC codes under iterative decoding.

The rest of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 presents a brief review of QC-LDPC codes. The improved method for constructing QC-LDPC codes based on Euclidean geometries is proposed in Section 3. Simulation results of several existing EG-LDPC codes and the improved EGLDPC codes designed by the proposed method are compared in Section 4, the distributions of cycles in the Tanner graphs of these codes are also analyzed. Finally, Section 5 concludes the paper.

3 Quasi-cyclic LDPC codes based on Euclidean Geometries
It is known that the iterative decoding has the optimal performance on the condition that the corresponding Tanner graph is free of cycles. Since the presence of short cycles in the Tanner graph has negative effect on information convergence. To improve the performance, LDPC codes should be designed to avoid short cycles, especially 4-cycles and 6-cycles. Although the QC-LDPC codes constructed based on the structural properties of Euclidean geometries in [8] are free of 4-cycles, they also contain a certain number of 6-cycles, which degrade the performance of iterative decoding.

To reduce the number of short cycles and increase the girth, we present an improved method to design QC-LDPC code with various lengths and rates. Two steps of the improved method are detailed described as follows. First, a large sparse matrix without 4-cycles is designed based on the structural properties of Euclidean geometry, and then the rows and columns are carefully selected to form the parity check matrix H after analyzing the distribution of short cycles.

Firstly, we introduce the construction of the initial matrix without 4-cycles. Let EG(m,ps) be an m-dimensional Euclidean geometry over Galois field GF(ps), where p is a
prime and m and s are two positive integers. This geometry consists of points and lines. A point is an m-tuple over GF(ps) and hence there are pms points in EG(m,ps). The all-zero mtuple is called the origin of the geometry. These pms points form an m-dimensional vector space over GF(ps). Therefore, EG(m,ps) is simply the m-dimensional vector space of all the m-tuples over GF(ps). A line in EG(m,ps) is either a onedimensional subspace of EG(m,ps) or a coset of a one-

dimensional subspace. Therefore, a line in EG(m,ps) consists of ps points and there are p(m-1)s(pms-1) ˋ (ps-1) lines in EG(m,ps). If a is a point on a line L, we say that the line L passes through a. If two lines have a common point a, we say that they intersect at a.
An m-dimensional Euclidean geometry EG(m,ps) has the following structural properties: 1) every line consists of ps points; 2) any two points are connected by one and only one line; 3) every point is intersected by (pms-1)ˋ(ps-1) lines; 4)
any two lines are either parallel (i.e., they have no point in common) or they intersect at one and only one point.
Let EG*(m,ps) be the sub-geometry obtained by removing the origin and the lines passing through the origin from EG(m,ps). Then EG*(m,ps) consists of n=pms-1 points and J=(p(m-1)s-1) (pms-1)ˋ(ps-1) lines. Let Į be a primitive element of GF(pms) and ai=Įi-1(1in) represent the i-th point in EG*(m,ps). The incidence vector of ai is defined as an n-tuple v(ai)=(v1, v 2,…, v n) over GF(2), where the i-th component is ‘1’ and all the other components are equal to ‘0’. A line L in EG*(m,ps) consists of ps non-origin points in EG(m,ps). The incidence vector of L is defined as an nps-tuple vL=(v1, v2,…, vps) over GF(2), which consists of ps sections such that the i-th section vi corresponds to the i-th point on L (i.e., vi is the incidence vector of the i-th point on L).
The lines in EG*(m,ps) have the following structural property: If {Įi,Įj,Įk,… } is a line in EG*(m,ps), then {Įi+1,Įj+1,Įk+1,… } is another line in EG*(m,ps). Therefore, the incidence vector of a line in EG*(m,ps) has the following structural property: a vector obtained by cyclically shifting each section vi of vL to the right by the same time is the incidence vector of another line in EG*(m,ps). Based on the structural property, the incidence vectors of all the J lines in EG*(m,ps) can be partitioned into K=(p(m-1)s-1)ˋ(ps-1) cyclic classes. Each of these K cyclic classes consists of n=pms-1 incidence vectors, each vector in a class can be obtained by cyclically shifting each section of any other vector in the class. Therefore, each incidence vector in a cyclic class can be used as the representative of the class.
Example 1: Consider the 2-dimensional Euclidean geometry EG(2,21). Let Į be a primitive element of GF(22×1). The incidence vector of the point a1=Į0 is (1 0 0). The incidence vector of the line L1={Į0,Į2} has two sections and vL1=(1 0 0 0 0 1). The sub-geometry EG*(2,21) has J=3 lines and the incidence vectors of these lines can be partitioned into K=1 cyclic class. 4 Simulation results

Secondly, we introduce the selection of submatrix from the initial matrix. From equation (1), we can see that H is a ps×K array of n×n circulant permutation matrices, each column corresponds to one code bit and each row corresponds to a parity check equation. Divide the rows and columns of H into groups with each group consisting of n rows or columns, then H has ps row groups and K column groups and each group (row group or column group) corresponds to an n×n circulant permutation matrix. The iterative method for selecting the submatrix is as follows. We choose the row groups first, and then select the column groups. At each iteration, the number of short cycles of each row group in the current submatrix is counted and the row group with the largest number of short cycles is removed, and then the number of short cycles of each column group in the current submatrix is counted and the column group with the largest number of short cycles is removed. The iterative process continues until the required numbers of rows and columns are reached.
As mentioned above, the initial matrix H has no 4-cycles, it follows that we need only count the cycles with length great than or equal to 6. The longer the cycles, the smaller the influence on the iterative decoding, therefore we simply need count the cycles with length 6 and 8. At each iteration, the number of short cycles with length 6 of each row group (or column group) in the current submatrix is counted, and if there are more than one row group (or column group) with the largest number of 6-cycles, the numbers of short cycles with length 8 of these row groups (or column groups) will be counted. The row group (or column group) will be selected random to remove if there are more than one row group (or column group) with the largest number of 8-cycles.

5 Conclusion
An algebraic method for the design of QC-LDPC codes based on the structural properties of Euclidean geometries has been proposed. Based on the structural properties of Euclidean geometry, a large sparse matrix without 4-cycles is designed. Then by deleting the rows and columns related to the maximum number of short cycles after analyzing the distribution of short cycles, the proposed method results in a class of QC-LDPC codes with much fewer short cycles than usual EG-LDPC codes with the same parameters. Simulation results show that codes constructed by using the proposed method perform very close to the Shannon limit with iterative decoding and perform better than the existing Euclidean geometry LDPC codes.
