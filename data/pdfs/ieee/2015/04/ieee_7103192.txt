Abstract-We analyze the performance of two short low­
density parity-check codes recently proposed for updating the 
telecommand standard for space links. We show that the most 
reliable basis decoding algorithm can be efficiently applied 
to these codes, permitting us to achieve a significant coding 
gain with respect to more conventional iterative algorithms. In 
particular, in order to maintain limited complexity, we suggest 
to use a hybrid approach, that combines the two decoding 
procedures in a sequential manner. Thinking in terms of practical 
implementation, we also investigate the impact of quantization, 
by considering two different quantization laws and comparing 
their performance. 
Index Terms-Low-density parity-check codes, most reliable 
basis decoding, space missions, telecommand links. 
I. INTRODUCTION 
Since a long time, error correcting codes have been used 
to increase the reliability of space links [1]. This is particu­
larly true for telemetry (TM) that exploits advanced coding 
schemes, from convolutional codes to Reed-Solomon codes 
(also in a concatenated configuration), to parallel turbo codes 
and, more recently, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. 
The situation is a bit different for telecommands (TCs). TCs 
are used to initiate, modify, or terminate equipment functions 
on an associated space object (e.g., a spacecraft, a satellite 
or a probe). In this sense, they are typically used in uplink 
communications. The data rates are usually very low and this 
implies a reduced criticality as regards to (the need for) error 
correction. In addition, TCs originate on the ground, with 
fewer limits and constraints on the available power. For these 
(and other) reasons, the only error correcting code included till 
today in the CCSDS recommendation [2] and the European 
Cooperation for Space Standardization (ECSS) standard [3] 
for TC synchronization and channel coding is the expurgated 
Bose-Chaudhuri-Hocquenghem (BCH) code with n 
= 63, 
k 
= 56 and hard-decision decoding. On the other hand, 
reliable TCs are of fundamental importance for any space 
mission. As a matter of fact, the success of a mission may 
be compromised because of the denial of TC signal reception

by the spacecraft. At the same time, the amount and quality

and the data rates are increasing as well. This implies the need 
to reinforce the error correcting schemes for TCs, too, and a 
deep discussion is currently in progress on this topic. 
The TC requirements depend on the type of data. The 
major criticalities are determined by emergency commands 
that must be very short, with a very low data rate and 
limited latency. This suggests the adoption of short codes. The 
required codeword error rate (CER) is in the order of 10-5. 
To reach this value with the standard BCH code, assuming a 
binary phase shift keying (BPSK) modulation over an additive 
white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel, signal-to-noise ratios 
(expressed as the ratio Eb/ No between the energy per bit and 
the one-side spectral density of the thermal noise) as large 
as Eb/No :::; 9.1 dB are required. By applying soft-decision 
decoding, that however is not considered in the standard, 
for example by using the BCJR algorithm [4], a gain in 
the order of 2.1 dB can be achieved. These signal-to-noise 
ratios, however, are too high for future missions, that shall 
be characterized by a significant increase of the supported 
data rate and/or maximum distance with respect to the present 
scenarios. 
For this reason, some new advanced coding schemes have 
been proposed and evaluated in [5], with the target to obtain 
high coding gains. Among the several options, short binary 
LDPC codes with rate Re = 1/2 have been recently identified 
as the most attractive candidates. These codes can be decoded 
by using classical iterative algorithms (lAs), like the sum­
product algorithm (SPA) or its simplified versions, e.g., min­
sum (MS) or normalized min-sum (NMS). This way, the 
coding gain with respect to the current standard code can 
be further increased, although performance remains rather far 
from the theoretical limits achievable. It is well known that 
more efficient decoders exist, which exploit the most reliable 
basis (MRB) algorithm [6]. MRB is a sub-optimal soft­
decision decoding algorithm, that is potentially able to achieve 
performance very close to that of the maximum likelihood 
(ML) decoder. The main limit of the MRB algorithm is 
complexity, that makes it practically unfeasible in the case 
of long codes.

performed through an lA, while the MRB algorithm is invoked 
only when the IA is not able to find any valid codeword. 
Recently, we have applied this approach also for effective 
decoding of non-binary LDPC codes [7]. The latter represent 
another alternative for updating the standard [8] but, due to 
their greater decoding complexity with respect to the binary 
codes, they are not given full priority. A preliminary discussion 
on the complexity of the non-binary LDPC codes for TC links, 
under different decoding algorithms, is reported in [5]. The 
proposed hybrid procedure has a performance never worse 
than that of the IA or the MRB algorithms when used alone 
(an explanation for this behavior can be found in [7]) and, 
even more important, it can permit us to reduce significantly 
the complexity. 
A further aspect we wish to discuss in this paper is the 
impact of quantization. To quantize the variables involved in 
the decoding process is clearly an obliged choice in view of 
practical implementation. Two types of mid tread quantization 
rules are considered: besides a "conventional" uniform charac­
teristic, that is, a linear quantization law, also a non-unifonn, 
logarithmic law is applied. The uniform law is obviously 
simpler to implement but the logarithmic law should provide, 
in principle, better performance. 
The organization of the paper is as follows. In Section II 
we describe the considered codes. In Section III we present 
the various decoding options and provide explicit expressions 
for their complexity. The performance of these schemes is 
then assessed in Section IV, also taking into account the 
quantization issues. Our conclusions follow in Section V.

III. ASSESSMENT OF DIFFERENT DECODING ALGORITHMS 
Our focus is on the MRB decoding algorithm, used alone 
or in hybrid form, that is in conjunction with an IA. SPA, MS 
and NMS, used alone, also serve as a reference. SPA has been 
implemented in the so-called log-likelihood ratio (LLR) form. 
A. MRB algorithm and hybrid decoding 
The rationale of the MRB algorithm is a transformation 
from the original code generator matrix, G, to a new matrix, 
G *, based on the k most reliable bits among the received bits. 
The algorithm can be summarized as follows: 
• Find the k most reliable received bits and collect them 
in a vector v*. 
• Perform Gauss-Jordan elimination on the matrix G, with 
respect to the positions of the k most reliable bits, in 
such a way as to obtain a systematic generator matrix G * 
corresponding to such bits. If G is full rank, the Gauss­
Jordan elimination always succeeds, although some of the 
last most reliable bits may be replaced with some other 
bit outside the initial set. 
• Encode v* by G * to obtain a candidate codeword c* 
= 
v*G*. 
• Choose the order i of the algorithm (we denote by 
MRB(i) an instance of the algorithm with order i). 
• Consider all (or an appropriate subset of) test error 
patterns (TEPs) of length k and Hamming weight 11) S i. 
• For each of them: add it to v*, encode by G *, verify if 
the Euclidean distance from the received vector is smaller 
than that of the previous candidate codeword and, if this 
is true, update the candidate. 
At the end of this process, the algorithm always provides 
a codeword, which is the codeword at minimum distance 
from the received vector within the considered set. Clearly, 
if i 
= k the set of all possible codewords is considered 
and the algorithm becomes coincident with ML decoding. 
Unfortunately, this requires to consider 2i = 2k vectors, then 
it is unfeasible unless the code is trivial. A key item for the 
algorithm complexity is the order i. In fact, the maximum 
number of TEPs to be tested, for each received word to be 
decoded, is equal to NTEP = L1=o (4) and this number is 
high even for i « k. Actually, it is normally not necessary to

The hybrid algorithm consists in applying iterative decoding 
followed by the MRB decoding. The idea is to use the iterative 
decoder as the main decoding algorithm, resorting to the MRB 
decoder only when the iterative decoder does not find a valid 
codeword [12]. A simple flow-chart of the procedure is shown 
in Fig. 2. A peculiarity of our implementation is that the 
invoked MRB decoder does not use the soft values delivered 
by the last iteration of the adopted IA. This is because we have 
heuristically verified that it is more effective, in terms of error 
rate, to start MRB decoding on the original received word. 
This in fact allows to avoid the effects of the deteriorated 
soft values resulting at the end of a failure of the iterative 
algorithm.

V. CONCLUSIONS 
We have verified that the MRB algorithm can be effectively 
applied for decoding the LDPC(128. 64) and the LDPC(512. 
256) codes proposed to update the standard for TC space links. 
We have also shown that rather small numbers of quantization 
bits are required in order to obtain performance very close 
to that of the ideal. unquantized case. The complexity issue 
has been faced looking at the average number of binary 
operations needed per each decoded codeword, and showing 
that the hybrid algorithm is an excellent option permitting 
to achieve, for both codes, the best performance with an 
acceptable complexity.
