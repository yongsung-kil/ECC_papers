ADMM decoding of error correction codes: from geometries to algorithms

Xishuo Liu Dept. of Electrical & Computer Eng.

Abstract—Many code constraints can be represented using factor graphs. By relaxing these factorable coding constraints to linear constraints, it is straightforward to form a decoding optimization problem. Furthermore, by pairing these factor graphs with the alternating directions method of multipliers (ADMM) technique of large-scale optimization, one can develop distributed algorithms to solve the decoding optimization problems. However, the non-trivial part has always been developing an efficient algorithm for the subroutines of ADMM, which directly relates to the geometries of the relaxed coding constraints. In this paper, we focus on summarizing existing results and distilling insights to these problems. First, we review the ADMM formulation and geometries involved in the subroutines. Next, we present a linear time algorithm for projecting onto an ℓ1 ball with box constraints.
I. INTRODUCTION
Linear programming (LP) decoding of low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes is proposed by Feldman et al. in [1], and is shown to have many performance guarantees (e.g. [2]–[5]). Moreover, LP decoding is observed to have better high-SNR performance than belief propagation (BP) decoding (e.g. [6], [7]). However, generic LP solvers are not efficient because they do not exploit the structure of the decoding problem. Therefore, reducing the complexity of LP decoding has been an important research direction. Related studies include [7]– [12].
In this paper, we focus on using the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) to decode error correction codes. ADMM LP decoding of binary LDPC codes is first proposed by Barman et al. in [13]. A subroutine of this algorithm – projection onto the parity polytope – is further improved in [14] and [15] in terms of computational complexity. In [16], ADMM LP decoding is extended to ADMM decoding of a penalized LP decoding problem, which empirically improves the error performance and reduces the computational complexity. An extension to non-binary codes in F2m is developed in [17]. Furthermore, modifications of ADMM decoding for high-density parity-check codes are proposed in [18]. Last but not least, ADMM decoding for LP-decodable multipermutation codes is developed in [19].
This work was supported by the National Science Foundation (NSF) under Grant CCF-1217058 and by a Natural Science and Engineering Research Council of Canada (NSERC) Discovery Research Grant.

ADMM decoding and BP decoding have many features in common: Both algorithms are iterative and distributed; both algorithms adopt a plain scheduling; and both algorithms scale linearly with block length. However, since ADMM is guaranteed to converge for convex optimization problems, it naturally keeps the provable guarantees and the high-SNR behaviors of LP decoding. Furthermore, due to the penalized LP technique, ADMM decoding can outperform BP decoding in terms of error performance at all SNRs. Therefore, we think ADMM decoding is competitive against BP decoding.
Although the subtitle of this paper is “from geometries to algorithms”, the complete phrasing should be “from ADMM to geometries to algorithms”, because we choose the ADMM algorithm in the first place. Due to this relationship, the structure of the paper is as follows. We first review a generic ADMM formulation with a particular focus on factor graphs. Based on factor graphs, ADMM decomposes decoding problems into subproblems. Due to the augmented Lagrangian in the ADMM formulation, the non-trivial subproblems in most cases are Euclidean projections onto some polytopes. Consequently, characterizing these geometries is important for developing efficient projection algorithms. We summarize geometries that have been used in decoding problems and point out the corresponding projection algorithms. Among these algorithms, we focus on the projection onto the ℓ1 ball with box constraints. We explain this algorithm in details and point out some insights to such problems.
II. FROM ADMM TO GEOMETRIES
A. Transform decoding problems to optimization problems
Before applying optimization techniques to decoding, one has to bring decoding problems to the realm of optimization. This process often consists of three steps. Step 1: Embed codes in R where optimization is performed. For codes in F2, a straightforward embedding is F2 → {0, 1} ⊂ R. For codes with a non-binary alphabet A, e.g., non-binary LDPC codes [20] or permutation codes [21], each symbol of A is represented by a binary indicator vector that admits (at most) one 1, where the index of the 1 uniquely maps to that symbol. After embedding a codeword, we obtain variables u ∈ {0, 1}N . Here N equals to the block length only for binary codes, and equals to |A| or (|A| − 1) times block

length for non-binary codes. By relaxing {0, 1} to [0, 1], we can use variables x ∈ [0, 1]N in the optimization problem. Step 2: Choose a decoding objective. A linear objective that is equivalent to the maximum likelihood (ML) objective is well known [1], [19]–[21]. We note that some useful penalized LP objectives are studied in [16], [17]. Global solutions to the penalized LP objectives agree with solutions to the ML objective, but the penalties increase the cost of fractional solutions, which is introduced by the relaxation in Step 3. Step 3: Relax integer constraints of codes to linear constraints. For example, parity-check constraints can be relaxed to parity polytopes [1], and permutations can be relaxed to Birkhoff polytopes [21]. In particular, we exploit the fact that most code constraints can be factored into local constraints. Factoring these constraints reduces the complexity of describing the optimization problem, which subsequently decreases the decoding complexity. However, these factored constraints may introduce fractional solutions that do not correspond to any codeword, and hence are treated as decoding failures (e.g. [1]).

B. ADMM formulations
ADMM is a classic distributed optimization algorithm originally proposed in 1970s [22], [23]. Recently, ADMM received renewed interests due to its applications in large-scale distributed convex optimization related to “big data” [24]. We refer readers to [24], [25] for more details on ADMM and related algorithms.
In this subsection, we review the ADMM algorithm by applying it to a generic decoding optimization problem. This problem has real valued constraints that are described using factor graphs. We note that this description can be considered as a proximal minimization problem, and applies to most decoding problems in [13], [16], [17], [19]. The only exception is the LP decoding problem of non-binary LDPC codes in [17, Sec. 5.4]. For simplicity, we skip this problem and the following formulation cannot be applied to it directly.
Consider a factor graph with N variable nodes and M constraint nodes, where each variable node represents a variable xi ∈ [0, 1], i = 1, . . . , N , and each constraint node represents a (convex) polytope Cj ⊂ Rdj , j = 1, . . . , M . At this point, we may assume that Cj are different from each other. By connecting variable nodes and constraint nodes, the graph specifies M local constraints that the variables x should satisfy. We represent this connection by selection matrices P j, j = 1, . . . , M : P j is a dj × N matrix such that P jx is a length-dj vector containing all variables that connect to the j-th constraint node. Given such a graph, we are interested in the following optimization problem:

min f (x) s. t. P jx ∈ Cj , ∀j = 1, . . . , M.

(1)

To solve (1) using ADMM, we first create “replicas” zj, each representing a “local” copy of x. In other words, P jx = zj. Then, we use Cj to constrain zj instead of x. As a result, (1) can be reformulated as follows.

min f (x) s. t. P jx = zj, zj ∈ Cj, ∀j = 1, . . . , M.

Furthermore, the x-update is element-wise and has linear complexity with respect to the number of edges in the factor graph. In addition, we note that although the x-update is non-trivial for the problem in [17, Sec. 5.4] due to the linear combinations of x variables, it can still be performed in linear complexity with respect to the number of edges [17, Appendix 11].
The λ-update is simple and can be performed in linear time with respect to the number of edges in the graph.
2) z-update: Since zj do not enter the objective function f (·), one can neglect f (·) in Lµ(xk+1, z, λk) and then complete the square. The minimization problem then becomes the Euclidean projection onto Cj. For different problems, [13], [17], [19], Cj are different. Furthermore, since this projection routine is executed M times per ADMM iteration, it is extremely important to develop an efficient algorithm for it. We focus on this problem in Section III and IV.
3) Practical aspects of ADMM: We briefly summarize some practical aspects related to ADMM decoding.
• The over-relaxation method has been applied to both ADMM decoding of binary and non-binary codes [13], [16], [17]. It reduces the number of iterations significantly in all these cases.
• Early termination is useful for decoders. In other words, we can stop ADMM whenever a codeword is found. We observed that this technique creates observable effects on error rates for some non-binary LDPC codes. Thus, one should examine this scheme before using it in practice.
• The initialization of ADMM affects the “all-zero” assumption for the penalized decoders in [16], [17].

III. FROM GEOMETRIES TO ALGORITHMS
In this section, we first review polytopes that have appeared in decoding problems. For each polytope, we point out the algorithms that project onto it. We then summarize these geometries by discussing an interesting insight on permutations.
1) The standard-d simplex: Let Sd denote the standard dsimplex,

Furthermore, a relaxed code polytope of non-binary single parity-check (SPC) codes in F2m is related to the standard-d simplex [17]. Consequently, as shown in [17], the ADMM decoding algorithm consists of subroutines that project onto Sd. We further note that the standard orthogonal simplex, i.e., the corner of [0, 1]d is closely related to Sd, and is also important in the ADMM algorithm proposed in [17]. Projections onto both simplexes can be done in linear time with respect to d by leveraging the algorithm proposed in [28].

CONSTRAINTS

In this section, we show a linear time projection algorithm onto Ld,r. There are two key ideas in this algorithm. First, using Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) conditions, the projection problem can be transformed to a waterfilling type problem, wherein one needs to perform a binary search over 2d possible points. Second, although one can first sort these points and then perform a binary search, the sorting operation is O(d log d) and is expensive. Instead, the binary search can be done based on linear time median finding algorithms (e.g., [31, Sec. 8.5]). These two key ideas are used in both [28], [29].
Algorithm 1 in this section is modified from [29, Algorithm 1] and hence is not new. We intend to show this algorithm as an example of the above two ideas. However, their algorithm is based on sorting instead of median finding. Due to these previous works, we only briefly describe the derivations. We refer readers to [32] for a nice waterfilling interpretation of the algorithm. V. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we review some tools and results on ADMM decoding of error correction codes. By embedding codes in reals and relaxing integer constraints to convex constraints, we first rewrite decoding problems as optimization problems. Next, we can use ADMM to decompose these optimization

problems because many of them have factorable constraints. After the ADMM decomposition, we only need to develop efficient algorithms for the subproblems, which means that we need to study the geometries of the local constraints. We summarize some polytopes of interests and algorithms for projecting onto these polytopes. Finally, we discuss key ideas of developing algorithms based on the characterizations of the geometries.
As a concluding remark, we list the tools used in ADMM decoding of binary LDPC codes, non-binary LDPC codes, and LP-decodable multipermutation codes in Table I, II, and III respectively. We note that the results in these works show that ADMM decoding is promising. We would like to encourage interested readers to further explore the possibilities of ADMM decoding.
