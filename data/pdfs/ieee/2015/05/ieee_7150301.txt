Performance Characterization of LDPC Codes for Large-Volume NAND Flash Data

Patrick R. Khayat, Mustafa N. Kaynak, Sivagnanam Parthasarathy, Saeed Sharifi Tehrani
Micron Technology, Inc., San Diego, CA, USA {pkhayat, mkaynak, spsarathy, ssharifi}@micron.com

Abstract—High bit error rates of next-generation flash devices necessitate the use of more powerful error correction codes (ECCs), such as low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, instead of the legacy Bose-Chaudhuri-Hocquenghem (BCH) codes. Unlike algebraic codes, the random nature of LDPC codes as well as their ability to use soft information requires the use of Monte Carlo (MC) simulations to evaluate code performance. Given a large volume of NAND data, this can pose resource challenges both in terms of simulation platforms and time needed for the Monte Carlo simulations. In order to overcome these challenges, we introduce a new channel metric in this paper to quantify the quality of soft information and propose a practical LDPC code performance characterization methodology.
Index Terms—Error correction code (ECC), low-density parity-check (LDPC) code, NAND flash memory
I. INTRODUCTION
Flash memory-based storage systems are becoming more widespread in a variety of areas. Today, mobile phones, tablets, digital cameras, music players, and thumb drives use flash based memory devices. Solid state drives (SSDs) based on flash are becoming more common in high-end laptops, and enterprise-class SSDs are starting to replace hard disk drives (HDDs) in the server industry because of their ability to deliver extremely high input/output operations per second (IOPS). Flash-based storage systems are superior to legacy magnetic recording systems in read/write throughput (especially in random reads/writes), reliability (in part, due to their lack of moving parts), latency, power consumption, and wide range of operating temperatures. The major disadvantage of flash devices is their cost.
The majority of flash memory controllers today use Bose-Chaudhuri-Hocquenghem (BCH) codes for error correction. With a reasonable error correction code (ECC) budget, BCH codes can correct all errors at the end of the flash device’s life, after cycling and retention. End-of-life raw bit error rates (RBERs) of next-generation flash devices are high due to smaller cell geometries, severe noise and disturb mechanisms, as well as storing more bits per cell. To account for the higher error rates, BCH codes require significantly higher ECC parity overhead, which makes them very costly for future flash controllers. For example, in order to correct an average RBER of 10-2, a 2KB BCH code requires more than 20% ECC parity overhead which is

significantly higher as compared to today’s typical ECC overhead of 8 - 12%.
Recently, there has been a significant effort to employ more powerful ECCs, such as low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, in next-generation flash controllers [1], [2]. Two major advantages of LDPC codes are their reasonable hardware implementation complexity and their near-channel capacity error correction performance in the presence of soft information.
In order to validate the choice of an ECC for a particular NAND technology, its performance must be characterized against a set of large-volume data encapsulating the NAND unit-to-unit variability (wafer-to-wafer, die-to-die, block-toblock, page-to-page, etc.). The target goal of this effort is to ensure that the ECC scheme of choice will deliver an uncorrectable bit error rate (UBER) below 10-15 where the UBER is defined as the codeword error rate (CWER) per user information bit. This target should be met for all conditions under the usage model for the product. These conditions are typically the number of program and erase (P/E) cycles, the duration that the data is retained, the number of read disturbs that the die needs to support, and the range of operating temperatures.
The challenge of evaluating LDPC codes is that the error correction performance cannot be mathematically characterized; therefore, it relies on extensive Monte Carlo (MC) simulations. MC-based LDPC code performance evaluation for each NAND unit and each of the NAND conditions (i.e., underlying noise channel) poses a significant challenge both in terms of the simulation platform needed as well as the time it would take for the characterization. In this paper, we propose a methodology that uses a soft information quality metric in addition to RBER to accurately characterize LDPC codes for large-volume NAND flash data while minimizing the resource impact.
The paper is organized as follows: Section II gives an overview of the performance characterization for algebraic codes such as BCH codes as well as highlighting the challenges posed by soft LDPC decoders. Section III details the proposed methodology for LDPC code performance characterization and the paper is concluded in Section IV.

Characterizing LDPC codes is not as straightforward as with BCH codes due to several factors. First, unlike BCH codes, LDPC codes have no guaranteed correction capability. Second, unlike hard decoders, the quality of the soft information received from the NAND flash plays a major role in the performance of the LDPC decoder. Third, the implementation of the LDPC decoder in terms of precision and decoder parameters (such as the maximum number of decoder iterations as well as the scalar and offsets used in min-sum decoding) may impact the LDPC code performance. Finally, the LDPC code design technique (i.e., factor graph cycle and/or trapping/stopping set removal) can affect the performance. For example, given the suboptimal nature of the LDPC decoder as well as the cycles of the LDPC code factor graph, an LDPC decoder may correct a codeword with e errors but fail to correct another codeword with e − 10 errors.
For these reasons, MC simulations are required to characterize an LDPC code/decoder for a given NAND channel. However, MC simulations are extremely timeconsuming for large-volume NAND flash data. For example, to evaluate a single NAND page to a CWER of 10-6, one would need to simulate roughly 100 million codewords. Performing this simulation for all the pages and stress

conditions in the data set poses a serious resource challenge and limits the feasibility for large-volume NAND flash data.
III. PROPOSED CHARACTERIZATION METHODOLOGY
In order to simplify the LDPC code performance characterization, the dominant sources of error in the NAND and their implications to the LDPC decoder performance must be understood. In this regard, we first review the NAND flash channel model.
A simple multilevel cell (MLC) NAND flash model storing 2 bits/cell is shown in Fig. 2 and used in the remainder of the paper for illustrative purposes. We assume a fixed voltage range (about 4 5 volts) between the peak of the erase distribution (L0) and the peak of right-most distribution (L3). The threshold voltage (Vt) distribution is modeled by using a single standard deviation for all the levels and a unique mean for each level such that the levels are equidistant from one another.

The hard read thresholds (solid vertical arrow) are used to determine the state of each cell. Soft information can be extracted from the cell with additional reads around the hard read thresholds; this helps determine how close the stored charge is to the hard read threshold. The optimal soft read thresholds (dashed vertical arrows) can be determined by maximizing the mutual information (MI) between the input (the stored data) and the outputs of the multiple reads [3]. With four soft reads around the hard read threshold, the Vt range is divided into six “buckets.” Each bucket receives reliability metrics, or log-likelihood ratios (LLRs), which is then used as the input to the LDPC decoder. The distribution of the occurrence of each bucket and the LLR values associated to each bucket encapsulate all the necessary information to characterize the performance of a given LDPC code and decoder.

Soft-LDPC decoder performance is vulnerable to errors with high reliability metrics, called high reliability errors (HREs). As these erroneous bits are presented to the decoder with high reliability, the soft decoder will refrain from flipping these bits in favor of lower-reliability bits. When strong enough, any of the previously mentioned noise mechanisms can cause HREs due to the widening of the Vt distribution.
Other potential sources of HREs are any error tolerance in the erase and programming algorithms as well as misplaced bits. Misplacement may occur when programming an MLC device [4]. A possible sequence to program a 2 bits/cell MLC device is to first program the lower page followed by the upper page. During upper-page programming, the lower page is first read (called a “preread”) and then used in conjunction with the upper page to program the cell. Any errors in the pre-read result in a “write-in” error to the cell. Fig. 3 shows the impact of misplacement to the Vt distribution (L0ÆL3 and L1ÆL2).

Fig. 4 shows the UBER performance of a 1KB, code rate Rc=0.88 LDPC code with high-reliability error rates (HRER) of 3x10-4 and 3x10-3. As the plot shows, for a fixed RBER, the UBER corresponding to the two NAND models are several decades apart. Therefore, the RBER metric alone is not sufficient to characterize an LDPC code’s performance.
Note that for hard input decoders, HREs are the same as any other error. In addition, HREs not only affect LDPC soft-decoders, but any soft decoding scheme.
Although the LLR distribution of a given page and stress condition fully captures the needed channel information to characterize a given LDPC code/decoder’s performance, the measure of HRER gives a simple first-order estimate. In addition to the RBER, we propose that the HRER be used as another channel metric to characterize a given LDPC code and decoder. HREs can be defined as all errors outside a ±400mV range of the ideal hard read threshold. Note that this range can be fine-tuned to the tolerance of a given system.
From an information theoretical point of view, one can use the mutual information instead to quantify the quality of the soft information. However, computing MI for large-

volume data is not practical due to the higher number of NAND reads required.

The proposed methodology first evaluates the performance of a given LDPC code and decoder using an appropriate NAND model. To this set of reference performance data, the system is evaluated by sweeping a range of added HRERs to the underlying NAND model, as shown in Fig. 3. In all, the reference performance data includes a single LDPC UBER vs. RBER curve for each amount of added HRER.
The curves can further be extrapolated to the target UBER of interest and also interpolated to gain more resolution of the RBER axis. In addition, the resolution of HRER can be increased by fitting more curves in between each of the simulated HRER curves. The interpolated and extrapolated LDPC UBER curves are then converted to lookup tables (LUTs) indexed by average RBER and HRER. Given the average RBER and HRER for each page of the large-volume NAND data as inputs, the lookup table returns the LDPC UBER estimate for the given LDPC code and decoder.

In ongoing MC simulations using actual NAND Vt distributions, we observe no codeword failures for pages whose (RBER,HRER) coordinates lie to the left of the capability curve. For the pages whose (RBER,HRER) coordinates lie to the right of the capability curve, we

observe UBER within two orders of magnitude of the estimated UBER from the proposed methodology. This implies that the capability curve can be efficiently used to distinguish the NAND pages that meet the target UBER from the pages that might require MC simulation for precise UBER evaluation. IV. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we proposed a methodology to characterize the performance of a given LDPC code and decoder for large volume of NAND data using minimal resources. We use a priori knowledge of NAND error mechanisms to define a new channel metric called HRER in order to quantify the quality of soft information. The metric is simple to obtain from the NAND and has the most significant impact to soft ECC decoder performance. This metric is used in conjunction with RBER to generate reference data based on the underlying NAND channel model, which can later be used as simple lookup tables to estimate the UBER for a given RBER and HRER pair from the actual NAND.
