Abstract—Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are one of the most powerful error-correcting codes for wireless communication of high-speed data. This power comes from an efficient decoding scheme, the sum-product algorithm (SPA), which runs over the bipartite graph defining the code. In this work, we propose to use a modified version of the decoding algorithm over the LDPC codes used in Long Term Evolution (LTE) and WiFi and demonstrate that with the same number of iterations, without any increase in time complexity, we can achieve an enhanced error-correcting performance. The only trade off of the system is the necessary storage of the decoding sets, for a total size of ns x n, where ns is the number of decoding sets used and n is the codeword length. In this work, the maximum value of ns used is 5, which makes the trade-off fairly acceptable, considering that the coding gain is obtained virtually free-of-charge.
I. INTRODUCTION
LDPC codes form a class of error-correcting codes originally developed by Gallager in 1962 and are usually identified by a parity-check matrix containing mostly ‘0’s and very few ‘1’s [1]. Decoding is achieved using the belief propagation algorithm also known as the message passing algorithm or the SPA. LDPC codes are adopted by many communication standards such as LTE [2] and WiFi [3] standards due to their near-capacity error correction performance and efficiency for high-throughput decoding. When compared to Turbo codes, the decoding algorithm of LDPC codes is much simpler, with high throughput and exhibits high decoding performance, and the hardware design can be achieved by parallel operations with high decoding speed [4].
High data rates together with good performance have always been the key elements of new standards in future wireless communication systems. The LTE of the 3rd generation partnership project (3GPP) is designed for high data rates and good quality of service [2]. Regarding WiFi (802.11n), this technology is evolving at a very fast rate with the fifthgeneration of Wi-Fi or 5G WiFi (802.11ac) up to three times faster and up to six times more power efficient [5]. To meet the demands of more users, devices and data, it is fundamental to consider link throughput and error correction performance of LDPC codes employed by these technologies to satisfy emerging and future requirements. For large blocklengths, the performance of LDPC codes is known to operate very close to the Shannon limit [6]. However, when it comes to short

blocklengths, sub-graph structures of the bipartite graph such as cycles and trapping sets decrease the performance of these codes [7].
The aim of the research is to demonstrate that the recovery algorithm based on [8] exhibits very good performance for the decoding of LDPC codes for LTE and WiFi through intensive simulations. The algorithm is made to utilize decoding parameters for LTE and WiFi coding schemes in terms of the usage of decoding iterations and percentage of participating nodes of the bipartite graph. Indeed, whereas using additional iterations does not usually help for conventional algorithm (with the decoder stuck in bad configurations from which it cannot escape), the same iteration resources are used more effectively in the recovery algorithm thereby bringing the dividends of an increased error-correcting capability.
The rest of the paper is organised as follows: in section II, an introduction to the recovery algorithm is provided. Section III gives a brief description of LTE and WiFi technologies while section IV highlights the further work done to show the capability of its integration for LTE and WiFi. The results are then presented in section V and finally a short conclusion is provided in section VI.
II. THE RECOVERY ALGORITHM
Under the conventional decoding regime of LDPC codes, the decoder will usually move in a certain direction during the first few iterations [9] and keep that direction until it will either converge to the correct state or reach a wrong state. Alternately, it can also oscillate between two states without any definite convergence. As such, the first iterations of the decoding algorithm are usually critical and provide the general direction the decoder will take. Subsequent iterations thus cause a waste of valuable computing resource. To combat this problem, the redundant set-up of the bipartite graph as described in [8] is established.
The redundancy of the graph allows the use of a subset of nodes during decoding while the other nodes wait for the decoding metrics to be communicated to them to start their processing. Hence, a percentage of the nodes are erased by setting their normalized log-likelihood ratio to 0.5 as will be described in section IV. IV. SIMULATION WORK
In a conventional decoder, when the decoder has started in a certain wrong direction, it is difficult for it to retract from its path. Hence, the number of iterations used becomes meaningless. The erasure of certain nodes allows the decoder to withdraw from a direction and choose another. This allows the destruction of cycles and trapping sets which contribute to bad structures of the bipartite graph. In [8], the recovery algorithm outperforms the conventional one and consumes fewer iterations. When one or more nodes of the bipartite graph are in error, they will convey the same wrong information to the other nodes to which they are connected. These receiving nodes will increase their false confidence, and they will in turn provide other nodes with wrong messages eventually leading to decoding failure. In addition to this, the use of many iterations do not serve any purpose, as the decoder goes into an oscillatory condition [9]. From an entropy (playing a central role in LDPC decoding) point of view, conventional LDPC decoders had trouble from escaping the bad bipartite graph configurations due to the low entropy (high confidence) of its bit nodes. When decoding is proceeding in the wrong direction, high confidence of the bit nodes will ultimately lead to decoding failure. Indeed increasing the number of iterations usually does not help much since the decoder has already reached a state of low-entropy from which it is very difficult to back off. However, when the recovery algorithm is used, the entropy of the different sets used is initially raised (since entropy of all erased bits is 0.5) [18]. On that account, the decoders are again given a chance for correct decoding. Therefore, the recovery algorithm is further tested with LDPC codes used for LTE and WiFi, and parameters to show its efficiency in these applications. The parameters are chosen at random to demonstrate the capability of the recovery algorithm

in LTE and WiFi, though their specific selection remains an open problem. The investigation whether resources utilized by the recovery algorithm are being used more efficiently, is undertaken. Consequently, it can be known whether computing power is being used more efficiently for LTE and WiFi.
The following notations were used for the simulation parameters:
• ns: number of sets used.
• Ic: number of iterations with all bit nodes used.
• Ir: number of iterations for each set of the recovery algorithm.
• IC : number of iterations for a conventional SPA run such that IC = Ir.ns + Ic [18]
• δ: the proportion of bit nodes that are not erased. For instance, δ=0.95 denotes that 95% of the nodes participated while 5% were erased.
Depending on the number of subsets used, the same number of bipartite graphs were created. Some nodes were erased while others participated in the decoding process. At first, Ic iterations were run by the conventional SPA where no node was erased. If a valid codeword was obtained, the process was stopped and success was declared. However, in case of conventional decoding failure, it was run for the different ns bipartite graphs with specific erased nodes for each, for a small number of iterations, Ir iterations. Any valid codeword retrieved from the sets was stored. The codeword closest to the received vector is chosen as the recovered one and success is declared. If no valid codewords were obtained, the process was stopped denoting failure.
In a conventional SPA, the number of iterations set do not usually serve any purpose especially if the decoder has chosen a wrong path. The aim of this work was to show that a performance gain could be obtained with the same number of iterations by the use of the recovery algorithm which partitions the iterations into ns bipartite graphs having δ erased nodes compared to conventional LDPC decoding. Better performance is obtained with the recovery algorithm using very few iterations Ir of the subsets, as will be demonstrated in section V.

Hence, the recovery algorithm is well suited for LTE and WiFi applications. Given that the only difference from the conventional decoding is the reassignment of the normalized log-likelihood ratio of the bit nodes for each of the ns sets, the enhanced performance is achieved with virtually no increase in decoding time complexity. On the contrary, the technique could even allow for a potential decrease in the overall decoding time by running the SPA over the various sets in parallel. This assumes however that the hardware resources are available for such parallelism. The results obtained show that LTE and WiFi technologies have a lot to gain with higher efficiency especially with mobile data traffic growth. Even for high data rates, a gain in performance is obtained thus allowing robust end-to-end transmission and connectivity along with the need to increase link throughput. All these benefits will allow an improved user experience.
