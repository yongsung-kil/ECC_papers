Abstract This paper investigates the VLSI architectures for energy efficient low-density parity check (LDPC) decoders with low voltage strategies, which is the most recognized and fundamental way to reduce decoder power dissipations. By carefully optimizing the decoder parallelism under the assumption of a prescribed minimum data throughput, a selected CCSDS LDPC decoder is implemented on FPGA and experiments are made with voltage supplies from 3.0v down to 0.9v. Power reports show that the increased parallelism permits the decoder to benefit from a lower supply voltage with constant data throughput and greatly decreased power consumption. In addition, some stopping iteration scheme is explored to detect the un-decodable blocks at early stages and hence reduce the dynamic power consumption of the LDPC decoders.

Introduction Since the last decade, low-density parity check (LDPC) codes have found wide usage in such areas as wired and wireless communications, high density non-volatile storage etc. Due to their excellent error correction performance, freedom from patent protection, and the inherently-parallel decoding algorithm [1]â€“[2]. Most of the research on LDPC topics have focused on dedicated code design, decoding algorithms, and decoder VLSI architectures that can improve decode throughput or memory usage. Fewer papers have discussed the low power architecture of the LDPC decoders systemically. As to the power reduction techniques of LDPC decoders, besides some non-mainstream methods like the analog LDPC decoder [3] or the asynchronous LDPC decoder [4], synchronous digital decoding method remains to be the best solution for future communication applications that demand performance near the limits of channel capacity. On the other hand, low voltage VLSI design is hottest technology in low power domain [5], because cut down supply voltage reduces the power consumption in a quadratic way. Researches of LDPC decoders with low voltage supplies are not widely carried out yet, design of an LDPC decoder with a voltage supply of 0.6V or lower is not

2.3 The decoder parallelism adaption for low voltage The partial-parallel LDPC decoder architecture is shown in Fig 3, where groups of variable node units (VNUs) and check node units (CNUs) swap the computed edge information iteratively. Usually we canâ€™t have all the variable and check nodes working simultaneously in order to avoid the routing overhead, and groups of variable or check nodes take turns to process their message via dedicated pipelining and shared extrinsic LLR memory. By increasing the number of VNUs and CNUs, the decoder performs more computations in parallel. If a fixed supply voltage is used, such increased parallelism often helps to achieve a higher decoding throughput with

subsequent increases in power and area, however, it is possible that increased parallelism can also permit a digital system to operate from a lower supply voltage with constant throughput resulting in greatly decreased power consumption. Fig 4 shows a qualitative relationship between decoder parallelism and supply voltage and decoding throughput [5].

2.4 Decoder architecture optimized for the CCSDS LDPC code under low voltage supply A (n=1280,k=1024), rate 4/5 LDPC code in CCSDS is chosen as experimental object in this paper. We assume that a decoder throughput of no less than 200Mbps is required, although the smallest circulant sub-matrix size is 32 in the original parity check matrix, a larger parallelism of 128 is preferred, which means 128 CNUs and VNUs work in parallel, and 3 layers in a single decoding iteration. After the whole decoder is implemented on FPGA, it goes through the power test under 3.0V, 1.8V, and 0.9V.
Fig 6 is the optimized decoder architecture proposed, and it has the following advantages: (1) Only one cyclic shifter is needed instead of two, because all of the signals needed for each iteration are available via sorted reads from extrinsic LLR registers. (2) No need to separate memory for the updated LLR value storage. (3) Value reuse is more efficient for old & new CNU outputs, and no redundant operations in the data path. (4) Low complexity serial CNU processor is used, as depicted in Fig 7. (5) Smaller pipeline depth due to increased parallelism.

5. Summary This paper investigates optimizations of LDPC decoder architecture under low voltage strategy. In depth analysis are made to the qualitative relationships of decoder parallelism, throughput and voltage supply; also there is some discussions on the early stopping criteria; FPGA implementations and results are obtained on some chosen CCSDS LPDC codes.
