Relationships among classes of structured LDPC codes and their application to data storage
Ivana Ojurdjevic, Alexander V. Kuznetsov and Erozan M. Kurtas Seagate Technology, 1251 Waterfront Place, Pittsburgh, PA 15222
E-mail: {Ivana.Djurdjevic, Alexander.V.Kuznetsov, Erozan.M.Kurtas}@Seagate.com

Abstract
In this paper, we show the equivalences and relationships among some of the well-known classes of structured Low Oensity Parity Check (LOPC) codes and consider application of these codes to magnetic recording channel. We make performance comparisons both in terms of the Bit Error Rate (BER) and the Sector Failure Rate (SFR). In these comparisons, we assume that a structured LOPC code is concatenated with a conventional Reed-Solomon (RS) code.

1 Introduction
S INCE the first application of Low Density Parity Check (LOPC) codes [1] to magnetic recording channels in 1999 [2], numerous papers have been published on the subject. The study in [2] has focused on the application of random LOPC codes. Since the circuit implementation of the random LOPC codes poses great complexity and design challenges, the focus of research has shifted to LOPC codes which have structure and are easier to implement, structured LDPC codes. In particular, in this work we consider the fol­ lowing classes of structured LOPC codes: Finite Geom­ etry (FG) LOPC codes [3], Repeat Accumulate (RA), Irregular RA (IRA) and extended IRA (eIRA) codes [4]�[6], Array LOPC codes [7], combinatorial LOPC codes [Chapter 38 in [8] and references therein], Reed­ Solomon based LOPC codes [9], and Turbo Product codes [10], [Chapter 37 in [8] and references therein). These classes of structured LOPC codes are known to perform well with iterative decoding algorithms, such as belief propagation. In this paper, we show that these classes of codes are not disjoint and that there are equivalences and relationships among them. Furthermore, we consider applications of these codes to magnetic recording channels. We compare both their Bit Error Rate (BER) and their Sector Failure Rate (SFR) performance. A comparison of SFR performance is more meaningful from a system analysis perspective. Numerous experimental results and complexity of the noise in magnetic recording system led us to believe that LOPC codes cannot fully replace Reed-Solomon (RS) codes. Therefore, in all comparisons, we assume an outer RS code is used.
The paper is organized as follows. In Section II, we provide the equivalences and relationships among classes of structured LOPC codes. The channel model is given in Section III. In Section IV, we give examples

of the BER and SFR simulation results for different structured LOPC codes. We conclude the paper in Section V.
2 Relationships and equivalences among several classes of struc­
tured LDPC codes
An LOPC code is defined by its sparse parity check matrix [1]. In other words, for a given m x n binary parity check matrix H with a small number of 1entries, the corresponding LOPC code C of length n is defined as a null space of H:
C = { c E (GF(2) ) n : c · HT = O}.
An LOPC matrix H can be constructed randomly, using a computer search, or systematically, using dif­ ferent objects in combinatorics or algebra. Many sys­ tematic constructions have been proposed by different researchers. In this section, we exhibit equivalences among some of them.
We say that two linear binary (n,k) codes are equivalent if and only if one of the following two statements is true: 1) The two codes consist of the same set of 2k binary n-tuples; 2) By permuting bits in all codewords of one code in the same way (using the same permutation), we obtain the set of codewords in another code.

3 Channel model
All above mentioned classes of the structured LOPC codes were extensively studied by the architects of the magnetic recording systems, and for each class of codes numerous simulation results were presented at the proper technical forums. Magnetic recording chan­ nels are usually modeled as partial response channels with special targets and extensive amount of jitter at magnetic transitions on top of the AWGN produced by the electronic circuits at the input of an equalizer. Such systems are more complex than the simple channels with AWGN used in many papers describing commu­ nication systems. Therefore, the conclusions regarding advantages and disadvantages of different classes of the structured LOPC codes may vary from paper to paper. In the next section for competitive comparisons of different architectures based on the LOPC codes we present the Bit Error Rates (BER) and Sector Failure Rates (SFR) obtained by advanced software simulators describing magnetic recording systems. After a very small number of global iterations the RS-based LOPC code and the array LOPC code outperform all other types of codes, but not the non-iterative channel at low SNR values. However, at moderate and high SNR values, all considered iterative channels outperform the non­ iterative channel, and the gain grows with the SNR. As the number global iterations increases, all iterative systems clearly outperform the non-iterative one (as shown in Fig. 4). After a large number of global iterations, the random LOPC code remains the best in class of compared codes. The EG-LOPC code with column weight 8 cannot compete with other types of codes at low and moderate SNR values, but looks very promising at the high SNR values. Not included in Fig. 3 and Fig. 4 are the BER and SFR simulation results for an eIRA code proposed in [6]. It provides performance similar to the RS-based or irregular array LOPC code of similar length and code rate. However, these two LOPC codes have the advantage of simpler encoding/decoding due to their highly structured parity­ check matrices.
The major observation from the SFR results is that the relationship between the BER characteristics and SFR characteristics is not straightforward. This is not surprising since the BER characteristics are based on the total number of bit errors in the sector, and their grouping in error events is not important, whereas the SFR characteristics definitely depend on how bit errors are grouped in error events, and how the error events split into symbol errors. In result, codes with similar BER characteristics can have different SFR characteristics, and even the code with the worse BER can have better SFR.
5 Conclusion
This paper studies relationships and equivalences among several well-known classes of structured LOPC codes. Here, we also made a competitive comparison of the "best" representatives from different classes used in advanced magnetic recording channels. In general, obtained BER and SFR simulation results indicate that all chosen structured LOPC codes behave similarly and can provide significant gains compared to the non­ iterative system. Simulations also indicate that the rela­ tionship between BER and SFR is not straightforward, and that the system with better BER performance can have worse SFR at a certain operating point. Finally, we should note that in order to reach significant gains with an iterative system, an extensive optimization of many factors, such as code rate of an LOPC code, number of LOPC codewords per sector, T-Ievel of an RS code, interleaving, precoding, and many others, is required.
