Convergence Speed and Throughput of Analog Iterative Decoders for Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) Codes

Abstract

Saied Hemati and Amir H. Banihashemi
Broadband Communications and Wireless Systems (BCWS) Centre Department of Systems and Computer Engineering, Carleton University
Ottawa, ON, KIS 5B6, Canada {hemati, ahashemi}@sce.carleton.ca

This paper is concerned with the implementation of iterative decoding algorithms in analog VLSI. We study the convergence speed and throughput of analog decoders for low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, and show that they depend on the code, the decoding algorithm, and the average time constant of the analog circuit inter­ connections. They however are not a function of the variance of the time constants and change only slightly with signal to noise ratio. The analysis presented here can be used for selecting suitable codes and decoding algo­ rithms for analog decoding. Furthermore, it can be used to estimate the throughput of an analog decoder, if the average time constant of the analog circuit is known.

1 Introduction
Iterative decoding algorithms can be used for decod­ ing low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes and turbo codes with affordable complexity. Conventional itera­ tive decoding algorithms are expressed in discrete­ time domain; however, there are many advantages in implementing these algorithms in continuous time by using analog VLSI chips (see [1]-[8] and the refer­ ences therein). In this work, we study the convergence speed of ana­ log decoders and estimate their throughputs. We use the term "convergence speed" to refer to the speed in which the error rate performance of an analog de­ coder improves with the decoding time and to address the settling behavior of performance curves into their asymptotic steady-state values, obtained as the decod­ ing time tends to infinity. Our study is based on a model for analog decoding developed earlier in [7]­ [8]. The model describes analog decoding as a system of first-order non-linear differential equations. The application of forward Euler method to this system of differential equations is equivalent to the application of the well-known method of successive relaxation to the corresponding fixed-point problem of iterative decoding. Using this equivalence, it is shown in [7], [8] that the performance and dynamics of analog de­ coding is different than those of conventional dis­ crete-time decoding. Conventional discrete-time itera­ tive decoding is in fact the well-known method of successive substitution applied to the fixed-point

problem of iterative decoding. The error rate results obtained based on the model of [7], [8] have been compared with the measured results for an analog decoder chip in [9], [10] and have appeared to be ac­ curate, particularly for lower signal-to-noise ratios (SNR), where the channel noise dominates the imper­ fections due to non-ideal circuitry. We relate the convergence speed and throughput of an analog decoder to the average time constant of the interconnections of the decoder circuit. Our study shows that near steady-state performance of the de­ coder is achievable if the decoder is allowed to oper­ ate on a given input for a certain multiple of the aver­ age time constant. This multiplicative factor is a func­ tion of the code and the decoding algorithm, but is rather independent of SNR and the variance of time constants. To realize the importance of this work, one should note that it is impractical to estimate the error correct­ ing performance of an analog iterative decoder for different values of decoding time by means of transis­ tor level simulations. Such simulations are very ex­ pensive computationally [11]-[13]. In [12] importance sampling method is used to reduce the simulation complexity for estimating the error correcting per­ formance of an analog decoder. Using this method, transistor level simulations are performed for a few hundred cases instead of millions, normally required at low error rates. But even this small number of cases is affordable only for small codes. On the other hand, simulation results based on conventional discrete-time

3 Convergence Speed and Throughput
In this section, by using (2) and Monte Carlo simula­ tion, we study the settling behavior of error perform­ ance of analog decoders. We perform experiments over an additive white Oaussian noise (AWON) chan­ nel with BPSK modulation for two LDPC codes: an optimized irregular (1268,456) LDPC code [13] and a regular (504,252) code [14]. For decoding algorithms,

we consider belief propagation (BP) and min-sum (MS) in likelihood ratio (LR) and log-likelihood ratio (LLR) domains, respectively. The same approach can be used for other codes or decoding algorithms. De­ coding is stopped if the algorithm converges to a codeword or if the maximum decoding time is reached. In our simulations, we observe at least 200 codeword errors per simulation point. In the follow­ ing, we consider two scenarios: I-equal time con­ stants; and 2-nonzero variance for the distribution of time constants.

4 Conclusion
In this paper, the relationship between throughput and error correcting performance of analog decoders for LDPC codes is studied. The study is based on a sim­ ple model of analog decoding which describes the dynamics of analog decoders as a set of first-order non-linear differential equations. Despite its simplic­ ity, this model has proved to provide good approxi­ mations for the performance of practical analog de­ coders with affordable complexity. The issue of com­ plexity is what makes the other alternative, transistor­ level circuit simulation, impractical. Our results indicate that the settling behavior and throughput of analog decoders is rather independent of the variance of the delay distribution and signal to noise ratio and is only a function of the code, the de­ coding algorithm and the average time constant of circuit interconnections.
