Abstract
In this paper we present two theoretical results that lead to the development ofgood w-rotation LDPC codes. Thefirst theorem derives bounds on the weight ofthe parity vector when the weight ofthe information vector is two. The second theorem establishes the expected number ofshort-loops in the parity check matrix. We then present construction tools that are used to reduce the complexity of searching minimum distance and girth for w-rotation LDPC codes. Several performance examples are given.

1. Introduction
The 7-rotation LDPC codes were introduced in [1] as a class of deterministic LDPC codes with easy description, simple circuitry and superb performance [2]. The 7-rotation pattern traces back to the semi-random matrix introduced in [3] and similar to the 'RA turbo-codes' of [4]. The advantage of the 7-rotation structure is the use of a single permutation matrix to define the entire code [5]. Irregular LDPC matrix densities are constructed through the extended 7-rotation structure which allows a variety of code rates and excellent performance [6]. Encoding is performed by shifting information bits around the perimeter of the permutation grid demonstrating an efficient high-speed circuit implementation [7].
In this paper we discuss two theorems that play an essential role in the development of good 7-rotation LDPC codes. The first theorem derives the bounds on the weight of the parity vector when the information vector has exactly two non-zero elements. The second theorem establishes the expected number of short-loops (girth-4) in the parity-check matrix. We also present construction tools specific to the 7-rotation pattern and conclude with several performance examples.

2.1. The Weight-Two Distance Representation
To facilitate our study we diverge for the moment from the fully deterministic form discussed above and consider the more general permutation sub-matrices. Thus we consider a general Hd matrix as a q x t array of arbitrary m x m permutation matrices. We can then represent each permutation matrix as a row vector of m elements, each element taken from the set of integers 1 to m. For example, Fig. 1(a) is an Hd matrix presented as 16 row vectors where q = t = 4. Each colunm of Hd can be treated as a point in a q-dimensional lattice.
It is easy to show [8] that the weight of the parity vector, d2, associated with a weight-two information vector can be obtained as the Manhattan metric [9] between the corresponding lattice points. An example is shown in Fig. l(b) with m = 5 where a, ..., h represent integers from the set 1 to 5.

6. 7-Rotation Construction Methods
We note that the 7-rotation codes are based on a constructed pattern and thus do not necessarily comply with the above theorems based on random permutations. However, our investigations revealed that both the loop count and distance profiles of the 7-rotation pattern displayed similar characteristics as those for random matrices. A significant advantage to using the 7-rotation pattern is the many symmetries that provide reduction in the complexity of searching for minimum code weight and matrix girth.

6.2. Short Loop Count
Referring to Fig. 3, we find 36 possible quads that contribute to the short loop count in the Hd matrix. However, only a small subset of those quads will need to be examined. For example, consider the first (top-left) quad indicated by the A-B-C-B permutation pattern.4 We have the same pattern in the bottom-right corner. However, when considering short loops, the quad represented by B-C-B-A contains the same number of short loops. To see this, consider that in any quad the right two permutations can be interchanged with the left two permutations without affecting the short loop count. In a similar fashion, the top two permutations can be swapped with the bottom two permutations to obtain a configuration invariant in short loop count. Therefore, B-A-B-C is another quad arrangement that contains the same number of short loops as the A-B-C-B quad. In Fig. 5 we list each quad and show where this quad or one of its invariants resides in the mosaic of permutations.

8. Summary
The structure of the 7-rotation LDPC codes is based on the parity-check matrix composed of the dual-diagonal sub-matrix and a composition of permutation matrices. This matrix pattern presents two fundamental characteristics of great value to the code designer. In this paper we present these results in the form of two theorems. The first result provides bounds on the minimum weight of codewords when the information vector is weight-two. With random permutations the weight-two information vector is the dominant contributor to low-weight codewords [8]. The second result proves that the expected number of short loops in matrices constructed from random permutations is constant. We then explain methods used specifically with the 7-rotation constructed pattern to select codes with large d2 and small number of short loops. These methods have proved to be very effective in creating high quality LDPC codes of arbitrary length.
