1 Introduction
Thirteen years after the first official presentation at ICC 1993 in Geneva [1], turbo codes in their original parallel and serial [2] version, together with the closely related low-density parity-check (LOPe) codes [3], have reached a consistent understanding of their be­ havior and design, through the work of thousands of researchers around the world. Although a few issues, mainly related with the iterative decoding algorithms that make their extraordinary performance reachable with linear complexity, still remain unsolved, turbo-like and LOPC codes have already found their way into numerous applications and standards, like space satel­ lite communications (CCSOS), third-generation cellu­ lar systems (UMTS), satellite return channel (OVB­ RCS) and digital satellite video broadcasting (DVB­ S2), local wireless networks (IEEE 802.16e), and are slowly replacing previous coding schemes in almost all applications requiring high coding gains.
In this relatively short-time path from theory to ap­ plications, parallel to the impetuous development of the Internet and its wireless connections requiring high data rates, high spectral efficiency, low power and flexible signaling formats, the designers have faced challenging algorithmic and architectural problems, whose solution

The scope of the paper is to highlight those problems with their most promising solutions, and to provide the reader with a rather comprehensive bibliography supporting what is only sketched in the paper for obvious space reasons.
The first topic is the implementation of high-speed decoders. In fact, many systems require very high data­ rates (in the order of several hundred Mbit/s) and low latency, to meet the constraints imposed, for instance, by multimedia applications or by magnetic recording applications or optical fiber based communication sys­ tems. When turbo-like codes are employed, either in their parallel (PCCC, Parallel Concatenated Convolu­ tional Codes) or serially (SCCC, Serially Concatenated Convolutional Codes) concatenated version, a possible solution is to realize the turbo-decoder in a parallel fashion: A block is divided into sub-blocks, and each of them is decoded by a SISO (Soft Input Soft Output) [4] module in parallel, according to a bidirectional sliding window technique. The throughput is increased and the latency of the system becomes the latency of each sub-block, at the expense of additional hardware and area (the SISO module has to be replicated, and the communication between the different modules has to be ensured). A similar approach has been pursued also for systems based on LOPC codes, such as, for instance, in the recently approved OVB-S2 [5] standard.

Communication systems may also require to support multiple values of frame duration (due to constraints on maximum latency) and multiple data-rates (due to limitations on available bandwidth, or because of different modulation schemes). An important case is
the adaptive coding and modulation, where the main
transmission parameters are modified on a frame-by­ frame basis, according to the time-varying channel conditions and user needs, thus giving rise to different values of the frame size. Therefore, it is important to be able to support different values of block length and code rate, with small additional complexity and keeping the performance good in a wide range of conditions.
In the following, the most effective approaches to tackle the issues of parallel implementation and of versatility (respectively in Section 2 and Section 3) will be described, while Section 4 will be devoted to discuss how both goals can be achived.

2.1 Collisions
A second problem emerges with the previously de­ scribed parallel architecture for turbo-like decoders. For the different SISO modules to be able to work in parallel, it is necessary to store the extrinsic information read and written by each module (whose number is
denoted as the parallelism degree of the decoder) in
different RAM memories. In particular, if in the first half of the iteration the SISO modules write and read data always on a memory bank, in the second half it happens, due to the interleaver law, that each SISO module accesses different banks to retrieve data. De­ pending on the specific permutation law, it may happen that different modules try to access simultaneously the same memory bank. In this case, none of them is able to retrieve data, so that the access to the memory has to be postponed and carefully arbitrated, thus slowing
down the decoding process. It is the so-called collision
problem [7]. A pictorial view of collisions is shown in Fig. 4.
The same problem arises when implementing an

LOPC decoder in a parallel fashion: A set of Variable Nodes (VNs) is assigned to each of the available processors, and a similar partition is made for the Check Nodes (CNs). The edge metrics can be stored in the memories so that, during the VN update phase, each processor retrieves data always from the same memory. Unfortunately, depending on the structure of the parity­ check matrix, during the CN update phase, there could be collisions in the access to the memories.
In the remainder of this section we will illustrate the possible approaches to tackle the problem of collisions for Turbo-like and LOPC codes. In both cases we can solve this problem either with a careful design of the code, if we are able to choose it at our will, or with proper modifications of the decoder architecture.
2.2 PCCC and SCCC
In the parallel implementation of PCCCs and SCCCs, the block is divided into sub-blocks, and each of them is decoded by a SISO module in parallel, according to a bidirectional sliding window technique. However, the parallelism degree cannot be imposed arbitrarily, because, as shown in [54], if the sub-blocks are too small, the sliding window technique does not work very well, due to the uncertainty of the initialization of the state metrics.

Several proposals belonging to this category have been described in the literature: In [7] a deterministic rule for the design of structured collision­ free interleavers was proposed, while in [10] the tradi­ tional algorithm for the design of S-random [11][12] permutations was modified to avoid collisions.

For example, in [14] the temporal permu­ tation is a deterministic pattern defined as "bit reversal pattern", while in [15] it is just a reverse interleaver. 2.2.2 Interleaver-independent parallel decoders
When a specific permutation must be implemented in a parallel decoder, as it is the case for standards such as UMTS [17] or 3GPP2 [18], or if a decoder able to cope with collisions for a large set of interleavers must be designed, then the solutions described in the previous subsection are not viable.
In [19] a system based on a routing network and buffers was described, that is configured at run-time on the basis of the current interleaver to manage collisions and reduce their effects on the throughput. This system pays a price in terms of area and memory and gives sub-optimal performance, with respect to the techniques previously described. While in [19] the buffers are designed to handle the worst case, the authors of [20] show with a statistical analysis that it is reasonable to design them on the hypothesis that typically only few modules (2 or 3) will conflict when accessing the same memory. Therefore, a large reduction of complexity can be achieved with respect to [19], with only a small penalty in terms of throughput.
In [21] the authors proposed an algorithm, based on the permutation decomposition, that permits to imple­ ment a decoder based on an arbitrary interleaver with any parallelism degree with optimal performance.
Consider the information flowing from the first con­ stituent decoder to the second one, since the informa­ tion flow from the second to the first is obtainable by simply reverting the blocks. In the permutation decom­ position approach, the interconnection between the two decoders is implemented through the cascade of three layers of blocks. 2.3 LDPC codes
Unlike PCCCs and SCCCs, LOPC codes lend them­ selves easily to a parallel implementation, since there is no serial dependency in the computation of the metrics of the decoding algorithm. It is possible, in fact, to assign a processor to each VN and CN of the code, to achieve very high throughput. This type of implementa­ tion is referred to as "fully parallel" decoder, since the maximum parallelism degree is achieved. Furthermore, since the metrics do not have to be stored in memories, but are exchanged between processors through wires that replicate the structure of the Tanner graph of the code, collisions are not an issue. Unfortunately, this approach has several shortcomings: The cost in terms of hardware resources is high, due to the very large number of processors, the interconnection between pro­ cessors is very complex and typically consumes a large area, and the flexibility of the code parameters is quite limited. Therefore, fully parallel decoders are hardly implemented when the block size becomes large.
On the other hand, a "partially parallel" decoder is
obtained if we assign a set (larger than 1) of nodes to
a single processor, which elaborates them serially. This architecture will achieve a lower throughput, but offers a good compromise between the advantages of fully parallel architectures, i.e., high throughput and low latency, and of serial implementations, i.e., hardware sharing, small areas and a certain degree of flexibility.
2.3.1 Fully parallel decoders
As in the case of PCCCs and SCCCs, there exist techniques involving a constrained design of the code that can be applied to any LOPC code.
A Ad hoc solutions: In [23] an example of fully
parallel decoder is described, where the structure of the Tanner graph of the code is exactly replicated on the
circuit layout. This circuit, which implements a rate 1/2 code with codeword size of 1024, reaches a throughput of 1 Gbit/s.
B Architecture-oriented code design: The intercon­
nection problems that affect [23], are dealt with in [24]: A code design algorithm is introduced, that produces LOPC codes optimized for a fully parallel decoder implementation. Some constraints are inserted to obtain an upper bound on the "height" of the code, a parameter related to the interconnection resources needed for the routing of messages; the limitation of this metric leads to a reduction of the physical length of wires required on the decoder. The authors of [25] address the issues of wiring overhead and routing congestion by designing the parity-check matrix of the code in order to reduce the exchange of information between processors far apart in the decoder layout. A similar criterion has been chosen in [26]: the parity-check matrix is composed of small identical modules placed along the diagonal in order to reduce the routing complexity of the decoder.

2.3.2 Partially parallel decoders
The same distinction between code-independent tech­ niques and joint co-decoder approaches can be made for partially parallel decoders.
A Code-independent solutions: The authors of [27]
try to minimize the conflicts in memory accesses, with a proper assignment of the nodes of the code to the decoder processors; this partition is obtained using a heuristic algorithm. In [28], instead, the throughput is increased through a modified scheduling, designed according to the parity-check matrix of the code.
Finally, it is possible to apply the techniques illus­ trated in [19] and [21][22] also to LOPC decoders: In particular, the first approach has been investigated in [29] while [21] directly considers also the case of LOPC codes.
B Architecture-oriented code design: Nearly all
the recently proposed LOPC decoder design schemes belonging to this category employ essentially the same approach, first introduced in [30]: The LOPC code parity check matrix is a block-structured matrix that can be partitioned into an array of square MxM block matrices (where M is the desired parallelism degree), where each block matrix is either a zero matrix or a permuted identity matrix. In [31][32][33] a more restrictive choice is made for the structure of the non­ zero block matrix, as only cyclically shifted version of the identity matrix are allowed. Although the class of codes considered in these papers is basically the same, the specific decoder architectures may largely differ. A similar class of codes was also investigated in [34], although for reasons not related to parallel implemen­ tation. Identity matrices are chosen as building blocks because they are characterized by the property that in each column and row there is only a 1. Therefore, if each row and column is assigned to a different processor, and their edges are assigned to a different memory, then there will be no collisions.
An approach similar to [30] was used to build the deterministic part of the codes introduced in [35]. Moreover, in [36] the approach of [30] was applied to elRA (extended IRA codes) [37][38], a class of codes that have become very popular because of their very nice properties (good performance and linear­ time encoding), and in fact have been selected for the OV8-S2 standard [5]. In [39] the authors provided additional guidelines on the design of parallel eIRA codes, pointing out that it is necessary to apply a permutation on the rows of the systematic part of the parity-check matrix of the code, in order to avoid high error floors. In [40] a different technique for the design of extended IRA codes suited for partially parallel decoders was provided.

3 Versatile iterative decoders
3.1 PCCC and SCCC
Code versatility mainly concerne the code rate and the information and/or the codeword block size. While the first issue is easily solved by puncturing the bits of the codeword (see [41] and references therein), the second is more problematic. Both will be treated in the following.
3.1.1 Puncturing
The puncturing approach for obtaining different rates from a single encoding scheme, well known in the convolutional code design, has the advantage of keeping the decoding complexity constant independently from the code rate.
For PCCC puncturing is performed at the output of both upper and lower encoders. Puncturing of the systematic bits should normally be avoided since it leads to worse performance of the code at low signal­ to-noise ratio (waterfall region). Systematic bits punc­ turing [42][43][44], however, can be a viable option if stringent constraints on the error floor must be respected.
For serial concatenation there is the possibility of puncturing the bits of the outer encoder to reach a desired output rate. This approach has however two drawbacks:
1) It requires different interleaver lengths to obtain different rates even in the case of a fixed infor­ mation block size K.
2) The resulting free distance of the punctured outer code may drop below 3, the minimum value that guarantees an interleaving gain larger than for parallel concatenation (see [2]), thus resulting in error floor performance similar to those of PCCC.
The best solution, which is also attractive for imple­ mentation purposes since it leaves the interleaver size constant for all rates, is then to delete only the bits at the output of the inner encoder. To obtain rates of the SCCC greater than the rate of the output encoder this approach requires that the inner encoder be punctured to rates above one. In [41] and [45] it is shown that this is possible through a careful design of the puncturing pattern. The resulting SCCC schemes have very good performance in both the error floor and waterfall region for a wide range of rates.
In Figure 6 we show the bit error probability perfor­ mance of the code scheme designed in [45]. It covers spectral efficiencies from 0.75 to 5.4 bit/sec/Hz (corre­ sponding to SCCC rates between 113 and 9/10), with two concatenated convolutional codes with 4 states. The performance have been tested extensively and did not show error floors down to frame error probabilities of
10-7. This scheme is a good candidate for applications
requiring adaptive coding and modulation in a wide range of spectral efficiencies.

3.1.2 Block size versatility
The change of the block size implies a change of the interleaver length. Some standards, like 3GPP2, support easily different block lengths, as they are based on permutations that can be computed on the fly with the desired parameters. In [46][47] a system to generate on the fly such interleavers is proposed. An alternative is to use a set of interleavers and a pruning technique [48]; unfortunately, pruning cannot be generally used on a wide range of block sizes, as it degrades the code performance; as a consequence, many "mother" inter­ leavers have to be stored.
On the contrary, pruning is a viable technique if we are free to design the permutation so that it is robust to this operation: This approach was followed in [49][50][51][52]. All techniques, with the excep­ tion of [50], are based on the expansion of a small interleaver with an incremental algorithm. In [53] an approach similar to [51] was used to build a family of prunable S-random interleavers. The results were significantly better than those of [51], and optimal spread properties were achieved for all ranges of block sizes, so that only one large interleaver has to be stored, and its pruned versions guarantee good performance for all block lengths.

3.2 LDPC codes
As in the case of PCCCs and SCCCs, also for LDPC codes implementation constraints prevent from storing the code descriptions corresponding to all possible com­ binations of code-rate and block size. Unfortunately, unlike PCCCs and SCCCs, these two parameters are not easily decoupled, as they both refer to the size of the parity-check matrix.
3.2.1 Code-rate versatility
The use of puncturing techniques in LDPC codes is somewhat trickier than in PCCCs and SCCCs. In [55] the effect of puncturing on the convergence threshold of LDPC codes is studied, and a framework for the design of puncturing patterns is provided. However, using a similar approach, the authors of [56] show that puncturing is not effective over a wide range of values; therefore, they use puncturing only in a narrow range and, to obtain lower code-rates in a wider range, extend the parity-check matrix by adding new columns and new rows according to a pre-determined criterion. A similar but more efficient approach was employed in [57].
In [56] it was noted that a critical factor for the performance of punctured LDPC codes is the fraction of

CNs connected to more than one punctured VN, which should be kept very low. This observation is justified in a more analytic way using the framework in [58], where the relationship between code performance and stopping sets is analyzed. It is shown that, if all the bits of a stopping set are erased, error-free decoding cannot be achieved on the Binary Erasure Channel, and also on the AWGN channel. The observation of [56] is then explained: If there are no CNs connected to more than one punctured VN, then no stopping set is completely erased.
The work in [59] took inspiration from this fact to provide a very easy rule to design puncturing patterns for rate-compatible extended IRA codes; a very similar approach for an affine category of LDPC codes was described in [60]. The approach in [59] is based on an alternate puncturing pattern; due to the structure of the parity-check VNs of extended IRA codes, this guaran­ tees that there are no CNs connected to 2 punctured VNs. This rather conservative rule (under a precise analysis of the specific parity-check matrix it could be possible also to allow a small fraction of CNs connected to multiple punctured VNs) has the shortcoming of permitting only a limited, even if quite large, variation of code rate, so that it may be necessary to employ a few mother codes to support all the needed code rates.

3.2.2 Block size versatility
When the parity-check matrix of the code is obtained with the expansion of a matrix, as shown in [30], an easy way to modify the information word length is to use a different expansion factor. The drawback of this approach, selected, for instance, in the codes proposed for the new IEEE 802.16e standard, is that there is not much control on the performance of the codes obtained with the expansion process; moreover, the block length cannot be varied with a fine granularity.
Another simple approach is the well-known tech­ nique of shortening: A given number of systematic bits is deleted. As shown in Fig. 9, in this case we will at same time reduce the block length and lower the code-rate. If the code-rate is too low, we can still apply puncturing. As to the choice of the systematic columns to be deleted, from a theoretical point of view, a good criterion could be to choose them in order to approximate as closely as possible the optimal VN degree distribution for the desired code-rate. Another alternative is to simply delete the columns with low degree. The latter approach was followed in [61] for the extended IRA codes.
Actually, versatility is supported also by determin­ istic code construction techniques, such as [62], that permit to obtain the parity-check matrix of the code, given the main code parameters. However, little at­ tention has been dedicated to this wide category of techniques in this context, because, as a general rule, they do not permit to change the code parameters with a fine granularity, and because some of them could not be implemented in a parallel decoder with low complexity.
4 Merging parallelism and versatil­ ity
When both versatility and parallelism must be achieved through a joint code-decoder design, it can be difficult to integrate the constraints introduced in the code design by these two different requirements.
This is not the case for LDPC codes, but it becomes an Issue III the case of collision-free variable-size interleavers.
4.1 PCCC and SCCC
In [63][64] an adaptation of the design in [53] for paral­ lel turbo decoders was proposed. Two techniques were described: In the first, following closely the approach of [53], the interleaver is built with an incremental algo­ rithm aiming at the optimization of the spread factor, but at each step the constraints related to parallelism are also enforced. As the interleaver size grows, the obtained spread factor increases optimally, almost as in [53],. However, due to the high complexity of the

algorithm, it is feasible only for moderate values of parallelism degree (typically up to 6).
To support higher values of parallelism (in the order of few tens) for large block lengths a second technique, suboptimal in terms of spread factor, was then devel­ oped. It is based on the combination of spatial and temporal permutation, as described in Section 2.2.1, where the temporal permutation has been obtained with the first algorithm. Due to specific constraints, the temporal permutation, when combined with any spatial permutation, retains its spread factor, while its parallelism degree is multiplied.
In [63][64] the decoder architecture is briefly de­ scribed. The connectivity between the memories and the processors is ensured through the cascade of two levels of crossbars.
A turbo decoder based on the interleavers obtained as described above can be rapidly re-configured in terms of code-rate, block size and even parallelism degree, with very small additional complexity.

5 Conclusions
The challenges in designing versatile, high-speed itera­ tive decoders, for both turbo-like and LDPC codes, has been the scope of this paper. Among them, the paper has focused on the parallel implementation of SISO and LDPC decoders, on the collision problem arising in the process of reading/writing into/from the memory by parallel processors, on the design of prunable in­ terleavers covering a wide range of information and/or code words lengths, and on the design of codes yielding a wide range of code rates with good performance for the range of probability of error of interest.
The paper has touched the previous points, offering state-of-the art solutions and examples showing their performance.
