Abstract
We present a new class of irregular low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes for finite block length (up to a few thousand symbols) that are well-suited for rate-compatible puncturing. The proposed codes are linear-time encodable with a simple shift-register implementation. In addition, the codes show excellent puncturing performance over a wide range of rates with systematic rate-compatible puncturing structure. For a blocklength of 1024 bits the codes outperform optimized irregular LDPC codes and extended irregular repeat-accumulate (eIRA) codes for all rates 0.5�0.9, and are particularly good at high puncturing rates where good puncturing performance has been previously difficult to achieve.

1

Introduction

Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are currently considered good candidates for the next­ generation communication systems and storage systems. Their excellent performance and parallelizable decoder make them appropriate for technologies such as DVB-S2, IEEE 802.16e [1], and IEEE 802.11n [2], [3]. While semiconductor technology has progressed to an extent where the implementation of LDPC codes has become possible, many issues still remain. First, there is a continual need to reduce complexity especially for implementing an encoder without sacrificing performance. Second, for many wireless applications, the system throughput depends upon changing channel conditions and hence the code needs to have the ability to operate at different rates. Third, while the LDPC decoder can operate in linear time, it may be hard to encode these codes efficiently. This problem can be alleviated somewhat at long block lengths (see [5]).
Irregular Repeat-Accumulate (IRA) codes were in­ troduced by Jin et al. [6]. These codes have a linear­ time encoder and their performance is almost as good as irregular LDPC codes. Building on this construc­ tion Yang et at. [7] introduced extended IRA (elRA) codes and were able to design high-rate codes with low error-floors. A popular technique for achieving rate-adaptation in a system is through the use of rate­ compatible puncturing. A rate-compatible punctured code (RCPC) has the same encoder and decoder while operating at different rates [8]. The number of parity bits that the transmitter sends depends upon the rate requirement. At the decoder end, the punctured parity bits that are not transmitted are treated as erasures. Thus puncturing provides a low-complexity solution

to the rate-adaptation problem. In addition to low­ complexity, rate-compatible puncturing has another advantage when it is applied to incremental redun­ dancy (IR) automatic repeat request (ARQ) scheme since the parity bits of the higher-rate code form a subset of the parity bits of the lower-rate codes.
In this work, we show that the performance of elRA codes can degrade significantly at high-puncturing rates. To alleviate this problem we introduce a new class of LDPC codes that have a linear-time encoder and have good performance under puncturing for a wide range of rates. We call this class of codes Efficiently-Encodable Rate-Compatible (E2RC) codes.

Extended IRA Codes

We briefly summarize elRA codes since our code design is inspired by them. For finite length codes, three design rules were proposed in [4], that is, assigning degree-2 nodes to non-systematic bits, forbidding short cycles involving only degree-2 nodes and avoiding length-4 cycles. The elRA codes achieve good performance by following the above design rules. Furthermore, they make the systematic bits correspond to variable nodes of degree higher than two. 2.2 Rate-Compatible Puncturing Algorithm

Rate-compatible puncturing of LDPC codes based on the degree distributions was introduced by Ha et al. [9]. However, this method assumes infinitely long block lengths and extending this to short block lengths is difficult. For finite block lengths (several thousand symbols) LDPC codes, Ha et al. also proposed efficient rate-compatible puncturing algorithms [10], [11]. Since our code construction technique is inspired by it, we present a brief description of the algorithm below.
The puncturing algorithm in [10], [11] is based on the following two definitions. A punctured variable node p is called I-step recoverable (l-SR) node if it has at least one neighboring check node (called survived check node) whose neighboring variable nodes are all unpunctured except for p. Generalizing this, a punctured variable node p is called k-step recoverable (k-SR) node if it has at least one survived check node that is connected to at least one (k-I)-SR node. Furthermore the other nodes that it is connected to need to be m-SR nodes, where 0 <:: m <:: k - 1. Under these conditions, note that the k-SR node will be recovered after exactly k iterations of iterative decoding assuming that the channel does not cause any errors. So a higher number of low-SR nodes will reduce the overall number of iterations, which results in good puncturing performance.

The general idea of the puncturing algorithm is to maximize the number of lower-SR nodes for a given parity-check matrix and puncture the lower-SR nodes first. By doing so, punctured nodes can be recovered with the help of other unpunctured nodes in lesser number of iterations. The puncturing algorithm presented above is focused on the selection of the nodes to be punctured for a given code. An important open problem here is the design of good codes for puncturing, or equivalently based on the above criterion, design of a code such that the lower-SR nodes are maximized. It is also important to ensure that the code has a simple encoder. This paper provides an example of such a code construction.

Lemma 2: Suppose there exists a length-2s cycle in a matrix which consists of only weight two columns. Consider the submatrix formed by the subset of col­ umns that participates in the cycle. Then, all the par­ ticipating rows in the cycle must have degree two in that submatrix. Proof To have a length-2s cycle, the number of col­ umns participating in the cycle needs to be s and the number of rows participating in the cycle needs to be s. Let us denote the submatrix formed by the columns participating in the cycle by U. Then, the number of edges in U is 2s since each of the columns has degree two. Each row participating in the cycle must have a degree greater than or equal to two in U since each row has to link at least two different columns in U. Suppose there is a row having degree strictly greater than two in U. Then, there should be a row having a degree less than two in U i.e. equal to one, since the average row weight in U is two (the number of edges / the number of rows = 2s / s = 2), which is a contra­ diction. This is because a row that has degree-one in U cannot participate in a cycle with the columns in U. Thus, every participating row must have degree two in U.

Conclusion

We have proposed a new class of codes, E2RC codes, which has several strong points. First, the codes are efficiently encodable. We have presented shift­ register implementation of encoder which has low­ complexity. We also showed that a simple erasure de­ coder can also be used for the linear-time encoding of these codes. Thus, we can share a message-passing decoder for both encoding and decoding if it is ap­ plied to the transceiver systems which require an en­ coder/decoder pair. Second, we have shown that the non-systematic parts of the parity-check matrix are cyc1e-free, which ensures good code characteristics. From simulations, the performance of the E2RC codes (mother codes) is as good as that of eIRA codes and other irregular LDPC codes. Finally, the E2RC codes having systematic rate-compatible puncturing struc­ ture show better puncturing performance than other irregular LDPC codes and eIRA codes in all ranges of code rates. From simulations, the puncturing of the E2RC codes outperform 2.8dB E,JNo than random puncturing of irregular LDPC codes at a BER of 10-5 with code rate 0.8. Even applying the best effort in­ tentional puncturing algorithm to the irregular LDPC codes, E2RC codes show l .2dB of E,JNo better than that of irregular LDPC codes at a BER of 10-5 with code rate of 0.9. Comparing with eIRA codes for code rate of 0.9, E2RC codes show l .l dB of E,JNo better than that of eIRA codes at a BER of 10-5•
