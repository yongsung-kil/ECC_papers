Design and Decoding of a Serial Concatenated Code Structure 
based on Quasi-Cyclic LDPC Codes 
Jean-Baptiste Dore, Marie-Helfme Hamon and Pierre Penard 
France Telecom, Research and Development Division, 4 rue du clos courtel, 35512 Cesson-Sevigne, France 
{jeanbaptiste.dore, mhelene.hamon, pierre.penard}@francetelecom.com 
Abstract 
In this paper, we introduce a new serially concatenated code structure, based on Quasi-Cyclic (QC) codes. This 
structure can be viewed as LDPC codes, and benefits from the advantages of structured QC LDPC codes: simple 
characterization, good performance for moderate code length and efficient implementation. These codes enable 
different types of decoding algorithms, from classical Belief Propagation to Turbo-Like decoding algorithms. A 
joint analysis, in terms of complexity, convergence and performance, has been performed to determine the best 
decoding choice. 
1 
Introduction 
Turbo codes and Low-Density Parity Check (LDPC) 
codes, have raised a significant interest in the scientific 
community in the past few years and are now increas­
ingly considered for introduction into communication 
systems [1]. This evolution towards industrialization 
motivates the design of codes suitable for hardware 
design. In this paper, we investigate a new subclass 
of LDPC codes, which can be viewed either as LDPC 
codes or serially concatenated codes. A main feature 
of these codes is their decoding flexibility, as they 
can be easily adjusted to different types of decoding 
algorithms. After providing an overview of these codes 
in a first section, we will then detail the decoding pos­
sibilities and finally compare them through complexity, 
convergence and performance evaluation.

2.1 Background 
The increasing interest in concatenated codes has shed 
a new light on Hybrid Concatenated Convolutional 
(HCC) code, first introduced in [2]. A particularly 
interesting code construction was described in [3]. The 
authors defined a new class of Turbo Like (TL) codes, 
which can be interpreted either as serially concatenated 
convolutional codes or structured LDPC codes, and are 
labelled Systematic with Serially Concatenated Parity 
(S-SCP) codes. The S-SCP codes intend to combine 
the best assets of each class of codes, i.e. structured 
LDPC codes and Turbo Codes. The S-SCP structure is 
based on the concatenation of a systematic outer code, 
an interleaver and an Inner Parity Generator (IPG), as 
depicted on Figure 1. A special case is detailed in [3], 
in which the inner parity generator is obtained through 
the concatenation of a Single Parity Check (SPC) code

another codeword. QC LOPC codes are of particular in­
terest as they simplifY encoding and decoding process. 
Hardware requirements are restricted to shift registers. 
QC LOPC codes from circulant permutation matrices 
have largely been studied [5][6]. More recently, authors

3 
Iterative decoding of QC S-SCP 
codes 
The original structure proposed by [3] allows many 
decoding opportunities, and this advantage is confirmed 
with the use of QC codes which enable efficient decod­
ing architecture. A first possible decoding method is 
the standard message passing belief propagation (BP) 
algorithm. A particular scheduling derived from the 
vertical shuffle scheduling of [7] and also known as 
Layered BP (LBP) is also well suited for QC LOPC 
decoding. On the other hand, turbo like decoding is also 
enabled. It can be easily verified that turbo decoding is 
a particular scheduling of the BP algorithm.

3.2 Layered Belief Propagation algorithm 
Layered Belief Propagation algorithm is similar to 
BP algorithm, but with a specific scheduling known 
as shuffle scheduling [7]. The two steps of the BP 
algorithm are done on groups of parity check equations. 
The structure proposed allows computation on groups 
of z parity check equations without memory clashes 
because there is only a single one per column on 
each layer. The number of operations per iteration 
is increased compared to the BP algorithm, but the 
algorithm converges quicker, reducing the number of 
iterations required to achieve good performance.

the outer to inner SISO decoders. The second step (SPC 
outward) determines the messages from inner to outer 
SISO decoders. This scheduling is a mixture of vertical 
shuffle scheduling on G1, flooding scheduling on V 
and vertical shuffle scheduling on G2. 
The structure proposed in section 2 allows several 
possible scheduling variants. We have described the 
three main ones, but other possibilities include the 
concatenation of an outer SISO decoder (on G1) and 
a LDPC decoder (on [V G2] using BP or LBP). The 
quasi cyclic nature of the code, with its natural division 
into layers, enables many opportunities for the choice 
of the decoding algorithm and/or scheduling.

5 
Conclusion 
We have proposed here a new sub class of S-SCP code 
based on circulant shifted identity matrices. A joint 
analysis of performance, convergence and complexity 
has been presented. This paper illustrates the interesting 
convergence and complexity properties of LBP and 
TL decoding algorithms and shows that the choice of 
the scheduling is a key factor for efficient hardware 
decoding. Moreover, quasi-cyclic codes are well suited 
to hardware implementation, enabling an efficient and 
simplified architecture design. Finally this new subclass 
of S-SCP codes offers competitive advantages to meet 
given hardware complexity and performance require­
ments.
