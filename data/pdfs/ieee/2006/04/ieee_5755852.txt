Design ofLDPC codes for BP algorithm with channel estimation error 
Hamid Saeedi and Amir H. Banihashemi 
Department of Systems and Computer Engineering, Carleton University, Ottawa, ON, Canada 
{hsaeedi,ahashemi@sce.carleton.ca} 
Abstract- When belief propagation (BP) algorithm is used to decode low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes over 
the binary input additive white Gaussian noise (BIA WGN) channel, the knowledge of the channel noise power is 
necessary for the algorithm to achieve its predicted performance. If the noise power is estimated erroneously, we 
refer to this as "signal to noise ratio (SNR) mismatch". In this paper, irregular LDPC codes are designed that 
perform better (have a lower SNR threshold) in the presence of mismatch compared to conventionally designed 
irregular LDPC codes that are optimized for zero mismatch. It is shown that with a negligible performance loss at 
zero mismatch, highly irregular codes that are more robust against mismatch can be designed. A method to generate 
EXIT curves in the presence of mismatch is proposed that will be used to design such irregular codes. Finite block 
length simulation results are provided to confirm the asymptotic analysis results.

Different methods have been proposed to 
design good iregular LDPC codes. One well-known 
method is density evolution (DE) [1]. For codes on 
BIA WGN channel this method has a high complexity. 
Other methods have been proposed in [8],[11] and [12] 
based on Gaussian approximation of densities to reduce 
the complexity. The method suggested in [8] is based 
on tracking the mean of the Gaussian estimated version 
of the messages while the approximation proposed in 
[11] and [12] is based on keeping track of the mutual 
information between the transmitted bit and the output 
messages of variable nodes and check nodes through 
extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) charts. The 
methods proposed in [8],[11],[12] to analyze and design 
LDPC codes are based on the assumption that the initial 
message at the input of the decoding algorithm is 
consistent. Moreover, the messages maintain this 
condition during the iterations [1]. In the EXIT chart 
method, the input a priori information to check nodes 
(variable nodes) is modeled as a consistent Gaussian 
variable with the given mutual information and the 
resulting mutual information at the output of check 
nodes (variable nodes) is computed. In the mismatch 
case, as the VMR of the initial Gaussian message is no 
longer 2, the consistency condition is violated. 
Therefore, to generate EXIT curves, we can no longer 
model the input a priori information to check nodes 
(variable nodes) by a consistent Gaussian variable and 
therefore EXIT charts can not be generated as explained 
in [11] and [12]. 
In this paper, we propose a method to generate 
EXIT charts in the presence of mismatch. We then use 
these charts to design irregular codes which perform 
well in the presence of SNR mismatch. We demonstrate 
through an example that irregular codes can be 
designed that not only have vary good performance 
when no mismatch is present, but also are more robust 
against mismatch compared to codes of equal maximum 
variable node degree, optimized for no-mismatch case. 
The rest of the paper is organized as follows: in 
next section we formulate the problem. In section III, 
after giving a brief review on EXIT charts, we propose 
a method to generate EXIT curves in the presence of 
mismatch. In section IV, we explain the code design 
using EXIT charts. In section V, simulation results are 
presented and section VI concludes the paper. 
2-PROBLEM FORMULATION 
Consider an offset range of [111> 112], where 111 and 112 
are real numbers, usually negative and positive 
respectively. In this paper we are interested in an 
irregular code ensemble for which the largest threshold 
over the offset range [111> 112] is minimized over all 
possible degree distributions with a fixed maximum 
variable node degree. This is a min-max optimization. 
We know that SNR offset degrades the performance of 
BP, and that the larger the offset the worse the 
performance. Therefore, if 0<111<112 [111<112<0], the 
problem is reduced to minimizing a(112) [a(11I)]. It is a 
well-known fact that underestimation of SNR is more 
deteriorating than overestimation [2-7]. In the case that 
1] 1<0<1]2 and if 1] IS-1]2, the optimization problem is then 
reduced to minimizing a(1]I). This includes the special 
case of 1]1=-1]2 which is of more interest and is 
considered here. For other cases in this category

We need to minimize the maximum of a(1]I) and a(1]2) 
but the offset for which the threshold has to be 
optimized is not necessarily one of the two sides of the 
interval. In this case, we need to minimize the threshold 
for a range of offsets and choose a degree distribution 
for which the maximum of a(1]I) and a(1]2) is 
minimized.

In this work, to design codes in the presence of

node and variables node curves stay open. We then 
choose the degree distributions that give the lowest 
possible threshold.

6-CONCLUSIONS 
In this paper, using EXIT charts, we design 
irregular LOPC codes that are robust against channel 
estimation errors, when decoded by belief propagation 
algorithm. In a min-max sense, the designed codes 
perform better than irregular codes, conventionally 
optimized for zero SNR mismatch, over the range of 
interest for SNR offset. This is at the expense of slight 
degradation in performance at zero offset. The 
performance improvement over conventional irregular 
codes in many cases continues even outside the 
mismatch range of interest.
