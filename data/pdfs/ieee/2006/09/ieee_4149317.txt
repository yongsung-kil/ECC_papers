Combinatorial Construction of Low-Density Parity-Check Codes for Short Block Length and
High Rate Applications

Suquan Ding, Zhixing Yang, Jian Song, Changyong Pan and Jun Wang Tsinghua National Laboratory for Information Science and Technology,
Department of Electronic Engineering, Tsinghua University, Beijing, 100084, China Email: dsq03@mails.tsinghua.edu.cn, yangzhx, jsong, pcy and wjun@tsinghua.edu.cn

Abstract— This paper presents a method to construct regular low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes of short block length and high rate based on a special type of combinatoric designs, i.e., balanced incomplete block designs (BIBDs) with λ = 2 whose construction are based on cyclic difference families. Although the Tanner graph of the codes constructed contains some cycles of length 4, simulation results show that they perform well under belief propagation (BP) decoding. Further, these codes are quasicyclic (QC) codes and hence can be encoded efficiently.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes first discovered by Gallager [1] had been overlooked for almost 35 years. After rediscovered by MacKay [2], this class of codes has been shown to achieve remarkable performance very close to the Shannon limit with iterative decoding. Hence, LDPC codes have become a focal point of research since their rediscovery.
According to methods of construction, LDPC codes can be divided into two categories: random codes [1] and structured codes [6]. Although long random LDPC codes in general perform closer to the Shannon limit than their equivalent structured LDPC codes, they usually do not have sufficient structure to allow simple encoding. On the other hand, structured codes in general have encoding advantage over random codes, especially cyclic or quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes [9]. In fact, well-designed structured LDPC codes perform equally well as random LDPC codes for the real applications where the block length is limited.
A Tanner graph displays the relationship between codeword bits and parity checks and provides a useful representation of LDPC codes [5]. Each of the code bits and parity checks are represented by a node in the graph, called bit node and check node, respectively. A graph edge joins a bit node to a check node if the code bit is included in the parity check. The number of edges connected to a node is called the degree of the node. An LDPC code is said to be (γ, ρ)-regular if the degrees of all bit nodes are γ and the degrees of all check nodes are ρ. A cycle in a Tanner graph is a sequence of connected bit nodes and check nodes which start and end at the same node in the graph, and which contain other nodes no more than once. The length of the cycle is simply the number of edges it contains.
LDPC codes perform well under belief-propagation (BP) decoding algorithm [4]. It is known that BP algorithm con-

verges to the optimal solution when the Tanner graph is free of cycles. Although Tanner graph of Long LDPC codes can be approximately considered as cycle-free, this is not true for codes of moderate and short length, of which a satisfactory theory is still lacking. In fact, some commonly believed principles for designing LDPC codes with good performance under BP decoding are used in the construction of LDPC codes. These principles specify the Tanner graph of codes with the following characteristics:
• the degree of each bit node is big. • the degree of each check node is small. • free of cycles of length 4.
For construction of regular LDPC codes with fixed block length and rate, the above characteristics can not be satisfied simultaneously, therefore, compromise is required. In most previous constructions of LDPC codes, the third characteristic is of the highest priority to be satisfied. However, in some cases, the first characteristic also plays an important role in improving the performance of LDPC codes [9]. In fact, some LDPC codes whose Tanner graph contains cycles of length 4 are constructed which also perform well under BP decoding [8].
It is shown in [3] that it is impossible to make a regular LDPC codes of short block length and high rate for certain code parameters if the third characteristic above must be satisfied. Also, as we will show, for short block length and high rate LDPC codes, codes with cycles of length 4 can outperform their equivalent codes whose Tanner graph is free of cycles of length 4. Hence, it is not necessary to always satisfy the third characteristic for construction of LDPC codes, especially for short block length and high rate cases.
A special branch of combinatorial mathematics, known as balanced incomplete block design (BIBD) [10], is used to construct LDPC codes [11]. However, only a special class of BIBDs, i.e., BIBDs with λ = 1, is used in construction of LDPC codes to ensure that the Tanner graph of the codes is free of cycles of length 4. In this paper, a new class of BIBDs, i.e., BIBDs with λ = 2, is used in the construction of LDPC codes of short block length (N < 1000) and high rate (R > 2/3). The Tanner graph of the codes constructed contains some cycles of length 4, yet, they still perform well

• Each Xi is a k-subset of X. • Each 2-subset of X is a subset of exactly λ of the sets
X1, X2, . . . , Xb. • The integers v, k and λ satisfy 0 < λ and k < v − 1.

Let G be an additive abelian group of order v. Then t kelement subsets of G, Bi = {bi,1, bi,2, . . . , bi,k} (1 ≤ i ≤ t) form a (v, k, λ) difference family if every nonzero element of G occurs λ times among the differences bi,x − bi,y (i = 1, . . . , t; x, y = 1, . . . , k). The sets Bi are base blocks. If t = 1, then B1 is an abelian (v, k, λ) difference set. If B1, . . . , Bt form a (v, k, λ) difference family, then the translations of the base blocks, namely Bi + g = {bi,1 + g, bi,2 + g, . . . , bi,k + g} (i = 1, . . . , t, g ∈ G), form a (b, v, r, k, λ) BIBD, where r = λ(v−1)/(k−1) and b = vr/k. The BIBD is cyclic if G = Zv, where Zv is the ring of integers modulo v, and in this case the difference family is a cyclic difference family.

IV. SIMULATION RESULTS

The error performances of some codes given in Table I are presented in this section. All the simulations are conducted over additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel with BPSK modulation. The sum-product algorithm (SPA) is employed for decoding with maximum iteration 50.
The error performances of the (155, 125) LDPC code constructed based on the (155, 31, 20, 4, 2) BIBD are shown in Fig. 1. For comparison, the error performances of an LDPC code constructed based on a (155, 31, 15, 3, 1) BIBD and a randomly generated LDPC code with column weight 3 are also shown in the figure. The LDPC code constructed based on the BIBD with λ = 2 is (4, 20)-regular and its Tanner graph contains 465 cycles of length 4. The LDPC code constructed based on the BIBD with λ = 1 is (3, 15)-regular and its Tanner graph is free of cycles of length 4. According to the three characteristics stated in the first section, the only advantage of the former code over the latter code is that its column weight is bigger. It is shown in Fig. 1 that the error performances of the two code are almost the same at high SNR and the latter code perform slightly better at low SNR. Also, both codes constructed based on BIBDs outperform the randomly constructed code by about 0.7 dB at a BER 2 × 10−6.
The error performances of two (252, 224) LDPC codes are shown in Fig. 2. One of the code is constructed based on the (252, 28, 27, 3, 2) BIBD and is (3, 27)-regular. Its Tanner graph contains 378 cycles of length 4. The other code is generated randomly with column weight 3. Although the former code contains some cycles of length 4, it is shown that this code outperforms the randomly generated LDPC code by 1 dB at a BER 1 × 10−6 and the former code outperforms the latter code at all range of SNR. Also, error floor appears at high SNR for both code.

Fig. 2. Error performance of (252, 224) LDPC codes.
The error performance of two (255, 204) LDPC codes are shown in Fig. 3. The code constructed based on (255, 51, 25, 5, 2) BIBD is (5, 25)-regular and its Tanner graph contains 1275 cycles of length 4. The other code is generated randomly with column weight 3. It is shown that the code constructed based on BIBD with λ = 2 outperform the random code by 0.4 dB at a BER 2 × 10−7 while the latter code outperforms the former code slightly at low SNR. Moreover, error floor appears for the randomly generated LDPC code at BER 1×10−6 while no error floor occurs for the structured LDPC code at this BER.
It is shown from the above simulation results that the deterioration of the performance caused by cycles of length 4 in the Tanner graph of the constructed LDPC code is relatively small because the number of these cycles is relatively small for high rate codes. Further, as cycles of the length 4 deteriorate the performance of the constructed codes slightly at low SNR, the deterioration is almost negligible at high SNR. V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, a class of LDPC codes of short block length and high rate is constructed based on BIBDs with λ = 2 and the BIBDs are constructed based on cyclic difference families. The LDPC codes constructed are regular QC-LDPC codes and hence can be encoded efficiently. Although the Tanner graph of the code contains some cycles of length 4, they still perform well under BP decoding. Simulation results shown that the relatively small number of cycles of length 4 in the Tanner graph of the constructed codes deteriorate the performance of the codes slightly at low SNR while their negative impact on the performance is negligible at high SNR. Further, the constructed LDPC codes outperform randomly generated codes when the code rate is very high. Hence, for the construction of LDPC codes of short block length and high rate with good performance, the property of free of cycles of length 4 in the Tanner graph is not necessarily always be satisfied.
