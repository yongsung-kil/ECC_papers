Early Stopping for LDPC Decoding: Convergence of Mean Magnitude (CMM)
Jin Li, Student Member, IEEE, Xiao-hu You, Senior Member, IEEE, and Jing Li (Tiffany), Member, IEEE

Abstract— A new early stopping criterion is proposed for low density parity check (LDPC) codes to reduce the number of decoding iterations while preserving a good decoding performance. The new criterion is based on the convergence of the mean magnitude (CMM) of the log-likelihood ratio messages at the output of each decoding iteration. Information-theoretic support and extensive simulations are provided to demonstrate the efficiency of the criterion.
Index Terms— LDPC codes, iterative decoding, stopping rule.
I. INTRODUCTION
S OFT iterative decoding enables near-optimal decoding for turbo and low density parity check (LDPC) codes with manageable complexity [1]. Since the decoding complexity and delay increases linearly with the number of decoding iterations, and since a pre-set maximum iteration number fails to capture the dynamics of the communication channel, early stopping criterion (SC), which adaptively adjusts the number of decoding iterations to the instantaneous channel condition and decoding status, arises as an appealing solution.
A variety of excellent stopping criteria have been proposed, but they almost exclusively target turbo codes, lacking explicit consideration for LDPC codes. For example, the cross entropy (CE) criterion due to Hagenauer et al [2] evaluates the CE between the log-likelihood ratio (LLR) messages at the output of the two sub-decoders of the turbo code to determine whether further iterations are necessary. The sign change ratio (SCR) criterion and the hard decision aided (HDA) criterion proposed by Shao et al [3] examine, respectively, the number of sign changes in the extrinsic LLR information and the hard decision of the overall LLR information at the output of the same sub-decoder of turbo codes. Other noteworthy work includes, for example, [4] by Gracie et al, which introduced a method based on maximum-likelihood sequence estimation, [5] by Zhai et al, which discussed the mean estimate (ME) method, the mean sign-change (MSC) method and a combined MSC and cyclic redundancy check (CRC) method, and [6] by Matache el al, which tested several stopping criteria for turbo codes.

We examine the evolution of the mean magnitude of the LLR messages along the messaging passing decoding process, and propose the convergence of the mean magnitude (CMM) of decoder LLRs as a figure of merit to judge whether further iterations are beneficial. We note that the ME method [5], [6] also make use of the mean magnitude of decoder LLRs. There the absolute value of the mean magnitude is compared with a threshold, where for optimal performance the threshold is adjusted from one code to another and from one channel signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) to another, and the decision can be vulnerable to channel mismatch. Instead, we consider the convergence speed, or the incremental change, of the mean magnitude, which is close in nature to the first order derivative of the mean magnitude. Our approach is supported by the inherent information-theoretic relation between the mean magnitude and the error rate, and extensive simulations demonstrate that the proposed CMM criterion is insensitive to SNRs, code lengths or code rates.

A. Adaption Two Existing methods to LDPC Codes
Before proceeding to the discussion of CMM, let us first adapt two existing methods, the hard decision aided criterion and the cross entropy criterion, to LDPC codes for simulation comparison with CMM.
Let Imax be the maximum number of decoding iterations. The application of the HDA criterion [3] to LDPC codes is straight-forward:
1) At the end of the ith iteration (i ≥ 1), compute the hard decision, Dji , of the decoder LLR for the jth bit Li(j).
2) If Dji = Dji−1 for all j’s or i ≥ Imax, terminate the iterative decoder; otherwise proceed to the next iteration.

3) If the counter reaches P or i = Imax, terminate the iterative decoder; otherwise proceed to the next iteration.
The computational complexity and the storage requirement are important in implementing a stopping criterion. For a code with length N , at each decoding iteration, the HDA method needs N binary operations, at most N logic operations and N integer memory units [3]. The (modified) CE criterion requires (5N−1) real-number operations and N+2 real-number memory units. The CMM criterion takes N+2 real-number operations, two integer memory units to store P and the counter, and two real-number units to store E[Li−1] and λ. V. CONCLUSION
Exploiting the convergence characteristics of the mean magnitude of the LLRs at the output of the soft decoder, we have proposed the convergence of mean magnitude as an efficient metric to early terminate iterative decoding for LDPC codes. Two existing methods originally proposed for turbo codes, the cross entropy and the hard decision aided criteria, are also adapted to LDPC codes. Extensive simulations show that CMM strikes the best balance between decoding complexity, delay and performance. Additionally, optimal parameters of CMM are insensitive to channel SNRs, frame sizes or code rates, making operations easy. Finally, although our theoretic support for CMM is based on LDPC codes, the method can be generally applied to other soft, iteratively decodable codes.
