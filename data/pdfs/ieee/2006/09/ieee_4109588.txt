A Perturbation Method for Decoding of LDPC Aided by CRC

Chunlei LIN, Dongliang XIAO, Hongbing CHENG, Meng MA and Bingli JIAO

Dept. of Electronics Peking University Beijing, 100871, P.R. China E-mail: jiaobl@pku.edu.cn Tel.: +86-10-6276 3030; Fax: +86-10-6275 9444.

Abstract- In this paper, we propose a new decoding method for LDPC aided by CRC and perturbation for improving FER performance. The CRC is used to check the decoding error of BP algorithm. Once an error is found, the perturbation method will be used to start re-decoding processing. Simulation results show that this method performs within about 1.2dB of the sphere-packing bound for codeword length of several hundred bits.
Key Words - LDPC, BP, perturbation
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have attracted great interest due to capacity-achieving performance at long codeword length [1][2]. However, at short codeword length (typically several hundred bits), LDPC codes significantly deviate from the predicted performance [3].
As has been known, the short cycles in Tanner Graph imposes a difficulty for BP algorithm in achieving the optimum solution [4]. For this reason, some work has been carried out for designing LDPC codes with large girth, i.e. the length of the smallest cycle [5]. However, the achievement is limited because there exists a lower bound of girth for a given codeword length [1]. Moreover, the exact relationship between girth and the error performance for short codes is unknown [6].
In alternation, some authors proposed to use concatenation method to short LDPC codes for improving FER performance. These methods include LDPC-LDPC-, LDPC-turbo- and LDPC-CRC concatenation in [7],[8] and [9], respectively. In this paper, we propose a new decoding method to the LDPC-CRC scheme by an introducing perturbation approach.
In the proposed method, the function of the CRC is to find the error among the decisions made by BP algorithm. Once an error is found, a perturbation vector will be added to the initial inputs of the decoder, then a

re-decoding processing starts. We facilitate a multi-decoding scheme by loop operation, thereby a statistically independent perturbation vector is added in each loop. The detailed discussions and the simulation results are given in the following sections.

III. PERTURBATION METHOD
In this section, we concatenate short LDPC code with CRC-k code, frame by frame, in the channel encoder and decoder (CODEC). At the encoder side, a k-bit CRC tailor is complemented to the (n-m-k)-bit Information as the input of the LDPC (n, m) encoder. The animation of the combination can be used to calculate the LDPC decoding values by BP algorithm and control the undetected error by the CRC at the decoder side. We should select an appropriate number of redundant CRC bits in considerations of the final FER, i.e. the remaining FER after the CRC, and the bandwidth efficiency. To guarantee that CRC error probability is at order of 10−2 lower than the result of the BP algorithm in SNR region 1.0-4.5dB, we need several redundant CRC bits. Unlike the conventional procedure, a perturbation method is used in the re-decoding scheme at the receiver, rather than the re-transmission, whenever an error is found. The successful operation of the abovementioned scheme is based on the fact that adding perturbations to the received bits can modify the belief distribution in initializing BP algorithm and, therefore, lead to another decoding result. Because the perturbation power plays, in fact, a role as noise in the re-decoding processing, one may wish to reduce the portion of the perturbed bits and power of the perturbation. However, the number of the perturbed bits can not be too small because there would be, otherwise, strong correlation between the results of decoding and re-decoding or among the re-decoding themselves. Consequently, the re-decoding is not effective for giving a “new” result. What we do here is to select a portion of bits (from all bits of a codeword), which have the lowest reliabilities, to the perturbation because these bits are more sensitive to the perturbation and, at the same time, have comparable reliability contribution in comparison with those having larger reliabilities [10, p. 15].

Again, based on the same considerations mentioned above, the power of the perturbation should be also optimized. In this research, we use simulation method to search the optimum number of perturbed bits and the perturbation power in a useful SNR range.

IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
To show an evidence of effectiveness of the proposed method, we take the short LDPC codes, having 192 or 384 bits in length with code rate of 1/2, which have been specified in IEEE802.16e [11]. The concatenating CRC-8 code is generated by X8+X4+X3+X2+1.

The number of the perturbed bits and the perturbation power are optimized by huge amount simulations taken for L = 24 +12i, 0 ≤ i ≤ 6 and σ p = 0.25 + 0.05 j, 0 ≤ j ≤ 5 , where i and j are integers.
For every pair of the parameters, i.e. L and σ p , the
FERs of the decoder are monitored in a SNR region from 1dB to 4.5dB. The best overall FER performance is

The FER performance is shown in Fig.2, in which comparisons are made for the corresponding results in IEEE 802.16e and sphere-packing bound, which is calculated according to [12]. In the simulation, the maximum iteration number of LDPC decoder is 50 and the maximum loop number of re-decoding is 100.
As shown in Fig.2, one finds that the proposed method shows FER improvement at about 0.5dB gain over those of IEEE 802.16e at FER=10E-4. Careful numerical comparisons show that the improved performances are 1.12dB and 1.19dB close to the sphere packing bounds at FER=10E-4 at the codeword length of 196 and 384, respectively.
Finally, it is noted that the object of this paper is to show the effectiveness of the perturbation method. The practical implementation may benefit bandwidth efficiency, but require more calculation power.
V. CONCLUSION
This study shows an evidence of the perturbation method for improving the FER performance. The simulation results on the two short LDPC codes specified in IEEE 802.16e show about 1.2dB SNR loss in comparison with the sphere bound.
