Layered Decoding of Non-Layered LDPC Codes
Massimo Rovini, Francesco Rossi, Pasquale Ciao, Nicola L’Insalata, and Luca Fanucci Dept. of Information Engineering, University of Pisa, via G. Caruso 16, Pisa, I-56122, Italy
{massimo.rovini, francesco.rossi, pasquale.ciao, nicola.linsalata, luca.fanucci@iet.unipi.it}

Abstract
The principle of “layered decoding” is extended to those codes not especially conceived for this practice, as to benefit of the increased convergence speed. Two different strategies are considered to solve the problem and the related architectures presented: one more straightforward, and based on the use for the soft output of the last value originated in a layer; the other based on the computation of the variation (or delta) of the soft output metrics to allow concurrent updates. Then, as in architecture-first approach, the performance are assessed for several widths of the layer, which remarks the robustness of the delta-mechanism to high parallelisation factors. As in the exact layered decoding, the average boost of two times in the convergence speed is shown.
1 Introduction
New advances in the field of digital transmission systems and microelectronic technology have recently led to the discovery or refinement of more and more powerful channel coding techniques based on iterative decoding, which achieve performance near to the Shannon limit of channel capacity. This stimulated the rediscovery of the LDPC codes. In the original regular form proposed by Gallager in 1963 [6], LDPC codes perform slightly worse than their competitors turbo codes [2], but the gap is inverted by irregular codes [10, 14].
The key advantage of LDPC codes is the lack of sequentiality among the elaborations of the decoding algorithm, which can, therefore, be fully parallelised and potentially implemented at high speed. However, an effective hardware realisation is still an open issue, since problems of gate complexity and especially routing congestion arise in fullyparallel solutions [3, 5], while throughput and memory collisions are the bottleneck of mixed serial-parallel or semiparallel solutions. In order to overcome these problems, the current trend is the construction of “implementation-

oriented” codes [4, 13, 21, 22] and more recently a growing interest has been put in the study of different schedules of the elaborations of the belief-propagation (BP) algorithm, as to speed up the decoder convergence and use a smaller number of iterations for an increased throughput.
The original flooding schedule updates first all the check–to–variable messages and then all the variable–to– check messages at any iteration. On the other hand, a single or a certain number of check nodes (called layer) can be first updated, followed by the whole set of connected variable nodes, and so on, layer after layer; dually, a certain variable node (or layer of variable nodes) could be updated first, followed by all his connected check nodes then. This two approaches are referred to as vertical and horizontal shuffle in [20]. Within the same iteration, decoding benefits from the use of more updated estimates, thus speeding up its convergence. The first relevant works on this subject have been by Mansour and Shanbhag [11], [12], who have used a single row as a layer. Hocevar [7] has overcome this limit designing the code in such a way that the rows of the parity check matrix can be grouped into subsets in which the column weight is at most one. So-defined codes allow the update of all the rows in a subset (or layer) with no losses in both error correction rate and convergence speed, similarly to the 1-row layered decoding. For this reason, we will refer to codes like these as “layered” codes, in opposition to “non-layered” codes, featuring column weights higher than one within layers.
Always in [7], the data flow of the BP algorithm is rearranged in order to obtain more efficient hardware architectures. Our approach is different, as we focus on “nonlayered” LDPC codes, which are also treated in a layered way. To this aim, we will describe the VLSI architecture of two different update strategies to efficiently approximate the layered decoding of “non-layered” codes. Although this analysis focuses on the family of codes in use in DVB-S2, it holds for any other class of “non-layered” codes, giving at the same time a methodology to check the applicability of the layered decoding and to perform it in an efficient way.

2.2 Layered Decoding

Introduced by Mansour [11,12] and Hocevar [7], layered decoding has been admirably analysed in [17]. The term layered decoding stems from thinking the parity check matrix as a sequence of elementary matrixes, or layers, which are updated in sequence. Layers are usually thought as horizontal. For this reason, others call this schedule horizontal shuffle [20]. Dually, vertical shuffle or layer has been considered in [9,20]. However, horizonal shuffle or CN–centric decoding is more suitable for serial implementations of the check node processor, and is therefore preferable for practical implementations.

In this case, layered decoding may be only performed with layers covering only a single row, as non-trivial layers could result in column weights greater than one. This is the case of the codes in use in DVB-S2, and it counteracts the inherent parallelism of the layered elaborations encouraged by the code definition.
To figure out the situation, let ρ1 and ρ2 denote two overlapping blocks, i.e., connecting the same variables and check nodes but with different rotations. Let B be the size of these blocks, and with a semi-parallel decoder architecture in mind, consider an array of L = B processors attempting to updates the messages of ρ1 and ρ2. Indeed, it means that to update block ρ1 and to perform the input marginalisation in (7) and (8), the array of processors would have to wait for decoding of block ρ2 to complete, by definition, as to use the most updated estimates for its soft outputs. But ρ2 itself depends on the update of block ρ1 because of the common SO. Accordingly, a paradox arises in the layered principle and this situation can’t be nominally handled under layered decoding.
The problem gets even worse when the architectural parallelism is pushed beyond the natural limit offered by the structure of the parity check matrix, i.e. for L = k · B with k a positive integer. Such codes are referred to as “nonlayered” throughout this paper, with the meaning that layered decoding fails with semi-parallel architectures featuring L > 1, as the weight-1 constraint on the columns of the layer is not met.

4 Hardware Architectures
Equations (7)–(9) are implemented in a node processor of a layered decoder. As mentioned above, the main advantages of this schedule is the increased converge speed, which boosts the decoder throughput. Moreover, the variable node phase is embedded into the check elaboration, which further halves the decoding latency of both serial and parallel solutions. However, this phenomenon is more crucial with serial architectures of the elementary processors. In this case, the flooding algorithm performs the two phases in sequence, which roughly doubles the iteration latency compared to the layered case. A possible back-up solution

not to spoil the system throughput consists in duplicating the size of the message memory, as to store the messages of two frames elaborated in parallel [16].
The top-level architecture of a semi-parallel layered decoder for structured LDPC codes like DVB-S2 or the ones simulated in next Sect. 5 is shown in Fig. 1. Locations of the SO memory and of the check–to–variable or message memory are arranged in rows to enable parallel elaborations. Within a memory row, check–to–variable messages are stored in the natural check order, as to avoid reordering before or after the elaboration. On the contrary, as SOs are used by different node processors with different rotations, they are stored in the variable node order within a row, and permutations (cyclic rotations) take place before and after update to match the order on the processors.

(a) the last SO value is always over-written, in such a way to use the most recent update;

A further gain in speed is offered when serial VLSI architectures are implemented, as the ones outlined in Sect. 4. Roughly speaking, layered decoding can be seen as a singlephase decoding, the variable phase being embedded in the check phase with almost no extra latency. Accordingly, the number of clock cycles of one iteration is one half that of a flooding algorithm, or, in other words, one flooding iteration takes two layered iterations. Comparing the two schedules for a given latency, or dually for a given throughput, the number of layered iterations can be normalised to the flooding one (see label Norm. in Fig.6). Thus, the average speed of layered decoding becomes four times the flooding one. For the same reason, a maximum of 50 flooding iterations or 100 layered iterations are run.
The δ-layered algorithm also allows degrees of parallelism higher than the natural width of a layer, or say, multiple of it, L = k · B. As a matter of fact, SOs in common to different macro-rows are managed in the same way as within a single macro-row by the δ mechanism1. Fig. 7 uses k = 2 to decode two macro-rows in parallel. The ratio of overlapping blocks is η1/2(2) = 24.6%, but no appreciable losses are noticed in performances. The increased η only reduces the convergence speed, which is about 0.5 iterations lower than for L = 1 or L = 27.

7 Conclusion
This paper extended the concept of layered decoding of LDPC codes to those codes named “non-layered”, which would not support it in principle. This definition has been given with the meaning that no semi-parallel architectures performing layered decoding can fit with the code construction. Of course, a fully serial architecture featuring 1-row layers would be always feasible, but highly impractical.
Starting from the original formulation, the rearrangement of the decoding operations led to specify two different but similar architectures, one directly producing the new estimate of the soft output, the other computing its incremental variation w.r.t. the previous estimate (δ-decoding). Although the former is suitable for true layered codes, it can be also used with non-layered codes when the last update of the SO in common overwrites previous in-layer updates. On the other hand, the second architecture exploits all the contributes of a certain layer to the shared SO, by summing different delta’s together.
As a benchmark, we used a small code blockwise structured, initially proposed for IEEE 802.11n, and very similar to the LDPC codes of DVB-S2. After defining a measure of how much a code is “non-layered”, found in the ratio of overlapping blocks η, we showed that the δ mechanism is very robust to high values of η (> 30%). At the same time, the average number of iterations is one half that of a flooding decoder, although the code is not cycle-free. Reordering the sequence of layers further relaxes the problem by minimising η: in this case, the same speed of exact layered (L = 1) is achieved. Conversely, SO-based approximation suffers from impairments in the BER/FER of about 0.1 dB, and must rely on layers reordering to support high-speed

decoding. The synthesis on 0.18 μm CMOS technology showed
that the complexity overhead of the ancillary circuitry to perform layered decoding is about 22-24% of the original check node processor, while the overall saving for not implementing the variable node processor is about 41-43%.
