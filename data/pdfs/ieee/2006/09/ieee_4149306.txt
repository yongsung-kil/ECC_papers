Construction of Large Girth (3,k) LDPC Codes Based on Cuboid

Tao Xiongfei, Liu Weizhong and Zou Xuecheng
Department of Electronical Science and Technology Huazhong University of Science and Technology Wuhan, China E-mail: tao-xiongfei@hotmail.com

Abstract—The girth and minimum distance are two important factors of LDPC codes. In this paper we construct a type of LDPC codes with girth 8 based on cuboid. The codes are regular, whose column weight is 3. The minimum distance of the code with exact cuboid structure is only 8 and the distribution of code word weight is not optimal, so we proposed a scheme to improve the codes. The simulation results comparing the bit error rate(BER) performance over AWGN channels with randomly constructed LDPC codes shows that our codes are better than the randomly constructed ones.
Keywords-LDPC codes; girth; minimum distance; cuboid
I. INTRODUCTION
LDPC codes were originally introduced by Gallager in his doctoral thesis[1]. LDPC codes are linear block codes, their bit error rate(BER) performance approaches Shannon limit when belief propagation(BP) algorithm is employed to decode. Since the rediscovery of LDPC codes by Mackay and Neal in 1995[2], there has been renewed interest in LDPC codes.
The structure of LDPC codes can affect the performance of iteratively decoding algorithm, Tanner and et al[3] analyzed the iteratively decoding algorithm based on graphics and point out that the short cycles in a Tanner graph prevent the iteratively algorithm from converging. So the girth, i.e. the shortest cycles, in a Tanner graph is an important factor which should be taken into account in the design of LDPC codes.
Song, Liu, and Kumar have constructed (n,2,k) LDPC codes with girth 6 and 8 based on Disjoint Difference Set(DDS)[4]. Zhang and Moura constructed (n,2,k) LDPC codes with lager girth, e.g. 12, 16 and 20, based on graphical models[5]. Although the encoders and decoders of (n,2,k) LDPC codes are simpler to implement, the minimum distance of the codes is small, which is half of the girth.
Kou, Lin, and Fossorier proposed quasi-cyclic LDPC codes with girth 6 base on finite geometries of lines and points of Euclidean and projective geometries over finite over finite fields[6]. In [7], (n,3,k) LDPC codes were constructed based on balanced incomplete block designs (BIBD) with girth 6.
In this paper, we introduce a scheme to design (n,3,k) LDPC codes with girth 8.
In section II, we describe the design of (n,3,k) LDPC codes with girth 8 based on cuboid. An improved construction

scheme is introduced in section III. Some simulation results are present in section IV. Section V is our conclusion of the paper.
II. LDPC CODES WITH GIRTH-8 The performance of LDPC codes depend on their structures, the check matrix of LDPC codes is sparse. For an (n,j,k) regular LDPC code, its check matrix contains j 1’s in each column and k 1’s in each row. Tanner considered LDPC codes and showed that they could be represented effectively by a so-called bipartite graph, now call a Tanner graph[3]. A Tanner graph is a graph whose nodes may be separated into two types and edges may only connect two nodes of different types. The two types of nodes in a Tanner graph are the variable nodes and the check nodes respectively. A check node i is connected to a variable nodes j if and only if the element of the ith row and jth column in the check matrix is 1. Fig. 1 is an example of a (6,2,3) LDPC codes and its corresponding Tanner graph. The Tanner graph for finite length LDPC codes exist cycles. A cycle of length l in a Tanner graph is a path comprising l edges which closes back on itself. The Tanner graph in Fig. 1 exist a length-4 cycle as exemplified by the four dashed edges in the figure. The girth of a Tanner graph is the minimum cycle length of the graph. The cycle in the Tanner graph can be denoted by points and lines, for example, a triangle containing three lines and three

The basic idea of our design of (3,k) LDPC codes is the construction of (2,k) LDPC codes based on the lines and points free of length-6 cycle. Assuming to construct an (2,k) LDPC code with n points and m lines, the following is the construction rules(2D-construction): 1) Each line is composed of k points. 2) Each point lies on 2 lines. 3) The m lines are separated into two sets, all the lines in the
same set are parallel with each other, and any two lines in different set are vertical. Fig. 3 gives a simple example of geometry complied with above construction rules, where n =9, m = 6, and k = 3. The check matrix H=[hi,j] corresponding to geometry complied with 2-D construction can be generated as follow:
1) each line and point correspond to each row and column respectively.
2) hi,j=1 if and only if the jth point is on the ith line in the geometry, otherwise hi,j =0. Having above construction method, now we extend such
geometry from 2-D to 3-D to obtain our (3,k) LDPC codes. Assume the number of viable nodes is k3, i.e., there are k3 points in the geometry, we divide these points into k subsets, namely S1,…,Sk, and each subset has k2 points. First, arrange the k2 points in each subset according to 2-D construction, second, align points in each subset in a vertical line, thirdly, draw a line through each aligned points set. Now, we get a 3-D geometry, Fig.4 shows an example of such geometry with k=3. The geometry looks like a large cuboid composing by an amount of small cuboids, so we call it cuboid structure.
The check matrix H=[hi,j] corresponding to the 3-D geometry can be easily generated. Now, we get a class of LDPC codes based on cuboid, we call the codes cuboid-based LDPC codes. Obviously, the girth of the Tanner graph

The geometry to the cuboid-based LDPC looks like being comprised by many small cuboids, we take out a cuboid from the geometry, see Fig. 5.

According to the generation method described previously, a point corresponds to a column vector, the vector weight is 3, the position of 1 in the vector corresponds to the 3 line crossing the point. The sub-matrix corresponding to cuboid in Fig. 5 is an m × 8 matrix. Each line in the cuboid connects with only two distinct points, so the weight of each row in the sub-matrix is 2 or 0, i.e. the modulo 2 sum of the 8 column vectors is a zero vector. So a code word of weight-8 exists.

From above analysis, we know that our cuboid-based LDPC codes contain an amount of cuboid structure, which result in the existence of quite a few code words with weight-8.

In section II, we construct a type of (3,k) LDPC codes with girth-8 and dmin=8. From Table I, we notice that the cuboidbased LDPC codes have quite a bit code words with weight<20. If we can decrease or eliminate the code words with small weight, the performance of the codes will be improved. In the following of the section, we will introduce a scheme to improve the weight distribution of the codes. Our scheme is similar to the construction proposed in[10], but the rate of our codes is more flexible.
Assume the number of viable nodes is tqk(t>k,q>k), i.e., there are tqk points in the geometry, we divide these points into t subsets, and each subset has qk points. We arrange these points complied with the rules described in section II, Fig. 6(a) shows an example of such arrangement. Fig. 6(b) is the bottom subset which belongs to the Z=0 plane.

We construct several lines in the bottom subset, the slope of the lines is s. Assume the start point of a line is p0=(x0, y0, 0), we draw a line on the bottom subset with the start point p0, the points on the line can be determined by (1).

So when a start point and line slope is given, a line is determined. V. CONCLUSION In this paper, we construct a type of LDPC codes with girth-8 based on cuboid, whereas, the cuboid-based codes have inferior performance due to the small dmin, so we propose a construction to improve the cuboid-base codes, the improved codes still have girth-8, and they have larger distance, simulation result shows that I-cuboid codes outperform the randomly constructed codes without error floors.
