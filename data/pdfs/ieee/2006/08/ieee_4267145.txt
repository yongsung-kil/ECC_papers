A High Speed Flexible Encoder for Low-Density
Parity-Check Codes
Sunitha Kopparthi and Don M. Gruenbacher
Department of Electrical & Computer Engineering Kansas State University, Manhattan, KS 66502-5204
Email: {sunithak, grue}@ksu.edu

Abstract- Encoder implementations of low-density paritycheck codes are typically optimized for area due to their high complexity. Such designs usually have relatively low data throughput. Two new encoder designs are presented here that achieve much higher data rates while requiring more area for the implementation. One of these design achieves encoding rates in excess of 400 Mbps. All of the designs presented can fit on FPGAs currently available. The methodology for both designs and performance results are presented.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1], [2] are being adapted for use in various applications such as telecommunications, magnetic storage, and digital video broadcasting. Even though LDPC codes are difficult to implement due to high encoder complexity, recent developments have led to more efficient encoder structures which are typically limited in their encoding rates. In this paper we propose an encoder implementation that allows encoding rates in excess of 400 Mbps. The encoder uses a direct implementation which sacrifices area for increased speed, but this is necessary as applications require more bandwidth.
LDPC codes have recently received much attention because of their efficient decoding algorithm, excellent error correcting capability and their performance close to Shannon limit (provided the information block lengths are long enough) [3]. LDPC codes are widely being considered as next-generation error correcting codes for many real applications. In [4], Europe's DVB standards group have selected LDPC codes because of their superior performance over turbo codes for next generation digital satellite broadcasting. In [5], it is formally shown that properly constructed irregular codes can approach the channel capacity more closely than regular codes.
One of the drawbacks of LDPC codes is high encoding complexity, and recent developments have made improvements in this area. Zhang et al. [6] proposed a systematic efficient encoding scheme by effectively exploiting the sparseness of its (3, k)-regular LDPC codes. In [7], Echard introduced as ensemble of quasi-regular low-density parity check codes called as 7 rotation LDPC codes. In [8], Kim presented high-performance parallel implementations of an encoder and decoder for the parallelly concatenated parity check class of LDPC codes. In [9], Miles implemented a radiation tolerant encoder in 0.25 ,um CMOS based on a novel method for deriving regular quasi-cyclic LDPC codes. In [10] and [11], it

is suggested that the encoding complexity can be significantly reduced by using an approximate upper triangular parity check matrix to construct LDPC codes. Using this idea, Zhang presented an efficient encoding scheme for generalized LDPC codes in [12]. Lee et al. in [13], described a hardware design of an efficient LDPC encoder based on methods proposed by Richardson and Urbanke [11].
The complexity of a design or algorithm is measured by the number of operations required per bit. A straightforward implementation of an LDPC encoder has complexity quadratic
O (n2) in the block length whereas Turbo codes can be encoded in linear order 0(n). In [11] it is shown that the linear
time encoding is achievable through careful linear manipulation of LDPC codes. In this paper, Richardson and Urbanke's methods are used to develop a hardware implementation of faster encoders for LDPC codes. First, the greedy algorithm is used to transform the parity check matrix to approximate lower triangular form. Hardware encoding is then performed on the approximated lower triangular parity check matrix. The parity and information bits are rearranged to obtain the final codeword with regard to actual parity check matrix H . This implementation allows a faster encoder to be built for both regular and irregular LDPC codes.
For hardware implemetation of LDPC encoder, most of the work in literature [6] - [9], and [12] consider a cyclic, quasicyclic or some regular pattern in the parity check matrix, H. Our method can be applied to any H matrix and is faster when compared with [13]. This paper is organized as follows. The background and the overview of the encoding process is described in section II. In section III, the preprocessing required for transforming the parity check matrix to approximate lower triangular form is explained in detail. The hardware implementation of the encoder is described in section IV. Results are summarized in section V.

IV. ENCODER IMPLEMENTATION
The hardware implementation of the encoder, as shown in Fig. 7 and Equation 4, is to multiply the information bits, s, with matrices P1 and P2 to obtained parity bits P1 and
P2 respectively. The multiplication and addition in binary
system can be performed with an AND gate and an XOR gate respectively. The length of information bits is n -m. Therefore, a serial input interface requires n -m clock cycles to read the information bits. These information bits are read using a faster clock, Clk5, and the encoding is done using a slower clock, Clke
Matrices P1 and P2 are computed using Matlab for both regular and irregular parity check matrices of different sizes. The number of one's in each matrix is shown in Table I. From Table I, it can be observed that the matrices P1 and P2 are not sparse. For example, the distribution of number of one's in the rows of a P2 matrix for an irregular parity check matrix of size 504 x 1008 is shown in Fig. 8. This indicates that approximately 220-290 ones are in every row vector of P2. While a sparse representation for the PI and P2 matrices can be utilized, it is more efficient to use a dense representation of the matrices due to the dense properties of the matrices themselves.
Once P1 and P2 have been defined, Verilog modules are generated using a Matlab script. The Verilog modules are then synthesized using Quartus 5.0 and implemented on a Stratix EPIS80F1508C5 FPGA. A computer is used to send the information bits and to read the codeword from the encoder. For verification, the hardware encoder output is then compared to the corresponding codeword generated by Matlab.

To maximize the parallelism, Matrix-Vector multiplication is performed by a Vector-Vector multiplication (Inner Product) in parallel. Vector-Vector multiplication can be performed in two ways. The first method, Multi Clocked Inner Product, requires m clocks to compute one inner product while the second method, Single Clocked Inner Product, computes the inner product in a single clock.
A. Multi Clocked Inner Product
In this method the inner product of length m vectors X and Y is performed one bit at a time as illustrated in Fig. 9. Bitwise mulitiplication is performed on each clock cycle with a single AND gate, and a running single-bit sum Z of the products is achieved using a single XOR gate. If vectors X and Y are stored in m-bit shift registers, then this method requires m clock periods to calculate their inner product. Multiple instantiations of this inner product module may be implemented in parallel on all the arrays of matrices P1 and P2 to obtain the parity bits P1 and P2. Therefore, the throughput is determined by the minimum period of the shift register clock, Clke
B. Single Clocked Inner Product
This method is similar to the Multi Clocked Inner Product except that the inner product is done in a single clock cycle. The illustration of this method is shown in Fig. 10. In a single clock, as shown in Fig. 10, all pairs of bits from vectors X and Y are ANDed and the output of each AND gate is XORed

to obtain the inner product value Z. This procedure may also be performed in parallel on all arrays of matrices P1 and P2. The throughput of this method is determined by the maximum propagation delay from any bit in X or Y to the output Z. This delay will set the minimum period of the encoder clock Clke.
After obtaining the parity bits P1 and P2 using either of the above methods, the information and parity bits are rearranged to obtain the final codeword with respect to the actual parity check matrix H. The number of clock cycles required for a codeword using the Multi Clocked Inner Product method is equal to m + 1 where the m clock cycles are required to compute the inner product and the additional clock is required to rearrange the codeword bits. When Single Clocked Inner Product is used the number of clock cycles required for codeword is two where inner product is obtained in one clock cycle and the codeword bits are rearranged in the second clock cycle.

The encoder implementation using both methods MCIP and SCIP assumes that all input data bits are available for encoding, so serialization factors are not included in the results shown in Tables II-III. The main observation one sees is the huge advantage the SCIP encoder has in throughput and latency over the MCIP encoder. This is somewhat expected for the defined architecture of each system. What may be surprising is the difference in area for each design. One would normally expect the MCIP to require fewer Logic Elements (LEs), but the converse is actually true. This is due to the implementation of huge multiplexer's required by MCIP in the FPGA. This may be due to the statically defined P1 and P2 matrices. Finally, it is observed that increasing the block length also decreases the clock speed, but an irregular LDPC code takes considerably more area than a regular LDPC code. Again this can be attributed to a difference in potential optimization for the two different codes as they are defined in P1 and P2.
If one wants to consider both encoders under serial input stream then an input shift-register needs to be added to both the MCIP and SCIP encoder. VI. CONCLUSIONS
This paper provides the details for a general LDPC encoder that can accomodate very high data rates for smaller block
sizes. While this design is relatively simple and straightforward compared to more complex designs, it was shown here that much higher encoder rates can be achieved in large FPGAs. As the density and size of FPGAs continue to increase and the demand from high-speed applications also increase, encoders similar to this will become more commonplace.
