Performance Evaluation of LDPC Codes in Bliss Scheme-Based Storage Systems Using Density Evolution

Haibin Zhang, Member, IEEE, Andries P. Hekstra, Member, IEEE, and Bin Yin

Abstract — Density evolution algorithms for scaled minsum decoding of both regular and irregular low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes in Bliss scheme-based storage systems are presented, to evaluate the performance of LDPC codes in terms of noise threshold. Firstly, with the assumption of fixed error propagation factor and independently and identically distributed (i.i.d.) messages, density evolution algorithms of regular LDPC codes are derived to compare performance among LDPC codes with the same code rate but different bit/check node degrees and determine the optimum scaling factor of scaled min-sum decoding. Secondly, with the looser assumption of fixed error propagation and independently distributed messages, density evolution algorithms of irregular LDPC codes are derived for performance evaluation of LDPC codes with arbitrary degree distributions. Comparisons with simulations show density evolution is a proper performance evaluation method for LDPC codes in Bliss scheme-based storage systems1.
Index Terms — low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, density evolution, error propagation, Bliss scheme, storage.
I. INTRODUCTION
In optical/magnetic storage systems, channel coding is normally composed of two parts: error-correction coding (ECC) and constrained coding (e.g., runlength-limited (RLL) coding). There are mainly two schemes of concatenating ECC with constrained coding: the standard concatenation scheme and the modified concatenation scheme [1]. In the standard concatenation scheme, where the ECC encoder is strictly put before the constrained code, error propagation introduced by look-ahead decoding of the constrained code significantly degrades performance. The modified concatenation scheme reverses the order of the ECC encoder and the constrained code, and thus prevents most error propagation. The modified concatenation scheme is also known as Bliss scheme, since it was first introduced by Bliss [2]. Later, the Bliss scheme was re-studied by Mansuripur [3], and considered by Fan et al. [4] as a method of preventing error propagation in magnetic recording systems. Recently, Coene et al. also applied the Bliss scheme to their two-dimensional optical storage (TwoDOS) [5] and near-field optical storage [6] systems.

Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are linear block ECC codes with very sparse parity check matrices [7]. LDPC codes have received more and more attention in optical/ magnetic storage recently, due to the continuous reduction of channel bit length and hence signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) [6], [8]-[11]. Song et al. [8] constructed (d, k) RLL constrained LDPC codes and reported their performance on an optical storage channel. Coene et al. [6] applied LDPC codes to their Bliss scheme-based near-field optical storage system, using a d=1 RLL code with a maximum transition-run (MTR) constraint equal to 2.
Density evolution techniques have been used to determine the analytic capacity of LDPC codes and find the optimum degree distributions. Density evolution was first introduced by Gallager [7] for binary symmetric channels (BSCs), and was further extended to a large range of binary-input channels and various decoding algorithms [10]-[16]. Kavcic et al. [10] and Tan et al. [11] applied density evolution to partial-response (PR) channels, which are usually used for channel modeling in storage systems. However, as will be discussed in Section II, in the LDPC-based Bliss scheme the parity part of an LDPC codeword will have a higher bit error rate (BER) than its systematic part at the input of the LDPC decoder. So the above-mentioned density evolution solutions cannot be directly applied to the LDPC-based Bliss scheme.
Among various decoding algorithms of LDPC codes, the scaled min-sum algorithm is very suitable for practical implementation due to its simplicity at the cost of acceptable performance loss. Heo [15] and Chen et al. [17] showed that with a proper scaling factor the performance of the scaled min-sum algorithm approaches that of the sum-product decoding algorithm on binary input additive white Gaussian noise (BIAWGN) channels.
In this paper, we present density evolution algorithms for scaled min-sum decoding of both regular and irregular LDPC codes in Bliss scheme-based storage systems, to evaluate the performance of LDPC codes in terms of noise threshold. With the assumption of fixed error propagation factor and independently and identically distributed (i.i.d.) messages, density evolution algorithms of regular LDPC codes are first derived to compare performance among LDPC codes with the same code rate but different bit/check node degrees and determine the optimum scaling factor of min-sum decoding. Secondly, with the looser assumption of fixed error propagation factor and independently distributed messages, density evolution algorithms of irregular LDPC codes are derived for performance evaluation of LDPC codes with arbitrary degree distributions. II. LDPC-BASED BLISS SCHEME
The LDPC-based Bliss scheme is depicted in Fig. 1 [6], where an RLL code is adopted as the constrained code. At the transmit side, the unipolar channel bitstream that results from the RLL encoder followed by the 1T-precoder is the input for the LDPC encoder. At the receive side, for the systematic part of the channel bitstream, a soft-in soft-out (SISO) channel detector is applied, whereas for the parity part of the channel bitstream, a concatenation of a SISO channel detector and a sliding window SISO RLL decoder is used. After passing the LDPC decoder and the pre-decoder, the channel bitstream is input to a hard-in hard-out (HIHO) RLL decoder. To protect against burst errors, a symbol-wise Reed-Solomon (RS) code can be used as an outer ECC code, which is put before RLL encoder at the transmit side. For simplicity, the RS encoder and decoder are not included in Fig.1.

IRA codes [19] are essentially a special kind of irregular LDPC codes, of which the parity part of the parity check matrix has a structure as described by Fig. 3. Recently, IRA codes have received much attention, since their encoding complexity is only linearly proportional to code length. Moreover, since the parity part of a codeword suffers more noise than its systematic part in the Bliss scheme, so naturally it is desirable to lower the column weight of the parity part to let the parity bits join less check equations. Thus IRA codes and their degree optimization are of great interest for our purpose.

In general, the comparisons with simulation results exemplify the importance of density evolution in the LDPC code evaluation in Bliss scheme-based storage systems.
VI. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we have developed density evolution algorithms for both regular and irregular LDPC codes in Bliss scheme-based storage systems. Comparisons between density evolution results and simulation results have been used to prove that density evolution is a proper performance evaluation method for LDPC codes in Bliss scheme-based storage systems. This provides us a simple and quick way to evaluate and select LDPC codes for future Bliss schemebased storage applications.
