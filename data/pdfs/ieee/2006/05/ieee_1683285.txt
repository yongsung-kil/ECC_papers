On the Performance of LDPC Codes with Differential Detection over Rayleigh Fading
Channels
Hiroshi Tatsunami, Koji Ishibashi, and Hideki Ochiai Division of Physics, Electrical and Computer Engineering
Yokohama National University
79-5 Tokiwadai, Hodogaya-ku, Yokohama, 240-8501, Japan Email: hiroshi@ochiailab.dnj.ynu.acjp

Abstract- In this paper, the performance of low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes with differential detection is studied over additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) and Rayleigh fading channels with particular emphasis on the sensitivity of signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) parameters required for metric calculation. Two simple metrics are derived and compared. Computer simulation results demonstrate that an SNR mismatch has a considerable effect on the performance of LDPC codes, and optimal SNR mismatches are explored in terms of bit error rate performances. It is shown that in some cases a pessimistic SNR estimation can improve the overall performance.
I. INTRODUCTION
Turbo codes [1] and low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [2] have gained significant attention due to its near-capacity error performance with a sufficiently long codeword length. In [1, 3], LDPC codes are shown to outperform turbo codes in many cases of interest, but discussions are mostly limited to their bit error performances over an additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel with ideal coherent detection. The performance of coherent detection relies on an accurate phase tracking and good estimation of channel state information (CSI). Many mobile communication channels are, however, characterized by time varying fading processes, which makes accurate tracking of phase and CSI difficult. In such a situation, differential detection may be an attractive alternative as it can operate without precise channel tracking.
In [4], the performance of turbo codes with differential detection over an AWGN channel is studied. Based on the assumption that the output from the differential detector follows a Gaussian distribution, a simple metric is derived. This metric is expressed by a form akin to the conventional metric for coherent binary phase shift keying (BPSK) and thus enjoys simple calculation. In [5], the authors consider the fact that a Gaussian probability density function (pdf) is not accurate for a differential phase shift keying (DPSK) waveform and propose a metric transformation that can adjust the actual output statistics of differential detector to Gaussian metrics over both AWGN and Rayleigh fading channels. However, in either case, Gaussian approximation is used for the metric calculation.

If the decoding metric is matched to an accurate pdf of the differential detector output, the overall performance of the channel decoder is expected to improve. In [6], the exact conditional joint pdf has been derived in the framework of differential amplitude and phase shift keying (DAPSK) over block Rayleigh fading channels. This pdf can be applied to DPSK modulation in a straightforward manner.
Since both turbo and LDPC decoders typically make use of maximum a posteriori (MAP) probability, noise variance of the channel must be known at the receiver for metric calculation. Most of work in the literature assumes that the noise variance is known perfectly at the receiver. In practice, the noise variance must be estimated from the received sequences, and thus the effect of estimation error should be considered. This is particularly important for fast fading channels where the instantaneous SNR changes rapidly. In [7], the sensitivity of the turbo decoder against estimation errors of the noise variance is demonstrated, and an SNR estimation method from the received signals is developed for an AWGN channel.
In this paper, we investigate the performance of LDPC codes over block Rayleigh fading channels with differential detection. We also propose a metric based on the exact pdf derived in [6], and investigate the performance with SNR mismatch and determine an optimal operation region for a given SNR. Since our main focus is on the decoding metric rather than code structure, throughout the paper we adopt wellstudied regular LDPC codes rather than irregular ones for simplicity.
The outline of this paper is as follows. The overall system model is given in Section II. Section III derives several simple metrics for DPSK, and simulation results with SNR mismatch are detailed in Section IV. Section V concludes the paper.

IV. NUMERICAL RESULTS
In the following computer simulations, a half-rate (3,6) regular LDPC code with N = 1008, M = 504 is employed [2]. Also, the sum-product algorithm is used for decoding LDPC codes with up to 20 iterations. For convergence of bit error rate (BER) obtained by Monte Carlo method, at least 1000 bit errors were counted in each simulation.

A. Performance of LDPC with different CSI measures
First, it is assumed that the noise variance is known at the receiver side. Fig. 3 shows the BER of each metric versus
SNR per information bit, EbINo, over AWGN and block
Rayleigh fading channels, where Eb is the average received energy per information bit. As observed, there is a slight difference between the BER of these metrics in the case of AWGN channel. However, the performance of the exact metric with average estimation is superior to the simplified metric. Therefore, incorporating the amplitude ratio of two successive
received symbols -yk, BER performance can be improved.
Also, for all the metrics considered, average estimation of CSI shows better performance than the other estimation. Thus, average estimation is employed in the following. It is
interesting to note that the Gaussian metric is always superior
to the exact metric in block Rayleigh fading channel even if perfect CSI is available at the receiver. However, as will be shown later, this performance gap can be compensated by introducing intentional SNR mismatch.

B. Effect of SNR mismatch
Next, we focus on the knowledge of noise variance incorporated in the metric and consider the effect of SNR mismatch. Fig. 4 and 5 show the effect of SNR mismatch on the performance of LDPC codes over AWGN and block Rayleigh fading channels using the Gaussian metric and exact metric with average estimation respectively. Note that the value of "SNR" in the figure indicates an actual SNR of the channel and horizontal axis indicates the gap from the actual value. A positive value of "SNR mismatch [dB]" means that the estimated SNR is higher than the actual channel SNR (and thus the noise variance is underestimated).
It is interesting to note that in the case of AWGN, the SNR mismatch giving optimal performance is not equal to zero when the exact metric is used. In the block Rayleigh fading, this phenomenon is observed more remarkably (see Fig. 5).

Also observed from the figure is the fact that the Gaussian metric is more sensitive to SNR mismatch than the exact metric, especially when the channel suffers severe fading.
To further examine these observations, Fig 6 shows BER performance with intentional SNR mismatch. By introducing intentional SNR mismatch, the exact metric tends to outperform that of Gaussian metric.
From these figures, it can be surmised that 3 dB underestimation of SNR yields best BER performance in the case of exact metric. This interesting offset phenomenon is also observed in the case of the Gaussian metric when the channel suffers block Rayleigh fading. One cause of this SNR mismatch phenomenon may be due to the mismatch between the LLR calculated by the decoder using average estimation of SNR and the true LLR. V. CONCLUSION
We have investigated the performance of LDPC codes with differential detection. The two metrics, exact and Gaussian, are considered and their effects of SNR mismatch over AWGN and block Rayleigh fading channels are evaluated by computer simulations. Even though Gaussian metric shows slightly better performance than the exact metric with perfect knowledge of channel SNR, the latter metric has more robustness against SNR mismatch than the former over block fading channels.
We have focused on ideal block Rayleigh fading channels throughout the paper, and further investigation may be necessary for more realistic channels where the ideal interleaving is not feasible.
