Algebraic Construction of Sparse Matrices With Large Girth
Michael E. O’Sullivan, Member, IEEE

Abstract—In this correspondence, we present a method for constructing sparse matrices that have a compact description and whose associated bipartite graphs have large girth. Based on an arbitrary seed matrix of nonnegative integers a new matrix is constructed which replaces each entry of the seed matrix with a sum of permutation matrices. Algebraic conditions that lead to short cycles in the associated bipartite graph are analyzed and methods to achieve large girth in two special cases are presented. In one, all the permutation matrices are circulants; in the other they are all affine permutation matrices. When used to define a low-density parity-check (LDPC) code the compact description should lead to efficient implementation and the large girth to good error correction performance. The method is adaptable to a variety of rates, and a variety of row and column degrees.
Index Terms—Bipartite graph, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes.

I. INTRODUCTION
The remarkable performance of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes under belief propagation decoding has been well documented. However, it remains unclear how best to construct a good parity-check matrix for specified rate and length. It was recognized at an early stage that cycles of length four in the bipartite graph associated to the paritycheck matrix had a very negative effect on decoding performance, e.g., [1]. Other properties are also associated to diminished performance, among them small stopping sets [2], low-weight codewords, near-codewords [3], [4], and pseudocodewords [5]–[7]. In this correspondence, we focus on construction of matrices whose bipartite graphs have large girth, but our method can be combined with other techniques—such as varying row and column degrees—that also improve performance. Recent experimentation on (3; 6)-regular codes [8] indicates that for fixed length and decoding with the sum-product algorithm, matrices with larger girth yield much lower error floors.

This gives an (L; R)-regular matrix. The method is explored in detail, extended to nonprime N and applied to convolutional codes in [10].
Fan [11], derived a necessary condition to achieve girth 2M when
the Arl are permutation matrices. Motivated by Reed–Solomon codes,
he studied the particular case where Arl is 0 0 I(r 1)(l 1). Mittelholzer
[12], and later Yang and Helleseth [13] derived upper bounds on the minimum distance of these codes. The upper bounds are rather low. Methods to achieve girth 8 and 10 as well as the connection with partition-regular equations appear in [14].
Fossorier [15] took each of the Arl to be a circulant permutation matrix, say Arl = Ia . He derived conditions for the bipartite graph of
the matrix to have girth at least 2M (a special case of Fan’s result when the matrices are circulant) and showed that the girth can be no larger than 12. For fixed R and L and girths 6 and 8, he found, via computer search, examples with the required girth and tabulated the minimal N achieved. He also did a computer search and tabulated the minimal N
for matrices of the form Arl = Ia b , without the requirement of
[9] that a and b be Rth and Lth roots of unity.
A more general approach was pursued by Bond, Hui and Schmidt [16] who use sums of affine permutation matrices (linear congruence matrices in their terminology). They derive a necessary condition on the affine permutations to achieve a desired girth (similar to Fan’s result). The method was extended in [17]–[19], where an algorithm was presented that achieves girths 6, 8, and 10.
There are a number of other methods for constructing parity-check matrices, and there are often links to the method investigated here. For example, certain parity-check matrices constructed from finite geometries may also be considered as matrices of circulants [20]–[22]. Vasic and Milenkovic [23] explore block designs and other combinatorial methods as well as techniques using circulants.
As mentioned earlier, the minimum distance of a code also affects
performance. It was shown in [24] that when the Arl are all permu-
tation matrices and they commute with each other, then the minimum distance of the code is at most (R + 1)!. This holds in particular for circulant permutation matrices. Smarandache and Vontobel [25] showed how to raise this bound, while maintaining (L; R)-regularity, by al-
lowing some of the Arl to be 0 and others to be the sum of two circu-
lant permutation matrices. Several codes were constructed that met the minimum distance bound and experimental results showed improved decoding performance.
The improvement on minimum distance along with evidence for the benefit of using irregular graphs [26] suggests a two step process in

VII. CONCLUSION

We have presented a formal description of a covering graph derived
2 from a seed graph and permutations e associated to each edge e E

of the seed graph. A cycle in the covering graph corresponds to a cycle

in the seed graph, along with a fixed point of the composite of permu-

tations associated to the cycle in the seed graph. We have shown that

balanced cycles in the seed graph always yield cycles in the covering

graph when the associated permutations commute. Conditions on the

seed matrix ensuring the nonexistence of balanced cycles of length 2m
  for 3 m 6 have been given. The method can be extended to larger

m although the number of cases to check becomes unwieldy.

When the permutations e are translations, by ce modulo N , we

have shown how to regard the as short vectors in a sublattice

coofndEiti.oIntswfoour ledlibmeinwaotirnthg

small cycles exploring an

adaptation of the method, which is straightforward, to provide unequal

protection of vertices. That is, it may be valuable to require certain sub-

graphs of the covering graph—for example the subgraph containing the

vertices of low degree—to have larger girth than the whole covering

graph. Another interesting question is whether symmetry in the lattice

could be used to derive the ce directly rather than by a recursive algo-

rithm as we have done.

We have also employed a method for breaking balanced cycles in the

seed graph using affine permutations. The particularly simple seed ma-

trices that we used, [3; 3], [3; 3; 3], etc., yielded longer codes for speci-
2 fied girth than codes constructed from circulants using a 3 3L matrix

of the same proportions. To achieve a specified girth, the coefficients

ae, be defining the affine permutations must not satisfy certain polyno-

mial congruences. It would be valuable to analyze these polynomials

to see if the ae, be might be derived directly, rather than recursively.
