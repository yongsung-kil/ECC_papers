Error Floor and Decoding with Message Scaling for Accumulator-Based and LDPC Codes
Motohiko Isaka1 Dept. of Informatics, Kwansei Gakuin University Sanda, 6691337 Japan. Email: isaka@ieee.org

Abstract — In this paper, we study the error floor of accumulator-based codes under iterative decoding and attempt to improve the performance. It is shown that the associated error events are often due to detectable errors, and proper design of an interleaver is effective for such weakness of the code. Subsequently, a simple modification of iterative decoding by scaling messages is shown to effectively achieve lower error probabilities in the error floor region, with an extension to LDPC codes.

I. Introduction
Since the introduction of the repeat-accumulate (RA) code [1], use of a rate-1 inner recursive encoder, known as an accumulator, is quite common in the iterative decoding literature. In [2], it was observed that the concatenation of extended Hamming codes and an accumulator provides near Shannon limit performance for high-rate coding [2]. However, a possible drawback is that an error floor could be found at relatively high error probabilities, and a similar fact is reported in [3] for non-systemtaic (irregular) RA codes.
In this paper, we study and improve the performance of the error floor region encountered in turbo-style iterative decoding for nonsystematic accumulator-based codes as [1, 2]. It is first pointed out that, unless the minimum distance is so small, error floor of these codes is often due to detectable errors including trapping sets [5] of small size as well as decoding failure influenced by cycles in the Tanner graph. It is shown that an appropriate interleaver design is effective for such weakness. Interleavers similar to the ones with a two-dimensional structure studied in [6][7] are particularly focused on, and some design for quasi-cyclic repeat-accumulate (QCRA) codes is also indicated.
Subsequently, we use a simple decoding by scaling the message in the iterative process that can further lower the error floor. The same approach can be extended to the belief propagation (BP) decoding for low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes, and we show the error floor performance of the Margulis code of length 2640 [8] and irregular LDPC codes [9] can be improved. II. Serial Concatenation of a Short Linear Block Code and Recursive Encoder
We illustrate a (N = ln, K = lk, Dmin) serially concatenated code in Fig. 2 in which l codewords of a certain relatively short (n, k, dmin) linear block code are interleaved and encoded by a rate-1 inner recursive encoder. In this paper, we restrict our attention to repetition [1] and extended Hamming codes [2].
Denote the input to the interleaver as c = (c0, c1, . . . , cN−1), where ci = (cin, cin+1, . . . , cin+(n−1)) is the i-th codeword of the outer constituent code for 0 ≤ i < l. An interleaver is defined by the permutation π : ZN → ZN which yields a sequence cπ = (cπ(0), cπ(1), . . . , cπ(N−1)) to be fed to the inner encoder. The overall codeword for transmission b = (b0, b1, . . . , bN−1) is given by b0 = cπ(0) and bi = bi−1 ⊕ cπ(i) for i > 0.

Trapping Set and Cycles
A large minimum distance Dmin is desired as it dominates the error rates under maximum likelihood (ML) decoding (at least for high SNR). Assume for simplicity that an all-zero codeword is transmitted throughout this section. Consider an input to the interleaver c of weight w, with non-zero symbols at positions s0, . . . , sw−1 such that π−1(si) < π−1(sj) for 0 ≤ i < j < N . Due to the nature of the inner encoder, for even w, the weight of the overall codeword b is simply determined as D = 0≤2i,2i+1<w Di where Di = π−1(s2i+1) − π−1(s2i). A low-weight codeword b is usually caused by an outer codeword c of a small weight.
On the other hand, unless the minimum distance of the code is small (which is presumably the case in the numerical result of Chap. 49 in [13] for RA codes with random interleavers and most error events are undetectable) the error floor of non-systematic accumulator-based codes under turbo-style iterative decoding is in many cases due to detectable errors, and the dominant error events are trapping sets or ones in which the number of erroneous symbols at each iteration oscillates. The trapping sets typically have a small weight with respect to c or cπ in the hard-decision of the outer decoder output, but in many cases the sequence by encoding this estimate by the accumulator has a large weight.
For illustration, Tanner graphs of a rate-1/3 RA code are depicted in Fig. 1. Squares are associated with three information bits as well as the symbols in cπ, whereas circles correspond to the symbols in the final codeword b. Black circles and squares represent non-zero symbols. The upper graph shows an overall codeword of large weight which would contribute little to error rates under ML decoding, whereas the lower graph illustrates how the same set of non-zero symbols can result in a trapping set. Observe in the latter that excluding the seventh black

square from the left associated with cπ, there are only six circles colored black. Consequently, the decoder may get trapped by such a set of incorrectly estimated symbols. We also point out that the hard-decision estimate for the outer code c based on the a posteriori probability in the decoding process often defines L valid constituent codewords.
The error events contributing to the error floor are not necessarily trapping sets of this kind, and those with an oscillation in the number of incorrect estimates can be more dominant. In fact, during the oscillation, the tentative decision for information bits obtained at each iteration is often of the form in the second graph of Fig. 1. In both cases, the cycles in the graph involving only a small number of symbols in the accumulator subgraph is problematic and should be eliminated in the interleaver design toward achieving low error floor. In the example of Fig. 1, there is a cycle involving seven symbols of cπ associated with two information bits and four symbols of the overall codeword b as indicated with bold edges.
III. Interleaver Design
Interleavers for these codes can be constructed by a computer search by examining proper conditions. However, this approach is possibly time-consuming and tends to leave weakness somewhere in the graph.
For the concatenation of a block code and an accumulator, we find that the two-dimensional interlaver design similar to the one recently proposed in [7] as well as in [6] is useful in avoiding the cycles due to the regularity in the indices of the outer constituent codewords.

Hamming outer code

We first consider extended Hamming outer codes for high-rate coding. With moderate code lengths in mind, it is suggested setting m = 1 and accordingly M = n, L = l. In this case, we regard the symbols in each ci as a single node in dealing with the condition of Eq. (1). The most important case for consideration is the cycles involving two symbols in one of the outer constituent codeword and two symbols from another.
Consider the use of (32, 26, 4) extended Hamming code with l = 97, resulting in a rate-0.8125 (3104, 2522) code. The constraint imposed on the selection of λi’s is that there is no pair of j and j such that µ1,j ± µ1,j ≡ 0 (mod L), and the number of pairs satisfying µ1,j ±µ2,j ≡ 0 (mod L) is small. Note further that the constraint posed in this way is likely to enhance the minimum distance. In Fig. 3, results of word error rate (WER) over the AWGN channel with maximum of 50 iterations are shown for several interleavers: two pseudo-random interleavers, and two-dimensional interlavers with random selection and with proper constraints of λi’s. One of the pseudorandom interleaver has Dmin = 3, which randomly generated interleavers frequently have. It is observed that by an appropriate design of an interleaver with the simple constraints described above improves the performance substantially.
It should be noted that except for that of the code with Dmin = 3, most of the error events in the error floor region are detectable errors and not due to low-weight valid codewords as the final estimate.

IV. Decoding with Message Scaling for Lower
Error Floor
While appropriate interleaver design improves the error floor performance significantly, the erroneous estimates do not yet correspond to valid codewords in most (or all) frames. This fact implies there is some possibility for further performance improvement by applying other (including ML) decoding approaches.
As in the example of Fig. 1, the extrinsic information with incorrect sign and large magnitude in the logarithm domain can give rise to trapping sets or decoding errors, as in short cycles the accumulator recognizes only a limited number of incorrect symbols and accordingly small discrepancies in terms of the received vector. Motivated

by this observation, the following decoding approach with message scaling is suggested with an aim of enhancing the performance in the error floor (or lower part of the waterfall) region: i-th stage decoding is defined as in the standard iterative decoding, except that the extrinsic information (L-value) delivered from the outer decoder is scaled by a multiplicative factor αi. If a certain stopping criterion is satisfied in the decoding process, the estimate obtained is determined as the final estimate and quit the decoding (we assumed a good stopping criterion which effectively detects a convergence: otherwise select a valid codeword or the one with the largest likelihood among the estimates obtained).
Note that scaling the message is a widely known approach to compensate for the performance loss due to the use of min-sum algorithm [10] or max-log-MAP [11] decoding in the component decoders, and for sum-product algorithm of LDPC codes to improve the waterfall region performance in [12].
A one-shot iterative decoding with αi < 1 often outperforms the normal iterative decoding (αi = 1) on its own, but the staged decoding can be advantageous in the following sense: (i) the conventional decoding in general requires smaller number of iterations before convergence and efficient with respect to the average decoding complexity, (ii) staged approach makes the decoding robust as a small value of αi can degrade the performance at low Eb/N0 values, (iii) employing several scaling factors or methods can result in better performance.
In the following, we employ a two-stage decoding with α1 = 1 in the first and αi < 1 in the following stages, and thus WER is not degraded compared with normal iterative decoding. Note that since the following stages are conducted only when the previous stage is recognized to fail, the increase in the average decoding complexity is marginal at low error rate regions.
The numerical result with and without two-stage decoding is shown in Figs. 3 and 4 for the code plotted with white squares. The scaling factor is selected to be α2 = 0.7 for all the extrinsic values in every iteration, and the maximum number of iterations is set to 25 for both stages for Fig. 3 (thus the peak complexity is the same as other plots), while it is 50 for RA code (doubling the number of iterations to 100 in the conventional approach brings a very limited improvement). It is seen that both cases result in an additional coding gain of 0.2 dB at the error rates depicted.
Consequently, together with the interleaver design, the error performance of accumulator-based codes under iterative decoding seems to be more promising than what is expected in the literature [13].

The rate-1/2 (2640, 1320) Margulis code [8] is a regular LDPC code with column and row weight three and six, respectively. It was observed in [4] that under BP decoding an error floor appears at WER of 10−6 and Eb/N0 = 2.4dB due to trapping sets of size 12 or 14. The numerical result is shown in Fig. 5, with maximum number of iterations 200 and α2 = 0.7. It is observed that at Eb/N0 = 2.4dB all the (more than 50) trapping sets of size 12 or 14 encountered in the simulation have been corrected by the second stage, and the flattening of the curve is not observed. Extrapolating the curve in Fig. 2 of [4], it seems that the additional gain becomes even more significant as the WER gets lower. Note that for this particular code, another decoding algorithm which averages the messages in the past few iterations has been proposed in [14] and successfully lowers the error floor.
In Fig. 6, the WER’s for a rate-0.8 (2000, 1600) systematic irregular LDPC code [9]. The parity-check matrix for this code has the form [H1 H2], where H1 is a matrix of column weight 4 and row weight 16, and H2 is the matrix of column weight 2 (except for the last column) due to the accumulator. The girth of the code is six, and the non-zero elements in each column of H1 are separated at least by six positions to avoid short cycles involving degree-2 nodes. Scaling is applied only to the messages from degree-4 variable nodes, although additional scaling to degree-2 nodes achieves a slightly better performance. The maximum number of iterations for iterative decoding is 25 and 50, while that for two-stage decoding is 25 for both stages with α2 = 0.7. We see from the figure that error floor is lowered. It is also observed that applying both of the scaling approaches in [10] and [12] can further improve the performance.
