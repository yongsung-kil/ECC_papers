Construction of Quasi-Cyclic LDPC Codes Based on the Primitive Elements of Finite Fields
Shumei Song, Lan Lan, Shu Lin and Khaled Abdel-Ghaffar Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering University of California, Davis, Davis, CA 95616, U.S.A.
Email: {ssmsong, squash, shulin, ghaffar}@ece.ucdavis.edu

Abstract— This paper presents an algebraic method for constructing quasic-cyclic LDPC codes based on the primitive elements of finite fields. The construction gives a class of efficiently encodable quasi-cyclic LDPC codes. Experimental results show that the constructed codes decoded with iterative decoding using the sum-product algorithm perform well over the AWGN channel.
I. Introduction
In the late 1950’s and early 1960’s, finite fields were successfully used to develop algebraic coding theory and to construct linear block codes, especially cyclic codes, with large minimum distances for hard-decision algebraic decoding, such as Bose-Chaudhuri-Hocqenghem (BCH) [1] [2] and ReedSolomon (RS) codes [3]. Recently, it has been shown that finite fields can also be successfully used for constructing quasicyclic (QC)-LDPC codes [4] [5].
QC-LDPC codes have encoding advantage over the other types of LDPC codes. Encoding of an LDPC code can be implemented using simple shift-registers with complexity linearly proportional to the number of parity bits for serial encoding and to the code length for parallel encoding [6]. Furthermore, well designed QC-LDPC code can perform as well as random LDPC codes [7] [8] in terms of bit-error probability, blockerror probability, and error-floor, collectively [4] [5].
In this paper, we present a new algebraic method for constructing QC-LDPC codes based on the primitive elements of finite fields. The construction method gives a class of binary QC-LDPC codes. Experimental results show that the constructed codes decoded with iterative decoding using the sum-product-algorithm (SPA) perform well over the AWGN channel.
A binary regular LDPC code [9] is defined as the null space of a sparse parity-check matrix H over GF (2) that has the following structural properties: (1) every row has weight ρ; (2) every column has weight γ; and (3) two rows (or two columns) have at most one 1-component in common. Such a sparse parity-check matrix is said to be (γ, ρ)-regular and the code generated by it is called a (γ, ρ)-regular code. The constraint on the rows and columns of H given by property (3) is called the row-column (RC)-constraint. The RC-constraint on H ensures that: (1) the minimum distance of the code given by the null space of H is at least γ + 1 [10] [11]; and (2) the girth of the code’s Tanner graph [12] is at least 6 [10] [11]. A

binary QC-LDPC code is given by the null space of an array of sparse circulants over GF (2) of the same size [4]- [6]. An LDPC code is said to be irregular if its parity-check matrix H has varying column and/or row weights [8].

II. Special Vector Representations of Finite Field
Elements
Consider the Galois field GF (q) where q is a power of a prime. Let α be a primitive element of GF (q). Then the powers of α, α−∞ 0, α0 = 1, α, . . . , αq−2, give all the elements of GF (q) and αq−1 = 1. The q − 1 nonzero elements of GF (q) form the multiplicative group of GF (q)
under the multiplication operation. For each nonzero element αi in GF (q) with 0 ≤ i < q − 1, we form a (q − 1)-tuple over GF (2), z(αi) = (z0, z1, . . . , zq−2) whose components correspond to the q −1 nonzero elements of GF (q), where the ith component zi = 1 and all the other components are equal to zero. The weight of z(αi) is equal to one. This (q −1)-tuple over GF (2) is called the location-vector of the field element αi [4] [5]. The location-vector of the 0-element of GF (q) is defined as the all-zero (q − 1)-tuple, z(0) = (0, 0, . . . , 0).
It is clear that the location-vectors of two different nonzero elements αi and αj are different and their 1-components are at two different locations, i and j.
Let δ be a nonzero element of GF (q). Then the locationvector z(αδ) of the field element αδ is the cyclic-shift (one place to the right) of the location-vector z(δ) of δ. Form a (q − 1) × (q − 1) matrix A over GF (2) with the locationvectors of δ, αδ, · · · , αq−2δ as its rows. Each row (or each column) of A contains one and only one 1-component. A is a circulant permutation matrix, i.e., A is a permutation matrix
but each row is the cyclic-shift of the row above it and the
first row is the cyclic-shift of the last row.

In the following, we give two examples to illustrate the above code construction. We assume BPSK transmission over the AWGN channel. Codes are decoded with iterative decoding using the SPA with the maximum number of iterations set to 50.

Lemma 1: For 0 ≤ j, k ≤ K, j = k, and 0 ≤ l, m < q − 1, two rows αlgj and αmgk from two different matrices Gj and Gk can only have at most one position where they both have the same symbol from GF (q), i.e., they differ in at least K positions.
Proof: Suppose there are two positions, s and t, where αlgj and αmgk have identical symbols. Then αl(αis−ij −1) = αm(αis−ik − 1) and αl(αit−ij − 1) = αm(αit−ik − 1). These two equalities imply that j = k or s = t which contradicts the assumptions that j = k and s = t. This proves Lemma 1.

Example 1: Consider the field GF (26). This field consists of K = 36 primitive elements. Based on these primitive elements, we can construct a 37 × 37 array Hqc of 63 × 63 circulant permutation and zero matrices. Choose γ = 6 and ρ = 37. IV. CONCLUSION AND REMARKS
In this paper, we have presented a method for constructing QC-LDPC codes based on the primitive elements of finite fields. Based on this method, we have constructed a class of QC-LDPC codes whose Tanner graphs have girth at least 6. Two example codes have been given and they perform well over the AWGN channel. QC-LDPC codes can also be constructed based on the additive and cyclic subgroups of finite fields.
