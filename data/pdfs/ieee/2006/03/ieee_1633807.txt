A New Fast Density Evolution
Hui Jin and Tom Richardson Flarion Technologies
Bedminster, NJ Email: {tjr,hjin}@flarion.com

Abstract- Density evolution for LDPC codes predicts asymptotic performance and serves as a practical design tool for designing top performing structures [1]. Many
papers advocate the use of exit chart methods and other
approximations, proclaiming that density evolution is computationally too intensive. In this paper we show that this is not the case: we present a highly efficient and accurate implementation of density evolution for LDPC codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
Forbeliefpropagationdecoding of LDPC codes, Density Evolution was introduced in [2] where a computational implementation was indicated. In [3] a somewhat different implementation is outlined. That implementation is computationally transparent and provides a practical LDPC design tool. Even so, that implementation of density evolution is fairly computationally intensive and many papers still dismiss density evolution on that basis, preferring exit charts and other one-dimensional approximations.
The method presented in [3] can be improved in several respects and we will indicate some of those improvements in the full version of this paper. The main focus of this paper is a different implementation of density evolution that gives much more accurate results for equivalent computational complexity than other known methods. A preliminary and primitive version of our method was presented in [4].
In general, LDPC density evolution requires implementation of two distinct convolution operations on probability
densities. Since the two convolutions arise from two
different domains over which the densities are naturally
defined, which we will refer to as the Variable domain and the Check domain, the density representations used for the two convolution operations are often different. In this case it is also necessary to be able to pass between the two representations. By using the appropriate Fourier transform, one can realize the convolutions as pointwise multiplication of functions. Under such an implementation, and our implementation is such, it possible
to exponentiate densities directly. Nevertheless, we will
focus on pairwise convolution.
The Variable domain is the reals together with a point at

+oo, denoted R+, and the densities are of log-likelihood
ratios. Here, convolution is standard convolution over
R+. In the method presented in [3] the convolution
of uniformly sampled distributions is accomplished us-
ing an FFT and multiplying the Fourier transforms of
the probability distributions point-wise (the point at
infinity being handled separately). One difficulty which
arises from this approach concerns aliasing. The probability distributions generally have positive mean and the probability mass tends towards larger values under
convolution. It was therefore found necessary in this implementation to perform an inverse Fourier transform on pair-wise products of Fourier transforms to properly handle the probability mass associated to log-likelihood ratios exceeding the chosen maximum supported value. One can transfer this mass to the point at infinity, for example. Convolving a pair of densities therefore requires (typically and in the worst case) three FFTs: The two forward FFTs of the probability densities and the inverse FFT of the product. A possible alternative to
performing the inverse FFT is to provide enough zeropadding to remove the aliasing threat. But this results in an even larger increase in complexity.
Perhaps surprisingly, the need to perform an inverse FFT after each pairwise convolution can be completely eliminated while still working in the Fourier domain, profoundly reducing the complexity of variable node updates. The main point is that we are really only interested
in the detailed values of the probability distribution in some finite range, e.g., [-40, 40].
The Check domain is GF[2] x [0, oc] (see [5]) giving rise to the second convolution operation. In [3] the proposed method for performing this convolution uses a look-up table to perform the operation directly in the space of discrete supported log-likelihood ratio probabilities. This method is viable only for pairwise convolution. Since quantization to the discrete support set is performed after each pair-wise convolution, fine quantization is required for high accuracy.

V. CONCLUSION
We have presented a computationally efficient method
for density evolution. Thresholds computed, up to nu-
merical accuracy, are lower bounds on the true thresh-
olds. For the regular (3, 6) structures computed thresholds for the BSC and AppBP are shown for BP and AppBP in Tbl. I. We observe that we can compute the thresholds to within a few thousandths of a dB and that the gap between the AppBP thresholds and the BP threshold is about 0.01 dB.
