Enhanced Low-Density Parity-Check Codes Based
on Sum-check Blocks

Jingli Lin, Longjiang Jing, Weile Zhu
School of Electronic Engineering
University ofElectronic Science and Technology ofChina
Chengdu, Sichuan, China

Chen Yang School of Mechanical Engineering and Automation
Xihua University Chengdu, Sichuan, China

Abstract-Based on some characteristics of decoding and error-floors of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, an approach of adding sum-check blocks to original LDPC blocks over GF(2) is proposed. The performance of this approach is analyzed and the circuits are also presented. Also, the simulation results show that the performance of the constructed LDPC codes can be improved significantly with low cost, as the block error probability (BEP) drops.
I. INTRODUCTION
In 1962, LDPC codes were first investigated by Gallager
[1]. As a class of linear block code with very sparse
parity-check matrices, LDPC codes can approach the Shannon limit [2]. Recently, these codes have attracted much attention. Especially, the regular LDPC codes over GF(2) are used in practical communication system much more, thus these codes are addressed in this paper. The sum-product [3][4] algorithm is practical for decoding LDPC codes on Additive White Gaussian Noise (AWGN) channel, which works with the framework of the message-passing algorithm [5]. In this way, after a number of iterations, almost all blocks can be decoded correctly, but for the remainder, whose incorrect message density distribution becomes stable, decoding will fail. Therefore, as for a decoded block, if its bits are not all correct, the number of error ones will be not small. Also, the BER can't reach the value of 0 asymptotically. Constructing LDPC codes with low error-floors is not a simple thing. According to these characteristics of LDPC codes, we propose an approach of adding sum-check blocks to original LDPC blocks over GF(2). As the BEP drops, the performance of the proposed LDPC codes can be improved significantly.
This paper is organized as follows: Section II describes this method in detail. The corresponding circuits are presented in section III. In Section IV, the performance is analyzed and the simulation results are given. Finally, Section V concludes this paper.

III. THE CIRCUITS
The circuits at the transmitter side and receiver side are shown in Fig. 2 and Fig. 3 respectively.
At the transmitter side, the original blocks shift from LDPC codes encoder bit by bit and the XOR-gate is used for sum operation. For a group of blocks (consisting of s original blocks and one sum-check block), at the beginning of first step, the counter sets a, such that the feedback shift-register works in feedback shift mode.
Also, The counter sets b , resets c until the s original blocks stream out completely. At this time, the sum-check block is contained in feedback shift-register. Then the counter resets b and sets c to shift out the sum-check block. When the sum-check block begins to
shift out, the counter resets a to clear the feedback shift-register. Now, the above process repeats for next groups.
At the receiver side, the circuit can be divided into two symmetrical parts: structure-0 and Structure-1. Therefore, Buffer-O and Buffer-I form ping-pong buffers. The number of the first coming error block can be restored in address register. The decoded blocks shift from the decoder bit by bit. The counter sets a to initialize circuit as it does at the transmitter side. As the s decoded blocks shift into Buffer-O completely, the data begin to move to DSP from Buffer-0. If there exists no or more than two error blocks when the data in Buffer-O are all moved out, the processing of this group reaches the end. If one and only detected error occurs

B. Simulation
Based on approaches of construction, LDPC codes can be divided into two general categories: random codes [3][6] and structured codes [7]-[9]. In view of the latter allowing low complexity encoding and being more practicable, we construct two Quasi-Cyclic(QC) LDPC codes [8][9] as the codes used in the simulation of this paper. They are (3744,3,6) with rate 1/2 and (3744,3,9) with rate 1/3 respectively. Every 55 original blocks are set as a group. The sum-product decoding algorithm is employed and the maximum numbers of decoding iterations is set to 20. We assume BPSK transmission over an AWGN channel. The simulation results are plotted in Fig. 4.
In Fig. 4, the original block error rates and the original bit error rate are showed with the curve of Original block and the curve of Original bit respectively. Correspondingly, the block error rate and the bit error rate, with the sum-check blocks added are plotted as the curves of Sum-Check block and the curves of Sum-Check bit respectively. Also, the curves of Sum-Check block counted are based on the calculations using (9). As shown in Fig. 4, the curves of Sum-Check block are asymptotical to the curves of Sum-Check block counted. At the region of low SNR, the original block error is in high probability. Improving performance with this method is almost impossible. With the SNR rising, the codes sum-check block added outperform the original ones gradually. Therefore, the steep water-fall region come into being eventually.
V. CONCLUSION
An approach of adding sum-check blocks to original LDPC blocks over GF(2) is proposed in this paper. As the block error probability drops, the performance of the constructed LDPC codes can be improved significantly. Also, the cost of both software and hardware is not high at all. Therefore, the LDPC codes can be more practical with this method.
