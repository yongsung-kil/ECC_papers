On the Robustness of Iterative Decoders

Pirouz Zarrinkhat John H. Chapman Space Centre
Canadian Space Agency Saint-Hubert, QC J3Y 8Y9, Canada E-mail: pirouz.zarrinkhat@space.gc.ca

Masoud Ardakani Dep’t of Elec. and Comp. Eng’g
University of Alberta Edmonton, AB T6G 2V4, Canada E-mail: ardakani@ece.ualberta.ca

Abstract— Robustness of iterative decoders, i.e., their ability to successfully decode the received signal in spite of unknown changes in the channel parameter, is investigated. More specifically, using low-density parity-check codes and iterative messagepassing decoders for error correction, we consider reliable communication over a binary-input power-limited Gaussian channel whose noise power is not perfectly known to the receiver. We propose iterative algorithms that can decode the received signal in the presence of a strong uncertainty in the channel parameter. Such decoders are of interest for wireless applications, where the channel attenuation could significantly vary with time.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] are known to be capable of approaching the capacity of many channel types [2]–[4]. However, this capacity-achieving ability is possible when the channel is perfectly known. There are two reasons that condition the near-capacity performance of LDPC codes to a perfect knowledge of the channel. First, channel knowledge is required to find the capacity and therefore select the code rate close to the capacity. Second, the channel state information is required for computing the initial bit log-likelihood ratios (LLR), defined as ln (P (x = +1|y)/P (x = −1|y)). With inaccurate LLR values, the code may not perform close to its decoding threshold [5]. The second reason remains valid even if a near-capacity performance is not necessary. In fact, a poor knowledge of the channel results in a gap between the performance and the theoretical threshold of the code, regardless of any existing gap between the threshold of the code and the capacity.
In many applications, channel estimation errors are unavoidable. Time-varying channels, fading channels, and impulsive noise environments are examples of such cases. In such cases, based on an inaccurate estimation of the channel parameter, the decoder computes the LLR values inaccurately and starts the decoding. One interesting question is that how much inaccuracy in the channel estimation different decoders can handle. More importantly, we wish to know whether it is possible to improve the decoders robustness to such channel estimation errors.
The decoding of LDPC codes under message-passing algorithms [6] is performed by (iteratively) exchanging messages between the check-nodes and the variable-nodes of the code’s factor graph [7]. There exist many different message-passing algorithms with varying performance and complexity. In a

conventional, or regular, decoder, based on the desired performance and the affordable complexity, one of these algorithms is used. In hybrid decoders, however, we use more than one algorithm to decode a single word. Hybrid decoders are successfully used to improve the performance for a given complexity, or to reduce the complexity for a given performance.
There are two main classes of hybrid decoders:
1) Gear-shift decoders [1], [8], [9], where different update rules are used in different iterations, but within a given iteration, the update rule is fixed for all the nodes of the same type in the code’s factor graph.
2) Irregular decoders [10], [11], where different nodes use different update rules, but for a given node, the update rule is fixed throughout the iterative process.
In [12], density evolution analysis of irregular decoders is presented and it is shown that irregular decoders are capable of reducing the decoding complexity of LDPC codes. In the present work, we compare the robustness of regular and irregular decoders to the uncertainty in the channel parameter. We focus on two standard soft message-passing algorithms, namely, sum-product and min-sum [13], [14], as well as irregular decoders with sum-product and minsum constituents. We show that compared to regular decoders, irregular decoders can better tolerate channel estimation errors. More precisely, we show that by using an irregular decoder, we can provide a more reliable decoding in the presence of channel estimation error on top of reducing the decoding complexity. Moreover, we present a “parallel-independent” decoding strategy that, compared to existing regular decoders, is significantly more robust to channel uncertainty. This strategy, however, has a higher decoding complexity compared to regular decoders.
Since the focus of this work is only on the robustness of decoding algorithms, to avoid unnecessary discussions, we limit ourselves to the case of regular LDPC codes. Our approach, however, is quite general and can be applied to irregular codes as well.
The organization of this paper is as follows. In Section II, we will briefly review some required backgrounds. In Section III, first, we will define our uncertainty model and our measure of performance, and then, we will compare, via examples, the robustness of different algorithms. The paper is concluded in Section IV.

III. CHANNEL UNCERTAINTY

We mentioned earlier that sum-product proffers the best error performance amongst all message-passing algorithms [13], [14]. We have to add that it does so only when the channel is perfectly known: in fact, a considerable degradation in sum-product’s error performance is observed if not an accurate estimate of σ is used for calculating the LLR values [5].2 min-sum, on the other hand, is universal and, at the expense of its inferior error performance, does not require any estimate of σ [13], [14].3 Intuitively, we expect IRN’s position to be, similar to the case for the decoding threshold values, somewhere in between: needing an estimate of σ, but not at the degree of accuracy that sum-product demands. This issue assumes great importance if one recalls that in many real world applications, an accurate estimate of the channel parameter is simply not available at all times. (For example, in wireless communications, the channel may, unbeknownst to the the receiver, undergo deep fades.) In this section, we compare sum-product and IRN, in terms of their convergence behavior over uncertain (BIPLG) channels.

One interesting approach to successful decoding over a wide

range of α could be using two (or more) algorithms in parallel

and independently. For instance, here, we can use, in parallel

and independently, both sum-product and IRN0.5. The first algorithm that finds a valid codeword stops the decoding

process and reports the decoded word. With this strategy, the

convergence range will be the union of the convergence ranges

of sum-product and IRN0.5, i.e., [−2.6, +2.7], which is significantly wider than any of the individual convergence ranges.

This, of course, comes at the expense of increased complexity.

However, for some applications with strong uncertainty in the

channel parameter (e.g., impulsive noise environments), this

“parallel-independent” strategy could be of interest.
