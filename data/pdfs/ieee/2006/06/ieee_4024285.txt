An IS Simulation Technique for Very Low BER Performance Evaluation of LDPC Codes

Enver Cavus, *Charles L. Haymes, Babak Daneshrad

UCLA Electrical Engineering Department *IBM T.J Watson Research Center

{cavus, babak@ee.ucla.edu}

haymes@us.ibm.com

Abstract-We introduce an Importance Sampling (IS) method that successfully simulates the performance of Low density Parity Check (LDPC) Codes in an AWGN channel at very low bit error rates (BERs). By effectively finding and biasing bit node combinations that are the dominant sources of error events, called trapping sets, the developed technique provokes more frequent decoder failures. Consequently, fewer simulation runs and higher simulation gains are achieved. Regardless of the block size of an LDPC code, only a few dominant trapping set classes cause decoder failures at low BER regions. Therefore, the proposed technique allows the performance evaluation for any size LDPC code at very low BER regions with remarkable simulation gains. For BERs of 10-20, we observed simulation gains on the order of 1014.
I. INTRODUCTION
The performance evaluation of Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) codes at very low bit error rates (BERs) are of great interest. Low BER estimation of LDPC codes exposes the error floor regions that are beyond the reach of traditional simulation and helps the feasibility studies of employing LDPC codes in applications requiring very low BERs. So far, no analytical tool is available to evaluate the performance of LDPC codes, and Monte Carlo (MC) simulations are the only way to assess the performance of these codes. However, at very low BER regions, traditional MC simulations cannot be performed due to prohibitive simulation times. In order to avoid long simulation times, a fast BER simulation technique, called Importance Sampling (IS), can be applied. IS methods are widely used in digital communications [11]-[13] and have also been effectively applied to FEC schemes [14]-[17]. If a proper biasing scheme can be developed for the required application, IS methods promise significant simulation gains with little to no loss in the accuracy of the results.
Recently, two independent IS approaches have been proposed to address the performance evaluation of the LDPC codes at very low BERs [1][2]. In [1], a parity-check biasing scheme has been first developed for the single parity check (SPC) codes and then applied to LDPC codes. Performance of a (96, 48) LDPC code is demonstrated up to the BER of 10-14 and simulation gains in the order of 105 are reported. However, the proposed method does not yield an optimum biasing scheme for LDPC codes and the efficiency of the IS method is impaired for longer codes due to “divide by N” problem [1]. In [2], a two phase adaptive importance sampling method based on Multicanonical Monte

Carlo (MMC) simulations is introduced. Unlike the scheme in [1], this method does not utilize a priori biasing scheme, instead first an unconstrained and then a constrained MMC algorithm adaptively iterates to find the optimum IS biasing scheme. In [2], BER and WER curves for (96, 48) LDPC code are illustrated for as low as 10-19 with simulation gains of 107. Unfortunately, the complexity of the adaptive search algorithm depends on the block size of the code and the efficiency of the algorithm gets deteriorated for bigger size LDPC codes since a larger noise space has to be searched for an optimum bias.
In this work, we propose a highly efficient IS scheme for LDPC codes, which is negligibly dependent of block size of a given LDPC code and achieves higher simulation gains. By effectively finding and biasing bit node combinations that are the dominant source of error events, the developed technique provokes more frequent decoder failures. Having more error events leads to statistically valid simulations in less time with higher simulation gains. It is well known that medium to long block size LDPC codes have large minimum distances and, the decoder failures are mainly due to near codewords [5][6][7]. In [3], the combinations of bit nodes that prevent the LDPC decoder from converging to the transmitted codeword are defined as trapping sets. Based on these trapping sets, a computational technique is developed to predict the error floor of a given LDPC code. In this paper, a two step algorithm is introduced to estimate low BER performance of LDPC codes. In the first step, a novel trapping search algorithm is used to identify all trapping sets and then, the trapping sets are classified based on the total number of bits involved in each trap. In the second step, IS is applied to only one trapping set from each class and the total BER of the code is estimated by adding the BER contribution of each trapping class. Regardless of the block size of an LDPC code, only a few dominant trapping set classes cause decoder failures at low BER regions. Therefore, the proposed technique allows the performance evaluation for any size LDPC code at very low BER regions with remarkable simulation gains in the order of 1010.

The most effective IS method used in ECC schemes is the mean translation (MT) method [13]. In mean translation, the mean of the original noise density, f(x), is biased such that the new noise density, f(x)*, moves the transmitted codeword towards its nearby codewords. Hence, more frequent decoder failures are encountered. Consequently, compared to a traditional MC method, low BER performance is effectively simulated with significantly fewer runs. As the bias associated with each noise sample is known, the error count per simulation run can be unbiased and translated back to obtain true BER estimate.

In the case of linear block codes, a practical choice of the biasing scheme for the optimal or near optimal decoders is to shift the mean of noise density at the boundary of the error region and transmitted codeword [16][17]. For LDPC codes, the employed soft iterative Sum-Product Decoder is non-optimal and the iterative process complicates defining an exact error region for the choice of a good biasing scheme.

III. PROPOSED APPROACH: IS WITH TRAPPING SETS

In order to develop an efficient algorithm, it is necessary to accurately define the error regions for an LDPC code. In this work, unlike the previous approaches in [1] and [2], an error set partitioning based on trapping set bits is considered. The error space of LDPC codes consists of trapping regions or states, and once captured the decoding algorithm is never able to recover from these states. In our approach, error space is partitioned based on these trapping regions and IS simulation is tuned for these specific regions by biasing the corresponding trapping set bits.

A. Definition of the Trapping Sets

The combination of error bits that leads to a decoder failure are defined as trapping set bits. We label the trapping sets as (x, y) trapping sets, where x is the number of error bits in a failure and y represents the number of unsatisfied checks caused by x error bits. The significant characteristic of these trapping sets is that they contain multiple “mis-satisfied” parity check nodes. A check node is called a “mis-satisfied check” if the parity check contains an even number of error bits. In a mis-satisfied check node, all extrinsic outputs are unreliable, which makes it difficult for the decoder to recover all the error bits. When specific missatisfied checks occur at the same time, a trapping set is formed and the decoder reinforces the error bits, resulting in a decoder failure. Fig. 1 illustrates a (4, 2) trapping set for (96, 48) LDPC code in a bipartite graph. The error bits are shown in black and only the edge connections of the error bits are included. The striped check nodes are the mis-satisfied parities that prevent the decoder from escaping the error state.

Note that the strength of the trapping set becomes stronger, as x is increased and y is decreased. For instance, given a regular LDPC code with degree-3 variable nodes (i.e each variable node has 3 edge connections), (5,1) trapping sets are more powerful traps than (4,2) sets. In a (5, 1) trapping set, 1 unsatisfied parity check strives to correct the error bits against 7 mis-satisfied check nodes, whereas 2 unsatisfied parity checks attempt to improve reliability of the messages against 5 mis-satisfied checks in a (4,2) trap. In the special case of y being 0, the decoder converges to a wrong codeword and the trapping set is formed by the wrong codeword bits.
B. Trapping Set Search
The set of bits that leads to a decoder failure can be identified for each error event. One simple method of locating the LDPC code’s trapping sets is described in [3], where the decoder failures of a standard Monte Carlo simulation at a particular SNR are recorded as the trapping set bits. However, the proposed technique in [3] is not a very efficient method to find out all the trapping sets at low BERs as MC simulations would require prohibitive simulation times.
In order to facilitate the trapping set search problem, we use a graph search technique combined with a IS simulation. If the trapping sets of a given LDPC code are investigated carefully, it is easy to see that these trapping sets formed by overlapping short cycles existing in the bipartite graph. Table I lists all the cycle-6s for bits 0, 1, and 61 and exemplifies one trapping set at the last row formed by the highlighted cycle-6s. The (4, 2) trapping set in Fig. 1 is formed by the two cycle-6s, (61 13 91) and (61 47 91), Table I.
It is clear that trapping sets of Table I are formed by bit combinations of 2 cycle-6s, though it is not obvious which of 2 cycle-6 forms a strong trapping set. In addition, in (5, 1) trapping sets, there is an additional bit, which does not belong to any cycle-6s but still appears in the trapping sets. For example, bits 31 does not form any cyle-6s with bit 0 but participates in the trapping set. Although combination of short cycles forms the basis for a trapping set, the final appearance of a trapping set is ambiguous. In order to effectively find the dominant trapping sets for a given LDPC code, we propose a combined graph search and IS simulation technique, which is summarized below.
1. Search the bipartite graph and list all the existing shortest cycles for each bit as in Table I.
2. List all the other bit nodes that form a cycle for a particular bit in the same row as in Table II, which shows the list of all cycle-6 bits for the first 5 rows of (96, 48) code. For an LDPC code of length N, there are N different rows.
3. Apply IS to all cycle-6 bits starting from the 1st row. A bias value of 1 is used for each cycle-6 bit so that the total bias value adds up to 2 in each parity check.
4. Run a fixed number of decoding simulation loops, say Ls=1000 loops, with biased cycles and observe the resulting trapping sets in the decoding algorithm.

Compared to the MC trapping set search described in [3], the proposed search method only involves at most (N*Ls) IS simulations, yet it is very effective for locating all the trapping sets involved. Although the search algorithm depends on the block size, N, the search time is negligibly small. For a (2000, 1000) LDPC code, this step takes less than 5 minutes. Note that it is also possible to identify the trapping sets using a purely graph search method without any decoding simulations. However, we prefer the proposed search technique because simulating the decoding algorithm with biasing all cycle bits verifies that the identified trapping set actually causes a decoder failure. Furthermore, using an IS simulation ensures that the identified trapping set is indeed a dominant set in the BER region of interest.

C. Classifiying Trapping Set Classes
Once all trapping sets are found, it is advantageous to classify all the trapping sets that have the same size and simulate only one set from each class with equally strong bias values of -1 (polarity of bias values depends on the applied BPSK modulation to the transmitted all-zero codeword). As the BER figures of the trapping sets belonging to the same class are approximately equivalent, simulating only one trapping set is sufficient. Fig. 2 illustrates the equivalence of the BER values of different sets that belong to same class at different SNRs. This will effectively reduce the complexity of the algorithm to be proportional to the number of dominant trapping set classes at very low BER regions.

Fig. 2. Ber equivalence of various (5,1) trapping sets of (96, 48) LDPC code
Cumulative BER of each class is obtained by multiplying the simulated BER of one set by the total number of trapping sets in that class. Finally, the total BER of the code is calculated as the sum of the BERs of each class.
IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
In this section, we present the performance of a regular (96, 48) and a (2000, 1000) LDPC code in an AWGN channel using IS technique with the trapping sets. (96, 48) LDPC code can be found in [8] and (2000, 1000) LDPC code is generated using the software provided in [9]. Both of these codes are random constructions and do not contain any cycle-4s.
We have implemented the quantized approximate-min algorithm presented in [10], 1 sign bit and 5 quantization bits, is used and the maximum iteration limit is set to 200 for all simulations. Allzero codeword transmission is assumed and BPSK modulation is employed. At high SNR regions, MC simulations were carried out until 20 codeword errors were collected. The IS simulations on the other hand were terminated when the normalized error estimator became less than or equal to 0.2236 as defined in equation (10).
Fig. 3 shows the BER performance of a (96, 48) LDPC code with a standard MC simulation and proposed IS method. This code is a very similar code that was studied in [1] and [2] and it has two codewords at the minimum distance of 6. The two minimum distance codewords form (6, 0) trapping sets and their BER contributions are also included in the results. As it is observed from Fig. 3, IS simulation matches closely to MC results. The small performance gap at 7 dB is most likely due to insufficient number of error event simulation involved in Monte Carlo simulation. Table III provides comparison data for IS and MC simulations of (96, 48) code, where it is clear that the IS estimates agree with MC counterparts at all SNR values. V. CONCLUSION
In this work we have presented a highly efficient IS method for performance evaluation of LDPC codes at very low BERs and provided simulation results for (96, 48), and (2000, 1000) LDPC codes. In contrast to previous works in [1] and [2], an error set partitioning based on trapping set bits is considered. By effectively finding and biasing trapping sets, the presented technique accurately estimates performance of any size LDPC code at very low BERs. Remarkable simulation gains of 1011 at BER of 10-14 for (96, 48) LDPC code and a gain of 1014 at BER of 10-20 for (2000, 1000) LDPC code are achieved.
