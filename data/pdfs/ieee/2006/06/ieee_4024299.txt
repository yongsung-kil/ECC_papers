Abstract— We investigate a new class of codes which is in a sense a hybrid between LDPC codes and turbo-codes. Some members of this new class have been shown to be asymptotically good and we conjecture that such a behavior holds for all classes of codes presented here. They all display excellent iterative decoding performances with no error floor at block error rates up to 10−6 for lengths of several thousand together with low average decoding complexity.
I. INTRODUCTION
It is now well known that several different coding schemes such as turbo-codes, low-density parity check codes, repeataccumulate codes or product codes are able to operate with very low error probability after iterative decoding at rates extremely close to the Shannon limit. However, it seems that in all cases, this comes at the cost of having a poor minimum distance, which itself gives a rather high error floor. This turns out to be quite apparent in the schemes which are the closest to the Shannon limit (either irregular LDPC codes [8] or irregular turbo-codes [4]). LDPC codes with a small fraction of edges with left degree 2 [9], [10] or Tanner codes built from local codes with minimum distance greater than 2 [5] are examples of code families with linear minimum distance (i.e. being asymptotically good). However, these ensembles operate with vanishing error probability after decoding at rates much further from the Shannon limit than aforementioned constructions.
This raises the issue if asymptotically good codes can approach the capacity with low complexity iterative decoding. Our aim here is to explore other families of codes to obtain asymptotically good codes approaching the Shannon capacity. For this purpose, we study a class of codes containing the class of LDPC codes (and more generally of Tanner codes) as well as the code family related to turbo-codes. This general class of codes is constructed so that irregular variable node degrees are allowed and thus the further optimization of the family is possible.
We define this family in Section II. Sufficient conditions for being asymptotically good are presented in [16]. In this paper we explore a particular code family which was proposed in [16] and called Tail-biting Trellis Low-Density Parity Check codes (TLDPC). This family, when its parameters are well chosen, combines such interesting features as very simple structure yielding low-complexity iterative decoding,minimum

distance linear in the code length andgood performances after iterative decoding beating the ones of standard turbo-codes for code lengths of several thousand.
We present examples of class members of rates 1/2, 1/3, 3/10, 1/4 where this fact is striking.
Our codes are defined by means of two ingredients : a base code and a bipartite graph. To avoid small weight codewords in our codes we have chosen the bipartite structure such that a certain graph, called the graph of codewords of partial weight 2, contains only cycles of linear size. We will see in Section III why this fact is crucial. It should be emphasized that the fact that standard parallel turbo codes have minimum distance growing only at most logarithmically in the block size [6], [12] is due to cycles of logarithmic size in their associated graph of codewords of partial weight 2 (see Section III).

III. THE GRAPH OF CODEWORDS OF PARTIAL WEIGHT 2
It turns out that in the presence of degree 2 vertices in the aforementioned bipartite graph the average 3 number of codewords of a given constant weight w denoted by a¯w and obtained from the previous construction is of order Ω (1). Thus, at least a constant fraction of such codes should have minimum distance at most w. On the other hand, in order to get good iterative performances of our codes and to come close to the channel capacity it is essential to have a rather large fraction of degree 2 vertices (for the erasure channel this can be checked by EXIT charts considerations, see for instance [13] where this fact is proved for LDPC codes). If one insists upon having a code family approaching with iterative decoding the channel capacity and almost all members of which are asymptotically good, it is therefore crucial to carefully choose the bipartite graph. We achieve this goal by considering a certain graph derived from the bits of degree 2 and certain codewords of partial weight 2 in the base code : the graph of codewords of partial weight 2. We define the partial weight of a codeword as the number of nonzero entries over the positions of degree > 1. The fact that a¯w is of constant order for constant w is due to a constant probability of having cycles of constant size in the graph of codewords of partial weight 2. In our construction we avoid this behavior by restricting the bipartite graph in such a way that the associated graph of codewords of partial weight 2 has no small cycles.
Before defining this graph we first define Definition 1: Two bits xi and xj of the base code are equivalent if and only if the base code contains a codeword for which the non zero entries at positions of degree > 1 are i and j. The ensembles of equivalent bits of the base code are called clusters. Definition 2: The graph of codewords of partial weight 2 is the graph G = (V , E ) where V is the ensemble of clusters defined above and E is the ensemble of edges eij between two different clusters vi and vj if and only if there exist two

bits xk and xl of the base code belonging to the clusters vi and vj respectively which join the same degree-2 variable node.
Note that any edge in the graph of codewords of partial weight 2 corresponds to a codeword of certain weight (say t) in the base code which is non zero in two positions of degree 2 and in t − 2 positions of degree 1. By putting the weight t − 1 on such an edge and by defining the weight of a cycle as the sum of weights of the edges belonging to this cycle, it is straightforward to check, that
Fact 1: A cycle of weight l in the graph of codewords of partial weight 2 induces a codeword of weight l in the code.
Proof: Consider a cycle of weight l in G . For the bipartite graph of the global code the vertices belonging to the cycle correspond to a set of degree 2 vertices. Assign them and the corresponding vertices 4 of degree 1 to 1 and all the remaining vertices to 0. It can be checked that : (i) the weight of this assignment is l, (ii) the induced assignment of the positions of the base code yields a valid codeword of the base code. This implies that our original assignment of the code positions yields a codeword of weight l.
The smallest weight of a cycle in G is therefore an upper bound on the code minimum distance.
The graph of codewords of partial weight 2 is nothing but a slight generalization of a graph considered in the case of LDPC code ensembles in [10]. It also turns out that for parallel turbo-codes the minimum distance, logarithmical in the block length, is a consequence of Fact 1. The considerations on GT B or GT B made in Section IV of [12] can be easily turned into a proof that the graph of codewords of partial weight 2 associated to a turbo-code has a cycle of logarithmic weight.

V. EXIT CHARTS

All the code families presented here have been optimized by using EXIT charts. This tool has been introduced by ten Brink (see for instance [14]), the basic idea is to analyze the iterative decoding by tracking down during the iterations the evolution of the expected value of the entropy of the extrinsic probability of a bit versus the average value of the entropy of the intrinsic probability of a bit. For the erasure channel, this can be done rigorously without making any assumption. On the Gaussian channel however, it is generally assumed that the log-likelihood ratios of the extrinsic and intrinsic probabilities conditioned on the value of the bit under consideration have Gaussian distributions. More precisely, in the case the all zero codeword was sent, it is assumed that the log-likelihood ratios are distributed as Gaussian variables with mean µ and variance ν2 = 2µ. where C is the capacity of the erasure channel with given erasure probability p0, R is the rate of the code and A is the area between two EXIT chart curves. Apparently such a relation holds also approximately over the Gaussian channel. Thus, if we want to minimize the gap to capacity we should strive for EXIT charts of the base code which can be well approximated by an EXIT chart of variable nodes.

VII. CODES DERIVED FROM FAMILY (B)
Trying to optimize the degree distribution of the previous family to obtain codes of rate 1/2 is not a good idea as there will always be a significant gap between the EXIT chart of the base code and the EXIT chart of variable nodes for large values of the average intrinsic entropy (say in the range 0.4-0.5). As a consequence, the resulting codes will have thresholds rather far away from capacity. However, this behaviour can be significantly improved by changing the base code. For instance, we reduce the aforementioned gap by taking the base code of family (B). However a simple combinatorial argument shows that in this case it is not possible to obtain asymptotically good codes when λ2 > 2/3 (the graph of codewords of partial weight 2 is of average degree > 2 in this case). We chose λ2 = 1/2, this value is close to 2/3 and in addition it makes possible to choose the structure of the graph of codewords of partial weight 2 so as to simplify the analysis of the average spectrum.
In this case, there are several different ways to put some structure on the bipartite graph in order to have a graph of codewords of partial weight 2 without cycles of sublinear size. Probably the simplest way would be to choose the graph

of codewords of partial weight 2 to be a union of disjoint paths. In this case, the same proof as in the previous case applies to show that the new code family contains almost only asymptotically good codes. However, it turns out that the prediction of the threshold given by the EXIT charts is underestimated by a bit more than 0.1dB. This might be due to the fact that that the EXIT chart implicitly assumes that we choose positions to be of degree 2 independently of each other with probability λ2. This would imply that the expected number of vertices of degree 3 in the graph of codewords of partial weight 2 would be λ23. However with the previous choice there are no vertices of degree 3.
This fact motivated us to choose the positions of bits of degree 2 in the base code in a more sophisticated way. To simplify the explanation, let us assume that the length m of the base code is a multiple of 24. We choose the positions of degree 2 so that the graph of codewords of partial weight 2 is a union of a cycle of length m/8, m/24 isolated vertices and m/24 3-stars5. In this case the proportion of vertices of degree 0, 1, 2 and 3 in the graph of codewords of partial weight 2 is 1/8, 3/8, 3/8 and 1/8 respectively. These figures are exactly the expected proportions we would have obtained if the positions of the base code were chosen to be of degree 2 with probability 1/2. The predictions of the threshold given by the EXIT chart seem to be accurate in this case.
Fig. 3 shows a family-B code of length 16000 and of rate 1/2 performing 0.6 dB better at WER of 2 · 10−4 than an 8-state duo-binary Turbo code of the same length and rate [1]. Note that for our family-B code, the maximum number of iterations is 100, but depending on the signal to noise ratio the average number of decoding iterations necessary to decode successfully is in the range 20 − 40 only. Each iteration is at most three times less complex than for the corresponding turbo-code.

VIII. CODES DERIVED FROM FAMILY (C)
The previous choices of the base code do not allow to have a constant fraction of degree 1 nodes as in this case the graph of codewords of partial weight 2 contains necessarily a cycle of fixed size and thus by Fact 1 there will be a codeword of constant weight. However, if we allow certain sections of the tailbiting trellis to be of length one (i.e the C0i’s associated to these sections are equal to {0}) then positions associated to these sections can be chosen to be of degree one without harming the minimum distance of the code.
This approach turns out to be particularly useful to lower the threshold of family (A). This is explained by EXIT charts considerations. Indeed, for family (A) a significant contribution to the area difference between the two EXIT charts at the threshold comes from different behaviors of these two curves at the origin: the EXIT chart of the base code is concave up whereas the EXIT chart of variable nodes is concave down.

IX. CODES DERIVED FROM FAMILY (D) AND (E)
The previous family displays quite good iterative decoding performances for rates around 3/10. EXIT charts seem to indicate that the gap to capacity in this case is about 0.18dB, which is quite good. For smaller rates the degree optimization yields even lower gaps to capacity at cost of increasing the maximum degree. This impacts not only on the complexity of iterative decoding (which increases) but also on the performances of iterative decoding : now, when the decoding algorithm fails, this is generally due to a failure of reducing the extrinsic entropy in the first steps of iterative decoding and a large number of errors usually remains. It has a negative influence on the bit error probability and the slope in the waterfall error region is also decreased. It is possible to give a heuristic explanation of these negative phenomena

X. CONCLUSION

In this article we presented a new family of codes which

we call tail-biting LDPC codes (being in a sense a tail-biting

version of LDPC codes). The point of the study was to try

to approach the capacity with codes having a linear minimum

distance in the code length and an iterative decoding algorithm

of low complexity. Two classes of codes (A) and (B) display

for instance better performances than standard parallel turbo-

codes at lengths of several thousand. We further improved

these families by allowing bits of degree 1 in their architecture

which has a positive impact both on the EXIT chart of the

base code and on the performances under iterative decoding.

At present we do not have a proof that these new families are

asymptotically good 6, but we conjecture that this is still the

case and simulations results did not show error floors behaviors
