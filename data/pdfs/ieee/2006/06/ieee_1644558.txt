Capacity-approaching LDPC Codes with Low Error Floors for High-speed Digital Communications

Zhiyong He, Sébastien Roy, and Paul Fortier

Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, Laval University Québec City, Quebec, Canada, G1K 7P4

ABSTRACT
This paper discusses the design of capacity-approaching irregular low density parity check (LDPC) codes constructed from circulant permutation matrices with low error floors. The experimental results indicate that the performance in the waterfall region of the error curve can be improved by increasing the maximum column degree or by decreasing the fraction of columns with maximum degree in the parity check matrix. To delay the onset of the error floor, several techniques in code constructions are proposed to optimize both the degree distributions and the cycle structures of irregular codes. Having encoder architectures supporting throughputs in the Gbits/s region, the proposed codes are suitable for high-speed applications of various digital communications.
I. Introduction
Because of their excellent performance and the existence of effective decoding schemes, low density parity check (LDPC) codes introduced by Gallager [1] have recently received a lot of interest for reliable high speed communication applications such as long-haul optical channels and magnetic storage. Several standards have recently been proposed which suggested the incorporation of LDPC codes: Gigabit Ethernet, broadband wireless access networks, and Digital Video Broadcast (DVB) satellite communications. For application in long-haul optical channels, which support data transmitting rates of 20 Gbits/s, and in Gigabit Ethernet, which are expected to provide a data rate up to 10 Gbits/s, high throughput of LDPC codes is a critical issue. Based on parallelizable decoding algorithms, decoders for LDPC codes can attain a throughput of several Gbits/s [2]. On the other hand, encoders of LDPC codes with linear encoding complexity can attain throughput over 10 Gbit/s [3].
By choosing proper degree distributions, irregular LDPC codes with non-constant column degrees and nonconstant row degrees which outperform regular LDPC codes are capable of transmitting information over noisy channels with a capacity close to the Shannon limit (e.g., [4]-[5]). However, irregular LDPC codes exhibit a relatively high error floor because of the existence of columns with small degrees. The error floors in the vicinity of a bit error rate (BER) of 10-7 constitute an obstacle for high-performance applications of irregular

LDPC codes. In this paper, we propose an efficient approach to the design of irregular LDPC codes with very low error floor. Since the proposed LDPC codes are associated with high-speed encoding and decoding architectures, their applications in digital communications are discussed in the last section.

A. Stopping set and trapping set It is accepted now that the error floor of LDPC codes
using the Belief-Propagation (BP) decoding algorithm is mainly due to some graphical structures, such as “stopping sets” or “trapping sets”. Some pseudo-codewords which do not constitute valid codewords but cause failures in the BP decoder are typically referred to as stopping sets [7] in a binary erasure channel or trapping sets [8] [9] in an AWGN channel or in a binary symmetric channel. Essentially, a stopping set is a subset of a bit nodes, for which the induced subgraph contains no check node of degree one, while an (a, b) trapping set is a subset of a bit nodes, for which the induced subgraph contains b check nodes of odd degrees, where a bit node (a check node) corresponds to a column (a row) in H.
In practical code construction, a straightforward search for problematic stopping sets and trapping sets requires extraordinary computation. We propose a fast and efficient approach to code construction, in which small stopping sets and small trapping sets are automatically avoided. The basic idea is to eliminate all small cycle sets by optimizing both the degree distributions and the cycle structures during code construction.
B. Constraints on the degree distributions Since columns of low degree are more likely to form
small cycle sets, the algorithm proceeds by generating columns in increasing order of degree, i.e. matrix H is constructed BC-by-BC from the rightmost BC having the smallest degree to the leftmost BC having the largest degree. To optimize the cycle structure, the coefficients for BCs with degree 2 and 3 are chosen to avoid cycles of length 4, 6, 8, or 10. Then, the coefficients for BCs with degree 4 are chosen to avoid cycles of lengths 4 and 6. Finally, the short cycle of length 4 must be composed of high-degree BCs.

V. Conclusion and discussion
This paper has proposed several approaches to improve the performance of irregular LDPC codes constructed from circulant permutation matrices. The experimental results indicate that the performance in the waterfall region can be improved by increasing the maximum column degree Wc or by decreasing the fraction of columns with degree Wc in H. To delay the onset of the error floor, we have developed techniques for code constructions by optimizing both the degree distributions and the cycle structures during code construction.
Based on the above techniques, we designed irregular codes having no BER floor down to 10-9 which can be used in broadband wireless access networks. To design irregular LDPC codes for magnetic and optical data storage where a BER floor of 10-15 is often required, or optical communications where a BER floor below 10-10 is

required, we can further optimize the cycle structures during code construction by reducing the fraction of columns with small degree in H and decreasing the size of each submatrix (i.e. increasing the number of block-rows).
