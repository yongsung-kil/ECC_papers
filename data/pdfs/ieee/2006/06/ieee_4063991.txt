A Class of Improved Low-Density Parity-Check Codes
Constructed Based on Gallager's Form

Guangen Wu
School of Electronics and Information Engineering Xi'an Jiaotong University Xi'an, China guangenwuWyahoo.com.cn

Pinyi Ren
School of Electronics and Information Engineering Xi'an Jiaotong University Xi'an, China pyrenWmail.xjtu.edu.cn

Abstract-Focused on the problem that short cycles degrade the iterative decoding performance of finite length LDPC, a class of improved Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) Codes constructed based on Gallager's form is designed. On the basis of keeping down the basic form of Gallager's construction, a quasi-cyclic form is used, then the new codes are free of cycles of length 4 and have good minimum distance. Simulation results demonstrated that the improved codes are better than Gallager's codes when block length is small.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) Codes were discovered by Gallager in early 1960s[1]. After being overlooked for many years, this class of codes have been rediscovered and proved to be a good code of Shannon limit approaching codes by Mackay[2]. So LDPC codes have become a focal point of research.
It has been confirmed that cycles in Tanner graph degrade the performance of iterative decoding, especially short cycles. Several recent works consider structured ways to design LDPC codes with large girth (the length of the shortest cycles in Tanner graph) Lin etc. give a construction based on Reed-Solomon codes[3]. Fossorier presented a general quasi-cyclic construction based on unit matrix[4].
In this paper, a class of improved LDPC Codes constructed based on Gallager's form is presented. As a compromise between of random constructions and algebraic ones, this construction inherits the basic structure and randomicity in Gallager's design and guarantees the Tanner graphs of LDPC codes are free of cycles of length 4, and hence have girth at least 6. These codes are simple on structure and have good minimum distances. Simulation results demonstrated that the improved codes perform better than Gallager's codes when block length is small.
The paper is organized as follows. The basic form and defects of Gallager's codes is briefed in Section II. The improved construction is presented in Section Im. In Section

VI, the results and analysis of simulations are given. Finally, concluding remarks are given in Section V.
II. LDPC CODES CONSTRUCTED BY GALLAGER
The binary LDPC codes C are decided by its sparse parity-check matrixH. If there are exactly y ones in each
column and p ones in each row, C is a regular
(N, K, y, p) LDPC code. Furthermore, if H satisfies the
constraint that no two rows (or two columns) have more than one 1-component in common, H is called satisfying the row-column-constraint (RC-constraint), which ensures that the Tanner graph of the parity-check matrix is free of cycles of length 4 and hence has a girth of at least 6. Gallager's construction is: For a regular (7, p) LDPC code, H is divided into y sub-matrices, each containing a single 1 in each column. The first of these sub-matrices contains all its "1" in descending order and the other sub-matrices are merely random column permutations of the first one.
The Gallager codes have excellent performance for large block length; however, the Tanner graphs of these codes have many cycles. When the block length is small
(n < 1 OOObit), the number of cycles with length 4 is very
big relative to the block length. For example, the average number of cycles with length 4 in Tanner graph is 34 as the block length is 126bits! Then it is an important problem that modifying Gallager's LDPC codes into satisfy the RCconstraint (namely there is no cycles of length 4).

IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
Several LDPC codes generated with two methods and their bit error performances with iterative decoding using the SPA are shown in Fig.2. Both of the code rates are one half and the channel is AWGN, with BPSK modulation. The
LDPC codes are (504, 252, 3, 6) and (126,63,3, 6). At
the same time the performance of several textbook codes are compared at close block length. The textbook codes used
here are BCH (127,64) and BCH (511,249) [9]
respectively, whose code rates are one half approximately.
It can be seen from Fig.2 that both of Gallager's codes and improved codes have better performance than the textbook codes at close block length. For the LDPC codes with block length of 126bit, the improved codes exceed Gallager's codes by 0.5dB at the BER of 10-4, which arrives at the expectation of design. However, Gallager's code is a little better than the improved one for the block length of 504bit.
Such performance of codes can be analyzed in this way. Results of computer search show that the numbers of cycles of length 4 for LDPC codes with two block length keep close. (The average numbers of cycles of length 4 is 34 and 36 respectively for block length of 126 bit and 504bit). When the block length is small, the density of short cycles is bigger and short cycles are the main cause which degrades the performance of codes. Because all the cycles of length 4 of the improved codes have been removed, it performs better than Gallager's codes. However, with the increase of block length, the density of short cycles decreases and its effect weakens. On the other hand, the encoding has more and more randomicity while the improved codes lose randomicity in some degree in order to remove the cycles of length 4. In the end, the improved codes perform worse than Gallager's codes slowly. In fact, the encoding design should not emphasize removal of cycles when the block length is larger.
V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, a simple construction of LDPC codes based on Gallager's form is presented. The new construction guarantees the Tanner graphs of LDPC codes are free of cycles of length 4, and hence have girth at least 6, which leads better performance. Compared to the Gallager's codes, the improved codes have better performance when block length is small..
