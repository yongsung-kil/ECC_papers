The Minimal Product Parity Check Matrix and Its Application
Morteza Esmaeili Department of Mathematical Sciences
Isfahan University of Technology Isfahan, Iran
Email: emorteza@cc.iut.ac.ir

Abstract— In decoding a linear block code C by iterative decoding algorithms, these algorithms are essentially applied on a graphical representation of C such as Tanner graph and factor graph. Due to the impact of the structure of these graphs
on the performance of code, we consider minimal parity check
matrices(matrices with minimum number of nonzero entries) of
product codes. An algorithm constructing such matrices is given.
It turns out that under the given construction method, product
coding technique is indeed a powerful method to construct irregular LDPC codes. Given two codes A and B, the construction method produces a Tanner graph with girth g := min {8, ga, gb} for the product code A ⊗ B, where ga and gb are the girth of Tanner graphs representing A and B, respectively. Simulation results confirm the positive practical impact of the minimality of
the parity check matrices.

I. INTRODUCTION

Product coding technique, introduced first by Elias in

1954[5], is a method for constructing long powerful codes

from shorter ones. Let Ci, 1 ≤ i ≤ j, be a binary linear [ni, ki, di] block code. The corresponding j−dimensional product code C is defined as follows[18]. The data to be

transmitted are arranged in a j−dimensional hypercube in

which the length of the ith dimension is ki. The ith dimension

is encoded by Ci, 1 ≤ i ≤ j. The code C is a binary

[Πni, Πki, Πdi] code. Suppose Ci, 1 ≤ i ≤ j, has generator

Concatenated codes and in particular product codes are

usually decoded by suboptimal soft-in, soft-out iterative de-

coding algorithms[10], [13], [17]. In decoding a given block

code C with an iterative decoding algorithm, such as sum-

product algorithm, the algorithm is basically performed on a

graphical representation of C(e.g., factor graph[8] and Tanner

graph[23]). Therefore, the performance of code under iterative

decoding algorithms depends very much on the structure of the

applied graph. Parameters such as girth, number of edges, cy-

cle distribution and degree-uniformity affect the performance

of iterative decoding algorithms.

The parity check matrix H = (hi,j ) of a given linear [n, k] code C is represented by a bipartite graph known as

Tanner graph(TG) [23]. We denote this graph by T G(H).

The bipartite graph T G(H) has two types of nodes, the parity

nodes and the symbol nodes. The jth symbol node is adjacent to the ith parity node if and only if hi,j = 0. This graph is called a minimal Tanner graph (MTG) if it has minimum number of edges among all TGs representing C[7].
Suppose that HA and HB are some parity check matrices for two given codes A and B, respectively. In Section 2 we use these matrices to construct two parity check matrices, denoted H1 and H2, for A ⊗ B. Construction of a minimal parity check matrix for A ⊗ B is addressed in Section 3. Relationship between the girth of the product code and that of the component codes A and B is given in Section 4. In Section 5 we apply the obtained results to the class of single parity check(SPC) codes followed by the construction of irregular low-density parity-check(LDPC) codes in Section 6. Finally in Section 7 simulation results are provided to show positive impact of the minimality of product parity check matrices on the performance of codes under sum-product decoding algorithm. For this purpose the minimal product parity check matrices are compared to the parity check matrices obtained via superposition method[26].

II. PRODUCT TANNER GRAPH

Let A and B be two binary linear codes of dimensions k

and k , and lengths n and n , respectively. Further, suppose

MA and MB are some generators of A and B, respectively. The direct product of A and B [2] denoted by A ⊗ B is the

linear code of dimension kk and length nn having a generator

matrix obtained from MA by replacing each entry aij of MA with aijMB. It is known that the two codes A ⊗ B and B ⊗ A are equivalent, A ⊗ B A ⊗ B. In this Section we use parity

check matrices of A and B to form a parity matrix for A ⊗ B.

In terms of TG representation, this means constructing a TG

for A ⊗ B based on given the same graph for the component

codes.

III. MINIMAL PRODUCT TANNER GRAPH
Let H be a parity check matrix for a given linear block code C. The matrix H is called minimal if the number of nonzero entries of H is minimum possible among all parity check matrices of C. Though C may have more than one minimal parity matrix, we refer to each of them as the minimal parity
check matrix since the rank of their associated cycle-spaces
(the rank of the cycle-spaces of the corresponding TGs) is
the same. Construction of a minimal parity check matrix for a given product code A ⊗ B is the subject of this section. It turns out that H1 and H2 are not necessarily minimal.
In the following lemma we show that a minimal parity check matrix HA⊗B for A ⊗ B is characterised by minimal parity check matrices of A and B.
Lemma 2: The rows of a minimal parity check matrix HA⊗B for A ⊗ B can be interpreted by the rows of minimal parity check matrices of A and B. Proof Let HA and HB be two minimal parity check matrices for a length n code A and a length n code B, respectively. Consider also a minimal parity check matrix HA⊗B for A⊗B. Suppose r is a row of HA⊗B, considered as a concatenation of n strings each of length n . Hence r is a string of n blocks each of which is of length n .
If only one of the blocks in r is not the all zero string then that nonzero block must be a codeword in B⊥, and hence due to minimality of HA⊗B and HB we may consider that block as a row of HB. On the other hand if r has at least two nonzero blocks then due to minimality of HA⊗B and HA we may assume that r is in the form ra ⊗en , where ra is a row of HA and en is a string of length n and Hamming weight one.
This Lemma shows that one can construct a minimal parity check matrix HA⊗B for A ⊗ B based on given minimal parity check matrices HA and HB. The following is one such algorithm constructing a minimal product parity matrix(MPPM).
MPPM Algorithm Given minimal parity check matrices HA and HB of length n and n , respectively, we make a minimal parity check matrix for A ⊗ B by the following process. Step 1 Sort the set of rows in HA and HB according to their Hamming weights in an increasing order. If two rows ra and rb from HA and HB, respectively, have the same Hamming weight then ra precedes rb iff n ≥ n(this affects only the running time of the algorithm). Step 2 Choose the first element v1 in the list. If v1 is a row of HA then set M1 := v1 ⊗In , otherwise define M1 := In ⊗v1. Step 3 Choose the second element v2 in the list. Define M2 :=

The structure of a MPPM is uniquely determined by a string S = (r1, r2, · · · , rn+n −k−k ) consisting of the rows of HA and HB, sorted in terms of their Hamming weight, plus two sets of coordinates A = {α1, α2, · · · , αn−k} and B = {β1, β2, · · · , βn −k } whose corresponding columns in HA and HB, respectively, are independent. IV. THE GIRTH OF A PRODUCT TANNER GRAPH

Theorem 1: The girth of T G(H1) and T G(H2) are lower bounded by min {ga, gb, 8} where ga and gb are the girths of T G(HA) and T G(HB), respectively. Proof Consider a cycle C in T G(H1) involved with at least one copy of T G(HA) and one copy of T G(HB) and assume that C does not contain a subcycle having this property. We show that C is of length at least 8.
Let define the number of connections between two given
matrices of the same length to be the number of columns in
each of which both matrices have at least one nonzero entry.
We also define matrix H by

H := In ⊗ HB + HA ⊗ In

where n and n are the length of A and B, respectively.
It is easy to see that in H each copy of HB has precisely one connection with each copy of HA. From TG point of view this means that in T G(H) each copy of T G(HA) is connected directly with each copy of T G(HB) by exactly one symbol node. This property can be seen in Figure 1.
Therefore, a cycle C in T G(H) having at least one vertex(symbol node or parity node) in a copy of T G(HA) and one vertex in a copy of T G(HB) is involved with at least two copies of T G(HA) and two copies of T G(HB). Thus such a cycle C will be of length at least 8; note that the number
of edges in common between C and each involved copy of T G(HB) is a positive even number.
To complete the proof we just need to consider the difference between H and Hi, i = 1, 2. The T G(H1), for instance, is obtained from T G(H) by simply removing k copies of T G(HA).
Corollary 2: The girth of a MTG of A⊗B is lower bounded by min {ga, gb, 8}, where ga and gb are the girths of T G(HA) and T G(HB), respectively. VI. APPLICATION TO LDPC CODES
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes were introduced first by Gallager in early 1960’s[9]. Due to their impressive performance under iterative decoding, the design and analysis of these codes have been addressed by many coding theorists in recent years (see, e.g.,[3],[12],[15],[16],[19], [20],[21]).

From methods of construction perspective, these codes are di-

vided into random ([3],[9],[16],[19], [20],[21]) and structured

([1],[11],[12],[22], [24]) codes.

As the performance of iterative decoding depends much on

the largeness of the girth of the associated TG, construction

of LDPC codes with large girth, in particular 4-cycle free

codes, is desired. It has been reported that the performance of

irregular LDPC codes under iterative decoding is better than

that of the regular codes[14],[19],[20].

Given two [n, k, d] and [n , k , d ] codes A and B with

parity check matrices HA and HB, respectively, let wa and wb denote the maximum row sums in HA and HB, respectively. Then each row sum in the MPPM of the product code

A ⊗ B is at most equal to max {wa, wb} and hence the density of HA⊗B is bounded above by max {wa, wb} /nn .
This shows that a recursive application of product operation

is indeed a powerful systematic method to construct irregular

LDPC codes. Consider, for instance, the [24, 6, 8] product code

[4, 3, 2] ⊗ [3, 2, 2] ⊗ [2, 1, 2] with parity check matrix H4,3,2 obtained using the MPPM algorithm. The density of H4,3,2,
