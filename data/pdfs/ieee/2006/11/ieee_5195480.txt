Abstract: Since the distance computation is a NP problem, most presented evaluations of LOPC codes are mainly based on the girth numbers of the codes. The computation for the distance of the codes is also depended on the girth numbers. There has been no simple way to obtain the distance of LOPC codes because the code length is very long. Based on generation matrices, this paper develops an algorithm to estimate the weights and distance of LOPC codes . The paper establishes the lower bounds of weights and distances of LOPC codes by using the vectors of generation matrices, which is different from the presented probabilitistic search methods for the weights' and distances of LCPC codes. The proposed algorithm can greatly reduce the searching time of weights and distances . Applying the algorithm we find that some LOPC codes get good BER performance although they are only free girth 4 but of great distances . Simulations verify the algorithms to be valid. Key words: Codes, LOPC coding, distance bounds, weights bounds
1. Introduction
Good LOPC codes should have large girths and large distances [1-5]. Applied on a Tanner graph of a linear block code [2,3], the belief-propagation algorithm gives an exact a posteriori probability mass function for a given probability density function of the observed variables , but only if the factor graph is girth free. The simulation results of MacKay [2] showed that the message-passing algorithm also performs welI in graphs with girths . Since the presence of short cycles may hurt the HER performance of LOPC codes, many papers try to find LOPC codes with large girths such as [4,5]. However, this paper will show that for a class of LOPC codes to be girth 4 free only wilI also have good BER performance if they have large distances.
Until now, the main evaluat ion weights and distances of LOPC codes have been based on probabilitistic search methods [6-10]. A linear (N ,j,k) LOPC code over GF(2) is a
M = Nj / k dimensional subspace of GF(2) having minimum weight
Wmin . The integer Wmin determines the number of errors correctable by the code. The amount of time required to determine
Wmin computationalIy, using known algorithms, increases
exponentialIy with Wmin' and there is evidence to suggest that the minimum weight problem may be NP-complete [II] . In any case

4. Simulations
4.1 Construction of random LDPC codes
A simple method of constructing LDPC codes is implemented by this paper, which operates according to the following steps.
Step 1: Create a preliminary parity check matrix: for each column of the parity check matrix, independently, it places a specified number of 1s in positions selected uniformly at random, with the constraints being that these 1s be in distinct rows as well

to keep the numbers of 1s in the rows approximately the same. It then assigns Is to successive columns by selecting randomly, without replacement, from this initial supply of 1s, subject only to the constraint that the 1s assigned to a column must be in distinct rows. If at some point it is impossible to put the required number of 1s in a column by picking from the 1s remaining, a 1 is set in that column without reference to other columns, creating a possible unevenness.
Step 2: Add 1s to the parity check matrix in order to avoid rows that have no 1s in them, and hence are redundant, or which have only one 1 in them, in which case the corresponding codeword bits will always be zero. The places within such a row to add these 1s are selected randomly.
Step 3: If the preliminary parity check matrix constructed in step 1 had an even number of 1s in each column, add further 1s to avoid the problem that this will cause the rows to add to zero, and hence at least one check will be redundant. Up to two Is are added (since it is also undesirable for the sum of the rows to have only one 1 in it), at positions selected randomly from the entire matrix. However, the number of 1s to add in this step is reduced by the number already added in step 2.
Step 4: If requested, try to eliminate situations where a pair of columns both have 1s in a particular pair of rows, which correspond to cycles of length four in the factor graph of the parity check matrix. When such a situation is detected, one of the 1s involved is moved randomly within its column. This continues until no such situations remain, or until 10 passes over all columns have failed to eliminate all such situations.
Note that regardless of how evenly 1s are distributed in the preliminary parity check matrix created in step 1, step 2 and step 3 can make the numbers of 1s in the both rows and columns be uneven, and step 4, if done, can make the numbers of 1s in rows be uneven.

5. Conclusions
The main theorem s of this paper show that the minimum weight and minimum distance of LDPC codes can be expressed by those of row vectors of generator matrices of LDPC codes, and the NP problem can be solved by the proposed algorithms which are analyzing basis vectors who construct the codes vectors space. Simulation verified the proposed approach to be valid.
