A Design ofLDPC Codes with Large Girth Based on the Sub-matrix Shifting

Jun Fan, Yang Xiao

Institute of Information Science, Beijing Jiaotong University, Beijing 100044, P. R. China E-mail: fanjun562@126.com

Abstract
In this paper, we present an approach for constructing LOPC codes with girth 8. First, we design 3 submatrices with different shifting functions given by our schemes, then combine them into a matrix according to our approach, finalIy, expand the matrix into a desired parity-check matrix using identity matrices and cyclic shift matrices of the identity matrices. The simulation results in AWGN channels show that these codes which can be obtained by the generator matrix derived from this check matrix for encoding the random information bits are as good as random LOPC codes.
Keywords: Communication, LOPC codes, 4-cycles, 6-cycles, tree,
check algorithm.
1 Introduction
Low-density parity-check (LOPe) codes were originally introduced by Gallager [1,2]. They have again become interesting because of the success of iterative decoding for turbo codes [3-7]. LOPC codes have a remarkable performance with iterative decoding that is very close to the Shannon limit over additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels [3-7]. LOPC codes can be described by a bipartite graph calIed Tanner graph [4]. Cycles in the Tanner graphs of LOPC codes prevent the sum-product algorithm from converging [8-16]. Extensive simulation results of MacKay and Neal show that the message-passing algorithm also performs well in graphs with cycles [6]. Most methods for designing LOPC codes are based on random construction techniques, the lack of structure implied by this randomness presents serious disadvantages in terms of storing and accessing a large parity-check matrix, encoding data, and analyzing code performance (e.g. determining a code's distance properties). One of the first attempts to design a deterministic LOPC code for iterative decoding is due to Kou, and it is based on projective and Euclidean geometries , the girth of the associated Tanner graph is six [3]. This LOPC codes can be decoded by belief-propagation algorithms [7]. Recent research results [8-16] about LOPC codes with large girths show that there are many possible ways to construct the LOPC codes with large girths. However, some of the constructions [8-16] are not satisfied for application due to the complicated constraints for the structures of parity check matrices. 2 Definition of the cycle

Gaiiager defined an (n, i . k) LOPC code with a matrix H of
n columns that has j ones in each column, k ones in each row, and zeros elsewhere. It follows from this definition that a (n,j ,k) parity-check matrix has m=nj /k rows [1,2]. A

bipartite graph with m check nodes and n bit nodes can be created with an edge between the bit and check nodes if there is a corresponding I in the parity-check matrix H . Such a graph is frequently called a Tanner graph [4]. A cycle in a Tanner graph refers to a finite set of connected edges that starts and ends at the same node, and satisfies the condition that no node (except the initial and final node) appears more than once [8-16]. The length of the cycle is simply the number of edges it contains, referring Fig.l. The length of the smallest cycle in the graph is called the girth of the graph. The computation of girth at a given node u is easy: a tree is "grown" step by step starting from the "root" u . At step k, alI the nodes at distance k from u are included
into the tree. This procedure is repeated until at step k , a
node connected to at least two nodes included at step k - 1 is included. This identifies the formation of the first cycle. Integer 2k is then the girth at node u . Fig.1 shows a cycle in tree. Fig.2 and Fig.3 shows a 4-cycle and 6-cycle in tree, respectively . We try to design a check matrix with no 4cycles and 6-cycles.

7 Conclusions
In this paper, we presented a method for constructing LDPC codes based on the sub-matrix shifting method. The Tanner graphs of these codes do not contain cycles of length 4 and length 6. BER performance comparisons show our codes are as good as MacKay's random codes [17]. Furthermore, these codes are quasi-cyclic and hence their encoding can be implemented with linear shift registers.
