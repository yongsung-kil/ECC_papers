Abstract— We investigate hard decision based lowdensity parity-check (LDPC) codes over an impulse noise channel, which is modeled as the concatenation of a Binary Erasure Channel (BEC) and a Binary Symmetric Channel (BSC). We propose a decoding algorithm based on three-state quantization of the traditional sum-product algorithm, where the three states correspond to “1”, “0”, and “E” (Erasure). As such, the proposed algorithm dramatically reduces the implementation complexity, while still keeps satisfactory decoding performance as shown by simulation results.
I. INTRODUCTION
Many communication channels suffer from impulse noises on top of traditional Additive White Gaussian Noise (AWGN). Good examples are Digital Subscriber Line (DSL) systems [1] and power line transmission systems [2]. Traditional coding solutions for such channels are to use trellis coded modulation schemes concatenated with outer ReedSolomon codes. The inner trellis codes provide coding gain to combat AWGN while outer ReedSolomon codes offers additional noise immunity, especially against impulse noises [2].
With the recent advances in coding theory [3], [4], there have been several papers proposing iterative decoding schemes for the impulse noise channels. For example, Turbo codes are applied in [5] and regular high rate Low-Density Parity Check (LDPC) codes are deployed in [6] with the sum-product decoding algorithm. These iterative methods have been shown to be quite effective in dealing with impulse noises. However, they usually require high

complexity algorithms such as the sum-product decoding algorithm. In [2], the impulse noise channel is modelled as the concatenation of a Binary Erasure Channel (BEC) (with erasure probability ε) and an AWGN channel. The frequency response of the channel is assumed to be flat. The authors use irregular LDPC codes and propose a soft decision based (belief propagation) sum-product decoding algorithm. The approach for constructing the codes is asymptotically optimal when the codeword length goes to infinity. Unfortunately, finite-length codes constructed in this way exhibit error floors, even in the case of very long codes, such as codes of length 30, 000 in [2]. Irregular codes of such long length are hard to implement due to complexity and delay.
In this paper we propose a hard decision based iterative decoding algorithm with much less complexity. We model the impulse noise channel (also with AWGN) as the concatenation of a BEC and a BSC. We use LDPC code together with a special decoding scheme, which is similar to the so-called “Gallager E” algorithm [7]. This algorithm operates with three states: “0”, “1”, and “E” (Erasure), and the details will be explained in Section III. Due to this hard decision nature, the proposed decoding algorithm has much lower complexity than the sum-product algorithm, with acceptable performance degradation.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. In Section II, the channel model is discussed. In Section III, the decoding algorithm is proposed. In Section IV, numerical simulation results are presented. Concluding remarks are given in Section V.

If we assume that the source is binary with uniform distribution, i.e., ones and zeros are equally likely, the bit sequence after encoding by an LDPC encoder also has ones and zeros uniformly distributed. There are many decoding schemes for LDPC codes. These codes perform the best under the iterative message passing algorithms [7]. Depending on the channel model, there are several variants of the message passing algorithm. For example, in AWGN channels, the sum-product algorithm performs the best. In BSC channels, the “Gallager B” algorithm performs the best [3], [4]. To visualize message passing decoding algorithms for LDPC codes, the parity-check matrix is usually represented as a bipartite graph with two types of vertices called the Tanner graph [12]. The first subset of vertices is comprised of code bits (variables), and the second subset of vertices is comprised of paritycheck equations (see Fig. 2). An edge between a bit and an equation exists if the bit is involved in the check. Let E(v) and E(c) denote the set of edges incident on nodes v and c respectively; an edge will be denoted by e. Also, let r(v) denote the received value of node v.

IV. NUMERICAL RESULTS
To evaluate the performance of the proposed decoding scheme, we use several of MacKay’s randomly-generated LDPC codes [3], [13], the Tanner code [14], and one of the quasi cyclic codes [15]. In order to compare the influences of the code length on performance, all of the codes are chosen to be regular, with column weight 3 and row weight 6.
Codes denoted by “MK” are MacKay’s LDPC codes of lengths 96, 204, 816, and 1008. Rate of all of these codes is 1/2. The Tanner code has length of 155 bits and rate of 0.4129. The quasi cyclic code is denoted by QC(900, 450). It has length of 900 bits and rate of 1/2.
In order to compare the performance with currently used coding techniques we include the decoding performance of two Reed-Solomon codes of length 2040: RS(255, 223) and RS(255, 191) [16].
In Fig. 3, we draw the bit error rate (BER) over the BSC channel cross-over probability for all the considered codes. The probability of erasure is fixed as 0.3%. As it can be seen, the performance of the LDPC codes is worse than the performance of Reed-Solomon codes for low BSC cross-over probabilities. However, if the required frame error rate (FER) is not extremely low the performance is comparable. This is especially true for explicitly constructed (not random) LDPC codes. Lower FER can be achieved with longer (explicitly constructed) LDPC codes.
Note that Reed-Solomon decoders are much more complex and not easily parallelized, such that the decoding delay is on average much higher than that of LDPC decoders. According to [17] the number of gates used in the construction of Reed-Solomon decoders of length 2040 ranges between 115,500 and 161,600 depending on the level of parallelization.

V. CONCLUSIONS
We propose the use of a three-state hard-decision based decoding algorithm for LDPC codes in an impulse noise channel that is modelled as the concatenation of a BSC channel and a BEC channel. Simulation results show that the system performance is robust to different erasure probabilities that represent different levels of impulse noises. The proposed scheme offers great flexibility in terms of code length and code rate, given the large variety of existing LDPC codes; it also leads to much

lower decoder complexity and shorter decoding delay compared with systems based on Reed-Solomon codes.
