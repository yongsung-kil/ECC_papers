Abstract
Low density parity check (LDPC) codes are one of the most powerful error correcting codes known. Recent research have pointed out their potential for a low cost, low latency hardware implementation. Due to this property, a lot of research has been done to find their suitability in different communication media. These codes have been shown to achieve near Shannon-limit performance in Wireless AWGN channels. At the same time these codes can result in significant power reduction in on-chip global and semi-global interconnects. These different applications demand a variety of LDPC coder decoder structures.
This paper presents a programmable design for a parallel implementation of an LDPC decoder. The programability implies that this decoder can adapt itself to any arbitrary LDPC code for a variety of applications. This paper also presents two example configurations, with a throughput of 2.28 Gbps and 4.37 Gbps respectively.
1 Introduction
Almost all communication in today’s world, be it deep space communication or on-chip communication, is digital. The channel over which such communication is carried out is always non-ideal, noisy and adds undesirable errors to the signal transmitted through it. The fidelity of the signal passing through a channel can be preserved in two ways, first is to protect the signal against the error inducing phenomenon, and the second is algorithmic correction of the errors generated.
The signal protection includes schemes like increasing the signal power. However, the recent drive towards low power mobile devices limits any increase of power. In addition to this, the transmission power is minimized to prevent any adverse effect on the other legitimate signals present. In the context of on-chip communication, another method of signal protection is bus-shielding. However, the limited area available for laying out the on-chip wires limits the use

of elaborate bus-shielding set-ups. This, in most cases leaves algorithmic noise tolerance
as the only means of reducing the errors in transmission. The methods of overcoming the existing errors may range from re-transmission to forward error correction schemes like channel coding. Turbo codes are perhaps the most powerful forward error correction tool known. They perform very close to the Shannon’s theoretical limit [1]. However, the hardware implementation of turbo codes is very complex and usually results in costly, low throughput devices.
Low density parity check (LDPC) codes, introduced by Gallager, are a class of linear block codes that perform very close to the turbo codes [2, 3]. In the recent past, several works describing the hardware implementation of the LDPC codes have been published [4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12]. All these works show the potential of the a low cost and low latency implementation of LDPC decoder.
As described in detail later, the LDPC decoding process consists of computing several updates called the check node updates and the variable node updates. The decoder architectures range from those that handle one update at a time (serial) [4] to those that compute all the updates at the same time (parallel)[5, 6]. While the serial architecture has a low hardware cost and are very easy to implement, they suffer from low throughput. On the other hand, while the parallel implementations have a very high throughput, they have a very high hardware resource requirement and have a very complex design. Several other designs that are a combination of the above are presented in [7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12].
The LDPC codes are linear block codes, meaning that the decoder is merely an implementation of a linear parity check matrix H. Different communication systems have different error control requirements, and hence have completely different structures of this matrix H. For the LDPC decoder hardware design to be versatile and reusable in a variety of applications, a easily programmable design is desired.

2 LDPC Codes
Gallager proposed a class of linear block codes that rival the performance of turbo codes. These linear block codes are called the LDPC codes [2, 3]. At the transmitter, redundancy bits are added to a data block of length k to derive a code word of length n. These redundancy bits are parity bits formed by linear combination of the k message bits. The rate of the code is R = k/n.
2.1 LDPC Decoding Process
The receiver has exactly n − k parity check equations to determine the correctness of a received word. These n − k linear equations can be uniquely represented by a parity check matrix H of size (n − k) × n, where the columns represent the received symbols and the rows represent the parity check equations. All elements of this matrix are either 0 or 1. A 1 in the (x, y) position means that the yth received symbol participates in the xth parity check equation.
The H of a regular (n, j, ρ) LDPC code has exactly j(≥ 3) ones in each column and exactly ρ(> j) ones in each row. Let C be the set of n bit long code words, then all

vectors x ∈ C satisfy the relation H.xT = O, where O is a vector having n − k zeros.
An H matrix can also be seen as a bipartite graph, where the rows form the n − k check nodes (CN) on one side and the columns represent the n variable nodes (VN) on the other. A 1 in the (x, y) position means a connection between the yth VN and the xth CN.
The decoding process is an iterative message passing algorithm, where updated messages run sequentially between the two sides of the bipartite graph. At each stage, updates are calculated and are called the VN update (VNU) or the CN update (CNU) respectively. The updates from the VN stage are passed on to the CN stage. The CN stage performs its own updates and passes the signal back to the VN stage.
These updated messages are incrementally improving estimates of the transmitted bits. Each node takes the a priori probabilities of the bits as inputs and calculates the a posteriori probabilities[2] based on them. These probabilities, also known as extrinsics, are expressed as a Log Likelihood ratio (LLR) defined as

2.2 LDPC Decoder Architectures
Several papers showing the implementation of the LDPC decoder have been presented recently. The architectures range from a completely serial architecture [4] to a fully parallel architecture [5, 6]. The serial architecture has the advantage of low hardware requirement. It however, has a low throughput. On the other hand, although the parallel architecture permits high throughput, it is expensive in terms of hardware requirements and complexity. Several other approaches that combine advantages of both have also been developed [7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12].
While all these approaches emphasize the potential of LDPC codes as a powerful, low cost, low latency solution for error control, they also bring out serious concerns that need to be addressed for better decoder design. The most important issues while designing a parallel decoder architecture are
2.2.1 Quantization
The performance of the LDPC code improves with the precision of the LLRs used at the decoder. Even though increasing precision results in better decoder performance, it also means higher hardware requirement and therefore higher cost. Fixing the precision of the LLRs is therefore an optimization problem and is an active research area. This work describes the process of arriving at the optimal precision for LDPC Decoders via simulations for implementation in an additive white gaussian noise (AWGN) channel and a Binary Symmetric Channel (BSC). However, a unified decoder architecture for all different applications should be programmable in terms of the precision of the LLR values carried in it.

2.2.2 Routing Complexity
The parallel design is nothing but the implementation of the bipartite graph, including all the nodes and edges. The two parts of the bipartite graph are connected by exactly n × j edges. Implementing this many edges even for a reasonable length code poses difficult routing problems. Moreover, for each unique H, a different routing solution is required. It is imperative that a basic building structure be designed, that can be applied to various situations with minimal modification. This paper provides a parallel decoding architecture that is built with simple identical programmable blocks. Any length decoders can be built by using arrays of this basic cell. Also, programmability of this basic cell implies that it can be used with any arbitrary H.

3 Programmable Parallel LDPC Decoder

A parallel implementation of the decoder has exactly n × j messages going from one stage to another. An arbitrary structure of H means that these messages can start from any arbitrary location on one side and end up going to any arbitrary position on the other side. Any parallel decoder implementation will involve carrying these n×j messages from one side to the other precisely reordering them to deliver the correct message to the concerned node. This operation is similar to a sorting operation, where the output is arrived at by reordering the input values. Based on this amazing similarity, this work describes the implementation of the channel router in a parallel LDPC decoder using a bubble sort algorithm.
3.1 Bubble Sort Algorithm

The bubble sort algorithm is based on comparison of two consecutive elements of a list. If they are found to be in the right order, they are left untouched, otherwise the values are swapped. Sorting the entire list of length x involves O(x2) such comparisons.
The reordering of n×j messages can be done in a similar fashion by doing O(n2 × j2) one-to-one comparisons. As an illustration, consider the fig. 3, where 6 messages are to be routed from one end to the other. The small cells are switching devices with two inputs and two outputs. The switch has 2 states, it either passes the messages through without swapping them, or alternately, it swaps them. The states of these 13 switches can be altered to route any of the 6 messages from any arbitrary starting position to any arbitrary ending position. Moreover, there is no critical path as all path lengths are equal.

3.4 The Decoder Architecture
The example circuit from fig. 3, that carries 6 messages from one end to another was simulated in SPICE. This involved 13 cells with the longest path passing through 5 cells. Spice simulations show that the latency of the signal passing through the 5 cells is 0.28 ns.
Further, lets consider an example of a (1024, 3, 6) code in an AWGN channel. There are exactly 1024 × 3 =3072 messages going from one stage to another. The longest path will run through 3071 stages. This path is broken down into pipeline stages, with each stage having 20 cells in the longest path. The latency of one such stage, as shown in fig. 6, is 1.12 ns, i.e., it takes 1.12ns for the signal to propagate from one end to another. The stage has roughly 1536 × 20 cells and is shown in fig. 6. 4 Conclusion

This paper addresses the quantization and routing problems in a typical parallel LDPC decoder design and presents a programmable and scalable LDPC decoder. The programmability makes the decoder design very versatile and allows it to adapt itself to any arbitrary H. Two example configurations of the decoder are also presented herein with 1024 bits and 264 bits as code lengths respectively. The throughput generated through them is 2.91 Gbps and 4.37 Gbps.
