Abstract— LDPC codes have excellent performance when iteratively decoded based on sparse parity-check matrices over both the AWGN channel and the erasure channel. In this paper, we propose a simple burst-correction decoding scheme for cyclic LDPC codes based on the same sparse circulant paritycheck matrices used to perform iterative decoding. Although the proposed scheme may not achieve the full burst-correcting capability of the codes, it is rather simple and fast. We also show that the full burst-correcting capabilities of some codes constructed from Euclidean and projective geometries are very close to the Reiger upper bound.
I. INTRODUCTION
Interest in low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, first introduced by Gallager in 1962 [1], has increased dramatically in the last few years, and is expected to continue to increase even more in the future. This is due to their near-Shannon limit (or capacity-approaching) performance when decoded iteratively based on sparse parity-check matrices of the codes. It has been demonstrated that excellent performance can be achieved not only over the AWGN channel but also over the erasure channel [2],[3].
Kou, Lin, and Fossorier [4] discovered a class of LDPC codes, called finite-geometry LDPC code, and have shown that they have good minimum distances and their Tanner graphs [5] are free of cycles of length 4. These properties allow them to perform very well over the AWGN channel with iterative decoding using the sum-product algorithm (SPA) [6]. Since this discovery, considerable success has been achieved in extending the use of finite geometries to construct codes that have good performance over other channels such as the random-erasure channel and the burst-erasure channel [7],[8].
In this paper, we explore the use of cyclic LDPC codes over burst channels. There are classes of efficient cyclic burstcorrecting codes, such as Fire codes, which can be decoded sequentially using error-trapping [6]. However, as stated in [9], sequential schemes may not be suitable for applications where a high speed parallel decoding is required. Such applications include holographic memories and optical bus lines. For these applications, it is preferable to use decoding that can be implemented using combinatorial logic circuitry. In this paper, we propose a simple decoding algorithm that can be easily implemented in this way. Furthermore, this decoder is based on the same sparse parity-check matrices used to decode LDPC codes over the AWGN and the erasure channels. The proposed decoder exploits the sparseness of the parity-check matrices

and the absence of cycles of length four in their associated Tanner graphs to yield good performance with low complexity.
As an example, we determine the burst-correcting capabilities of the proposed decoder when applied to cyclic LDPC codes constructed from Euclidean and projective geometries. These capabilities are less than the full burst-correcting capabilities of the codes, which can be achieved using errortrapping. However, since the proposed decoder never miscorrects any burst that can be decoded using error-trapping, then it can be used to correct bursts within its burst-correcting power and to relegate the correction of longer bursts, which typically occur less frequently, to an error-trapping decoder. This arrangement is interesting in cases in which the full burstcorrecting capabilities of the codes are quite large. We show that this is indeed the case for cyclic LDPC codes constructed from Euclidean and projective geometries. Actually, the full burst-correcting capabilities of some of these codes are very close to the Reiger upper bound [10].
It should be stressed that the goal of this paper is to construct neither burst-correcting codes nor finite-geometry LDPC codes, as the literature is abundant with such constructions, see [6] and the references therein. Rather the goal is (1) to present a simple decoding method for cyclic LDPC codes based on the same parity-check matrices used for decoding over other channels; and (2) to show, through examples, that finite-geometry codes have inherently excellent burstcorrecting capabilities, although these capabilities may not be captured by the simple decoder proposed here.
In Section II we describe circulant parity-check matrices of cyclic LDPC codes. The decoding algorithm is presented in Section III. Circulant parity-check matrices are constructed from Euclidean and projective geometries in Section IV. The burst-correcting capabilities of some codes with such parity-check matrices are listed in Section V followed by a conclusion in Section VI.

VI. CONCLUSION

In this paper we proposed a simple method for burst-error

correction based on sparse parity-check matrices of cyclic

LDPC codes. The motivation is that LDPC codes are known

to perform very well when iteratively decoded using such

matrices. Therefore, it is advantageous to use the same sparse

parity-check matrices for decoding bursts. We emphasize that

our goal is not to construct burst-error correcting codes but

rather to show that LDPC codes, and in particular cyclic LDPC

codes constructed from Euclidean and projective geometries,

are suitable for burst-correction, in addition to their excellent

performance over AWGN and erasure channels.

The decoding technique proposed here essentially reads the

erroneous bits in a burst either directly from the syndrome or

after very simple computations as specified in (2). Basically, if

the ’s in any column or row are far from each other, i.e., the ¦

lengths of the zero-spans are large, then the proposed decoder

will be able to correct long bursts with very small complexity.

The RC-constraint ensures the uniqueness of the maximum-

length zero-span in a column. If this condition is not met, then

the decoder will not be able to correct bursts. In summary, this

decoder exploits the structure and the sparseness of the parity-

check matrix of LDPC codes to achieve good performance

with low complexity.

We should mention that the decoder described in this paper

produces the erroneous bits from the syndrome bits following

its maximum-length zero-span. Other bits in the syndrome may

also be used and this may simplify the computation of the

erroneous bits. It should also be mentioned that although the

proposed algorithm fails to correct bursts of length greater than

, while a sequential decoder can correct bursts of length up
o

to o

Limit, the proposed decoder never miscorrects any burst

of length up to o

Limit. This is because, if it does, then this

would imply that the code has a codeword which is the sum

of two distinct bursts of length o

Limit or less, contradicting

the definition of o

Limit. Hence, if a burst of length m

occurs, then this burst will be corrected by the proposed

decoder if

is useful if the channel causes long bursts.

We also show that o

clidean and projective geometry codes is quite large and is

close to the Reiger upper bound. The excellent performance

of these codes when decoded with the SPA over the AWGN

channel is well documented in the literature [4],[6],[13]. They

also perform well over the randon-erasure channel and the

burst-erasure channel [7],[8]. In fact, an efficient decoding

scheme is developed in [7] that can correct any burst-erasure
