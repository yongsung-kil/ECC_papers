Abstract‚Äî In this paper, we study rate-compatible puncturing of finite-length Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes. First, we derive simple and yet good bounds on the expected performance of punctured codes (constructed by random puncturing) over Binary Erasure Channel (BEC) as a function of the performance of their parent LDPC code. We then present a novel rate-compatible puncturing scheme that is very easy to implement. Our scheme uses the idea that a more uniform distribution of punctured bits across the Tanner graph results in punctured codes with better performance. Although the puncturing scheme tailored to regular codes is presented, it is also directly applicable to irregular parent ensembles. By simulations, the proposed rate-compatible puncturing scheme is shown to be superior to the existing puncturing methods for both regular and irregular LDPC codes over BEC and Additive White Gaussian Noise (AWGN) Channel.
I. INTRODUCTION
In certain time-varying channels where Channel State Information (CSI) is available, using a coding scheme of a fixed (code) rate is not optimal. It is desirable in such conditions that the employed error correcting scheme be flexible in terms of its rate so that one can encode at a higher/lower rate when the channel becomes more/less reliable, respectively. Using several encoder-decoder pairs for achieving this flexibility in rate is, in most cases, undesirable. A practical technique for circumventing the above problem is rate-compatible puncturing. Rate-compatible family of codes derived from a single parent code satisfy the property that the Tanner graph of a code of higher rate is a subgraph of the Tanner graph of any code of a smaller rate.
Puncturing is a technique that has been studied extensively for various classes of error-correcting codes (and in specific, convolutional codes) [1], [2]. For the class of LDPC Codes, asymptotic analysis of the effects of puncturing has been studied by many authors [3]‚Äì[6]. Just as is the case with the design and analysis of finite-length LDPC Codes [7], [8], the design and analysis of punctured LDPC codes of finite-length is also difficult. Ha et al. have previously studied puncturing patterns for finite-length LDPC codes [9], [10]. They have

From the figures, it is clear that punctured codes derived from scheme A perform

Figures 4 and 5 show the deterioration due to puncturing in terms of the difference in probability of erasure required for the punctured codes (from ensembles C1, C3 from scheme A) to attain a BER of 10‚àí5. It is interesting to note that under our scheme the difference in erasure probabilities required for a BER of 10‚àí5 for the mother and the daughter codes is almost identical to the difference in their rates implying that the punctured codes are as good at the lower rate as the parent code is at its rate. It is also interesting to note that the difference between our codes (A) and those of Ha et. al. (B) become stark when the puncturing fraction is higher therefore showing that codes derived from scheme B deteriorate more due to puncturing than ours. In fact, our scheme offers up to almost two orders of magnitude improvement at a higher SNRs (lower Œµ).

We then presented a simple rate-compatible puncturing scheme
