On the rank of LDPC matrices constructed by Vandermonde matrices and RS codes

Ernst M. Gabidulin Moscow Institute of Physics and Technology (State University), Russia
ernst.gabidulin@gmail.com Martin Bossert
Telecommunications and Applied Information Theory, University of Ulm, Germany martin.bossert@uni-ulm.de

Abstract— We calculate the rank of low-density parity-check (LDPC) matrices based on Vandermonde matrix like constructions. In the case of prime fields the rank is given exactly. We show that LDPC codes based on RS codes are a special case of the Vandermonde based construction, thus also for these LDPC matrix construction, the rank caculation is valid. However, for extension fields the calculation is more sophisticated because of the nilpotent property of the parity check matrix. Therefore we can give presently only a bound for the rank in case of binary extension fields.
I. INTRODUCION
Recently several constructions of LDPC codes were published. In [2], [1], [3], [4], and [5], a class of LDPC codes based on Vandermonde-like block matrices are proposed and in [6], a class based on RS-codes. For all constructions it was unknown how to calculate the real rank of the constructed matrix analyticaly and not by performing Gaussian elimination. Here we present an analytical way to determine the rank for LDPC constructions based on Vandermonde matrices. Then we show that the construction from [6] is a special case of Vandermonde constructions, thus also for LDPC codes based on RS-codes the rank calculation is directly possible.
We consider regular LDPC codes and a (γ, ρ)-regular parity check matrix has column and row weights γ and ρ, respectively.
The paper is organized as follows: first we describe the construction of LDPC matrices using Vandermonde matrices. Then we relate this construction to the one based on RS codes, however in a more general description than in [6] by using generator matrices instead of polynomials. This means we do not restrict the construction to cyclic RS codes. Afterwards we calculate the rank of the matrices when prime fields are used. Then we extend the considerations to binary extension fields. For this case we describe some more results, however, for the case of constructions based on binary extension fields the problem of nilpotency of finite order occurs which makes no exact calculation possible. Therefore in this case only a bound is given. The case of prime extension fields is not considered here but is under work and some first results exist.

Corollary 1: The rank of the matrix H is equal to p + (p − 1)2.
Proof: It is clear that the ranks of the matrices H and DH are identical. In the later one, each non zero row of the ith block is linearly independent of the rows of the preceding block. The first block contains p linearly independent rows while, by Lemma 6, the remaining blocks contain p−1 linearly independent rows each. From this the statement of the lemma
follows.

B. General case
If the matrix (II.2) is restricted to the first γ block rows and first ρ block columns, then one gets a (γ, ρ)-regular matrix of size γp × ρp with column and row weights γ and ρ, respectively. We can apply the restriction to the matrix DH.
In this case, rank of a parity check matrix is equal to p + (γ − 1)(p − 1).

Lemma 9: For m = 0, 1, . . . , 2s − 2, the matrix Am has 2s−1 linearly independent eigenvectors of Hamming weight 2 each corresponding to the eigenvalue 1.
Proof: It follows from (IV.15) that all eigenvalues of Am are equal to 1. Let the (i, j)-th entry of Am be ti,j = 1. Then by symmetry tj,i = 1. The vector ai,j = (0, . . . , 0, 1, 0, . . . , 1, 0, . . . , 0) contains only a 1 at positions i and j. The inner products of ai,j and columns of Am are equal to 0 except the products with the i-th and the j-th columns. Therefore ai,j Am = ai,j . The matrix Am contains exactly 2s−1 couples {(i, j), (j, i)} such that ti,j = tj,i = 1. Moreover all such couples are disjoint, i.e. different couples have no common indices i or j.
