A Compact Construction for LDPC Codes using Permutation Polynomials
Oscar Y. Takeshita
Dept. of Electrical and Computer Engineering 2015 Neil Avenue
The Ohio State University Columbus, OH 43210 Takeshita.3@osu.edu

Abstract‚Äî A construction is proposed for low density parity check codes using quadratic permutation polynomials over finite integer rings. Graph isomorphisms and automorphisms are identified and used in an efficient search for good codes. Graphs with girth as large as 12 were found. Upper bounds on the minimum Hamming distance are found algorithmically. The bounds indicate that the minimum distance grows with block length. One of the new codes has a similar error performance as the best known PEG LDPC code. Finally, the new codes are quasi-cyclic.
I. INTRODUCTION
This paper addresses a new construction for the class of capacity-approaching low density parity check (LDPC) codes invented by Gallager [1].
Many LDPC codes were initially designed with random constructions [2]‚Äì[5]. ‚Äúrandomness‚Äù was perceived as an important feature. However, requirements such as ease of implementation, large girth for the associated graph, and better error performance quickly spawned other construction methods. Many good algebraic, combinatoric, and geometric constructions for LDPC codes have been proposed in the literature [6]‚Äì[10]. Additionally, good algorithmic constructions have also been proposed [11]. Most constructions focus on the maximization of the girth of the associated graph and employ computer simulations for validation. We follow the same path in this paper. One of the advantages of the new construction is a very compact description of a code by permutation polynomials over finite integer rings [12]. A similar technique has successfully produced some impressively good turbo codes with several interesting and useful characteristics [12]‚Äì[15]. Another construction that has a similar compact description was proposed in [16] and extended in [17].

In this paper, we investigate an LDPC construction when f (¬∑) is a quadratic permutation polynomial over integer rings [12], [14].

Theorem 1 also leads to an intuitive design rule: by minimizing the number of equivalent classes, the code may look less uniform and more ‚Äúrandom.‚Äù This is achieved by selecting f2 as small as possible. We have redefined the notion of randomness by a new non-linearity measure for interleavers in [13]. Following Proposition 1, we may select f2 to be the product of every prime factor of N repeated exactly once, say this number is f2min.1 Moreover, increasing f2 with more factors of N also means f2 approaches N , eventually becoming a multiple of N and hence zero modulo N . The QPP then reduces to a linear permutation polynomial [19], [20] losing the ‚Äúrandomness‚Äù that the nonlinear quadratic polynomial provides. Our search for good coefficients confirmed this trend where the girth of the resulting graph increases with f2 but only up to a certain point. Often, f2min is the value that maximizes the girth of the corresponding graph.

C. A Search Procedure for Good Coefficients
We give an outline of an efficient search procedure for graphs with large girth using Propositions 3 and Theorem 1.
1) Choose the degree distribution (Œª, œÅ) 2) Choose the code size (n, k) and set the interleaver
length N = nŒª 3) Set f2 to be the product of every prime factor of
N repeated exactly once 4) Search the f1 that maximizes the girth of the
corresponding graph using Propositions 3 and Theorem 1 5) Try a larger f2 by including more factors2 of N and repeat the previous step. If no improvement in girth is noted, proceed to the next step otherwise repeat this step 6) Compute the rank of the parity check matrix to find the true dimension of the code

In addition to degree-6 check-nodes, there are checknodes whose degrees are 5, 7 and 8 in some small quantities. We proposed a new construction for regular LDPC codes using quadratic permutation polynomials (QPP) over finite integer rings. The new construction only requires the code size (n, k), the degree distribution (Œª, œÅ) of the associated bipartite graph with N edges,

and two integers representing the coefficients of a QPP f (x) = f1x + f2x2 (mod N ) as the code defining parameters. The new construction provides virtually the shortest description for an LDPC code among all known constructions in the literature. The algebraic structure of the new codes allows the identification of graph isomorphisms and automorphisms, which significantly simplify the search for the coefficients of f (x) that yield good parameters for the codes. The degree of f (x) being two ensures a non-linearity in the graph structure, which we believe makes it as good as random constructions with the additional advantages of an algebraic construction. LDPC codes with the corresponding bipartite graphs with girths as large as 12 are easily obtained. Further, we show in [18] that the new codes are quasi-cyclic. Algorithmic upper bounds on the minimum distance were computed for the new codes. The new codes appear to have a minimum distance growing with the block length as opposed to a fixed small upper bound on the minimum distance of 24 for a particular class of quasi-cyclic LDPC codes investigated in [23]. Computer simulations show the new codes have excellent error rate performance similar to the best known codes such as PEG LDPC codes [11].
