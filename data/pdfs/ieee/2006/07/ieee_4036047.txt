Abstract— The design of generalized low-density parity-check (GLDPC) codes at both check and bit nodes over AWGN channel is considered in this paper. The new codes are referred to as doubly generalized LDPC codes. EXIT charts are used to optimize the parameters of these codes. Both analysis and simulations show that this method can provide more flexibility in constructing codes with good threshold.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, originally proposed by Gallager [1], have received significant attention since rediscovered by MacKay and Neal [2]. A standard LDPC code is characterized by the random connection between variable nodes and check nodes, which can be viewed as repetition codes and single parity check (SPC) codes, respectively. Tanner generalized this principle by replacing SPC codes with other short subcodes [3]. Hamming codes are used as subcodes in [4], and BCH codes are also considered in [5][6]. GLDPC codes with Reed-Solomon (RS) codes as component codes are also considered in [6]. Low-rate GLDPC codes with Hadamard constraints whose performance is very close to the ultimate Shannon limit are proposed in [7]. In [8], a hybrid construction is presented for the Gilbert-Elliott channel, where the check nodes are composed of a mixture of SPC codes and Hamming codes. Hybrid constructions for the AWGN channel have been studied in [9]. All these constructions are based on strengthening the check node component codes at the expense of a rate loss. In [10], GLDPC codes referred to as molecular codes are constructed by allowing both variable and check nodes to represent codes more general than repetition and SPC codes and regular molecular codes are mainly discussed. In this paper, GLDPC codes more closely related to conventional LDPC codes and with more diversities in variable and check nodes are studied. We refer to them as doubly GLDPC codes. Extrinsic mutual information transfer (EXIT) charts [11] are used to optimize the parameters of the codes. From curve matching obtained from EXIT charts, we observe that the threshold of the new codes is much better than that of the original GLDPC codes with the same component codes in the check nodes and the same rate. Compared with irregular LDPC codes with the same maximum variable node degree and the same rate, the new codes can also have a better threshold.

Let the number of super variable and check nodes be N and M , respectively, the node degrees be dv1, dv2, . . . , dvN and dc1, dc2, . . . , dcM , respectively, and the corresponding subcodes be (dv1, kv1), (dv2, kv2), . . . , (dvN , kvN ) and (dc1, kc1), (dc2, kc2), . . . , (dcM , kcM ), respectively. In [14], it is shown that the EXIT curve of the variable nodes must match with that of the check nodes in order to approach capacity for the BEC. This curve-fitting technique also works well for BPSK signaling over an AWGN channel. Using this technique, two doubly GLDPC codes have been constructed, a rate-3/4 length-100000 code C1 and a rate7/15 length-7650 code C2. C1 uses (6,1), (6,3), (6,4), and (6,5) codes as subcodes for the super variable nodes with λ1 = 0.69, λ2 = 0.01, λ3 = 0.22, λ4 = 0.08, respectively, and (12,11) SPC codes for all super check nodes. Fig. 7 depicts its curve fitting at its threshold 1.9dB. C2 uses (6,1), (6,2), (6,4), and (6,5) codes as subcodes for the super variable nodes with λ1 = 0.425, λ2 = 0.075, λ3 = 0.075, λ4 = 0.425, respectively, and (15,11) Hamming codes for all super check nodes. Fig. 8 depicts its curve fitting at its threshold 0.3dB. Compared with the threshold 0.88dB of a (2,15) GLDPC code with the same rate, it improves by 0.58dB. The improvement of threshold results from introducing the (6,5) SPC code in the super variable nodes, whose EXIT curve is convex. V. SIMULATION RESULTS
Fig. 9 depicts the performance comparison of C1 with a rate-3/4 and length-100000 LDPC code constructed by the progressive edge-growth (PEG) method [16]. The variable node degree distribution of this LDPC code is λ(x) = 0.252458x+0.074827x2 +0.672715x4, which is the optimum one returned by [17] when the maximum left degree is set to 6, i.e. the same value as C1. The threshold of this LDPC code is about 2.0dB. For both codes, IMax = 200. The doubly GLDPC code C1 outperforms its LDPC counterpart, especially in the error floor region.
Fig. 10 depicts the performance comparison of C2 with a rate-7/15 length-7680 (2,15) GLDPC code, both with IMax = 50: C2 outperforms its counterpart by about 0.35dB.
