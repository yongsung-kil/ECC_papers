Efficient Encoding and Termination of Low-Density Parity-Check Convolutional Codes
Zhengang Chen, Stephen Bates, Duncan Elliott and Tyler Brandon Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering University of Alberta, Edmonton, T6G 2V4, Canada
Email: {zgchen, sbates, elliott, brandon}@ece.ualberta.ca

Abstract— Low-density parity-check convolutional codes (LDPC-CCs) have been shown to have similar capacityapproaching performance to LDPC block codes. Their encoder structure is simple and efficient. However, the encoder termination, which is required when applied to finite length data frames, increases the encoder complexity and reduces the effective code rate. The LDPC-CC encoding and termination problems are discussed in this paper. A novel all-phase termination scheme is proposed with less implementation complexity and less loss in code rate, compared to existing methods. Finally a system architecture for the LDPC-CC encoder with all-phase termination is given with some analyses.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check convolutional codes (LDPC-CCs), although less renowned than LDPC block codes (LDPC-BCs), are gradually attracting more attention due to both theoretical studies and implementation efforts. LDPC-CCs were first proposed in [1] as time-varying convolutional codes defined by a low-density parity-check matrix. Using similar iterative message passing decoding algorithms, LDPC-CCs can achieve performance comparable to LDPC-BCs [1]. It has been shown in [2] that the free distance of regular LDPC-CCs increases linearly with their memory, i.e. they are asymptotically good. On the implementation side, LDPC-CC encoders and decoders have very different architectures than LDPC-BCs. The original encoder structure based on shift registers proposed in [1] is very simple compared to the encoder of randomly constructed LDPC-BCs. The iterative decoding of LDPC-CCs can be done through a pipelined architecture [1], [3]. In [4], a few comparisons were carried out and LDPC-CCs have shown comparable performance under the same implementation constraints as LDPC-BCs.
As the name suggests, the fundamental property of LDPCCCs is that they are “convolutional”, which implies that they can operate over arbitrary lengths of data bits and they can be continuously encoded/decoded. This property makes LDPCCCs suitable candidates for streaming applications as well as packet based communication systems with variable length frames [5]. However, this also brings an extra requirement when applied to finite length data frames: the encoder has to be driven back to the all-zero state to conclude a frame. This is known as termination. For conventional convolutional codes, the feedforward encoder can be terminated by feeding zeros to its input. In contrast, LDPC-CC encoders cannot be terminated in such a simple way because of their special

structure. A sequence of “tail bits” has to be used, whose pattern is determined by the encoder state. Obtaining such a termination sequence generally involves solving a number of linear equations [5]. Possible termination methods have been discussed in [5], [6]. In this paper, the termination problem is further investigated. For the first time an efficient termination scheme capable of terminating the encoder from all phases is proposed. The new termination scheme can be readily incorporated into the LDPC-CC encoder architecture for hardware implementation. Compared with existing termination methods, both the implementation complexity and the rate loss caused by the termination overhead are improved.
The paper is organized as follows: In Section II we introduce the LDPC-CC definition and the encoder structure. The existing termination methods are discussed in Section III. In Section IV the all-phase termination method is proposed and analyzed. Conclusions are given in Section V.

III. LDPC-CC ENCODER TERMINATION
In most real world applications, a data frame has a start and an end. Therefore so must the LDPC-CC encoder and decoder. As mentioned in Section II, the encoder starts from the all-zero state. At the receiver, the pipelined decoder makes the same assumption and the LLRs in the decoder FIFO delay lines are initialized to infinity, representing deterministic zeros. (The LDPC-CC decoder architecture was discussed in [1].)
Similarly, termination drives the encoder back to the all-zero state by appending tail bits (called the termination sequence) to the frame. Termination is essential for good error performance near the end of the frame [5]. Two termination methods have been proposed so far ([5], [6]), based on two different encoder structures. Both of them involve solving linear equations based on the encoder state. In this paper we focus on the termination method based on the direct encoder structure in Fig. 1, [1], [5]. Take systematic rate-1/2 codes as an example in solving the termination problem. Suppose the length of the input information bit frame u is n. The encoded frame is v = {u(0), v(0), · · · , u(n−1), v(n−1)}. At time t = n, the last bit of u has been sent to the encoder, and the termination sequence starts to be fed to the input of the encoder. Although the bits for termination are not necessarily all zeros, the encoder is supposed to reach the all-zero state in finite time. Therefore, at some point during termination, the encoder reaches a “partialzero” state, where a zero information bit input will always generate a zero parity bit output. From this partial-zero state, at most ms information bits are needed to flush the encoder to the all-zero state. These ms zero bits do not need to be transmitted, because they are deterministic zeros. The decoder can simply generate infinite LLRs as input to the FIFO delay lines after the partial-zero state is reached. Based on this fact, the ms flushing zeros are not counted in the termination overhead. This partial-zero state is actually the state at which the partial syndrome former defined in [6] is all-zero.

That is Ts × L × 2ms. Usually Ts and L are close to ms, making the storage requirement O(m3s ). This is too high for building practical LDPC-CC encoders. On the other hand, if only one phase is chosen to be the starting phase of termination, a single F matrix needs to be saved. Since a variable length frame could end at any phase, padding becomes necessary to reach a specific phase before the termination starts, which will further reduce the effective code rate.

IV. LDPC-CC ENCODER WITH ALL-PHASE TERMINATION
A. A new termination approach
An LDPC-CC encoder capable of termination from all phases without padding is desirable because it minimizes the rate loss. It has been proved in [2] that almost all the (ms, J, 2J) rate-1/2 regular LDPC-CCs can be terminated with a tail length no greater than 2(ms + 1) . Also, the linear equation defined by (7) is solvable if L ≥ ms and D is full rank. Although the solution existence proof for the entire LDPCCC ensemble has not been given, it is very unlikely that the system is unsolvable at any phase. In addition, if the code can be terminated from one phase, it can also be terminated from any other phase through padding. In this paper, we make a reasonable assumption that all the LDPC-CCs discussed can be terminated from all phases and leave the general proof to future study. Actually, all the simulated LDPC-CCs in this paper can be terminated from all phases when choosing L to be slightly larger than ms: L = ms +∆, ∆ ms. In the following discussion, we always assume that the linear system defined by (7) is consistent
As the LDPC-CC encoder is also an FSM, an LDPC-CC can be categorized as a trellis code from the encoding point of view. The trellis starts from the all-zero state and termination is supposed to bring it back to the all-zero state. The solution xT = F φ,LsT corresponds to a path in the trellis starting from state s and ending at a partial-zero state.
Instead of preserving the entire ending state vector, generating the L termination bits and feeding them to the encoder, we can perform the process iteratively. At the ending state of the encoder (s(n)), the first termination bit z(0) = x(0) is obtained from (8) using the first row of F φ(n),L and is fed to the encoder. After the encoder state is updated , the second termination bit z(1) = x(2) is again obtained from (8), with a different state vector s(n + 1), different phase φ (n + 1) and thus different termination matrix F φ(n+1),L. In the state trellis, each step is a single state transition. After each transition, the next transition is recalculated based on the current state. This process forms a termination path in the code trellis, as illustrated in Fig. 2. We call this approach all-phase termination.

The storage requirement for the termination matrices is Ts × 2ms (not dependent on L), i.e., O(m2s ), making all-phase termination a feasible option. Compared to single-phase termination, all-phase termination saves padding processing units at both the encoder and decoder. Although the termination matrices are not sparse, synthesis results on ms = 128 and ms = 512 LDPC-CCs show that the encoder with termination is still very small compared to the iterative decoder and will not be a bottleneck for the throughput.
D. Analysis
The all-phase termination is simulated on different LDPCCCs with memory ms from 128 to 2048. The termination length L is chosen to be just large enough to make the linear system defined by (7) consistent for every phase. The choice of L is code dependent, but for all the codes simulated, L is only slightly larger than ms. For instance, L = 134 for a rate-1/2 (128,3,6) LDPC-CC. The termination matrices are generated by using Gaussian elimination with all the free variables set to zero, and only the first row of each termination matrix is saved. For verification purposes, the number of encoder states are tremendously high when ms is large. Instead of traversing all the states, we randomly generate variable length frames and simulate encoding and termination them with all-phase termination. No failure has been observed on the LDPC-CCs simulated, including rate-1/2 and rate-2/3 codes constructed differently [1], [7].

V. CONCLUSIONS
The encoding and termination of LDPC convolutional codes have been discussed. Based on existing methods, we have proposed an efficient scheme capable of termination from all possible phases. It reduces the termination overhead, and can be applied to LDPC-CCs with different rates and different construction methods. Initial efforts on hardware implementation have also shown that it can be built into circuits with acceptable complexity.
