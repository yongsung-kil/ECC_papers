EXIT Chart Analysis for Doubly Generalized LDPC Codes
Yige Wang and Marc Fossorier Department of Electrical Engineering University of Hawaii at Manoa, Honolulu, HI 96822 Email: yige, marc@spectra.eng.hawaii.edu

AbstractŌĆö The design of generalized low-density parity-check (GLDPC) codes at both check and bit nodes over AWGN channel is considered in this paper. The new codes are referred to as doubly generalized LDPC codes. EXIT charts are used to optimize the parameters of these codes. Closed forms of EXIT functions for some subcodes are presented. Both analysis and simulations show that this method can provide more flexibility in constructing codes with good threshold.

I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, originally proposed by Gallager [1], have received significant attention since rediscovered by MacKay and Neal [2]. A standard LDPC code is characterized by the random connection between variable nodes and check nodes, which can be viewed as repetition codes and single parity check (SPC) codes, respectively. Tanner generalized this principle by replacing SPC codes with other short subcodes [3]. Hamming codes are used as subcodes in [4], and BCH codes are also considered in [5][6]. GLDPC codes with Reed-Solomon (RS) codes as component codes are also considered in [6]. Low-rate GLDPC codes with Hadamard constraints whose performance is very close to the ultimate Shannon limit are proposed in [7]. In [8], a hybrid construction is presented for the Gilbert-Elliott channel, where the check nodes are composed of a mixture of SPC codes and Hamming codes. Hybrid constructions for the AWGN channel have been studied in [9]. All these constructions are based on strengthening the check node component codes at the expense of a rate loss. In [10], GLDPC codes referred to as molecular codes are constructed by allowing both variable and check nodes to represent codes more general than repetition and SPC codes and regular molecular codes are mainly discussed. In this paper, GLDPC codes more closely related to conventional LDPC codes and with more diversities in variable and check nodes are studied. We refer to them as doubly GLDPC (DGLDPC) codes.
Extrinsic mutual information transfer (EXIT) chart [11]-[13] is an effective way to analyze the performance of an LDPC code. The closed form of check node EXIT functions is given in [14] and [15]. In [16], a general expression is derived for extrinsic mutual information at the output of a MAP decoder using Taylor series expansion. Two decoding models are constructed in [17], based on which EXIT functions for BEC can be calculated through information functions. A concept termed information combining is discussed in [19]-[21],

II. STRUCTURE OF DGLDPC CODES
The Tanner graph of DGLDPC codes can be obtained from that of LDPC codes with original variable and check nodes replaced by super variable and check nodes, respectively. The super variable and check nodes are actually subcodes with lengths equal to the degrees of their correponding super nodes, as shown in Fig. 1. V. SIMULATION RESULTS
Fig. 9 depicts the performance comparison of C1 with a rate7/15 length-7680 (2,15) GLDPC code, both with IMax = 50: C1 outperforms its counterpart by about 0.35dB.
Fig. 10 depicts the performance comparison of C2 with an optimized rate-1/2 length-1504 (2,4)-LDPC code over GF(16) [24]. C2 outperforms its counterpart by about 1dB at the WER of 10ŌłÆ6 due to a lower error floor. The computational complexity per check node of C2 is O(dc ├Ś 2dcŌłÆkc ) = O(15 ├Ś 215ŌłÆ11) = O(15├Ś16), while that of the (2,4)-LDPC code over GF(q) is O(dc ├Ś qlog2q) = O(4 ├Ś 16log216) = O(16 ├Ś 16). So the computational complexity in check nodes of these two codes is comparable. The variable node processing remains lower. The low error floor of C2 is mainly due to the improved minimum distance by introducing Hamming codes in the check nodes. This leads to a loss of rate, but since for a DGLDPC code subcodes are also used in the variable nodes, we can make up for the rate loss.
