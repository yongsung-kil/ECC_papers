On Stopping Criteria for Low-Density Parity-Check Codes

D. Alleyne and J. Sodha, Member, IEEE
Department of Computer Science, Mathematics and Physics University of the West Indies, Bridgetown, Barbados damien.alleyne@gmail.com, jsodha@ieee.org

Abstract-- A stopping criterion for short-length Low-Density Parity-Check Codes (LDPC) is developed based on accumulating evidence which indicates whether the decoder should be allowed to iterate beyond a predefined iteration limit that is lower than the normal maximum number of decoder iterations. Without any additional decoding complexity, we find that the average number of iterations at low SNRs can be reduced without sacrificing performance. Simulation results are presented for the rate ½ (288, 576) and the rate ½ (1152, 2304) WiMAX 802.16e LDPC code over an AWGN channel with binary phase shift keying (BPSK) modulation.
Index Terms - Stopping Criteria, LDPC Codes, Iterative Decoding.
I. INTRODUCTION
The decoder for a Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) [1]-[2] code will iterate until either a valid code word has been found or the predefined maximum number of iterations Nmax has been reached before stopping. To avoid unnecessary iterations, and thereby reduce the decoding latency and receiver power consumption, recent research has focused on stopping criteria for LDPC codes [3]-[4]. The research presented in this paper is based on the idea of using the number of satisfied parity-check constraints N spc(l) after the lth iteration to establish a
stopping criterion as in [3]. However, unlike the strategy adopted in [3], which relies on the oscillation of N spc(l)
to fade away before making a stopping decision, we present a method which accumulates evidence on whether

the decoder should be allowed to iterate beyond a
predefined iteration limit that is lower than Nmax . Our motivation is to develop a stopping criterion that can quickly identify slow convergence [3] for a given LDPC code.

In the next section, we present details of the practical WiMAX 802.16e standard LDPC Code [5] that will be used to test our stopping criterion. Thereafter, in Section III, the stopping criterion is presented with the simulation results in Section IV, V and VI. Finally, we present our conclusions in Section VII.

VI. RATE ½ (1152, 2304) LDPC CODE
In this section we present the simulation results for the rate ½ (1152, 2304) WiMAX LDPC code. To determine
the optimum thresholds ( Tup , Tlow ), the variables of the
stopping criterion algorithm were set to Nmin = 10 ,
Nmax = 20 , d = 2 , Ndec = 15 , Tup = 1125 : −25:1050 and
Tlow = 950 : 25 :1100 , where a : b : c indicates a range of numbers between a and c in steps of b. To determine the optimum parameters for Shin et. al.’s algorithm, the variables were set to θd = 12:-2:6, θmax = 6:-1:3 and θspc = 1125:-25:950. The parameters which minimized the ANI at low SNRs, and only slightly degraded the Pe at high SNRs, were selected to be the optimum values. The corresponding simulation results are presented in Figs. 9 and 10, where our proposed stopping criterion not only provides a near optimum Pe performance, but also has the smallest ANI at low SNRs.

Shin et. al.’s algorithm has a slightly better Pe performance at high SNRs but incurs not surprisingly, the
penalty of an increased ANI at low SNRs.

VII. CONCLUSIONS

Unlike the algorithm presented in [3], which relies on

monitoring the level of oscillation of

N

(l spc

)

to develop a

stopping criterion, we have presented an algorithm which

accumulates evidence in favor of iterating the received

noisy word towards a valid codeword. The advantage is

that we need not wait until the oscillation of N spc(l)

becomes small enough to make a stopping decision. The

N spc(l) variation is a good indicator of whether further

iterations of the decoder will significantly help to

eliminate decoding errors. Indeed, the algorithm

presented in this paper is of course only one of many that

could be devised to utilize the N spc(l) variation. Ongoing
research will focus on utilizing the received noisy code digits to estimate the channel SNR and thereby automatically optimize the stopping criterion’s parameters for a given channel SNR. The aim is to achieve the smallest ANI at low SNRs and a near optimum Pe performance over all SNRs.
