Efficient Layers-Based Schedules for Iterative Decoding of LDPC Codes

Noam Presman, Eran Sharon and Simon Litsyn School of Electrical Engineering, Tel Aviv University, Ramat Aviv 69978 Israel;
e-mail: {presmann,eransh,litsyn}@eng.tau.ac.il

Abstract— Efficient serial decoding schedules for LDPC codes are described. The schedules are based on dividing the Tanner graph to sub-graphs. This yields an improvement in complexity and performance over the standard schedules. An application of the introduced schedules to decoding codes based on lifted graphs is described. An analysis based on density evolution is presented and is used to predict the behavior of different schedules.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes exhibit near Shannon limit performance under low-complexity iterative message passing decoding algorithms. These algorithms use a bipartite graph defining the code, called Tanner graph. In the decoding, the nodes of the graph exchange messages with their neighbors. The order of passing the messages between the nodes is referred to as a schedule. The message passing schedule directly affects the algorithm’s convergence rate (which is inversely proportional to expected number of iterations necessary for convergence to a code-word) and hence its complexity. The standard schedule is the flooding schedule in which each iteration is divided into two phases: in the first one all the variable nodes send messages to their neighboring check-nodes, and in the next phase the check-nodes send messages to their neighboring variable nodes. Another type of more efficient schedules are serial schedules [2], [5], [9], [13]. Simulations and analysis show that at low signal-to-noise ratio conditions where the number of iterations is high, serially scheduled decoders converge in approximately half the number of the iterations (compared to flooding) on the average. We say that a schedule S has the speedup factor of q, if the quotient of the convergence rate of S and the convergence rate of flooding is q. Hence, the typical speedup factor of the serial schedules at the limit of large number of iterations is about 2.
In this paper we propose a family of schedules which generalizes the serial schedules. This family can provide speedup factors greater than 2 without compromising the code’s error correction performance. Simulations of schedules from this family, on several standardized LDPC codes show that they converge (on the average) in about 70% of the number of iterations it took the serial schedules to converge over the BEC, and about 80% over the AWGN channel. This yields higher decoding throughput or smaller processing logic and power by the same factor. For the proposed schedules, there exists an efficient implementation with similar computational and storage complexities as in the serial schedules. We also suggest how to incorporate them in decoding of codes from

lifted graphs. Such codes are used in practical standardized applications, since this structure enables efficient implementation of high throughput decoders, e.g. [5],[6]. For this construction we analyze the asymptotic expected behavior of the schedule using a Density Evolution technique.
In an accompanying paper [11], we provide a combinatorial theoretical analysis of the computation tree evolution under the proposed schedules. We derive exact expressions for the distribution of the computation tree depth, study its asymptotic properties (in the number of iterations) and prove that it is asymptotically concentrated around its expected value. The analysis shows that impressive acceleration factors in the computation tree growth rate can be obtained and these are manifested in a similar acceleration in the schedule’s convergence rate. Based on the analysis we identify LDPC ensembles for which appropriate generalized serial schedules can give speedup factors of up to 6. We further propose specific constructions of LDPC codes that can achieve the predicted convergence rates under the appropriate schedules.
The presentation is organized as follows. In Section II we begin by presenting the relevant background and notations that are used throughout the paper. Then in Section III we introduce the family of layers-based schedules, and present simulation results on the convergence time on some graphs. In Section IV we describe an efficient implementation for general schedules. In Section V we describe how schedules can be used with codes that are generated by graph lifting.
II. PRELIMINARIES
An LDPC code C of length n is a binary linear block code defined by a parity-check matrix Hm×n. An equivalent representation of the code is by bipartite (Tanner) graph G = (V ∪ C, E), where the set V consists of variable nodes vi, i ∈ [n], and the set C consists of check nodes node cj, j ∈ [m]. An edge connects a variable node vi to a check node cj iff Hj,i = 1. For a node u in the graph we denote by N (u) the set of nodes that are connected to the node u by an edge. Also, it is useful to denote by Gd = (Vd Cd, Ed) the directed graph induced by the undirected graph G, which is defined as follows:

V. LAYERS-BASED SCHEDULES AND LIFTED GRAPHS
LDPC codes that are based on lifted graphs are considered very good as they hold a regularity that can be utilized in an efficient hardware implementation of a decoder [5],[6] and are amenable to optimization [10].

The DE algorithm is computationally involved. Using straightforward development that is based on our presentation, one may adjust the other methods of LDPC performance analysis such as Gaussian approximation for the lifted ensemble. One may also analyze the behavior of different schedules over the the same base graph in terms of the speed of convergence and error-behavior. It is also possible to evaluate different ensembles based on different base-graphs. Simulations [7] show that, at least on the BEC, even for small M the DE algorithm usually gives very close results to the one observed in simulations on a specific realization of the graph.
VI. CONCLUSIONS
The layers-based schedules improving on the standard schedules are introduced. They can be efficiently implemented, in particular for the codes obtained by graph-lifting.
