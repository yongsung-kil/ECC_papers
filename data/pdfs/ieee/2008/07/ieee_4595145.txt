Abstract— In this paper, we propose a new construction of (3, t)-regular low-density parity-check convolutional codes by properly including binomial entries in the parity-check matrices. Both of the upper and lower bounds on free distance are derived for the new codes. Compared with previous constructions, our design can not only avoid codes of girth less than 8 but also provide enlarged free distances for some code rates. Simulation results show that the codes based on the new construction can achieve better bit-error-rate performance and lower error floor.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes were first invented by Gallager [1] in early 1960s. Although they were ignored for about thirty years, probably except the graph representation by Tanner [2] in 1981, the rediscovery of LDPC codes in 1990s [3] has sparkled a flurry of interests in the related research. LDPC codes can achieve near-Shannon-limit performance with iterative massage-passing decoding [4] and sufficiently long block length. However, most of good LDPC codes are based on random constructions, which can bring many disadvantages in encoding and decoding due to the need of storing and accessing a large unstructured paritycheck matrix. Quasi-cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes [5]– [13], whose parity-check matrices are usually composed of permutation matrices, can be encoded efficiently with simple feedback shift-registers. The algebraic structure of QC-LDPC codes also allows for an efficient high-speed very-large-scaleintegration (VLSI) implementation. With QC-LDPC codes as the counterpart, LDPC convolutional codes (LDPC-CCs) [11]– [19] are convolutional codes with sparse parity-check matrices. By using a modified iterative message-passing algorithm, LDPC-CCs can be decoded continuously with only a small delay [12], [15], and the repetitive nature of the decoder architecture makes it well-suitable for VLSI implementation. Moreover, LDPC-CCs can provide better bit-error-rate (BER) performance than their QC-LDPC counterparts [12].
A construction of LDPC-CCs with parity-check matrices consisting of monomials only was proposed in [11], [12]. The convolutional code counterparts of the type-II QC-LDPC codes proposed in [13] have parity-check matrices allowing binomial entries. Even though a larger upper bound on minimum distance (or free distance) than that in [11], [12] was claimed in [13], not all codes considered in [13] can truly have a larger bound. Also there is no guarantee of girth larger than 6 for codes in [13]. In this paper, we propose a new

explicit construction of (3, t)-regular LDPC-CCs with paritycheck matrices consisting of binomial entries. Our constructed codes can indeed have a larger bound on free distance than that in [11], [12]. Furthermore, lower bounds on free distance are also given. We can also guarantee the constructed codes have girth at least 8. From computer simulations, our new codes have better BER performance and lower error floor than previously constructed codes at high signal-to-noise ratios (SNRs).
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Basics of LDPC-CCs together with previous constructions are described in Section II. Section III presents the new construction of (3, t)-regular LDPC-CCs, where both of the upper and lower bounds on free distance are derived. Moreover, we provide conditions for the constructed codes to have girth at least 8. Simulation results are shown in Section IV for performance verification. Finally, the work is concluded in Section V. Please note that details of most of the proofs in the paper are omitted due to the length limitation.

The scalar parity-check matrix of an LDPC-CC satisfies the

following properties [16]: (1) each column has s 1’s; (2) each

row has t 1’s except the first (n − k)M rows; (3) s and

t are small compared to (n − k)M and nM . The number

of 1’s t in a row of the scalar matrix is exactly the weight

sum in a single row in the polynomial form, and this also

holds for each column. The LDPC-CC defined above is called

an (s, t)-regular LDPC-CC. If the elements of H(D) are all

monomials, we can have the degree parameters t = n and

s = n − k.

Similar to LDPC block codes, a Tanner graph based on the

parity-check matrix can be obtained for LDPC-CCs. It is also

a bipartite graph with two sets of nodes representing check

nodes and variable nodes, respectively. Since the scalar parity-

check matrix of the LDPC-CC is infinite and repeated, the

corresponding Tanner graph extends infinitely with repeated

nodes in the same structure [12].

For (s, t)-regular QC-LDPC codes constructed from mono-

mials, it can be shown that the minimum distance is upper-

bounded by (s + 1)! [6]. Hence the free distance for LDPC-

CCs associated with these (s, t)-regular QC-LDPC codes is

also limited by (s + 1)!.

In [13], type-II QC-LDPC codes were considered, with

parity-check matrices consisting of monomials, binomials, and

zeros. It was shown in [13] that the upper bounds on minimum

distance for (3, 4) and (3, 5)-regular type-II QC-LDPC codes

are 32 and 28, respectively. The upper bounds on free distance

for the associated (3, 4) and (3, 5)-regular LDPC-CCs are also

32 and 28, respectively. These bounds are larger than those by

(s + 1)! for codes in [11], [12]. However, not all (3, 4) and

(3, 5)-regular LDPC-CCs with binomials considered in [13]

can really have the larger upper bound on free distance. For

example, consider a (3, 4)-regular LDPC-CC of rate 1/4 with

IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
In this section, computer simulations are conducted for iterative message-passing decoding [12] on AWGN channels with BPSK modulation.
Fig. 1. shows the performance of (3, 4)-regular LDPCCCs by our Construction 1 and by the previous algebraic construction in (1), as described in Example 1. Both codes have girth 8 and rate 0.25. It can be seen that our constructed code has better performance at high SNRs since its free distance is probably larger.
Fig. 2. shows the performance of (3, 5)-regular LDPCCCs by our Construction 1 and by the previous algebraic construction in (1), as described in Example 2. Both codes have girth 8 and rate 0.4. It can be observed that our new code can have larger coding gain at high SNRs and the error floor tends to occur later than the previously constructed code.
