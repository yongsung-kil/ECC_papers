2008 5th International Symposium on Turbo Codes and Related Topics
Pinning Techniques for Low-Floor Detection/Decoding of LDPC-Coded Partial
Response Channels
Yang Han and William E. Ryan Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering
University of Arizona, Tucson, AZ 85721 {yhan,ryan}@ece.arizona.edu

Abstract— There is a well-known error-floor phenomenon associated with iterative LDPC decoders which has delayed the use of LDPC codes in certain communication and storage systems. Error floors are known to generally be caused by so-called trapping sets, subsets of code bits which induce a subgraph in a code’s Tanner graph that have the effect of locking up the decoder. In earlier work, the authors proposed three decoderbased techniques that lower the LDPC error floors on binaryinput AWGN channels. In this paper, we introduce two techniques that lower the error-rate floors for LDPC-coded partial response (PR) channels, which are applicable to magnetic and optical storage. The techniques involve, via external measures, “pinning” one of the bits in each problematic trapping set and then letting the iterative decoder proceed to correct the rest of the bits. We present two classes of pinning solutions: (1) a pre-pinning technique which fixes (pins) selected trapping set bits prior to transmission and (2) a post-pinning approach which utilizes information from outer BCH decoders to pin bits in trapping sets. Our simulations on PR1 and EPR4 channels demonstrate that the floor for the code chosen for this study, a 0.78(2048,1600) quasicyclic LDPC code, is lowered by orders of magnitude, beyond the reach of simulations.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] have been intensely researched by coding theorists and practitioners over the past decade due to their near-capacity performance when decoded with low-complexity iterative decoders. Many of their properties have been well studied, yet the errorrate floor phenomenon has remained an open problem. This phenomenon can be characterized as an abrupt decrease in the slope of a code’s performance curve from the moderateSNR water-fall region to the high-SNR floor region [2]. Since many systems such as data storage devices and optical communication systems require extremely low error rates, solving the error floor problem has been a critical issue during the past decade.
It is well known that the error floors of LDPC codes under message-passing decoding are usually due to low-weight nearcodewords [3], or trapping sets [2], rather than low-weight codewords. A (w, v) trapping set is a set of w variable nodes

which induce a subgraph with v odd-degree check nodes (unsatisfied check nodes, USC nodes) and an arbitrary number, usually substantially larger than v, of even-degree check nodes (mis-satisfied check nodes, MSC nodes). Throughout the paper, to simplify the language, we shall often use “trapping set” when we are referring to the induced subgraph. When w and v are relatively small, errors in each of the w variable nodes create v parity check failures and tend to lead to situations from which the iterative decoder cannot escape.
Trapping sets are unions of cycles. Iterative decoders are susceptible to trapping set problems because they work locally in a distributed-processing fashion to (hopefully) arrive at a globally optimum decision. Due to the presence of unions of cycles in trapping sets, conflicting decisions from different locally operating check nodes are introduced. Such conflicts often lead the iterative decoder away from the ML codeword. As an example, Fig. 1 depicts the two most dominant trapping set classes of the quasi-cyclic (2048, 1600) code studied in this paper, where each (5,5) trapping set has 10 MSC nodes and 5 USC nodes, and each (5,7) trapping set has 9 MSC nodes and 7 USC nodes. One solution for freeing an iterative decoder from a trapping set is to disturb the stabilized subgraph structure. As shown in Fig. 2(a-b), when one of the variable nodes in a (5, 5) trapping set is pinned by setting its bit value to 0 (or 1) prior to encoding and correspondingly setting to the maximum (or minimum) value the log-likelihood ratio input to the decoder, the entire trapping set is corrected with two additional LDPC decoder iterations. Similarly, a (5, 7) trapping set needs at most two additional iterations as in Fig. 2(c-d). In this paper, we apply and extend such ideas to partial response channels.
Much research has been dedicated to solving or mitigating the floor problem by attempting to design LDPC codes with low floors (for example, [7][8][9]). However, the problem is usually the decoder, not the code, and only a few papers focus on decoder-based strategies for lowering floors. For example, [10] and [11] attempt to lower the floors of LDPC codes by modifying the scheduling of the decoders. Reference [12] proposes an averaged decoding algorithm which decreases the frequency of trapping set error events by averaging the messages over several iterations. In [13], the floors are reduced by utilizing linear programming-based decoding. Also, in [4] a post-processor is proposed which, after the decoder becomes trapped, exploits the knowledge of the dominant trapping sets by inverting the bits of the trapping set associated with the decoder’s failed parity checks .
In an earlier work [14], the authors proposed a generalizedLDPC decoder based on the theory of graph-cover decoding analysis in [15]. A bi-mode decoder which utilizes erasure decoding in a second decoding mode was also shown in [14] to lower floors. In [14], we also examined a bit pinning approach for which the decoders of outer BCH codes were used to pin trapping set bits to specific values, after which the LDPC iterative decoder would correct the remaining trapping set bits as in Fig. 2. All three of these techniques were applied to the binary-input AWGN channel. The current paper extends the pinning approach to two LDPC-coded partial response channels with AWGN: PR1 and EPR4. We compare two pinning alternatives: (1) a pre-pinning technique which fixes (pins) selected trapping set bits prior to transmission and (2) a post-pinning approach which utilizes information from outer

BCH decoders to pin bits in trapping sets. We also compare the performance attained when pinning is performed at the channel detector and at the LDPC decoder.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section II introduces the LDPC code under study. The performance of a quasi-cyclic IRA 0.9(4544, 4096) code is presented together with its dominant trapping sets. A 0.78(2048, 1600) quasicyclic LDPC code derived from the IRA code is examined instead for quicker simulation purposes. Section III discusses the pre-pinning technique and demonstrates its low-floor performance on PR1 and EPR4 channels. In Section IV, we propose the post-pinning solution and investigate its ability to lower the floor on these channels. Finally, in Section V, we make some concluding remarks and discuss open problems.
II. LDPC CODE UNDER STUDY
The original LDPC codes of Gallager [1] were unstructured, but structured LDPC codes are preferred because of their lower encoder and decoder implementation complexities. The code we consider is quasi-cyclic (QC) and it possesses a paritycheck matrix H that can be conveniently arranged into an M × N array of Q × Q circulant permutation matrices and Q × Q zero matrices. In addition to simplifying encoder and decoder implementations, this structure facilitates the search of dominant trapping sets for our code because its Tanner graph possesses an automorphism of order Q. We will refer to the Q variable nodes associated with a column of permutation matrices as a variable node group and the Q constraint nodes associated with a row of permutation matrices as a check node group. A variable node of such a code can be represented by an integer pair (vp, vo), where vp ∈ {0, 1, · · · , N − 1} is the index of the variable node group and vo ∈ {0, 1, · · · , Q − 1} is the offset within the variable node group. Similarly, a check node can be represented using the pair (cp, co), where cp ∈ {0, 1, · · · , M − 1} and co ∈ {0, 1, · · · , Q − 1}.
Targeting the 4096-bit sector size of disk drives, a rate0.9(4544, 4096) quasi-cyclic IRA code was designed via the algorithm proposed in [16] which uses a progressive edge growth-like algorithm [8] and the approximate cycle extrinsic message degree algorithm [9]. The column weights of this code are 5 and 2, and its parity check matrix is a 7 × 71 array of 64 × 64 circulant matrices. Because simulations of this (4544, 4096) code are extremely time-consuming, we instead studied a derivative QC code whose H matrix is a submatrix of the original H matrix. The derived code is a rate-0.78 (2048, 1600) QC code that possesses the 10 most-dominant trapping set classes of size 64 from the original matrix. With this shorter code, the error floor is more easily reachable using software simulations, and any floor improvement method demonstrated to be effective on this shorter code will have a similar effect on the longer rate-0.9 code.
Before we introduce our floor-lowering techniques, we first emphasize that the trapping sets indicated above for the (4544, 4096) code and its derivative (2048, 1600) code were found via simulations on the binary-input AWGN channel. In general, when one applies a code to a different channel, or even a different decoder, the trapping sets can change. We have observed that the dominant trapping sets observed on the binary-input AWGN channel are also the dominant trapping sets for the PR1 and EPR4 channels. Thus, our pinning techniques for these two PR channels will target the aforementioned classes of (5, 5) and (5, 7) trapping sets.
III. PRE-PINNING
As mentioned in the Introduction, the idea behind pinning is, via external measures, to tell the iterative decoder the value of one of the bits within a trapping set, thus allowing the decoder to clean up the residual errors in the trapping set. In this first

pinning approach, which we call pre-pinning, we select one bit per trapping set and fix each of these to ‘1’ in every codeword [6]. This is done at the encoder. Accordingly, the decoder sets the LLR magnitudes for these pinned bits to the maximum possible value (with the appropriate sign). For the binary-input AWGN channel, these pinned bits need not be transmitted and, hence, pre-pinning is in some sense equivalent to code shortening for this channel. Where shortening and pinning differ is that shortening usually involves fixing consecutive data bits to 0’s (or 1’s) without regard to the location of trapping set bits. But there is an additional difference in the case of partial response channels: as will be made clearer in the next paragraph, because PR channels are not memoryless, the pinned bits must be transmitted.
The pinned bit locations can be made available at the LDPC decoder (bit pinning) or the PR channel detector (trellis pruning). The pinned bits at the detector prunes branches from the detector trellis, but also passes the pinned bit information to the decoder. Thus, the trellis pruning option also provides bit pinning at the decoder. We examine the performance of the bit pinning case to investigate the gain that the pruned detector trellis provides relative to bit pinning only at the decoder. Trellis pruning was explored in detail in the context of convolutional codes by Collins and Hizlan in [17]. For their scenario, the pruning of trellis branches in the convolutional decoder was done by a high-reliability outer Reed-Solomon code. A similar approach, which we call post-pinning, is explored in the next section. Also, Bertand and Labeau [18] apply these ideas to trellis-based decoding of linear block codes based on removing some edges and states from the trellis representation of the code.

IV. POST-PINNING
Although there is effectively no code rate loss due to the pinning bits for the trellis-pruning option of the prepinning technique, it is possible to provide trellis-pruning in a more power-efficient manner. In this section, we study trellispruning with the assistance of an outer algebraic code via a method we call post-pinning. As discussed in Section III, 256 specific bits cover the 640 trapping sets of the (2048, 1600) code and, if their values are known to the decoder, then the decoder is immune to the trapping sets. However, as opposed to pre-pinning where those bits are known at the outset due to bit pinning, with post-pinning the values of the 256 bits can be learned through the addition of a short BCH code which protects those bits with great reliability. Specifically, we used a (256, 240, t = 2) BCH code to protect the 256 bits. Because at most one trapping set error occurs at the LDPC decoder output, at most one of the 256 bits is expected to be in error after a sufficient number of LDPC decoder iterations. Thus, the BCH decoder will see at most one error per BCH codeword and, upon successful decoding, can inform the LDPC decoder (bitpinning option) or channel detector (trellis-pruning option) that these 256 bits can be assumed to have the highest reliability.
The system diagram with the trellis-pruning option is shown in Fig. 8, where the LDPC decoder is followed by a BCH decoder. When the LDPC decoder fails to get past a trapping set, the BCH decoder corrects the bit error due to the trapping set and then feeds back all 256 correct bits value to the channel detector, effectively pruning its trellis. Eventually, the residual errors in the trapping set will be corrected by additional LDPC decoder iterations.

V. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
This paper examined bit-pinning and trellis-pruning techniques for mitigating the error floors on partial response channels. Given the knowledge of the dominant trapping sets of an LDPC code of interest, trellis-pruning/bit-pinning can be applied to the detector/decoder by directly pinning certain bits prior to transmission or utilizing outer BCH codes. We demonstrated that these techniques lower the error floor of our (2048, 1600) LDPC code on the PR1 and EPR4 channels efficiently. A power transfer gain which offsets any code rate loss was observed for the trellis-pruning case.
Future work includes extending the use of the generalized decoder and the bi-mode erasure decoding technique of [14] to PR channels. Other planned work involves evaluation of the performance of these techniques with fewer LDPC decoder iterations, optimal scheduling between detector and decoder, and research on how to determine the minimum number of trapping set bits to pin so that the iterative decoder can correct the residual errors. Another interesting topic is to investigate the relationship between trapping sets on the AWGN channel and trapping sets on partial response channels.
