Abstract: In this paper, two versions of a new class of easily encodable polynomial codes are presented and their performance compared. Both versions are characterized by a sparse parity check matrix that, when specific design criteria are respected, corresponds to a Tanner graph free of shortcycles. These characteristics make such codes LDPC codes and, therefore, decodable through efficient iterative belief propagation algorithms, able to ensure very good error correction performance.
1. INTRODUCTION
Many error correcting codes exploit the action of a certain number of component codes. The resulting code is a subcode with respect to each of them. Bose-Chaudhuri-Hocquenghem (BCH) codes [1], product codes [2], and even serial or parallel concatenated codes [3], [4] can be considered as examples of application of this principle.
Focusing attention on the class of block codes, softdecision decoding has been recently proved to be applicable, by means of various techniques, typically based on Belief Propagation (BP) algorithms. In this respect, arranging the parity check matrix of these codes in such a way it is characterized by a low density, in terms of 1-symbols, and free of short-cycles, can yield significant advantages. Very efficient Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) codes, independently invented, have been designed starting from an idea of this type (see [5], for example).
BCH and Reed-Solomon (RS) codes, in particular, can benefit by the same BP principle, on condition their parity check matrix, that is generally not sparse, is suitably scaled in density [6]. So, it is possible to say that practically all codes can be efficiently decoded by using soft-decision techniques, and many of them can be seen as LDPC codes.
In this scenario, however, the design problem is still an open issue; the desire to achieve very good Bit Error Rate (BER) performance, close to the theoretical Shannon limits, must be combined with the need for easy encoding and decoding. BER performance is related with the code minimum distance. BCH codes exhibit a Hamming distance which increases linearly with the number of component codes, and show good features as for rate and block size. Product codes, instead, are characterized by a Hamming

distance which increases exponentially with the number of component codes, but they suffer strong limitations in the rate, which can be overcome only assuming very long block lengths [2]. Parallel concatenated convolutional codes (traditionally named “turbo codes”) and serially concatenated convolutional codes, equipped with proper interleavers, are rarely used with more than two component codes, because of complexity issues. In this case, they exhibit a rather small distance, that in fact is responsible for a pronounced BER floor [7]. As a counterpart, however, the number of codewords with minimum weight is usually very small, and this permits to have an excellent BER water-fall behavior for small/medium signal-to-noise ratios (SNRs). The design of proper interleavers is very important in order to reach a good trade-off between the floor and the water-fall behaviors.
In this paper, we present a new family of codes, which can be easily encoded, being intrinsically polynomial codes. Two different implementations are proposed and compared: the first one exploits the similarities of the considered scheme with Single Parity Check (SPC) product codes, while the second one can be seen as a special case of serially concatenated codes with elementary component codes. For both proposals, we show that the parity check matrix can be sparse and free of short-cycles, thus permitting the application of efficient iterative BP algorithms. Numerical examples are given by considering codes with different length and rate.
The organization of the paper is as follows. The proposed class of codes is presented in Section 2, emphasizing the differences between the two possible implementations; some design issues are also discussed. Section 3 is devoted to show performance examples, distinguishing between the cases of small, medium and large size codes. Finally, Section 4 concludes the paper.
2. PROPOSED CODES
Like for product codes, the encoding interleaver can be considered as the core of the design of the proposed codes. It can be thought as a memory, organized in form of a twodimensional matrix, and fed by the information source, that writes on it by columns, from up to bottom, and from left to right.

In the proposed codes a more complex texture is exploited for the reading-encoding procedure, but always on the same written memory. For any component code, we adopt a different reading rule, in such a way as to collect the control symbols close to the right vertical side of the rectangle: the whole memory is scanned, included the control symbols of the previous component codes; at the end of a string of rj bits, the last one is taken as a parity check control with respect to the previous (rj − 1) bits.
The first submatrix has column length r1, and the corresponding encoding procedure (that is associated to the first component code) is made on each row. This means that the parity check symbol will act on preceding symbols, one any r1.
The second encoding procedure is made taking r2 > r1, with the same principle; so its trace will be a staircase trajectory in the matrix, characterized by an average slope imposed by the difference (r2 − r1).

2.1 Layered Codes
Considering the analogy in the mechanism of the encoding procedure between the second component code in a 2dimensional single parity check product code and the first component code in a BPG code, the parity check matrix of the latter will exhibit a first layer equal to the second layer depicted in Fig. 1.
The subsequent (M – 1) layers will be very similar, but just with a different (larger) period in comparison with the first one. An example of the parity check matrix, for the particular case of M = 3, is shown in Fig. 3. This matrix is certainly sparse and, for a proper choice of the rj values, it has no length-4 cycles; therefore, it is suitable for application of the BP algorithm.
However, this parity check matrix has not full rank: all the rows in each layer sum into the all-one vector, so the parity check matrix contains at least M − 1 rows linearly dependent on the others.

4. CONCLUSION
The proposed class of codes has the flexibility to be seen as a variant of product codes or as a special version of serially concatenated codes based on a subclass of single parity check codes. These ties, combined with the polynomial nature of the codes, ensure easy encoding. However, the peculiarities of their design permit to work with sparse parity check matrices, that also ensure decoding with limited complexity and high performance by using classic iterative belief propagation algorithms.
