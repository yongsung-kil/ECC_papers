Abstract
We implemented a soft-decision decoder of (204,188)-Reed-Solomon code, which is used widely in standards for satellite, terrestrial, and other broadcasting systems. The decoder employs a list decoding technique using iterative adaptive belief propagation and bounded distance decoding. One decoded word is chosen from the list by MAP decoding, and some ideas are applied to reduce its complexity. When the channel throughput is 32 Mbps, the decoder works at 88 MHz in an FPGA, and obtains 1dB performance gain compared with ordinary hard-decision decoders.

1 Introduction
Reed-Solomon codes are widely used in many applications such as satellite and terrestrial broadcasting systems. They are known to be maximum distance separable codes, and normally decoded by bounded distance (BD) decoding.
In recent years, some researchers have been interested in soft-decision decodings of Reed-Solomon codes. Koetter and Vardy introduced an algebraic softdecision (ASD) decoding [5], which needed interpolation and factorization processes of one variable functions and provided enormous performance gain against the ordinary one, and El-Khamy and McEliece showed the optimum way to obtain multiplicity of each interpolated point [1]. Meanwhile, Fossorier and Lin studied soft decoding based on ordered statistics [2].
In 2004, Jian and Narayanan introduced an efficient decoding method called adaptive belief propagation (ABP) [4], which can work well not only on low density parity-check codes but also on general linear block codes such as Reed-Solomon codes. ABP can be used as an SNR developer, since it is a soft input soft output decoding and seems as if reliabilities of received words were developed after the decoding. According to this aspect, Jian proposed the concatenated use of ABP and BD decoding, besides El-Khamy did Koetter-Vardy ASD decoding with ABP, and huge performance gain were obtained in the both cases.
Some researchers began to study hardware implementation of a soft decision decoder for Reed-Solomon codes. Ahmed, Koetter, and Shanbhag introduced an efficient architecture to implement ASD decoding [6], and especially efficient architectures of interpolation

were published recently [7][9]. We introduce an efficient hardware implementation
architecture of an iterative ABP decoder. We succeeded to implement it for the famous (204,188)-ReedSolomon code, and it achieved feasible complexity and better performance than ordinary BD decoders.

3.4 Tradeoff between Complexity and Per-
 =  % ­O­ formance, and ABP decoder
To realize ABP decoder of RS( , ), we need to make a tradeoff between area, throughput, and performance. 3 ideas are employed here.
® restriction of index replacement ®  ­ only bits update ®  no update process of variable node messages
First, we accurately sort only in the first inner iteration,
  ­ but after that, we restrict the number of replacements to
be at most 4 in order to reduce operations, though we observe performance loss. Besides, we only update unreliables in each belief propagation process. Since almost all errors are supposed to be contained in the unreliable set, the performance loss will be small. In addition, in order to omit the update process of variable
  node messages and to avoid the bad influence of short
cycles in the Tanner graph, we set the iteration number of each belief propagation to be . Figure 4 shows our proposed ABP decoder archi-
tecture. Each updated LLR goes through the BD decoder after the ABP unit, and one decoded word is obtained in each iteration. Since ABP sometimes leads LLR in the wrong direction, both syndrome check and BD decodability check are capricious as the stopping criteria of ABP iterations. Thus we choose the stopping criterion (d) in 2.3, and make a candidate list consisting of all decoded words by iterations. After assembling the candidates, maximum a posteriori probability (MAP) decoding is done to choose one decoded word from the list.
