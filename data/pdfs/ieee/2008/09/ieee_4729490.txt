LDPC Codes and Stochastic Decoding for Beyond 100 Gb/s Optical
Transmission
Ivan B. Djordjevic (1), Lei Xu (2), and Ting Wang (2) 1: University of Arizona, ECE Department, Tucson, AZ 85721, USA; E-mail: ivan@ece.arizona.edu
2: NEC Laboratories of America, Princeton, NJ 08540, USA; E-mail: leixu@nec-labs.com

Abstract: We present a modified stochastic LDPC decoding algorithm suitable for implementation at 100-Gb/s and above. The stochastic decoding is invented for low-precision digital circuits, and as such is suitable for beyond 100-Gb/s implementation to avoid the problem of nonexistence of high-precision analog-to-digital converters operating at those speeds.

Introduction The development of a novel powerful forward error correction (FEC) scheme suitable for beyond 100Gb/s transmission and 100Gb/s Ethernet is of high importance [1]. To keep the BER quality for those systems comparable to that of standard RS(255,239) FEC scheme operating at 40 Gb/s, an FEC scheme providing at least 4 dB improvement in coding gain is necessary [2]. The iteratively decodable codes [1,2], turbo-product codes (TPC) and LDPC codes, are excellent candidates for use in high-speed optical communications. Although those schemes provide excellent performance improvement, they require soft bit reliabilities, and as such are still not implementable at data rates above 100 Gb/s. Given the lack of A/D converters operating at data rates t100 Gb/s, we propose an FEC scheme based on the stochastic LDPC decoding [3,4]. The stochastic arithmetic was introduced in 1960’s, as an option for the implementation of low-precision digital circuits [5]. Because the optical signal transmission at high-speed is severely deteriorated by impact of intrachannel nonlinearities and polarization-mode dispersion (PMD), the use of FEC is of utmost importance. By using the stochastic representation, the complex operations on probabilities, such as the multiplication and division, required in sum-product LDPC decoding algorithm can be implemented using simple Boolean functions, the same functions used in hard-decision decoding. It has recently been shown [4] that stochastic LDPC decoding can provide near sumproduct algorithm decoding performance for girth-6 codes, selecting this decoding approach as an excellent candidate for 100Gb/s+ implementation.
The main problems related to stochastic LDPC decoding are the hold-state problem, and the applicability to degree-3 variable and check nodes in parity-check matrix of an LDPC code. To solve the first problem, we propose to implement the second half-iteration of sum-product algorithm (SPA) using 3bit precision, while implement the rest using the stochastic logic. To solve the degee-3 node problem, instead of introducing the hidden states as it was done in [3,4] that lead to increased complexity of bipartite graph, we propose a modified stochastic decoding algorithm that is applicable to arbitrary degree nodes, without any change in bipartite graph. The LDPC codes exhibit the error floor phenomena. Because the girth-10 LDPC codes do not exhibit error floor down to BER of 10-15 (see ref. [2]), we design a girth-10 LDPC code of rate 0.8 suitable for above 100-Gb/s implementation.
Proposed stochastic LDPC decoding algorithm
The motivation of using stochastic decoding is the possibility of performing two basic probability-domain

sum-product algorithm (SPA) operations, the multiplication and division of probabilities, using simple Boolean circuitry. In stochastic decoding, the probability P is represented by Bernoulli sequence of length N with probability of ones being P=m/N, where m is the number of ones.

The SPA is an iterative LDPC decoding algorithm in which extrinsic probabilities are iterated forward and back between variable and check nodes of bipartite (Tanner) graph representation of a parity-check matrix. A major difficulty related to stochastic decoding is so called latching (hold-state) problem of circuit shown in Fig. 2(b), in which the bipartite graph correlate the probability messages so that a group of nodes are locked into a fixed state. To deal with this problem the concepts of supernodes, scaling channel LLRs, and randomization by edge memories have been introduced [3,4]. However, those approaches lead to conventional SPA approaching performance of girth-4 and girth-6 LDPC codes only, but face significant performance loss for large girth codes. Because the girth-4 and girth-6 exhibit error floor phenomena, we have to use large girth (8 or 10) LDPC codes. To solve the performance loss due to latching problem in large-girth LDPC codes, we propose to implement the second-half iteration with 3-bit precision, and all other steps with stochastic logic.

Evaluation of proposed scheme and conclusions The results of simulations for an additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel model are given in Fig. 3, where we compare the girth-10 quasi-cyclic LDPC codes against RS, concatenated RS, turboproduct, and girth-8 LDPC codes. The results of simulations are obtained for 30 iterations, and Bernouli sequence length of 1000. The girth-10 LDPC(24015,19212) code of rate 0.8 outperforms the

concatenation RS(255,239)+RS(255,223) (of rate 0.82) by 3.35 dB, and RS(255,239) by 4.75 dB, both at BER of 10-7. At BER of 10-10 it outperforms lattice based LDPC(8547,6922) of rate 0.81 and girth-8 by 0.44 dB, and BCH(128,113)xBCH(256,239) TPC of rate 0.82 by 0.95 dB. The net effective coding gain at BER of 10-12 is 10.95 dB.

In conclusion, we present a modified stochastic LDPC decoding algorithm suitable for high-speed implementation, at 100-Gb/s and above. Given the lack of A/D converters operating above 100-Gb/s, the stochastic decoding seems to be a reasonable option for implementation at those speeds. We propose a version of stochastic decoding algorithm that is applicable to arbitrary column- and row-weight. To avoid the error floor problem inherent in LDPC decoding we employ girth-10 quasi-cyclic codes. In order solve the hold-state problem, we propose to implement the second half-iteration step with 3 bit precision, and the rest using the stochastic logic.
