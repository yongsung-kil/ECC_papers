2008 5th International Symposium on Turbo Codes and Related Topics

Codes That Have Tanner Graphs With Non-Overlapping Cycles

Srimathy S, Andrew Thangaraj Department of Electrical Engineering Indian Institute of Technology, Madras Email : srimathy93@gmail.com, andrew@iitm.ac.in

Abstract— The Sum-Product Algorithm (SPA) for the decoding of Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) codes produces exact posterior probabilities when the underlying Tanner graph is cycle-free. However, it has been shown that cycle-free Tanner graphs cannot support good codes as they have poor minimum distance properties. When a Tanner graph has cycles that do not overlap (no two cycles have any node in common), the corresponding code has managable minimal tree complexity so that optimal decoding can be achieved. In this paper, we consider codes whose Tanner graphs contain non-overlapping cycles. We derive upper bounds on the minimum distance for such codes, and show that the upper bound can be at most one more than that of a tree code for any given block length and rate. Our results imply that Tanner graphs of good codes will contain several cycles that overlap.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low Density Parity Check codes [1] are typically decoded by the celebrated Sum-Product Algorithm (SPA) [2]. The SPA is an iterative message-passing algorithm on a Tanner graph that computes exact a posteriori probabilities of bit nodes only when the graph is cycle-free. But codes with cycle-free Tanner graphs have been found to be bad [3] in the sense that they have very poor minimum distance. So there appears to be an inherent trade-off between optimality of a SPA decoder and minimum distance.
The correspondence between the cycles in Tanner graphs and minimum distance have been studied in [4] and [5]. Asymptotic analysis of codes relating the cycle rank and minimum distance can be found in [3]. In this paper, we study codes of arbitrary block length and rate whose Tanner graphs have cycles that do not overlap, that is, no two cycles have a common node. By cycles, here in this paper, we mean only simple cycles.
It is shown in [4] that the minimal tree complexity of a code C which has a binary graphical model GC, is upper bounded by,
t(C) ≤ XT
where XT is the size of any tree inducing cut, the subset of edges in GC whose removal yields a tree with precisely two components and is given by
XT = |E| − |V | + 2
where E and V are the number of edges and vertices respectively in GC. We note that when the cycles in a Tanner graph are non-overlapping the quantity XT is relatively small

pushing down the upper bound for t(C). This paves way for optimal decoding with reasonable complexity. Study of minimum distance properties of such codes therefore becomes interesting.
The main result of our study is an upper bound on minimum distance for (n, k, d) codes that have a Tanner graph with nonoverlapping cycles. We show that

Our proof method is based on an iterative decomposition of Tanner graphs with non-overlapping cycles into base graphs and base loops. Using the decomposition, we show that the corresponding codes have a critical set of codewords implying the upper bound on minimum distance. The proof method applies to codes of all rates.
From the upper bound, we observe that the minimum distance cannot grow with block-length for a fixed rate. Hence, we infer that codes with only non-overlapping cycles in their Tanner graph will be asymptotically bad. Asymptotically good codes will necessarily have cycles that overlap.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. We present some basic definitions and preliminary results in Section II. The notion of critical set of codewords is introduced in Section III. The minimum distance bound is presented in Section IV. We conclude with discussion and remarks in Section V.

II. PRELIMINARIES
We define certain types of graphs called base graphs, base loops and branches. We then define two operations called gluing and joining. Any Tanner graph with non-overlapping cycles can be constructed by applying gluing and joining to base graphs, base loops and branches.

A. Definitions
Base graph: A Tanner graph with non-overlapping cycles such that the check nodes participating in cycles have degree 3 and all other check nodes have degree 2. Branch: A tree whose check nodes have degree 2. A single node is also a branch since it is a tree and does not have any check nodes. Base loop: A base graph with exactly one cycle. B. Tanner Graph Decomposition
Let G be the Tanner graph with non-overlapping cycles representing a (n, k) code. We assume that all the check nodes of cycles have degree at least 3. Otherwise, by clever row operations of the parity check matrix we can find an equivalent graph with check nodes of the cycle having degree at least 3. This is described in detail in the appendix.
Given any graph G with non-overlapping cycles, we find one subgraph B of G that is a base graph. Typically, there can be more than one subgraph of G that is a base graph. We then note that G can be constructed by recursively joining branches or base graphs to the base graph B. In addition, any base graph can be constructed by recursive gluing of base loops.
