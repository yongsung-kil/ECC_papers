VLSI Decoding Architecture with Improved Convergence Speed and Reduced Decoding Latency for Irregular LDPC Codes in WiMAX
Yeong-Luh Ueng, Chung-Jay Yang, Zong-Cheng Wu, Chen-Eng Wu, and Yu-Lun Wang Dept. of Electrical Engineering, National Tsing Hua University, Hsinchu, Taiwan, R.O.C.

Abstract— In this paper, we modify a previously proposed decoding algorithm and propose a VLSI architecture to decode the quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) code C used in the IEEE 802.16e standard. The modified decoding algorithm sequentially decodes a plurality of block codes for which its code length is much smaller than that of C. The proposed decoder can achieve a faster speed of convergence, lower decoding latency, higher throughput, and lower number of memory access as compared to the decoders using conventional turbo decoding message passing (TDMP) based on similar hardware complexity.
Keywords— Iterative decoding, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, decoder, very large-scale integration (VLSI) architecture, WiMAX.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] have been suggested in many standards such as IEEE 802.16e (WiMax) [2] because of their excellent error-correcting performance. The parity-check matrix (PCM) H of the irregular quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC code C selected by the standard of IEEE 802.16e is constructed based on an Mb × Nb base PCM Hb, where M = zMb, N = zNb, and z is a positive integer. In Hb, each 0 is replaced by a z × z zero sub-matrix and each 1 at the position (i, j) is replaced by a z × z sub-matrix that is obtained by right cyclic shifting a z × z identity matrix by p(i, j) ≥ 0 columns, 0 ≤ i ≤ (Mb − 1),0 ≤ j ≤ (Nb − 1). Hb and p(i, j) can be found in [2]. In this paper, we use the rate-1/2 and length-2304 LDPC code as example.
LDPC codes can be decoded by iterative two phase message passing (TPMP) algorithm which involves check-node operation and variable-node operation as two phase schedule [1][4] or turbo decoding message passing (TDMP) ( layered MPD (message passing decoding)) algorithm [5]. TDMP algorithm achieves about two times faster decoding convergence and significant memory advantage as compared to TPMP algorithm. Sum-product algorithm (SPA) [4], minimum sum algorithm (MSA)[8], and maximum a posteriori (MAP) algorithm can be used in the check-node operation. TDMP is applied for different LDPC codes using BCJR, SPA, MSA or its variations in [5][6][7][8]. A TDMP-like decoding was proposed in [13].
In [9], the authors proposed to decode C by sequentially decoding linear block codes Cl(0), Cl(1), · · ·, and Cl(z − 1) which are decoded by the same Mb ×Nl PCM Hl. Noticeably, Nb < Nl < N and Hl which can be derived from H is not equal to Hb. In this paper, Cl(p) and Cl(p + 1) are decoded simultaneously. We divide Hl into two (Mb/2) × Nl matrices Ho and He which consist of the odd rows and even rows of Hl, respectively. We decode Cl(p) based on He, Ho, and He,

sequentially. Extrinsic messages obtained in the decoding of Cl(p) based on He (Ho) can be used in the decoding of Cl(p) based on Ho (He). Cl(p+1) can be decoded similarly. In this paper, we also propose a memory-based VLSI architecture instead of register-based architecture in [9] for the modified decoding. The proposed decoder can exploit the property that most of columns of Hl have weight one and the weight of last 12 columns of Hl is 2 and hence can achieve a faster speed of convergence, lower decoding latency, higher throughput, and lower number of memory access as compared to the decoders using TDMP based on similar hardware complexity. We also implement and synthesize the LDPC decoder to demonstrate the advantage of the proposed decoding architecture.

III. PROPOSED DECODER
A. VLSI decoder architecture
The proposed decoding of C is implemented by sequentially decoding (Cl(0), Cl(1)), (Cl(2), Cl(3)), · · ·, (Cl(z − 2), Cl(z − 1)). Such one-round decoding of (Cl(p), Cl(p + 1)), p = 0, 2, · · · , z − 2, is called an iteration for the decoding of C. After decoding (Cl(z − 2), Cl(z − 1)), we then redecode (Cl(0), Cl(1)) and so on. For p = 0, 2, · · · , z − 2, we can use the hardware in Fig. 1 to decode Cl(p) and Cl(p+1) simultaneously by reading the associated Λj and Rij from memory banks M BΛ and M BR, respectively, to the Mb check-to-variable message update (CVMU) units which are used to calculate Rij, Qji, and Λj according to the check equations defined by the rows of Hl. The calculated Rij(or Qji) in one CVMU unit can be passed to other CVMU units by using the exchange network. The updated Rij and Λj are writing into M BR and M BΛ, respectively.
For shortening the decoding latency, we propose the archi-
tecture in Fig. 2 to implement the CVMU unit by using the property that most of columns of Hl have weight one and

the weight of last 12 columns of Hl is 2. Hence, for each check node i of Hl, we divide set R[i] into sets RA[i] and RB[i], where RB[i] includes the variable nodes (code bits) corresponding to the last 12 columns of Hl and RA[i] ≡ R[i] \ RB[i]. Divide Hl into two (Mb/2) × Nl matrices Ho and He which consist of the odd rows and even rows of Hl, respectively. Mb/2 CVMU units are used in parallel to calculate Rij, Qji, and Λj of code bits of Cl(p) based on the rows in He, Ho, and He, sequentially. The same operation is done simultaneously for Cl(p + 1) by using the other Mb/2 CVMU units. Since the column weight of the last 12 columns
of Hl is 2, the Rij (or Qji) for the code bits in Part B calculated in the module of B-Part extrinsic value calculator
(EVC) in the decoding of Cl(p) based on He (Ho) can be used in the decoding of Cl(p) based on Ho(He). The operation of Mb/2 CVMU units for the decoding of Cl(p) is described as follows.
Step 0: For the code bits of Cl(p), read the associated Λj[k − 1] and Rij[k − 1] from M BΛ and M BR, respectively, and calculate Qji[k] according to (2). Calculate λj = Qji[k] − Ri+1,j[k − 1] for j ∈ RB[i].
Step 1: For each even row i in Hl, use the module of m1m2 selector to find m1 = minj ∈RA[i] |Qj i[k]| ≡ |Qjm1i[k]| and m2 = minj ∈RA[i]\{jm1} |Qj i[k]| ≡ |Qjm2i[k]| and then use B-Part EVC to calculate Rij[k], j ∈ RB[i], according to (1). Note that the values of Rij[k] for j ∈ RA[i] are not calculated at this moment. m1, m2, jm1, and jm2 are stored in the A-part-register for later usage.
Step 2: For each odd row i in Hl, use the module of BPart EVC with Rij[k], j ∈ RB[i], obtained in Step 1 and λj obtained in Step 0 to calculate Qji [k] = λj +Rij[k], where i is an even number and i ∈ C[j], for all j ∈ RB[i ], and use the modules of m1-m2 Selector and Λ − R calculator to calculate Ri j[k] and Λj[k] for all j ∈ R[i ].
Step 3: For each even row i in Hl, use the module of BPart EVC with Ri j[k], j ∈ RB[i ], obtained in Step 2 and λj obtained in Step 0 to calculate Qji[k] = λj +Ri j[k], where i is an odd number and i ∈ C[j], for all j ∈ RB[i].
Step 4: For each even row i in Hl, use modules of m1m2 Selector and Λ − R calculator with the m1, m2, jm1, and jm2 stored in the A-part-register and Qji, j ∈ RB[i], obtained in Step 3 to calculate Rij[k] and Λj[k] for all j ∈ R[i].
Step 5: For the code bits of Cl(p), write the associated Λj[k] and Rij[k] to M BΛ and M BR, respectively.
B. Decoding timing diagrams and latency
First consider the decoding latency of (Cl(p), Cl(p + 1)). Let BΛ and BR be the number of bits used to represent Λj and Rij, respectively. M BΛ consists of 2Nb pieces of (z/2) × (BΛ) memory. Since the largest row weight of H is 7, M BR consists of Mb pieces of z × (7RΛ) memory. It takes 6 cycles to read (write) the associated Λj from (into) M BΛ

In the proposed decoder, we use a simple address controller instead of a complex flexiblepermutator [11] or a cyclic shifter to read (write) Λj from (to) M BΛ since enough cycles can be used for such reading (writing) process.
IV. PERFORMANCE EVALUATION
A. Bit error rate (BER) in additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels
Fig. 5 shows the BER results of the length-2304 LDPC code using the proposed decoding, TPMP, TDMP, and the decoding in [9] with Nlo = 1. For all cases, binary phase shift keying (BPSK) and offset MSA [10] are used. We find that the proposed decoding can achieve the best performance among these decoding algorithms.
B. Comparison with decoder using TDMP algorithm
Fig. 6 shows the timing diagram of the decoding of C using the partial parallel architecture [11] and TDMP (layered MPD) algorithm. Under the constraint of similar hardware cost (Mb CVMU units), the decoding latency is (Ni×6×z)+2L cycles, where extra 2L cycles account for the first (last) pipeline stage of reading (writing) Λj from (to) M BΛ and L = 1 if a complex flexible permutator is used. From Fig. 5, we find that the proposed decoding with Ni = 12 can achieve the same BER as the TDMP algorithm with Ni = 15. Hence, the decoding latency and the number of memory access of the proposed decoder is only 80 percent of those of the decoder using TDMP.
C. Implementation results
Based on the proposed architecture, we implement and synthesize the length-2304 LDPC code in the IEEE 802.16e standards by using the TSMC 0.18μm technology. Since BΛ and BR are 9 and 6 bits, respectively, M BΛ consists of 48 pieces of 48 × 9 memories with size of 1.104mm2 and M BR consists of 12 pieces of 96 × 42 memories with size of 0.684mm2. The gate counts of the control logic for memory reading/writing and decoding is 35K. We use 12 CVMU units to decode Cl(p) and Cl(p + 1) and the gate counts of these CVMU units is 70K. The total gate counts of LDPC decoder is 105K and the maximum clock frequency is 200MHz. Since the proposed decoder can achieve a BER of 2×10−6 at an Eb/N0 of 2 dB using 4.6 iterations (average), the proposed decoder can achieve a throughput of 106 Mbps. We can increase the decoding throughput by decoding Cl(p), Cl(p + 1), Cl(p + 2) and Cl(p + 3) simultaneously. The estimated gate counts and

the throughput are 175K and 212 Mbps, respectively. The hardware in our decoder can be configured to implement the TDMP-based decoder with the schedule given in Fig. 6. The estimated hardware results are shown in Table I. The decoder based on TDMP can only achieve a throughput of 71 Mbps. In [12], the ASIC results for the LDPC decoders in IEEE 802.16e standard were also presented. The total gate counts is 420K and throughput is 111.1Mbps under the conditions of 2 iterations and operation frequency of 83.3MHz. Note that the proposed decoder can also be used to decode rate-1/2 LDPC codes with z = 96, i.e., N = 2304.
V. CONCLUDING REMARKS
In this paper, we modify the decoding algorithm in [9] and propose a VLSI architecture for the modified decoding algorithm. The proposed decoder can achieve a faster speed of convergence, lower decoding latency, and higher throughput based on similar hardware resource as compared to the decoder using the TDMP algorithm. In addition, the proposed decoder can reduce the number of memory access. The proposed decoding architecture can be applied to other LDPC codes in the IEEE 802.16e and 802.11n standard. Similar advantage is expected.
