On the implementation of bus-based architectures for LDPC decoding

G. Aggouras and V. Paliouras
Electrical and Computer Engineering Dept., University of Patras, Greece

Abstract—Three families of architectures for LDPC decoding are presented in this paper, aiming at the reduction of the interconnection complexity dominant in an LDPC decoder. The proposed architectures explore tradeoffs between the interconnection complexity, delay, and decoding performance. A graph-based technique is introduced that allows the formation of groups of calculations, such that inter-group communication is minimized. The formed groups are subsequently mapped onto processor units. Furthermore, a technique to achieve full utilization of processors under a constraint on the number of busses is discussed and a corresponding architecture, as well as a hybrid of the multi-bus and grouped-calculations architecture.

I. INTRODUCTION
LDPC codes [2] have attracted the interest of the research community as they offer channel capacity limit [1] approaching capabilities with limited computational complexity in comparison to codes of similar decoding performance such as turbo codes. LDPC codes are becoming popular in international standards for applications such as satellite digital video broadcasting (DVB-S2), WiMAX (IEEE 802.16e standard for microwave communications) and 10GBase-T Ethernet (802.3an). Several VLSI architectures have been proposed in the literature for the implementation of LDPC decoders [3]–[6]. A main issue in LDPC decoder design is the implementation complexity of the interconnection network that connects the simple processing units. In this paper a family of architectures is proposed, which do not require a particular LDPC code structure. The proposed architectures are obtained by trading delay for interconnection complexity with minimal degradation on decoding performance.
The remainder of the paper is organized as follows: in section 2, the proposed grouped-calculations architecture is detailed, in section 3, the proposed multi-bus is introduced, while a combined architecture is discussed in section 4. Section 5 presents simulation results quantifying the impact of the proposed architectures on decoding performance, and conclusions are discussed in section 6.

II. THE GROUPED CALCULCATIONS (GC) ARCHITECTURE
The basic architectural unit of the grouped calculations (GC) architecture is the processor pair (PP) of Fig. 1. GC architecture is derived by grouping the calculations of the message-passing algorithm in different groups and subsequently assigning each group to a different PP. In a PP, the processor marked Q-CPU calculates bit-to-check node messages (Q messages) while the R-CPU processor calculates check-to-bit node messages (R messages). All message exchange is implemented on the bidirectional bus; messages are transmitted as soon as they are calculated.

The Q messages are calculated and transmitted in the first part of a message-passing algorithm iteration. In the second part of the iteration, R messages are calculated and transmitted. When the decoding architecture comprises only a single PP, all messages can be transmitted on a bidirectional bus. For practical cases and due to delay constraints, however, more than one PPs are required, all operating in parallel as shown in Fig. 2. In this case an interconnection network is required to communicate messages among PPs. The need of communicating R messages between the calculation groups, and hence PPs, can be handled in three ways: i) provide no busses between PPs, which results to decoding performance degradation due to message loss but also to reduced area, ii) provide complete interconnection between the PPs to accommodate message exchange with no decoding performance degradation but increased area in comparison to case (i), and iii) provide limited interconnection to support selected busses with the heaviest traffic between PPs achieving a decoding performance between solutions (i) and (ii). To minimize decoding performance degradation, it is obvious that calculations should be grouped and allocated to PPs in such a way that the required exchange of messages between groups is minimal. A technique to derive such a grouping of calculations is proposed in the following.
Message calculations can be grouped in such a way that when the R messages are calculated, all Q messages are available. This can be achieved by placing the calculations derived from each row of the parity-check matrix in the same group. However, when the Q messages are computed, some R messages will not be available. In order to minimize the number of the missing R messages, the rows of the paritycheck matrix H must be grouped in a way that the R messages needed in calculations from one to another group is minimized. This is achieved by using the methodology proposed in the following.
The parity-check matrix H is exploited to construct a proposed graph G containing nodes and weighed edges. Each row of the matrix is represented by a node. Edges connect all node couples that correspond to different H rows having at least one element equal to one, in identical position. The edges have weights. The weight of an edge is equal to the number of elements that have the value one, and are located at the same position of the rows corresponding to the two nodes connected with the particular edge. For example for two rows of a paritycheck matrix that have values 11010 and 11100 and are represented with the nodes n1 and n2 respectively the corresponding edge (n1,n2) assumes weight w12=2 since both rows have the value 1 at positions 1 and 2.
Following the construction of G, the groups of calculations with the minimum message traffic between them are determined by cutting graph G in appropriate sub-graphs. This is achieved by finding the cuts (sets of edges the removal of which partitions the original graph into disjoined subgraphs) that have the minimum total weight of constituent edges. An illustrative example of such an operation is shown in Fig. 3. After calculation grouping, some R messages are omitted, as it was stated before. The elements that cause this problem are highlighted in Fig. 3c. It is noted that experimental results show that the decoding performance degradation due to the proposed procedure, can be moderated by increasing the number of decoding iterations.

TN = T/N + TR,
where T is the time needed when no groups are used and TR the time needed for transmitting the missing R messages. Of course TR is zero when no bus lines are implemented between PPs.
III. THE MULTI-BUS (MB) ARCHITECTURE
The organization of the MB architecture is shown in Fig. 4. The busses between the processors are common busses that only one processor uses as an output at any given time. Either one Q-CPU is transmitting and all the R-CPUs are receiving or one R-CPU is transmitting and all the Q-CPUs are receiving. As the number of busses increases, the number of the calculating CPUs has to be increased too, in order to accommodate the increased numbers of messages that can be calculated due to the increase in message traffic speed.

Experiments performed using several LDPC codes and for various values of the number of busses show that the number of processors needed for the case of n busses, is n QCPUs and n R-CPUs. A schedule is possible that guarantees the complete utilization of the employed processors. This is achieved by appropriately delaying the message calculation operations in the schedule list. A simple example of such a schedule list with two busses is shown in Fig. 5.

Figure 5. Example of a schedule list on a decoder using an architecture with many busses: (a) before the re-scheduling calculations and (b) after proposed re-scheduling.
The reason that the empty locations in the schedule listing of Fig. 5(a) exist is that the processors sometimes have to wait to receive one or more particular messages before a message can be computed. At some other times a message is transmitted that allows the computation of several messages. By re-scheduling the computations from the latter case to the former, the number of the processors becomes the minimum, without an effect of overall schedule latency.

Assume the computation of R messages. The same argument applies for the computation of Q messages. Let b be the number of busses. Then n Q messages are communicated in n/b cycles, therefore n R messages can be computed in the same time. Therefore, since n R messages should be computed in n/b cycles and each RCPU computes one R message per cycle, b processors are needed, i.e., equal to the number of busses.
The total memory required is independent of the number of busses, since the same amount of total messages has to be stored on every iteration of the decoding algorithm regardless of the number of busses or processors. If n denotes the number of busses, the memory needed on every processor is divided by n, however memory remains constant as a total for the overall architecture. The total area of the decoder is of the order of n2 , increasing due to the increase of the number of both the processors and busses. Finally, the execution time is divided by n.
IV. THE COMBINED GROUPED CALCULATIONS–MULTI-BUS (GCMB) ARCHITECTURE
The GC architecture can be combined with the MB one, as shown in Fig. 6 to derive the GCMB architecture. The GCMB architecture is obtained by replacing the PPs of the GC architecture with groups of processors, each organized in an MB manner. The interconnections between the PPs can be implemented by adopting any of the three strategies described in section 2, i.e., no busses, complete busses or selected busses. In each case the same advantages and disadvantages apply.

V. SIMULATION RESULTS

The decoding performance of the GC architecture with no interconnections between groups is compared to that of an architecture with a complete interconnection network and no message loss. The GC architecture is compared to a single-PP architecture, over which it has the advantage of increased speed. The total delay is divided by the number of the groups.

Simulation results for a code with a parity-check matrix H of 48 rows and 96 columns and weights of 8 and 4 respectively are shown in Fig. 7. When two calculation groups are formed, the performance of GC architecture and three iterations is better than that of full interconnections and two iterations. For four iterations the GC architecture performance is almost the same with that of full interconnections and three iterations. When three groups are used, the GC architecture with four iterations has almost the same performance as that with full interconnections and two iterations.

In Fig. 8, simulation results for a code with a paritycheck matrix of 102 rows and 204 columns and a weight of 10 and 5 respectively are shown. With two groups the GC architecture has the same performance with the full connections one, since the two graphs match. With four iterations the GC architecture has almost the same performance as that with full connections and three iterations. When three groups are used, the GC architecture with three iterations has almost the same performance as that with full connections and two iterations.

VI. CONCLUSIONS

Three architectures for LDPC decoding have been presented, namely GC, MC, GCMB, focusing on reducing the interconnection complexity which is dominant in an LDPC decoder. The three architecture exhibit different area and time complexities. The proposed architectures explore a trade off between the interconnection complexity, delay, and decoding performance. Interconnection complexity is the main obstacle in the implementation of message-passing decoders for large LDPC codes. A graph-based technique has been introduced that allows the formation of groups of calculations, of minimal inter-group communication, subsequently mapped onto processor pairs.
