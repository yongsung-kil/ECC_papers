Graph-Matched LDPC Codes for Partial-Response Channels
Andrei P. Legg and Bartolomeu F. Uchoˆa-Filho Communications Research Group — GPqCom Dept. of Electrical Engineering Federal University of Santa Catarina Florianopolis, SC 88040-900 Brazil
Email: andrei.legg@gmail.com, uchoa@eel.ufsc.br

Abstract— In this paper, we propose an LDPC coding scheme based on repeat-accumulate (RA) codes for partial-response (PR) channels. We modify the original RA encoder in such a way that there exists a one-to-one relationship between a sequence called the D-codeword (which is not the transmitted codeword but contains the information bits) and the noiseless received sequence at the output of the PR channel. A consequence of this is that the graph used for decoding the D-codeword, which is different from the encoder graph, is of low complexity. Moreover, with the appropriate modification of the RA encoder, the graph used for decoding is the same of any PR channel, which makes sense calling these codes graph-matched. The simplicity of the decoder, resulting from the “binary” interference removal (precoding) and the linear, as opposed to quadratic, complexity of the encoder make the proposed scheme attractive. For a code rate R = 3641/4096 = 0.89 and for the PR4 channel, an LDPC code has been designed and a simulated coding gain of about 5 dB for a bit error rate less than 10−5 was obtained.
I. INTRODUCTION
It is well-known that partial-response (PR) systems have been adopted as a model for magnetic recording channels and have been used for pulse shaping in digital transmission over bandlimited channels. For instance, high-density magnetic recording channels have been modeled as the PR system with transfer function Pn(D) = (1 − D)(1 + D)n, where D is the delay operator and n a nonnegative integer [1].
Error-correcting codes are used to boost the error performance of these systems. For example, a coding system consisting of a convolutional encoder followed by a precoder with transfer function 1/Pn(D) (mod 2) has been considered for the Pn(D) channel in [2]–[7]. In these works, a convolutional code with a conventional trellis containing 2ν states is said to be trellis-matched to the Pn(D) channel if the decoder trellis for the composite system has only 2ν states, as opposed to 2n+1+ν states [3]. Trellis-matched convolutional codes not only have low decoding complexity, but also the topology of the decoder trellis is the same for any n.
Motivated by the excellent performance of turbo codes and low density parity-check (LDPC) codes over the Gaussian channel, researchers have become interested in designing coding schemes based on these codes for PR channels. The first works considered the so-called turbo-equalization [8], wherein precoding makes the PR channel (considered as the inner code) appear recursive, a necessity for good error performance in

such encoding systems. It has been shown in [8] that the choice of the precoder impacts the error performance of PR turboequalization schemes.
Turbo product codes based on single-parity check codes (TPC/SPC) for PR channels have been proposed by Li et al. [9]. While the complexity of the maximum a posteriori (MAP) decoder for turbo codes is notoriously high, the turbo product codes and the LDPC codes emerge as interesting solutions as their decoding complexities are much lower. In particular, the decoding complexity of LDPC codes increases as O(N ), where N is the block length. However, the memory of the PR channel must be taken into account in the decoding process of the LDPC codes, increasing the decoding complexity.
In this paper, we propose a class of LDPC codes, called graph-macthed LDPC codes for PR channels, which is the LDPC analog of the trellis-matched convolutional codes. More specifically, using the appropriate precoder for the Pn(D) channel, the Tanner graph used for decoding is of low complexity and is exactly the same for any n. The a posteriori probabilities (APPs) needed by the sum-product algorithm (SPA) [12] for decoding can be calculated in a simple fashion from the output of the PR channel, due to the “binary” interference removal (precoding). Although previous work has already considered precoding with LDPC codes for the Pn(D) channel [8], taking advantage of precoding for reducing the decoding complexity has not been explored before.
On the other hand, the encoding complexity of LDPC codes is usually O(N 2). Several researchers have come up with efficient ways of encoding LDPC codes with lower complexity [10]. Repeat-accumulate (RA) codes [11] is one such approach that possesses a simple linear-time encoding algorithm. We show that the LDPC codes proposed in this paper are related to RA codes.
We observe that in the encoding algorithm of an RA code the parity-check bits correspond to the precoding with transfer function 1/(1⊕D) of a certain sequence of bits which does not include the information sequence, where ⊕ denotes addition modulo 2. We show that a simple modification to the RA scheme extends the precoding in the original RA encoding algorithm to the information sequence without loosing the linear encoding complexity. II. THE RA SCHEME AND THE RA-BASED LPDC CODING SCHEME FOR PR CHANNELS
A. The Original RA Encoder
For the sake of description simplicity, we consider a short code of rate 4/8. The Tanner graph for the original RA encoder [11] is shown in Figure 1. As usual, the variable nodes are denoted by whereas the function (or check) nodes are denoted by . For the encoding, the check nodes are seen as adders modulo 2. The variable nodes x1, x2, x3, x4 are associated with the information bits. The encoding proceeds as follows.
The information bits xi, i = 1, . . . , 4, are first triplicated (in this example). The twelve bits are then permuted and grouped into four blocks of three bits each. The three bits in the first (uppermost) block are added modulo 2 to form x5. In order to obtain x6, the bit x5 and the three bits in the second block are added modulo 2. The encoding algorithm proceeds in a similar way until x8 is obtained. The complexity of this encoding algorithm for a block length N is O(N ).

D. Transmission Through the PR Channel
For the sake of simplicity, we describe the transmission through the PR4 channel, which is the channel considered in the simulated error performance section. It is similar for

the other PR channels. First, the E-codeword, (x1, . . . , x8), is transformed into the bipolar form (±1) and then transmitted through the 1 − D2 channel. It should be noted that the E-
codeword is exactly the sequence produced when the sequence
(x1, . . . , x8), called the D-codeword (D stands for “decoder”), is passed through the precoder with transfer function 1/(1 ⊕ D2). The last four bits of the D-codeword are given by:

F. The Proposed Scheme Revisited
If we now consider the Tanner graph in Figure 4 as the graph of an LDPC encoder, we see that the corresponding codeword, i.e., the variable nodes (x1, . . . , x8), is exactly

the sequence of bits (D-codeword) that is transformed into the transmitted sequence (E-codeword) through the precoding operation. Therefore, the proposed coding scheme could be implemented alternatively as shown in Figure 5. First, the systematic LDPC encoder described by the Tanner graph of Figure 4 produces the codeword (x1, . . . , x8). The codeword is passed through the precoder with transfer function 1/Pn(D) (mod 2), and then, after being transformed to the bipolar form, the output is transmitted over the Pn(D) channel. Decoding is performed in the same way as described in Section II-E.
Since the precoder does not yield a complexity increase, the LDPC coding scheme in Figure 5 has both linear encoding complexity and linear decoding complexity. Moreover, due to (5), decoding is as simple as in a memoryless channel.
III. SIMULATED ERROR PERFORMANCE
We designed a graph-matched LDPC code for the PR4 channel following the techinque described in Section II. The code rate is R = 3641/4096 = 0.89, following a common practice in the literature of codes for magnetic recording [9]. The repetition degree of the information bits was 3, and after the pseudo-random permutation groups of 24 bits were formed to generate the parity bits, yielding a parity-check matrix with at most three ones in each column. The maximum number of iterations in the SPA was set to 5. The BER versus Eb/N0 for the proposed scheme, the scheme TPC/SPC and the LDPC code without precoding given in [9], all of them designed for the PR4 channel, are plotted in Figure 6. In this figure are presented the curve obtained and the curves of for the PR4 channel. The curves in [9] presents a LDPC code without precoding and a TPC/SPC code with precoding, both with the same degree and length of the one presented here. Notice that the error performance of the three schemes is practically the same: Eb/N0 = 5dB at a BER = 10−5.
IV. CONCLUSIONS AND FINAL COMMENTS
In this paper, we have proposed an LDPC coding scheme based on modified repeat-accumulate codes for PR channels. Both the encoder and the decoder complexities are linear in the block size, which makes this scheme attractive. Good error performance with the proposed scheme has been observed from computer simulations for the PR4 channel. The extension of the proposed technique to the extended PR4 channels and other PR channels is fairly straightforward. It should be clear from Section II that the coding technique proposed in this paper works well for any integer-valued partial-response polynomial. However, many works have recently considered as a model for very high density magnetic recording channels the so-called generalized partial-response (GPR) systems, described by real-valued polynomials [13]– [15].
Of course, the reduction modulo 2 in the precoding operation could not be accomplished for GPR systems. The coded scheme proposed herein could still be used if the GPR polynomial were of the form F (D) = (1 − D2)G(D), where G(D) is a real-valued polynomial, as considered in [13], [14]. The LDPC code could be designed for the PR4 channel (integral part of F (D)), in combination with a maximum likelihood detector using the Viterbi algorithm matched to the G(D) channel. Since it is more difficult to realize the Viterbi algorithm with real coefficients [15], and the decoding complexity increases with the degree of the polynomial, having the Viterbi detector matched to G(D) yields a detector with lower complexity.
