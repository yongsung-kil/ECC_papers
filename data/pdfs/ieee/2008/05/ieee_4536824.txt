ABSTRACT
In this paper, quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QCLDPC) codes are applied to a novel rate-compatible (RC) scheme for multimedia transmission. To ensure that the RC codes over the entire range of rates can be decoded by a single decoder, modified array codes in a lower triangular form are employed as the mother codes. The constructed rate-compatible QC-LDPC codes based on our novel RCscheme can also be encoded by a single encoder. Hardware design of QC-LDPC encoder for this RC-scheme is proposed in detail. The hardware complexity is determined by the lowest-rate code for serial encoding and by the mother code for parallel encoding. Part of hardware units is disabled in certain cases to reduce power consumption.
Keywords—Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) Codes, Quasi-Cyclic (QC), Rate Compatible (RC), Encoder, Array Codes
I. INTRODUCTION
Multimedia transmission has to handle a variety of compressed and uncompressed source signals such as data, text, image, audio, and video. On wireless channels the error rates are high and the channels introduce specific additional constraints. In order to address the different error protection requirements of various source signals and the time varying nature of wireless channels, error-correction codes are required to be flexible with respect to their code rates depending on the current channel state. Rate adaptability can be realized with several pairs of encoders and decoders for the desired code rates, which is sometimes unacceptable due to complexity. Complexity-constrained systems that undergo variations in link budget may benefit from the adoption of a rate-compatible family of codes. Since 1970’s, RC-schemes have been widely proposed by using Reed-

Solomon codes, BCH codes, convolutional codes, turbo codes, turbo product codes, and LDPC codes [1].
Much attention has been paid to LDPC codes recently due to their excellent performance and the simplicity of iterative decoding. Practical approaches for encoding these codes, however, have not received much attention. Implementing brute force multiplication using the generator matrix is not practical, especially when considering multiple codes. QC-LDPC codes have an encoding advantage over other types of LDPC codes. They can be efficiently encoded using simple feedback-shift registers with linear complexity. QC-LDPC codes also have advantages in integrated circuit (IC) decoder implementations due to their cyclic symmetry, which results in simple regular wiring and modular structure. Well-designed QC-LDPC codes perform as well as computer-generated random LDPC codes, in terms of biterror performance, word-error performance, and error floor, collectively.
In this paper, QC-LDPC codes are applied to RC scheme in view of strong competitors to the random LDPC codes in practical applications. Based on our novel proposed scheme, a sequence of rate-compatible QC-LDPC codes can be encoded and decoded using a single encoding/decoding pair. The paper is organized as follows. Section II gives a brief introduction of QC-LDPC codes and gives a particular kind of QC-LDPC codes which are significant to our RCscheme. Section III proposes a novel RC-scheme using this kind of QC-LDPC codes while Section IV presents hardware design of QC-LDPC encoders for this RC-scheme. Section V concludes this paper.

III. NOVEL RC-SCHEME USING QC-LDPC CODES

RC codes are a nested sequence of codes where the parity bits of higher rate codes are embedded in those of lower rate codes [1], [5]. RC codes have many applications in packet data communications where adaptive coding and/or unequal error protection is required. One of the main advantages of using RC schemes is that all the codes in the sequence can be encoded and decoded using a single encoding/decoding pair.
Rate-compatible LDPC (RC-LDPC) codes were first introduced by Li and Narayanan in [1]. Ha et al. [5] study the problem of finding puncturing distributions for RCLDPC codes that optimize the threshold over a range of rates. However, Li and Narayanan in [1] also point out that puncturing alone cannot provide a sequence of well-

The total number of flip-flops required is (n − m)p. The total number of XOR-gates required is (n − m)p − 1 and total number of AND-gates required is (n − m)p. The encoder complexity is linearly proportional to the number (n − m)p of information bits of the mother code. Some shiftregisters corresponding to aftermost generators hi,j's are disabled during lower-rate encoding.
If all the parity sections are generated in parallel at the same time, a circuit that completes encoding in p clock cycles can be implemented. In this case, m*(n* − m*) feedback shift registers are needed. To start the encoding, all the first columns hi,j's of the m*(n* − m*) circulants in GQC are loaded into the feedback shift-registers in one clock cycle and the first bits, p1,1, p2,1, …, pm*,1, of all the m* parity sections are formed. Then by cyclically shifting all the feedback registers p − 1 times in parallel, the other m*( p − 1) parity bits are generated. In this way, the encoding is completed in p clock cycles. This implementation requires a total of mmax(n − m)p flip-flops, mmax[(n − m)p − 1] XOR-gates and mmax(n − m)p AND-gates. Much more hardware units are required in the structure of this encoding scheme.
V. CONCLUSION
This paper presents the hardware encoding schemes designed for a novel RC-scheme. The lower-rate codes are constructed by adding new rows to the original parity check matrix of the mother code while the higher-rate codes are derived from deleting lowermost rows of the parity check matrix of modified array codes which is equivalent to implement parity puncturing. Based on our novel proposed scheme, a sequence of designed rate-compatible QC-LDPC codes can be encoded and decoded using a single encoding/decoding pair. The hardware complexity is determined by the lowest-rate code for serial encoding and by the mother code for parallel encoding. Part of hardware units is disabled in certain cases to reduce power consumption.
