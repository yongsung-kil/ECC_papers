Efficient FEC for Optical Communications using Concatenated Codes to Combat Error-floor

Yoshikuni Miyata, Wataru Matsumoto, Hideo Yoshida, and Takashi Mizuochi
Information Technology R&D Center, Mitsubishi Electric Corporation, 5-1-1, Ofuna, Kamakura, Japan Miyata.Yoshikuni@ak.MitsubishiElectric.co.jp
Abstract: We propose concatenated LDPC(9252,7967)+RS(992,956) codes for application to systems beyond 40 Gb/s, taking practical implementation into account. Simulation shows that the Q limit is 7.1 dB, and that the concatenation effectively suppresses unwanted error-floor.

1. Introduction

Very high speed optical transport systems, e.g. 100GbE, need increasingly powerful forward error correction (FEC)

to enhance the transmission reach, with practical circuit implementation essential. In recent years, several types of

powerful FEC have seen intensive development. For example, Block Turbo code LSI was reported with a Q-limit of

6.3 dB at 10 Gb/s [1]. OTN compatible enhanced FEC LSI has been developed with a Q-limit of 9.1 dB at 43 Gb/s

[2]. However, no FEC LSI applicable to long-haul systems in the 100 Gb/s class and showing comparable

performance has been seen yet. In order to keep the BER quality of 100GbE the same as that of a standard

RS(255,239) FEC-based 40 Gb/s system having a Q-limit of 11.2 dB, at least 4 dB (=10log10(100/40)) stronger coding gain (Q-limit > 7.2 dB) would be necessary.

Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes are being studied intensively as potential candidates for 100 Gb/s

class systems. The LDPC code is a linear code, defined by the sparse parity check matrix invented by Gallager. This

technique was ignored for a long time, then was re-discovered by MacKay, leading to further intensive research for

wireless communication systems. Later, studies into applying LDPC to optical communications were instituted by

Vasic and Djordjevic [3]. The irregular LDPC(3367,2821) code with 19% redundancy was studied [4], and with the iterative soft-decision min-sum algorithm yielded a Q-limit of 7.5dB at a post-FEC BER of 10-9. This was improved

on by the generalized LDPC(3639,3213) code with 23.6% redundancy, with which a record Q-limit of 5.5dB at a post-FEC BER of 10-12 was demonstrated in a Monte Carlo simulation [5].

The expected merits for LDPC codes are not merely confined to such high error correction capability but also fit

them for parallelization to reduce circuit complexity. In an LSI for high speed optical communications, ease of

parallelized signal processing is mandatory for practicality. Among the various coding schemes, it is said that LDPC

is the most suitable code for parallelization.

Although the LDPC codes show superior potential as indicated above, there is a difficult problem to solve:

“error-floor”, frequently observed in the measured post-FEC BER. Even though an FEC may show superior

performance at a post-FEC BER of 10-7, if there is an error floor at

In the case of message-passing decoding, the girth dominates the error correction performance in the low post-FEC region. In order to reduce the error floor, larger g (~10) would be necessary.
There are two ways to increase the girth. One is expansion of the codeword length. According to a rough estimate, gs of 6, 8, and 10 are obtained from codeword lengths of 460, 4000, and 20000, respectively. By increasing the codeword length, a possible large loop can be discovered in the parity check matrix. However, longer codewords require increased circuit size. For 100 Gb/s class LSI, a codeword length g of 10 is very difficult to implement.
Another way to increase g is increase the redundancy. Regular LDPC codes having redundancy of 3%, 10%, and 35% enable their girths to be 6, 8, and 10, respectively. However, larger redundancy causes higher bit rate. In the case of 100GbE, 35% redundancy cannot be allowed because its bit rate approaches 140 Gb/s.
In slower speed systems, e.g. mobile and wireless, we can use either of the above methodologies, because their circuit implementation is not so difficult. However, in high speed optical communication systems, in particular 100GbE, very long codewords and/or large redundancy can never be allowed.

At the transmitter side, input information is encoded with the low- redundancy RS code, then the encoded

codeword is interleaved and put into the LDPC encoder. We selected the irregular Quasi-Cyclic (QC) LDPC code

[9] as the inner code, as this has the potential for very strong error correction performance.

At the receiver side, the O/E-converted signal is sampled by a k-bit soft-decider. Then the soft-input signal is

launched into the LDPC decoder. We use the cyclic approximated δ-minimum algorithm [10,11], which has the

potential to reduce the circuit complexity drastically without sacrificing error correction capability compared to the

conventional shuffled belief propagation (BP) algorithm [12]. The required number of operations and memory size

for the cyclic approximated δ-minimum algorithm are respectively 10 times and 5 times smaller than those for the

shuffled BP algorithm. After iterative decoding in the LDPC decoder, the output codeword is de-interleaved and

presented to the RS decoder. At this point, small number of errors (but which represent a serious error floor) still

remains. Concatenation of the RS code attempts to eliminate the error floor.

Although concatenated codes are well known to show good error correction performance, we should consider

carefully whether the residual number of error bits after the LDPC decoder can be corrected by an RS code. We

investigated the post-LDPC decoder behavior by Monte Carlo simulation. The test code was the irregular QC-LDPC

code having codeword and information lengths of 8148 and 6984, respectively. The redundancy is 16.7%. The

number of iterations was set to only 16 (including initialization). The number of soft-decision bits was only two.

Additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) was assumed for the communication channel.

5. Conclusions

We have proposed an efficient FEC for application to 100 Gb/s class optical transport systems using concatenated codes to combat error-floor effects. Thanks to the concatenation, unwanted error-floor can be eliminated without increasing the codeword length and/or the redundancy. According to the computer simulation, the proposed LDPC(9252,7967) + RS(992,956) having a redundancy of only 20.5% shows no error floor and is expected to achieve a Q-limit of 7.1 dB. Simple 2-bit soft decision and a cyclic approximated δ-minimum algorithm bring a high possibility of practical LSI implementation for 100GbE. It is anticipated that the proposed FEC will open up new vistas for higher speed, long distance transport systems with adequate system margins.
