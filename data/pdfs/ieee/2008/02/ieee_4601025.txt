Buffering Requirements for Variable-Iterations LDPC Decoders
Sarah L. Sweatlock, Sam Dolinar, and Kenneth Andrews Jet Propulsion Laboratory
California Institute of Technology Pasadena, CA, USA
Email: sarah@its.caltech.edu, {sam, andrews}@shannon.jpl.nasa.gov

Abstract— Low-density parity-check (LDPC) decoders, like iterative decoders for other block codes, can be designed to stop after a variable number of iterations, dependent on the difficulty of decoding particular noisy received words, also called frames. The number of iterations the decoder spends on a given frame determines both the probability of successful decoding, and the time expended. But whereas the speed of an LDPC decoder without a buffer is determined by its most difficult frames, the speed of a variable-iterations decoder with sufficient buffering approaches that determined by frames of average difficulty.
It is relatively straightforward to analyze this as a D/G/1 queuing problem combined with empirically measured probability distributions of iteration counts for specific LDPC codes. Our analysis parallels that of other researchers, e.g., [1], [2], [3], and examines the resulting implications on LDPC decoder design choices. We find that a buffer large enough to hold only B = 2 or 3 additional frames is sufficient to achieve near optimal performance. We prove a strong monotonicity condition: not only does a variable-iterations decoder with buffer size B + 1 frames outperform one with buffer size B in terms of average error rate, every single frame is guaranteed to receive at least as many iterations from the decoder with the larger buffer, if needed. Significantly, at low error rates, a variable-iterations decoder with buffer size B can keep pace with an input data rate B + 1 times faster than a fixed-iterations decoder with the same processing speed.
I. INTRODUCTION
Most performance results for low-density parity-check (LDPC) decoders are reported in the literature for a fixed number of iterations Imax. In actuality, many researchers save simulation time by incorporating a stopping rule that truncates the decoder’s iterations whenever it has already settled on a solution. The typical stopping rule is to check for satisfaction of all of the code’s parity constraints (the socalled codeword condition). The resulting variable-iterations decoder Dv(Imax) achieves essentially the same performance as the fixed-iterations decoder Df (Imax), but requires fewer iterations on the average, Iavg. In hardware, this advantage translates directly into a power savings. The variable-iterations decoder Dv(Imax) consumes average power proportional to Iavg, while the fixed-iterations decoder Df (Imax) consumes average power proportional to Imax.

If the received words could be fed to a variable-iterations decoder on demand, then its inherent advantage over a fixediterations decoder could be converted into a speed improvement rather than a power reduction. The throughput of the fixed-iterations decoder Df (Imax) is proportional to 1/Imax, while that of the variable-iterations decoder Dv(Imax) would be proportional to 1/Iavg. However, a real-world variableiterations decoder, faced with constant-rate streaming input, requires the use of buffering to achieve this potential speedup, as illustrated in Figure 1.

After synchronization, symbols are collected by a deserializer into a noisy codeword, hereafter called a frame, for decoding. When complete, this frame is instantaneously transferred to the buffer if used, or directly to the decoder, so that the deserializer may collect the channel symbols of the next frame. The first in, first out (FIFO) buffer stores up to B received words (B = 0 if there is no buffer), and normally each word is eventually transferred instantaneously to the decoder. The decoder performs some number of iterations according to its stopping rule, and then transfers the decoded result to the data sink, where it may be stored to memory, or further processed, or serialized again in streaming applications. The data transfers between the deserializer, buffer, decoder, and data sink may be genuinely instantaneous if implemented as pointer updates in circular buffers; otherwise, the finite delay may be included in the decoding time for analysis.
The maximum sustained data rate that a buffered decoder system can support is determined by the probability distribution P (I) of iterations required to decode each frame. This in turn is a function of the code, its rate and size, the signal-tonoise ratio Eb/N0 of the channel, and a technology-dependent factor giving the time required for the decoder to perform an iteration. To eliminate the technology dependence, we measure time in units of iterations. Instead of data rate, for example, we measure the frame interarrival time as Iarr iterations.

This process of individual frames claiming a potentially large number of iterations, but then yielding back the ones not needed, happens automatically in the variable-iterations decoder with preemptive buffer control. We need to back off the arrival rate a little (but not much) more than 2% from the decoding rate in order to achieve near-ideal WER, as will be shown Section IV.

Even if buffering cannot be used, perhaps due to a latency constraint, a stopping rule still decreases the decoder’s computation.

VI. CONCLUSION
Whereas the speed of a fixed-iterations LDPC decoder without a buffer is determined by its most difficult frames, the speed of a variable-iterations decoder with a reliable stopping rule and a buffer that can store a few frames approaches that determined by frames of average difficulty. The input buffer allows the decoder to average the necessary decoding time among several codewords, providing reliable communication based on this average, rather than on the worst case. We found that a buffer large enough to hold only B = 2 or 3 additional frames is sufficient to achieve near-optimal performance, at a throughput very close to that determined by the decoder’s required average number of iterations Iavg.
