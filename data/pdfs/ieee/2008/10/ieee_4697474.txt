A Flexible and Overlapped QC-LDPC Decoder
Gabofetswe Malema Department of Computer Science, University of Botswana
Gaborone,Botswana. E-mail: malemag@mopipi.ub.bw

Abstract
There are many low-density parity-check(LDPC) decoder architectures in the literature most of which are based on structured codes. Besides been specific to a particular class of codes, suggested architectures are limited in scalability. The major challenge in decoder design and implementation is the consideration of several strongly interrelated factors. These factors affect decoder computation and communication complexity, and error-correcting performance. In this paper we present a low-complexity, flexible and scalable LDPC decoder architecture for quasi-cyclic codes that supports multiple code designs (size, weights, rates, regular and irregular). The architecture is a result of a combination of flexible code construction and computation overlapping techniques. The architecture is based on overlapping techniques that allow sequential processing of check and variable nodes. The overlapping techniques lead to a low-complexity decoder interconnect compared to the existing techniques as they allow serial processing of code sub-matrices as compared to parallel processing of all sub-matrices in the existing overlapping technique. Although the architecture is based on quasi-cyclic codes, it can also be applied to random or other structured codes making it the most flexible, scalable and high-throughput partly-parallel LDPC decoder architecture.
1. Introduction
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have been shown to have good error correcting capabilities coming closer to the Shannon limit[1]. Because of their performance and relatively ease of implementation, they have been recommended for several applications such as broadband video broadcasting [2] and wireless networks [3]. However, for LDPC codes to be widely used in these applications and others they must meet their hardware implementation constraints as well as error-correcting performance. Hardware constraints include area,throughput, low-power and scalability. Most of these applications require several block lengths and rates. It is therefore important to have a flexible decoder architecture that also meets the mentioned constraints. This has proved to be a difficult task due to the strong interrelation of several factors that are considered in the design and implementation of a decoder architecture. There are several ways in which each factor can be optimized. Most often decoder architectures give more priority to one factor than others. Partly-parallel architectures are often implemented as they offer the best tradeoff between decoder complexity and throughput compared to serial and fully-parallel architectures. They are based on structured codes hence their design is as varied as structured code constructions. Because of the strong link between code construction and decoder design and implementation, the resulting decoder architectures have limited flexibility. They are often targeted for a class of codes with a single block length and rate. If other rates are executed extra hardware is needed[4]. In [5] a multi-rate and size architecture is designed only for irregular codes.
In this paper, we combine flexible code construction with computation overlapping to design a low-complexity, flexible, scalable and high-throughput quasi-cyclic LDPC decoder architecture. A flexible construction algorithm for quasi-cyclic codes developed in [6][7] is employed. It could be used to construct codes over a wide range of sizes and rates for both regular and irregular codes. Obtained codes have a girth of at least six which improves decoding performance of constructed codes. The most important advantage of this method compared to other methods is that it can be used with any sub-matrix configuration or arrangement. The flexibility in arranging sub-matrices allows the use of the employed overlapping techniques.
This paper is organized as follows. Section 2 presents the concept of overlapping in LDPC codes. In Section 3 we give an overview of existing overlapping technique for quasi-cyclic codes and its limitations. Section 4 discusses suggested overlapping techniques and their strengths and limitations. A decoder architecture based on the suggested overlapping techniques is introduced in section 5. Section 6 has concluding remarks.

Generally it cannot be guaranteed that all messages for a particular row or column would be available when needed if overlapping of computations is done arbitrarily. Figure 1 (a) shows conventional node computation scheduling. In the first half of each iteration check nodes are processed and variable nodes in the second half. Without overlapping one iteration is the time it takes to process all check nodes plus the time to process all variable nodes. Without overlapping the total decoding time is nT1 + nT2, where n is the number of iterations, T1 is the time for check nodes computations and T2 is the period for variable node computations. With overlapping the decoding time is equal to the number of cycles for processing check nodes plus the number of cycles check nodes processing is stalled(Wc). Nodes are stalled when they have to wait for their messages to be updated before computations. The decoding time could also be calculated as the number of variable nodes processing plus stalls(Wv).With overlapping the overall decoding time is less that nT1 + nT2.
For overlapping to work all messages for some columns must be available while some rows are still being processed. The same is true with rows. In [8] overlapping is achieved by matrix permutation for any matrix while in [9] the choice of starting rows and columns allows overlapping in quasi-cyclic codes. Overlapping does not only increase throughput by reducing processing time, it also improves hardware utilization efficiency. The increase in throughput is directly proportional to hardware utilization efficiency. Without overlapping processing nodes are unused half of the time. When check nodes processing units are computing, variable nodes processing units are idle.

4 Proposed QC-LDPC Overlapping Techniques
4.1 Matrix Permutation
A LDPC code matrix could be rearranged such that rows and columns that do not have connections in common are separated and are at extreme ends of the matrix space. With such a separation, rows and columns with no connections in common can be processed in parallel. Figure 4 shows a matrix space in which some rows and columns do not have connections in common. Row-column connections are in the shaded area. Since top-left rows do not have connections to extreme right columns, the rows and columns could be processed at the same time as they do not have data dependencies.

An algorithm was suggested in [8] to rearrange a given random parity-check matrix such that row-column connections with no connections in common are separated. The algorithm effectively moves row-column connections closer to the diagonal such that empty spaces are left at the left-bottom and top-right corners. Since top-left rows do not have connections at top-right corner, their processing could be overlapped with that of columns at bottom-right corner.
We have noticed that the algorithm in [8] is similar to sparse matrix reordering algorithms such as Reverse Cuthill Mackee and Gibbs-King’s algorithms[10]. These algorithms are used in several fields of study to reduce average row-bandwidth or matrix connection profile. We can thus use known sparse matrix permutation algorithms such as the ones mentioned here to reorder LDPC code matrices. The effectiveness of the permutation algorithms depends on the structure of the code (row-column interconnections and row and column weights). We expect well connected and high row and column weight codes to have less overlap. Well connected codes will have a few rows and columns without connections and high row and columns weights reduce the chances of having rows-columns without connections in common. In [8] a time reduction of 25% was obtained for a (N, 3, 6) example matrix. We apply the matrix permutation technique to the construction of quasi-cyclic codes by using the permuted matrix as a basis matrix. To construct quasi-cyclic codes we design a small code such as the example in Figure 5(a) to be the basis matrix. It is rearranged using a sparse rearranging algorithm to obtain a matrix similar to that one of Figure 5(b). An expanded matrix is then obtained from the rearranged basis matrix by replacing each ‘1’ by a p × p shifted identity sub-matrix and each ‘0’ entry by a p × p zero sub-matrix. To ensure girth of at least six, a flexible construction method suggested in [6][7] is used. The construction algorithm can work with any configuration of sub-matrixes. The expanded code has the same overlap and reduction in processing time as the basis matrix with the number of processing units remaining the same.

5 Decoder architectures for Proposed
Overlapping techniques
Our goal is to design a flexible and high-throughput decoder architecture for quasi-cyclic LDPC codes based on developed overlapping techniques. The architecture is flexible in that it supports multiple code sizes, rates, weights and both regular and irregular codes. Although matrix permutation and matrix space restriction techniques are limited in overlapping compared to the technique in [9], they require a relatively less complex architecture regardless of the code design. In these techniques, overlapping is achieved by processing sub-matrices with no connections in common in parallel. To have overlapping, sub-matrices are scheduled and processed sequentially in both check and variable processing nodes. This property allows us to have more compact decoder design and also have flexibility in the code design. Figure 10 shows a block diagram of our decoder. All the check and variable processing nodes compute the same sub-matrix at a time. The number of nodes could be one or more. Many processing nodes reduce the processing time. The interconnection between the processing nodes could be a shared memory as in the figure or a multistage interconnection network. With this architecture any LDPC code matrix resulting from matrix permutation or matrix restriction would be easily be mapped on to the decoder regardless of the code rate, number of sub-matrices or weights.

Processing can be divided into three stages of reading, computation and storing of messages and scheduled. Instead of waiting for each set of data to be read, computed and stored, different and consecutive sets of vector data are pipelining to further reduce decoding time. While data vector i is computed, the next vector i + 1 can be read. And as vector i is stored, vector i + 1 is computed and vector i + 2 is read.
Although the proposed decoder architecture is based on quasi-cyclic codes, it can be used for any structured or random code. The code is first constructed and permuted according to some permutation algorithm and mapped on to the architecture according to the processing schedule and the number of processing units. Or the code is designed and constructed according to some space restriction permitting overlapping. In both cases the architectures of Figure 12 and 13 will execute the codes regardless of its design (code size, rate, weights, regular or irregular). To the best of our knowledge, this architecture is the most flexible, scalable and high-throughput for partly-parallel LPDC decoders. However, with respect to matrix space-restriction, the loss in error-correcting performance has to be evaluated. For random codes, random construction algorithms such as bit-filling[11] and progressive-edge growth[12] could be used to maximize girth and improve error-correcting performance. These algorithms are good at constructing short LDPC codes with optimized girth, rates and performance.
6 Conclusion
A flexible and high-throughput LDPC decoder architecture has been developed in this paper. The architecture has high throughput by using overlapping of computations and pipelining the decoding process. Flexibility is achieved by the combination of flexible construction methods and overlapping techniques. Error-correcting performance is improved by improving the girth of constructed codes. We showed that overlapping techniques affect the decoder architecture. Overlapping techniques based on internal connections of sub-matrix connections require that all submatrices be processed in parallel. This property complicates the decoder architecture. With varying code sizes, rates and weights, it becomes difficult to distribute all sub-matrices to processing units and communicate efficiently. The tech-

nique is also applicable to quasi-cyclic codes only. On the other hand, matrix permutation and space restriction overlapping techniques process sub-matrices in serial to achieve overlapping. They separate unconnected row and columns which are then processed in parallel. The number of submatrices and weights of the code do not affect the decoder structure. Sub-matrices can be processed with same computation schedule regardless of the number of sub-matrices and code rate or weights. The resulting architecture could also be applied to other structured or random codes. The architecture is more flexible, scalable with high-throughput compared to existing architectures. However, with matrix space-restriction, the error-correction performance needs to be evaluated.
