A new algorithm of counting the number of small Stopping sets and girth in QC-LDPC Codes

Lingjun Kong Yang Xiao

Institute of Information Science, Beijing Jiaotong University Beijing 100044, P. R. China E-mail: kljangel401@163.com

Abstract: It is well known that the performance of low-density parity check (LDPC) codes under iterative decoding is determined by certain combinatorial structures (such as stopping sets and girth) in the Tanner graph. The difficulty in enumerating all possible combinations of the columns may prevent an efficient search of good LDPC codes with small stopping sets and girth. To solve the problem, this paper presents a new algorithm of counting the number of small stopping sets and girth by analyzing the shapes of the cycles of Tanner graph in parity check matrix for designing good LDPC codes which is less complex than the existing algorithms. This method can be used effectively to evaluate the performance of LDPC codes according to their small stopping sets and girth distributions. Key words: Quasi-Cyclic low-density parity-checkЛ„LDPCЛ… codes, circulant matrices, stopping sets, stopping distance, girth, cycle, check algorithm
I. INTRODUCTIONS
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes were originally introduced by Gallager [1-2]. They have again become interesting because of the success of iterative decoding for turbo codes. LDPC codes have a remarkable performance with iterative decoding that is very close to the Shannon limit over additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels. Quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check codes are a special class of LDPC codes whose parity-check matrices consist of circulant matrices. They are more favorable for hardware implementation because of the efficient encoding of QC-LDPC codes by simple shift-register circuits [3-6]. LDPC codes can be described by a
Tanner graph which is formally denoted as (Vv вҲӘ Cc , E) ,
where Vv is set of variable nodes. Cc is the set of check nodes and the set of E is the set of edges such that E = Vv Г— Cc , with edge (ci , v j ) вҲҲ E if and only if
hi, j вү  0, hi, j вҲҲ H .
With iterative decoding, the bad performance of the codes is due primarily to the small stopping sets that LDPC codes have. The girth of the code, defined as the length of the shortest cycle in its factor graph, is a crucial parameter. Cycles, especially short cycles, degrade the performance of LDPC decoders, because they affect the independence of the extrinsic information exchanged in the iterative decoding.1
Although the stopping sets and girth are studied in many papers [6-12], there is no efficient algorithm to construct QC-LDPC codes without small stopping sets and girth. Our work was first motivated by the difficulty which needs enumerating all possible combinations of the columns. Instead of enumerating all possible combinations, we develop a systematic and efficient algorithm to count the number of the

II. TRIANGLE ANTENNA ARRAY MODEL
An LDPC code is called (dv , dc ) -regular if in its Tanner graph, every variable node participates in dv check nodes and every check node involves dc variable nodes.
Stopping sets were introduced in [6] for the analysis of erasure decoding of LDPC codes, where, it was shown that the iterative decoder fails to decode to a codeword if and only if the set of error positions is a superset of some stopping set in the Tanner graph for the code used in decoding.
Definition 1(Stopping set): A stopping set S in an LDPC code is a subset of the variable nodes of the associated Tanner graph, such that all neighbors of S are connected to S at least twice.

Definition 2(Stopping distance): The smallest size of a
nonempty stopping set, denoted by dS , is called the stopping
distance of the LDPC code. The stopping distance is an important performance indicator for iterative decoding alike the minimum distance for maximum-likelihood decoding. In particular, a good LDPC code must have few, or better yet, no small stopping sets.

LDPC codes and commonly constructed QC-LDPC codes of the same block length for a BPSK-modulated AWGN channel with SNR. The iterative BP algorithm was used for decoding.
From Figure 3 we can see that the designed QC-LDPC codes based on our proposed theorems which have no small stopping sets and no small girth take on the absolute predominance than the general QC-LDPC codes.
