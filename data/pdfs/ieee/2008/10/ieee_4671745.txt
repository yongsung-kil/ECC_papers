ABSTRACT
In this paper the impact of the roundoff error on the decisions taken by the Log Sum-Product LDPC decoding algorithm is studied. The mechanism, by means of which roundoff alters the decisions of a finite word length implementation of the algorithm compared to the infinite precision case, is analyzed and a corresponding theoretical model is developed. Experimental results prove the validity of the proposed model.
Index Terms— Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, finite wordlength effects, error correction coding.

1. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity check (LDPC) codes [1] have recently become popular and have already been adopted in a variety of standards ranging from satellite video (DVB-S2) to 10GBase-T Ethernet (802.3an). They are expected to play even more important role in the future, due to favorable computational complexity and performance that closely approximates the Shannon limit [2].
Decoding LDPC codes by using message-passing algorithms is a common technique. As their name indicates, message-passing algorithms exchange messages between computation units, which correspond to operations modeled by nodes of a bipartite code graph.
Several VLSI architectures have been proposed for implementing the decoding of LDPC encoded messages, either for general codes [3], or for codes of special structure [4].
The determination of the finite word lengths required to represent data is a tedious and time consuming procedure, which requires extensive simulations. It is the first of a series of steps that lead from a given algorithm to an appropriate VLSI implementation. 3. IMPACT OF ROUNDOFF ON DECISIONS

As Φ(x) is transcendental, approximation error and finite word length effects affect its computation. A trade-off arises between the accuracy in the computation of Φ(x) and the complexity of the corresponding hardware implementation. Reduction in accuracy reduces complexity, but only at the cost of reduced decoding performance of the algorithm. Various schemes have been proposed in order to efficiently realize Φ onto hardware, such as piece-wise linear approximation [9] or non-uniform quantization [10]. A way to compute Φ(x) is to store it in a lookup table using a uniform quantization scheme [11]. Quantization of Φ(x) and finite word length effects have an impact on the performance of the algorithm, by means of two interleaved mechanisms, studied in [12]. In this paper the roundoff impact is related to performance of the algorithm by studying the probabilities that decisions taken by the finite word length implementation of algorithm differ from those made by the accurate implementation at the end of each iteration.

5. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
In order to check the validity of the proposed theoretical model, several simulations were carried out for different quantization schemes of Φ(x). For each quantization scheme two different decoders were simulated. The first one uses infinite accuracy for the representation of Φ(x) and the second one uniformly quantizes Φ(x) using i integral and f fractional bits. Each decoder executes one iteration. The decisions taken by both decoders are compared and the ratio of different decisions is determined.
The parameters of Gaussian Mixture (μ, σm) and Gaussian (σe) distributions that approximate the original decision messages and the roundoff error pdfs respectively, are depend on code parameters (variable and check node degrees) and Φ(x) quantization scheme. In this paper, parameters values are determined experimentally for each simulation scenario. These values are used in the model given by (15), in order to approximate the probability of changing the decision of the algorithm due to roundoff.

6. CONCLUSIONS
This paper investigates the way in which the finite word length representation of Φ(x), alters the decisions reached by the Log Sum-Product algorithm at the end of each iteration with respect to the decisions of an infinite precision implementation. The mechanism through which roundoff alters decisions is analyzed and a mathematical description of this mechanism is developed. The result is a theoretical model that computes the probability of changing decisions due to finite word length representation. The proposed model can be used as a tool that computes the expected degradation of the
