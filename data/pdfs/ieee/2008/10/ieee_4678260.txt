Modified Decoding for Low Density Parity Check Codes over Power Line Channels

Wen-miao Song, Lei Huang, Cheng-qun Yin, Gui-yun Wang School Of Electric And Electronic Engineering North China Electric Power University Baoding, China hl830203@163.com

Abstract—Power Line Communication for home networking is a new convenient method. However, power line communication makes degradation because of the impulsive interference generated by electrical appliances. Therefore, it is the first problem we need to be solved. In this paper, we apply LDPC (Low Density Parity Check) codes to AWAN (Additive White Class A Noise) channels which is usually used as the model of impulsive noise environment of power line channels. We propose a modification to Log-BP (Log domain Belief Propagation) decoding which is suitable for AWAN channels. At last, we show the BER (Bit Error Rate) performance of the modified Log-BP decoding over AWAN channels by Monte-Carlo simulation.
Keywords-power line communication; AWAN; LDPC; Log-BP
I. INTREDUTION
With the fast development of the Internet, home networking attracts more and more attention. Now, low voltage power line has accessed common family, residential power line is one of the most attractive communication media for home networking. However, many electrical appliances frequently cause manmade electromagnetic noise on power line channels. Such manmade noise has the impulsive characteristics. This is one of technical problems to realize the power line communications with high rate and high reliability. Middleton’s Class A noise is usually used as the model of impulsive noise environment of power line channels. Class A noise model proposed by Middleton is one of non-Gaussian noise models and is currently applied to the modeling of man-made impulsive noise channels. Class A noise and Gaussian noise are different, predecessors have made many achievements in AWGN channels, but the research of AWAN [1] channels is quite rare. In this paper, we will apply LDPC codes and modified Log-BP [2] decoding to the AWAN channels. LDPC codes were introduced to the coding community by Gallager in 1962 [3], but it was not until 1995, when MacKay and Neal rediscovered them, for their full potential to be understood. It has received a lot of attention lately due to its excellent error-correcting capability and implementation friendly decoding algorithms. With coding gain approaching Shannon limit and lower hardware complexity than Turbo codes [4], LDPC has been considered by one of next generation communication standards. So we consider that LDPC codes could solve the problem which Power line channels suffer from impulsive interference. As we know, the statistical feature of Class A noise is so different from that of Gaussian noise, and the

conventional BP (Belief Propagation) decoding is suitable for AWGN channels. Therefore, we must make a modification of the conventional decoding for AWAN channels. In the end, by simulation results, we compare the performance of modified Log-BP decoding with that of the conventional BP decoding over AWAN channels, and conclude that the modified Log-BP decoding has better performance for suppressing impulsive interference over power line channels.
II. MIDDLETON’S CLASS A NOISE MODEL
Middleton’s Class A noise model could be a statistical model of impulsive noise environment. It is widely applicable by adjusting parameters, and provides fine closeness to experimental values. Middleton’s noise model is composed of sum of Gaussian noise and impulsive noise. The model is categorized into three types by the bandwidth of impulsive noise, Class A, B, and C. The bandwidth of Class A noise is narrower than the bandwidth of the receiving system, The bandwidth of Class B noise is larger than the bandwidth and the bandwidth of Class C noise is comparable to the bandwidth of the receiving system. Among them, Class A noise model is used to represent the noise on power line [5]. III. LDPC CODES
LDPC codes are invented by Gallager in 1962, which are now one of intensely studied areas in coding due to their near shannon limit performance and parallel decoding architecture. LDPC codes can be described by their corresponding bipartite graphs called “Tanner graphs” [6] or by their sparse parity check matrix H. The bipartite graph is a graph whose nodes are separated into two classes and whose edges only connect two nodes between different classes. The length of the shortest cycle in a Tanner graph is referred to as girth of the graph, and the LDPC codes with large girth imply good codes. The Tanner graph corresponds to the parity-check matrix H. The column weight means the number of “1” included in each column of the parity check matrix. A regular LDPC code has a paritycheck matrix whose column weight is constant and an irregular LDPC code does not have constant column weight. Generally, it is known that a regular LDPC code whose column weight is 3 has the best BER performance regardless of the code length. In this paper, we use regular LDPC codes and avoid short cycles in the Tanner graph. We compose a sparse parity check matrix H whose size is M×N by Gallager’s methods, and calculate the generator matrix G whose size is K×N from the parity-check matrix H. VI. CONCLUSION
For suppressing impulsive interference on power line channels, we have studied the modified Log-BP decoding for LDPC codes over AWAN model, which is usually used as the model of impulsive noise environment of power line channels. By simulation results, we have confirmed that the conventional BP decoding over AWAN channels has the degradation about 13dB compared with AWGN channels. We have modified the LLR for Log-BP decoding which is suitable for AWAN channels. Then, we have compared the BER performance of conventional BP decoding with that of modified Log-BP decoding and uncoded BPSK, and conclude that the modified Log-BP decoding can have excellent performance over AWAN
channels. At last, we see that A and * , the parameters of Class
A noise model are very important for the modified Log-BP decoding. If the values are not suitable, the impulsive noise will not be suppressed well over AWAN channels.
