Abstract—A low power, current-mode memory element for analog discrete time iterative decoders is proposed. In the circuit a high-speed power-down mechanism has been implemented that enables a significant increase of the operation speed without increasing the power dissipation. During the power down phase the data stored in the memory is maintained in the capacitor. The proposed memory element works at a sampling frequency of 10 MSps. During the normal operation the memory cell dissipates power of 1.5-ȝW, while in the standby phase 50-nW.

I. INTRODUCTION
Analog memory elements are used in various applications, where different optimizing criteria are important. For instance, for analog/digital converters speed, write precision and linearity are the most important criterions [1, 2, 3] resulting in a relatively complex structure. On the other hand, in analog adaptive systems (filters and low speed neural networks), the adaptation mechanism ensures compensation of the writing errors and high precision although important is not the main criterion; these elements must rather be simple and power efficient due to the large number of them used in the circuit. A long storage period is important as well [4] in this case. For analog iterative decoders, where data processing is parallel and thousands of bits are evaluated at the same time (see e.g. [8]), thousands of analog memory elements are needed. Thus there is a strong demand for a high speed, ultra low power elements with simple structures to minimize overall power dissipation and chip area.
In this paper we propose a sampling circuit for an analog iterative decoder structure based on discrete-time processing of log-likelihood messages (a decoder architecture proposed in [8]), shown conceptually in Fig. 1. It contains two layers of calculation nodes, A and B, representing equality calculations and parity check calculations. In time, when one layer is working, the second layer, together with associated memory elements is switched off to save power. The speed of the circuit is also an important criterion. We assume that input data are delivered to the decoder with a frequency equal to 1 MHz, representing a “typical” scenario of 1Gbps decoding with code length of 1000 bits. During the period of 1 Ps,

The decoder realized in ASIC, containing thousands of calculation nodes, has relatively long connecting paths, whose parasitic capacitances are on the order of 100-500 fF. The proposed circuit contains two novel mechanisms, which enable a reduction of the power dissipation without reducing the circuit sampling frequency. The first mechanism, which enables a power down phase, is realized by switches controlled by the x signal. These switches are placed only in PMOS type current mirrors, and therefore their influence on the storage capacitor, placed in the NMOS type current mirror, is strongly minimized. When x is a logical 1, nodes p1 and p2 are shorted to the supply VDD voltage, suppressing currents I1 to I4. At the same time potentials in points p3 and p4 go to ground, but the capacitor CP (potential at node p5) is not discharged. This enables the data stored across this capacitor to be only updated during the writing phase (signal y = 1) by a small factor of charge in each calculation iteration. This is one of the main advantages of the proposed circuit, looking from a power dissipation point of view. In power down phase the power dissipation of the entire element is reduced to only 4% of its value during normal operation.
One of the problems encountered here is a long wake up process after a power down phase, when parasitic gate-tosource capacitances (CGS) in PMOS transistors are discharged and must be charged again. This problem is especially visible for relatively small input currents. Because these transistors are switched off, and currents I1 - I4 are very small, the charging process is very slow at the beginning and speeds up, while transistors are gradually switching on, as illustrated in Fig. 3 (a - see current I4).
The other problem is that even for large input currents there is a current threshold, below which the circuit is very slow, meaning that for the assumed sampling frequency it does not work. This threshold depends on the transistor model and temperature, but its average value is equal to about 450 nA for VDD = 1V. This problem can be typically solved by increasing the currents level, but this increases the power dissipation. To solve this problem an additional precharging mechanism has been implemented. One of the ways to speed up the circuit is to minimize the time constant for charging the parasitic capacitor CP, which in practice means minimizing the on-resistance of switch S1 (see Figure 2) by increasing transistor dimensions in the switch. The capacitance that serves as memory storage cannot be reduced. This unfortunately increases the charge injection effect across the CP storage capacitor. This negative effect has been strongly minimized by use of a dummy switch DS, which is controlled by signals of opposite polarity. Because reading-out of the data always occurs in the same circuit phase

It is worth noting that the proposed power-down mechanism does not distort the information stored across the capacitor CP. This capacitor is insulated from both places where the precharge mechanism operates by a large gate-tochannel resistance of transistors MN2 and MP2. The information stored across this capacitor after power-down differs by only 0.01% and can be neglected in this application.

V. CONCLUSIONS
A low-power current-mode S&H element with a highspeed power down phase has been presented. Introducing a precharging mechanism significantly speeds up the wake up phase after a power down phase, which enables a decrease in input current and a reduction in the power dissipation. For a 10-MSps sampling frequency the average power dissipation when not in power down phase is 1500-nW per cell. In the power down phase the power dissipation is about 40-nW. Without a precharge mechanism the cell needs about 140-ns to charge the parasitic capacitances after power down phase, when the precharge mechanism is used this time is shorten to about 50-ns. This enables increasing sampling frequency by about 2.5 times.
Assuming a 50% duty cycle, for a 3,6-regular LDPC code with differential signaling (24 S&H cells per information bit), this represents an addition of only 0.5(401500)24 18.5pJ per decoded information bit. This does not represent a significant increase to the energy cost per bit of typical reported analog decoders. Using the proposed memory cell the discrete time analog decoder can be implemented, where roughly half of the overall analog circuitry is in a power down phase at any time, which compensates the energy that is lost in the memory array.
