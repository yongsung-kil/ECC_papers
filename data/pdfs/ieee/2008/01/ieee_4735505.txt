Abstract—Irregular-repeat-accumulate (IRA) codes are randomlike codes having extremely good performance over AWGN channel. They can be viewed as special kinds of LDPC codes in the way their parity-check matrix are sparse. Like LDPC codes, they can be decoded using the messages-passing algorithm efficiently, while the existence of short length circles in their corresponding factor graph representation has a deleterious effect on the BER performance of the codes. Progressive edgegrowth (PEG) algorithm is a famous method to construct factor graph without short circles. In this paper, we use PEG algorithm to construct the parity-check matrix of IRA codes firstly, and then deduce the interleaver design of the codes. Experimental results show significant improvement on the BER performance of the IRA codes with designed interleaver over the IRA codes with randomly choose interleaver.
I. INTRODUCTION
Since 1993, more and more sophisticated classes of turbo and LDPC codes have been forwarded by coding theorists. One such class of codes, called repeat-accumulate (RA) codes, was investigated by Divsalar et al [1] as simple turbo-like codes. It was shown that their performance was comparable with that of other codes defined on sparse graphs. RA codes can be represented as low-density partity-check (LDPC) codes. The main advantage of RA codes over LDPC codes is that the complexity of encoding is linear in the code length. And, RA codes enjoy an advantage over turbo codes in that they offer a more flexible code structure, with many parameters that can be tuned to achieve a particular design objective.
An RA code is a serial concatenation of a repetition code and an accumulator through an interleaver: a length-k
information block is repeated q times, scrambled by a interleaver 3 , and then re-organized by a modulo 2 combiner, in sets of a bits, before being passed to the accumulator.

B PEG Algorithm
Constructing a factor graph with maximized girth is combinatorially difficult. A sub-optimum algorithm named by PEG has been designed to construct a factor graph with relatively large girths. In order to adapt PEG to design factor graph for IRA codes, we will review the idea of PEG first.
In the PEG algorithm, the local girth of a variable node is maximized whenever a new edge is added to the node.
Suppose we have finished constructing edges of the first l
variable nodes on a factor graph, i.e., edges. Let g' be the temporary girth under the current graph, i.e.
g' min{g0 , g1,..., gl1}. The problem now lies in selecting
the edge set Esi such that adding these new edges to the
current graph setting does not excessively impair the
current g ' . This means optimizing dsj edges of Esi to
maximize the local girth. It is noted that if adding to the
current graph results in a cycle shorter than g ' , this new

III. SIMULATION RESULTS
In this section, some simulation results will be given to show that the IRA codes with proposed interleavers performs better than the IRA codes with randomly chosen interleavers. We use BPSK modulation over additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel with messages-passing algorithm for iterative decoding[10]. The maximum number of iterations for the decoder is set to 50. We use two groups of parameters based on optimized degree distribution in [1] to construct IRA

codes. To have a fair comparison between PEG and random design interleaver, the codes lengths are both 1000.

Fig.5 shows the performance of IRA codes with code parameters f ( f3 0.252744, f11 0.081476, f12 0.327162 , f46 0.184589, f48 0.154029) and a=8, with code rate 1/2[5].
In Fig.4 and Fig.5, the round lines are the performance with PEG interleavers while the triangle lines are the performance with random interleavers. It can be seen that the designed PEG designed interleavers perform better than random ones, especially in high rate IRA codes. In PEG designed interleavers, there are no length-4 circle exists, and the local girths is 6, this is the reason why it performs better.

IV. CONCLUSIONS
IRA codes are candidates for future communication systems. Constructing good IRA codes at finite block lengths has been the subject of intense research. In this paper, we use factor graph to analyse IRA codes and conclude that the short length cycle will affect the detection performance of the codes.
