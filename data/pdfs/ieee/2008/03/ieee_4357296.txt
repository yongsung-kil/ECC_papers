Girth-10 LDPC Codes Based on 3-D Cyclic Lattices
Fan Zhang, Xuehong Mao, Wuyang Zhou, Member, IEEE, and Henry D. Pfister, Member, IEEE

AbstractвҖ”In this paper, we propose a new method based on combinatorial designs for constructing high-girth low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. We use a 3-D lattice to generate balanced incomplete block designs based on planes and lines in the lattice. This gives families of regular LDPC codes with girths of at least 6, 8, and 10, whose parity-check matrices are all block circulant. The main advantage of this construction is that the algebraic structure leads to efficient encoders and decoders. Based on the block-circulant structure of a parity-check matrix, we present an efficient encoder that can be parallelized to improve the speed of encoding. The simulation results show that these families of LDPC codes perform very well on additive-white-Gaussian-noise channels (roughly 0.45 dB from the channel capacity) and Rayleigh fading channels (roughly 0.51 dB from the channel capacity).
Index TermsвҖ”Channel coding, combinatorial design, lowdensity parity-check (LDPC) codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
A. Background
L OW-DENSITY parity-check (LDPC) codes were first introduced by Gallager in 1962 [1] and were rediscovered by MacKay [2]. Gallager [1] also provided a messagepassing decoding algorithm whose resource complexity and time complexity remain linear in the block length. Recently, it has been shown that the encoding complexity of certain irregular LDPC codes can also be linear in the block length [3]. Irregular LDPC codes can be designed so that the relationship between code bits and parity-check equations optimizes the flow of information in the decoding process. Some authors have shown that carefully designed LDPC codes can be efficiently decoded close to the Shannon limit for various channels and code rates [4]вҖ“[6]. In [7], Luby et al. designed irregular LDPC codes, which achieved the capacity of erasure channels. They also optimized codes for the binary erasure channel and the binary-input additive white Gaussian noise (BI-AWGN) channel [5]. In [9], Hou et al. optimized irregular LDPC codes for the uncorrelated Rayleigh fading channel and showed that LDPC codes can achieve excellent performance and even outperform turbo codes on correlated Rayleigh fading channels. Another main advantage of LDPC codes is that LDPC-based STC has lower receiver complexity and more flexible scalability compared with a turbo-codebased STC scheme. The conclusion was that the system is a promising solution for high-efficiency data transmission over mobile wireless channels. Many state-of-the-art protocols now include LDPC codes as an optional channel-coding scheme. We can see that LDPC codes have advantages on a variety of channels and have been widely applied in many wireless communication systems.
Irregular LDPC codes typically outperform turbo codes when the block length is large (e.g., greater than 4000). In general, irregular LDPC codes may lead to complicated hardware implementations because of the uncertainty of the node degrees. It is proven in [1] that regular LDPC codes with a column weight of j вүҘ 3 have a minimum distance that linearly grows with block length N . On the contrary, the minimum distance of regular LDPC codes with a column weight of j = 2 logarithmically grows with N . Since the minimum distance is important for diversity on fading channels, we consider, in this paper, the construction of regular LDPC codes with j = 3.
B. LDPC Codes
The Tanner graph shows the relationship between code bits and parity checks in an LDPC. Each of the n bits and the m parity checks in H is represented by a node in the graph. The edges in the graph connect all code bits to the paritycheck vertices that include them. The graph is bipartite since no parity check (or bit node) is connected to another parity check (or bit node) by an edge. The length of the cycle is simply the number of edges it contains, and the girth of a Tanner graph is the length of its shortest cycle. Generally, LDPC code construction methods are based on one of four approaches: 1) graph theory; 2) finite geometry; 3) experimental design; or 4) combinatorial design theory. With the graph-theoretic approach, codes are constructed based on random bipartite graphs, specific graphs, or constrained paths of connected graphs. Zhang and Moura [12] provided a new family of (n, 2, k)-regular LDPC codes with girths of 12, 16, and 20. Because the column weight of the parity-check matrix is 2, the performance, even with a large girth, is not as good as expected. The construction based on finite geometries defines LDPC codes in terms of the points, lines, and planes of a finite geometry over a finite field. In [13], regular and irregular codes are constructed using finite geometries, and simulation results show that they have very good performance over AWGN channels. VI. CONCLUSION

Fig. 7. Iterations needed to successfully decode under the SPA algorithm.

In this paper, we have presented constructions of LDPC codes based on combinatorial designs with girths of 6, 8, and 10. The main principle is to carefully design the lines in a 3-D lattice to avoid triangles and quadrangles. This way, we generate a number of BIBDs and use the point-block incidence matrices as the parity-check matrices. This method provides a large range of code lengths and code rate. Due to the regularity of the construction, the resulting codes are regular LDPC codes with a block-circulant structured parity-check matrix. In the second part of this paper, we have demonstrated how to design a linear time and resource complexity encoder and proposed the detail architecture of the encoder. The decoder design is not described in detail in this paper. Simulation results show that 3-D lattice LDPC codes have excellent performance over Rayleigh fading channels and BI-AWGN channels. It is also shown that the high-girth codes have better performance (less than 0.45 dB from the channel capacity) over BI-AWGN channels and fewer decoding iterations, on average, than low-girth codes.
