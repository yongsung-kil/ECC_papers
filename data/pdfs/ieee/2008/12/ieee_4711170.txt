Design of Versatile eIRA Codes for Parallel Decoders
Libero Dinoi, Francesco Sottile, and Sergio Benedetto, Fellow, IEEE

Abstract—In this paper we propose a semi-random technique for the generation of a class of eIRA codes (a popular class of irregular LDPC codes that can be encoded in linear time) suited to partially parallel decoder implementations. The suggested technique tries to avoid both low-weight codewords and nearcodewords, which limit the performance of the belief propagation decoder. Its effectiveness is verified by comparison with literature results. The obtained codes are versatile, in terms of code-rate and block length, and they are characterized by a low error floor.
Index Terms—Low-density parity-check, LDPC, minimum distance, near-codewords, parallel decoders.
I. INTRODUCTION
L OW-DENSITY PARITY-CHECK (LDPC) [1] codes are the most serious competitor to turbo codes [2] in the field of channel coding, as they have been shown to approach channel capacity [3]. Moreover, attractive features of LDPC codes are their low-complexity iterative decoding algorithms and the ease with which they can be matched to parallel decoder architectures in order to achieve a very high throughput.
In fact, a common requirement of many important system applications is the need for very high data rates, in the order of several hundred Mbit/s, accompanied by low latency; this is the case for fiber optic communication systems or multimedia applications requiring the transferring of large batches of data. A very common strategy, pursued also in the case of turbo codes, is to implement the decoder in a parallel fashion: the decoding complexity is shared between several processors, which operate in parallel on different portions of the codeword. It is customary to define the number of processors as the parallelism degree of the decoder.
Communication systems may also require to support multiple values of frame duration (due to constraints on maximum latency) and multiple data rates (due to limitations on available bandwidth, or because of different modulation

Therefore, it is crucial to support several values of block length and code rate, with small additional complexity and good performance.
In this paper we propose a design algorithm for eIRA (extended Irregular Repeat Accumulate) codes [4][5][6], a special class of LDPC codes that has become very popular because of its very nice properties (good performance and linear-time encoding), and in fact has been selected for the DVB-S2 standard [7]. The paper is structured as follows: After a brief introduction to LDPC codes and to eIRA codes, provided in Sections II and III, respectively, in Section IV we will propose a new pseudo-random design algorithm for the generation of codes with low error floor. Then in Sections V and VI we will discuss issues related to parallelism and versatility. Finally in Section VII we will justify the validity of our approach by comparisons with literature results, and concluding remarks will be provided in Section VIII.
II. LDPC CODES OVERVIEW
A. Basic Definitions
eIRA codes can be interpreted in many different ways: As shown in [6], they are a special case of Irregular Repeat Accumulate Codes [8], or a special type of SCCC [9], or irregular LDPC codes, and, to be more precise, members of the class of the so-called “Multi-edge” type LDPC codes introduced in [10].
In this paper we consider them as a type of LDPC codes. An LDPC code is a linear block code defined by an m × n sparse parity-check matrix, which represents the relation between the n bits of the codeword and the m parity-check equations that specify the code. Another description of LDPC codes can be provided using Tanner graphs: each of the bits and of the parity-check equations, defined respectively as Variable Nodes (VNs) and Check Nodes (CNs), is represented by a vertex in the graph; VNs are connected only to CNs and vice versa. The number of edges connected to a vertex of the graph is defined as the degree of that node; the VN (or CN) degree distribution of a code specifies how the edges are distributed among the variable (or check) nodes of the code. In a regular (c, r) LDPC code all VNs have degree c and all CNs have degree r in the graph. Irregular LDPC codes have been shown [3] to

IV. THE PROPOSED DESIGN ALGORITHM
Our design algorithm consists of a trial-and-error technique to design the systematic part of the parity-check matrix of eIRA codes: A new column is generated according to the desired degree distribution and some constraints, that we will describe later on, are enforced. If the new column does not meet the constraints, a new one is generated. If excessively strict constraints are imposed, then it may happen that no valid column is obtained and the algorithm gets stuck in an infinite loop. In this case the algorithm is stopped, and milder constraints are imposed. The constraints enforced in the code design algorithm can be divided into two categories: constraints which limit the set of the admissible degree distributions and constraints which are applied only to the cycle structure of the code.

B. Cycle Structure Constraints
Three constraints on the cycle structure are proposed: Two of them have been inherited by papers that have previously appeared in the literature, while the third one is novel and original.
The first constraint is just a simplified version of one of the design criteria described in [13]: To guarantee a high EMD value for short cycles means fundamentally that short cycles should be composed of high-degree VNs. We do not retain the rather complicated rules of [13], but simply enforce two different values of girth for cycles made only of systematic VNs and for cycles that include also parity-check bits which, having only degree two, do not contribute to an increase of the EMD. These two values of girth will be referred to in the following as Gs and Gm, respectively (with “s” and “m” standing for “systematic” and “mixed”). We will keep Gm > Gs, to make sure that the cycles with parity-check bits (i.e. with an average lower EMD) are longer than the ones made only of systematic bits.
Secondly, we enforce the same test of [5], to avoid lowweight codewords: When generating a new column of the matrix, we check that the codewords with information weight 1 and 2, involving it and the already assigned columns, have at least D1,2 parity check bits equal to 1.

VI. ADDING VERSATILITY TO EIRA CODES
A. Variable-rate Codes
The rate of LDPC codes can be changed at least according to two different strategies, puncturing and extension.
1) Puncturing: Puncturing is a very common solution to achieve flexibility in terms of code-rate, but the puncturing patterns have to be designed very carefully, in order to avoid dramatic losses. In [41] the effect of puncturing on the convergence threshold of LDPC codes is studied, and a framework for the design of puncturing patterns is provided. However, using a similar approach, the authors of [42] show that puncturing is not effective over a wide range of values; therefore they use puncturing only in a narrow range and, to obtain lower code-rates in a wide range, extend the paritycheck matrix by adding new columns and new rows according to a pre-determined criterion. A similar but more efficient approach was employed in [43].
In [42] it was noted that a critical factor for the performance of punctured codes was the fraction of CNs connected to more than one punctured VN, which should be kept very low. This observation is justified in a more analytic way using the framework in [14], where the relationship between code performance and stopping sets is analyzed: It is shown that, if all the bits of a stopping set are erased, then error-free decoding cannot be achieved on the binary erasure channel; this obviously holds also for punctured bits on the AWGN channel. The observation of [42] is then explained, because if there are no CNs connected to more than one punctured VN,
