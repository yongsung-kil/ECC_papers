Abstract
For the construction of some quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check codes, it is desired to find all the shortest balanced cycles in a base matrix. In this paper, we give some necessary and sufficient conditions for the existence of balanced cycles. Furthermore, we show that any of the shortest balanced cycles in a base matrix consists of the points of a sub-matrix of some patterns which are completely determined.
1. Introduction
Many low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes with the capacity-approaching performance were proposed in the last decade. However, for many LDPC codes, their encoding complexities are very high. It is desired to design efficiently encodable LDPC codes. Among these codes, quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes receive more attention due to their linear-time encodability and small size of required memory. For an LDPC code C with a specified parity check matrix H, its girth is the length of the shortest cycle in the Tanner graph corresponding to H. A well-known method for constructing QC-LDPC codes is to expand a small matrix, called base matrix or mother matrix, with circulants so as to lift the girth[1, 2, 3, 4]. However, because of the existence of balanced cycles (also called inevitable cycles), the girth of a base matrix cannot be lifted up some value if only using circulants. To overcome this weak point, affine permutation matrices or general permutation matrices are suggested to be used in the expansion. Anyway, it is important to give all the shortest balanced cycles of a base matrix. In [2, 3, 4], some sufficient conditions for the existence of balanced cycles

2. Paths and Cycles
A point of a binary matrix M means a position where the element of M is nonzero. The set of points of M is also denoted by M . A subset of M are said to be matchable if they are in the same row or in the same column. Let RC denote the set of matchable subsets of M . Clearly, for Ω1, Ω2 ∈ RC with |Ω1 ∩ Ω2| ≥ 2, we have Ω1 ∪Ω2 ∈ RC. If γ is a sequence of points in M , let γ−1 denote the sequence γ(|γ|)γ(|γ| − 1) · · · γ(1), where |γ| is the length of γ and γ(i) is the i-th point of γ. For Ω ⊂ M , a sequence γ of points in Ω is called a path in Ω if γ(i) = γ(i + 1), {γ(i), γ(i + 1)} ∈ RC for 1 ≤ i < |γ| and {γ(j), γ(j + 2)} ∈ RC for 1 ≤ j ≤ |γ| − 2. Clearly, a sequence γ is a path if and only if γ−1 is a path. We say that the path γ starts at γ(1), also denoted s(γ), and terminates at γ(|γ|), also denoted t(γ). The points s(γ) and t(γ) are called ends of γ. For convenience, the null sequence, denoted φ, is also called a path of length 0 in Ω. Let Γ(Ω) denote the set of paths in Ω. The set of paths consisting of points on a given path γ is also denoted by Γ(γ). We note that a path defined in this paper corresponds to the list of edges on a path in the Tanner graph of M , and vice versa. The following lemma can be shown easily.

3. Necessary and Sufficient Conditions for the Existence of Balanced Cycles

A cycle γ is called a balanced cycle if for any P ∈ M

|{i : γ(2i) = P }| = |{j : γ(2j + 1) = P }|.

Clearly, in a balanced cycle the number of occurrences of any point is even. Hence, the length of a balanced cycle is at least twice the number of the distinct points on the cycle. The length of the shortest balanced cycle in M is called the B-girth of M , and denoted by B(M ). If M has no balanced cycle, we say that the B-girth of M is ∞. It is known [3, 4] that the B-girth of any matrix is not smaller than 12. In [2, 3, 4], some sufficient conditions for the existence of balanced cycles are given. For any i with 6 ≤ i ≤ 10, the minimal matrices which have balanced cycles of length 2i but shorter have been determined in [4] by an exhaustive search.

A matrix W with B(W ) < ∞ is said B∗C-minimal if B(E) = ∞ holds for any matrix E which is covered by W and satisfies E = W . Now, the following corollary can be shown easily.

Corollary 3 Let M be a matrix with B(M ) < +∞. If C is one of the shortest balanced cycles of M , then the least sub-matrix of M which contains all the points on C is equivalent to a matrix of form S(a, b, c) with s(a, b, c) = B(M ).

This corollary suggests a simple way to find all the shortest balanced cycles by comparing the sub-matrices with the minimal matrices of form S(a, b, c).
