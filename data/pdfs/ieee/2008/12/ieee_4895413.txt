A class of invertible circulant matrices for QC-LDPC codes
Marco BALDI, Federico BAMBOZZI and Franco CHIARALUCE
Università Politecnica delle Marche Dipartimento di Ingegneria Biomedica, Elettronica e Telecomunicazioni (DIBET)
Via Brecce Bianche, I-60131 Ancona, Italy E-mail: m.baldi@univpm.it, f.bambozzi@gmail.com, f.chiaraluce@univpm.it

Abstract
This paper presents a new class of easily invertible circulant matrices, defined by exploiting the isomorphism from the ring Mn of n×n circulant matrices over GF (p) to the ring Rn = GF (p)[x]/(xn − 1) of the polynomials modulo (xn − 1). Such class contains matrices free of 4-length cycles that, if sparse, can be included in the parity check matrix of QC-LDPC codes. Bounds for the weight of their inverses are also determined, that are useful for designing sparse generator matrices for these error correcting codes.
1. INTRODUCTION
Quasi-Cyclic Low-Density Parity-Check (QCLDPC) codes combine low complexity encoding with the outstanding performance of iterative soft decoding. These codes are highly structured, and this permits the implementation of simple encoders, for example based on a barrel shift register followed by a combinatorial network. Such network is determined by the generator matrix G, and its complexity is low if the density of symbols 1 in G is small. On the other hand, any LDPC code is specified in terms of its parity check matrix, that must be sparse to have efficient decoding; no guarantee is given “a priori” on the sparseness of G.
In general, deriving G from H involves matrix inversion. For this reason, a valuable challenge is to design sparse circulant matrices whose inverses are sparse. A particular choice is represented by Low-Density Generator Matrix (LDGM) codes [1], that have a paritycheck matrix in the form H = [P|I], where P is a sparse matrix and I the identity matrix. In this case, the generator matrix can be written as G = [I|PT ], where T denotes transposition, and it is sparse, thus yielding very low encoding complexity. However, the inclusion of the identity block in H penalizes the minimum distance, and the only way to avoid high error floors consists in the adoption of concatenated schemes [2].
In the QC-LDPC codes here proposed, in place of

identity blocks, we use properly defined ‘ψ-unitary’ circulant matrices, with row/column weight greater than one, but still characterized by sparse inverses.
From the decoding viewpoint, H must be free of 4-length cycles. Not all linear block codes admit a representation free of 4-length cycles [3]. For this reason, LDPC codes are typically designed by directly producing a parity-check matrix with such property. This can be achieved through random searches or, more efficiently, with the help of suitable algorithms. This strategy is adopted, for example, in the Progressive Edge Growth algorithm [4] or in some variants of the design based on Extended Difference Families [5].
Orthogonal circulant matrices, studied in [6] and [7], are a special case of circulant matrices. They can be constructed with an almost arbitrarily small density while, being the inverse coincident with the transpose, they remain sparse after inversion. Unfortunately, their use for LDPC codes is inefficient, as these matrices always exhibit 4-length cycles. In this paper, we generalize the analysis in [6] and [7], through the introduction of the ψ-unitary circulant matrices mentioned above. These matrices can be inverted at low computational cost and, if properly designed, they are free of 4-length cycles. The weight of their inverse is estimated, as a function of the weight of the original matrices.
The organization of the paper is as follows. In Section 2 we remind the definition and properties of circulant matrices. In Section 3 we introduce the concept of ψ-unitary circulant matrix and, in Section 4, we study its inversion. In Section 5 we discuss the problem of the 4-length cycles and, in Section 6, the weight of the inverse matrix. In Section 7 we provide a numerical example for an LDPC code designed through the proposed approach. Finally, Section 8 concludes the paper.

A 4-length cycle in matrix A is a closed rectangular path linking non zero elements. Explicitly, this means that a 4-length cycle exists when two rows have a pair of non zero symbols at the same positions (i.e., belonging to the same columns). Obviously, each matrix can have manifold loops of this kind, depending on the distribution of the non zero symbols. It is possible to verify [8] that 4-length cycles in matrix A do not appear if and only if the distances between any pair of non zero symbols in each row of the matrix are different from each other. So, if δi,k represents the distance (mod n) between the i-th and k-th non zero elements, it must be δi,k = δj,l if and only if i = j and k = l.
Orthogonal matrices are a special case of circulant matrices, for which A · AT = I. So, the inverse of an orthogonal matrix coincides with its transpose.
Let us denote by W [A] the “matrix weight”, that is defined as the number of non zero symbols in each row of matrix A. The following theorem holds:

Theorem 2.1 An orthogonal circulant matrix A over GF (p), with W [A] > 1, has always 4-length cycles.

7. AN EXAMPLE
By introducing a ψ-unitary block in the paritycheck matrix, we are able to design QC-LDPC codes characterized by sparse generator matrices, that is, low encoding complexity.
Each QC-LDPC code in the proposed family has length n, dimension k and a parity-check matrix H formed by a row [H0 . . . Hn0−1] of n0 sparse circulant blocks with size z = n/n0. The circulant blocks in the right part of G (see (17)) have row/column weight 75, that is small compared to their size.

8. CONCLUSIONS
The proposed class of ψ-unitary circulant matrices has great potentialities. Their inversion complexity is determined by the weight and is basically independent of the size, which makes them particularly suitable for processing operations in LDPC codes. They permit to design QC-LDPC codes free of 4-length cycles and characterized by low-density generator matrices.
