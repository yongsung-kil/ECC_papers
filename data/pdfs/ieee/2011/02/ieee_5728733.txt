Abstract—Past work focused on the asymptotic enumeration of trapping sets under random ensembles for specific codes. In this paper, we extend the asymptotic analysis of trapping sets to find the distribution of elementary absorbing sets and elementary fully absorbing sets, which are a special class of trapping sets. The asymptotic results for elementary (fully) absorbing sets are presented, and compared with the results of trapping sets. The distribution of fully absorbing sets allows one to determine the error rates for bit-flipping decoders more accurately.
I. INTRODUCTION
Sparse codes such as low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are a class of codes that can have high performance close to the Shannon limit [1]. The outstanding performance can be achieved by suboptimal message-passing decoding schemes for moderate bit error rates (BER). However, at sufficiently high signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), there exists an error-floor, where the BER vs. SNR plot exhibits a dramatic change of slope.
In [2], near codewords were introduced to explain errorfloors in terms of non-codewords. The concept was then extended to trapping sets in [3]. The relation between the frame error rate and the trapping sets were drawn in [3], [4] and [5], where simulation results show that small trapping sets dominate the error-floor level. In [6], the authors introduced an asymptotic approach to analyze trapping set distributions for regular and irregular LDPC code ensembles. Finite length trapping set enumerators for protograph-based LDPC codes were found in [7]. The authors of [8] introduced a subclass of trapping sets, called absorbing sets, and showed that absorbing sets dominate decoding failures. In particular, the bit-flipping decoder stated in [10] is guaranteed to be stable in fully absorbing sets. Therefore, it is of great interest to further investigate absorbing sets and fully absorbing sets, and to see how they differ from trapping sets.
In this paper, we bring up the analysis of the absorbing set distributions in order to contrast the difference between the absorbing sets and the trapping sets. When the absorbing set distribution is almost identical to the trapping set distribution, most of these trapping sets are in fact absorbing sets, and these trapping sets can represent the error distributions more directly.

On the other hand, when the discrepancy between the trapping set and absorbing set cardinalities is large, many trapping sets are not stable under iterative decoding. The results can be useful for simulations that search for dominant absorbing sets. The analysis method here is extended from [6].
The following are the contents of this paper. In Section II, we provide the necessary background including absorbing sets and the asymptotic matrix enumeration that can be applied to our problem. In Section III, we present an analytical approach for the asymptotic enumeration of absorbing sets and fully absorbing sets. We also discuss asymptotic scaling of elementary absorbing sets and elementary fully absorbing sets. In Section IV, we provide numerical results for our analysis, and we also compare the distributions of absorbing sets, fully absorbing sets and trapping sets. Section V contains the conclusion.
II. BACKGROUND
A. Absorbing Sets and Fully Absorbing Sets
Following the definition of absorbing sets in [8], let GH = (V, F, E) denote the Tanner graph [9] of a code. The sets of vertices V and F are associated with the variable nodes and the check nodes of the graph. The set E is defined as a set of edges e(i, j) where i ∈ V and j ∈ F . For any subset D ⊂ V , let E(D) (resp. O(D)) be the set of neighboring vertices of D in F from the graph with even (resp. odd) degree with respect to D. The following defines absorbing sets and fully absorbing sets.
Definition 2.1 [8]: An absorbing set of size (a, b) is a subset D ⊆ V with size a that connects to a subset O(D) ⊆ F with size b, where each element of D also has strictly fewer neighbors from O(D) than from F \O(D). Moreover, for fully absorbing sets, in the subset of variable nodes V \D, each element has to have strictly fewer neighbors from O(D) than from F \O(D).

IV. NUMERICAL RESULTS
The result in [6] shows that most of the trapping sets are elementary trapping sets for small θ (e.g., θ = 10−3). Recall that the additional constraint for an absorbing set is to have each node in the set connect to more satisfied checks than unsatisfied checks. When θ is small, most of the check nodes connect to the sets at most twice. It follows that most absorbing sets in this regime are in fact elementary absorbing sets. Therefore, we can use Corollary 3.3 and 3.4 to approximate the distribution of (fully) absorbing sets. The comparison among trapping sets, absorbing sets, and fully absorbing sets with respect to the set size (θ, λ) and the ensemble size E(r, l) is further discussed in this section. We focus our studies on regular E(3, 6) ensemble and the E(3, 15) ensemble [3]. V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we presented a method to compute the asymptotic distributions of absorbing sets and fully absorbing sets for regular ensembles based on the method in [6]. A matrix enumeration method, along with techniques to manipulate the rows and columns, are used to count the parity check matrices with certain absorbing set properties. We analyzed different ensemble sizes of trapping sets, elementary absorbing sets, and elementary fully absorbing sets by fixing different parameters related to the absorbing set size. Final results show that the trapping sets can be good approximations for the (fully) absorbing sets when the sets are small or when the number of unsatisfied checks is small. Therefore, most trapping sets in

this regime indeed satisfy the absorbing set constraints, and
can be used as proxies for performance analysis.
