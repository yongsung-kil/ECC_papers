Abstract—An approach for studying quasi-cyclic codes based on matrix transformations via Fourier transforms and row and column permutations is presented. These transformations put a parity-check matrix in the form of an array of circulant matrices into a diagonal array of matrices of the same size over an extension field. The approach is used to characterize certain structural properties of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes such as the girths of their Tanner graphs. Many constructions of quasi-cyclic LDPC codes can be unified under the proposed approach.
I. INTRODUCTION
Once the remarkable performance of random low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, first discovered by Gallager [6], has been demonstrated [20], researchers put considerable effort in developing explicit constructions of LDPC codes that yield performance close to, and in some aspects even better than, random or pseudo-random LDPC codes. It has been recognized early on that quasi-cyclic (QC) codes [17], [26] have good performance as LDPC codes decoded with iterative message-passing algorithms [4],[31]. Since then, several algebraic constructions of QC-LDPC codes have been developed and their simulations over noisy channels show excellent performance [2], [5], [9], [10], [11], [12], [14], [15], [18], [19], [22], [23], [24], [26], [28], [29], [33], [34], [35]. Furthermore, QC codes can be encoded efficiently using shift registers [16]. For all these reasons, QC-LDPC codes are the most promising class of structured LDPC codes known. Therefore, it is important to study the structural properties of QC codes which are pertinent to their use as LDPC codes.
QC codes are characterized by a parity-check matrix H composed of an m0×n0 array of circulant matrices of the same size. In most constructions of QC-LDPC codes, the circulant matrices are either permutation matrices, where each row and each column has a single non-zero component which equals 1, or zero matrices, all of whose components are zeros. In this case, the parity-check matrix H is typically represented compactly in terms of an m0 × n0 matrix, each of its entries specifies a circulant matrix in the array H by identifying the circulant as a zero matrix or as a circulant permutation matrix

and specifying the location of the non-zero component in its first row. There are much fewer constructions of QC-LDPC codes in which the circulants may have two or more nonzero entries. In this case, the parity-check matrix H can be also represented compactly by an m0 × n0 matrix each of its entries specifies the locations of the non-zero components in the first row of a corresponding circulant matrix in the array H [27],[13].
In this paper, we show that the relation between a paritycheck matrix H composed of an array of circulant permutation and zero matrices and its compact form is much richer than what is hitherto known. In particular, we show that each entry in the compact matrix can be viewed as a component in the Fourier transform of the first row of the corresponding circulant matrix. This transform technique extends naturally to the case in which the circulant matrices have two or more non-zero entries, in which case two or more components of the Fourier transform are needed to characterize each circulant. Thus, we present a transform-based approach to describe QCLDPC codes. In this work, we develop the foundations of this approach and use it to explore one of the important properties of parity-check matrices of LDPC codes, namely the girths of their associated Tanner graphs.
In the following, we consider only QC codes over fields of characteristic two having a parity-check matrix consisting of circulants of odd size. The generalization to other characteristics which do not divide the size of the circulants is straightforward.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. Section II presents a one-to-one matrix transformation that puts a paritycheck matrix of a QC code over GF(2) in the form of an array of circulants of the same size into a diagonal array of base matrices of the same size over an extension field in a transform domain and vice versa. This matrix transformation forms the basis of our study of QC codes in the transform domain using spectral techniques. Section III characterizes some structural properties of the parity-check matrices of QC codes. An example of the construction of QC-LDPC codes from base matrices is given in Section IV. The paper is

III. CHARACTERIZING QUASI-CYCLIC LDPC CODES IN THE TRANSFORM DOMAIN
The one-to-one correspondence between arrays H = [Ai,j] of circulant matrices and diagonal arrays Hˆ = diag(B0, B1, . . . , Be−1) of matrices stated in Theorem 1 is the basis of our study of QC codes. In essence, we would like to study and construct QC codes based on the diagonal arrays associated with their parity-check matrices. For this purpose, it is important to know how certain structural properties of parity-check matrices which are arrays of circulants transform into properties on diagonal arrays of matrices. This enables us to characterize properties of the QC codes based on the matrices B0, B1, . . . , Be−1.

A. Binary Parity-Check Matrices
First, we would like to know the necessary and suffi-
cient conditions that need to be imposed on the matrices B0, B1, . . . , Be−1, which are generally nonbinary, such that the associated circulant matrices are all binary. Recall that the inverse Fourier transform (a0, a1, . . . , ae−1) of a vector (b0, b1, . . . , be−1) over GF(q) is binary, i.e., al ∈ GF(2) for all 0 ≤ l < e, if and only if b(2t)e = b2t for 0 ≤ t < e. This condition on the bt’s is known as the conjugacy constraints [1]. To apply these constraints, it is convenient to partition the set {0, 1, . . . , e−1} into cyclotomic cosets modulo e [21]. The cyclotomic coset containing t is Ct = {t, (2t)e, (22t)e, . . . , (2pt−1t)e}, where pt is the least positive integer satisfying 2ptt ≡ t mod e. Each coset has a smallest member which we call the coset representative. The
conjugacy constraints constrain the components of the Fourier
transform of a binary vector whose indices are in the same
cyclotomic coset. All these components should be powers of
the component whose index is the coset representative. The Hadamard product of two matrices M = [mi.j] and
N = [ni,j ] of the same size, denoted by M ◦ N, is defined as their element-wise product, i.e., M ◦ N = [mi,j ni,j]. The Hadamard product of t copies of the matrix M, where t is a nonnegative integer, denoted by M ◦t, is M ◦t = [mit,j ]. We allow t to equal 0 and in this case m0i,j = 1 if mi,j is a non-zero element in GF(q) and mi0,j = 0 if mi,j = 0.
Theorem 2. A necessary and sufficient condition for H to be binary is that B(2t)e = Bt◦2 for 0 ≤ t < e.
Proof: Let H = [Ai,j], where Ai,j = circ(ai,j ), 0 ≤ i < m0, 0 ≤ j < n0. Then, the vector ai,j = (ai,j,0, ai,j,1, . . . , ai,j,e−1) is binary if and only if the vector (bi,j,0, bi,j,1, . . . , bi,j,e−1), which is the Fourier transform of ai,j as specified in Lemma 3, satisfies the conjugacy constraints bi,j,(2t)e = b2i,j,t. Since Bt = [bi,j,t], 0 ≤ i < m0, 0 ≤ j < n0, it follows that all the arrays Ai,j are binary if and only if B(2t)e = B◦t 2 for 0 ≤ t < e.
Theorem 2 states that binary arrays of circulant matrices
correspond to diagonal arrays of matrices satisfying conjugacy constraints. In particular the matrices, B0, B1, . . . , Be−1, on the main diagonal of these diagonal arrays are not arbitrary; they satisfy the condition that B(2t)e = B◦t 2, i.e., the (i, j) entry in B(2t)e is the square of the (i, j) entry in Bt. Thus, all matrices Bt whose indices are in the same cyclotomic coset are determined by the matrix whose index is the coset
representative.

C. Girth of Associated Tanner Graph
For iterative decoding techniques to achieve good performance, it is desirable that the Tanner graph [30] of the associated parity-check matrix used for decoding should have large girth. The Tanner graph associated with a parity-check matrix H has girth at least g, where g is an even positive integer, if and only if, for every even positive integer g < g, there is no sequence of g distinct positions
(i0, j0), (i0, j1), (i1, j1), (i1, j2), . . . ,
(ig /2−1, jg /2−1), (ig /2−1, j0)
in H, viewed as an em0 × en0 matrix, whose entries are 1’s. Based on extensive simulations, it has been recognized that it suffices for the Tanner graph to have girth at least six to ensure good performance. This condition is typically imposed on the parity-check matrix H and it is equivalent to the condition that no two rows (or two columns) have more than one place where they have non-zero components, i.e., no four non-zero components form the corners of a rectangle. We call this condition on H the row-column (RC)-constraint. We are interested in necessary and sufficient conditions on the base matrix, B, such that the associated parity-check matrix consists of circulant permutation and zero matrices and satisfies the RC-constraint or, more generally, their associated Tanner graphs have girth at least equal to g.

V. CONCLUSION
In this work, we develop an approach to study QC-LDPC codes using transform techniques. This approach is suitable to characterize several properties of the parity-check matrices of the codes that are relevant to their performance when decoded using iterative algorithms. As an example of the application of this approach, we consider the girth of the Tanner graphs

of the parity-check matrices.
