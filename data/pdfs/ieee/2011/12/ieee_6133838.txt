Noise Adaptive LDPC Decoding Using Expectation Propagation
Shuang Wang, Lijuan Cui and Samuel Cheng School of Electrical and Computer Engineering
University of Oklahoma, Tulsa, OK, 74135 Email: {shuangwang, lj.cui, samuel.cheng}@ou.edu

Abstract—Belief propagation (BP) is a powerful algorithm to decode low-density parity check (LDPC) codes over additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels. However, the traditional BP algorithm cannot adapt efficiently to the statistical change of SNR in an AWGN channel. This paper proposes an adaptive scheme that incorporates expectation propagation (EP) into the BP based LDPC decoding process. The proposed scheme is able to perform online estimation of both stationary and time-varying SNR at the bit-level, and enhance the BP decoding performance simultaneously. Moreover, the proposed EP estimator shows a very fast convergence speed, and the additional computational overhead of the proposed decoder is less than 10% of the standard BP decoder.
Index Terms—Signal processing, Channel coding, Estimation, Adaptive decoding
I. INTRODUCTION
As a type of error-correcting codes, low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes were first proposed by Gallager in the early 1960s [1] and revived by Mackay and Neal in 1996 [2]. Since then, LDPC codes have attracted wide spread interests in the research community.
For LDPC decoding problems, knowing the signal to noise ratio (SNR) is necessary to achieve the best performance. In many previous studies, the SNR is assumed to be perfectly known prior to decoding. In reality, however, the perfect knowledge of the SNR may not always be available at the decoder, as the channel SNR may vary over time. In the presence of an SNR mismatch, the studies in [3], [4] showed that the decoding performance of BP can be degraded. Thus, many decoding algorithms [4], [5], which can perform SNR estimation, were proposed to avoid the degradation of decoding performance caused by SNR mismatch. While one may argue that the actual SNR may be able to be obtained through a pilot signal or feedback channel under varying channel conditions, a fast varying channel implies potentially a large communication overhead if we want to take full advantage of the channel state information.
Regarding to the SNR estimation of LDPC codes, Summers et al. in [4] proposed a simple estimator of the unknown SNR in Turbo decoding, which is based on the sums of squared receiver values and sums of their absolute values. Ramon et al. presented an EM-based estimation of the carrier phase, amplitude and noise variance in multiuser turbo receivers in [5]. In [4] and [5], it was found that the channel state information extracted by their proposed estimator effectively improved the decoding performance. However, in the above studies they all

assumed that the SNR is fixed within each codeword block. In practice, in many communication systems such as orthogonal frequency division multiplexing (OFDM) and synchronous code-division multiple-access (CDMA) systems, noise varies with time quickly [6], [7]. The papers [6] and [7] consider a randomly varying noise variance in OFDM and CDMA systems, respectively. Similar to the aforementioned works, these two studies also assume that noise variances are the same for all subcarriers. However, it is possible to consider the change of SNR differs at the bit-level for each codeword block. Recently, a particle based online SNR estimator is proposed in [8], which performs online SNR estimation at the bit-level and improves BP decoding performance by updating the estimated SNR iteratively. However, the particle based bit-level SNR estimator in [8] usually needs a large number of iterations to converge.
In Bayesian inference, the estimation of SNR in an AWGN channel corresponds to the estimation of the posterior distribution of channel noise variance. One general method for fast computing approximate posterior distribution in Bayesian networks is assumed-density filtering (ADF) [9]. However, ADF is sensitive to the sequence order of input observations, which may yield an extremely poor approximation. Expectation propagation (EP) [10] algorithm, as an extension of ADF, computes the approximate posterior distribution by incorporating iterative refinement on the solution produced by ADF. Thus, EP does not depend on the data order and is much more accurate than ADF. Moreover, EP also performs better than other rival approximation methods such as Laplace’s method and Monte Carlo.
Originally, EP algorithm in [10] is proposed to approximate the mixture of Gaussian distributions. In this paper, we are interested in the estimation of channel SNR (i.e. noise variance) and Inverse Gamma (IG) distribution is the conjugate prior for likelihood function with variance as parameter. Thus, we choose IG distribution to model the posterior distribution of noise variance to make the approximation tractable. Then we derive EP algorithm to approximate the mixture of IG distributions for channel noise variance estimation. In our scheme, we consider binary LDPC codes over an AWGN channel with a BPSK modulation. Suppose a channel input X takes values ±1 and the channel output is Y = X+Z, where Z is a Gaussian random variable N (0, vt), and the noise variance vt evolves over time at the bit-level. [6] and CDMA [7] may have a time-varying noise variance vt 1. However, the perfect knowledge of time-varying SNR (i.e. noise variance vt) may not always be available at the decoder. Thus, in the case without feedback channels, it is
necessary to perform an online noise variance estimation to
avoid the degradation of decoding performance. It also means

Finally, we investigate the decoding performances of BP decoder and EP based BP decoder with time-varying SNR, where σnoise = 0.2. By changing σ0, different time-varying

SNRs are sampled from the aforementioned model in the study of Fig. 3. The mean values of different time-varying SNRs used in this study change from 0.9 dB to 1.75 dB, and these mean values were then used as initial values in BP decoder and EP based BP decoder. In this case, the EP estimator starts working after 50 BP iterations. Moreover, we perform EP estimator periodically for every 20 BP iterations until BP decoder successfully decodes the codeword or reaches its maximum number of iterations (i.e. 150 in our experiment). Since EP and BP steps have similar complexity, the additional computational overhead of the proposed decoder is less than 10% of the standard BP decoder. Fig. 5 shows that our proposed EP based BP decoder obtained a much better performance than BP decoder with the known mean of time-varying SNR. The gap between BP with and without the knowledge of true SNR is about 0.55 dB, however, the gap between a known true SNR BP decoder and the EP based BP decoder is less than 0.06 dB. This result indicates that knowing only the mean of the time-varying SNR is not enough for a standard BP decoder to achieve its best decoding performance, if the SNR in a channel varies at the bit-level.
V. CONCLUSION
This paper presents a BP algorithm associated with an EP estimator in factor graphs for LDPC decoding over stationary and time-varying AWGN channels. By incorporating the EP algorithm in Region I of the proposed factor graph, the estimate of channel SNR can be updated periodically. Finally, both the stationary and the time-varying channel SNR can be precisely tracked. Thus, our algorithm is not sensitive to the initial estimation of the channel SNR, and it yields a better decoding performance (in terms of lower BER) than the standard BP algorithm. Moreover, the proposed EP estimator shows a very fast convergence speed with only few iterations.
