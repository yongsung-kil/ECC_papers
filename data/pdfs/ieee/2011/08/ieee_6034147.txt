A Transform Approach for Computing the Ranks of Parity-Check Matrices of Quasi-Cyclic LDPC Codes

Qiuju Diao State Key Lab. of Integrated Service Networks
Xidian University Xi’an, 710071, CHINA Email: qiujudiao@gmail.com

Abstract—Several classes of quasi-cyclic LDPC codes have been proposed in the literature and shown to have excellent performance over noisy channels when decoded with iterative message-passing algorithms. However, by and large, important properties of the codes, including their dimensions, are only given for specific codes based on computer programming. Using Fourier transforms, it is shown that the ranks of parity-check matrices of quasi-cyclic codes can be computed. From these ranks, the dimensions of the codes can be determined. The approach, which unifies most of the known algebraic constructions, is given in detail for three large classes of quasi-cyclic LDPC codes which appear in the literature.
I. INTRODUCTION
Once the remarkable performance of random low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, first discovered by Gallager [6], has been demonstrated [18], researchers put considerable effort in developing explicit constructions of LDPC codes that yield performance close to, and in some aspects even better than, random or pseudo-random LDPC codes. It has been recognized early on that quasi-cyclic (QC) codes [17], [22] have good performance as LDPC codes decoded with iterative message-passing algorithms [4],[27]. Since then, several algebraic constructions of QC-LDPC codes have been developed and their simulations over noisy channels show excellent performance [5], [9], [10], [12], [13], [14], [15], [19], [20], [22], [23], [24], [28], [29]. Furthermore, QC codes can be encoded efficiently using shift registers [16]. For all these reasons, QC-LDPC codes are the most promising class of structured LDPC codes known and they deserve to be studied.
In spite of the large volume of literature that presents construction of QC-LDPC codes and document their performance, many important parameters of these codes are not known in general. For example, the dimension of the codes is typically lower bounded based on the dimensions of the codes’ paritycheck matrices. This lower bound is not sufficient since in all applications the dimensions of the codes need to be determined exactly. If the parity-check matrices have full ranks, then the bound is exact. However, since LDPC codes are usually characterized by parity-check matrices having large number of redundant rows, this bound may deviate from the exact value. For a given code, some form of Gaussian elimination has then to be performed using a computer program to compute the rank of the parity-check matrix from which the dimension of the

code can be determined. Definitely, explicit expressions for the ranks of these matrices that generally hold for classes of QC-LDPC codes are welcome. In this paper, we provide such expressions for three large classes of QC-LDPC codes.
QC codes are characterized by a parity-check matrix H composed of an array of circulant matrices. We show that by applying Fourier transforms over finite fields followed by row and column permutations, one can put such a paritycheck matrix in the form of a diagonal array of matrices. These transformations preserve the rank. Hence, the rank of the parity-check matrix equals the rank of the diagonal array of matrices, which is the sum of the ranks of the matrices on its main diagonal. Using Fourier transforms to study QC codes was first proposed by Tanner [25],[26]. Our aproach generalizes the approach of [11] which led to determining the dimensions of some MDS (maximum-distance-separable) based QC-LDPC codes [15], [17], [28] and the approach in [29] which led to determining the dimensions of some QCLDPC codes based on Latin squares. It was used in [3] to construct and analyze QC-LDPC codes. In the following, we consider only QC codes over fields of characteristic two having a parity-check matrix consisting of circulants of odd size. The generalization to other characteristics which do not divide the size of the circulants is straightforward.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. Section II presents a one-to-one matrix transformation that puts a paritycheck matrix of a QC code over GF(2) in the form of an array of circulants into a diagonal array of matrices over an extension field in a transform domain. In Section III, we determine the ranks of parity-check matrices of some classes of QC-LDPC codes that appeared in the literature. The paper is concluded in Section IV.

In most constructions of parity-check matrices of QC-LDPC codes, each circulant matrix is either a zero matrix or a circulant permutation matrix, i.e., a circulant matrix with exactly one non-zero entry in each row and each column and this entry is 1. We are interested in necessary and sufficient conditions on the matrices B0, B1, . . . , Be−1 such that the associated circulant matrices are circulant permutation or zero matrices. In the following, we show that if H is an array of circulant permutation and zero matrices, then it is specified completely by B1. We allow t to equal 0 and in this case bi0,j = 1 if bi,j is a non-zero element in GF(q) and b0i,j = 0 if bi,j = 0.

IV. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we show that arrays of circulant matrices can be put, through Fourier transforms, and row and column permutations, in the form of a diagonal array of matrices. These transformations are rank preserving. Hence, the rank of the array of circulant matrices equals the rank of the associated diagonal array, which is the sum of the ranks of the matrices on its diagonal. Based on this, we determine the ranks of paritycheck matrices of three large classes of QC-LDPC codes that appear in the literature. From these ranks, the dimensions of the codes can be readily determined.
