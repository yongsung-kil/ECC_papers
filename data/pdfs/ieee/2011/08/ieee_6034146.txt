Abstract—In the analysis of maximum-likelihood decoding performance of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, the weight distribution is an important factor. Some methods based on the error impluse method have presented in order to estimate the weight distribution of LDPC codes. However, these methods have no scheme which determine the number of codeword in LDPC codes. In this paper, we propose a reliability-based computation method for the weight distribution of LDPC codes using the probabilistic algorithm. Using the proposed method, we can determine the weight distribution of LDPC codes with small failure probability.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are linear block codes defined by sparse parity-check matrices, and exhibit excellent performance under iterative decoding [1]. The maximum-likelihood (ML) decoding performance of LDPC codes is also analyzed in order to investigate the error performance of the codes designed by certain constructions. The upper and lower bounds on the bit and block error probabilities have be given for some LDPC codes under the ML decoding [2].
The minimum distance and the weight distribution of linear block codes are important factors to evaluate their error performance. The error probability of a linear code under the ML decoding is evaluated by its weight distribution. In the evaluation, the low part of weight distribution is the most important factor. Unfortunately, it has been well known that no algorithm computes exactly the weight distribution of long linear codes in polynomial time. Generally, the problem for any linear code is NP-hard [3]. In the analysis of LDPC codes, the average weight distribution in the ensemble of codes is used instead of the exact weight distribution, and formulas of the average weight distribution have been derived for some LDPC code ensembles [4][5]. But the formulas cannot be applied to computing the weight distribution of an LDPC code.
As for the evaluation of the minimum distance of LDPC codes, Hu et al. have proposed an algorithm, called nearest nonzero codeword search (NNCS) approach, to approximate the minimum distance of LDPC codes [6]. It is based on the error impulse (EI) method [7] which computes the minimum distance of long linear block codes, and adapts the iterative reliability-based decoding [8] to finding low-weight codewords of LDPC codes. Furthermore, Daneshgaran et al. have presented a algorithm modifying the EI method [9]. It

uses error patterns constituted by two error impulses located in two different bit positions at two level search. However, these methods cannot guarantee to find all of codewords included in the low-part weight distribution since the EI method do not have scheme to determine the number of codewords of a given weight. Thus the weight distribution cannot accurately be determined by the above methods.
In this paper, we propose a reliability-based computation method for the weight distribution of LDPC codes using the probabilistic algorithm. It estimates the low part of the weight distribution by selecting the most probable number of codewords of a given weight corresponding to the frequency of codewords found by the modified Stern’s algorithm. Further, the proposed method gives a probability of failing to determining the weight distribution. Using the proposed method, we were able to determine the low part of the weight distribution of (504, 252), (1008, 504) and (4896, 2474) LDPC codes with small failure probability.

VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we have proposed a reliability-based computation method for the weight distribution of LDPC codes using the probabilistic algorithm. It can determine the weight distribution for found low-weight codewords with small failure probability. To authors’ knowledge, there is no conventional method to determine the weight distribution for lowweight codewords found by a certain scheme. Using the proposed method, we were able to determine the low part of the weight distribution of (504, 252), (1008, 504) and (4896, 2474) LDPC codes with small failure probability.
