Block-Triangularization of Parity Check Matrices for Efficient Encoding of Linear Codes
Tomoharu Shibuya Department of Information and Communication Sciences
Sophia University Email: tshibuya@sophia.ac.jp, Tel: +81 3 3238–3425, Fax: +81 3 3238–3933

Abstract—In this paper, we propose a new encoding algorithm for linear codes whose computational complexity is O(w(H)) where w(H) denotes the number of non-zero elements in a parity check matrix H of a code. The proposed algorithm is based on the block-triangularization — an efficient technique to solve a system of linear equations — of a parity part of a parity check matrix, combining additional row and column permutations. As a result, the proposed algorithm can encode any linear codes defined by sparse parity check matrices, such as LDPC codes, with O(n) complexity where n denotes the code length.

Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are celebrated for their excellent performance. By exploiting the sparseness of parity check matrices, LDPC codes have decoding algorithm with O(n) computational complexity. On the other hand, a straightforward encoding method for LDPC codes, for example, solving Eq.(1) by LU factorization, requires O(n2) computation. In order to overcome the imbalance in encoding and decoding complexity, a considerable number of researches have investigated efficient encoding of LDPC codes [1, 5]. Recently, Lu et al. [3] succeeded to develop a linear time encoding method for binary LDPC codes.
In this paper, we propose a new encoding method that can be applied to any linear codes over arbitrary finite field. The proposed method consists of two parts: block-triangularization and rearrangement, both of which are procedures of row and column permutations for parity check matrices. More

precisely, the former employs Dulmage-Mendelsohn (DM) decomposition [2, 4] to transform HP into the block-triangular matrix, denoted by A. In the latter part, A is rearranged so that sub-blocks on its main diagonal become approximate upper triangular form. Then by utilizing the resulting matrix, we can realize an encoding algorithm with O(w(H)) complexity where w(H) denotes the number of non-zero elements in H. This means that the proposed encoding has linear complexity if H is sparse like LDPC codes.
This paper is organized as follows. In Section II, we review block-triangularization for non-singular matrices and show how to apply it to solve a system of linear equations. In Section III, we study rearrangement of the block-triangular matrix, which is the key device to realized a linear time encoding for LDPC codes. The entire encoding algorithm with O(w(H)) complexity is presented in Section IV and the concluding remark is given in Section V.
II. BLOCK-TRIANGULAR MATRICES
A. Decomposition of bipartite graphs with a perfect matching
Let G = (R ∪ C, E) be a bipartite graph with a vertex set R ∪ C and an edge set E, where R and C are vertex sets with R ∩ C = ∅, and E is a subset of R × C, that is, every edge of G has one end in R and the other in C. For a given graph G, we sometimes denote by V (G) and E(G) the vertex and edge set of G, respectively.
A set of edges M ⊂ E is called a matching of G if no two edges of M have a vertex in common. A vertex v ∈ R ∪ C is said to be covered by a matching M if some edge of M is incident to v. A matching of G is perfect if it covers all vertices of G. An edge of G is said to be allowed if it lies in some perfect matching of G. A graph G is called elementary if its allowed edges form a connected subgraph of G.
For a bipartite graph G with a perfect matching, let E′ ⊂ E be the set of all allowed edges of G and consider a subgraph G′ = (R ∪ C, E′) of G. Decompose G′ into connected components L1, L2, . . . , LK (K ≥ 1). Define sets of vertices Rk ⊂ R and Ck ⊂ C by Rk := R ∩ V (Lk) and Ck := C ∩ V (Lk) for k = 1, 2, . . . , K, respectively. Then the following lemma holds.
Lemma 1: [2, Subsection 4.3] For a bipartite graph G with a perfect matching, Lk (k = 1, 2, . . . , K) defined above is an elementary graph.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we have proposed a new encoding method for linear codes over arbitrary finite field that can be executed with O(w(H)) complexity. Moreover, by applying the proposed encoding algorithm, any linear codes defined by sparse parity check matrices, such as LDPC codes, can be encoded with O(n) complexity where n denotes their code length.

Assume that ℓt > 0, i.e., C(1) ̸= ∅. Since mij = 0 for all (i, j) ∈ R(2) × C(1), there is no edge in G(Mt) between R(2) and C(1). Recall that G(M ) is a bipartite elementary

(ii) Assume that ℓt = 0. Then the first wt−1 (> 0) elements of the (n − t + 1)-th row of Mt are non-zero. Moreover, it can be shown that the first wt−1 elements of the first (n − t) rows of Mt are also non-zero. Otherwise, one of those rows would be chosen earlier than mt−1 because some of them involve the smaller number of non-zero elements in their first
ℓt−1 (= wt−1) elements than mt−1 does, which contradicts the selection of mt−1 in Step 2 of Algorithm 2.
We also note that the first wt−1 elements of the last t − 1 rows of Mt are all zero. Hence by combining (i) of this lemma, we conclude that Algorithm 2 yields (n−t)-approximate upper
triangular matrix for t such that ℓt = 0. Since the maximum column weight of M is δ∗, it can be easily obtained from the above argument that n − t + 1 ≤ δ∗ holds.
