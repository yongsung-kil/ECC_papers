Improved Shortening Algorithm for Irregular QC-LDPC Codes Using Known Bits

Yin Xu, Student Member, IEEE, Bo Liu, Member, IEEE, Liang Gong, Student Member, IEEE, Bo Rong, Member, IEEE, Lin Gui, Member, IEEE

Abstract вҖ” Shortening is a technique to achieve rate and length adaptable low-density-parity-check (LDPC) codes. Other than the puncturing and extending techniques, shortening aims to remove certain information columns, i.e., the columns standing for the known information bits, from a given parity check matrix. In this paper, we first review the recently suggested largest-extrinsic-sum (LES) algorithm which is especially designed for quasic-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes, and then propose a method based on smallest-rowvariance priority (SRVP). Moreover, we obtain both theoretical analysis and simulation results of the codes in 802.11n and 802.16e standards, which shows that: 1) the proposed SRVP algorithm always achieves better Eb/No threshold than the existing LES algorithm and generally, the shortened codes outperform the original codes; 2) severe performance degradation occurs when too many information bits are shortened for low rate codes. The proposed algorithm as well as the corresponding performance evaluations is instructive to practical applications1.
Index Terms вҖ” Shortening, known bits, smallest row variance priority algorithm (SRVP), irregular quasi-cyclic (QC) lowdensity-parity-check (LDPC).
I. INTRODUCTION
Conventionally, there exist two methods to generate rate and length adaptable low-density-parity-check (LDCP) codes from a given parity-check matrix. One is called puncturing, which removes some of the parity bits from the encoded LDPC codeword; another is called extending, which increases code length by means of expanding the given parity-check matrix. In practice, puncturing is widely used because it increases the transmission efficiency. Extending, on the other hand, is often applied to quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes as it takes full advantages of the cyclic property [4].

Recently, an alternative method, named shortening has been explored, its underlying idea is to obtain a new error-correction code with flexible length, code rate as well as improved performance by putting infinite reliability to (i.e., removing) some of the known information bits from the encoded codeword. Those known information bits usually appear in transmission frames (control frames or data frames) in the form of system configuration information, control messages, signaling bits and bits which are with fixed value and unused.
Shortly after its introduction, shortening was also applied to turbo codes to achieve variable encoding size [5]., then it was further borrowed by Milenkovic to eliminate the short girths of regular LDPC codes [3], and by T. Okamura to evaluate the performance of LDPCbased hybrid ARQ systems [2].
In [1], based on the previous ideas from [2], [3], and [5], the authors stated that shortening according to the criterion of less short girths [3] will no longer work well on irregular QC-LDPC codes, which are adopted widely in many communication standards [6], [7], [8], and [13]. Instead, a вҖңlargest extrinsic sumвҖқ (LES) algorithm with вҖңlowest column degreeвҖқ (LCD) criterion has been suggested in [1] to apply shortening to irregular QCLDPC codes, and the simulation results seem intriguing.
In this work, we confirm that the performance of irregular QC-LDPC depends mainly on the degree distribution instead of the girths problem. However, we challenge the point of view that the information columns with the lowest degree should be considered as shortening candidates with priority. In this paper, quite different from the LCD criterion [1], all the information columns are equally evaluated, and instead of applying the LES algorithm, we propose an algorithm of smallest-rowvariance-priority (SRVP).
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section II briefly introduces the irregular QC-LDPC codes and the idea of shortening. In Section III, we first revisit the LES algorithm from [1] and based on the analysis of its weakness, we further propose the SRVP method. In Section IV, we present both the theoretical analysis and simulation results of the LDPC codes in 802.16e and 802.11n standards, to show the advantages of shortened codes over original codes, and the better performance of SRVP over LES. Finally, Section V concludes this paper.

III. SHORTENING ALGORITHMS
Previous works [2] [3] [5] on shortening have hardly taken the degree distribution into consideration until вҖңlargest extrinsic sumвҖқ (LES) algorithm is proposed in [1]. In this section, LES algorithm is first revisited and then the smallestrow-variance-priority (SRVP) algorithm is proposed.
Theoretical predictions of both algorithms rely on extrinsic information transfer chart (EXIT Chart) [9] [11] theory instead of Density Evolution [10].
A. LES Algorithm
The main idea of LES algorithm is summarized as follows.
a). Information columns with the smallest column degree should be considered as shortening candidates with priority.
b). Among all the candidate columns, the one which has the largest value of extrinsic sum should be considered as the final shortening candidate.

B. Proposed SRVP algorithm
Although in paper [1], simulation results present LES with good performance, the algorithm itself suffers some weak reasoning.
First, the presumption in LES algorithm that shortening priority should be given to the information columns with the smallest degree is not convincing. It is conceded that columns with large degree promises high correction capacity for the corresponding information bits. However, large column degree also contributes to the increase of average row degree, which in turn could impair the codeвҖҷs overall decoding ability. Similarly, columns with small degree not only reduce the correction capability of the corresponding information bits but also maintain the small value of average row degree which can increase the overall correction performance. Thus, in all, it is a trade-off problem, any hasty conclusion that candidacy priority should be given to the column with the smallest or highest column degree is not reasonable.
Moreover, the вҖңlargest extrinsic sumвҖқ criterion in LES algorithm is mainly used to keep the row degree from changing too sharply. In our proposed algorithm, the вҖңsmallest row variance-priority (SRVP) criterion is used instead of LES criterion.

IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
In this section, without loss of generality, QC-LDCP codes of rate 1/2, 2/3 in 802.16e and 802.11n standards will be used to test the LES and our SRVP algorithms.
Base matrices of 1/2 codes in 802.16e and 802.11n standards are both of size 12 пӮҙ 24 , and the base matrices of 2/3 codes are of size 8пӮҙ 24 . Thus the total numbers of
information columns for the base matrices of 1/2 codes is 12 and for that of 2/3 codes is 16. Here, we try to shorten 4 and 8 information columns. Both theoretical analysis and simulation results will be presented in tables and figures. Here, when referring to theoretical analysis, we mean that EIXT chart [9] is used as a tool to predict the Eb/No threshold.
Generally, from the theoretical results shown in Table I and Table II, we can conclude that either using SRVP or LES, shortened codes outweigh the original codes in terms of Eb/No threshold. Specifically, compared with the threshold of original codes, the shortened codes using LES have 0.2120dB to 0.8470dB gain and the codes using SRVP have greater gain ranging from 0.3650dB to 1.0410dB.
Additionally, the proposed SRVP algorithm achieves a lower threshold compared with LES. Actually, except for the case of вҖңshortening 4 columns 1/2 code in 802.11nвҖқ that the two algorithm have the same Eb/No threshold, the proposed SRVP algorithm ensures greater gain (0.009dB to 0.414dB larger) than the LES algorithm.
For the item of вҖңShortening positionвҖқ in each table, the content вҖңa(b)вҖқ means removing the column вҖңaвҖқ which has the degree вҖңbвҖқ. Then for LES, the shortening priority is given to the information columns with the smallest degree. For example, we have вҖң1(3),2(3),5(3),11(3)вҖқ (here the smallest column degree for information columns is 3 for all the base matrices) for LES in the case of вҖңShortening 4 columns 1/2 code in 802.16eвҖқ. And in the case of вҖңShortening 8 columns 1/2 code in 802.16eвҖқ, the value of вҖңShortening positionвҖқ for LES is вҖң1(3),2(3),4(3),5(3),6(6),7(3),9(3),11(3)вҖқ, among which column 6 has the degree of 6, which may seem deviate from LES criteria. Actually the total number of information columns which are of degree three is seven for the base matrix of 802.16e 1/2 code, thus when removing the eighth column, the smallest degree in the remaining information columns is six.
On the other hand, also seen from the case of вҖңShortening 4 columns 1/2 code in 802.16eвҖқ, though we have вҖң5(3),6(6),7(3),9(3)вҖқ for SRVP algorithm that removes the column 6 which is not the column with the currently smallest degree, the threshold in Eb/N0 is 0.1390dB, still lower than 0.2040dB when using LES algorithm.
In short, as predicted by theoretical analysis using EXIT chart, the criteria in LES that shortening priority should be given to columns with lowest degree value is tested to be unconvincing, and our SRVP algorithm indeed improves the LES.

Fig. 3 illustrates the BER comparison among the original codes, shortened codes using LES algorithm and shortened codes using proposed SRVP algorithm. The simulations are based on the case of shortening-4-columns for 1/2 (33.3% information bits are shortened) and 2/3 (25% information bits are shortened) codes in both 802.11n and 802.16e standards. And simulation results show that the shortened codes are better than the original codes, and the proposed SRVP algorithm slightly improves the LES algorithm in terms of decoding gain (except for the case of equal gain in вҖңshortening 4 columns 1/2 codes, 802.11nвҖқ which is also well predicted by the theoretical results).
However, it can also be seen that in simulation results, the threshold for each code is not as good as that in theoretical

results, which is due to the reason that the lengths of these codes are very short, while on the other hand, the Exit-Chart analysis is based on the assumption of infinite code length. Actually, the phenomenon can also be explained by Finite Length Scaling Law [12] which well-infer that the performance of the code with finite length is dramatically better than that of the code with small length.
Additionally, though the practical gains for the SRVP codes over LES codes are approaching the theoretical predictions, the improvements for the shortened codes over original codes are actually smaller than the theoretical results. This is due to reason that, shortened codes, either using LES or our SPVP, have the same length, while the length of shortened codes is shorter than that of the original codes.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we analyze the LES algorithm for shortening QC-LDPC codes and propose an improved method called SRVP. We shorten 4 and 8 columns from the base matrices of 1/2 and 2/3 codes from 802.11n and 802.16e standards. At theoretical level, as opposed to the original codes, the shortened codes using LES have 0.2120dB to 0.8470dB gain and the ones using SRVP have better gain ranging from 0.3650dB to 1.0410dB. Therefore, our SRVP algorithm can achieve better performance, i.e., 0.009dB to 0.414dB gain, than the LES algorithm.
At simulation level, similar conclusions can be drawn on the performance improvement for SRVP algorithm over LES algorithm. However, due to the reason of finite code length (shorter than 2000) and the length differences between shortened codes and original codes, the actual threshold for each code and the gain for shortened codes over original codes are smaller than the theoretical predictions. Moreover, we also find out that, shortening 67% of the whole information bits from the 1/2 codes causes severe performance degradations in high SNR region. This implies that we need to be very careful when shortening a large proportion of information bits out of a code with low code rate, short length and degree-2 parity columns.
The proposed algorithm as well as the analysis and simulation results is instructive to industrial applications.
