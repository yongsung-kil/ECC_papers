Near Optimum Majority-Logic Based Decoding of Low-Density Parity-Check Codes
T. M. N. Ngatched, Attahiru S. Alfa, and Jun Cai Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering
University of Manitoba Winnipeg, MB R3T 5V6
Canada Email: {ngatched, alfa, jcai}@ee.umanitoba.ca

Abstract—A reliability-based iterative majority-logic decoding algorithm for regular low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes was recently proposed by Huang et al. In this paper we present an improved version of that algorithm by introducing a different reliability measure for each check-sum of the parity-check matrix, and taking it into account in the computation of the extrinsic information that is used to update the reliability measure of each received bit in each iteration. Some simulations results are given, which show that the new algorithm, while requiring very little additional computational complexity, not only achieves a considerable error performance gain over the standard one, but also, more importantly, outperforms the iterative decoding based on belief propagation (IDBP), for short and medium block length finite-geometry (FG) LDPC codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
First introduced by Gallager [1] in 1962, low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes were recently brought back into prominence by MacKay and Neal [2] and shown to form a class of Shannon limit approaching codes for a wide range of channels when decoded with iterative decoding algorithms also known as message-passing algorithms [3]. Since their rediscovery, design, construction, encoding, decoding, generalizations, performance analysis and applications of LDPC codes have been attracting a great deal of research interest. Many classes of LDPC codes have been constructed, and a wide spectrum of decoding algorithms, ranging from low to high complexity and from reasonably good to very good performance, have been designed.
The algorithms for decoding LDPC codes can be classified into three general categories: hard-decision decoding( [1, p. 39], [4, pp. 273-304], [5]), soft-decision decoding( [1, pp. 40-48], [3], [6]–[9]), and hybrid decoding (also known as reliability-based decoding) ( [10]–[17]). From an implementation point of view, hard-decision decoding is the simplest in complexity; however, its simplicity results in a relatively poor performance that can be as far away as a few decibels from that of soft-decision decoding. Soft-decision decoding provides the best performance but requires the highest computational complexity. Hybrid decoding is in between the two

extremes and provides a good trade-off between performance and complexity.
Among hybrid decoding algorithms, the soft reliabilitybased iterative majority-logic decoding (SRBI-MLGD) algorithm recently introduced in [17] offers one of the best tradeoff between performance and complexity. The algorithm is designed based on the orthogonal check-sums concept used in the one-step majority-logic-decoding (OSMLGD) algorithm ( [4, pp. 273-304], [10]). The decoding function of the SRBI-MLGD algorithm includes some kind of soft reliability measures of received symbols which are improved through decoding iterations. Simulation results show that SRBI-MLGD performs just as well as many variants of weighted bit-flipping (WBF) algorithms and the differential binary message-passing decoding (DBMPD) algorithm [16], with much less decoding complexity. Furthermore, it has a faster rate of decoding convergence. An important feature of SRBI-MLGD is that it is a binary message-passing algorithm and requires only logical operations and integer additions. This feature significantly simplifies the decoder implementation, as it can be achieved using simple combinational logic circuits. Another feature of SRBI-MLGD is that it allows parallel decoding of all the received symbols in each decoding iteration, which is important to achieve a very high decoding speed.
This paper presents a modification of the SRBI-MLGD algorithm. The modification is achieved by introducing a reliability measure for each check-sum of the parity-check matrix, and taking it into account in the computation of the extrinsic information that is used to update the reliability measure of each received bit in each iteration. Then the total extrinsic information contributed to each received bit is appropriately normalized to update its reliability measure. The normalization factor is chosen in such a way that the main features of the SRBI-MLGD algorithm are preserved. This modification, while requiring very little additional computational complexity, results in a considerable performance improvement over the SRBI-MLGD algorithm. Moreover, results better than those of iterative decoding based on belief propagation (IDBP) have been obtained for short and medium block length finitegeometry (FG) LDPC codes. To the best of the authors’ knowledge, such results have never been reported.

II. SRBI-MLGD ALGORITHM [17]

A. Notation and Basic Definitions

A binary LDPC code is completely described by its sparse

binary parity-check matrix H. For an (N, K) LDPC code,

H has N columns and M ≥ N − K rows. For a regular

LDPC code, H has the following structural properties: (i) every

row has weight ρ; (ii) every column has weight γ; (iii) no

two rows (or two columns) have more than one 1-component

in common; and (iv) both ρ and γ are small compared to

the length N of the code. The set of bits that participate in

check m is denoted as N (m) {n : Hmn = 1}. Similarly, the set of checks in which bit n participates is denoted as

M(n) {m : Hmn = 1}. Suppose a binary (N, K) LDPC code is used for error

B. Algorithm
Suppose the samples of the received sequence y are symmetrically clipped at a threshold yth, and then uniformly quantized in the range [−yth, yth]. There are 2b−1 quantization intervals, symmetric with respect to the origin. Each quantization interval has a length Δ = 2yth/ 2b − 1 and is represented by b bits. For 0 ≤ n ≤ N −1, let qn = Q (yn) denote the quantized value of the sample yn which is an integer representation of one of the 2b − 1 quantization intervals. The range of qn is

therefore − 2b−1 − 1 , + 2b−1 − 1 . If the magnitude |yn| is larger than the quantization range 2b − 1 Δ, then qn is set at − 2b−1 − 1 or 2b−1 − 1 , depending on the sign of yn. The magnitude |qn| of the quantized sample qn gives a soft measure of the reliability of the hard-decision received bit zn. III. MODIFIED SRBI-MLGD (MSRBI-MLGD) ALGORITHM
A. Algorithm
In the SRBI-MLGD algorithm, the check-sums in M(n) orthogonal on zn are treated equally. This implicitly assumes that these check-sums have the same reliability. However, this could be true only if the reliabilities of the received symbols were equal. In fact, since the reliabilities of the received symbols are different, the reliabilities of different check-sums are also different. Thus, introducing a reliability measure for each check-sum in M(n), and taking it into account in the computation of the total extrinsic information, En(k), can improve the accuracy of the latter and enhance the performance of the SRBI-MLGD algorithm. In consideration of message passing, the reliability measure of a check-sum passed to a symbol should exclude the symbol itself. Therefore the reliability of a check-sum should be defined with respect to a symbol. B. Computational Complexity
From the above description, one can find that compared with the standard algorithm, the new one requires more 3M ρ − 3M integer comparisons to compute the weighting coefficients wm,n and one integer division to compute the normalization factor α in the initialization part, (γ − 1) N integer additions to compute the extrinsic information, and N integer divisions. Ignoring the cost of the computation of the weighting coefficients wm,n and the normalization factor α, since it is done only once at the initialization, one iteration of the proposed algorithm requires a total of 2δ + N − M logical operations, γN integer additions, and N integer divisions.

IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
In this section, using Monte Carlo simulations, the error performance in terms of bit-error rate (BER) and frameerror rate (FER) as a function of the rate-normalized SNR per information bit (Eb/N0), of the proposed algorithm is compared with that of the standard SRBI-MLGD on a number of high-rate FG-LDPC [10] codes of various length. The following codes are simulated: the (255, 175) code, the (1023, 781) code, and the (4095, 3367) code. The codes are labeled as C1, C2, and C3, respectively. The rates of these codes are 0.686, 0.763, and 0.822, respectively. The performance of the codes using IDBP and the improved modified weighted bit-flipping (IMWBF) of [13] are also included. The IMWBF is one of the best performing variant of the WBF algorithm for FG-LDPC codes in terms of error performance. For the proposed algorithm and the standard SRBI-MLGD, we use 8bit uniform quantization with 255 levels; the quantization step Δ, which is obtained through simulations, is 0.0787, 0.0308, and 0.015 for C1, C2, and C3, respectively. The maximum number of iterations is set to 50 for both the proposed and the standard algorithm, and 200 for IDBP and IMWBF; for C1 it is however set to 100 for the IDBP and IMWBF. At each SNR value, at least 200 codewords errors are detected for C1, and 100 codewords errors for C2 and C3.
Figs. 1 - 3 show the BER and FER performances of C1, C2, and C3, respectively. It can be observed that, at the BER of 10−5, the new algorithm outperforms the standard one by about 1.4 dB, 1.1 dB, and 0.65 dB for C1, C2, and C3, respectively. The same observation can be made as far as the FER is concerned. Compared to the IMWBF, the improvement in performance is about 0.9 dB, 0.85 dB, and 0.5 dB for C1, C2, and C3, respectively. It should be noted that the proposed algorithm requires less computational complexity than the IMWBF algorithm. We recall that one iteration of the IMWBF algorithm requires δ+M binary operations, δ real additions, 2δ real comparisons, and N real multiplications [17]. Moreover, the average number of iterations is much larger for IMWBF as illustrated in the following. Of paramount importance is the observation that the proposed algorithm outperforms IDBP by about 0.3 dB for C1 and C2. For C3, the proposed algorithm and IDBP perform practically the same at high SNR. It is well known that IDBP performs better for long block length codes, which may explain why the proposed algorithm does not outperform IDBP for C3.
With these performance studies in mind, we move on to the average number of iterations comparison. Figs. 4 - 6 depict the average number of iterations for C1, C2, and C3, respectively. We see that, at low to medium SNR, the proposed algorithm converges much faster than the standard one. However, at high SNR, the situation is reversed with the new algorithm converging slightly slower than the standard one. It can also

be observed that, while the proposed algorithm converges
faster than IDBP at low SNR, at medium to high SNR, IDBP
converges faster than all algorithms.
V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, a modification of the SRBI-MLGD algorithm
for regular LDPC codes has been proposed. Simulations results
performed on a number of high-rate FG-LDPC codes of
various length show that the new algorithm, while requiring
very little additional computational complexity, results in a
considerable performance improvement over the SRBI-MLGD
algorithm. More importantly, the new algorithm outperforms
IDBP for short and medium block length FG-LDPC codes. In our ongoing work, we are investigating the performance
of the proposed algorithm on other classes of LDPC codes
and developing a theoretical understanding of its behavior.
