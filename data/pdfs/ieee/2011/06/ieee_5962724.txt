Comparison of the Detrimental Effects of Trapping Sets in LDPC Codes

Kuntal Deka, A. Rajesh and P. K. Bora Department of Electronics and Communication Engineering, Indian Institute of Technology Guwahati, Guwahati – 781039, India.
E-mail: {kuntal, rajesha, prabin}@iitg.ernet.in

Abstract—This paper presents a method to compare the detrimental effect of different trapping sets in the LDPC codes for an AWGN channel. The messages from the check nodes in the trapping set induced subgraph are divided into two groups: one from the mis-satisfied check nodes and the other from the unsatisfied check nodes. The message densities for these two groups are computed to find out the joint probability of all the variable nodes in the trapping set being in error in the successive iterations. These joint probabilities are used to compare the detrimental effects of different trapping sets.
Index Terms—Low density parity check (LDPC) codes, Gaussian approximation, error floor, iterative decoding, trapping sets.
I. INTRODUCTION
The low density parity check(LDPC) code was first presented by Gallager in 1962 [1] but came into the focus of researchers only after Mackay and Neal rediscovered it in 1996 [2, 3]. These codes approach Shannon capacity when decoded by iterative decoders. The iterative decoding is done by the sum product algorithm(SPA) [2]. For infinite-length codes, the SPA approaches the maximum likelihood decoding which is optimal. But for finite-length codes with the Tanner graph containing cycles, the SPA is only sub-optimal. This fact eventually leads to the problem of error floor which stands as a limiting factor for the successful implementation of several LDPC codes in practical systems.
The error floor performance of the LDPC code is dominated by some bad substructures lying in the Tanner graph of the code. For the binary erasure channels(BEC), the error floor performance can be completely evaluated in terms of the stopping sets [4]. In fact, Changyan Di et al [4] have derived the expressions for the bit error rate(BER) and the frame error rate(FER) for LDPC Codes over the BEC considering the contributions from the stopping sets only. But for other channels like the binary symmetric channels(BSC) and additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels, the error floor phenomenon is not completely understood. In [5] Richardson introduced the trapping sets (TS) in order to predict the error floor performance. Depending upon the way of analysis, several other authors have proposed slightly different configurations like the pseudo-codeword [6], the near codeword [7] and the absorbing set [8] as roots of the error floor problem.
In an LDPC code, there may be a large number of TSs of different classes. But no analytical tool is available in the literature to compare the detrimental effects of different TSs.

In this paper, an analytical method is proposed to compare different types of TSs. This comparison is done probabilistically considering the message densities to be Gaussian distributed.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. In section II, a brief review of SPA is presented. Section III presents the definition of the TS and explains how it leads to decoder failure. Section IV gives a detailed description of the proposed analytical method for the comparison of TSs. Section V shows numerical results for some well-known codes and section VI concludes the paper.

III. TRAPPING SETS IN LDPC CODES
Definition : An (a, b) TS is a set of a VNs which induces a subgraph of the Tanner graph with b odd-degree CNs and an arbitrary number of even-degree CNs [9].
The sub-graph induced by the TS is a peculiar kind of substructure. If a and b are small and all the VNs of the subgraph are received incorrectly, then it is almost impossible for the iterative decoder to correct these decisions. A. The Proposed Method
Let T be an (a, b) TS and ETl denote the event that all the VNs of T are in error after lth iteration. Now the joint probability P ETL, ETL−1, . . . , ET0 for some fixed maximum iteration ‘L’, can be used as a measure of harmfulness of the TS ‘T ’. Calculating the joint probabilities for different TSs, their detrimental effects can be compared. The term P ETL, ETL−1, . . . , ET0 can be decomposed as in the following using the chain rule:

Fig. 1. A (5, 3) TS
It is obvious from Figure 1, that corresponding to a TS T , there are two groups of CNs, one is of even-degrees and the other is of odd-degrees. If all the VNs connected to the CNs in the TS-induced subgraph except those in the TS are correctly decoded, then the even-degree CNs in the subgraph will be mis-satisfied and the odd-degree CNs will be unsatisfied. In the SPA, the mis-satisfied CNs will try to mislead the decoder and the unsatisfied CNs will try to bring the decoder to the correct state [11]. If the number of unsatisfied CNs is very small compared to that of mis-satisfied CNs, then the decoder will never be able to recover from the incorrect state, i.e. it gets trapped.
IV. COMPARISON OF DIFFERENT TYPES OF TRAPPING SETS
It is important to note that all the TSs of an LDPC code are not equally detrimental. Following are the two cases where we can directly compare the harmfulness of two TSs without going for an analytical method [12]:
• (a1, b) and (a2, b) TSs with a1 < a2 If a is small, then the joint probability that a number of VNs are incorrectly received is higher compared to when a is large. Thus the (a1, b) TS will be more detrimental than the (a2, b) TS when a1 < a2.
• (a, b1) and (a, b2) TSs with b1 < b2 If b is small, then the number of unsatisfied CNs which have the capacity to correct the decoder decisions gets reduced. Thus the (a, b1) TS will be more detrimental than the (a, b2) TS when b1 < b2.

B. Mackay (120,64) Code
The parity check matrix for the Mackay (120,64) code (120.64.3.109) [10] contains some small TSs like (3,3), (4,2), (5,1), (5,3) and (8,0). The small TSs of this code are found out using the method proposed in [11]. It is observed from Figure 4 that in the high SNR region, the (8,0) TS is the most detrimental as it involves no unsatisfied CNs whereas the (5,3) TS is the least detrimental as it involves largest number of unsatisfied CNs. It can also be observed that the (5,1) TS is more detrimental than the (5,3) TS as the former involves fewer number of unsatisfied CNs than the latter corroborating the analytical comparison done in the last part of the Section IV. Also, the (3,3) TS is more detrimental than the (5,3) TS as the size of the former is less than that of the latter.
