Design of Turbo Codes without 4-cycles in Tanner Graph Representation for Message Passing Algorithm
Ying Cui#1, Xiao Peng#2, Satoshi Goto#3
Graduate School of Information, Production and Systems, Waseda University Kitakyushu, Japan
1shellycui@asagi.waseda.jp 2pengxiao@suou.waseda.jp 3goto@waseda.jp

Abstract— Turbo code and Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) code are both recommended as FEC code in many communication standards owing to their impressive error correcting performance. Aiming at the common architecture which can decode both of the two codes, this paper describes the method of decoding turbo codes with message passing algorithm which is conventionally used for LDPC codes. In this method, turbo codes are viewed as block codes and the sparse parity check matrices are constructed through Smith Decomposition or GBT (Generator matrix based transformation). In order to guarantee decoding performance, we propose the criterion of turbo codes with no 4-cycles, which is mathematically proved and demonstrated by the simulation results.
Keywords— turbo code, LDPC, parity check matrix, 4-cycle, message passing.
I. INTRODUCTION
Turbo codes and Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) codes both become part of most modern wireless communication standards, such as WiMAX (IEEE 802.16e) and WiFi (802.11n) because of the excellent Shannon limit approaching performance with reasonable decoding complexity.
Although Turbo codes and LDPC codes appears quite different: Turbo codes are generally encodes with two or more parallel concatenated convolutional codes, while LDPC codes are linear block code specified by the sparse parity check matrix (PCM), they definitely share common properties.
It is claimed in [1] that Turbo codes actually can be regarded as LDPC codes and also have a construction of sparse random trellises. Moreover, the decoding algorithms for both of the two codes mainly contain iterative process dealing with probability propagation. Conventionally, turbo code is decoded using BCJR algorithm [2] based on trellis structure, while LDPC code employs message passing (MP) algorithm [3] working on Tanner graph [4]. It will be significant to construct a common decoder for both codes by exploiting their common properties, especially in systems where they are both candidates as FEC code.
For the architecture of common decoder, it is hard to determine the state and trellis of LDPC code. However, it is promising to make Tanner graph and PCM for convolutional codes and turbo codes. Tanner graph is a graphical depiction

of PCM and also aids in the description of the MP decoding algorithm. It contains two sets of nodes—one is variable node corresponding to each column of the matrix, representing the codeword bits; the other is check node corresponding to each row of the matrix, representing the parity check constraints. As stated in [1], convolutional codes and turbo codes have sparse random trellis structure similar with LDPC codes. Thus the PCM of them could be constructed through Smith Decomposition [5] or GBT (Generator matrix based transformation). In this process, the presence of short cycles should be considered since it is generally thought that the presence of short cycles especially 4-cycles in the Tanner graph is one of the main parameters affecting the coding gain achievable by the LDPC code.
Therefore, in addition to the general method of constructing PCM, we proposed the criterion of turbo codes with no 4cycles. Moreover, the approach of decoding turbo code using MP algorithm is demonstrated and compared to the conventional BCJR algorithm in simulation results.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. The PCM constructing method for convolutional codes and turbo codes are explained in Section II. The criterion of convolutional codes and turbo codes with no 4-cycles is proposed in Section III. The experimental results are given in Section IV. Finally, the conclusion is drawn in Section V.
II. PCM FOR CONVOLUTIONAL CODES AND TURBO CODES
A. PCM of Convolutional Codes

However, it is relatively complicated; moreover, due to Smith decomposition, it’s hard to analyze the relationship between the PCM and the code structure. In this work, we proposed a more direct and simpler method, named as GBT (Generator matrix based transformation).

III. CRITERION OF CONVOLUTIONAL CODES AND TURBO CODES WITHOUT 4-CYCLES IN TANNER GRAPH REPRESENTATION

A. Criterion of Convolutional Codes without 4-cycles
Considering the general form of PCM in (4), Rule 2 is proposed with a simple proof.
Rule 2: Describe an (n, k, m) systematic convolutional code with generator matrix G(D) in the form (1), where
m
∑ gij (D) = αt Dt , i = 1, 2,", k , j = 0,1,", n − 1 and αt ∈{0,1} . t =0
We denote N1 as the total number of αt that equals 1. If the condition N1 ≤ 3 holds, then the PCM has no 4-cycles.
Proof: Fig. 2 shows a graphic representation of PCM H of a convolutional code. According to GBT method, there are
exactly N1 1’s in the submatrix H1 = [h0 h1 " hm 0 "]T .
Suppose there is a 4-cycle in H, we denote the position of the four 1’s as (i1, j1), (i1, j2), (i2, j1) and (i2, j2), where i1 < i2 and j1 < j2 . Without loss of generality, we suppose the four 1’s are in submatrices H1 and H3. According to the structure of H, the two 1’s in H3 — (i1, j2) and (i2, j2) are generated by shifting down H1 by 2 × (n − k) positions. So
the elements in H1 of the positions (i1 − 2× (n − k), j2 − 2× n) and (i2 − 2 × (n − k), j2 − 2× n) are also 1. Therefore, there are totally four 1’s in H1, which contradicts the condition N1 ≤ 3 .
Furthermore, Rule 3 is proposed. Rule 3: Describe an (n, k, m) convolutional code with PCM H in the form (4), which is characterized by a (n − k) × n
matrix H1 = [h0 h1 " hm 0 "]T . Denote the difference
of the rows of two 1’s in the same column as di, there are no 4-cycles in the Tanner graph of this code if:
(a) There are no 4-cycles in H1. (b) All the di are different. Proof: Suppose there exists a 4-cycle, we denote the positions of the four 1’s as (i1, j1), (i1, j2), (i2, j1) and (i2, j2), where i1 < i2 and j1 < j2 . Without loss of generality, we suppose the two 1’s of position (i1, j2) and (i2, j2) are in H1 with row difference d1 = i2 − i1 . Obviously, the two 1’s of position (i1, j2) and (i2, j2) are not in H1 since condition (a) is satisfied, so this two 1’s are generated by down shifting of H1. Therefore, there exists another pair of 1’s with row difference d2 = i2 − i1 in H1, which contradicts the condition (b).
B. Criterion of Turbo Codes without 4-cycles
One necessary condition for the absence of 4-cycles is that there must be no 4-cycle in Tanner graph of the component code, which has been discussed in part A.

However, even if the Tanner graph of the component code has girth six, the overall Tanner graph of the turbo code may contain 4-cycles due to the interleaver in the encoder. To ensure the absence of 4-cycles in the Tanner graph of turbo code, Rule 4 is proposed with a simple proof.
Rule 4: For a turbo code with (n, k, m) convolutional code as component, denote the PCM of the convolutional code as Hc, which is separated into two parts – Hi and Hr. There are no 4-cycles in the Tanner graph of this code if:
(a) There are no 4-cycles in Hc. (b) Hi contains only one 1 in each row and each column. Proof: Fig. 3 shows a graphic representation of Hturbo in (5). Since (a) is satisfied, the only possible position that 4-cycle appears is in [Hi HiΠT ]T. According to (b), Hi contains only one 1 in each row and each column, which ensures no 4-cycle. By careful design of interleaver, 4-cycles can be avoided as well. Rule 5 is proposed for turbo code composed of 1/2 systematic convolutional code. Rule 5: For a turbo code with 1/2 convolutional code as component, Hi in (5) is generated by down shifting its first column h1. Denote the difference of the rows of two 1’s in h1 as di, there are no 4-cycles in the Tanner graph of this code if: (a) There are no 4-cycles in the PCM of convolutional
code. (b) The interleaver represented by ΠT (i.e., the
deinterleaver of this code) in (5) is an S-random interleaver with S=max{di}, for all i.

Here, an S-random interleaver [8] guarantees that if i − j ≤ S , then after interleaving, π (i) − π ( j) > S .
Proof: If there exists a 4-cycle, suppose the four 1’s have positions (i1, j1), (i1, j2), (i2, j1) and (i2, j2) in PCM, where i1 < i2 and j1 < j2 . From (a), we know (i1, j2) and (i2, j2) are in Hi while (i2, j1) and (i2, j2) are in HiΠT. Since Hi is generated by shifting h1 down (1 position a time), any pair of 1’s appear in the same row have column difference no larger than S, so we have j2 − j1 ≤ S . However, according to the
property of S-random interleaver, we also have j2 − j1 > S , which causes contradiction.
IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
In this section, we select different kinds of turbo codes considering the criterion proposed in Section III.
In simulation, we set code rate as 1/2, code length 3072, channel model AWGN and maximum iteration as 10 for both BCJR algorithm and MP algorithm. Fig. 4 shows that MP algorithm can decode turbo code with PCM constructed using GBT method. Moreover, the performance is greatly affected by the existence of 4-cycles.

On one hand, the turbo code TC1 with constituent convolutional code G1(D) = ⎡⎣1 (1+ D2 ) / (1+ D + D2 )⎤⎦ violates the condition (b) in Rule 3, and thus it contains 4-cycles in its Tanner graph. As shown in Fig. 4(a), the BER performance of MP algorithm is greatly degraded by the presence of 4-cycles.
On the other hand, TC2 with constituent code
G2 (D) = [1 1/ (1+ D)] satisfies the conditions in Rule 4, which
ensures no 4-cycles exist in its Tanner graph. TC3 is composed of constituent code G3 (D) = ⎣⎡1 (1+ D2 ) / (1+ D3)⎦⎤ which satisfies Rule 3, and a random interleaver which results in no 4-cycle in the whole Tanner graph for TC3. As shown in Fig. 4(b), MP algorithm can achieve good decoding performance close to that of conventional BCJR algorithm, owing to the absence of 4-cycles in Tanner graph.
V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, for the common decoder of turbo codes and LDPC codes, we proposed GBT method to construct PCM for convolutional codes and turbo codes. In addition, to guarantee decoding performance, the criterion of convolutional codes and turbo codes with no 4-cycles is proposed with mathematical proof. The method of decoding turbo codes using MP algorithm is demonstrated. Simulation results show that the decoding performance of MP algorithm is comparable to that of conventional BCJR algorithm if the criterion of turbo codes with no 4-cycle is satisfied. Considering the fact that MP algorithm has much lower per-iteration complexity than that of BCJR algorithm, MP algorithm is very effective in decoding turbo code without 4-cycles.
