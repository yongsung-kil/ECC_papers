Study of stopping criteria in LDPC coded iterative MIMO OFDM receiver

Abstract—In this paper, we address the problem of scheduling a LDPC coded iterative MIMO OFDM receiver. Iterative reception is an efficient way to approach optimal performance avoiding exhaustive algorithms. In such receivers using LDPC codes, complexity and delay could be critical since decoding and equalization tasks are repeated at each equalization loop. A LDPC decoder stops once it reaches a maximum number of iterations or fulfills a preset stopping criterion. In this paper we propose and evaluate the use of different stopping criteria based on reliability metrics to reduce the complexity and delay of the receiver by minimizing the total number of iterations while preserving the same performance. We show that, at low signal to noise ratio, the use of reliability based stopping criteria reduces complexity and delay by an early reuse of MIMO equalization. Also, stopping criteria can be chosen dynamically to match with channel conditions and the corresponding outer loop step.
I. INTRODUCTION
Multiple Input Multiple Output (MIMO) systems are the strongest candidates to achieve increased data rates in emerging and future wireless communication systems. MIMO enables us to view the transmission channel as a set of parallel channels, thus increasing the data rate by a factor depending on the number of transmitting and receiving antennas and the correlation between these parallel channels. Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiplexing (OFDM)[1] is today used in many standards due to its robustness against multipath fading. On each sub-carrier Cyclic Prefix (CP-OFDM)[2] transforms the MIMO frequency selective channel into a flat fading channel. The association of MIMO and OFDM consists on applying OFDM modulation to the transmitted signals on each antenna followed by OFDM demodulation on each receiving antenna.
Optimal MIMO equalization is achieved using Maximum Likelihood (ML) algorithm [3], which suffers from a high complexity. Some variants of this algorithm exist, with reduced complexity using sequential detection (list sphere decoding), [4],[5]. On the other hand, linear algorithms like Minimum Mean Squared Error (MMSE), offers a good performance/complexity tradeoff [6] making them attractive for practical use. Optimal reception is achieved by jointly detecting and decoding transmitted symbols at the expense of a huge complexity. However, joint reception could be achieved using turbo-equalization. Channel decoder’s outputs are used

to provide symbol estimates that are re-injected as a-priori information in the MIMO detector.
In a turbo-equalizer, complexity remains of major importance since detection and decoding tasks are repeated as many times as the number of equalization stages. These constraints make the choice of channel codes sensitive to complexity. Low Density Parity Check codes (LDPC) are today among the most attractive channel error correcting codes, and have been adopted in many standards like DVB, WiMax and WiFi. LDPC codes of very high length achieve the best performance. In fact, such code’s graph can be assumed to have a tree structure on which exchanged messages are independent and Sum-Product decoding is optimal. Density Evolution [7] and EXIT charts fitting [8] are the most common techniques for LDPC performance optimization, but the resulting code graph could lead to heavy encoding and decoding complexity. In practice, LDPC code structure is chosen to agree with some throughput and hardware implementation constraints, this makes some families like Repeat and Accumulate (RA) codes advantageous [9]. RALDPC offers a simple encoding thanks to the bi-diagonal characteristic of the parity check matrix. In addition this special structure provides a sufficient amount of variable nodes (of degree 2) necessary to use parallel decoding techniques [10].
In [11], it has been shown that turbo-equalization of LDPC coded MIMO could achieve gain with a complexity equivalent to a non iterative system. A method for static scheduling is given in order to minimize the total number of iterations. Because of its calculation overhead and its sensitivity to code parameters, static scheduling could be replaced by a dynamic method based on real-time reliability statistics.
This paper is organized as follows: section II gives the system model to be used, in section III static and dynamic scheduling are presented and several criteria are proposed for decoding stopping. Section IV gives simulation results and section V concludes the paper.

III. RECEIVER SCHEDULING
MIMO equalizer’s output is used by LDPC decoder to calculate symbol estimates sˆ, so, LDPC decoder performs Ni iterations and calculate the syndrome at the end of each iteration to check whether to stop the decoding process or not. Note that i refers to the ith outer loop. The estimated complex symbol sˆ is then re-injected as a-priori information to the MMSE-IC equalizer. Due to hardware constraints imposed by the electronics available architectures, it’s very important to reduce as much as possible, the individual complexity of each processing stage. The receiver scheduling plays an important role in this optimization.

Figure 1. LDPC coded iterative MIMO-OFDM receiver
By receiver scheduling we mean: "finding a strategy for inner and outer loops, determining when LDPC decoder has to stop before starting the next outer loop . The resulting schedule has to preserve performance and minimize the total number of iterations"
A. Static Scheduling
In [11], a scheduling method has been given taking into account the EXIT chart of the used LDPC code. First, the number of outer loops has been optimized. In fact, after the 4th outer loop, no significant gain can be obtained [11]. Second, it is possible to limit the complexity by imposing strict optimized bounds on the number of LDPC iterations at each outer loop. In this case, no significant loss in performance is observed. Note that the bounds were found using off-line simulations and are calculated as an average over a very large number of blocks and channel (SNR) realizations. These bounds are sensitive to code rate and length, meaning that they need to be calculated for all possible code configurations. In [11], an optimization example is given for a code of length 1296 bits, rate 1/2 and optimal schedules are able to preserve performance with an important saving of the total number of iterations.
B. Dynamic Scheduling and stopping criteria
Instead of using static schedules to control the total number of iterations, we suggest to use a dynamic scheduling. This means that the LDPC decoder takes decoding stopping decisions in real time based on some metric calculated over the decoded codeword. LDPC codewords realiability show different behaviors during their decoding. As shown in [15], some of them converge quickly while others oscillate, converge slowly (decodable codeword) or don’t converge at all (undecodable codeword) even after an important number of iterations, their reliability values are not monotonic in function of iterations. In this case it could be more tricky to stop at the first maximum value of reliability and go anew to the MMSE-IC detector and thus saving useless decoding iterations.

IV. SIMULATION AND RESULTS
The above proposed stopping criteria are simulated in the MIMO LDPC iterative receiver described in II and compared to the syndrome criterion. We use a 4x4 Spatial Multiplexing configuration. The number of outer loops is limited to 4. We use the IEEE 802.11n LDPC code (length 1296 bits and rate 1/2) with a maximum of 50 iterations and QPSK modulation.
Figures (2) and (3)give the Packet Error Rate performance (PER) after one outer loop and four outer loops respectively. We see that all the criteria based on MR, MRi, WMR and WPMR achieve the same performance as the syndrome criterion except the FMMR criterion which degrades performance. In comparison with a non iterative system (indicated as Non-Iter in Figure (3)), the iterative system achieves a gain of 0.5 dB.

A. First Outer Loop
At the 1st outer loop (figures (4) and (5)), average numbers of iterations increase with Eb/N0 until reaching peaks (at −2 dB for all, except FMMR whose peak is at −1dB), then the averages decrease and converge at −0.5dB. As expected the average decreases for the SC. We note that FMMR’s curve has lower slope and peak. For Eb/N0 < −2dB: all the criteria outperform the SC in terms of average number of iterations. MRi and CMR are also more advantageous than WMR and WPMR. FMMR could be well adapted in this first outer loop thanks to its low average and standard deviation but for Eb/N0 > −2dB FMMR’s performance degrades. At −2dB, MRi and SC intersect and using the syndrome for LDPC stopping becomes the best choice.

B. Fourth Outer Loop
At the 4th outer loop (figures (6) and (7)), average number of iterations shows higher slopes and lower peaks in comparison with the first outer loop. The curves reach their peaks at −3 dB and converge at −1 dB. Standard deviations shows also the same behavior.
From the 1st to the 4th outer loop, the peaks decreased and migrated from −2dB to −3dB which means that a significant amount of codewords which were unreliable(undecodable) at the first outer loop become reliable (decodable) after 4 loops of MIMO equalization. In contrast to the first outer loop the intersection point between the SC’s curve and MRi’s one happens at −2.5dB. From the above observations, we can conclude that in a MIMO LDPC iterative receiver, optimal stopping could be achieved by adapting its stopping criteria based on channel

conditions and from one outer loop to another (e.g, changing the stopping criterion at the intersection points of between MRi and SC).
Using the above techniques may reduce the average number of iterations and its standard deviation. This could be particularly beneficial in systems using Automatic Retransmission Request (ARQ) and its hybrid version (HARQ). In fact, a low Eb/N0 the receiver can decide to reject a data packet after (10 - 25) LDPC iterations instead of (50 iterations using the syndrome) and then request an earlier packet retransmission (or redundancy incrementation in HARQ), which increases the throughput of the receiver.
V. CONCLUSION
The problem of dynamically scheduling inner and outer iterations in a LDPC coded MIMO iterative has been considered in an attempt to minimize the total mean number of iterations while preserving performance. Dynamic scheduling based on mean reliability and other metrics has been evaluated. For low signal to noise ratio, significant savings on the total number of iterations can be achieved in comparison to classic stopping syndrome criterion while this latter becomes optimal for higher SNR. Stopping criteria has to be adapted to the received signal to noise ratio and the actual outer loop to exploit the gain achieved by MIMO equalization. Receiver’s throughput could be increased, accordingly, delay and energy consumption are reduced. The studied criterion could be applied to other MIMO configurations such as, Space-time coding, different numbers of antennas and also using other LDPC code rates and lengths which we don’t address in this paper and will be studied in a future work.
