Ultra Low Power QC-LDPC Decoder with High Parallelism

Ying CUI, Xiao PENG, Zhixiang CHEN, Xiongxin ZHAO, Yichao LU, Dajiang ZHOU and Satoshi GOTO Graduate School of Information, Production and Systems, Waseda University Kitakyushu, Japan

ABSTRACT This paper presents a novel high parallel de-
coder architecture for the quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes defined in WiMAX system. Based on the turbo-decoding message passing (TDMP) algorithm, this architecture costs 8~16 clock cycles for each iteration in the decoding process. In the normalized comparison with the state-of-art work, this design achieves up to 6.5x higher parallelism and 76% power reduction. The energy/bit/iteration of this design is only 1/5 of the previous work.
I. INTRODUCTION For application of the low-density parity-check
(LDPC) codes, the outstanding performance and hardware friendly property make the structured QC-LDPC codes become channel coding scheme of many modern wireless standards, such as the WiMAX, WiFi and WPAN. As one of the approved 4G standard, the WiMAX encounters challenges both on high throughput and low power. Thus, the QC-LDPC decoder for WiMAX system should integrate both the capability of high throughput and high energy efficiency. Fast convergent decoding algorithm and high parallel architecture are the most efficient ways to satisfy both of the trends.
In algorithm level, the TDMP algorithm in [1] achieves about two times faster convergent speed compared with the original two phase message passing (TPMP) algorithm. Based on this algorithm, partial parallel architecture realizes balance between high throughput and reasonable hardware cost, which is one of the major advantage of [2] compared with [3] and [4].
In architecture level, generally the decoding process of TDMP algorithm is carrying out layer by layer. The partial parallel architecture proposed in [5] revealed that its parallelism is equal to the layer size. In this block by block architecture, the number of clock cycles for one iteration is approximately equal to the number of nonzero sub-matrix, varying from 76 to 88 for different code rates defined in WiMAX. The implementation in [2] uses two sets of

processing units to improve the parallelism. The reordering scheme helps to partially solve the data dependency problem and thus the number of clock cycles for one iteration is reduced to 48~54.
In this work, a higher parallel architecture is proposed and implemented based on the TDMP algorithm and normalized min-sum approximation [6]. Instead of the block by block scheduling, 12 blocks are processed at the same time. By introducing pipelined architecture, it takes only 2 clock cycles to process one layer. Through matrix reordering and two-layer parallel processing, the clock cycles per iteration could be reduced to 8~16. Accordingly, the register-based message storing structure guarantees high parallelism and avoids interconnection problem.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section II describes the proposed decoder architecture, message storing structure and matrix reordering method. Section III presents the critical modules including the processing engine and permutation network. Section IV gives the implementation results and comparisons. Finally, Section V draws the conclusion of this paper.
II. SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE
A. Decoder Architecture Fig. 1 shows the block diagram of proposed
LDPC decoder architecture. In this design, 24 blocks in each layer are treated as two clusters, each of which contains 12 blocks. Two clusters are processed serially and messages within each cluster are updated in parallel. Since there are at most 96 rows in one layer, 96 clustered block processing engines (CBPEs) are utilized to update the messages of 12 blocks within one layer on the control of the central controller. Permutation network (PN) contains 12 6-bit 96×96 barrel shifter based rotators to cyclically shift 12 block messages according to the entry of basic matrix. In PN, the cross-bar switch array is implemented to divide 12 blocks of messages into 2 groups for two-layer parallel processing. Register-based memory is merged into

B. Message Storing Structure Due to the storing cells reusing scheme of APP
message and variable message, the total memory bits for APP/VN and CN messages of this design are 47,232 bits, which are much less than previous works. Conventionally SRAMs are used to store the messages for the iterative decoding. However, SRAM is not suitable for high parallel design. The high parallel architecture requires large word width which can be hardly provided by general SRAM. If the storing blocks are composed of plenty of SRAM banks, the hardware overhead brought by peripheral circuitry of SRAM would be significant since the word number of each SRAM is small [7]. On the other hand, too many SRAMs would bring serious interconnection problem which lead to the difficulty in placement and routing. Thus, all the messages are stored in registers in this design.

III. MODULE ARCHITECTURE

A. Architecture of CBPE As shown in Fig. 3, architecture and function of
processing engine are described in detail with pipelined flow chart. Based on the architecture described in section II, it takes 4 clock cycles to update all messages within one layer: in the first 2 clock cycles, VN messages are updated by 12 VN updaters and stored in the APP/VN REG array, and also the first and second minimum values are found out; in the consequent 2 clock cycles, CN messages and APP messages are updated by 12 CN/APP updaters. Since VN message updating of current layer and CN/APP message updating of previous layer can be processed at the same time, the processing of each layer occupies 2 clock cycles in this pipelined scheduling.
In this design, we utilized a 16-input min-sorter based on the algorithm described in [8]. For the code rate 1/2 and 2/3B, due to the two-layer parallel processing scheme, the min-sorter finds the minimum values among the VN messages from 2 layers respectively; while for other code rates, it searches minimum values from all the VN messages. B. Architecture of PN Fig. 4 shows the construction of PN, which
contains two kinds of networks: barrel shifters for cyclic permutation and the cross bar switch array (CBSA) for two-layer parallel decoding.
The cyclic shifter is composed of two parallel 96×96 barrel rotators (BR) to support the various input number and shift number. The two groups of shifted data are merged by the last stage of multiplexers. Unlike the 128×128 logarithmic shifter, this BR is realized by 7 stage 96-multiplexer arrays, which are capable of shifting number 32, 32, 16, 8, 4, 2, and 1 respectively. As shown in Table 1, compared with the previous designs in [9], [10], this structure could reduce both the gate count and the critical path.
To support the two-layer parallel processing scheme, 12 blocks of messages are divided into two groups by the CBSA network before they are transmitted to CBPEs for next layer updating. Also, the newly updated messages need to be recovered to its original order by reversed CBSA network before they go to PN network. Fig. 4 gives an example to illustrate the function of the CBSA network and its reverse, both composed of 6 CBSs. The total gate count of the CBSA and its reverse is about 70k, occupying only about 5% of the whole decoder.

IV. IMPLEMENTATION AND COMPARISON

A. Chip Implementation This design is demonstrated with the SMIC
65nm low leakage HS RVT Logic Process. Fig. 5 shows the layout of the proposed QC-LDPC decoder, whose area is 2.89 mm2 (1.7mm×1.7mm). As the layout shown, the 96 CBPEs and permutation network are the major part of the decoder, which are distributed all over the chip. Registerbased memory is combined with associate CBPEs, which reduces inter-connection complexity problem. The area utilization ratio achieves 80.3%.

V. CONCLUSION This paper introduces a much higher parallel
architecture for QC-LDPC decoder than previous published designs. Combined with the matrix reordering and two-row parallel processing scheme with minor hardware cost, it takes 8~16 clock cycles per iteration. Because of the high parallel architecture, APP messages and CN messages are all stored in registers, which helps increase the layout area utilization ratio. High parallelism and reasonable hardware cost make this design realize high energy efficiency, which presents significant improvement of energy efficiency than the state-ofart work.
