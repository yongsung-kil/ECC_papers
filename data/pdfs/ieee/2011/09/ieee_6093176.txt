Abstract‚ÄĒSingle-carrier frequency domain equalization (SCFDE) has been standardized as an alternative of orthogonal frequency division multiplexing (OFDM) in IEEE 802.11ad and 802.15.3c. In addition, with the use of low density parity-check (LDPC) codes, multi-Giga bits transmission can be supported in 60-GHz millimeter-wave (mmW) WLAN/WPAN with desired decoding complexity. In this paper, we present and evaluate an enhanced log-likelihood ratio (LLR) calculation method to facilitate the LDPC decoding in SC-FDE. In contrast to the virtual channel (VC) based LLR calculation approach (i.e., the Unique Word (UW) tracking scheme), our proposed method exhibits better bit error rate (BER) performance, and is more spectrally efficient.
I. INTRODUCTION
Single-carrier block transmission (SCBT) (a.k.a., SC-FDE) and orthogonal frequency division multiplexing (OFDM), have been adopted in IEEE 802.11ad/15.3c [1][2] as two physical layer (PHY) transmission schemes. Comparing with OFDM, SCBT exhibits lower peak-to-average power ratio (PAPR), and is less sensitivity to the carrier frequency offset (CFO) [3]. By employing frequency domain equalization (FDE), SCFDE has comparable receiver complexity with OFDM, and the inter-symbol interference (ISI) caused by the multi-path effects can be well suppressed. Due to these facts, SC-FDE is being considered as one important transmission scheme to support high data-rate, high performance millimeter-wave (mmW) WLAN/WPAN operating at 60-GHz.
Low density parity-check (LDPC) codes were first invented by Robert G. Gallager [4] in his doctoral dissertation. In the past decades, the idea of LDPC was rediscovered [5] and its use has been adopted in IEEE 802.11n [6] and 802.16 [7] as a promising channel coding scheme. This is due to the fact that LDPC can not only support highly reliable information transfer in the presence of data-corrupting noise, but also (most importantly) has lower decoding complexity than other near-Shannon-limit channel codes. In general, LDPC coding has following advantages over convolutional/Turbo coding, making it better suited for very high-rate data transmission:
‚ÄĘ stronger error detection and correction capability; ‚ÄĘ lower decoding complexity;

‚ÄĘ more flexible coding rate and block length. However, in order to perform the LDPC decoding in SCFDE, log-likelihood ratio (LLR) has to be known as a priori [8]. The LLR calculation involves the estimation of the effective channel gain and noise power, which will be changed due to FDE. Virtual channel (VC) based LLR calculation is proposed in [9] to facilitate the LDPC decoding in SC-FDE. In the VC based scheme, the so-called ‚ÄĚUnique Word (UW) [10]‚ÄĚ is used to track the post channel variations by taking the effects of FFT, FDE and IFFT into account. However, the performance improvement obtained by the VC based approach is still limited. This is due to the limited length of the UW and the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) degradation caused by the insertion of the UW. Therefore, there is a need for LLR estimation/calculation approach to facilitate the LDPC decoding in SC-FDE such that the error rate performance can be further improved. In this paper, we present and evaluate a simple method to calculate the LLR without changing the conventional frame structure. In our proposed scheme, starting with the preamble estimated channel gain and noise variance [11], the post effective channel gain and noise power are directly calculated by taking the transformations of FFT, FDE and IFFT into account. Simulation results show that the enhanced LLR calculation method outperforms the VC based scheme in terms of the bit error rate (BER) performance, and approaches that of the ideal case. Furthermore, without changing the frame structure, our proposed method is more spectrally efficient than the VC assisted scheme per FFT/IFFT block. However, these improvements come at the cost of a moderate increase in the computational complexity. Therefore, our proposed nonvirtual channel (NVC) based approach can be considered as an alternative to the VC aided scheme regarding practical concerns. II. SYSTEM MODEL AND VIRTUAL CHANNEL BASED LLR CALCULATION

The block diagram of the LDPC coded SC-FDE receiver is given in Fig.1. The received data stream is first pulse filtered. Then, the preambles are de-multiplexed from the payload data to facilitate the channel estimation. After the guard interval (GI) removal, fast Fourier transform (FFT) transfers the payload data from the time domain to the frequency domain. Then, the minimum mean squared error FDE (MMSE-FDE) is performed on the data to eliminate the ISI. The MMSE-FDE coefficients are calculated based upon the preamble estimated channel frequency response (CFR) and the SNR. We note here that since MMSE-FDE exhibits much better performance than zero-forcing FDE (ZF-FDE), we only consider MMSE-FDE in this work. After that, inverse FFT (IFFT) is employed, transforming the data stream from the frequency domain back to the time domain. After constellation de-mapping and LDPC decoding, the desired bit stream is obtained. However, in order to facilitate the LDPC decoding, the LLR has to be calculated in advance. Therefore, in this paper, we mainly focus on the LLR parameters calculation/estimation part, which is highlighted in Fig. 1.

In this section, we evaluate the BER performance of conventional, VC and NVC based LLR calculation methods. The system parameters are shown in Table I. In addition, we provide the ideal case as the benchmark for comparison. The ideal case reveals that both the channel gain and noise variance are perfectly obtained at the receiver end and transformed by FFT, ideal MMSE-FDE and IFFT, forming the post effective channel gain and noise power as the LLR parameters.
Fig. 3 shows the BER performance of the ideal case, conventional, VC and NVC based LLR estimation/calculation approaches under various LDPC coding rates. From Fig. 3, we observe that our proposed method outperforms both the conventional and VC based schemes in terms of the BER. For example, at the BER level of 10‚ąí4 and LDPC coding rate of 1/2, the NVC based approach exhibits 0.5dB SNR gain over the VC based approach. This gain grows to nearly 2dB in contrast to the conventional method. Furthermore, our proposed NVC based scheme approaches that of the ideal case. For instance, at LDPC coding rates of 7/8 and 3/4, the NVC based scheme shows almost the same BER performance as the ideal case.
