2011 8th International Symposium on Wireless Communication Systems, Aachen

A Flexible Layered LDPC Decoder
I. Tsatsaragkos and V. Paliouras University of Patras, Electrical and Computer Engineering Department, 26500 Patras, Greece

Abstract— We introduce a flexible layered decoder architecture for Quasi-Cyclic Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) codes. An iterative construction of the parity check matrix is exploited by the proposed decoder to achieve various degrees of parallelism, characterized by a high utilization of variable and check processing nodes, absence of memory conflicts, and a simple and scalable interconnection network. Furthermore, the proposed LDPC decoder supports variable code rate, information-word length and order of modulation. A comparison to prior-art decoders proves the efficiency of the proposed scheme.
Index Terms—QC-LDPC, layered decoding, partly parallel, flexible I/O, multi-level parallelism, iterative construction.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-Density Parity Check (LDPC) codes [1] [2] are a subcategory of linear block error correction codes, characterized by a sparse parity check matrix H and a corresponding Tanner graph [3]. Decoding is performed through an iterative process of information exchange between two types of processing units, called variable and check nodes [1]. This procedure is called belief propagation (BP). In the traditional fully parallel, two-phased decoding scheduling the calculation of all check nodes follows the calculation of all variable nodes and vice-versa [4], while in layered decoding the rows of H get processed in subsets, or layers, in successive order [5]. Layered decoding improves the convergence speed due the increased number of exchanged messages within a single iteration [6], at the cost of additional processing per iteration.
A considerable attribute of LDPC codes is that the decoding algorithm, and as a consequence the circuit that implements this algorithm, greatly depends on the structure of matrix H. LDPC code design techniques are divided into two main categories: (i) random code construction and (ii) structured code construction. LDPC matrices constructed randomly achieve performance close to the theoretical Shannon limit [2], at the cost of increased hardware complexity. Structured LDPC codes exhibit properties that may be exploited to achieve low complexity as well as linear-time encoding and parallel decoding processes. A drawback is that the structured codes with long block length suffer from coding gain degradation and error floors, when compared with random codes [7]. A subcategory of structured LDPC codes are the Quasi-Cyclic LDPC codes. QC-LDPC codes are comprised of square sub-matrices of size z [8]. Each sub-matrix is either a z × z zero sub-matrix or a z × z identity sub-matrix circularly right shifted by a factor s. QC-LDPC codes are widely used in present error correction systems due to lower hardware complexity. Furthermore, it has been shown that QC-LDPC codes can achieve comparable performance to randomly constructed codes if the codeword length is less than 10 Kbits [9]. Miladinovic and Fossorier [10] propose a systematic recursive method to construct good LDPC codes, using a QC-LDPC parity check matrix as a core upon which a larger code is constructed with extensive use of pseudorandom permutation matrices. Two analogous methods [11] construct a QC-LDPC code of larger length, by lifting a given QC-LDPC core code by proper floor or modulo operations, which have no serious performance degradation as compared with randomly constructed LDPC codes. Xu et al. [12] present a superposition method for constructing LDPC codes of girth at least six.

Chia-Yu et al. [7] propose a class of implementation-friendly structured LDPC codes, based on hierarchical QC-LDPC codes. By adding a two-level hierarchy in the parity check matrix, the decoder in [7] achieves improved coding and throughput performance and adjustable degree of decoding parallelism. Sha et al. [13] present the construction of a high-throughput decoder, implementing a class of implementation-oriented LDPC codes, known as shift-LDPC codes. Authors in [8] propose a scheduling algorithm that explores the maximum concurrency of the two stages of two-phased BP, aiming to avoid memory access conflicts. Sun et al. [14] present a parallel, scalable layered decoding system that supports various combinations of block lengths and code rates. A parallel layered decoding approach in [15] uses parallel processing among all layers and serial processing within each layer, improving throughput. Adding a proper constraint to the classic progressive edge-growth (PEG) algorithm, Ding et al. construct a QC-LDPC code with increased degree of parallelism and little performance loss [16].
In this paper we propose the design of an LDPC decoder that exploits the properties of an iteratively constructed H matrix to achieve a scalable decoding throughput with moderate hardware requirements. In particular, properties of the H matrix are exploited to achieve memory conflict-free operation for various degrees of parallelism, while a distributed local memory scheme permits highly parallel operation without any cost in additional storage. Furthermore, efficient designs for the variable node, the check node and the I/O circuitry are described. Particularly focus is given to I/O circuitry as it used to efficiently provide significant flexibility in terms of information block size, code rate and modulation support, essential characteristics of modern communication standards.
II. LDPC CODE CONSTRUCTION METHOD We use an iterative construction of LDPC matrix H. By properly exploiting multiple levels of matrix H expansion, the designer is able to increase the randomness of the parity check matrix, which enhances the decoding performance of the code, while exploring decoder complexity-throughput tradeoffs. For a given l×c base matrix H(i), an H(i + 1) matrix of size zl × zc is constructed as follows: For each column k (k:1 to c) of H(i) a zl × z size sub-matrix Ck is constructed according to the following steps: (a) Replace each zero by a zero z × z sub-matrix. (b) Replace each unit by a z × z right shifted identity sub-matrix, having a shifting factor s, where s is particular to each sub-matrix. (c) Construct Hk(i + 1)=[Hk−1(i + 1)Ck], where sub-matrix Ck is constructed according to algorithm A given below. Algorithm A selects a number T of sets of shifting factors that introduce no cycles of size e to the corresponding Tanner graph of the extended matrix Hk(i + 1), and among these T sets selects the set that introduces the least number of cycles of size e + 2. It is here used to reduce the number of short cycles in the Tanner graph, since short cycles may deteriorate the error correction capability.

RAM cpu includes max(dc) locations, 1-bit wide, and stores the signs of the variable-to-check (v2c) messages (dc: check degree). CP U includes two registers (R M and R M 2), a T-flip flop and sequential logic, used to store the minimum and the second minimum incoming message. A flagging technique is used to rename each register to avoid updating both the registers when a message, having absolute value lower than the values in the two registers, enters the CP U . Additionally, a sub-circuit, having two registers (R conv1 and R conv2), an OR and a XOR gate, is used to calculate convergence signal. A codeword is valid only if it meets all the parity check constraints that correspond to the rows of code H. Therefore, XOR of all the signs of the v2c messages (hard desicions) that are input during execution of each row of matrix H, in every CP U unit, must return a ‘0’ result. The convergence signal is asserted at the end of an iteration in case the algorithm converges to a valid codeword (early termination), or else, decoding continues until a predetermined maximum number of iterations is reached. With minor modifications and a simple multiplexing logic, the proposed decoder is capable to support normalized and offset Min-Sum layered decoding [19].
Permutation network: The permutation network is composed of several Logarithmic Barrel Shifters (LBS) operating in parallel. LBS is a network of multiplexers, which circularly reorders its inputs for as many positions as required by the code. For B inputs/outputs the total number of multiplexers is log2 B . Multiplexer i (i: 0 to B − 1) of stage j (j: 0 to log2 B -1) receives as inputs the outputs of multiplexers i and mod(i + s, B) of the previous stage, where s = 2j . For B = 6, there are log2 B = 3 multiplexer stages. For a code of length N and K information bits, it is greatly desirable for a given LDPC decoder architecture to be able to support shorter information block sizes (Ks). For shorter information blocks, the F LEX IN P U T unit puts maximum reliability on some variable nodes. Particularly, this sub-circuit writes the maximum positive value in the memory locations that correspond to the K − Ks (shortened) first bits of the information word, not transmitted by the encoder. In order to support QAM of order q, and efficiently exploit the internal parallel organization, the decoder uses units that re-group groups of X bits (or LLRs) into groups of Y bits (or LLRs) and vice versa. The algorithm implemented by the flexible input/output units is based on a queue of bits or LLRs, an output multiplexer, an input network and a finite state machine. The scheduling policy implemented is that as soon as a set of Y items is available, it is dequeued. F LEX IN P U T sub-circuit supports Q different modulation schemes in the input of the decoder. For a q-th order modulation, only d = log2 q of the t inputs of the decoder are active. M QAM OU T P U T sub-circuit (Fig. 6) supports Q different modulation schemes. The hard decision bits derived by the variable nodes are re-grouped into d output bits. When t = 8, d of the 8 1-bit outputs (signal sel out) are

active, according to the modulation. Thus, the decoder outputs the estimated codeword serially in d-bit sub-frames. In Fig. 6 we assume Q = 7 (4-QAM up to 256-QAM modulation). F LEX IN P U T and M QAM OU T P U T units provide significant design flexibility and reduce complexity, because the same hardware accommodates the various code rates, information block lengths and orders of modulation. Code rate, information-word length and modulation can change on-the-fly for consecutive codewords.
C. Parallel configurations
The iterative construction of H allows the decoder to implement different levels of parallelism. Assume that H is constructed in two steps. For a given L × K base matrix Hb, the first step of the matrix extension uses z1 × z1 sub-matrices, and the second step uses z2 × z2 sub-matrices. The M × N matrix H consists of M/(z1 · z2) sub-matrices, or layers, of size (z1 · z2) × N . The particular code construction method ensures that there is a unique ‘1’ element in every column of these sub-matrices, allowing the parallel processing of z1 · z2 lines without data conflicts. For example, we construct in two steps a rate-3/4 QC-LDPC code with codeword length N = 2016 and two levels of extension. The iterative construction method uses a 6 × 24 core base matrix (Fig. 7(a)). The first step of the algorithm, assuming z1 = 21 (Fig. 7(b)), gives a 126 × 504 matrix. The second step, with z2 = 4, (Fig. 7(c)) gives the 504 × 2016 QC-LDPC matrix, which consists of six 84 × 2016 sub-matrices. The corresponding decoder architecture is capable of parallelly processing k = z1 · z2 = 84 rows of H, accelerating decoding by a factor of k.
Fig. 8 depicts a simplified version of an one-level z-parallel decoder architecture. V P U bank unit includes z V P U s and the CP U bank unit includes z CP U s. They are interconnected through a z × z LBS network. Larger values of p produce highly parallel decoder architectures, hence larger and faster, while smaller values of p lead to less hardware demanding decoder architectures, at the cost of throughput reduction.

IV. IMPLEMENTATION RESULTS AND COMPARISONS
The proposed decoder architectures have been designed in VHDL and mapped into a Virtex5-XC5VLX330T FPGA. Degree distribution polynomials of 802.16e (W iM AX) LDPC codes have been used for the construction of the multi-level QC-LDPC codes. Table I summarizes the hardware implementation results — device resources utilization and throughput — of rate-0.75 codes, for various block lengths N and degrees of parallelism. All decoder implementations support the mentioned in Section III-B flexible I/O circuitry (R1 = 0.75, R2 = 0.95, step = 0.01, shortening, Q = 7). The proposed decoder uses early termination, therefore the average number of decoding iterations depends on the noise level. Experimental results of the decoder performance show that at high SNR levels the average number of iterations is 2.8, independently of the predetermined maximum number of iterations. For the throughput estimations, an average of 3 iterations is assumed. The LDPC codes employed have been constructed in two steps, where z1 = 21 and z2 = z. The degree of parallelism k equals to z · p, where p is the aforementioned in Section III-C design parameter. Fig. 10 illustrates the dependence of p on the area complexity and the throughput of the 2K (N = 2016) decoding system. Increase F actor is the ratio of area complexity (or throughput) for p > 1 over area complexity (or throughput) for p = 1. Namely, the Increase F actor of the LUTs utilization is 2.59 (15980/6160) and 6.24 (38446/6160) for p = 7 and p = 21, respectively. The corresponding factors of the throughput increase are 7.07 (106/15) and 20.6 (309/15). The increase of the throughput is more than three times higher than the corresponding increase of the hardware complexity. The results are similar for the 4K, 8K and 16K codes. In a comparable work [7], the hierarchical decoder boosts the throughput by a factor of 32 while the resource usage is increased 20 times (1.6 times higher throughput increase). Therefore, the proposed decoder is almost twice more efficient. Table II compares the proposed flexible layered decoder to prior art. A detailed comparison is difficult, because the implementation parameters (code rate, frequency, iterations, prototyping platform) vary between different works. However, it is obvious that the proposed decoder offers a wide range of design choices, providing comparable throughput with existing highly parallel decoder architectures, or very low complexity circuits suitable for lower speed applications. Moreover, experimental results (Fig. 11) show that the performance of the proposed iteratively constructed codes is equivalent to the corresponding performance of the two rate-0.75 LDPC codes of the WiMax standard.

V. CONCLUSIONS The proposed decoder exploits an iterative parity-check matrix construction technique, providing parallel flexible multi-level architectures. It succeeds to very effectively manage the trade-off between decoding speed and hardware complexity. Efficient design of processor nodes, interconnection network and I/O circuitry allows highly parallel, conflict-free operations and provides increased flexibility in terms of supported block size, code rate and modulation, with moderate hardware requirements.
