Degree Distribution Design for LDPC Codes:
A Derivative Matching Approach
Enrico Paolini, Member, IEEE, Marco Chiani, Fellow, IEEE, and Marc P. C. Fossorier, Fellow, IEEE
Abstract—A deterministic method to design degree distribu-
tions for low-density parity-check codes over the binary erasure
channel is proposed. This method consists of matching the first
and high-order derivatives of the extrinsic information transfer
(EXIT) function of the variable node set to the corresponding
derivatives of the inverse EXIT function of the check node
set, in order to reduce the gap between the two curves in
the EXIT chart. A sufficient condition for a check-concentrated
distribution to achieve derivative matching up to some order
is first obtained, and then a deterministic design algorithm,
enabled by the Fourier-Budan theorem, is developed exploiting
this sufficient condition. A comparison with other deterministic
design techniques is also provided, revealing the potential of the
proposed algorithm.
Index Terms—Binary erasure channel, channel coding, erasure
decoding, iterative decoding, low-density parity-check codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
S
INCE the discovery that sequences of low-density parity-
check (LDPC) code ensembles can achieve the capacity
of the binary erasure channel (BEC) under low-complexity it-
erative decoding [1], [2], the problem of constructing capacity
achieving sequences has been deeply investigated. A related
problem, which represents the subject of this work, consists
of designing a single LDPC ensemble approaching the BEC
capacity as much as possible, under given constraints.
The principal analytical techniques proposed so far to
design capacity achieving sequences for the BEC may be
found in [3]–[9]. The constructions in [2]–[4], [7]–[9] lead
to sequences achieving capacity with an unbounded com-
plexity per information bit [11]. Among them, the Tornado
sequence and the “right-regular” sequence were introduced in
[1] and [3], respectively. Slightly modified versions of these
two sequences were successively regarded in [4] as special
instances of a much more general technique to systematically
construct capacity achieving sequences. Notably, the LDPC
code ensemble sequences developed in [7], [8] (see also the
previous work [10]) represent an extension of those from
[4], in that the ensemble design technique in [4] may be

over those proposed in [4] is their capability to achieve
thresholds arbitrarily close to the capacity with a smaller
maximum variable node (VN) degree and a smaller number
of active VN degrees, for the same check node (CN) degree.
Furthermore, in regard to the increase of decoding complexity
as their gap to capacity vanishes, the sequences in [7], [8],
as well as those in [4], can be asymptotically optimal upon
a proper choice of the function defining their maximum
variable node degree [8, Theorem 5]. (For the notion of
asymptotical optimality the reader can refer to [4].) It has been
also shown that the same design principle proposed in [7],
[8] (namely, “successive maximization”) can be extended to
puncturing patterns, resulting in universally capacity achieving
rate compatible sequences [9, Theorem 3].1 In contrast with
[2]–[4], [7]–[9], the sequences proposed in [5], [6] are capable
to achieve capacity with a bounded complexity per information
bit, taking advantage of an ensemble parametrization different
from that offered by a simple degree distribution pair (DDP).
An ensemble picked from any of the above sequences exhibits
a threshold that is usually very close to the Shannon limit.
In parallel, numerical optimization techniques have been
proposed, that allow to construct DDP’s whose threshold
approaches the BEC capacity under a given set of constraints.
Among them, we may cite the iterative procedure based on
linear programming described in [2] and an evolutionary,
parallel optimization algorithm known as differential evolution
(DE) [12], which was first applied to LDPC codes in [13].
In this paper, a class of capacity-approaching check-
concentrated (i.e., all check nodes in the bipartite graph have
the same degree) LDPC degree distributions for the BEC is
introduced together with an algorithm for their design. These
distributions are closely related to those proposed in [3], [7]
and are referred to as pseudo-binomial distributions. Their
construction algorithm is based on the concept of deriva-
tive matching. According to the area theorem [14, Theorem
1], the BEC capacity can be asymptotically approached by

There are several reasons for our interest in check-

The paper is organized as follows. Some preliminary defi-
nitions are introduced in Section II. Conditions for derivative
matching are developed in Section III. Here, some known
DDP’s are discussed from a derivative matching perspective
and it is also shown that, among the check-concentrated
DDP’s, the right-regular one from [3] is optimum in terms of
derivative matching. The proposed design method is described
in Section IV, while numerical results comparing the proposed
approach to other deterministic design techniques are provided
in Section V. Concluding remarks are given in Section VI.

VI. CONCLUSION
An algorithm to generate check-regular LDPC DDP’s for
the erasure channel, satisfying given constraints and character-
ized by good thresholds, has been proposed. The algorithm has
been developed assuming a derivative matching perspective
of the EXIT charts. Given the CN degree, the maximum
VN degree and the design rate, the algorithm returns a DDP
such that the derivatives of the corresponding EXIT functions
are matched up to a desired order. The algorithm has been
compared with other analytical optimization techniques. It has
been illustrated that, in several cases, the algorithm is capable
to return DDP’s with a better threshold under the same set of
constraints.

Marco Chiani (M’94-SM’02-F’11) was born in
Rimini, Italy, in April 1964. He received the Dr. Ing.
degree (magna cum laude) in Electronic Engineering
and the Ph.D. degree in Electronic and Computer
Science from the University of Bologna in 1989 and
1993, respectively. Dr. Chiani is a Full Professor at
the II Engineering Faculty, University of Bologna,
Italy, where he is the Chair in Telecommunication.
During the summer of 2001 he was a Visiting Scien-
tist at AT&T Research Laboratories in Middletown,
NJ. He is a frequent visitor at the Massachusetts
Institute of Technology (MIT), where he presently holds a Research Affiliate
appointment.
Dr. Chiani’s research interests include wireless communication systems,
MIMO systems, wireless multimedia, low density parity check codes (LD-
PCC) and UWB. He is leading the research unit of CNIT/University of
Bologna on Joint Source and Channel Coding for wireless video and is a
consultant to the European Space Agency (ESA-ESOC) for the design and
evaluation of error correcting codes based on LDPCC for space CCSDS
applications.
He has chaired, organized sessions and served on the Technical Program
Committees at several IEEE International Conferences. He was Co-Chair of
the Wireless Communications Symposium at ICC 2004. In January 2006 he
received the ICNEWS award “For Fundamental Contributions to the Theory
and Practice of Wireless Communications”. He was the recipient of the
2008 IEEE ComSoc Radio Communications Committee Outstanding Service
Award.
He is the past chair (2002-2004) of the Radio Communications Committee
of the IEEE Communication Society and past Editor of Wireless Communi-
cation (2000-2007) for the IEEE TRANSACTIONS ON COMMUNICATIONS.
Marc Fossorier (F’06) received the B.E. degree from the National Institute
of Applied Sciences (INSA.), Lyon, France, in 1987, and the M.S. and Ph.D.
degrees in 1991 and 1994, respectively, all in electrical engineering. His re-
search interests include decoding techniques for linear codes, communication
algorithms, and statistics.
He is a recipient of a 1998 NSF Career Development Award and became
IEEE Fellow in 2006. He has served as Editor for the IEEE TRANSACTIONS
ON INFORMATION THEORY from 2003 to 2006, as Editor for the IEEE
COMMUNICATIONS LETTERS from 1999 to 2008, as Editor for the IEEE
TRANSACTIONS ON COMMUNICATIONS from 1996 to 2003, and as Treasurer
of the IEEE Information Theory Society from 1999 to 2003. From 2002 to
2008, he was also an Elected Member of the Board of Governors of the
IEEE Information Theory Society which he served as Second Vice-President
and First Vice-President. He was Co-chairman of the 2007 International
Symposium on Information Theory (ISIT), Program Co-chairman for the 2000
International Symposium on Information Theory and Its Applications (ISITA)
and Editor for the Proceedings of the 2006, 2003, and 1999 Symposiums
on Applied Algebra, Algebraic Algorithms, and Error Correcting Codes
(AAECC).
