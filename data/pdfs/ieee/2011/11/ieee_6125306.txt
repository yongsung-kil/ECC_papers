Finite-Length Rate-Compatible LDPC Codes Based on Extension Techniques
Jingjing Liu, Rodrigo C. de Lamare Department of Electronics University of York York, UK
Email: {jI622.rcdI500@ohm.york.ac.uk}

Abstract- We propose two innovative extension schemes for Rate-Compatible (RC) Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes with short/moderate block lengths. The proposed designs are based on the counting cycles algorithm and the Approximate Cycle Extrinsic Message Degree (EMD). Our layer-structured extension schemes enjoy a linear-time encodable ability and relatively low decoding complexity thanks to low degree profiles with limited decoding iterations, such that they intrinsically fit in a type-II hybrid automatic repeat-request (ARQ) system. Simulation results show that the proposed approaches manage to yield a performance gain of up to 0.3 dB when compared to the existing extension technique.
I. INTRODUCTION
Rate-Compatibility has always been desired in wireless communications with channel variations. Provided with the channel state information (CSI), higher rate codes are applied to achieve higher data throughput when the channel condition is good otherwise lower rate codes are used to guarantee a reliable transmission. Thus both capacity and reliability are realized in such a scenario. However, deploying many pairs of encoders and decoders is never considered feasible in practical applications due to its high cost. As a consequence, the solution comes up with Rate-Compatible (RC) codes which refers to a family of codes where the bipartite graph of a higher rate code is embedded in that of a lower rate code, or in other words the parity bits of higher rate codes are embedded in the parity bits of lower rate codes [1]. For example, Lin and Yu [2] designed an RC coding scheme for a hybrid automatic repeat-request with forward error correction (ARQ/FEC) system where the transmitter would keep sending additional redundant bits under the request as long as the decoder claims a failure on the decoding. Having been applied to a variety of codes, RC techniques are proven to not only present the high capacity in quality communication but also incur substantial complexity reduction because of the single pair of encoder/decoder structure.
The Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes are introduced in [3], [4] as an advanced coding technique for its Shannon capacity-approaching performance over the Additive White Gaussian Noise (AWGN) channel as well as less decoding complexity in comparison with the Turbo codes. Generally speaking, RC-LDPC codes can be obtained by either puncturing or extension. By removing partial parity-check bits puncturing is able to create high rate LDPC codes from a low rate mother code or parent code. On the other hand, extension applies by taking extra parity-check bits to increase the size

of the parity-check matrix of the mother code which ends up with lower rate codes stemming from a high rate mother code. In the past few years, there are many researchers who put in efforts on designing efficient RC-LDPC algorithms. In [5], a simple but efficient technique is offered for regular RC-LDPC codes in packet data communication, while in [6] a joint shortening/puncturing scheme brings about excellent performance through improving the nodes' degree distribution. Other works such as [7] and [8] are also considered as good alternatives but can only be implemented on specific LDPC codes, e.g. the semi-random codes or the repeat-accumulate (RA) codes.
The most recent work on RC-LDPC codes has been reported in [9] where we propose three puncturing schemes based on the optimization of the puncturing pattern. Even though the results demonstrate that all our schemes can generate finite-length irregular RC-LDPC codes with good decoding performances at high rates (ranging from 0.5 to 0.9), effective solutions for low rate codes (ranging from 0.1 to 0.5) are still an open problem. In this paper, we leverage that gap by proposing two extension methods and the main contributions include:
• The proposed techniques manage to create RC-LDPC codes that are highly flexible in the block length, regularity (available for both regular and irregular codes) and rates (across a wide range from 0.1-0.9 when applied with puncturing schemes in [9] together.
• Combined with Density Evolution, the counting-cycle algorithm as well as the Approximate Cycle Extrinsic Message Degree (EMD) spectrum are employed to extend the parity-check matrix H, which means for the first time extending RC-LDPC codes relies on refining the cycle structure.
• Our simulation results illustrate that by taking advantage of similar encoding configurations in [10] our proposed schemes achieve better performance while saving many iterations (by using the same decoding algorithm).
• We expand the original parity-check matrix in a way such that additional rows and columns are padded in a level by level fashion, which are most likely to be developed for a type-II hybrid automatic repeat-request (ARQ) system.

On the other hand, the ACE as a new metric has changed the view regarding the impact of short cycles to a Tanner Graph. Paying attention to short cycles results from the analysis of the BP algorithm, known as the most effective decoding algorithm for LDPC codes, where an iteration refers to an information exchange between all variable nodes and their relevant check nodes. During this process, variable nodes deliver the message to connected check nodes regarding estimated values of corresponding bits while check nodes feedback the message to connected variable nodes regarding a guess on their values based on messages from other variable nodes. As the BP algorithm operates under the assumption that every variable node is independent, the existence of short cycles definitely break that assumption especially the length-4 cycles are the most unwelcome. However, in fact not all short cycles necessarily impair the BP decoding. Some of them, on the contrary, can help to obtain faster convergence since check nodes in such cycles have considerable connections to the rest of the Tanner Graph which are beneficial to exchange of the extrinsic information. Consequently, the ACE spectrum is introduced to quantize a short cycle's connectivity so as to evaluate whether it is detrimental in the iterative decoding. v. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we investigate the CC-based extension and ACE-based extension by jointly applying the counting cycle algorithm, the ACE spectrum, and the density evolution. Simulation results show that both extension designs are suitable
for creating RC-LDPC in low rates' range (R < 0.5). As

a consequence, combining our short-cycle based puncturing
techniques we manage to generate RC-LDPC with a wide
range of rates (0.1 < R < 0.9).
