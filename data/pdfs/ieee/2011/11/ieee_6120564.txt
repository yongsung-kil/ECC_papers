Erasing Bit Nodes on the Bipartite Graph for Enhanced Performance of LDPC Codes

P. C. Catherine Electrical and Electronics Department
Faculty of Engineering University of Mauritius

Abstract—The proposed work is based on the fact that the complete set of bit nodes for an LDPC code may not always be required at the receiving side for successful decoding. A corresponding strategy is therefore built up. In contrast to common practice, the total number of iterations available is shared among different sets. The first set runs the decoding algorithm with all its bit nodes. Successive sets (in case of decoding failure) runs each with a different selection of “erased” bit nodes, leading to an overall non-monotonic behavior. The end result is a system capable of effectively dealing with the problem of cycles and trapping sets without even being aware of their existence. Reported results show an important coding gain over conventional systems.
I. INTRODUCTION
LDPC codes, originally designed by R. Gallager in 1962 [1] and rediscovered by D. MacKay and R. Neal in 1996 [2], are known to perform arbitrarily close to the Shannon limit for sufficiently long blocklengths [3], [4]. However, when it comes to shorter codes, the weaknesses due to certain sub-graph structures such as cycles and trapping sets are made apparent, and negatively affect the performance of the decoding algorithm [5], [6], [7]. Much effort is therefore devoted to mitigate their effects.
One class of methods works at the code creation (bipartite graph edge-filling) stage to decrease the cardinality of bad sub-structures e.g. [8], [9], [10]. Another class operates at the decoding stage by biasing the algorithm in such a way as to minimize the effects of incumbent cycles and trapping sets e.g. [11], [12], [13]. While the proposed method, based on [14], also works at the decoding side, it is very different from methods described in the second category in that it requires no characterization or even awareness of the existence of bad sub-structures.
In addition, the proposed method seeks to address an older problem faced by the conventional decoder: monotonic behavior. Indeed, for each run of the algorithm, the decoder usually moves in a given direction and can rarely retract from it. And the sub-structures, having the ability to attract the decoder into bad states, easily prey upon this weakness.

But what if a method could be devised that empowers the decoder to choose direction and not be attracted towards bad configurations? This is precisely the objective of this work: To transform the decoder so that it utilizes the bipartite graph in a non-monotonic fashion, hence providing the basis of an algorithm that escapes the influence of bad sub-structures. As inspired by J. Pearl [15], the present work also views “the belief network to be a computational architecture for reasoning about knowledge”.
The paper is organized as follows. In section II, a short description of cycles and trapping sets is provided, so as to understand their bad effects on the decoding algorithm in general. A rationale for the proposed modified algorithm, as well as a description of the latter are provided in sections III and IV respectively. Results are presented in section V. We conclude with section VI.
II. OVERVIEW OF CYCLES AND TRAPPING SETS
A. Cycles
A cycle of length 2d is a set of d bit nodes and d check nodes connected by edges such that a path exists that goes through each node in the set and connects each node to itself without traversing an edge twice.
Consider Fig. 1. For ease, only edges corresponding to two cycles are shown, and are of length 4 and 6 respectively. To better understand the bad effects of cycles, assume that the three bit nodes c5, c8 and c11 are in error. Check nodes f4, f6 and f7, being satisfied, will trigger no message to alert other neighboring bits. On the contrary, these check nodes will send supportive messages to c5, c8 and c11, eventually increase their (false) confidence. The cascading effect eventually drives other nodes in the graph to revise their beliefs wrongly and attract the decoding process to an ultimate failure.

III. RATIONALE OF THE PROPOSED SCHEME: EMPOWERING THE DECODER WITH NON-MONOTONIC

CAPABILITY

To have someone change her mind (i.e. to adopt a non-

monotonic perspective), she must first be infused with a

sufficient degree of doubt with respect to the belief she

currently holds. Only then will she be in a position to

consider other possibilities. The same is true for the LDPC

decoder. The latter usually finds it extremely difficult to

retract from bad bipartite graph configurations (see section

II) because of the low-entropy of its bit nodes. Low entropy

is equal to high confidence. Unfortunately for the decoding

process, a high confidence in a bad configuration is usually

fatal.

To escape the attraction of these states therefore, it is

necessary that the combined entropy of all bit nodes be

increased. This can be achieved by setting the normalized

likelihood

ratio

of

selected

bits

to

1 2

,

effectively

erasing

their

influence in the graph. Practically this would be similar to a

situation where not all bit nodes are used for the decoding

process. This is indeed an envisaged scenario, and even a desired one. As proved in [14] indeed, not all bit nodes are required at receiving end for successful decoding. In fact, the minimum ratio of nodes required is given by δ, such that:

δ > R+

(1)

where R is the code rate and is a strictly positive constant which decreases when the signal-to-noise ratio of the link increases.
Bit nodes, once erased, are perfect listeners in that they do not alter received messages with their own data. Rather they forward messages legitimately throughout the graph. While erasing selected nodes thus, the decoder may well be breaking down cycles and trapping sets, or at least tearing down parts of these bad structures. The decoding result will then be a function of the set of erased bits. One may conjecture that the procedure may erase “good” bit nodes. This may indeed happen. The strategy however is to use many sets with different erasure configurations (see Fig. 3). Non-monotonic behavior is ensured by the different decoding paths of the decoder when different sets of erased bit nodes are used.
The proposed technique also makes a more efficient use of iteration runs. Strictly, as specified in [16], the number of independent iterations of the sum product algorithm is equal to half the girth of the graph. However even contemporary methods makes use of quite large number of iterations and using up to 500 is not uncommon. In addition to disrupting the independence assumption of the decoding algorithm, and helping the effects of cycles and trapping sets to strengthen, using large number of iterations usually do not help. Indeed, the decoder usually settles to some stable or oscillatory state after a few iterations [17] in case of decoding failure.
Instead, the proposed technique partitions the available number of iterations available among the different sets. Thus each decoder configuration (with its set of erased bit nodes) is given a chance to run the decoding algorithm. While some will fail, some will succeed making a big difference in terms of decoding performance (see section V).

IV. DEPLOYING THE PROPOSED SCHEME
The basic idea is relatively simple: The sum-product algorithm (SPA) [18] is initially launched with all bit nodes (no erasures). If a valid codeword is found, the procedure is stopped and success is declared. Otherwise, the recovery algorithm is launched for different runs (each with a selection of erased bit nodes) with the expectation that some will return with a valid codeword. If so, the one closest in hamming distance to the received vector is selected and success is declared. Else, if no valid codeword is found, failure is declared. The procedure is now described stepwise.

2) Run the SPA for Ic iterations (no bit node erasure). If a valid codeword is retrieved, stop the procedure and declare success. Else go to step 3.
3) Run the SPA for Ir iterations for each set of the recovery algorithm (pattern of erasure dictated by the erasure matrix). Store valid codewords retrieved during the process. If no valid codeword is retrieved, stop the procedure and declare failure. Else go to step 4.
4) Select the codeword which is closest to the received vector as the recovered one and declare success.
It is interesting to note that the present work builds upon and refines the scheme originally proposed in [14] as follows:
• The recovery algorithm is substantially simplified while keeping the gist of the concept.
• The number of iterations used in [14] was large (of the order of thousands). However, very good results are still obtained with much less iterations (of the order of hundreds) as demonstrated in section V.
• Instead of using random sets, a specific technique is used to generate sets used for decoding (see table I).

We complete this section with a brief (time and space) complexity analysis of the algorithm.
First consider the time complexity. Note that for an (n, k) LDPC code, the only task of the recovery algorithm is to set the likelihood ratio values of n terms and hand over these values to the sum-product algorithm for each decoding run. This can be performed in linear time. As such, a comparison with the conventional decoding algorithm can be made by considering the number of iterations used per block. Taking the case of the (504, 252) MacKay code of Fig. 5 as an example, we note that at Eb/No = 3 dB, the average number of iterations of the recovery algorithm with Ic = 100, Ir = 20 and ns = 20 was 5.90 per decoded block. The conventional method, on the other hand, consumed 6.01 iterations on average for IC = 500. Moreover, at Eb/No = 3.5, both the recovery algorithm and the conventional system consume about 4.67 iterations per decoded block. This result demonstrates that the recovery algorithm enables a more efficient use of iteration runs (and therefore of processing power).
With regards to space complexity, the additional requirements of the proposed scheme are: 1) an erasure matrix of size n by ns as defined in table I and 2) an array of length n to store the valid codeword closest in Hamming distance to the received vector.

VI. CONCLUSION
The sum-product algorithm requires two resources to operate: Bit nodes (which are the first to send decoding metrics into the bipartite graph, from received channel data), and iteration runs (which are essential to propagate decoding metrics via the arsenal of edges connecting nodes together over the bipartite graph). The proposed scheme makes a more efficient use of both resources. Sets, each carrying a different selection of “erased” bit nodes runs the decoding algorithm. To each set, a portion of the total number of iterations available to the system is assigned. As such, from an external point of view, it appears that the proposed system is utilizing the same computing resources as its conventional counterpart.
While promising results are obtained in terms of coding gain, it might be an interesting topic to investigate whether knowledge of bad-subgraph locations on the bipartite graph could help in choosing better erasure sets with better results. Another interesting feature would be to adapt the system so that the number of sets allocated is varied according to the amount of time and resources available for each particular reception. Indeed each new set holds renewed hope for decoder convergence, non-monotonicity being ensured.
