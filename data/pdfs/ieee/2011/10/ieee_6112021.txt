Graph-Based Iterative Reconstruction of Sparse Signals for Compressed Sensing
Anantha Raman Krishnan∗, Swaminathan Sankararaman+ and Bane Vasic∗

Abstract— In this paper, we consider the problem of reconstruction of sparse signals in compressed sensing. In particular, we introduce a novel iterative algorithm based on graph-based decoding of low-density parity-check codes which possesses desirable properties like good performance, low complexity and running time, and ease of implementation. In this work, we outline the reconstruction algorithm, and analyze its performance on some measurement matrices. Furthermore, we also provide some initial results on the theoretical performance limits of this algorithm.
I. INTRODUCTION
Compressed sensing (CS) is a technique for the reconstruction of sparse signals from small set of measurements. Introduced by Donoho et al. [1], CS is powerful in that, under the conditions of sparse input, it provides a methodology that enables undersampling of input – hitherto not possible by traditional reconstruction techniques – while providing theoretical limits on the number of measurements required.
Although CS exhibits good performance and robustness to noise, it suffers from one impediment, namely, the use of linear program for signal reconstruction. In particular, reconstruction of the sparse signals from measurements involves solving a linear program (LP) that finds the lowest L1-norm solution that agrees with the measurements. The use of linear programming for reconstruction makes CS disadvantageous from an implementation standpoint because (i) cubic complexity of LP solvers renders them impracticable in scenarios involving large-scale inputs and/or real-time reconstruction, and (ii) LP solvers use floating point operations that are not amenable to on-chip implementation. There has been considerable research to address these issues. Several reconstruction algorithms, e.g., Orthogonal Matching Pursuit [2], iterative thresholding [3], and their variants have been proposed as alternatives to LP reconstruction. Typically, these algorithms trade reduced complexity with reduced performance. Improvements in performance have been shown by incorporating prior information about the inputs source into the reconstruction algorithms. Nevertheless, these algorithm remain largely infeasible for real-time and on-chip applications.
In the recent past, several connections between low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes and compressed sensing have emerged (e.g. [4], [5], [6]). For instance, Dimakis and Vontobel showed that a binary matrix which is a good parity check matrix for an LDPC code is also a good zero-one measurement

In this paper, we introduce an iterative reconstruction algorithm inspired from parallel bit-flipping algorithm (see [11] for details) for decoding LDPC codes. This algorithm possesses linear complexity and is amenability to fixed-point implementation. We provide performance analysis of the algorithm, and provide a preliminary theoretical analysis of the algorithm. Let H be a measurement matrix as defined previously. It is

C. Stopping Sets
In this paper, we show that there is a close connection between the performance of our algorithm and the presence of certain subgraphs – known as stopping sets – in the Tanner graph of the measurement matrix. Stopping sets were originally introduced in the context of decoding of LDPC codes for the binary erasure channel [13], and are defined thus:

Definition 1 (Stopping Set [13]): A stopping set T is a subset of V such that all neighbours of T are connected to T at least twice.

A formal inductive proof can be readily obtained from the above discussion.
Remark: Algorithm 1 , along with Eqns. 5 through 8, has been defined for elements hi,j ∈ {0, 1}. It is easy to modify the algorithm to incorporate other non-zero values of hi,j as well. Admittedly, requirements of fixed-point operations, and issues related to numerical stability can cause limit the choices of hi,j. However, choices without these impediments do exist, e.g., even-valued hi,j.

In order to evaluate the performance of the IP algorithm, we conducted simulated experiments with various measurement matrices. As measurement matrices for our experiments, we considered LDPC parity-check matrices obtained by randomized construction techniques [14], [15]. Thus, each component of the input vector was involved in 3 measurements and each measurement summed 6 components of the input. V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we introduced an iterative algorithm for reconstructing sparse signals from undersampled measurements. We analyzed the performance of our algorithm over various measurement matrices and compared the performance of our algorithm to that of L1-minimization. From the analysis, it is evident that there is a good trade-off between performance and reduction in complexity. Finally, we provided a preliminary graph-theoretic analysis of our algorithm that established an upper bound on the reconstruction capability of our algorithm. Given the closeness of the nature of this result and those present in coding theory, it is hypothesized that further results with regard to guaranteed reconstruction may be possible by understanding the evolution of the algorithm in small subgraphs – an approach that has been used in the past to provide guarantees on error-correction in LDPC codes (c.f. [16], [17]).
