AbstractвҖ” In this paper we propose a computationallyefficient, iterative decoding algorithm that is well-suited for storage systems with very stringent reliability constraints and low redundancy/high code rate requirements. The proposed DualScaling Min-Sum (DS-MSA) overcomes certain deficiencies of the Min-Sum approximation when used for decoding graph-based codes. We observe that a small but non-negligible fraction of check-to-variable messages is underestimated by the Normalized Min-Sum algorithm in the low error rate region. By carefully adjusting the scaling factor for the variable-to-check message with the smallest magnitude, we develop the DS-MSA algorithm characterized by two scaling parameters. The proposed algorithm (1) outperforms Sum-Product and (Normalized) Min-Sum algorithms in the very low error rate regime, (2) maintains the low-complexity feature of the Min-Sum, and (3) can be easily combined with existing decoder implementations.
I. INTRODUCTION
Sparse codes defined on graphs, such as celebrated lowdensity parity-check (LDPC) codes [1], have been demonstrated to perform very close to the Shannon limit when decoded iteratively using message-passing algorithms. Such high-rate codes are particularly amenable for practical systems with very stringent reliability and redundancy constraints, as in storage applications. The original iterative decoding is performed using the sum-product algorithm (SPA). Due to the high implementation complexity of the SPA, Min-Sum algorithm (MSA) [2] was subsequently developed as a lowcomplexity approximation of SPA. However, the overestimation of check-to-variable messages leads to performance loss of MSA relative to SPA. Several correction methods were subsequently proposed in the literature to recover the performance loss of the Min-Sum decoding. The most popular correction methods are the Normalized Min-Sum (N-MSA) and the Offset Min-Sum algorithms (O-MSA) [3]. These algorithms share the idea that the performance loss can be recovered by reducing the magnitudes of the check-to-variable messages, which are often overestimated in the Min-Sum operation.
Several methods based on algorithms in [3] were subsequently developed to further improve the performance of the baseline MSA. Papers [4]-[6] propose algorithms that adapt scaling factors in N-MSA and the offset factors in O-MSA according to the degree distributions of variable and check nodes. Such degree-based algorithms naturally reduce to N-

MSA since for codes are regular and near-regular. Algorithms in [7]-[9] utilize the values of messages to determine the offset factors, scaling factors and nulling operations, respectively. Work in [10] investigates the effect of different quantization schemes on the MSA performance. In this paper we focus on regular and near-regular codes with high rates, since highrate, (almost) regular codes are of primary interest in highreliability storage applications.
While N-MSA successfully compensates for the overestimation in MSA for the majority of the messages, a small but nonnegligible fraction of messages are in fact negatively affected by the induced N-MSA scaling. We observe that this phenomenon is exacerbated in the low error rate region demanded by very reliable systems. For improving the performance of high-rate codes in very reliable systems, we discover that the edge that contributes the message with the smallest magnitude with respect to the neighboring check node, should in fact acquire a higher scaling factor than other edges.
By studying the ratio of magnitudes between the check node outputs of SPA and MSA, we propose a simple decoding algorithm called Dual-Scaling MSA (DS-MSA), which applies different scaling factors to different check-to-variable messages along the edges of the Tanner Graph [11] associated with the code: (a) one carefully chosen scaling factor is assigned to the check-to-variable message along the edge corresponding to the smallest-magnitude variable-to-check message, chosen among all messages sent to that check node, and (b) another (default) scaling factor is assigned to all other check-tovariable messages sent from that same check node. A nice feature of our computationally-efficient method is that it can be easily combined with the existing modifications of the MSA, including those from [3], [4], [5], [6], [7] and [10] for improved performance of very reliable systems.

In Section III-A we present simulation results of MSA, SPA, optimized NMSA, and proposed DS-MSA for several high-rate LDPC codes of practical interest. III. SIMULATION RESULTS

In this section we present Monte-Carlo floating point simulations of SPA, MSA, N-MSA, and DS-MSA with flooding schedule [12]. We set the simulation environment as the AWGN channel with the BPSK modulation. Optimizing over the parameter space at SNR = 5.2 dB, for the quasi-cyclic (QC) codes [14], we select 0.76 as Оұ in N-MSA and (0.76, 0.83) as the pair (Оұ1, Оұ2) in DS-MSA. We likewise select Оұ = 0.74 for the N-MSA, and Оұ1 = 0.74 and Оұ2 = 0.82 for the DSMSA for the progressive-edge-growth (PEG) codes [13]. The parameter values used in the simulations are chosen from the numerically obtained ranges of the parameters fij and are consistent with the choice of the scaling factor in [15]. The maximum number of iterations is 100.
A. Frame Error Rates and Error Floors

Fig. 2 illustrates the frame error rates (FER) at different SNR points for regular Quasi-Cyclic (QC) [14] (1369, 1221) code with degree (37, 4). As expected, the performance of SPA decoding is better than its low-complexity approximation given by the baseline MSA. Most of the error events of the SPA are a consequence of the message overflow/underflow. Note that this is an inherent property of the algorithm: no matter where the saturation and truncation points are set for the function ОҰ, amplitudes of the messages eventually reach the saturation point as the SNR and the number of iterations increase. This phenomenon effectively turns all hardware SPA implementations into hard decision decoding for very high SNR values.
The other two Min-Sum based algorithms, N-MSA and DSMSA, outperform SPA at all FER levels of 10вҲ’3 and below, while maintaining the low-complexity attribute of the baseline MSA. Their performance curves start separating around FER 10вҲ’6. The N-MSA curve starts leveling off and approaches the error floor regime, while the DS-MSA curve maintains steep slope without the error-floor. B. Convergence Speed
Fig. 4 shows the FER performance of different algorithms in terms of the allocated number of iterations at the representative value of SNR = 5.4 dB. As already shown in Figure 2, the FER of SPA is higher than the FER of N-MSA and DS-MSA. After about 35 iterations, DS-MSA becomes better than NMSA and continues to outperform N-MSA with the increased number of iterations. As shown in Fig. 4, the FER of DS-MSA keeps going down with the iteration number while the FER of N-MSA levels off. The initially wrong messages that have the larger scaling factor (Оұ2) delayed the decoding process in early iterations. By properly classifying and scaling c-tov messages, DS-MSA prevents certain N-MSA errors from converging to non-codeword states, with the cost of slightly delaying the decoding process of decodable error events.

In this paper we proposed an algorithmic modification of
the Min-Sum decoding algorithm. The main idea behind the
proposed dual-scaling scheme is to apply a larger scaling
factor along the edges that contribute the smallest-magnitude
message to the incident check node, in order to compensate for
these underestimated messages in the conventional Normalized
Min-Sum decoding. The proposed algorithm, while simple to
implement, provides extended waterfall region and improves
the performance. This idea can be seamlessly combined with
