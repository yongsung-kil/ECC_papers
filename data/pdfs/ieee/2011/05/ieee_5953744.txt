Decoding of Long-Block Soft Decision LDPC Codes for Optical Communication Systems
M. N. Sakib, A. J. Wong, W. J. Gross and O. Liboiron-Ladouceur Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, McGill University, Montreal, Quebec, Canada.
e-mail address: meer.sakib@mail.mcgill.ca

Abstract- We investigate a soft decision decoder for LDPC codes with long-block size. The results show a coding gain of 5.9 dB compared to an uncoded system and 1.5 dB over a hard decision receiver.
I. INTRODUCTION
Deploying advanced error-correction technologies in optical links is one of the most cost-effective methods to combat system impairments [1]. Current optical networks employ forward error-correction (FEC) based on classical errorcorrecting codes such as different variants of Reed-Solomon codes [2]. Since the rediscovery of capacity-approaching errorcorrecting codes in 1993, wireless communications has been realigned to use new powerful class of codes including turbo codes and low density parity check (LDPC) codes [3]. Following this new paradigm, LDPC codes have been proposed for optical communication systems to mitigate optical channel impairments in the next-generation optical communication systems [3]. In an effort to standardize FEC for high bit-rate dense wavelength division multiplexing (DWDM) submarine systems the International Telecommunication Union (ITU) recently proposed two enhanced forward error correction codes [2], 1) concatenated Bose-Chaudhuri Hocquenghem (BCH) super FEC code, BCH(3860,3824) + BCH(2040,1930) and 2) low density parity check LDPC(32643,30952). Concatenated BCH super FEC was extensively studied by V. Gol, et al. [4] and they showed that the code requires a fairly complex decoder structure challenging their practical implementation. The LDPC(32643,30952) as defined in ITU G975.1 is a systematic binary LDPC code of length 32643, with 30592 information-carrying bits, identical to the length and dimension of the standard interleaved RS(255,239) code and deployable within the current optical transport unit (OTU) frame structures. Although LDPC codes can be very powerful in theory, their practical implementation for optical communications links at ultra-high data rates remains a challenge since the decoding of the code requires a complex structure and a large silicon area for an integrated on-chip product. For low-complexity high-throughput hardware implementation a partially parallel decoder structure is desirable. Recently, offset min-sum (OMS) emerged as an promising solution for decoding long-block LDPC codes due to its modular architecture with simple processing modules [5].
In this paper, we, for the first time to our knowledge present both simulation and experimental results for the 2-bit soft

decision decoding of an LDPC(32643,30952) code using low complexity OMS iterative decoder. Results are presented in terms of post-FEC bit error rate (BER) versus pre-FEC Qfactor. A 40 Gb/s non return to zero (NRZ) on-off keyed (OOK) optical system consisting of 60 km of single-mode fiber (SMF-28) with chromatic dispersion compensation is used to experimentally correlate the simulation results.
II. DECODING OF LDPC CODES
Long-block length LDPC codes perform very close to the Shannon limit under iterative decoding algorithms [6]. The sum-product algorithm (SPA), also known as belief propagation, and min-sum (MS) algorithms are the best known iterative decoding algorithms [5]. While SPA generally shows the best decoding performance, it requires functions such as tanh and tanh-1 which results in hardware complexity. The MS algorithm is an approximation that replaces those complex functions for a simpler hardware implementation at the cost of a slight penalty in decoding performance compared to SPA. The OMS is a variant of MS algorithm that employs a slight modification to compensate for the inherent overestimation problem of the MS algorithm. A correction factor is applied to the original check node equation resulting in a performance very close to that of the SPA; however, with a much reduced hardware complexity for a possible FPGA implementation.

III. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
The considered system setup for the performance evaluation of LDPC(32643,30592) is shown in Fig. 2. The tunable multichannel WDM DFB laser source emits continuous wave (CW) light at wavelengths of 1536.61, 1537.4, 1538.19 nm. The CW light is then injected into a 40 Gb/s Mach-Zehnder modulator (MZM) to generate NRZ OOK signal, driven by the baseband signal from the output of the programmable pulse pattern generator (PPG). The average total optical power after modulation before being launched into the fiber is -4.71 dBm or -9.5 dBm per channel (point C in Fig. 2). The modulated NRZ-OOK optical channels are first sent through 32.6 km of single mode fiber (SMF). After the fiber transmission, the signal is optically amplified by an erbium doped fiber amplifier (EDFA) with noise figure (NF) of 5 dB. This EDFA roughly compensates all the fiber and connector losses for 32.6 km and the output power at point E in Fig. 2 is -0.3 dBm. The optical signal after the first EDFA is then transmitted through 24.6 km of SMF and a spool of dispersion compensating fiber (DCF). The DCF has a chromatic dispersion of -1035 ps/nm and is designed to perform dispersion compensation for 60 km of SMF–28. The total length of optical fiber we have in our setup is 57.2 km. After the DCF (point F in Fig. 2), the optical signal is amplified by another EDFA (NF: 5 dB) to compensate for

the losses incurred in the fiber and DCF. Then a tunable grating filter with bandwidth of 0.55 nm and insertion loss of 6 dB is used to filter out the middle optical channel (centered at 1357.4 nm) and remove out of band amplified spontaneous emission (ASE) noise. The frames are captured using an 80 GHz time domain sampling scope, Agilent DCA 86100J which captures 4 samples per bit. In total, the number of captured bits was close to the order of 108 bits. Capturing more frames is not currently feasible due to the requirement of a very large disk space and long record times. After capturing the frames the frames were first processed using a MATLAB program which we developed for interfacing purposes. From the samples of the signal we decode the signals by soft decision algorithm for decoding LDPC codes. The results show that for Q values of 4 and 9 dB the output BER was 0.04 and 0.0007, respectively. There is a discrepancy between the simulated and experimental results due to the fact that at low error rates, we had a few long burst of errors which degrades the overall performance. The Q values described are very close to the start the waterfall region of the decoder and performance is expected to improve beyond these points. For any Q values slightly above the waterfall region, i.e. 9 and 10 dB the decoded signal is error free (<10-8). Our work can be extended to any long-block length LDPC code that is well suited for high speed optical communications because of their high code rate, large minimum distances and simpler architecture.
IV. CONCLUSION
We have investigated a decoder for decoding the LDPC codes with large block size. The decoder exploits all the advantages of a soft decision receiver with low receiver complexity. The simulated and experimental results show that this decoder can be effectively used with a 40 Gb/s optical system with a coding gain of ~5.9 and 1.5 dB compared to the uncoded system and hard decision based receiver, respectively. The decoding scheme also exhibits a 1.9 dB advantage over standard RS(255,239) based decoders.
