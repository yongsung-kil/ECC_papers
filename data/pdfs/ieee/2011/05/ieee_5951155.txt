Low Complexity Soft Decision Circuit for LDPC decoders
M. N. Sakib, V. Mahalingam, A. J. Wong, W. J. Gross and O. Liboiron-Ladouceur
Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, McGill University, Montreal, Quebec, Canada. e-mail address: meer.sakib@mail.mcgill.ca
Abstract: A reduced complexity implementation is proposed of an optical receiver for the soft decoding of low density parity check codes. Coding gain of 9.4 dB is achieved using 20 % of the incoming optical signal.

1. Introduction
With the rapid growth of data-centric services and the general deployment of broadband access networks, there has been a strong demand driving the dense wavelength division multiplexing (DWDM) network upgrade from 10 Gb/s per channel to more spectrally efficient 40 Gb/s or 100 Gb/s per channel, and beyond. Migrating to higher transmission rates comes along with numerous challenges such as degradation of the signal quality due to different linear and nonlinear channel impairments and increased installation costs. Deploying advanced error-correction technologies in optical links is one of the most cost-effective methods to combat system impairments [1]. Current optical networks employ forward error-correction (FEC) based on classical error-correcting codes such as ReedSolomon codes [1]. In recent years, iteratively decodable codes, like low density parity check (LDPC) codes, have generated significant research attention [1,2]. It has been shown that soft-decision based LDPC exhibits significant coding gain over hard decision based conventional codes. However, most of the research activities related to LDPC algorithms for optical communication are focused on simulation studies with few demonstration of soft-decision circuits where the soft decision bits are provided by a complex custom designed 2 bit encoder with a power consumption of 14W [1]. In this paper, we present a low complexity soft decision circuit for experimental investigation and deployment of LDPC codes in next-generation optical transmission systems. The proposed optical receiver design can be implemented in deployed hard decision based optical systems by simply tapping the incoming optical signal prior to the photodetector.
2. Low Complexity Soft Decision Circuit
Conventional direct-detection receivers provide only one bit of information corresponding to a hard decision. The receiver consists of a photodiode followed by a transimpedance amplifier and a limiting amplifier. The limiting amplifier digitizes the analog signal. Most of the advanced error correction codes such as LDPC require soft decision decoding which involves more than one bit of information. The additional bits provide a confidence level of the digitized hard decision. In this paper, we propose to tap out some optical power from a conventional optical system and use it for determining the confidence levels in the LDPC decoder as shown in Fig. 1. The proposed scheme requires no modification of the current optical infrastructure. The efficiency of the system for different coupling ratios is assessed through the power penalty. The truth table for the decoder is given in the table in Fig. 1. The final confidence bit is implemented using an exclusive-nor (XNOR) of the confidence levels VTH1 and VTH2 which are set at 16 % above and 13 % below the hard-decision level with respect to the peak to peak input voltage. The ratio of the upper to lower threshold is approximately 1.3 and optimized for minimum error.
The soft decision circuit is implemented for operation at 12.5 Gb/s. The signal from the optical system is optically split between the soft decision and hard decision segments. The main building blocks include RF amplifiers, an 1:2 fan-out buffer (HMC744), two high speed comparators (ADCMP572), and one XNOR (HMC745). The hard decision and soft decision are combined using a high speed 1:2 multiplexer (HMC748). The delay between the hard and soft decision segments is compensated and data is captured using an 80 GHz sampling oscilloscope (DCA 86100J). The overall power consumption of the soft decision circuit is 5.79 W where 62 % of the power is consumed by the buffer and amplifiers. For performance analysis, the LDPC has a block length of 29,759 with 7 % overhead for a block length of 32,000 [3,4]. We choose this code for several reasons: i) long block-length LDPC codes perform very close to the Shannon limit under iterative decoding algorithms [3]; ii) the code has very low error floor [3,4] and iii) the code length is similar to the LDPC codes proposed in the ITU recommendation while the 7 % is the same as currently used Reed-Salomon RS(255, 239) codes [5]. The LDPC code is decoded by a sum-product algorithm (SPA) with 32,000 degree-4 variable nodes and 2241 check nodes with 58 degrees. The number of iterations is set to 50.

3. Experimental Results

The considered system setup for the performance evaluation of the LDPC(32000, 29759) optical transmission system consists of 60 km of single-mode fiber (SMF-28) with dispersion compensating fiber based chromatic dispersion compensation. Three channels at 1538.98, 1538.19 and 1537.4 nm are used and the performance is evaluated for the middle channel. The LDPC code is loaded onto a programmable pulse pattern generator and used to modulate a Mach-Zehnder modulator at 12.5 Gb/s (NRZ-OOK). The frames are captured after transmission using a high speed oscilloscope with 4 samples per bit. In total, the number of captured bits is in the order of 107. Capturing more frames is not currently feasible due to large disk space requirement and long record times. After capturing the frames, they are processed in MATLAB using soft decision algorithm for decoding LDPC codes [3]. Fig 2 shows the simulated and experimental results for the decoder. Post-FEC bit error rate (BER) is studied as a function of Q-factor. To investigate the performance of the alternate approach and possible tradeoffs, the performance is compared to the electrical fan-out case where a 1:3 electrical fan-out is used.

Results show that if 20% of the optical power is coupled for soft decision, there is a degradation of 0.35 dB in the Q-factor with respect to the electrical fan-out case. When compared to the ideal unquantized case this penalty is approximately 0.85 dB. Fig. 3 shows the optical power penalty for different coupling ratio for error free transmission (BER < 10-7). The minimum received optical power for error free transmission is first measured when the photodetected signal is electrically fanned out to the soft and hard decision segments. We then observe that for 20% coupling ratio the penalty is about 0.9 dB. The Q-factor degradation increases to 0.54 dB if 10% of the optical power is used. Hence as expected for 10% coupling ratio the penalty is also higher, i.e. 1.6 dB. The performance degrades due to low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) for the soft decision bit and peak power below the sensitivity of the photodetector. For 30% coupling ratio and above the performance degrades due to lower performance of the hard decision receiver. For a coupling ratio of 40% and 50% the optical power penalty reaches about 3.1 and 4.2 dB, respectively. Hence, a coupling ratio of 20% is optimum for our proposed architecture which is beneficial as it reuses the hard decision circuitry.

The coding gain achieved by extrapolation in Fig. 2, are 6.73 dB and 9.4 dB for post-FEC BER of 10-9 and 1015, respectively. In addition, the coding gain over hard decision is 0.82 dB. This simple approach which is implementable with conventional direct detection receivers is cost effective and power efficient.

4. Conclusion

We have investigated a low complexity 12.5 Gb/s soft-decision circuit for decoding LDPC codes that reuses conventional hard-decision receivers. The simulated and experimental results show that the proposed optical receiver exhibits a coding gain of approximately 9.4 dB and 0.82 dB over an uncoded system and hard decision receiver, respectively, for an optimum coupling ratio of 20% at a post-FEC BER of 10-15.
