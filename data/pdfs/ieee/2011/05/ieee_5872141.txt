Abstract—We examine various algebraic/combinatorial properties of Low-Density Parity-Check codes as predictors for the performance of the sum-product algorithm on the AWGN channel in the error floor region. We consider three families of check matrices, two algebraically constructed and one sampled from an ensemble, expurgated to remove short cycles. The three families have similar properties, all are (3, 6)-regular, have girth 8, and have code length roughly 280. The best predictors are small trapping sets, and the predictive value is much higher for the algebraically constructed families than the random ones.
I. INTRODUCTION
As is well known, the performance of the Sum-Product Algorithm (SPA), measured in either bit-error rate or frame-error rate as a function of the signal-to-noise ratio, tends to have two regions: a “waterfall” portion, where the curve descends ever more steeply until it reaches the second region, the “error floor,” where the curve flattens out considerably. Obtaining an accurate estimate of the error floor using simulation is costly, since decoding failure is so rare; therefore, there has been a great deal of effort to identify properties of the check matrix that are associated with decoding failure. This article compares algebraic and combinatorial characteristics of the check matrix to see how well each serves as a predictor for performance.
II. COMBINATORIAL PROPERTIES USED AS PREDICTORS
We consider binary codes defined by a check matrix H. The bipartite graph of H has a check node for each row r of H and a bit node for each column of H, with an edge between r and when Hr, = 1.
An early observation was that short cycles in the bipartite graph of the parity-check matrix are undesirable since inaccurate received values on such a cycle are self-reinforcing [14]. Therefore, it is considered desirable that the bipartite graph have large girth. Our earlier results [9] showed that the girth was certainly not a definitive indicator of decoding performance; matrices constructed using the same algebraic method and with the same girth could have performance differing by orders of magnitude, while a code of girth 6 could outperform one of girth 8. It is still possible that the number of small cycles might be a determining factor for the error rate. In our study, all graphs have girth 8, so one predictor variable we consider is the number of 8-cycles in the graph.

Good expansion properties are also considered desirable

(see, e.g., the survey of expander graphs [5] and asymptotic

results of Burshtein and Miller [1]). In contrast with the

argument that short cycles are disadvantageous, the point here

is that the SPA on a good expander will efficiently propagate

correct information about bit values throughout the graph.

One well studied code is the Margulis construction of a good

expander [7], but it was shown to have a high error floor [12],

so again, expansion is not definitive.

There is a close relationship between the expansion coef-

ficient of a graph and the spectral gap, the difference of the

moduli of the two largest eigenvalues of the adjacency matrix

for the graph (see e.g. [5]). We use predictor variables derived

from two different matrices. The first is the adjacency matrix

For a bipartite graph, there are two eigenvalues of largest

magnitude, one positive and one negative, so the predictor

variable we use is the difference between the two largest

(distinct) moduli, which we call the Tanner gap.

The other matrix is more directly related to the message

passing of the SPA. Consider each edge of the bipartite graph

as consisting of two edges, one running in each direction,

and form the edge-edge adjacency matrix for this graph. Now

modify this matrix to remove the adjacency between each edge

and its reverse, to reflect the fact that the algorithm takes into

account all incident edges besides the edge itself in calculating

the new edge message; call the resulting matrix the flow matrix

for the graph. The flow matrix also has one positive and one

negative eigenvalue of maximum modulus. The next largest

eigenvalue is not necessarily real, since the flow matrix is not

symmetric. We use the difference of the two largest distinct

moduli in the flow matrix, which we call the flow gap, as

another predictor.

Trapping sets for the Gallager A algorithm were identified

as a cause of the error floor in SPA decoding by Richardson

[10] who used them to predict error performance of the SPA.

Subsequent work used methods for eliminating small trapping

sets to lower the error floor, [4], [6]. Analysis of trapping sets

led to accurate prediction of decoding performance of LDPC

codes on the binary symmetric channel using the Gallager A

algorithm [2]. Formally, an (a, b) trapping set is the subgraph

III. THE PARITY-CHECK MATRICES

In order to keep our regression analysis focused, we experi-

mented with three families of (3, 6)-regular graphs, described

in detail below, each with girth 8 and rate approximately 0.5.

Random (3, 6)-regular graphs with 282 bits were con-

structed by sampling from the (3, 6)-regular ensemble, in the

language of [11], with some “expurgation” rules. Specifically,

the algorithm assigned 3 edges to each bit and randomly chose

a check to connect to each edge. However, if acceptance of the

randomly chosen check would lead to the formation of a 4- or

6-cycle, the choice was rejected and blacklisted, and another

random choice was re-attempted up to a maximum number of

tries; if this maximum was reached the graph was abandoned

and the process re-started.

A standard algebraic method for constructing LDPC codes

is to use a block circulant matrix such as

Using methods described in [8] (see also [3]) we developed an algorithm, implemented in Magma, for choosing the aij sequentially (where i, j precedes i , j if j < j or j = j and i < i ) to produce graphs with no cycles of length 6. For each i, j a list, B, of values that would lead to short cycles is created, then aij is chosen to be an element of {0, . . . , p − 1} \ B. Interestingly, if the minimum acceptable value for each aij is always chosen, the resulting parity check matrix performs very poorly under the SPA. We therefore used random choices (outside of the set B that yields small cycles) for each aij. The best of these matrices yields performance better than randomly generated matrices. Alternating between the minimum choice and a random choice sometimes produced good decoding performance, and sometimes poor performance.

For this paper we analyzed 74 random graphs, 21 Fano cover graphs and 107 K3,6 graphs.

The inflection points for these graphs, marking the transition from the waterfall region to the error floor, occur at roughly SNR 3.5. In the following section, we examine the power of the various graphtheoretic properties described in Section II to predict decoding performance, that is log(FER), at SNR 4.5. We chose an SNR of 4.5 for these experiments, since this value is well into the error-floor region, but the FER is not so low that running simulations of several codes becomes impractical.
The FER was calculated based on simulations over the AWGN channel that were run as follows. For an individual frame, the algorithm terminated if all update log-likelihoods were either less than 10−3 or larger than 1000; otherwise the algorithm stopped after 500 iterations. Each simulation for the random graphs used 100 million total frames (that resulted in approximately 2000 frames with errors). Each simulation for a Fano-cover graph ran until there were 500 frames with errors. Each simulation for a K3,6-cover graph ran to a target of 500 frames with errors or 10 million total frames, whichever came first. Confidence intervals for all data points are consequently within 10% of the measured value, and therefore smaller than the symbol sizes in the plots below. The simulations were run on a 4-CPU Dual-core AMD Opteron 2.8 GHz machine running Linux kernel 2.6.18. The code was custom-written by us in the C programming language, and the random number generator used was drand48().
Throughout, we will be using the standard Pearson correlation coefficient r, and its square, as the primary measure of predictive power. To compare ordinary single-variable regressions to multiple regressions with differing numbers of variables, we use the “adjusted r2” statistic r¯2 = 1 − (1 − r2)(n − 1)/(n − 1 − v), where n is the sample size and v is the number of predictor variables. In order to test whether

the difference in the mean value of a variable in two data sets is statistically significant, we will use a Welch’s T-test (to account for the fact that the variances of these variables tend to differ substantially) with significance level α = 0.05, and report the p-value.
V. RESULTS
A. Spectral gaps
Since the adjacency and flow matrices are both reducible, being constructed from adjacency of bipartite graphs, their top two eigenvalues are equal in magnitude. It is therefore natural to study the difference between the magnitudes of the top eigenvalue and the third largest in each case.
For those K3,6-cover graphs having Tanner gap less than 0.5, the third eigenvalue e3 of the flow matrix is real; moreover, the flow gap seems to be in perfect correlation (r = 0.99) with the Tanner gap. For the K3,6-cover graphs with Tanner gap larger than 0.5, the flow gap is constant, while e3 is pure imaginary and equal in magnitude to the third eigenvalue of the adjacency matrix. The distinction between real and imaginary e3 maps to performance: K3,6-cover graphs with a real e3 have, on average, approximately an order of magnitude higher FER than graphs with imaginary e3 (p ≈ 7 × 10−11).
The Tanner and flow gaps are uncorrelated for the random family (r = 0.04) and moderately correlated for the Fano family (r = 0.57). e3 is complex or pure imaginary for all the random and Fano family members, except for the Fano graph created using the minimum choice of parameters. The fact that this is the worst performing Fano graph (see Fig. 2) supports the conclusion that real e3 is correlated with poor decoding performance. Aside from the condition of e3 being real or not, the Tanner gap and the flow gap had relatively weak predictive value (see Table I).
B. Low-Weight Codewords
Of the 202 codes that we consider, only 5 have (6, 0) trapping sets and only 11 have (8, 0) trapping sets (5 of the 11 are the codes with (6, 0) trapping sets). All of the 11 are K3,6-covers, and all but one have real e3. The 11 worst performing codes are those with weight-8 codewords, and the codes with weight 6 codewords have log(FER) > −2.7, while all other graphs have log(FER) < −3.2. Consequently, we censored the 11 codes with low-weight codewords (weight6 and weight-8) from correlation computations. The data for these codes are shown in the plots so that they may be compared to other codes.

(5, 3) trapping sets is a better regressor across families (all) and subfamilies (K3,6) than it is within the random family, the Fano family or the two K3,6 subfamilies.
(6, 2) trapping sets occur in 130 of our 202 graphs: all the random graphs, half the Fano graphs, and slightly over a third of the K3,6-cover graphs. VI. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
In summary, for the codes tested, we found: (1) Expander properties, as measured by two different spectral gaps, provide only weak predictive value in general, but within the K3,6 covers a real e3 had a strongly negative influence on decoding performance; (2) The count of 8-cycles provides only mild predictive value; (3) Codewords of weight 6 and 8 (corresponding to (6, 0) and (8, 0) trapping sets) produce outliers, with weight 6 yielding extremely poor performance; (4) The (6, 2) trapping set was the most consistent predictor for individual families, while the (6, 4) and (5, 3) trapping sets were good predictors across but not within families or
