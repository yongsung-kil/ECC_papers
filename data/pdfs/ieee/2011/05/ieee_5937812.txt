A High-Throughput LDPC Decoder Architecture for High-Rate WPAN Systems

Kyung-Il Baek, Hanho Lee and Chang-Seok Choi
Department of Information & Communication Engineering Inha University, Incheon, 402-751, Korea hhlee@inha.ac.kr

Department of Electrical & Computer Engineering University of Minnesota, Minneapolis, MN 55455, USA

Abstract‚ÄĒ This paper presents a high-throughput memoryefficient decoder architecture for Quasi-Cyclic Low-Density Parity-Check (QC-LDPC) codes in the high-rate wireless personal area network applications. Two novel techniques which can apply to our selected QC-LDPC codes are proposed, including four-parallel block layered decoding architecture and simplification of the switch networks. The proposed architecture based on a block parallel decoding scheme replaces a crossbarbased interconnect network with a fixed wire network for a switch network. In addition, two-stage pipelining is used to improve the clock speed. A 672-bit, rate-1/2 LDPC decoder is implemented using 90 nm CMOS technology. The design achieves an information throughput of 1.45 Gbps at a clock speed of 285 MHz with a maximum of 16 iterations.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low density parity check (LDPC) codes, first proposed by Gallager in 1962 have attracted much attention because of their excellent error correcting performance, inherently parallelism and high throughput potentials. Therefore, they are being widely used in communication standards such as DVB-S2, IEEE 802.16e and IEEE 802.11n. In addition, millimeter wave (mmWave) Wireless Personal Area Networks (WPANs) described by the IEEE 802.15.3c Working Group [1] are considering LDPC codes as the preferred choice for forward error correction (FEC).
Recently, many studies have been accomplished to simplify the VLSI implementation of the related decoders called ‚ÄúArchitecture-Aware LDPC codes‚ÄĚ or ‚ÄúBlock-LDPC codes‚ÄĚ. Based on these design approaches, quasi-cyclic LDPC (QCLDPC) codes have received significant attentions due to their efficient hardware implementations. Furthermore, QC-LDPC codes can provide comparable error-correction performance compared with random LDPC codes.
With the increasing demand for high-data-rate wireless application, an overlapped decoding architecture and a layered decoding architecture are popular for high-throughput LDPC decoding architectures using QC-LDPC codes. An overlapped decoding architecture based on the partially parallel architectures using the belief-propagation (BP) algorithm, where the message updating computations in the check node unit (CNU) and in the variable node unit (VNU) are partially

overlapped, can increase the decoding throughput by maximizing the hardware utilization efficiency without any performance degradation.
Compared with the overlapped two-phase decoding scheme, a layered decoding algorithm and architecture have been proposed to achieve a faster convergence [2]. Generally, layered decoding algorithm is the horizontal layered decoding algorithm which is favorable for the Min-Sum algorithm. In [2], the layered decoding algorithm offers 2x throughput and significant memory advantages, compared with the BP algorithm. Also, it can reduce the average number of iteration using intermediate check-node (or variable-node) message values. However, conventional layered decoders use a bidirectional network or two switch networks for shuffling and reshuffling messages, which increases the hardware complexity. Also, due to the data dependency between consecutive rows in layered decoding, the parallel and pipelining techniques cannot be applied directly. In this paper, we propose a four-parallel block layered decoding architecture (4-PBLDA) for a 672-bit, rate-1/2 QC-LDPC code in IEEE 802.15.3c standard. 4PBLDA enables to process concurrently four block rows of H matrix and replace all of crossbar-based interconnect networks with fixed wire networks. Furthermore, two-stage pipelining technique is employed to achieve high clock speed and high data throughput.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. Section II describes overview of QC-LDPC codes for high-rate WPAN applications. Section III describes the proposed four-parallel QC-LDPC decoder architecture and parallel block layered decoding scheme. In Section IV, the implementation and comparison are presented. Then, conclusions are presented in Section V.

III. PROPOSED FOUR-PARALLEL QC-LDPC DECODER ARCHITECTURE.
A. Four-parallel block layered decoding algorithm
The QC-LDPC codes which are composed of sub-matrices enable to implement block parallel layered decoding architecture. In the QC-LDPC codes, consecutive rows in a block row of H matrix (a layer) operate independently because the column weight of each sub-matrix by which a block row of H matrix is constructed is at most 1. In other words, instead of one row, one block row which is composed of z rows can be updated simultaneously at 1 clock cycle.
The decoding process for a 672-bit, rate-1/2 QC-LDPC code is shown in Fig. 1. A CNBPs which consists of z check node-based processor (CNBP) [3] performs computation of a block row and each CNBP performs computation of a row. Fortunately, for the H matrix of our selected LDPC code, the column weight of four block rows is at most 1 without reordering of the H matrix. Thus, four block rows can be computed simultaneously by four CNBPs at 1 clock cycle.
In this way, we propose four-parallel block layered decoding architecture (4-PBLDA). 4-PBLDA which consists of four CNBPs is proposed for the proper trade-off between decoding throughput and hardware complexity. Also, 4PBLDA has the several following advantages. First, 4PBLDA could dramatically reduce the number of clock cycles required for the iteration by 75%, compared with a block layered decoding architecture [4]. Consequently, 4-PBLDA can increase the throughput approximately 4x. Second, 4PBLDA can increase hardware efficiency because four block

rows of H matrix share a CNBPs and four CNBPs share a memory. Third, crossbar-based interconnect networks are replaced with fixed wire networks, resulting in low routing complexity of switch network (SN).
The dataflow of 4-PBLDA is shown in Fig. 2, where k = {1, 2,‚Ä¶, Z}, l = {1, 5, 9, 13}, yl[k] rotated by SN1 denotes the variable message in variable node v‚Äô at the k-th iteration, Rl[k1] denotes the C2V message conveyed from the check node c of l-th layer to variable node v‚Äô at the k-th iteration and Ll[k] denotes the V2C message which has entered the decoding update block (DUB). The DUB designed with the Min-Sum algorithm (also called ‚ÄúMin-Sum block‚ÄĚ) needs long computation time because of the tree structure of comparison operations. Overall decoding procedure is performed from the top to the bottom and the proposed 4-PBLDA mainly consists of memory block for storing message, CNBPs for processing intermediate message, SNs for routing message, Bit updating register array for storing intermediate message, a parity check module and a decoder control module.
B. Low routing complexity of switch networks
By increasing parallelism of H matrix, the main issue in hardware implementation is how to reduce routing complexity. A conventional layered decoding architecture needs two SNs for shuffling and reshuffling messages. Especially, the proposed 4-PBLDA needs 12 SNs with pipelining technique. If all of the SNs in the proposed 4PBLDA are implemented as the crossbar-based interconnect network [4], the hardware and routing complexity are very high.
As illustrated in Fig. 1, lst CNBPs performs {l, l+4, l+8, l+12} block rows of H matrix which have only same order and same permutation number. For example, 1st CNBPs performs {1, 5, 9, 13} block rows of H matrix which have only same order and same permutation number {5, 18, 3, 10, 5, 4, 5, 7}. A SN consists of eight sub-switch networks in which a sub-switch network is used for switching z messages in a sub-matrix. Since 1st CNBPs of each group shares same order and same permutation number as shown in Fig. 1, the first sub-switch network in the SN1 of 1st CNBPs always performs 5 right cyclic shifts to the z messages. For this reason, all of the SNs for 4-PBLDA can be replaced with fixed wire networks. As a result, the proposed 4-PBLDA not only

For the layered decoding algorithm, the computations of CNBPs shown in Fig. 2 are sequentially performed and the newly updated variable messages fed into the bit updating register array are used immediately to the next layer. If pipelining technique is used among three consecutive computations without an effective method, the next layer waits for the number of pipeline until the newly updated variable messages is passed to. To solve this problem, the approximate layered decoding scheme for two-stage pipelining is proposed in [5]. IV. IMPLEMENTATION RESULTS
The proposed 4-PBLDA was modeled in Verilog HDL and simulated to verify their functionality using a test pattern generated from a C simulator. After complete verification of the design functionality, it was synthesized using appropriate time and area constraints. Both simulation and synthesis steps were carried out using a SYNOPSYS design tool and 90-nm CMOS technology optimized for a 1.08 V supply voltage. Based on the simulated performance results of Fig. 4, we use a (q, f) = (6, 2) quantization scheme, where q and f are the total bit size of the intrinsic message and the number of fractional bits, respectively. 7 bits are used for representing the values of the total bit size of the extrinsic message and 9 bits are used in CNBPs block.
Table I shows performance comparison results between 4PBLDA with and without pipelining. The proposed 4-PBLDA with pipelining operates at a maximum frequency of 285 MHz. The information decoding throughput is estimated to be approximately (285MHz) √ó 336 / (4 √ó 16 + 2) = 1.45 Gbps using a maximum of 16 decoding iterations and the pipeline latency of 2 clock cycles. For 4-PBLDA without pipelining, the maximum clock frequency is 120 MHz and the information decoding throughput is also estimated to be approximately (120MHz) √ó 336 / (4 √ó 8) = 1.26 Gbps only using a maximum of 8 decoding iterations. Thus, the data throughput of proposed 4-PBLDA with two-state pipelining is about 13% higher than that of 4-PBLDA without pipelining. However, it has only 24% increased gate counts compared to 4-PBLDA without pipelining.

V. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we have proposed four-parallel block layered decoding architecture for high-rate WPAN applications. The proposed 4-PBLDA with two-stage pipelining enables high throughput and very low decoding latency. The routing complexity was reduced significantly by replacing a crossbarbased interconnect network with a fixed wire network for SN. Hence, the proposed decoder architecture has high throughput, low interconnect complexity and very low decoding latency. The proposed architecture is expected to be incorporated in next-generation high-rate WPAN applications.
