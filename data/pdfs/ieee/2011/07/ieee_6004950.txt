I. Tsatsaragkos, N. Kanistras and V. Paliouras ∗
University of Patras Electrical and Computer Engineering Department
26500 Patras, Greece

ABSTRACT
This work improves the performance of LDPC decoders that implement iterative algorithms dominated by oscillatory behavior - such as offset Min-Sum algorithm - in cases of unsuccessful decoding of received words. The proposed LDPC decoder is applied on the decoding procedure of an LDPC algorithm by selecting the one of the N different estimated codewords (each produced at an iteration of the decoder) that corresponds to the syndrome with the minimum number of unsatisfied check constraints, as the output of the decoder. This method provides a decoder that achieves a notable performance improvement in the error floor region of operation for the offset Min-Sum decoding algorithm, with an insignificant increase of the hardware complexity.
Index Terms— LDPC decoding, message-passing, oscillation, syndrome, error-floor, low BER.
1. INTRODUCTION Low-density parity-check (LDPC) were originally invented by Gallager in 1960s [1] but left aside for over thirty years, until late 1990s when MacKay and Neal rediscovered them [2]. Due to their excellent error correction performance, that approaches the Shannon limit [3], LDPC codes have been recently included in many industrial standards for next generation communication systems, such as Digital Video Broadcast (DVB) DVB-S2, IEEE 802.3an (10GBASE-T), IEEE 802.16e (WiMAX) and IEEE 802.11n (WiFi). An LDPC code is a linear block code characterized by a sparse parity check matrix H. Each column in the matrix H corresponds to a bit within the codeword, while each row corresponds to a parity-check equation. Every valid codeword u satisfies all the check equations of the code, which is mathematically expressed by the equation u ⊗ HT = 0, where ⊗ indicates the modulo-2 addition. Each block code, including LDPC codes, can be fully described by a bipartite graph, called Tanner graph. Many algorithms have been developed for the decoding of LDPC codes. The most popular of them belongs to the category of message-passing decoding algorithms, where decoding information is iteratively exchanged between the two sets of decoder’s processing elements, called variable and check nodes [1]. This procedure is also called Belief-Propagation (BP). Updating the nodes can be done with a fully parallel, two phased iterative scheduling scheme or by processing the rows of the parity check matrix in groups, or layers, in successive order (layered decoding) [4].
The main target of the current research effort is to improve the decoding performance of LDPC codes. Efficient decoding algorithms and new code construction methods are studied for this purpose. The error correction performance of an LDPC decoder is commonly quantified by the bit-error rate (BER) and is strongly affected by the decoding algorithm. There is a variety of

decoding algorithms, which can be grouped into the following main categories, the Bit-Flipping (BF) and the Message-Passing (MP), or Belief-Propagation (BP), algorithms. Some of the MP algorithms are the Sum-Product algorithm (SP) [5], its logarithmic version, called Log-SP algorithm [6], and the Min-Sum algorithm (MS) [7]. SP and Log-SP algorithms exhibit a good decoding performance, but require high hardware complexity. The hardware complexity is reduced by Min-Sum algorithm, but at the cost of performance degradation. The sub-optimality of the Min-Sum algorithm in performance comes from the approximation errors accumulated at check nodes. The introduction of a normalization, or offset, operation at check nodes, leads to normalized (NMS) [8] or offset Min-Sum (OMS) algorithm [9], respectively, which compensate the approximation errors of Min-Sum algorithm and improve significantly its performance, with negligible increment of the hardware complexity.
A drawback of LDPC codes, is that they suffer by the error floor, a region in the BER vs. noise (SNR) level plot, where BER reduction slows down as the noise level decreases [10]. The error floor behavior is commonly attributed to the sub-optimality of iterative decoding algorithms on graphs with cycles, which become trapped to local minimum solutions, such as the trapping sets and absorbing sets, and may not reach the correct solution, i.e., a valid codeword. The decoder enters a non-codeword steady state from which it cannot escape [11]. A particular algorithm may succeed to avoid particular trapping sets, while another fails to reach the correct solution. Trapping of the decoder depends, among other factors, on the particular noise to which data were exposed, the properties of the LDPC code, numerical issues in the decoder implementation and the decoding algorithm. Cavus et al. [12] combine a graph search technique with importance sampling, introducing a method for performance evaluation of LDPC codes at very low BER. In order to improve the error floor performance, Zhang et al. [13] propose a post-processing technique, which reduces the reliability of the messages from falsely satisfied checks and increases the reliability of the messages from the unsatisfied checks, so that the bits of an absorbing set can reverse the wrong values. Kang et al. propose a two-stage iterative decoding procedure, in order to eliminate some trapping sets [14]. Although this work obtains improved BER results, it adds a moderate hardware complexity and decreases the decoding throughput. The impact of the quantization scheme is studied in [15] and an adaptive quantization approach, that mitigates the effects of weak absorbing sets and lowers the error floor, is proposed. Han et al. present three classes of decoders (bi-mode, bit-pinning, generalized-LDPC), each one of them introducing a different decoding strategy for lowering floors induced by trapping sets [16]. MacKay et al. study the concepts of near-codewords and low-weight codewords [17]. It is important to mention that the error floor is usually observed at low noise levels, i.e., high SNR regions, corresponding to BER values below 10−9. Software simulations in these regions are extremely time consuming. Often, the error floor is out of reach of the simulation performed. Therefore, there is a need of high speed hardware platforms, in order to take reliable BER measurements in the error floor region.

The belief propagation (BP) decoding procedure starts after the loading of the received, channel distorted, word. Each bit of the received word is usually represented as a log-likehood ratio (LLR), and referred to as intrinsic message. New messages are calculated during the exchange of information between the variable and check nodes, updating the values of the LLRs (posterior messages) at the variable nodes. At the end of the last iteration of the decoding algorithm a hard decision upon each LLR value gives the corresponding decoded code symbol. There are three main cases of BP decoding behavior in terms of the evolution of the posterior values at the variable nodes [18]. In the first case, called convergence, the average magnitude of posterior LLR values increases at each iteration and the decoder converges to a valid codeword after a number of iterations. In the second case, called stuck, the average magnitude of the LLR values remains stuck to a fixed value, after a number of iterations, and the decoding process does not terminate successfully. In the last case, referred to as oscillation, the average magnitude fluctuates between a maximum and minimum value. In some cases, the decoding behavior changes from oscillation to convergence, otherwise the decoder fails to converge to a valid codeword. Gounai et al. [19] propose two oscillation-based decoding algorithms, improving the decoding errors caused by oscillations in the LLR-BP and the OSD decoding algorithms, while exploitation of oscillations to avoid wasteful iterations and save energy consumption was proposed by Glikiotis and Paliouras [20].
Several authors have proposed stopping criteria, based on the monitoring of syndrome vectors and the number of satisfied parity-check constraints. These approaches target to the decoding speed-up and saving of power consumption, reducing the average number of decoding iterations without, or with a small, loss of BER performance. Shin et al. propose a stopping criterion to predict decoding failure of LDPC codes, before reaching the maximum number of iterations, using as measure the number of satisfied check constraints, improving the efficiency of type-II ARQ protocol [18]. Amador et al. [21] propose an iteration control policy that is driven by the combination of two decision metrics - the number of satisfied parity-check constraints and the number of erased messages of the Self-Corrected Min-Sum decoding algorithm. Chen et al. [22] use a syndrome accumulation vector, which verifies that all the information bits in the codeword are correct, stopping immediately the decoding process, even if there are still some errors in parity bits. By saving the unnecessary decoding iterations for correcting parity-bit errors, the proposed stopping criterion reduces the average number of decoding iterations without loss of BER performance.
The aforementioned works use monitoring of syndrome vectors, in order to increase decoding speed and energy efficiency, sometimes at the cost of BER performance degradation. In this paper, our goal is to improve the BER performance of classic MP LDPC decoding algorithms. Adjudeanu et al. [23] analyse the correlation between the error weights and the syndrome weights, for the Sum-Product algorithm only and without taking into consideration any hardware implementation related issues. Exploiting this correlation, we propose a Syndrome-Based LDPC Decoder, which uses the number of unsatisfied check constraints as a metric, in order to eliminate the degradation of the BER performance caused by oscillatory behavior. We extend our research to various MP LDPC decoding algorithms, for different values of characteristic parameters (e.g. the offset factor), especially in the error floor region. As the next sections show, the improvement of the BER performance leads to throughput increase, due to reduced number of decoding iterations. The bit-error performance of the proposed Syndrome-Based LDPC Decoder is evaluated, via FPGA prototyping, in comparison to the performance of simple decoder implementations without the use of the proposed metric. The use of the particular custom-build high-speed hardware tool allows the study to extend to BER values below 10−9, where flattening effects manifest themselves. To our knowledge, there are only a few works in the literature with experimental results in the error floor region, below 10−9 [15] [24].
While the proposed on this work decoding scheme reduces the decoding errors in case of unsuccessfully decoded codewords,

another decoding scheme, proposed by the authors in [25], targets to successful decoding of such codewords and results to greater decoding performance improvement, but at the cost of significant hardware complexity increase.
The remainder of the paper is organized as follows: In section 2 there is a description of the proposed Syndrome-Based Decoder. In section 3 experimental results are presented, while conclusions are discussed in section 4.

achieves very low BER, especially in the error floor region, that

is necessary for high performance next generation communication

systems, by exploiting the UC estimate in order to compensate

the oscillatory behavior. The proposed decoder uses the number of unsatisfied check constraints (U C), computed at each iteration,

as a metric, in order to achieve this goal. When the decoder

fails to converge after a maximum number of decoding iterations, it uses U C as a criterion to select among the various estimated

codewords derived at each of the executed iterations, the one that will be output by the decoder, expecting that this will be closer
to the valid transmitted codeword. In particular, assuming that the decoding algorithm stops after a maximum number of N iterations, the proposed decoder selects the one of the N different estimated

codewords (each derived by an iteration) that corresponds to the syndrome with the minimum value U C. We define codeword j

as the estimated codeword derived by the jth decoding iteration and U C j as the corresponding U C value. If no convergence is

achieved, the decoder returns as output the codeword k, where k is such that U C k = min(U C j, j=1, ...,N ).

Fig. 1(c) illustrates the decoding behavior of the Min-Sum layered decoding algorithm, for another codeword c2. Following an initial oscillation, after 36 iterations the algorithm converges to a valid
codeword. Fig. 1(d) shows the corresponding results for a third codeword c3, decoded by the normalized Min-Sum layered decoding algorithm, with normalization factor a = 0.75. This is the stuck case
mentioned previously. Three basic conclusions are derived from these case studies. The
first one is that sometimes there is a non-monotonic dependence between the number of bit errors and the number of iterations. For example, for the case of Fig. 1(b), the estimated codeword of the 4th iteration is closer to the valid codeword than the corresponding estimation of the 16th iteration - N bE equals to 6 for the 4th iteration and to 154 for the 16th iteration. The estimated codeword of the last iteration is not necessarily the best decision when the
algorithm fails to converge and the decoding process of a particular received word suffers from oscillations.
The second conclusion is that there is an obvious relationship between the metrics N bE and U C (i.e., N bE is proportional to U C); although a minimum value U C does not always correspond to a minimum N bE value. Consequently, it is feasible to improve the BER performance of classic LDPC decoding algorithms (MS, NMS, OMS, etc.), using the value U C as the selection criterion of the final estimated codeword, when the decoder fails to converge after a maximum number of N decoding iterations.
The ascertainment that the corresponding to the minimum weight syndrome codeword is not always the one with the minimum
number of bit errors, constitutes the last conclusion. Analytical results of the metrics N bE and U C for the codeword c2 of Fig. 1(c) – which converges after 36 iterations – for the first 18 iterations of the decoding algorithm are given in the Table 1. If
the predetermined maximum number of decoding iterations is set to be 18, the estimated codeword of the 5th iteration (codeword 5) is the one that corresponds to the minimum value U C (minimum U C equals to 3). This decision leads to an output with 14 bit errors. The classic MS decoder would select the result of the last iteration, i.e., a codeword with 9 bit errors, which in this case happens to be a better decision, although U C18 > U C5.
The proposed Syndrome-Based Decoder improves the decoding performance of iterative algorithms dominated by oscillatory
behavior in cases of unsuccessful decoding of received words. The proposed decoder simply keeps in memory the minimum U C value (U C k) and the corresponding estimated codeword (codeword k) – which means that there is almost no overhead in terms of hardware resources and area required when it comes to implementation. Specifically, it uses an extra memory (M inU CM emory) to store the bits of the current best estimated codeword, a register (Reg M inU C) to keep the current minimum U C value, and a comparator to compare the last derived U C value of the jth iteration with the minimum U C value of the previous j − 1 iterations, which is stored in the Reg M inU C register (Fig. 2). This estimated codeword will be the output of the decoder when the algorithm terminates. We call the proposed decoder M U CD (Minimum U C Decoder). Fig. 3 presents the hardware component that

3. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS We have experimentally evaluated the bit-error performance of the proposed Syndrome-Based LDPC Decoder (M U CD) via FPGA prototyping. The system has been designed in VHDL and mapped into FPGA using Xilinx design flow. The overall test system consists of the Encoder, an AWGN Channel [26], the Decoder and the Errors Counter module (Fig. 4). The test system uses forward error correction based on a rate-0.75, 2K (2016 bits) irregular LDPC code, based on degree distribution polynomials of WiMAX LDPC codes. We use a (5.1) quantization scheme, i.e., a 6-bit representation of LLR values with 5 integer and 1 fractional bits [27].
We begin our study with the Min-Sum and the normalized Min-Sum (NMS) algorithms. Fig. 5 illustrates the bit-error rate (BER) performance of the proposed M U CD (minU C) that implements the MS layered decoding algorithm, or the NMS with normalization factor a = 0.75, in comparison to the performance of simple decoders that implement the same algorithms without the proposed selection criterion, for maximum 10 (iter10) and 30 (iter30) iterations. In order to ensure reliable BER estimation, we have observed at least 1000 errors for each noise level

between 2 and 4.2dB (corresponding to BER down to 10−11), with a step of 0.2dB, while a number of errors of the order of 200-300 are measured at 4.6dB (BER 10−12). As we can see in Fig. 5, M U CD does not lead to BER performance gain in the case of NMS algorithm. The results for 10 iterations show that the decoding
performance of MUCD is slightly worse than simple NMS decoding.

always the one with the minimum number of bit errors overcomes
the improvement due the proposed criterion. In case of Min-Sum
algorithm, there is a insignificant BER performance improvement in case of 10 iterations. When the algorithm performs maximum 30
iterations, the improvement is considerable and the coding gain tends

a range of some other values of offset factors. Figs. 7 and 8 depict results for OMS implementations with offset factors b = 1, 1.5, 2 and 2.5. All the M U CD OMS implementations outperform the

decoding algorithm is that OMS embodiments with smaller values of offset factors show better performance in the waterfall region, but
the appearance of the error floor is observed at higher noise levels (Fig. 9). The selection of an appropriate offset factor depends on the
particular application. For example, an application specification of BER performance below 10−4 in the SNR region between 3 and 4dB leads to the selection of a small value for the offset factor. Another embodiment with offset factor greater than 2 could be suitable for a very-low-BER application, aiming at BER below 10−10.
The throughput of a decoding system is inversely proportional
to the number of iterations. There is an argument for which one of the maximum or the average number of decoding iterations should
someone use to estimate the decoding throughput. In case the decoder uses early termination, we can use the average number of
iterations, increasing the decoding throughput. This is the average throughput at a specific SNR point - the average number of decoding iterations depends on the noise level (it is higher at low SNR levels).
Most systems demand fixed data transfer rate. If the throughput of the system is set to be the average throughput, then additional FIFO

buffers are added to handle the potential long latency because of burst noise [24]. A choice is to use maximum number of iterations when we estimate decoder’s throughput, or the average number when the buffers’ size is large enough to avoid BER degradation at low SNR levels. As the signal-to-noise ratio increases, M U CD further improves decoding performance, even with less decoding iterations, using the proposed criterion for the selection of the best estimation as decoder’s output codeword. Therefore, this is an important technique for systems that require codes and decoding algorithms with very low BER.
The overall system reaches a peak clock rate of 100MHz. The Virtex5-XC5VFX70T FPGA device resources utilization for the OMS MUCD is 36% (16, 319 used of 44, 800 available) for LUTs and 55% (6, 226 used of 11, 200 available) for number of occupied Slices. The corresponding resources utilization for a simple OMS decoder is 35% (15, 979 used of 44, 800 available) for LUTs and 54% (6, 157 used of 11, 200 available) for number of occupied Slices. These results demonstrate that the overhead imposed by the proposed M U CD, in terms of required additional hardware resources and area, is indeed minimal.
4. CONCLUSIONS The proposed MUCD LDPC decoder exploits the correlation between U C and N bE, achieving a notable BER performance improvement in Min-Sum and offset Min-Sum decoding algorithms, while the performance of the normalized Min-Sum is not affected. The impact of the proposed technique is studied for the first time for the particular algorithms and in the error floor region, where benefits are shown to be more important than in the waterfall region of the BER plots. The BER performance improvement, combined with throughput increase due to reduced required number of iterations, with an insignificant increment of the hardware complexity, makes the proposed decoder very suitable for low-BER high-data-rate communication systems. The use of the particular high speed hardware platform allows the study to extend to BER values below 10−9, in the error floor region.
