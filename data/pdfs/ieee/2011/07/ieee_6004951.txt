ABSTRACT
The error correcting capability of LDPC based systems at low noise levels is often dominated by the so-called error floor, a region in the BER vs. noise level plot, where BER reduction slows down as the noise level decreases. The error floor behavior is commonly attributed to the sub-optimality of iterative decoding algorithms on graphs with cycles, which become trapped to local minimum solutions. Trapping of the decoder depends on several factors, including the decoding algorithm. A particular received word that is not decoded by a certain algorithm may be decoded successfully by a different algorithm. The proposed Multiple Decoder exploits this diverse behavior, by decoding a particular received word with N different algorithms, composing an LDPC decoder that achieves very low BER in the error floor region of operation, less iterations and higher throughput than the equivalent single decoder system.

low BER, soft-decoding.
1. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes were originally invented by Gallager in 1960s [1] and rediscovered in late 1990s by MacKay and Neal [2]. LDPC codes show excellent error correction performance, very close to the Shannon limit. Recently, LDPC codes have been considered for many industrial standards for next generation communication systems, such as Digital Video Broadcast (DVB) DVB-S2, IEEE 802.3an (10GBASE-T), IEEE 802.16e (WiMAX) [3] and IEEE 802.11n (WiFi) [4]. The decoding of an LDPC code allows a high degree of parallelism, which makes it very suitable for high-data-rate applications such as wideband wireless multimedia communications. An LDPC code is a linear block code characterized by a sparse parity check matrix H and can be fully represented by a bipartite graph (known as Tanner graph) [5] with two sets of nodes, called the check nodes and the variable nodes. Each column in the matrix corresponds to a bit within the codeword, while each row corresponds to a parity-check equation. Every valid codeword u satisfies the equation u ⊗ HT = 0, where

A very common decoding algorithm for LDPC codes is the message passing algorithm, where information is iteratively exchanged between the two sets of decoder’s processing elements, variable and check nodes [1]. This procedure is called belief propagation (BP). Nodes can be updated in a fully parallel, two-phased iterative scheduling scheme. During the first phase of an iteration all the variable nodes are updated, while in the second phase of the same iteration update of all check nodes takes place. A different message schedule than the two-phase decoding is employed by layered decoding [6]. The basic idea is to split each decoding iteration into sub-iterations by processing a subset of check nodes and pass the newly calculated messages immediately to the corresponding variable nodes. The variable nodes update their outgoing messages thus completing the current sub-iteration. The next check-node subset will thus receive newly updated messages within next sub-iteration. The total number of sub-iterations required to fully accomplish an iteration equals to the number of check-node subsets. Layered decoding improves the convergence speed due the increased number of exchanged messages during a single iteration [7].
Current research efforts target to improve the performance of LDPC codes, by finding new code construction methods and efficient decoding algorithms. The decoding algorithm strongly influences the performance of an LDPC decoder. Error correction performance of an LDPC decoder is commonly quantified by the bit-error rate (BER). There is a variety of decoding algorithms, such as the Sum-Product algorithm (SP) [8], the Log-SP algorithm [9], the Min-Sum algorithm (MS) [10], and two variants of Min-Sum algorithm, the normalized (NMS) and the offset Min-Sum algorithm (OMS) [11]. The SP algorithm exhibits a good decoding performance, but requires high hardware complexity. The Min-Sum algorithm reduces the hardware complexity at the cost of performance degradation. The sub-optimality of the Min-Sum algorithm in performance comes from approximation errors at check nodes. The error correcting capability of LDPC based systems at low noise levels, i.e., high SNR regions, is often dominated by the so-called error floor, a region in the BER vs. noise (SNR) level plot, where BER reduction slows down as the noise level decreases [14]. The error floor behavior is commonly attributed to the sub-optimality of iterative decoding algorithms on graphs with cycles, which become trapped to local minimum solutions, such as the so-called trapping sets, and may not reach the correct solution, i.e., a valid codeword. Past work has studied concepts such as near-codewords [15], trapping sets [16] and absorbing sets [17]. Zhang et al. in [18] propose a post-processing technique, which reduces the reliability of the messages from falsely satisfied checks and increases the reliability of the messages from the unsatisfied checks, so that the bits of a absorbing set can reverse the wrong values. Trapping of the decoder depends on several factors, including notably, the transmitted codeword, the particular noise to which data were exposed, the properties of the LDPC code, numerical issues in the decoder implementation and, of course, the decoding algorithm. It is noted that a received word, i.e., the received channel distorted codeword, that is not decoded by a certain algorithm, may be decoded successfully by a different algorithm. This behavior is strongly associated with the particular received word and the decoding algorithm, and not with the average decoding capability of the algorithm, as quantified by the BER metric. The error floor is usually observed at low noise levels, corresponding to BER values below 10−9. Software simulations in these regions are extremely time consuming, consequently, sometimes the error floor is out of reach of the simulations performed. There is a need of high speed hardware platforms, in order to take reliable BER measurements in the error floor region.
Chen et al. propose an implementation that integrates three compatible decoding algorithms in a single decoder and works under three modes, not in parallel, to meet different users’ requirements, aiming at flexibility [19]. Hehn et al. [20] use a collection of parity-check matrices in parallel, rather than decoding a received word in terms of only one parity-check matrix, performing joint output processing, in order to achieve better BER performance. Gunnam [21] proposes a check-node algorithm that is selectively configurable. If the LDPC decoder fails to

decode a received word within a predefined number of iterations, a controller selects a new scaling and/or offset value, performing some extra iterations, thus improving the error floor behavior. Zhou and Gao [22] study the augmented belief propagation (ABP) algorithm and variations as a means of efficient decoding. In this context, they combine ABP variations in parallel. However, they report that the BER improvement is not important. Furthermore, the ABP and variations are highly complex algorithms, not very much suited for hardware implementation.
In this work, we extend the parallel decoding ideas to hardware-friendly variations of the min-sum algorithm and investigate the particular cases under which a multiple decoder scheme substantially improves BER performance. The study focuses in the error floor area, facilitated by extensive hardware-accelerated simulations. We show that the appropriate selection of decoding algorithms leads indeed to substantial performance improvement. Furthermore, we describe an easy-to-implement technique to resolve possible disagreement among decoders.
The proposed schemes achieve BER performance improvement at a cost of substantial hardware complexity increase. The BER performance achieved is not possible by single-algorithm decoders, even for high number of decoding iterations. The authors have proposed a low-cost architecture however targeting a particular type of errors and particular algorithms in [23].
In this paper we propose a Multiple LDPC Decoder, which consists of N LDPC decoders, each one of them performing a different decoding algorithm. The bit-error performance of the proposed Multiple LDPC Decoder is evaluated, via FPGA prototyping, in comparison to the performance of single decoder implementations. The use of the particular hardware tool allows the study to extend to BER values below 10−9. To our knowledge, there are only a few works in the literature with experimental results in the error floor region, below 10−9 [18] [24].
The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 describes the proposed Multiple Decoder. In section 3 experimental results are presented, while conclusions are discussed in section 4.

A very interesting conclusion, derived from these results, is that increasing the maximum number of the decoding iterations of the NMS decoder leads to insignificant BER performance improvement in the error floor region, i.e., for BER values below 10−10.

system consists of an LDPC-codec and an Additive White Gaussian Noise Channel [25] designed and implemented using Virtex5-XC5VFX70T FPGA. The system has been designed in VHDL and mapped into FPGA using Xilinx design flow. We use Xilinx ISE 11.5 for synthesis and implementation. More specifically, the overall test system consists of the Encoder, an AWGN Channel, the Decoder and the Errors Counter module (Fig. 3). A Random Number Generator (RNG), implemented as a Linear Feedback Shift Register (LFSR), feeds the input of the system. The RNG module is synchronized with the Decoder’s output and generates the corresponding input frame at the Encoder, when the Decoder’s output is active, in order to compare Encoder’s inputs and Decoder’s outputs, without the use of extra memory for the storage of input bit-streams (2K frames). BPSK modulated symbols are transmitted over an Additive White Gaussian Noise (AWGN) channel. The Errors Counter module calculates the number of the bit errors (NbE), by comparing the hard-decision bits of the Decoder’s output with the corresponding bits in the Encoder’s input. We use a (5.1) quantization scheme, i.e., a 6-bit representation of LLR values with 5 integer and 1 fractional bits [26]. The test system uses forward error correction based on a rate-0.75 and a rate-0.8 2K (2016 bits) irregular LDPC code. We use degree distribution polynomials of WiMAX LDPC codes for the construction of these two codes.

We have incorporated into the test system two implementations of the Multiple LDPC Decoder, both of them for N =2. The first embodiment (M LDP CD emb1) consists of two sub-decoders, both of them employing the NMS layered decoding algorithm with normalization factors a1=0.75 and a2=0.875. Depending on the application, the maximum or the average number of decoding iterations that can be used is determined, in order to derive the decoding throughput. In case the decoder uses early termination, i.e., the decoding process terminates when the algorithm converges to a valid codeword before reaching the maximum number of iterations, we can use the average number of iterations, increasing the decoding throughput. This is the average throughput at a specific SNR point - the average number of decoding iterations depends on the noise level (it is higher at low SNR levels). Most systems demand fixed data transfer rate. If the throughput of the system is set to be the average throughput, then extra FIFO buffers are added to handle the potential long latency because of burst noise [24]. In this content, we use the maximum number of iterations when we estimate decoder’s throughput, or adopt the average throughput including in the design large enough buffers in order to avoid BER degradation at low SNR levels. Therefore, the reduction in the number of decoding iterations, due to the MLDPCD (Fig. 5), leads to throughput increase.
4. CONCLUSIONS This paper proposes a Multiple LDPC Decoder that substantially improves the performance in the error floor region. By exploiting the fact that particular received words in the error floor can be successfully decoded by at least one of the implemented algorithms, significant improvement in error correcting capability has been reliably demonstrated, by FPGA prototyping, at very low BER. In case that an algorithm fails, another may successfully decode the received word, at no additional delay penalty. While the hardware cost is almost doubled for the case N =2, the substantial BER improvement, not possible with conventional single decoders, combined with substantial throughput increase due to reduced number of iterations, render the proposed technique a strong candidate for adoption in low-BER high-speed communication systems. The use of the particular high speed hardware platform allows the study to extend to BER values below 10−9, where flattening effects manifest themselves.
