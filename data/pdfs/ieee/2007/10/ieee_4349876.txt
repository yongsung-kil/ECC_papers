Abstract— In this paper, we introduce and investigate the tailbiting low-density parity-check convolutional codes (LDPCCCs) based on protographs. We show a construction algorithm for these codes, which is based on a modified version of the PEG algorithm [1] and on the unwrapping [2] technique. The basic ideas behind the decoder architecture for these codes are also discussed. Finally, the performances of the tail-biting LDPCCCs based on protographs are evaluated through computer simulations, where we also examine a family of rate-compatible codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
In the last years, the low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes invented by Gallager [3] have been through a renaissance. Currently, they are being intensively studied by the coding community and are serious candidates to be the error-correction scheme of future communication and storage systems. The renewed interest in these codes is due to their simple structure and capacity approaching performance with a low-complexity decoding algorithm, which is called belief-propagation.
The convolutional counterpart of Gallager’s LDPC block codes (LDPCBCs) are the low-density parity-check convolutional codes (LDPCCCs). These codes were introduced in [2]1 and have some properties that are advantageous in certain communication systems. For instance, they are very well suited for coding streaming data or packetized data of different lengths, since the same encoder can be used to encode data sequences of different lengths. However, the introduction of the zero-tail that is needed for bringing the encoder back into the zero-state results in the so-called rate-loss. This effect is specially noticeable for small frame lengths. The introduction of the tail-biting technique allows to avoid this loss.
In his seminal work [3], Gallager only considered regular constructions of LDPC codes, i.e., codes that have parity-check matrices with a constant number of ones in each row and in each column. In [5] and [6], Luby et al. introduced the irregular LDPC codes. Using the analytical techniques summarized in [7], it can be observed that irregular constructions show improved thresholds under iterative decoding. Within this context, Thorpe [8] devised a method to construct irregular LDPC codes based on a ”copy-and-permute” operation of degreeoptimized small code templates called protographs. The aim of this construction technique is to obtain larger codes that inherit

This paper presents a novel construction method of irregular tail-biting LDPCCCs (TB-LDPCCCs) based on protographs. These codes represent an elegant way to circumvent the rateloss problem and they still hold most of the properties arising from their underlying convolutional structure. In particular, as we will show, the decoding is less complex and they perform well when compared against their block counterparts.
II. PROTOGRAPHS AND LDPC CODES BASED ON PROTOGRAPHS
A protograph is a Tanner graph with a relatively small number of nodes and edges that is used as template to construct larger graphs. Differently from the Tanner graphs defining conventional codes, parallel edges between variable nodes and check nodes are allowed. IV. TAIL-BITING LDPC CONVOLUTIONAL CODES BASED
ON PROTOGRAPHS
In order to obtain the tail-biting LDPCCCs (TB-LDPCCCs),
we first need to generate the syndrome former of the under-
lying LDPCCC, which we call mother LDPCCC. The syndrome former HT[0,∞] of a rate R = b/c mother
LDPCCC with syndrome former memory ms is generated as follows. Firstly, we create a zero-matrix with dimension c · (ms + 1) × (c − b) · (ms + 1). This matrix is then divided into sub-matrices of dimension c × (c − b), as shown in Fig. 4(a).
Observe that the dimension of the sub-matrices is determined by the dimension of the parity-check matrix HProto associated with the protograph GProto = (V, C, E) that we want to use in our construction, i.e., |V| = c and |C| = (c − b). The next step
is to fill the matrix in Fig. 4(a) with ones according to edges defined in GProto.
We define ε as being the set containing all types of edges of GProto. The edge types are distinguished from each other depending on the type of variable and check nodes that they
connect. Observe also that the cardinality of ε can be smaller than the cardinality of E (i.e., |ε| ≤ |E|) because of parallel edges. We also define a set Wε that contains the weights of all edge types of GProto. There is a one-to-one correspondence between the elements of ε and the elements of Wε. For the protograph in Fig. 1(a), Wε = {1, 1, 1, 1, 1, 1, 1, 2}. Obviously, the sum of all elements in Wε is equal to |E|. The ones are then placed in the zero-matrix of Fig. 4(a) considering that each of its sub-matrices is an ”empty” copy of HPTroto. The operation of the circular pipeline decoder is as follows. The code sequence of a TB-LDPCCC corresponding to N time instants enters the decoder, so that its memory is completely written. Each parallel processor Di is attributed a memory section (Fig. 5), which is large enough to avoid the memory access collisions between the processors and, thus, enable their parallel operation. Considering the graph representation of the TB-LDPCCC, we can easily note that the size of the memory sections should be at least ms + 1 time instants to permit simultaneous operation of all processors. Then, the processors Di hop over the memory addresses in steps corresponding to the amount of data transmitted in one time instant of the TBLDPCCC. After each processor has completed IR rounds over the complete memory, a number I = IR · IP of decoding iterations has been performed. Clearly, a circular pipeline decoder with IP = N /(ms + 1) parallel processors can also decode TB-LDPCCCs with blocklengths smaller than N time instants. In this case, the processor that is performing the operations at the borders of the active memory region must neglect the inactive memory region in between by performing a wraparound in the address generation. Moreover, the processors that are located in the inactive memory region can be turned off.

VII. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we presented the tail-biting LDPC convolutional codes (TB-LDPCCCs) based on protographs. A

construction algorithm based on a modified version of the
PEG algorithm and unwrapping technique was presented, from
which we can obtain TB-LDPCCCs of different lengths based
on a mother LDPCCC. The decoding of these codes was also
discussed. Simulation results were presented and we could
observe that these codes perform well at a lower implemen-
tation complexity than irregular LDPCBCs with comparable
performances. We also showed an example of a family of
rate-compatible TB-LDPCCCs. Our further research includes
the study of efficient encoders for TB-LDPCCCs as well as a
theoretical analysis.
