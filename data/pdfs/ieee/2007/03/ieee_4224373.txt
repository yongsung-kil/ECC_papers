Abstract— In this paper, a novel decoding algorithm using perturbation techniques for LDPC concatenated with CRC (LDPCCRC) is proposed. The CRC is used to check the residual error of the BP algorithm, while a new parameter is introduced to LDPC decoding to describe the reliability of each bit in the iterative decoding algorithm. Upon an error is detected, redecoding process with perturbation method will be aroused. Simulation results show that this method lowers the error floor for codeword length of several hundred bits effectively. Index Terms− LDPC, BP, reliability, Chaos, error floor
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have attracted great interest due to excellent performance with long codeword length [1] [2]. However, the performance of LDPC codes with short codeword length of several hundred bits typically deviates significantly from what is predicted [3].
It is well known that short cycles in Tanner Graph prevent the BP algorithm from achieving the optimum solution [4]. For this reason, some work has been carried out to design LDPC codes with large girth, i.e., the length of the smallest cycle [5]. However, the achievement is limited by a lower bound of girth for a given codeword length [1]. Moreover, the relationship between girth and the error correction performance for short codes is unknown yet [6].
As an alternative, some authors proposed to use concatenation method to improve the performance of short LDPC codes. These methods include LDPC-LDPC, LDPC-turbo and LDPC-CRC concatenation in [7], [8] and [9], respectively.
In this paper, we propose a new decoding method of the LDPC-CRC scheme with perturbation approach. The function of the CRC is to find the error among the decisions made by BP algorithm. While an error is detected, a perturbation vector will be added to the initial inputs of the decoder to begin a redecoding process. We facilitate a multi-decoding scheme with loop operations, i.e., a statistically independent perturbation vector is added to each loop. The detailed discussions and the simulation results are given in the following sections.

III. ESTIMATE THE RELIABILITY OF A RECEIVED BIT
FROM ITS INITIAL BELIEF AND CONSTRAINT EQUATIONS
It is observed that bit l is involved in checks m1 , m2 and m3 from figure 1, and { li,j } is the set of bits that participating in check mi except l. Based on the bipartite graph, we give the follows definitions:
(1) U (l): The number of the bit l’s unsatisfied constraints, i.e., the number of the error check nodes connecting to the variable node l in the bipartite graph;
(2) S(l): the bit l’s initial belief; (3) D(a,b): the distance between node a and node b in the bipartite graph, where a and b are neighbor nodes of each other when 0<D(a,b)≤2. Generally, when a block is transmitted over a noisy channel, some variables are received incorrectly. Therefore, it’s very important to analyze the bit’s reliability in a codeword for the iterative decoding algorithm. A simple analysis method depends on the bit’s initial belief S(l) and the parameter U (l). For two bits, S(l) is the only measure to compare their reliability when their U (l) values are equal. But sometimes S(l) is not enough to get the bit l’s reliability exactly. Here, we estimate the bit l’s reliability by using the constraint nodes satisfying D(l,m)=3, where m is the check node that the bit l is involved in. When the bits l1 and l2 satisfy U (l1)=U (l2) and T (l1)<T (l2), l1 is less reliable than l2. Obviously, for the bits having the same value of U (l), the error probabilities of these bits are greatly different if they have different values of T (l). Therefore, we can determine the reliability of each bit according to its values of U (l) and T (l) in the iterative decoding process.
The selection of the threshold of U (l) and T (l) will exert an influence on the decoding performance. The rigorous threshold is T (l)=0, U (l)=0, which means the bit l is seemed as a reliable bit only when T (l)=0, U (l)=0. Here U (l)=0 means that there is no unsatisfied check node connecting to l and T (l)=0 means that every bit node connecting to the same check node with node l satisfies U (l)=0. If an error occurs on a bit which satisfies U (l)=0 and T (l)=0, it leads to an undetected error.
Therefore, in the iterative decoding scheme, the initial belief S(l) is not the only criterion for identifying the reliability of the decoded bit. The other parameters, such as U (l) and T (l), can also be utilized to determine the reliability of a certain bit.
IV. NONLINEAR DYNAMICS OF ITERATIVE DECODING
In this section, we study the dynamics of iterative decoding algorithm, qualitatively and quantitatively, for a relatively large range of SNRs.
The performance of the iterative decoding can be classified into three distinct regions [10].
Low SNR Region: For very low values of SNR, the extrinsic LLRs often converge to values leading to a large number of incorrect decisions. The corresponding performance curves decrease slowly with SNR.
High SNR Region: For very high SNRs, the extrinsic LLRs often converge to values leading to mostly correct decisions. However, the corresponding performance curves reach an ”error floor” and decrease slowly with SNR.
Waterfall Region: The transition between the aforementioned SNR regions is called the ”waterfall region,” as the performance curves decrease sharply with SNR in this region.

Iterative decoding algorithms may be viewed as highdimensional nonlinear dynamical systems, depending on a large number of parameters. A whole range of phenomena known to occur in nonlinear systems, such as existence of multiple fixed points, oscillatory behavior, bifurcations, chaos, and transient chaos are found in iterative decoding algorithms [11] [12].
The fundamental property of a chaotic system is its sensitivity to initial condition. From a practical point of view, this property implies that the trajectories (time evolution) of two nearby initial conditions diverge from each other quickly and their separation increases exponentially on the average [13].
In the following section, we consider an application of nonlinear control theory to speed up the convergence of the iterative decoding. A modified BP decoding scheme is proposed to control transient chaos in order to reduce the long transient behavior in the iterative decoding algorithm, which leads to a substantial improvement in performance. In this scheme, after the standard BP decoding, the reliability of the decoded bits is identified by three parameters, i.e., S(l), U (l) and T (l), and an additional perturbation step is then performed on those unreliable bits to control transient chaos and facilitate the convergence of iterative decoding.
V. PERTURBATION METHOD
In high SNR environment, when the code rate is high and the code length is short, more than 30% of the LDPC errors are undetected errors. The Cyclic Redundancy Check (CRC) code is famous of its strong error detecting capability with low complexity in implementation. In this section, we concatenate short LDPC code with CRC-k code, frame by frame, in the channel encoder and decoder (CODEC), the CRC is designed to be a complementary of LDPC codes in error detecting, where CRC-k is used as the outer code while LDPC is used as the inner code. At the encoder side, a k-bit CRC tailor is complemented to the (n-m-k)-bit information as the input of the LDPC (n, m) encoder. The animation of the combination can be used to calculate the LDPC decoding values by BP algorithm and control the undetected error by the CRC at the decoder side. To guarantee that CRC undetected error probability is at order of 10−2 lower than the result of the BP algorithm in SNR region 1.0∼4.5dB, we should select an appropriate number of redundant CRC bits. Unlike the conventional procedure, an order perturbation belief propagation (OPBP) decoding algorithm is used in the re-decoding scheme at the receiver by destroying or alleviate the positive feedback in short girth loops, rather than the re-transmission, whenever an error is found. The successful operation of the abovementioned scheme is based on the fact that adding perturbations to the received bits can modify the belief distribution in initializing BP algorithm and, therefore, lead to another decoding result. Because the perturbation power plays, in fact, a role as noise in the redecoding processing, one may wish to reduce the portion of the perturbed bits and power of the perturbation. However, the number of the perturbed bits can not be too small because there would be, otherwise, strong correlation between the results of decoding and re-decoding or among the re-decoding themselves. Consequently, the re-decoding is not effective to give a ”new” result. What we do here is to select a portion of bits (from all bits of a codeword), which have the lowest reliabilities, to the perturbation because these bits are more sensitive to the perturbation and, at the same time, have comparable reliability contribution in comparison with those having larger reliabilities [14].
Again, based on the same considerations mentioned above, the power of the perturbation should also be optimized. In this research, we use simulation method to search the optimum number of perturbed bits and the perturbation power in a useful SNR range.

VI. SIMULATION RESULTS
To show an evidence of effectiveness of the proposed method, we take short LDPC codes, having 192 ,288 and 384 bits in length with code rate of 1/2, 2/3, 3/4 which have been specified in IEEE 802.16e respectively [15]. The concatenating CRC-8 code is generated by X8+X4+X3+X2+1.
Table 1 gives the undetected error statistical characteristics of the LDPC and CRC-LDPC codes. Simulation results show that the CRC-LDPC can detect more than 99% of the LDPC undetected errors, and the detecting efficiency is even higher at high SNR. The CRC-LDPC achieves great improvement in error detecting capacity at the cost of trivial loss in code rate.
The number of the perturbed bits and the perturbation power are optimized by huge amount of simulations taken L=24+12i,0≤i≤6 and σp=0.25+0.05j, 0≤j≤5 where i and j are integers. For every pair of the parameters, i.e. L and σp, the FERs of the decoder are monitored in a SNR region from 1dB to 4.5dB. The best overall FER performance is found by
LDPC(192,96)+CRC-8 : L=48, σp=0.40 LDPC(288,192)+CRC-8: L=60, σp=0.35 LDPC(384,288)+CRC-8: L=72, σp=0.30 which are used to complete the decode process. The FER performance is shown in figure 3, in which comparisons are made for the corresponding results in IEEE 802.16e and the sphere-packing bound, which is calculated according to[16] . As shown in figure 3, one finds that the proposed method shows FER improvement at about 0.7dB gain over those of IEEE 802.16e at FER=10−4. It can be observed in figure 4 that the average iterations decrease as the SNR increases. At high SNR region, the computational complexity only increases by approximately 10%, and the OPBP also has a lower decoding error floor than that of BP.
Finally, it should be noted that the objective of this paper is to show the effectiveness of the perturbation method. In the practice, the OPBP method can improve the bandwidth efficiency, but may involves more calculation power.
