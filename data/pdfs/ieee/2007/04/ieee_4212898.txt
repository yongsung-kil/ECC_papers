Optimized LDPC Codes for OFDM and Spread OFDM in Correlated Channels

Abstract— This paper analyzes optimized short block length lowdensity parity-check (LDPC) codes in spread OFDM systems that operate in frequency correlated Rayleigh fading channels. Extended irregular repeat-accumulate (eIRA) codes and codes constructed with progressive edge-growth (PEG) algorithm are optimized using density evolution and differential evolution techniques and any error floors are improved using girth conditioning. It is shown here that error floors in LDPC coded OFDM is lowered by introducing spreading on top of channel coding. It is also demonstrated that by using the distribution of the number of faded bits per OFDM symbol, the similarity in performance between codes in uncorrelated and correlated Rayleigh fading channels can be determined a priori.
I. INTRODUCTION
The use of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] in spread OFDM systems has been shown to provide significant performance gains when compared to similar systems employing small block length codes [2][3]. The LDPC codes for these systems were not optimized in any way for the fading channel environment they were operating within. Techniques have recently been conveyed which address the design of LDPC codes for use in time correlated Rayleigh fading channels [4][5]. Frequency correlated fading channels are often appropriately used in OFDM and spread OFDM system models. In this paper, a method for optimizing LDPC codes for use with spread OFDM in a frequency correlated channel is addressed for the first time, and results showing the system performance are provided. Codes with short block lengths (approximately 1000 bits) are emphasized.
The design and construction of a well-performing LDPC code takes place at several different steps. One of the key initial steps is the determination of optimal degree distribution polynomials that define the code’s Tanner graph. Density evolution [6] and differential evolution [7] techniques have been widely used to obtain the polynomials optimized for various channels, like uncorrelated Rayleigh fading [4] and AWGN [6].
Once optimal distribution polynomials are obtained, the next challenge lies in constructing a good code with the polynomials. Unlike LDPC codes with large block lengths, randomly constructed short block length codes have a higher chance of suffering from error floors at high signal-to-noise ratios (SNRs) due to their increased likelihood of cycles in the code. Several techniques have been proposed [8]-[10] for improving the performance of short block length LDPC codes, especially at high SNRs. These methods, referred to as girth conditioning, all deal with maximizing the girth (shortest cycle length) of the code’s

graph. Maximizing the girth will remove short cycles in the code so that messages arriving at each node will be more independent, an assumption made in the message-passing decoding algorithm.
In this paper, the use of the progressive edge-growth (PEG) algorithm [9] for generating codes is compared to extended irregular repeat-accumulate (eIRA) codes [11] for the same polynomial distribution. The eIRA codes exhibit an error floor at high SNRs. This error floor is lowered by using the girth conditioning method of [8]. In this work, we demonstrate that the error floor observed in eIRA coded OFDM systems can be further lowered by employing spreading in addition to coding. Specifically, we analyze the performance of optimized PEG constructed and eIRA codes in both uncorrelated and correlated (two outdoor models, Hilly Terrain and Typical Urban [12]) channels. We consider rate 1/2 LDPC codes having block sizes of 1024 bits. We show that by using codes generated with the PEG algorithm, both OFDM and spread OFDM systems achieve the most gains relative to systems with no optimization in their codes. We demonstrate that spread OFDM with PEG generated codes provide the best bit error rate performance among all systems.
While it is possible to design optimal LDPC codes for uncorrelated channels, the complexity of density evolution in both time and frequency correlated channels makes it infeasible to perform the optimization for these channels. Therefore, one approach is to employ the codes optimized for the uncorrelated channels in correlated environments. In this work, we identify a channel statistic, the number of faded bits per OFDM symbol Kf{OF DM}, that helps us determine the performance of codes optimized for uncorrelated fading channels in correlated channels. We derive the distribution of this statistic and illustrate that whenever the Kf{OF DM} distribution in correlated channels approaches that of the Kf{OF DM} in an uncorrelated channel, then the performance in the correlated case approaches the uncorrelated case. Hence, by observing the distribution of Kf{OF DM} we can determine beforehand how close the performance of the systems in correlated and uncorrelated fading channels will be.

III. LDPC CODE OPTIMIZATION
Optimization of LDPC codes can be accomplished in two steps, as shown in figure 3. Part of the first step is to calculate the noise threshold value for the given system. This value is the maximum noise level σ (σ is the standard deviation of the noise) such that probability of error goes to zero as the number of iterations and block length reach infinity. For this we used the discretized density evolution method of [15]. This method uses the densities of the messages (extrinsic probabilities) passed during the decoding algorithm to find the threshold.
Differential evolution is used to obtain the node degree distribution polynomials, λ(x) and ρ(x). This method actually works in coordination with discretized density evolution so that these distribution polynomials are iteratively adjusted until a degree distribution pair that correspond to the threshold are found. These are labeled λbest(x) and ρbest(x).
The final step is to construct the code using the distribution polynomials obtained. This research concentrates on eIRA codes and codes constructed using the PEG algorithm, both of which have been shown to perform well with short block lengths.
The results in sections IV and VI will show that eIRA codes have error floors at high SNRs. In [11], error floors were eliminated by removing low degree nodes from the code’s graph at the expense of performance degradation in the waterfall region. In this research, the search method of [8] is used to find better codes without any loss in performance. The code with the highest girth average is picked as the best code. For 6000 codes analyzed in uncorrelated Rayleigh fading channel, the mean of the girth averages is 7.59 for eIRA codes and 8.11 for codes generated with the PEG algorithm.
Selecting the code with the highest girth average improves the performance at high SNRs, although error floors cannot be completely eliminated. Only a smaller part of the parity-check matrix of an eIRA code is random, and the constraints imposed by the differential evolution method do not allow many variations in code construction. Therefore, the deviation of the average girth distribution of all the codes is small and the resulting performance improvement is minimal.

VI. RESULTS
Performance results of optimized coded OFDM and coded spread OFDM in Hilly Terrain and Typical Urban outdoor channel models are given in figures 7 and 8. Total bandwidth, BW, is 5 MHz. and the coherence bandwidths of the Hilly Terrain and Typical Urban channels are 39.72 kHz. and 188 kHz., respectively. The number of carriers, N , is set to 1024 and BPSK modulation is assumed. Code rate is 1/2.
From figure 7, it is clear that codes optimized with PEG algorithm are the best in all cases, giving a 0.5 dB improvement in spread OFDM systems over codes with no optimization at 10−5 bit error rate. In OFDM systems, optimized eIRA codes result in error floors for SNRs higher than 6 dB in both channels. Even in low SNRs, they are not much better than codes with no optimization. On the other hand, in OFDM systems with spreading, both optimization methods improve the performance over codes with no optimization. Similar to the performance in uncorrelated Rayleigh fading channel, spreading also lowers the error floors associated with eIRA codes in correlated fading channels.

VII. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, spread OFDM systems using optimized LDPC codes have been analyzed in frequency correlated fading channels. It is shown that by using codes generated with the PEG algorithm, both OFDM and spread OFDM systems achieve the most gains relative to systems using no optimization in their codes.
