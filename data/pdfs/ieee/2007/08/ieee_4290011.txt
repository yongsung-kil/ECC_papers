Serially-Concatenated Low-Density Generator Matrix (SCLDGM) Codes for Transmission Over AWGN and Rayleigh Fading Channels
Miguel González-López, Member, IEEE, Francisco J. Vázquez-Araújo, Student Member, IEEE, Luis Castedo, Member, IEEE, and Javier Garcia-Frias, Member, IEEE

Abstract— Low Density Generator Matrix (LDGM) codes are a particular class of Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) codes with very low encoding complexity. Single LDGM codes present high error-floors, which can be substantially reduced with the serial concatenation of two LDGM (SCLDGM) codes. We propose a technique to obtain good SCLDGM codes using EXtrinsic Information Transfer (EXIT) functions in a novel way. Although the optimization is performed for AWGN channels with binary signaling, the resulting codes are also optimal for AWGN and perfectly-interleaved Rayleigh fading channels with non-binary signaling and perfect CSI at reception, provided that Gray mapping is utilized. Optimized regular and irregular SCLDGM codes outperform heuristically-designed LDGM codes existing in the literature, and have a performance similar to or better than that of Irregular Repeat Accumulate (IRA) codes.
Index Terms— Code design, Gaussian channels, LDGM codes, LDPC codes, Rayleigh fading channels.

Although decoding of standard LDPC codes can be performed at reasonable complexity using easily parallelizable algorithms, encoding is a much more involved operation. On the contrary, encoding is extremely simple in Low Density Generator Matrix (LDGM) codes [3], which are a particular class of LDPC codes. LDGM codes per se have limited applicability because, in general, they present an unacceptably high error floor, although for high-rates this error-floor may already be sufficiently low [3]. These error floors, however, can be substantially reduced by concatenating an LDGM code with a high rate outer code [4], [5]. Since the performance of LDGM codes is very good precisely for high-rates, it is natural to consider them as outer codes thus resulting in a very homogeneus coding scheme that we will denote SeriallyConcatenated LDGM (SCLDGM).
SCLDGM codes share some similarities with another class of low encoding complexity LDPC codes known as Irregular Repeat Accumulate (IRA) codes [6]. IRA codes are also constructed from a core LDGM code but reduce the errorfloor by appending an accumulator. Extended IRA (eIRA) [7] and Block-LDPC [8] codes are of particular interest because of the staircase structure of the parity check matrix that enables very efficient encoding implementations [9]. Forward Error Correcting (FEC) techniques considered in recent standards such as IEEE 802.11n or DVB-S2 utilize these types of codes and have an encoding complexity similar to that of SCLDGM codes. However, as opposed to SCLDGM codes, IRA codes require the use of an interleaver between the code and the constellation mapper in systems where the receiver performs iterations between the decoder and the demapper [10]. This is necessary to make independent the log probability ratios (L-values) at the input of the demapper, which due to the accumulator present dependencies at the output of the IRA code.
Two basic techniques have been proposed for the design of LDPC codes: Density Evolution (DE) [11] and Extrinsic Information Transfer (EXIT) charts [12]. DE is a more exact method but application of EXIT charts is less computational demanding. EXIT charts have been successfully applied to the design of good LDPC [12] and IRA [10] codes. In this work,

Code convergence is tested by simulating the evolution of the mutual information through the iterations of the SPA. For a fixed Eb/N0 we start with all I’s equal to zero and, then, we iteratively compute their values. This is exactly what the SPA does, except for using the associated I’s of the messages instead of their actual values. We say that the code converges when we find a sequence of I’s that finally leads to Iu = 1, where Iu is the mutual information associated to the a posteriori L-values of bits u [see (5)]. If Iu remains almost equal to, but strictly less than 1 for several Eb/N0’s, convergence is achieved but with a significant error-floor.
