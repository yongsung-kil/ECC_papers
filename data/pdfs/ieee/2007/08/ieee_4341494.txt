Low-Complexity Architectures of a Decoder for IEEE 802.16e LDPC Codes
Giuseppe Gentile, Massimo Rovini and Luca Fanucci Department of Information Engineering - University of Pisa
Via G. Caruso, I-56122 Pisa - Italy {giuseppe.gentile, massimo.rovini, luca.fanucci}@iet.unipi.it

Abstract
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have recently been included as error-correcting codes in IEEE 802.16e, for wireless metropolitan area networks. This paper proposes a flexible, low-complexity LDPC decoder fully compliant with all 114 codes defined by the standard. The decoder runs the layered decoding algorithm to increase the convergence speed, and relies on a semi-parallel implementation with serial processing units working in pipeline to reduce the latency. Particularly, two different architectures are considered, and their RTL/memory complexity tradeoffs are analyzed. The resulting design yields a throughput ranging from 93 to 497 Mbps by means of 15 iterations at the clock frequency of 400 MHz. Synthesis on 65 nm CMOS technology, shows a chip area less than 0.59 mm2, despite the high flexibility, which compares favourably with similar implementations.
1 Introduction
During the last few years, the availability of broadband access has become crucial to many users all over the world. The worldwide interoperability of microwave access (WiMAX) described by the IEEE 802.16e standard for wireless metropolitan area networks (WMAN) [2], will provide wireless alternative to conventional DSL and cable Internet connections, especially in rural and suburban areas. The long-range coverage requirements (up to 50 Km from the base station) and the high data-rate (about 70 Mbps) make a very robust forward error correction (FEC) system mandatory; indeed, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have been included in [2].
First introduced by Gallager [7], and recently rediscovered by MacKay and Neal [10], LDPC codes are nowadays considered one of the most attractive FEC solutions by the industrial and scientific community. Compared with other competitors, such as Turbo codes [4], LDPC codes feature lower implementation complexity and bet-

ter error correction performance for relatively short blocklengths and low signal-to-noise ratios (SNRs) [13].
LDPC are linear block codes defined by a sparse paritycheck matrix, specifying the parity-check constraints of the bits in the codewords. They can be effectively represented by a bi-partite graph, also referred to as Tanner graph [16], in which the transmitted bits and the paritycheck constraints are represented by interconnections between variable and check nodes, as shown in figure 1. Decoding of LDPC codes goes iteratively by exchanging messages (message-passing) between the connected nodes, to improve the reliability of the received bits.

Some families of LDPC codes, known as architectureaware codes (AA-LDPC) [11], are particularly suitable for VLSI implementation; they are structured codes, whose parity-check matrix is built according to specific patterns. AA-LDPC codes reduce the area, the power and the memory requirements of the resulting decoder, and above all, decoding can be easily parallelized, allowing an effective trade-off between throughput and complexity. Finally, thanks to the negligible loss in correction performance w.r.t. random codes, AA-LDPC codes are the preferred choice of modern communication standards, such as DVB-S2 [1], IEEE 802.16e and IEEE 802.11n [3].
Although the decoding operations can run in parallel, a serial processing unit is preferable to a parallel one, because of the lower power consumption, the reduced area and the higher flexibility in the support of different codes. As a drawback, the resulting throughput is fairly low, thus making the use of pipelining techniques mandatory.

2 The IEEE 802.16e LDPC Codes

Actually, four code rates are supported, 1/2, 2/3, 3/4, 5/6 but two different alternatives, labelled with “A” and “B”, are defined for rates 2/3 and 3/4. The key point of layered decoding is the overlap of the traditional variable node (VN) and check node (CN) phase, so that each iteration is viewed as the concatenation of several layer elaborations or micro-iterations. Within a layer, the reliability of a bit in the received codeword, once updated, is immediately made available to next layers, thus always working on the newest information. This mechanism doubles the convergence speed w.r.t. the two-phase decoding. 4 Decoder Architecture

The layered decoding algorithm is implemented with a pipelined semi-parallel architecture able to process all codes in the IEEE 802.16e standard. The large variety of codeword sizes zf , ranging from 24 to 96, calls for an high degree of flexibility in the decoder architecture. Figure 4 shows the reference template of a semi-parallel architecture, in which a cluster of z0 = 96 serial node processors (NPs), is used to support the maximum parallelism.
Since several layers update a same block of zf SO messages but with different orders given by the prototype matrices, SOs need to be properly rotated. To this aim, a multi-dimensional circular-shifting network (MS-CS) has been inserted before the SO memory to rotate an array of zf messages so that it is stored ready for its next occurrence; in this way, differential rotations are performed. Note that C2V messages don’t need to be rotated as each block of data is used only once per iteration and always with the same order.
Focusing on memories, they are vectorized for the maximum size z0 = 96, so that a single location of the SO and C2V memory contains z0 × NSO and z0 × Nc2v bits, respectively, with NSO and Nc2v the bits used to represent the related messages. Anyway, if a code with codeword size zf is decoded, then only zf × NSO and zf × Nc2v bits are used.
During the acquisition of the channel LLRs, the NPs are by-passed and the MS-CS network is fed by the input buffer, which provides zf input LLRs per clock cycle.

5.2 Synthesis results
The two decoder architectures described in section 4, have been synthesized on 65 nm CMOS technology using 1.20 V standard-cell library at the clock frequency of 400 MHz. The bit-true parameters have been set to Nc2v = NLLR = 5; NSO = 7 aiming at good error-correction perfor-

mance. Table 5 details the synthesis results of the RTL design
and memories for both the FIFO-based and memory-based architecture. The complexity of the RTL design is given in µm2 as well as in equivalent Kgates2, while the overall number of bits is specified for each memory unit.
By removing the FIFO-buffer from NPs, the memorybased architecture saves about 27% of the RTL complexity; as a drawback, the SO and C2V memories are augmented because of the added redundancy, and extra info are stored in the configuration ROMs to properly map the position of the messages within the two banks. As a result, the size in bits of the RAMs and ROMs increases of 12% and 26%, respectively. However, the reduced RTL complexity makes this solution more efficient, with an overall saving of about 7% in the decoder area.
6 Discussion and Conclusion
In this paper we have presented the design of a lowcomplexity decoder for IEEE 802.16e LDPC codes. Conversely to [6], the decoder implements the layered decoding algorithm for all six classes of codes; this has been achieved with the use of serial processing units working in pipeline, properly scheduled with the introduction of idle cycles to cope with the dependence between layers. Furthermore, the decoding operations have been vectorized according to the codeword size of the AA-codes defined by the standard.
Two different architectures, named FIFO-based and memory-based, have been proposed and compared. The RTL complexity of the latter is considerably lower, but it also requires, in principle, three-port memories. However, these have been effectively implemented with two dual-port banks, with only few redundant elements stored in both the banks.
Finally, the synthesis on 65 nm CMOS technology showed that, despite the high flexibility demanded by the IEEE 802.16e LDPC codes, the complexity of the proposed architectures remains under 0.6 mm2 and outperforms similar implementations: actually, with the same 0.13 µm CMOS technology used in [6], their complexity is about 1.83 mm2 (FIFO-based) and 1.72 mm2 (memorybased), with a saving greater than 52%.
