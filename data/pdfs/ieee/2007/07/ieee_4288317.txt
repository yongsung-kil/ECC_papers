Performance Optimization of Soft-decision FEC Receivers
Kiyoshi Onohara and Takashi Mizuochi Information Technology R&D Center, Mitsubishi Electric Corporation, 5-1-1 Ofuna, Kamakura, Kanagawa 247-8501, Japan, Onohara.Kiyoshi@eb.MitsubishiElectric.co.jp

Abstract We present threshold tracking of a soft-decision based forward error correction system as an application of digital signal processing for optical communications. The 3-bit soft-decision thresholds are closely controlled to obtain the best possible error-correction performance.
1. Introduction The coming age of digital signal processing (DSP) for optical communications holds promise of a future generation of optical networking without using bulky optical components. One of the most useful DSP applications is forward error correction (FEC) [1]. One state-of-the-art FEC is based on soft-decision decoding employing a high speed analogue-to-digital converter (ADC). The best possible error-correction performance can be obtained only when the multi-level decision thresholds are well aligned with the log-likelihood ratio (LLR) of the received symbols. The decision thresholds vary continually due to the changing waveform distortion due to optical signal-to-noise ratio (OSNR) variations, chromatic dispersion and/or polarization mode dispersion changes, and fiber nonlinearity changes. Therefore, automatic tracking of the decision thresholds is essential to maintain the optimum error-correction capability. Although a number of investigations into automatic tracking of decision thresholds have been reported for harddecision FEC, few reports on soft-decision FEC have been seen in optical communications to date [2].
In this paper, we present an advanced FEC based on performance optimization of the soft-decision process. The concept of LLR, an important parameter for this process, is explained. An error filter function for the soft-decision ADC is proposed to reduce mis-correction of bit errors. Automatic threshold tracking is demonstrated using a block turbo code LSI with 3-bit soft-decision at 12.4 Gb/s. As another important application of the soft-decision approach, a low-density paritycheck code (LDPC) is discussed.
2. Optimum Thresholds for Soft-decision Decoding Soft-decision is performed by setting 2M-1 decision thresholds, where M is the number of quantization levels. For M = 3, the two possible received signals, “mark” and “space”, lie in regions represented by a binary vector ranging from [011] to [111]. The left-most bit is the hard decision digit, and the other two digits are confidence bits indicating the probability of mark or space. In optically amplified on-off keying (OOK) systems, the probability density function (p.d.f.) of the received signals is represented by a non-central χ2-distribution as shown in Fig. 1. The χ2-distribution, the sum of squares of independent Gaussian random variables with identical variances, is applicable here because the optical noise is additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN). However, because the numerical calculation of this p.d.f. is not straightforward, it is often assumed that the electrical currents in the marks and spaces are asymmetric Gaussian distributions having different variances

for mark and space. On making a soft-decision, the LLR of the received signals is calculated to obtain their confidence bits by:

3. Error Filter for Impossible Outputs from the Deciders The truth table of the 3-bit encoder for “mark” is shown in Table 1. Four sets of regenerated data should be expected as reasonable output patterns in regard to each hard-decision result for 0 and 1. However, impossible outputs from the deciders can in fact be caused by the effects of signal noise and variation in the decision sensitivities. These impossible outputs cause severe degradation of the error-correction performance. Therefore, we embedded an error filter into the 3-bit encoder. When an impossible output arises, it is automatically corrected to the most probable value according to Table 1. A numerical simulation based on the Monte Carlo method with and without the error filter predicted that the correction performance is improved by at least 0.5 dB by the embedded error filter.

4. Threshold Tracking Experiments Fig. 3 illustrates the experimental setup for automatic threshold tracking for soft-decision decoding. The tracking procedure is described below [3]: • Step 1 - The bit counter counts the number of marks and spaces received by the hard decision decider (D4). The control processor continuously adjusts the decision threshold D4 so as to obtain equal numbers of marks and spaces. • Step 2 - A reference decider (Ref) sweeps the threshold cyclically. Those thresholds at which the ratio of marks to spaces is 1:3 or 3:1 represent the average values for mark (µ1) and space (µ0). The distance between the two thresholds is stored as the current waveform amplitude. • Step 3 - The remaining six decision thresholds are calculated and set according to the theory presented in [2], using the optimum hard decision threshold (Step 1) and the current waveform amplitude (Step 2). • Step 4 - With the hard decision threshold tracked as in Step 1, the remaining, soft-decision, thresholds take pre-calculated time-varying values. If an amplitude change is observed, the soft-decision thresholds are re-calculated.
A 231-1 pseudorandom binary sequence (PRBS) test signal at 9.95 Gb/s is encoded at 12.4 Gb/s with 23.6% FEC redundancy by the BTC LSI [1], and is then converted by the optical sender (OS) to a return-to-zero OOK signal at λ = 1554 nm. The SNR of the OOK signal is intentionally degraded by AWGN noise generated by the EDFA. Waveform distortion due to chromatic dispersion is emulated by a variable dispersion generator. After reception by the 12.4 Gb/s optical receiver (OR), the electrical signal is launched into the 3-bit soft-decision IC. This has seven deciders for 3-bit soft-decision plus a reference decider, which is independent of the live traffic. The output from the seven deciders is first error filtered, encoded by the 3-bit encoder, and then decoded by the BTC LSI. A bit counting function is integrated into the BTC LSI, which cyclically counts the number of marks and spaces of all the deciders’ outputs for use in automatic threshold tracking. A control processor unit provides appropriate decision thresholds for all of the deciders in real time.

5. Soft-decision for LDPC Codes We have to date focussed on the block turbo code to study the nature of the soft-decision based advanced FEC. However, soft-decision is essential not only for BTC but also for LDPC

codes, which are being studied intensively as potential

candidates for optical communications at 40 Gb/s or more. The

calculations showed that LDPC had the potential to be

improved yet further, e.g. the project geometry PG(2,26)-

LDPC(4161,3431) code with 21.2% redundancy, with which an

NCG of 11.27 dB was obtained [4]. Although some simulations

reported previously have shown high error-correction

capability, no experimental demonstration has been carried out

yet. This is because the superior error-correction performance

is based on an unrealistic soft-decision process having tens or

more of bits. Circuit implementation of such a large number of

bits is very difficult for an LSI for 40 Gb/s or more. In order to

obtain better performance with few soft-decision bits, we

proposed a cyclic approximated δ-minimum algorithm [5].

Simulation shows that soft-decision decoding with only 3 bits provides the required Q of 6.2 dB at 10-6 BER.

6. Conclusion Automatic tracking of soft-decision FEC thresholds has been presented. We demonstrated a soft-decision encoder with an embedded error filter function. BER performance has been successfully stabilized in conjunction with a 10 Gb/s BTC FEC. Soft-decision also plays an important role in LDPC codes for higher speed optical communication systems.
