Analysis and Design of Finite-Length LDPC Codes
Guosen Yue, Ben Lu, and Xiaodong Wang, Senior Member, IEEE

AbstractŌĆöWe consider the performance analysis and code construction of finite-length low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. First, by convergence analysis based on the extrinsic information evolution, we analyze the performance of both regular and irregular finite-length LDPC codes under iterative decoding. Next, by focusing on a special class of LDPC codes, namely, systematic irregular repeat-accumulate (IRA) codes, we propose a design procedure to construct finite-length LDPC codes. In addition to giving rise to a simple encoding structure, the special structure of IRA codes can be exploited to introduce unequal protection with cycle control for different types of nodes in the factor-graph code representation. We propose a modified bit-filling algorithm that leads to the construction of a set of finite-length IRA codes with low error floors.
Index TermsŌĆöCode design, finite length, irregular repeataccumulate (IRA) codes, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, performance analysis.
I. INTRODUCTION
L OW-DENSITY parity-check (LDPC) codes were first discovered by Gallager in 1962 [1]. Since the discovery of turbo codes in 1993 [2] and the rediscovery of LDPC codes in 1997 [3], there has been renewed interest in the research of LDPC codes. While turbo codes have been adopted in third-generation cellular standards, LDPC codes are under scrutiny for potential applications in fourth-generation cellular standards. Overall, both turbo codes and LDPC codes belong to the class of random linear error-correction codes, whose performance improves as the code length becomes very large; that is, both turbo codes and LDPC codes can asymptotically approach the Shannon capacity limit. However, LDPC codes become more attractive primarily due to parallelizable decoding and more tractable code design/optimization methodologies that are based on the Tanner graph (also known as the bipartite graph or factor graph) representation of LDPC codes [4]. A remarkable example is the design of large-code-length capacityapproaching LDPC codes by optimizing the ŌĆ£degree profileŌĆØ of an LDPC factor graph through density evolution [5]. With the parallelizable decoding, LDPC codes can potentially achieve high decoder throughput and low latency without increasing computational complexity and memory size. It has been shown that LDPC codes can easily achieve over 100-Mb/s throughput due to parallel operation, while the throughput of turbo codes

is less than 10 Mb/s [6]. Although it is also possible to parallelize the decoder of turbo codes, the resulting decoding complexity becomes very high in order to achieve the same decoding throughput as that of LDPC codes [7]. Due to their attractive features, LDPC codes have been included in IEEE 802.16 [8].
Although LDPC codes exhibit impressive capacityapproaching performance for very long code lengths, such long code lengths are not appropriate for many practical bandwidthefficient communication applications. In this paper, we are concerned with the analysis and construction of short-tomoderate-length LDPC codes, which are of particular interest in delay-sensitive wireless communications. The asymptotic performance of LDPC codes (as the code length goes to infinity) can be obtained from density evolution [9], [10]. Less is known about the performance of finite-length LDPC codes. In [11], the finite analysis of LDPC codes is approached by solving a combinatorial problem for some simple regular ensembles over the binary erasure channels under iterative decoding. In [12], it is shown that the performance of iteratively decoded finite-length LDPC codes in the so-called ŌĆ£waterfall regionŌĆØ follows a basic scaling law. In this paper, we present the performance analysis of finite-length LDPC codes based on the Gaussian approximation [13] of extrinsic information that is exchanged between variable nodes and check nodes.
On the other hand, for infinite-length LDPC codes, once we have obtained the optimized degree profiles, we can randomly construct the parity-check matrix. According to the theorem of concentration around the ensemble average and the theorem of convergence to cycle-free ensemble average [5], such randomly constructed LDPC codes are guaranteed to have vanishing probability of error above the threshold when its code length goes to infinity. Early work on LDPC code design [1], [3], [14], [15] focused on codes with very large block lengths. However, practical systems employ finite-length LDPC codes, with block lengths ranging from several hundred bits to several thousand bits. Completely randomly generated finite-length LDPC codes are prone to error floors. The primary issue in designing finitelength LDPC codes is to achieve better tradeoff between lower decoding thresholds at low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and lower error floors at high SNR. Recently devised finite-length LDPC code-construction methods generally fall into two main categories: 1) random construction with cycle control, such as [16]ŌĆō[19], and 2) construction of structured LDPC codes using algebraic and combinatorial technologies as those in [20]ŌĆō[23]. In this paper, we consider the random construction of a special class of LDPC codesŌĆösystematic irregular repeat-accumulate (IRA) codes, with unequal protection and cycle control for different nodes. We introduce a new parameter called differential girth to achieve unequal protection. We then propose a modified bit-filling algorithm for the code construction.

A. LDPC Codes

Denote N , K, and M as the length of the codeword, infor-

mation sequence, and parity constraints, respectively. An LDPC

code is a linear block code that is specified by an M ├Ś N very

sparse parity-check matrix H. The parity-check matrix can also

be represented by a bipartite graph, which consists of two types

of nodes, namely: 1) variable nodes and 2) check codes. Each

code bit is a variable node, while each parity check or each

row of the parity-check matrix represents a check node. An

edge in the graph is placed between variable node i and check

node j if Hj,i = 1; no parallel edges are allowed between a

particular pair of variable node and check node. Each check

node is connected to code bits whose sum modulo 2 should be

zero. Irregular LDPC codes are specified by two polynomials

The extrinsic information evolution chart consists of the curves of the means of the extrinsic messages as the input and output from the variable and check-node decoding. Fig. 1 shows the extrinsic information evolution chart for several different rate-(1/2) regular LDPC codes. The solid line represents the extrinsic mean output mu from the check nodes versus input mv. The dashed line represents the extrinsic mean output mv from the variable nodes versus input mu. IV. DESIGN OF FINITE-LENGTH LDPC CODES
A. Protecting Degree-2 Variable Nodes

The essential idea of the finite-length IRA code design that is considered here is to protect the degree-2 variable nodes, which is similar to [19] and [30]. We propose a different approach. In iterative decoding, the degree-2 variable nodes only connect to two check nodes, which means degree-2 variable nodes have limited extrinsic information exchange with the rest of the bipartite graph compared to the variable nodes with higher degrees. We hence are motivated to give degree-2 variable nodes more protection. On the other hand, the existence of degree-2 variable nodes is very critical for better performance at low SNRs. Balancing the pros and cons of degree-2 variable nodes is a key issue in constructing finite-length LDPC codes to have lower error floors at high SNRs and smaller decoding threshold at low SNRs.2 We propose heuristic principles in random construction of IRA parity-check matrices H.

The goal of finite-length LDPC code construction (or IRA code construction, as we only focus on this special type of LDPC codes) is to achieve good performance at both low-SNR and high-SNR regimes. It is well known that strong codes, i.e., codes with larger dmin, perform better at high SNRs but may perform worse than weak codes at low SNRs. On the other hand, typically, an LDPC code or turbo code performs well at low SNRs but may suffer from error floors at high SNRs. One way of solving this problem is to introduce a concatenated code but that will introduce extra decoding delay. Here, we focus on the design of good short-length LDPC codes without resorting to code concatenation. In addition to possessing lower error floors while preserving low decoding thresholds, a more interesting question is identifying and imposing efficient controls on some key parameters to obtain a better performance tradeoff between low and high SNRs.

B. Error Floor of Finite-Length LDPC Codes
The effect of error floor can be roughly described in a logŌĆōlog scale plot of BER/FER decoding performance versus SNR; the curves become flatter at high SNRs. The performance of LDPC codes depends on the distance spectrum of the LDPC codes if using maximum likelihood (ML) decoding. However, ML decoding cannot be implemented in practice due to prohibitive complexity. The performance of the iterative decoder depends on the properties of the parity-check matrix (the factor graph) as well as the properties of the LDPC codes. In general, there are three possible types of errors that may contribute to the LDPC error floor of iterative decoding at high SNRs: 1) Decoder converges to an invalid word: This is a detectable error. 2) Decoder converges to a valid but wrong codeword: This corresponds to undetectable errors. 3) Decoder does not converge: The decoder never converges to an equilibrium, which happens almost exclusively at low SNRs. V. CONCLUSION
We have considered the performance analysis and code construction of the LDPC codes of short-to-moderate length (500 Ōēż N Ōēż 10 000). First, by incorporating the finite-length effects into the extrinsic information evolution chart analysis, we have presented a new framework for the iterative decoding performance analysis of both regular and irregular finitelength LDPC codes. Next, by focusing on a special class of LDPC codes, i.e., systematic IRA codes, we have proposed a design procedure to construct good finite-length LDPC codes. In addition to giving rise to simple encoding structure, the special structure of IRA codes can be exploited to introduce unequal protection with different levels of cycle control for different types of nodes in the factor-graph representation of IRA codes. We have proposed a modified bit-filling algorithm that leads to the construction of a set of finite-length IRA codes with flexible tradeoff of low decoding thresholds and low error floors.
