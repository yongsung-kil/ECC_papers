Performance of Iterative Algebraic Decoding of Codes Defined on Graphs: An Initial Investigation1

Xiangyu Tang Department of Electrical and Computer Eng. University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign
Email: xtang2@uiuc.edu

Ralf Koetter Institute for Communications Eng. Technische Universitaet Muenchen
Email: ralf.koetter@tum.de

Abstract— We investigate iterative algebraic decoding of codes defined on graphs. Practical codes that can achieve the Shannon limit are codes defined on graphs with its associated iterative decoding algorithms. Yet, the complexity of these algorithms is high, especially for long codes that attain capacity. For the foreseeable future, algebraic decoding will still be a standard in industry. We aim to harness the power of iterative processes and the low complexity of algebraic techniques by decoding codes on graphs using iterative algebraic techniques. We study the performance of codes on graphs decoded with this method taking into account the case of undetected errors. More specifically, we examine the threshold under which decoding is successful for the BEC and the BSC and compare these with that of certain LDPC codes. Our initial investigation shows that although a significant loss of performance is incurred on the BEC when compared to belief propagation decoding, for transmission over the BSC the threshold value is somewhat close to belief propagation. Especially for high rate codes iterative algebraic decoding could have good performance while maintaining low decoding complexity.
I. INTRODUCTION
The determination of the capacity of memoryless channels 50 years ago by Shannon spurred intense efforts for the search of capacity-approach coding schemes. The result of that search produced several classes of codes that can approach the Shannon limit. Practical codes of this nature are all concatenated codes defined on graphs with associated iterative decoding schemes. Codes on graphs consist of component codes that are connected by a graph. The code itself does not require any decoding computation, but the component codes use simple, localized decoding. The power of iterative processing is well demonstrated by these capacity achieving codes. Yet, the space complexity of iterative decoding algorithms is high, especially for long codes that approach capacity. For storage applications where the throughput is 1Gbit/sec or optical transmission where the throughput is 50Gbit/sec, the implementation of highly complex iterative processes is difficult. Currently, such systems use Reed-Solomon product codes. Future generations of such system use optical-electrical conversion and thus soft information is unavailable. Hard-decision decoding is therefore the only choice. For the foreseeable future, algebraic hard-decision decoding techniques will still be a standard in industry. The advantage of algebraic decoding lies in the fact that it can correct a given number of errors in a codeword fast with low complexity. This advantage is especially pronounced in storage and communication systems where long codes are increasingly being used.

We aim to harness the power of iterative processing and the low complexity of algebraic techniques by performing algebraic decoding in an iterative fashion. We can reduce the complexity of the decoder so that high quality codes can be decoded effectively by a process whose complexity grows very slowly with increasing code length. Low complexity decoding is achieved by decomposing the decoding process into an ensemble of partial independent decoding processes that can be executed in an iterative fashion. Often, the gain in simplicity achieved by decomposing the decoding process results in preventing the code from achieving the best decoding capability known to be possible. Modifications to the code and the iterative decoding process can improve the performance of the code at the cost of increasing complexity. The art is to strike a balance between simplicity and decoding capability.
Iterative algebraic techniques are not new. In particular, iterative algebraic decoding is employed extensively in product codes. Most recent research in the decoding of product codes focuses on the soft-decision decoding of its component codes. This interest is sparked by the amazing performance of turbo codes, and product codes are regarded as block turbo codes. Reddy et al [1] studied the hard-decision iterative algebraic decoding of product codes in 1972. Chaehag et al and Andersen [2,3] studied ordered hard-decision algebraic decoding on the component codes of product codes. Kousa [4] looked at decoding failures and correction capabilities of single parity-check (SPC) product codes. Recently, the high performance of LDPC codes has attracted attention to a class of codes called the generalized low-density (GLD) or Tanner codes [5]. GLD codes are a generalization of LDPC codes in that the single parity check code at the check nodes are replaced by a more complex linear block code. Most studies on GLD codes focus on the SISO decoding technique similar to those used in LDPC codes [6,7,8]. Some studies focus on the hard-decision decoding of the GLD codes that are defined on regular bipartite graphs and allow only one type of component code [5,9-12]. Current academic research does little in the study of codes on graphs with non-uniform redundancy or algebraic decoding of codes defined on irregular graphs, which are shown to be more powerful in the study of LDPC codes.
In this paper, we examine iterative algebraic decoding of codes defined on irregular graphs. We concentrate on large graphs with long component codes. We take into consideration cases of undetected errors, which have not been considered in previous works. Cases of undetected errors are especially important when using BCH or Hamming codes as component codes since its probability of occurrence cannot be ignored.

II. DEFINING CODE ON GRAPHS
We define codes using bipartite graphs. We designate the variable node partition as the left partition and the check node partition as the right partition. For an irregular bipartite graph, an edge has degree i on the left (right) if it is connected on the left (right) to a node with degree i. Suppose the irregular bipartite graph has maximum left degree dl and maximum right degree dr. Following the notation in [13], we describe our irregular graph by sequences λ = (λ1, λ2, ..., λdl ) and ρ = (ρ1, ρ2, ..., ρdr ), where λi (ρi) is the fraction of edges with left (right) degree i. Moreover, we define ρ(x) := i ρixi−1 and λ(x) := i λixi−1.
A code on graph, C, is composed of component codes which are represented by a bipartite graph with the variable nodes denoting the symbols and the check node denoting the constraints the symbols must satisfy. The overcall code C = C(n, λ, ρ, ϕ) is constructed by requiring that the component codes share common symbol nodes. An example is shown in Figure 1. Let n denote the number of variable node in the graph, let m denote the number of check nodes or component code present the graph, and let E denote the total number of edges in the graph. Let L denote the total number of type of {c}iL=1 = [ni, ki, di]iL=1 component code in the overall code. The code is also described by the sequence ϕ = (ϕ1, ϕ2, ..., ϕL) where ϕi is the fraction of edges in the graph that is connected to a ci = [ni, ki, di] component code.
The overall code is decoded by decoding the components codes in an iterative fashion. The component codes are decoded using sub-optimal algebraic techniques. We study the performance of codes defined on graphs by examining the threshold of the probability of symbol error below which the code can successfully decode for the binary symmetric and erasure channels. We compare these thresholds to that of certain LDPC codes with the same rate.

Iterative Step: 1. Find a check node, c, that is marked “unprocessed” and is
reported to have errors less than its error correcting capability. 2. Decode c using bounded distance algebraic decoding and
send the resulting values to its associated variable nodes. 3. Mark check node c “processed”. 4. If the variable node is marked “unprocessed”, accept the
value from the check node and mark “processed”. Else, do not change the value of the variable node. Output: After a predetermined number of steps or when step 1 of the Iterative Step fails, output the codeword with the corrections.
Conceptually, at each step, we correct a check node that has errors less than its decoding capability. Occasionally, we process a check node that has undetected errors, and this erroneous decoding could result in new errors introduced in the graph. The decoding process stops when all variable nodes are corrected or when no check nodes can be found with errors less than its decoding capability. Note that in our analysis we have taken into consideration undetected errors. The case of undetected errors is especially important when BCH or Hamming codes are used as component codes as the probability of undetected error is quite high.
Because an algebraic bounded distance decoder can produce undetected error, when algebraic decoding is used on a check node, c, the check node could report that all errors are corrected when in actuality errors remain in its variable nodes. However, other check nodes could correct some of the shared variable nodes with check node c causing it again to report that it has errors. When enough of check node c’s variable nodes are corrected, c can proceed to correctly decode its remaining variable nodes. This self correcting process requires check nodes to be processed multiple times. However, processing check nodes and changing variable nodes more than once cause dependencies between the steps of the iterative decoding process, thus making the decoding process difficult to analysis. For this reason, and for analysis purposes we only process a check node and variable node once in the iterative decoding process. We note however, that in practice the check nodes should be allowed to process as many times as necessary in a predetermined order and this would improve the performance of the code. Furthermore, to simplify the analysis, we only consider graphs with no 4-cycles.
