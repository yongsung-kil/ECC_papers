A Differential Binary Message-Passing LDPC Decoder
Nastaran Mobini, Amir H. Banihashemi and Saied Hemati Department of Systems and Computer Engineering, Carleton University
1125 Colonel By Drive, Ottawa, ON, K1S 5B6 Canada

Abstract - In this paper, we propose a binary message-passing algorithm for decoding low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. The algorithm substantially improves the performance of purely hard-decision iterative algorithms with a small increase in the memory requirements and the computational complexity. We associate a reliability value to each nonzero element of the code’s parity-check matrix, and differentially modify this value in each iteration based on the sum of the extrinsic binary messages from the check nodes. For the tested random and finite-geometry LDPC codes, the proposed algorithm can achieve performance as close as 1.3 dB and 0.7 dB to that of belief propagation (BP) at the error rates of interest, respectively. This is while, unlike BP, the algorithm does not require the estimation of channel signal to noise ratio. Low memory and computational requirements and binary message-passing make the proposed algorithm attractive for high-speed low-power applications.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check codes [5] have been studied extensively in recent years. They perform very well when decoded with iterative decoding algorithms also known as message-passing algorithms [10]. Iterative decoding algorithms have relatively low complexity due to the low density of the parity-check matrix and the associated Tanner graph [11] of the code. An iterative decoding algorithm is identified by its message alphabet A, the operations performed in the variable nodes and the check nodes of the Tanner graph, and the maximum number of iterations Imax.
The message alphabet can be as simple as binary, e.g., A={+1,-1}, considered in this work, or can consist of infinite uncountable number of symbols such as the set of real numbers. If A is binary, the algorithm is referred to as harddecision; otherwise, it is called soft-decision. The computational complexity of iterative algorithms usually increases with the alphabet size of messages.
A proper choice of an LDPC code and its decoding algorithm depends on the application and is mainly driven by the performance/complexity considerations. From the implementation point of view, hard-decision algorithms are particularly attractive due to their low complexity, high speed and low power consumption in hardware. Examples of harddecision algorithms are the so-called Gallager algorithms A (GA) and B (GB) [5], [10], and majority-based (MB) algorithms [12]. The downside of hard-decision algorithms however is their relatively poor performance that can be as far away as a few decibels from soft-decision algorithms. At the other end of the performance/complexity spectrum are belief

propagation (BP) [10], [11], and its variants [4]. These algorithms have very good performance but are highly complex. Approximations of BP, most notably min-sum (MS) and its modifications [3], [14], hybrid soft-hard algorithms [7], [9], weighted bit-flipping algorithms and their variants [6], [7], [13] are in between the two extremes, each striking a different balance between the performance and the complexity.
The main idea of this paper is to combine the simplicity of binary message-passing with the good performance of softdecision algorithms. Binary message-passing is particularly attractive for VLSI implementations as it translates to a smaller number of wires between the variable nodes and the check nodes. This in turn simplifies the routing of the wires and can significantly reduce the area and the power consumption of the decoder [1]. To achieve the good performance with the binary message-passing, we introduce memory elements at the variable side of the Tanner graph, one for each edge, to store reliability information which is initialized by the received values at the output of the channel and is then updated differentially at each iteration based on the binary extrinsic messages of the check nodes. The check node operation is a simple exclusive OR (XOR) similar to that of MB algorithms. The variable node operation is only slightly more complex than that of MB algorithms and involves additions of binary and integer numbers. The messages sent from the variable nodes to the check nodes are also binary and represent the sign of the soft information stored in the memory elements. We refer to the proposed algorithm as “differential decoding with binary message passing,” or DD-BMP, in short. Compared to purely hard-decision algorithms, the proposed scheme provides improvements of up to about 3 dB and 1.5 dB at bit error rates of about 10-5 for the tested randomly constructed and finite-geometry (FG) codes, respectively. These results are as close as about 1.3 dB and 0.7 dB to the performance of the floating-point implementation of BP for the two categories of codes, respectively.
The reminder of this paper is organized as follows. In section II, the proposed algorithm is presented in detail and its complexity is studied and compared with other algorithms. The simulations results for variety of codes are reported in Section III. Finally, concluding remarks are presented in Section IV.

III. SIMULATION RESULTS AND DISCUSSIONS
To study the performance of the DD-BMP algorithm, we use Monte Carlo simulations. The application of analytical tools such as density evolution [10] and EXIT charts [2] to this problem is not straightforward due to the existence of memory in the algorithm. Using simulations, we have tested a large number of codes with different rates, block lengths and degree distributions. We have also tested both randomly constructed and finite-geometry (FG) codes. In the following, we report our results for four codes: two randomly constructed and two FG codes. The randomly constructed codes are taken from [8] and in the order of increasing rate have parameters (n, k )(dv ,dc ) = (3000,1000)(4,6) and (1057,813)(3,13). These codes are labeled by C1 and C2, respectively. The FG codes have parameters (273,191)(13,13) and (1023,781)(32,32) and are labeled by C3 and C4, respectively. For all the simulations the maximum number of iterations is set to 100 and for each simulation point, 100 code word errors are found.
Our simulation results show that the optimal quantization step ∆∗ , which minimizes the BER, is rather independent of the SNR, and changes only slightly with the number of quantization bits q for larger values of q. This is demonstrated for C1 in Fig. 1, where the BER is plotted vs. ∆ for different values of q at SNR values 4 dB and 4.25 dB. For C1 – C4, ∆∗ is about 0.08, 0.22, 0.04 and 0.01, respectively. Fig. 1 also shows that by increasing q, the performance improves until it reaches a saturation point beyond which the improvement in performance is very small. For C1 – C3, we select q=6, while for C4, q=8 is selected. These values appear to strike the right balance between the performance and the complexity.
The BER curves of DD-BMP are given in Figures 2 – 5, for C1 – C4, respectively. In the figures, we have also given the performance curves for BP, MS and MB algorithms for reference. For each code, the MB algorithm with the best performance is selected. For the degree distributions of C1 and C2, GA has the best performance (threshold) among all the MB algorithms [12]. For C3 and C4, MB3 and MB5 are used, respectively. The results show that for all the codes the performance of DD-BMP is substantially better than that of

MB algorithms. For random codes, the performance gap is as large as about 3 dB at higher SNR values, while this gap is reduced to up to about 1.5 dB for the FG codes. In particular for C1, the GA algorithm demonstrates an early error floor while DD-BMP does not. Similar trends are observed for the word error rate curves.
Compared to soft-decision algorithms, the results show that for the random codes, the performance of DD-BMP is within 1.9 dB of the BP performance, with smaller gaps for C1. For the FG codes, the performance gap between DD-BMP and BP is much smaller, and is about 0.7 dB at high SNR values. The performance of DD-BMP for random code C1 is only about 0.4 dB inferior to that of MS. While for C2, the gap is larger and is about 1.9 dB, since for this code; BP and MS have almost identical performance for large values of SNR. For FG codes, however, the performance of DD-BMP is superior to that of MS by about 0.2 dB and 0.5 dB, for C3 and C4, respectively.
The average number of iterations vs. BER for BP, MS, DDBMP and MB algorithms are given in Figures 6-9 for C1-C4, respectively. These figures show that the average number of iterations for DD-BMP is in general larger than those of the other algorithms. The difference however is smaller, and in the cases of C2 and C3 is negligible, for lower BER values.
WBF algorithms and their variants and modifications [6], [7], [13] have proved to perform well for decoding FG codes. The best performing algorithm in this category is the improved modified WBF (IMWBF) algorithm of [6]. The performance of this algorithm is given in Figures 4 and 5 for C3 and C4, respectively. As can be seen, IMWBF slightly outperforms DD-BMP by about 0.3 dB and 0.15 dB for C3 and C4, respectively. This improvement in performance however is counter-balanced by the much larger average number of iterations as illustrated in Figures 8 and 9, for C3 and C4, respectively. It is also important to note that all the stored and computed values in IMWBF are floating-point real numbers. One would expect the performance to deteriorate for a fixedpoint implementation of IMWBF. For random codes, DDBMP usually outperforms IMWBF handily. From the computational complexity point of view, IMWBF requires about 2E real comparisons, E real additions and N real multiplications for preprocessing before each iterative decoding to compute the reliability information of check nodes and the initial values of the flipping functions. During each iteration, IMWBF needs about dvdc real additions and N real comparisons. It also requires 2M real memory elements to store the reliability information of the check nodes.
A variant of DD-BMP is to assign one memory to each variable node instead of each edge in the graph and update its content per iteration based on the binary messages of the check nodes. The outgoing messages from a variable node are all identical and are the sign of the memory content. This algorithm is simpler and requires less memory but the performance for random codes is considerably worse than that of the original algorithm. IV. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we propose a binary message-passing iterative algorithm for decoding LDPC codes. The algorithm incorporates soft information by storing the quantized received values and differentially updating them in each iteration based on the extrinsic binary messages from the check nodes. While the binary message-passing is particularly attractive for its low complexity and simple implementation (translating to lower area and power consumption in VLSI chips), the incorporation of soft information provides more coding gain and improves the performance. The results for a large number of tested codes indicate that the proposed algorithm provides an attractive solution in terms of performance/complexity tradeoff for a wide range of rates for both random and FG LDPC codes. This tradeoff is between those of the two extremes of purely hard and purely soft decision algorithms.
