A Scalable Decoder Architecture for IEEE 802.11n LDPC Codes
Massimo Rovini, Giuseppe Gentile, Francesco Rossi and Luca Fanucci Department of Information Engineering, University of Pisa Via G. Caruso, I-56122 Pisa, Italy
e-mail: {massimo.rovini, giuseppe.gentile, francesco.rossi, luca.fanucci}@iet.unipi.it

Abstract—This paper describes a scalable IP of a decoder for LDPC codes compliant to IEEE 802.11n and running the wellknown layered decoding algorithm. The decoder architecture is arranged in clusters of serial processing units, which are configurable to process all the codes in the standard and, at the same time, to support multiple frame decoding. An optimization methodology of the iteration latency is also described, which relates to the order of the messages updated by the processors, as well as to the sequence of layers the decoder goes through. The logic synthesis on 65 nm CMOS technology with lowpower standard-cell library, shows that the proposed design is suitable for portable devices, the throughput ranging from 180 to 410 Mbps, and the power consumption being below 235 mW.
I. INTRODUCTION
The ever-increasing demand of data-hungry mobile communications has fostered the deployment of high-throughput wireless local area networks (WLAN), desirably reaching datarates up to 100 Mbps in short-range links (hundreds of meters). The physical layer interface of WLAN for high-throughput is currently being standardized in IEEE 802.11n, and drafts are already available [1]. In line with the recent trend opened by DVB-S2 and followed by wireless metropolitan area networks (WMAN), WLAN also relies on low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [2] as error-correcting codes.
The renewed interest in LDPC codes is due to their remarkable performance, even at low signal–to–noise ratios (SNR) and with small block-lengths, to the flexibility in the design of the code parameters, and to the relatively simple decoding algorithm, suitable for parallelization. Structured, or architecture-aware (AA) codes [3], further reduce the area and power consumption of the related decoder, improve its memory efficiency, and, above all, offer increased decoder scalability, allowing the full exploration of the complexity/throughput design trade-offs. Furthermore, AA-codes perform very close to random codes. Still in [3], the turbo-decoding messagepassing (TDMP) algorithm is described, in which a code is decoded as the sequence of several super-codes, or layers, thus performing the so-called layered decoding [4].
In this paper we consider the design of a scalable architecture for a layered LDPC decoder compliant to IEEE 802.11n. For mobile devices, with strict restrictions on area and power consumption, a block-serial implementation that serially processes blocks of parity-check constraints, is preferable to fully parallel architectures [5]; moreover, serial processing units are very flexible in the support of different codes. As a

drawback, they increase the decoding latency, thereby limiting the throughput, and are then typically operated in pipeline [6], [7]. However, in a pipelined layered decoder, hazards may arise in the memory access, and the dependence between layers must be carefully handled; as an improvement of [6] and [7], this paper proposes a decoder architecture and an efficient schedule of the decoding messages to counteract these issues.

II. LDPC DECODING BASICS

Decoding of LDPC codes is achieved in application of the maximum a posteriori (MAP) algorithm, commonly formulated in the form of belief propagation (BP). BP is optimal if the graph of the code does not contain cycles, but it is still used and considered as a reference for practical codes with cycles. In this case, the sequence of the elaborations, known as schedule, highly affects performances in terms of convergence speed and (although less) error correction.
Recently, BP has been re-formulated in the so-called shuffled or layered decoding [3], [4], [8], [9]. Layered decoding is preferable to the original flooding schedule (FS) [9], because of the increased convergence speed, which is almost double, both for codes with cycles and cycle-free [10]. This is achieved through the early propagation of intermediate, a posteriori messages, made available to the next layers immediately after their computation, and not only at next iteration as in FS.
Layers can be organized by either check-nodes (CNs) or variable-nodes (VNs), and, accordingly, CN-centric (horizontal) or VN-centric (vertical) decoding algorithms are defined in [8] and [9]. However, CN-centric solutions are more desirable since they allow serial, flexible and simple, architectures of the CN processor. III. AA-LDPC CODES FOR IEEE 802.11N
Three codeword lengths, N0 = 648, N1 = 1296 and N2 = 1944 are defined in [1], referred to as short, middle and long codeword in this paper, and for each length, four code rates are specified, 1/2, 2/3, 3/4 and 5/6, for a total of 12 different codes. These are AA-LDPC codes, block-wise partitioned into smaller Bi × Bi sub-matrices, with B0 = 27, B1 = 54 and B2 = 81 for the short, middle and long codeword, respectively.
Particularly, the parity-check matrix H is arranged in n = Ni/Bi = 24 block columns and mi = (1 − r) · n block rows, with r the code rate, and each sub-matrix is either a rotation of the identity matrix or an all-zero matrix. This allows the very compact description of the parity-check matrix, known as prototype matrix, which only specifies locations and rotations of the non-zero sub-matrices, or blocks. IV. DECODING PIPELINING & IDLING
To work properly, practical implementations of HLD must respect dependence between layers, and always use the most updated total information. However, because the elaborations of a serial check-node processor, or simply node-processor (NP), are typically pipelined, one SO could be accessed before being updated, thus corrupting performances. The arising pipeline hazard can be solved with the insertion of null or idle cycles between two consecutive layers [7], where the NP is idled to wait for updated inputs. In opposition to this route, re-formulations of HLD are also viable, as shown in [6] and [13], but extra complexity is brought into the design.
In this work, we propose an efficient method to arrange the order of the messages in a layer, and accordingly, the sequence of layers the decoder goes through, in order to minimize the overall number of idle cycles, and then, the decoding latency.

V. DECODER ARCHITECTURE
Aiming at high-throughput, decoding operations are vectorized thanks to the use of structured codes, which allow the parallel update of at most Bi parity-check equations without memory collisions. Higher parallelism is also viable, but it is shown to perform sub-optimally in [13]. Therefore, a set of Bi consecutive rows is always decoded in parallel, and this will be referred to as layer in the remaining of this paper. However, since B0 = B1/2 = B2/3 = 27, NPs can be conveniently arranged in clusters of B0 units each, so that a multi-length decoder needs one/two/three clusters to process the short/middle/long codeword, respectively.

In the case of middle (B1 = 2B0) or long (B2 = 3B0) codeword, data-paths are merged in pairs or triplets, respectively, and are twisted in the multi-size circular-shifting (MSCS) network. This constrains K to be at least 3 to get full compliance with IEEE 802.11n. For the same reason, memories are tailored to the worst-case code, and one single c2v memory bank must store up to Q · B0 data, with Q = 88 the maximum number of non-null sub-matrices among the 12 codes. On the other hand, one SO memory bank must always store n · B0 = 648 data.
To boost the throughput, the architecture of Fig. 3 is also capable of serving several frames in parallel, in the number of Fi(K) = K · 27/Bi . However, only for K a multiple of 6, all HW resources are exploited for every code; otherwise, |K|(Bi/B0) data-paths remain unused.
A. Pipelined Processor Architecture with ROP
The architecture of the serial NP is shown in Fig. 4. Idle cycles are inserted with two synchronization strobes, named start and stop, and signalling the first and the last message of one layer: upon reception of stop, the NP suspends the acquisition of new data until the next start is asserted.
The CN core implements the 2-output algorithm [12] and it works in pipeline with the acquisition of input data; the two computed magnitudes are used along with the updated signs and the knowledge of the index of the minimum input to update the c2v messages. Finally, SOs are computed by adding the output c2v to the input v2c messages.
The pipeline of the elaborations is also allowed by the presence of the buffer of v2c messages, which is a multi-length shift-register with size the maximum CN degree, dc,max = 22, and ruled with a last-in first-out (LIFO) policy to reverse the output data for the ROP strategy.

VII. CONCLUSION
This paper has described the design of a semi-parallel, scalable, LDPC decoder IP for IEEE 802.11n. The decoder implements the horizontal layered decoding algorithm and idle cycles were used as a means to get independence among layers, and clear pipeline hazards; then, to minimize their number, the reversed output processor was introduced and the layers scrambled for optimal performance: in this way, only from 0% to 14.5% of the iteration time is spent in idling.

As a result of the careful exploration of the throughput/ complexity/error-correction trade-offs, a very efficient decoder configuration has been proposed, using K = 4 clusters of serial processing units, with c2v messages on 5 bits and SOs on 8 bits. While the IL is only about 0.17 dB at FER = 10−4, the useful throughput ranges from 178 to 410 Mbps, and is well balanced over the different codeword lengths. This, along with the low power consumption, makes the proposed design very attractive for mobile applications.
