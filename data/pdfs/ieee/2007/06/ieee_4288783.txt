A Modified Bit-Flipping Decoding Algorithm for Low-Density Parity-Check Codes

T.M.N. Ngatched, F. Takawira School of Electrical, Electronic and Computer Engineering
University of KwaZulu-Natal Durban 4041, South Africa Email: ngatchedt@ukzn.ac.za, ftakaw@ukzn.ac.za

M. Bossert Department of T.A.I.T
University of Ulm Albert-Einstein-Allee 43, 89081 Ulm, Germany
Email: martin.bossert@e-technik.uni-ulm.de

Abstract― In this paper, a modified bit-flipping decoding algorithm for low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes is proposed. Both improvement in performance and reduction in decoding delay are observed by flipping multiple bits in each iteration. Our studies show that the proposed algorithm achieves an appealing tradeoff between performance and complexity for many constructions of LDPC codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, originally introduced by Gallager [1], and brought into prominence by MacKay and Neal [2], have been attracting a great deal of research interest. The interest in these codes stems from their near Shannon limit performance, their simple descriptions and implementations, and their amenability to rigorous theoretical analysis. LDPC codes can be decoded with various decoding methods, ranging from low to high complexity and from reasonably good to very good performance. These decoding methods include hard-decision ([1], [3]), soft-decision ([1], [4]-[8]), and hybrid decoding ([9]-[16]) schemes.
The algorithm in [15], which we call improved weighted bit flipping (IWBF) was shown to offer one of the most appealing performance versus cost tradeoffs. Though it was designed for high-rate finite geometry (FG) LDPC codes [10], we noticed that it offers good performance for any LDPC code whose parity-check matrix has reasonably large column weights. FGLDPC codes are algebraically generated and allow effective, low-cost, hardware encoder implementations. In general, an LDPC code whose parity-check matrix has relatively large column weights, with IWBF, performs less than 1 dB away from its performance with iterative decoding based on belief propagation (IDBP). IWBF uses the fact that the codewords of an LDPC code are sparsely distributed in an N -dimensional space over GF (2), where N is the code length. Thus, in almost all cases the decoding leads to either a correct codeword or it cannot find any codeword.
In this paper, a modification of IWBF is proposed. The modified algorithm, which we called multiple bit-flipping (MBF), updates multiple bits in each iteration. The modification is based on the observation that, for low-density parity-check matrices which satisfy the row-column (RC) constraint (the RC constraint is the requirement that no two rows or two columns of a matrix have more than 1-component

in common), the syndrome weight increases with the number of errors on average. Therefore, the idea is to use the syndrome weight in each iteration during the decoding process to approximately estimate the number of bits to be flipped. This modification is achieved with a small increase in complexity, but significantly speeds up the decoding process. Surprisingly, it also leads to significant improvement in performance in terms of both the bit-error rate (BER) and frame-error rate (FER).

Some simulation results are provided in Section IV to illustrate the performance of the modified algorithm and, finally, conclusions are drawn in Section V.

IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
The error performance, in terms of both the bit-error rate (BER) and frame-error rate (FER) as a function of the ratenormalized signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), of the modified algorithm presented in the previous section is compared with the standard IWBF algorithm on a number of LDPC codes. However, the results are only presented for two FG-LDPC codes [10]. The performance of the codes using the IDBP algorithm is also included. The maximum number of iterations is set equal to 200 for all algorithms. At each SNR value, at least 100 codewords errors are detected. The first code is the Type-I 2-D (1023, 781) Euclidean geometry (EG)-LDPC code with rate 0.763, and the second code is the Type-I 2-D (4095, 3367) EG-LDPC code with rate 0.822 and minimum distance 65. Figs. 1 and 2 show the BER and FER performances of the two codes. It should be noted that, in these two figures, the algorithms are used without the loop detection (LD) mechanism. It can be observed that, in both cases, at the BER of 10-5, the presented MBF outperforms the standard IWBF by about 0.25 dB. The same observation can be made as far as the FER is concerned. To gain an insight into this performance improvement, the algorithmic behavior of the two schemes on blocks, that were incorrectly decoded by the standard algorithm but successfully decoded by the modified one, was investigated. The investigation was carried on many LDPC codes and for various SNR. The following observations were made:
• A good number of decoding failures of the standard algorithm are attributed to infinite-loop traps.
• Infinite-loop traps do not appear as often with the modified algorithm as with the standard one. Thus by flipping multiple bits in each iteration, many infiniteloop traps are avoided.
• Most of the bits flipped in each iteration by the modified algorithm correspond to actual errors. Occasionally new errors are introduced, but these errors are corrected in some later stages of the decoding.
• The number of bits flipped by the modified algorithm decreases as the iteration number increases.
Figs. 3 and 4 show the performance of the two codes when the algorithms are used with the LD mechanism. It can be observed that the effect of the LD mechanism on the two codes is not the same. On the first code, the performance of the standard algorithm with the loop detection mechanism is similar to that of the modified algorithm without the loop detection mechanism. Applying the loop detection mechanism to the modified algorithm provides an additional 0.1 dB gain in terms of both the BER and the FER. On the second code, even with the loop detection mechanism the standard algorithm performs poorer than the modified algorithm without loop detection. On the other hand, the gain achieved by applying the loop detection mechanism to the modified algorithm is not significant (less than 0.1 dB). This

With these performance studies in mind, we move on to the average number of iterations comparison. Figs. 5 and 6 depict the average number of iterations of the standard IWBF algorithm and the proposed MBF algorithm for decoding the two codes respectively. This number is closely related to the BER after decoding, and therefore, it is plotted against the BER after decoding rather than against the SNR. Thus, though the cost per iteration of MBF is higher than that of IWBF, the average number of iterations is drastically reduced. As a matter of fact, the running time of the modified algorithm was found to be about four times less than that of the standard one. This is of paramount practical importance since less decoding delay may be required to achieve satisfactory performance for a given application, thereby allowing for higher data rates. It can also be observed that the improvement in performance obtained by applying the loop detection mechanism to the standard algorithm comes at the cost of not only an increased complexity, but an increased decoding delay as well.
V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, a modification of the IWBF algorithm is proposed, in which multiple bits are updated in each iteration during the decoding process, while only one bit is updated in the standard algorithm. Simulation results show that the modified algorithm results in significant performance improvement in terms of both the BER and FER, and drastically reduces the decoding delay. The modification introduced here can be applied to many bit-flipping algorithms.
