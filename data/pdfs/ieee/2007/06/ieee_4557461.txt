Abstract- A natural relationship between weighted bit-flipping (WBF) decoding and message-passing decoding is explored. This understanding can help us develop a dual WBF decoding algorithm from one type of message-passing decoding algorithm and vice versa. For min-sum decoding, one can find that its dual WBF algorithm is the algorithm proposed by Jiang et al. For belief-propagation (BP) decoding, we propose a new WBF algorithm and show its performance advantage. For some high-rate low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes of large row weight, it is shown that the WBF algorithm proposed by Liu and Pados performs extraordinarily well. However, its dual messagepassing decoding does not work well. Furthermore, we propose a parallel implementation framework for various WBF algorithms. Compared to serial implementations, various WBF algorithms in their parallel form converge significantly faster and often perform better.
I. INTRODUCTION
Iterative decoding of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes has received much attention in recent years. The standard belief-propagation (BP) decoding can achieve excellent performance but often with heavy implementation complexity. Bit-flipping (BF) decoding is simple, but it often incurs considerable performance loss compared to the BP decoding. To bridge the performance gap between BF decoding and BP decoding, weighted BF (WBF) decoding [1] and its variants [2]-[8] were proposed.
Extensive simulations show that for many LDPC codes, including finite-geometry (FG) LDPC codes [1] and Mackay's randomly constructed LDPC codes [9], the improved version of the modified WBF algorithm proposed by Jiang et al, referred to as IMWBF, performs best so far [5]. However, for some high-rate LDPC codes of large row weight [10], the WBF algorithm proposed by Liu and Pados [3] and its improved version [4], referred to as LP-WBF and SZJLP-WBF respectively, perform even better than the IMWBF algorithm.
In the past, various WBF algorithms were proposed heuristically, and one has to resort to simulations for their performance validation. In this paper, we explore the relation between WBF decoding and message-passing decoding. For message-passing decoding, we use this notion throughout this paper for BPlike decoding algorithm, in which all bit and check nodes are activated in the exchange of soft messages. The relation can

help us develop a dual WBF decoding algorithm from one type of message-passing decoding algorithm and vice versa. For min-sum decoding, we find that its dual WBF algorithm is the IMWBF proposed in [5]. For belief-propagation (BP) decoding, we show that its dual WBF algorithm is new, which have performance advantage over the IMWBF algorithm. For some high-rate LDPC codes of large row weight [10], it is shown that the LP-WBF algorithm and its improved version [4] perform extraordinarily well. Unfortunately, its dual message-passing decoding does not work well.
Furthermore, we propose a parallel implementation framework for various WBF algorithms. Compared to serial implementations, various WBF algorithms in their parallel form converge significantly faster and often perform better.

III. PARALLEL WEIGHTED BIT-FLIPPING DECODING
A. Motivation
So far, various reported WBF algorithms suffer from slow convergence, mainly due to their serial flipping strategy.
To accelerate the rate of convergence, multiple bits should be allowed for flipping in each iteration. One may borrow the idea from [13] and allow the algorithm to flip a set of bits,
say T, for which En, n e F is larger than a predetermined
threshold. However, simulations show that this scheme does not work well. The main shortcoming of this scheme is that it may flip multiple bits subject to a common unsatisfied constraint.
For a given unsatisfied check node, the constraint becomes satisfied if one of the variables subject to it is flipped. However, this may not be the case if one flips multiple variables subject to a common unsatisfied check node. In particular, the unsatisfied constraint remains if one flips two variables subject to this common constraint.
We propose a method to overcome this main shortcoming [14]. Our idea can be stated as follows. Each unsatisfied check node sends only one "flip" signal to the most unreliable variable node subject to it. Then, each variable node accumulates the "flip" signals from all possible constraints to form a decision metric, and it is flipped once the decision metric Fn >
Fth (k), where the thresholds Fth(k), k = 1, 2, , Imax are
positive integers to be optimized and generally depend on the iteration counter k. Note that Imax denotes the maximum number of iterations.
