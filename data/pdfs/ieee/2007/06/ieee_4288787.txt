An Efficient Analysis of Finite-Length LDPC Codes
Raman Yazdani, Masoud Ardakani Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering
University of Alberta Edmonton, Alberta T6G 2V4, Canada Email: {yazdani, ardakani}@ece.ualberta.ca

Abstract— An efficient method for finite-length low-density parity-check (LDPC) code analysis is proposed. This method is based on studying the channel variations when observed during a finite-length codeword. To this end, channel parameters are interpreted as random variables and their distributions are found. Assuming that a decoding failure is the result of an observed channel worse than the code’s decoding threshold, the block error probability of finite-length LDPC codes is estimated. Using an extrinsic information transfer chart analysis, bit error probability is obtained from the block error probability. Our results suggest that by considering only the channel variations around its expected behavior and even ignoring the effects of cycles, one can closely predict the performance of LDPC codes of a few thousand bits or longer in the waterfall region.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are one of the most powerful and flexible classes of codes which can perform very close to the capacity on many channels [1], [2]. LDPC codes decoding is done iteratively using message-passing algorithms. In most of the design and analysis methods, it is assumed that the code’s block length is large. The performance of these codes and the accuracy of these methods degrade as the block length gets smaller. In practice, where finite-length codes are used, the infinite-length analysis can be quite inaccurate.
It is fair to say that the asymptotic behavior (when the block length tends to infinity) of LDPC codes is well understood by now. However, very little is known about the finitelength behavior. There is no general method to estimate and analyze the finite-length behavior of LDPC codes on different channels. Conventional methods such as density evolution [1], [3] or Gaussian approximation [4], which are used for asymptotic analysis, can not be used for the finite-length case. The inaccuracy of asymptotic behavior analysis methods and the performance degradation for finite-length LDPC codes are usually blamed on the weaknesses in the factor graph [5] of the code such as cycles and stopping sets.
For the case of binary erasure channel (BEC), a combinatorial analysis has been presented in [6] for regular finitelength ensembles and they have been generalized in [7] for irregular ensembles. These methods give the average performance (block and bit erasure probability) of an ensemble using recursions. Unfortunately, these recursions are computationally complex and even for practical block lengths only very simple ensembles can be analyzed.
In a recent work [8], it is shown that the performance curves in the waterfall region follow a scaling law. This scaling law is

motivated by existing results in statistical physics and is proved for iteratively decoded LDPC ensembles when transmission takes place over the BEC. It is stated that empirically such a scaling seems to be applicable to other channels such as the binary symmetric channel (BSC) and binary-input additive white Gaussian noise (BIAWGN) channel, and that any function of the channel parameter can be used for stating the scaling law. To hold consistency with the BEC, the capacity of the channel C(σ) (σ is the channel parameter) is suggested to be used. The scaling law consists of two extra parameters α and β which should be calculated. The procedure of calculating these two parameters, which is only known for the BEC, could be cumbersome especially for irregular codes [8]. Also, this procedure cannot be used for other channels such as BSC or BIAWGN. For these cases, the parameters are simply fitted to the data.
In this paper, an efficient method for predicting the performance of finite-length LDPC codes is presented. The main idea is based on considering the variations of the channel around its expected behavior. To be more specific, since the realization of noise varies from one codeword to another, one can find the probability that the channel—observed during one codeword—is worse than the decoding threshold of the code. This probability can be used as an estimate of the block error rate. Different channel parameters can be used to find this probability. In this work, we compare two of these parameters and show that very accurate estimates of the performance can be obtained.
A similar idea has been used for obtaining the performance of turbo codes [9]. The main difference is that we model the channel parameters as random variables and we find the distribution of these random variables analytically. Using these distributions, we easily estimate the block error rate of the code.
We present methods for predicting both the block error probability and bit error probability. Our results show that by considering only the channel variations during different codeword transmissions and ignoring the effect of cycles, it is possible to predict the performance of finite-length LDPC codes closely. Since in this method the effects of cycles are ignored, the results become much more accurate as the code length gets large. Our method mainly uses the decoding threshold of the code and the code length N and it is applicable to both regular and irregular codes. Also, no fitting of parameters is required. We consider two different cases based on the effect of code’s finite length on the channel variations. In the first case, channel variations can be described by a single parameter, hence, we call such channels one-dimensional channels. Other channels define the second case: multi-dimensional channels. The BEC and BSC are examples of one-dimensional channels, and the BIAWGN channel is an example of multi-dimensional channels.

B. Block error probability
In this section we present a method to predict block error probability. We discuss the method for multi-dimensional channels. The same method applies to one-dimensional channels.
Following the discussion about the channel variations we represent the channel C(σ) by either the random variable Pobs or Cobs with pdf fPobs (C(σ), N, x) and fCobs (C(σ), N, x) respectively. Also, The cumulative density functions (cdf) are given by Pr(Pobs ≤ x) = FPobs (C(σ), N, x) and Pr(Cobs ≤ x) = FCobs (C(σ), N, x). Notice that the pdf and the cdf are parameterized by the channel, its parameters and the block length of the code.
To calculate the average block error probability of an ensemble of LDPC codes we calculate the probability that the observed channel behaves worse than the code’s decoding threshold. We can use either Pobs or Cobs to calculate the probability that the channel behaves worse than the decoding threshold of the code. For one-dimensional channels, it does not matter what parameter be used to model the channel variations. For multi-dimensional channels, however, the choice of parameter can affect the results.

Example 3 Consider BIAWGN(σ) channel and a rate 1/2 irregular code specified by the degree distribution pair λ(x) = 0.4x2 + 0.4x5 + 0.2x8 and ρ(x) = x8. The decoding threshold of this codes is σ∗ = 0.8461 and we have pth = 0.1186 and cth = 0.5985 and α = 0.083. The code is randomly constructed and any cycle of length 4 is removed.
Fig. 4 shows the performance curves for N = 104 and compares our results with the simulation results. As in the regular case, we see that our prediction method closely approximates the actual performance. Again, Pobs gives more

accurate results. At a bit error rate of 10−5, the gap between the actual result and the best prediction is about 0.035dB.
It is worth mentioning that for highly optimized codes whose EXIT charts are very tight or close at multiple points near the threshold, we will not be able to accurately calculate α and predict bit error probability from the block error probability. C. Discussion
As the results show, by considering the channel variations around its expected behavior, we are able to describe the code’s performance for different lengths. In other words, even by ignoring the presence of cycles in the code’s factor graph, it is possible to predict the performance of LDPC codes of a few

We conjecture that the gap between the prediction and simulation results is mainly due to the cycles in the code’s factor graph. The concentration theorems of [3] show that for large N , the average behavior of individual instances of the code converges to the cycle-free case. As stated in [3], the concentration theorems predict pessimistic values for N and effects of the cycles are milder than predicted. As our results suggest, for even medium length LDPC codes the effect of cycles is negligible in the waterfall region.
While by using Pobs and (9) the performance can be measured quite accurately, Cobs and (10) do not give such accurate results. The error probability predicted by Cobs is less than what is predicted by Pobs. One conclusion here is that the decoder is more sensitive to the number of errors rather than the capacity.
It is also worth mentioning that our investigation shows that usually there are more cases that Cobs ≥ cth and Pobs > pth than the cases that Pobs ≤ pth and Cobs < cth. In words, when the channel capacity is better than its threshold, Pobs can still be worse than its threshold. But, it is less likely to have Cobs worse than its threshold when Pobs is better than its threshold. This observation implies that the error probability predicted by Cobs is less than what is predicted by Pobs, however, it does not fully describe the gap between these two prediction methods. The gap is larger than the gap implied by comparing the number of cases that Cobs ≥ cth and Pobs > pth with the number of cases that Pobs ≤ pth and Cobs < cth.
Although the scaling law [8] gives slightly more accurate results, from any practical point of view, our method gives accurate enough estimation of the performance for codes of a few thousand bits. In addition, our method is much simpler, do not have any additional parameters, and do not require any curve fitting.

V. CONCLUSION
A simple method for finite-length LDPC codes analysis was introduced. First, we studied the channel behavior for the finite-length case and we modeled the channel as a varying channel from the codewords perspective. Using this interpretation, we studied two parameters of the channel (probability of error and capacity) and modeled them as random variables. After obtaining the distribution of these random variables analytically, we were able to predict the performance (block and bit error probability) of finite-length LDPC codes. Our results suggest that most of the performance degradation for finite-length LDPC codes can be described by the channel variations around its expected behavior and even by ignoring the effects of cycles we are able to predict the performance in the waterfall region closely.
This simple method is applicable to both regular and irregular codes. We also suggested a method to choose the rate or the decoding threshold of the code, based on its block length.
We observed that Pobs results in a more accurate prediction of performance on the BIAWGN channel. However, we conjecture that Cobs gives a lower bound on the error probability of finite-length LDPC codes, since capacity is a more fundamental property of a channel.
