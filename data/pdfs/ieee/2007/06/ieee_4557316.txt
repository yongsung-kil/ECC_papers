ISIT2007, Nice, France, June 24 - June 29, 2007

A Combinatorial Family of Near Regular LDPC
Codes
K. Murali Krishnan, Rajdeep Singh, L. Sunil Chandran and Priti Shankar
Department of Computer Science and Automation Indian Institute of Science, Bangalore 560012, India.

Abstract- An elementary combinatorial Tanner graph construction for a family of near-regular low density parity check (LDPC) codes achieving high girth is presented. These codes are near regular in the sense that the degree of a left/right vertex is allowed to differ by at most one from the average. The construction yields in quadratic time complexity an asymptotic code family with provable lower bounds on the rate and the girth for a given choice of block length and average degree. The construction gives flexibility in the choice of design parameters of the code like rate, girth and average degree. Performance simulations of iterative decoding algorithm for the AWGN channel on codes designed using the method demonstrate that these codes perform better than regular PEG codes and MacKay codes of similar length for all values of Signal to noise ratio.
I. INTRODUCTION
The fact that iterative decoding on LDPC codes performs well when the underlying Tanner graph [7] has large girth is well known [10]. The recent revival of interest in LDPC codes owing to their near capacity performance on various channel models has resulted in considerable research on the construction of LDPC code families of high rate and large girth for short and moderate block lengths. These constructions may be classified as random code constructions (for example see [3], [13]), construction of codes based on projective and combinatorial geometries (see [14], [15], [16] and references therein), heuristic and search based constructions [1], [4], [5], constructions based on circulant matrices [11], algebraic constructions (see [18], [9]), code constructions based on expander graphs [17], [20], and the progressive edge growth constructions (PEG) [19]. The more sophisticated constructions based on expander graphs and finite geometries provide regular LDPC codes with provable lower bounds on parameters like girth, but are generally weaker in performance than the elementary methods like heuristic and PEG constructions. However, for the latter, the bounds have to be obtained experimentally. This naturally raises the question as to whether efficient elementary constructions achieving good performance as well as provable bounds can be obtained.
In this note, we present an efficient algorithm for constructing near regular LDPC codes. The construction is elementary, yet achieves provably high girth and performs better than regular PEG codes of similar length. These codes are almost regular in the sense that the degree of a vertex is allowed to differ by at most one from the average. We shall refer to these codes as ARG (Almost Regular high Girth) codes. The

construction gives flexibility in the design parameters of the code like rate, block-length, and average degree, and yields an asymptotic family. We prove bounds on code parameters achieved by the construction. The complexity of the graph construction algorithm grows only quadratically with the block length of the code.
The construction here may be viewed as an improvement of the progressive edge-growth (PEG) algorithm proposed in [19] by specific tuning for obtaining large girth with minimal sacrifice in regularity. Although for any given rate and code length, irregular codes are known to perform better than regular codes, regularity is of interest as it simplifies the implementation of codes. In [19] no technique for simultaneously bounding the maximum left and right degrees of the graphs constructed is provided, and hence the girth bounds depend on the values of the degrees obtained experimentally.
The bounds on the node degrees in the Tanner graph construction proposed here are achieved by adapting a high girth graph construction technique known in the graph theory literature [8]. The following section presents the construction and establishes the bound on the girth of the Tanner graph
constructed. Simulations indicate that rate 1/2 ARG codes perform better than regular codes of the same block length
reported in [2]. Although the bound on girth proved here is not tight, the
practical advantage of having a provable lower bound on the girth seems to be that the good performance reported here could be obtained just by using codes obtained in the first run of the algorithm for each block length without any further tuning, thus making the approach free of any trial-and-error or heuristics. This along with the low complexity of the algorithm enables generation of good codes for any block length fairly quickly.

The algorithm repeatedly picks up a vertex of minimum degree (chosen alternately from L and R) and adds from it an edge to the farthest vertex on the opposite side in such a way that the vertex degrees are not allowed to become excessive. During phase (i, j), the degree of a left vertex never exceeds i + 1 and the degree of a right vertex never exceeds j + 1. We will prove that at the end of left phase i, every vertex in L has degree at least i -1 and at the end of right phase j every vertex in R has degree at least j -1. Hence, when the algorithm terminates, the left and the right degrees are bounded above by pd + 1 and qd + 1 respectively, and bounded below by pd -1 and qd -1 respectively yielding a near-regular graph. III. COMPLEXITY
Assuming an adjacency list representation for the graph, the selection of a farthest non-neighbor satisfying the degree bound necessary during each edge addition may be performed by a simple breadth first search in 0(n) time when the graph is sparse (as it is in the case of LDPC codes) . Since the total number of edge additions is linear when d is fixed constant, the overall construction complexity is 0(n2).
IV. PERFORMANCE SIMULATIONS
We shall refer to the code of block length n defined by a Tanner graph of girth g and average left degree d as an (n, g, d) code. Performance simulations for (504,8,3), (1008,8,3) and (4000,10,3) ARG codes of designed rate 1/2 (corresponding to p = 1, q = 2 in the algorithm) are reported below.
The ARG codes perform better than the MacKay and regular PEG codes of the same length [2]. The bit and word error rate curves for the regular MacKay and PEG codes of the same length are plotted together with those of the ARG code for easy reference. The simulations are for the sum-product decoder on the binary input additive white noise Gaussian noise channel for 80 iterations (or less when convergence is achieved earlier). The bit/word error rates are plotted against the signal to noise ratio (in decibels).
V. DIscusSION
The codes presented for simulation were obtained by running the algorithm just once without any fine tuning of parameters. It seems that the robustness is due to the girth guaranteed by the algorithm irrespective of the choice of connections actually made in any particular run of the algorithm. The constants hidden in the asymptotic complexity analysis are small. The algorithm is thus capable of generating good codes for even large block lengths fairly quickly with no heuristic tuning or trial and error optimization necessary.
Note that the first two ARG codes for which performance curve is shown achieves a girth of 8 for average left degree 3 and average right degree 6 even as the value of girth predicted by Theorem 2 is 6. The third code achieves girth 10 whereas Theorem 2 predicts 6. These clearly show that the girth bound is not tight. However, the numerical value given by the bound indeed seems to reflect directly the quality of the code. It is easy to see from the bound in [7] that the girth can be at most
2+4 log(pd -2)(qd-2) m. Thus, the bound proved here is weaker
by about a factor of two from the theoretical limit. The main difficulty in improving the girth bound lies in the fact that there are very few elementary techniques known in graph theory that enables the girth bound to be improved substantially.
VI. CONCLUSION
We have presented an algorithm for constructing nearregular LDPC codes of large girth. From a theoretical point of view, the algorithm yields an asymptotic family with a provable Q(logn) girth bound and quadratic complexity.
