Systematic Modification of Parity-Check Matrices for Efficient Encoding of LDPC Codes

Mohamed Shaqfeh and Norbert Goertz
Institute for Digital Communications Joint Research Institute for Signal & Image Processing
School of Engineering and Electronics The University of Edinburgh
Mayfield Rd., Edinburgh EH9 3JL, Scotland, UK Email: {M.Shaqfeh, Norbert.Goertz}@ed.ac.uk

Abstract— An algorithm for efficient encoding of LDPC codes is presented that does not impose any restrictions on the construction of the parity-check matrices. The algorithm modifies the parity check matrix, without changing the subspace spanned by its rows, by removing linear dependent rows and adding a small number of new rows such that the graph-based message-passing encoder will not get stuck in a stopping set. The added rows are designed by a new algorithm which is based on the notion of the “key set”. The encoder exploits the sparseness of the paritycheck matrix, and the encoding complexity grows almost linear with the blocksize, because the number of added rows, which may not be sparse, is relatively small.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1], [2] can, for large block size, achieve near-capacity performance at acceptable decoding complexity. They are flexible in terms of design as it is possible to find good LDPC codes for a large variety of block sizes and code rates. The sparseness of the paritycheck matrix (PCM) allows for decoding with low complexity by graph-based message-passing decoders [3].
The concept is to properly design the PCM representing the code, or, equivalently, to design the Tanner graph [4] representing the PCM. The design should ensure that the PCM is sparsely populated with “ones” (“low-density” of “ones”) so that decoding can be carried out with low complexity. Moreover, the Tanner graph should have a small number of short cycles to achieve excellent error-correction performance.
A drawback of LDPC codes (and block codes in general) is their high encoding complexity for large blocksize, when encoding is carried in the classic way by multiplication of the data bit vector with the generator matrix [3]. As LDPC codes are defined by their PCM in the first place, the generator matrix (GM) is not known from the design and must be obtained by Gauss-elimination and column-permutations from the PCM. In this process the sparsity of the PCM is lost, and hence the generator matrix obtained is not sparse, causing high complexity in a matrix multiplication for large block size.
One approach for efficient LDPC encoding is to construct the code with an algebraic structure. This may lead to some degradation [5] in the achievable code performance as additional constraints have to be imposed on the PCM. Another

method, reported in [6], is based on approximate lower triangulation of the PCM by row and column permutations, which maintains the sparsity in a large part of the PCM. The scheme does not impose any restrictions on the shape of the PCM, and the encoding complexity is manageable in most cases.
Again another way of efficient encoding by exploiting the sparseness of the PCM is to use the graph-based messagepassing decoder as an encoder [7], assuming the unknown parity bits are erased. The problem of this concept is, however, that it does not work with all PCMs because of stopping sets in the Tanner graph. In this work we develop a new conceptual framework, the notion of the key Set, and an novel, systematic and efficient algorithm to modify the PCM in such a way that (i) we do not change the code (null-space of the PCM) (ii) we do not lose the PCM’s sparseness and (ii) the modified PCM can be used for encoding by the message-passing decoder.
The rest of the paper is organised as follows: In Section II we introduce the mathematical notation and review fundamental definitions. In Section III the encoder design task is discussed in detail, and in Section IV our new encoding scheme is introduced, followed by a detailed example illustrating the concept. Simulation results are presented in Section V.
II. BASIC DEFINITIONS AND NOTATION
LDPC codes belong to the class of linear codes. A linear code, over a finite field, Fq, with q elements, is a linear subspace C ⊂ Fqn, with n the blocksize of the code. In this work, the binary field is assumed. Hence, there are 2k codewords forming the code C ⊂ F2n, with k the number of information bits. A codeword is denoted by w ∈ F2n, and the k-dimensional information bit-vector is denoted by u ∈ F2k. The encoding step is a linear mapping of the input information vector u into a codeword w.
Given a linear code, C, of codeword length n with k information bits (k < n), a PCM, H, of the code, C, is a matrix whose rows span the orthogonal complement (null space) of C. Thus, the multiplication of any codeword w ∈ C with the PCM, H, gives the “all-zero” vector, i.e., H · wT = 0. If the PCM, H, has full rank, then it has the dimensions (n − k) × n. 2) the first non-zero entry (a “leading one”) of each nonzero row below the first row, occurs to the right of the “leading one” in the row above,
3) all entries in the column above and below a “leading one” are “zeros”,
4) if any “all zero” rows exist (because the matrix may be rank-deficient), they are all at the bottom of the matrix.

Property 3) implies that every bit-position in the codeword

corresponding to the column-index of a “leading one” must

be a parity bit, as its value can be computed from and only

from the non-zero bit positions in the same row of the PCM.

III. ENCODER DESIGN FOR LDPC CODES
A. Introduction
An LDPC code is designed by a construction rule that makes sure the PCM is sparsely populated with “ones”. The PCM will, however, not be in systematic form, so it is not clear where the information bits can be placed in the codeword (assuming systematic encoding). It is not even clear from the dimensions of the PCM how many data bit positions exist, as the PCM may not be full-rank.
As discussed in Section II, any PCM can be transformed into reduced echelon form by Gauss-elimination and, from there, into systematic form by additional column permutations. Although, for large block size, this requires large computational load this is not a major problem, as it is a pre-processing step that is carried out only once. However, during the transformation into systematic form, the sparseness of the PCM is lost, i.e., encoding by matrix multiplication with a systematic but partially dense GM requires large computational load, which, for the non-sparse part, is of the order O ((n − k) · k).
It may appear that this high encoding complexity is a major drawback of LDPC codes, but this problem exists actually for all long block codes when they are encoded in the classical way by matrix multiplication.
There are a number of possible routes which enable encoding with moderate-to-low complexity. A code design with an algebraic structure is one solution. Examples among a large variety of this approach are LDPC block and convolutional codes based on circulant matrices [5] and finite geometry (Euclidean and projective) LDPC codes [3]. Most of these construction techniques produce cyclic or quasi-cyclic codes, which can, e.g., be encoded by a shift-register implementation. The additional constraints on the PCM design may, however, lead to degradation in the achievable performance; for large

Furthermore, achieving capacity-approaching performance requires some irregularity in the PCM, which does not lend itself to any systematic construction. Thus, it is still required to find efficient ways to encode any LDPC code, independent of the construction technique of the PCM.
Richardson and Urbanke [6] showed that, even without the need of an algebraic construction or any other restriction on the shape of the PCM, the encoding complexity is quite manageable in most cases. The concept is that, similar to the decoding case, the sparseness of the PCM allows for encoding at low-complexity. The method described in [6] is based on a preprocessing step, in which the PCM is brought into an approximate lower triangular form by performing row and column permutations only, and an actual encoding step based on matrix and vector multiplications. The encoding complexity grows “almost” linearly with the block size, and the difference to linear growth depends on a “gap” which depends on the code construction.
Another way of efficient encoding by exploiting the sparseness of the PCM is to use the graph-based message-passing decoder for encoding. In this work we follow that route.

This method has a complexity that grows linearly with the block length since the PCM is sparse. The problem with the iterative message-passing encoder is that it does not work with all PCMs. If there is more than one unknown bit connected to a check node, no decision can be taken. Hence, the parity bits nodes in the Tanner graph should be free of “stopping sets” (explained below) so that the encoder can successfully recover all erasures. This puts a constraint on the selection of the PCM representing the code; however there are many PCMs which represent the same code.

B. Iterative Graph-Based Message-Passing Encoder
The idea ([7], [5]) is basically to apply the graph-based message-passing (or belief-propagation) decoder for the case of a Binary Erasure Channel (BEC). In this case, the bits are either known or completely unknown (erased). The information bits are known and all the parity bits are assumed to be erased, and the message-passing decoder is used to recover the missing parity bits. As well known, this decoder structure is efficient when applied on low-dense graphs, and this is indeed one of the main motivations behind LDPC codes. Furthermore, applying this iterative graph-based structure over an erasure channel involves less complexity than over a noisy channel. This is simply because the exchanged messages from the variable nodes to the check nodes are either known or unknown, or in terms of probabilities either 1 or 0.5.
The operating principle of the message-passing encoder is that a bit can be recovered, due to the parity-check constraint, when the bit is connected to at least one check node at which all bit values, apart from the bit considered, are known. IV. A NEW APPROACH TO ENCODE LDPC CODES
A. Concept
For the graph-based message-passing encoder to work, the parity bit nodes in the Tanner graph representing the PCM should be free of stopping sets so that all “erased” parity bits can be recovered from the information bits. This puts a constraint on the PCM. There are many PCMs (and corresponding Tanner graphs) which all represent the same code C. Thus, in principle, two different PCMs could be used: one for efficient decoding, and the second for efficient encoding.
Therefore, the key question is: how can we select, for any code C, a PCM H that (i) is sparse and (ii) allows for an efficient application of the message-passing encoder?
We will start from the PCM, H, which is used for the decoder since we know (from the construction of LDPC codes)

that it fulfills the sparseness condition. Our aim is to modify this matrix H in a way that we do not lose its sparseness feature and at the same time it can be used with the messagepassing encoder.
Our approach to achieve this goal is based on the observation that knowing (or guessing) a relatively small number of bits belonging to the stopping set lets the stopping set vanish and allows all other erased bits to be recovered. For example, if only the first node of the stopping set in Figure 3 is assumed to be known, then all the other three nodes could be recovered by the iterative decoder.
The problem is, of course, which bit-value should be assumed for a bit from the stopping set. One way is to make a guess: if it is false this will lead to a contradiction during the encoding iterations that can be detected. On the other hand, if the guess is correct, all bits could be recovered. Although the method will, in principle, work in any case, it is infeasible in practice with long codes, as even for a relatively small number m of bits that have to be guessed, 2m different possibilities exist of which only one is correct.
There is a much better alternative which is calculating these few bits rather than guessing them. From the reduced echelon form Hred of the PCM, we can obtain any parity bit as a function of the information bits only. Thus, adding check nodes connected to the required parity-bits will provide the values of these parity-bits after the first “encoding” iteration, and the stopping set will vanish.
Therefore, all we need to know is which of the parity bits need to be determined to let the stopping set vanish and then to add (to the graph or, equivalently, to the PCM) check nodes representing these parity bits as functions of the information bits only. The task of choosing the locations and the smallest possible number of the parity bits that must be known in advance to overcome the stopping set problem, is discussed in the following section, where the notion of the “key set” is introduced.
It should be noticed that the proposed modification of the PCM does not change the code as the the null space of the PCM is maintained. Although the added rows may not be sparse, as they are obtained from the systematic reduced echelon form of the matrix, their number is usually (see simulation results) relatively small compared to the total number of parity bits and, therefore, the complexity of the encoder remains practical.
B. Key Set
Lets consider the largest stopping set of the Tanner graph, which is the complete set of variable nodes. Furthermore, let’s assume that we have not selected in advance the information and parity bits.
Definition 2: The key set is a subset K of the set of variable nodes V , such that the maximal stopping set included in V \K is the empty set.
The name key set is suggested because the prior knowledge of the key set is the key to have an empty stopping

set for the unknown nodes (bits). There are many possible key sets with different sizes for a given Tanner graph; the set V is a key set of itself. For example, the sets of nodes {v1, v2, v3}, {v4, v5, v6}, {v1, v4, v5}, {v1, v2, v4, v6} are all key sets of the graph in Figure 2. Moreover, the union of a key set with any other set is also a key set.
There is at least (but perhaps more than) one “optimum” key set, which has the smallest possible number of nodes, and this is the key set we wish to find. Having a small number of elements in the key set means that there is only a small number of parity bits that have to be determined by rows added to the PCM, before the message-passing encoder can be applied. Thus, low additional complexity results.
The number k of information bits is, of course, a lower limit for the size of the key sets, as at least the information bits must be known before a codeword can be computed. However, the optimum key set can have a number of nodes (bits) larger than the number of information bits, i.e., it will, additionally, contain some parity bits. As all information bits are necessarily key bits, the non-key bits must all be parity bits.
Finding the “optimum” key set is a complicated task, especially for long randomly constructed codes. Therefore, we rather propose a greedy algorithm to find a “good” key set with a small number of nodes. The suggested algorithm is always applicable. The concept is to virtually run the encoding process. Whenever, the encoder stops due to a stopping set, one of the variable nodes connected to lowest-degree check nodes is declared as key bit in order to let the stopping set vanish.
C. Algorithm Description
The encoder operation can be divided into two main parts; the preprocessing step, which is done only once and offline, and the message-passing encoding.
For preprocessing, the following steps are carried out:
1) Obtain, according to some construction rule, a lowdensity PCM, H, representing the code.
2) Apply the greedy algorithm (proposed below) to find a key set for H and to identify any linearly dependent rows in H (unneeded check nodes in the Tanner graph).
3) Re-arrange the columns of the PCM, H, as follows: H∗ =[non-key node columns, key node columns].
4) Compute the reduced echelon form Hr∗ed of the PCM H∗ and decide the locations of the information bits: these are those bit-positions in the key-node-part of the PCM H∗red which do not have a single “one” in one of the columns. If there is a single “one” in one of the columns of the key-node-part of the PCM Hr∗ed, this identifies a key-parity bit and the row defines the data bits which can be used to compute this key-parity bit.
5) Modify the original PCM and obtain the PCM H used for message-passing encoding by deleting the unneeded rows (identified in step 2)) and by extending H with the new rows representing the parity key bits in terms of the data bits (determined in step 4)).

Greedy algorithm to find a key set:
A) Find the check nodes with the lowest degree. If the lowest degree is one, go to step C) otherwise go to B).
B) If there is more than one check node with the lowest degree, choose the one which is connected to a variable node with the highest degree (applicable in case of irregular LDPC codes since in case of regular LDPC codes all variable nodes have the same degree). If there exists more than one check node satisfying this condition, choose any of them. Declare any of the highest degree variable nodes connected to this check node as a key bit. Go to step D).
C) Declare the variable nodes connected to the check nodes of degree one as non-key bits. If there are more than one degree-one check nodes connected to the same variable node, then select any one of them and declare the others as unneeded check nodes. Delete the degree-one check nodes from the graph. Go to step D).
D) Delete the declared (“key” and “non-key”) variable node (or nodes) and all its (or their) edges from the graph. If after deleting these nodes, check nodes with no edges results, then declare these check nodes as unneeded nodes and delete them from the graph. If the graph has completely vanished then stop, otherwise go to step A).

V. SIMULATION RESULTS

The goal in this section is to determine by simulation the

required number of check nodes or rows which have to be

added to the PCM H (of an LDPC code constructed by

randomly generating H) so that the iterative message-passing

encoder can be used. This number can be obtained by applying

our algorithm to find a key set for the code graph. The number

of key parity bits corresponds to the number of new rows to be

added. As randomly-constructed LDPC codes are examined in

the simulation, it can be predicted that different code graphs

could have different number of key parity bits even if they

have similar parameters (ρ, γ, and n). Thus, the framework

of the simulation was to generate 100 randomly-constructed

regular LDPC codes having similar parameters (ρ, γ, and n)

and to apply our algorithm in each case to find the number of

key parity bits.

The codes were constructed using the following simple

method. The H matrix is divided into γ submatrices, having

the following structure:

The ratio of the average number of key parity bits to the total number of parity bits is shown as well in the table. The results in Table II show that the ratio of the key parity bits to the total number of parity bits is, in general, small. Thus, although the rows added to the PCM H may not be sparse, the overall complexity of the encoder is still practical, and much lower than classical encoding by matrix multiplication.
Moreover, we observe that for codes having the same numbers of ones in their rows and columns (ρ and γ) respectively but having different codeword lengths (n), the ratio of the average number of key parity bits to the total number of parity bits is almost the same.

VI. CONCLUSIONS
We have investigated the systematic modification of the PCM in order to enable encoding of LDPC codes by applying the belief-propagation decoder on a binary erasure channel on which the parity bits are assumed to be erased. Our algorithm solves the stopping set problem by adding, without changing the code, some check nodes, or correspondingly rows of the PCM, which let the stopping set vanish. Furthermore, our algorithm deletes some check nodes, which are unnecessary for the iterative encoding process.
We have shown by simulations that the new algorithm works and that the number of rows added to the PCM, and hence the complexity increase compared with message-passing decoding with the original PCM, is usually small.
