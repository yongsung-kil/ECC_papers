Abstract-In this paper, we apply generalized Kronecker
product recursively to construct low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes with arbitrarily large girth. The parity check matrices of these codes are block matrices consisting of circulant permutation matrices, thus may benefit from efficient encoding. It turns out that the LU(m, q) codes proposed in the literature is a special case of this construction for prime q. Connectivity of Tanner graphs of these codes are investigated.

I. INTRODUCTION AND RELATED WORKS
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are well-known for their good performance. An LDPC code has a bipartite graph representation corresponding to its parity-check matrix. This graph is called the Tanner graph of the parity-check matrix and the belief propagation decoding algorithm can be performed on it. Consequently, properties of the Tanner graph are related to the decoding performance [1]. Among them, a large girth makes not only the belief propagation decoding algorithm perform well, but also the minimum distance of the code be large [2]. Construction of graphs with large girth is a classical problem of graph theory, a brief survey can be found in [3]. Some of these graphs can be applied to define LDPC codes [3]-[5]. Lazebnik and Ustimenko gave explicit construction
of a family of bipartite graphs, D(m, q), with arbitrarily large girth [3]. Kim et al. derived properties of LU(m, q) codes defined by the graphs D(m, q). However, the disconnectivity of D(m, q) makes them difficult to define LDPC codes [6].
Girth bounds for some types of QC LDPC codes have been investigated [7]-[10]. These works show that the girth bounds depend not only on the circulant matrices, but also on the structure of the weight matrix (mother matrix) whose entries indicate the weight of circulant matrices which define the code.
In this paper, we apply generalized Kronecker product to parity-check matrices recursively to construct low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes with arbitrarily large girth. It is shown that when q is a prime, the construction of LU(m, q) codes in [6] can be categorized into this construction. We also derive conditions for the associated Tanner graphs to be connected. These ideas have their related graph theoretical formulations but in different names (seed graphs, protographs) [11]-[13]. V. OTHER ISSUES
There are a couple of issues which have to be considered further. First, we do not have an efficient method yet to choose block matrices of permutation circulant matrices. However, the
increasing girth bound of LU(m, q) codes sheds some light
