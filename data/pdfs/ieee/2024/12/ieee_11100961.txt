Abstract—The immersive communication scenario in 6G, stemming from the enhanced mobile broadband (eMBB) scenario in 5G, demands low-complexity high-throughput channel coding scheme due to its huge data traffic. The current data channel coding scheme of eMBB, i.e., 5G new radio (5G-NR) lowdensity parity-check (LDPC) codes, shows limitation on further improvements of high throughput due to their highly irregular base graph structure. In this paper, we first introduce a rate compatible quasi-cyclic (QC-) spatially-coupled (SC-) LDPC code structure, and then apply an adaptive quantized and normalized min-sum algorithm (AQNMSA) with very low message passing bit-width of 4-bit to the QC-SC-LDPC codes. The asymptotic decoding thresholds provided by multi-edge-type density evolution tool and the simulation results both show that QC-SC-LDPC codes with 4-bit AQNMSA have better decoding performance than conventional non-adaptive normalized min-sum algorithm as well as approaching decoding performance compared to 5GNR LDPC codes with optimal belief propagation decoding. In general, QC-SC-LDPC codes with AQNMSA, row-parallel architecture, and layered-scheduling sliding window decoding could achieve superior decoding performance than current coding scheme, including at least 30 times of throughput gain and better decoding performance with lower decoding complexity.
Index Terms—high throughput, immersive communication, low complexity, spatially-coupled low-density parity-check (SCLDPC) codes, adaptive quantized and normalized min-sum algorithm (AQNMSA)
I. INTRODUCTION
The huge data traffic of metaverse [1] and Extended Reality (XR) [2] leads to a demand of low-complexity high-throughput channel coding scheme for the immersive communication scenario [3] of the coming 6th generation (6G) communication network [4]. Specifically, channel coding schemes for 6G is facing the challenge of Tbps level data throughput [5], which is about 50 times of the peak data rate of current enhanced mobile broadband (eMBB) scenario. The coding scheme of 5G eMBB data channel is 5G new radio (5G-NR) low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [6], a special class of LDPC codes [7]. LDPC codes, a class of linear block codes featuring sparse parity-check matrix (PCM), show Shannon-limit approaching performance under iterative belief propagation (BP) decoding.
Corresponding author: Jian Song.

5G-NR LDPC codes could support a wide range of code rate and code length with good threshold performance due to their Raptor-like base graph structure. [8] However, this highly irregular base graph structure brings difficulty to the further improvement on parallelism level, which limits their capability of higher throughput in hardware implementation.
Conventional LDPC codes are usually designed and optimized under specific channel model and code rate, which limits their universality. Recently, spatially-coupled (SC-) LDPC codes [9] have attracted worldwide attentions for their universally capacity-approaching performance over different channels under BP decoding due to their threshold saturation phenomenon. The decoding complexity of conventional full BP decoding for SC-LDPC codes is unacceptable in practical implementation. A suitable decoding scheme, i.e., sliding window decoding (SWD) [10], takes advantage of the wave-like decoding process of SC-LDPC codes due to their spatially-coupled structure, and owns rather low complexity and marginal performance loss compared to full BP decoding.
BP decoding of LDPC codes, also called sum-product algorithm (SPA) [11], could provide near optimal decoding threshold performance but requires relatively high computational complexity. By comparison, min-sum algorithm (MSA) [12] has low computational complexity but degraded threshold performance. Offset MSA (OMSA) [13] and Normalized MSA (NMSA) [14] use a fixed offset factor β or normalization factor α to improve the threshold performance of MSA, leading to a performance tradeoff between the decoding threshold and error floor. An improved MSA called adaptive NMSA (ANMSA) [15], which utilizes a preconfigured look-up table (LUT) between the normalization factor α and the unsatisfied check nodes rate check-ratio, could achieve good decoding threshold performance and low error floor simultaneously.
Decoding under limited complexity and power consumption is particularly concerned in real implementation. Thus, the input signal and intermediate signals of LDPC decoders are quantized to fixed-point signals with limited bit-width, which reduces the computation and storage overhead and also enhances the data throughput due to higher operating frequency and parallelism level. However, quantized decoding algorithms

have degraded threshold performance and higher error floor compared to original float-point algorithms.
To alleviate the performance loss caused by quantization, an improvement on quantization strategy is to apply different bit-width to the input and output signals of the variable node unit (VNU) and check node unit (CNU), and choose the minimum bit-width as the message passing bit-width, e.g., binary message passing (BMP) [16] and quaternary message passing (QMP) decoding [17]. To further improve the decoding performance, adaptive quantized and normalized MSA (AQNMSA) [18] is proposed, which applies asymmetric quantization bitwidth and check-ratio based adaptive quantization intervals and normalization factor.
In this paper, we propose a low complexity and high throughput decoding scheme based on QC-SC-LDPC codes and AQNMSA, which is especially suitable for 6G immersive communication. We first present a special quasi-cyclic (QC-) SC-LDPC code structure with rate compatible edge spreading pattern, and explain the potential high throughput of these codes under SWD with layered-scheduling.
Then we introduce the principle and process of the low complexity AQNMSA, and apply it to the aforementioned QCSC-LDPC codes. Note that the computation process of checkratio is modified according to the SWD process, which will be elaborated in the following sections. We provide the final LUT for AQNMSA with a very low message passing bit-width of 4-bit, and present the asymptotic decoding thresholds of AQNMSA and QC-SC-LDPC codes, obtained with the tool of multi-edge-type density evolution (MET-DE) [19].
Finally, we present the simulation results of AQNMSA applied to QC-SC-LDPC codes, which owns a superior decoding threshold and lower error floor compared to its non-adaptive counterparts. We also compare the proposed scheme with other schemes with regard to the decoding complexity and block error rate (BLER) performance, to show that our scheme could achieve a better overall performance.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section II introduces the rate compatible QC-SC-LDPC code structure and the potential high throughput of row-parallel SWD. Section III elaborates on the details of AQNMSA applied to the above QC-SC-LDPC codes, and provides the final LUT design result and the asymptotic performance prediction results of 4-bit AQNMSA applied to QC-SC-LDPC codes. Section IV presents the simulation results of AQNMSA applied to QC-SC-LDPC codes, and compare our scheme with other improved schemes. Section V concludes the paper.

C. Layered-scheduling SWD Scheme

We apply layered-scheduling and SWD scheme to the QCSC-LDPC codes to further reduce the decoding complexity as well as improve decoding efficiency.
SWD is a decoding scheme originally designed for codes with spatially-coupled structure. The key idea of SWD is to only deal with a local-centralized subset, i.e., a decoding window, of a spatially-coupled code at one iteration, which takes advantage of the local constraints of the specific spatiallycoupled code structure. After the decoding process within the current window is finished, the decoder slides to the next window, and repeats this process till the decoding ends.
We employ layered-scheduling in the SWD scheme instead of conventional flooding-scheduling. In flooding-scheduling, the variable nodes are updated after the check node processing of all rows is finished. By comparison, in layered-scheduling, the variable nodes are immediately updated after the check node processing of a layer is finished, which leads to a near 50% reduction of the iteration number to achieve a competitive decoding threshold. Based on the aforementioned QC-SCLDPC code structure, it is natural to consider Z PCM rows, which corresponds to a row in the base graph, as a decoding layer. Thus, the mSC = MSC · Z rows of the final PCM HSC are divided into MSC = (L + w)Munc layers, where the tth layer represents the Z consecutive rows numbered from (t − 1)Z + 1 to tZ in HSC.
A decoding window consists of check nodes from W ·M unc consecutive layers, where W denotes the window size. For simple implementation, we assume that the window size W and the iteration number Iiwteinr in a decoding window remains the same for all windows. After the decoding process in the current window is finished, the window slides a fixed step of Munc layers, which corresponds to the uncoupled code. Note that the decoding window contracts at both ends of the QC-SCLDPC code to ensure that all rows are involved with the same number of iterations. III. AQNMSA APPLIED TO QC-SC-LDPC CODES
Routing networks and shift networks are the key factor that determines the area and routing delay of a parallel decoder. Given the specific LDPC code structure and decoding parallel architecture, the routing complexity and operating frequency are mainly determined by the message passing bit-width, i.e., the quantization bit-width of the intermediate signals. Generally an 8-bit or higher bit-width is chosen for the intermediate signals aiming at good decoding threshold performance, while a reduction of 2 bits could bring around 50% throughput gain [23]. Besides, row-parallel architecture leads to a large decoder internal data flow, which indicates high computational complexity in decoder implementation. The internal data flow F of the row-parallel LDPC decoder is given by

F = 2 · nc · q · dv,

(5)

where nc denotes the code length, q denotes the message passing bit-width, and dv denotes the average variable node degree. Note that dv = dv for our QC-SC-LDPC with identical variable node degree. From the above equation we have F ∝ q. Therefore, the idea of reducing the quantization bitwidth to achieve higher throughput and lower complexity is highly desirable. However, it confronts the possibility of severe performance degradation.
In this section, we apply a novel adaptive asymmetric quantized and normalized min-sum algorithm, i.e., AQNMSA, to the QC-SC-LDPC codes, to significantly reduce the quantization bit-width of intermediate signals and meanwhile maintain the good decoding performance. With an extremely low message passing bit-width of 4-bit, our AQNMSA could have at least 2.25 times of throughput gain and highly reduced decoding complexity compared with conventional algorithms with 8-bit or higher message passing bit-width.

A. Principle and Process of AQNMSA
AQNMSA applies the check-ratio based adaptive asymmetric quantization strategy to design the quantization parameters of input and intermediate signals. A high bit-width qH and fixed quantization intervals T0 are chosen for VNU’s input and output, and a low bit-width of qL and check-ratio based adaptive quantization intervals Tcur are chosen for CNU’s input and output. It also combines the check-ratio based adaptive normalization technique to achieve better decoding threshold performance and lower error floor.

In practical implementation, the LUT for the quantization and normalization parameters (check-ratio, (T, α)) is preconfigured in the decoder. Before each iteration, the current unsatisfied check nodes rate check-ratiocur is calculated and serves as the index of the LUT for the decoder to obtain the current quantization interval Tcur of CNU’s input and output and current normalization factor αcur. Then, the decoder uses qL, T0, and Tcur to convert VNU’s high bit-width output into CNU’s low bit-width input. After the CNU process is done, the decoder uses qH, T0, and Tcur to convert CNU’s low bitwidth output into a high bit-width intermediate variable, and multiplies it by αcur to obtain VNU’s high bit-width input.
B. LUT Design Method and Result
A LUT design algorithm based on the MET-DE tool is applied to AQNMSA and the aforementioned QC-SC-LDPC codes with layered-scheduling and SWD scheme under binary input additive white Gaussian noise (BI-AWGN) channel. Note that although we employ a highly regular SC-LDPC code structure, only MET-DE can give the exact prediction of AQNMSA’s asymptotic decoding performance, since different rows/layers in a decoding window would go through different number of iterations in the SWD process and thus are in different decoding states.

IV. SIMULATION RESULTS AND COMPARISON WITH OTHER SCHEMES
The simulation results of the aforementioned scheme, with QC-SC-LDPC code lifting factor Z = 768 and 5G-NR LDPC code Base Graph 1 with code rate 2/3 and Z = 384, are shown in Fig. 3. To make a fair comparison, we choose W = 10, Iiwteinr = 3, and thus IiStW er D = 3 × 10 = 30 = Iiter for AQNMSA and fixed-point NMSA, and choose the optimal fixed α = 0.675 and Tfixed = 10 for fixed-point NMSA.
From the simulation results, we can observe that our scheme outperforms its non-adaptive counterparts, i.e., 4-bit fixedquantized NMSA, in both waterfall region and error floor, which conforms to the asymptotic performance prediction results provided by the MET-DE tool. Concurrently, our scheme shows competitive decoding threshold performance compared with conventional 5G-NR LDPC code and layered-scheduling float-point NMSA, and a minor 0.3 dB performance loss compared with float-point SPA. We can also observe that the effect of the adaptive strategy becomes more advantageous as the code rate decreases. Thus, we expect a greater gain of our scheme compared with its non-adaptive counterparts in lower code rate conditions.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we first introduce a class of rate compatible QC-SC-LDPC codes with regular edge spreading pattern and one-step QC-lifting, which is particularly suitable for rowparallel decoding, and demonstrate the layered-scheduling SWD scheme, which could achieve 14 times of throughput gain compared with block-parallel. Then we apply a checkratio based adaptive asymmetric quantized and normalized MSA, i.e., AQNMSA, to the aforementioned scheme, and observe a superior decoding threshold compared to conventional 5G-NR LDPC code with layered-scheduling optimal BP decoding. With an extremely low message passing bit-width of 4-bit, our AQNMSA is expected to have at least 2.25 times of throughput gain in hardware implementation compared to algorithms with 8-bit or higher message passing bit-width, and maintain competitive decoding performance simultaneously.
In conclusion, we propose a low complexity and high throughput decoding scheme based on rate compatible QC-SCLDPC codes and AQNMSA, which could achieve moderate decoder internal data flow, capacity-approaching decoding threshold performance, and at least 30 times of throughput gain in total, compared to conventional 5G-NR LDPC code under layered-scheduling optimal BP decoding.
In future work, we will consider the message passing bitwidth of 3-bit or lower, and meanwhile reduce the iteration number, to further explore the potential low complexity and high throughput of AQNMSA. The tradeoff between the quantization bit-width and decoding performance will also be carefully studied in the future, and the results will be used to guide the parameter design of AQNMSA applied to QC-SCLDPC codes and row-parallel layered-scheduling SWD.
