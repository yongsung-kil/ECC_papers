Analysis of Efficient Scheduling in Layered Decoding of LDPC Codes
Dongxu Chang †, Guanghui Wang ∗†, Guiying Yan ‡, Zhiming Ma ‡ ∗School of Mathematics, Shandong University †Data Science Institute, Shandong University ‡Academy of Mathematics and Systems Science, University of Chinese Academy of Sciences Email: dongxuchang@mail.sdu.edu.cn, ghwang@sdu.edu.cn, yangy@amss.ac.cn, mazm@amt.ac.cn

Abstract—In this study, we analyze the characteristics of scheduling sequences for Layered belief propagation (LBP) that can result in efficient decoding on Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. Specifically, we claim that scheduling sequences leading to high decoding efficiency should prioritize updating check nodes with lower error probabilities aggregated from neighboring variable nodes. We prove this conclusion separately in both BEC and BI-AWGN channels. Some observable characteristics in “good” scheduling sequences regarding row weights, rows connected to punctured columns, and column weights can serve as corollaries to this conclusion. Based on these, we design a dynamic scheduling algorithm, which can further demonstrate the effectiveness of these characteristics through simulation experiments.
Index Terms—Low-density parity-check code, scheduling policy, decoding efficiency
I. Introduction
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) code [1] has been widely used in communication fields, such as 5G New Radio (NR) [2], IEEE 802.11 (WiFi) [3]. It was demonstrated by D. MacKay and M. Neal in 1996 [4] that LDPC codes can approach the Shannon limit. The belief propagation (BP) decoding algorithm [5] of LDPC code is most commonly used due to its low complexity and good performance in practice.
The conventional BP algorithm employs a flooding scheduling strategy to conduct message-passing between variable nodes and check nodes. This strategy updates all variable-tocheck (V2C) messages or check-to-variable (C2V) messages simultaneously in one iteration, achieving a high degree of parallelism. However, the flooding BP algorithm is known for its slow convergence speed. This is because the latest information available in the current iteration can only be utilized in the subsequent iteration. The delayed information may hinder the benefits of information transmission, thus affecting the algorithm’s convergence rate [6].
In 2004, D. Hocevar introduced the concept of layered belief propagation (LBP) decoding [6], which updates information in a sequential order. This decoding strategy can accelerate the decoding process as it ensures that the most recent information is disseminated. In comparison to the flooding scheduling strategy, LBP can reduce decoding complexity by nearly 50% while achieving the same performance.
This work is partially supported by the National Key R&D Program of China, (2023YFA1009600).

Despite its benefits, LBP does not have a specific design for the order of decoding sequence. To further reduce decoding complexity, scheduling the order of decoding sequences is a key direction for improving LBP.
In [7], through heuristic analysis, updating the check nodes with fewer connected punctured variable nodes and larger degrees first is believed to enable broader dissemination of information. Based on this idea, the Least-punctured and highest-degree (LPHD) algorithm was designed. In [8], it was observed that low-degree check nodes could transmit more mutual information to their neighboring variable nodes after updating. Based on this idea, a scheduling algorithm was designed by updating the check nodes with smaller degrees first. In [9], by using two metrics – average entropy and gap to maximum a posterior [10], to assess the quality of a scheduling sequence, the scheduling problem is transformed into an optimization problem, and the successive-searching BP (SSBP) algorithm based on local search algorithm was proposed. In [11], a greedy approach to designing nested scheduling sequences by utilizing the raptor-like structure of 5G NR LDPC codes was proposed.
Despite the considerable amount of existing research on scheduling, much of it is based on heuristic approaches. Moreover, the fundamental question of scheduling – specifically, which message-passing order leads to higher message-passing efficiency and why – remains inadequately addressed in current studies, lacking comprehensive analysis.
In this study, we explore the question of what characteristics can lead to a “good” scheduling sequence. Specifically, we claim that a “good” scheduling sequence should prioritize updating check nodes with lower error probabilities aggregated from neighboring variable nodes. We prove this by tracking the average error probability for variable nodes with the updates of two adjacent check nodes in both the binary erasure channel (BEC) and the binary-input additive white Gaussian noise (BI-AWGN) channel. Specifically, in BI-AWGN channels, we adopt the Gaussian approximation [12] to simplify the analysis. Furthermore, starting from the characteristic of updating check nodes with lower error probabilities aggregated from neighboring variable nodes first, we illustrate that some observable changes in “good” scheduling sequences regarding row weights, rows connected to punctured columns, and column weights can serve as corollaries of this characteristic. Finally, a dynamic scheduling algorithm is designed based

B. Layered Belief Propagation and Scheduling Sequences

In each decoding iteration, LBP [6] sequentially selects each check node in the graph and updates the information on all edges connected to the selected check node. Specifically, it performs V2C message updates along all edges connected to the selected check node, followed by C2V message updates. The order in which check nodes are selected for updating in LBP is referred to as the scheduling sequence, defined as follows:

Definition 1 (scheduling sequences). The scheduling sequence is a sequence composed of the indices of check nodes. It represents the order in which check nodes are selected for decoding in LBP, with the decoder selecting check nodes for decoding according to the order in which they appear in the scheduling sequences.

III. Scheduling Characteristics over the BEC
On the BEC, under different scheduling sequences, the same number of message-passing iterations will result in different erasure probabilities of variable nodes after decoding. Therefore, for different check nodes, the order in the scheduling sequences where they can maximize their contribution to message updates should vary. We claim that one important characteristic that influences the optimal position of a check node in the scheduling sequence is the magnitude of the sum of erasure probabilities of variable nodes connected to the check nodes.
To simplify the analysis of decoding performance for different scheduling sequences, the subsequent analysis in this section will be conducted under the conditions of Assumption 1.

Remark 1. Theorem 1 can be understood heuristically. When the check node with a smaller sum of erasure probabilities from its neighboring variable nodes is updated first, it is more likely to recover the information of a neighboring erased variable node. Consequently, this recovered variable node can assist other check nodes in decoding, which leads to higher decoding efficiency. In Fig. 1, an example is provided. On the BEC, there are two check nodes, A and B, on the graph. A is connected to one erased variable node, while B is connected to two erased variable nodes. By first updating A, which is connected to fewer erased variable nodes, and then updating B, all variable nodes’ information can be recovered in one iteration. In contrast, updating B before updating A leads to the inability to recover the information of all variable nodes in a single iteration.

By adjusting the message-passing order, some nodes’ information in the graph can converge quickly, completing decoding, and driving the remaining nodes to complete decoding. Therefore, high decoding efficiency can be obtained.

IV. Scheduling Characteristics over the BI-AWGN Channel

By approximating the information transmitted during decoding as Gaussian distributions, the process of performance analysis can be greatly simplified while maintaining a high degree of accuracy. VI. Conclusion
In this paper, we show that scheduling sequences with high decoding efficiency should prioritize updating check nodes with lower error probabilities aggregated from neighboring variable nodes. By allowing check nodes that are less prone to errors to update earlier, they are more likely to correct current decoding errors, assisting other check nodes in the decoding process. This conclusion is proved for check nodes with adjacent order in scheduling sequence on both BEC and BI-AWGN channels.
Furthermore, some observable characteristics in highefficiency scheduling sequences can be regarded as corollaries of this conclusion. For example, in “good” scheduling

sequences, row weights are generally arranged in ascending
order, check nodes connected to fewer punctured variable
nodes are updated first, and check nodes connected to variable
nodes with smaller degrees are updated later.
This conclusion can help us better understand how to conduct efficient message passing. Further research on message passing efficiency and how to utilize the characteristics of efficient message passing to design a better scheduling
algorithm is left for future investigation.
