The Hybrid Decoder of Belief Propagation and Ordered Statistics Decoding for LDPC Codes
Xuanyu Li, Kai Niu, Yuxin Han Key Laboratory of Universal Wireless Communications, Beijing University of Posts and Telecommunications
Email: {lixuanyu, niukai, hanyx}@bupt.edu.cn

Abstract—To enhance the performance of short low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, we introduce an innovative hybrid decoder that seamlessly integrates belief propagation (BP) with ordered statistics decoding (OSD). This hybrid approach initially employs BP to decode received vectors until successful or upon reaching specified conditions, either a maximum iteration limit or a trapping iteration threshold, indicating the stagnation of BP’s hard output. Upon these conditions, the decoder transitions to a pre-configured error pattern (PEP) OSD. The PEPOSD phase then pursues a valid result, flexibly utilizing either the received vectors or the soft values of BP as input, contingent upon the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). Given the minimal error overlap between BP and OSD, simulations reveal that this hybrid decoder achieves a notable gain of 1.5dB over BP and 0.3-0.6dB over PEPOSD, while maintaining comparable complexity to BP alone.
Index Terms—LDPC, OSD, BP, short block codes
I. INTRODUCTION
To address the demands of ultra-reliable and low-latency communications (URLLC), achieving high reliability in short codes has emerged as a pivotal research focus [1]. Among the arsenal of channel codes, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes occupy a prominent position [2], with quasi-cyclic LDPC being the chosen data channel code for 5G New Radio’s enhanced Mobile Broadband (eMBB) [3]. Consequently, short LDPC codes have garnered significant attention from academia.
However, for short to moderate LDPC codes, traditional decoding algorithms such as belief propagation (BP) [4] often encounter performance limitations due to the inherent message dependence within their code structures. Attempts to alleviate this have been made, such as the variants presented in [5] and [6].
Ordered statistics decoding (OSD) [7], on the other hand, offers a performance close to that of maximum likelihood (ML) decoding, making it an attractive candidate for parallel implementation. Its exceptional performance, particularly for short and high-rate codes, is noteworthy. Nevertheless, as the code length increases and the code rate decreases, OSD’s decoding complexity escalates significantly. Recognizing this challenge, recent works have proposed tailored OSD schemes specifically for short codes. To further mitigate complexity, [8]- [11] introduce advancements, while pre-configured error pattern (PEP) OSD [12] stands out as a solution that preselects

error patterns based on index weight (IW) and Hamming weight (HW) criteria. This strategy enhances parallelism and excludes error patterns with low success probabilities, thereby optimizing performance.
To better utilize the decoding capabilities of BP and PEPOSD and to release the potential of short LDPC codes, we propose the hybrid decoder of PEPOSD and BP in this paper. The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
• In this hybrid decoding framework, the initial step involves utilizing the belief propagation (BP) algorithm to attempt decoding the received vector. Should BP fail to yield a valid result within the specified conditions, the decoder seamlessly transitions to employing the preconfigured error pattern ordered statistics decoding (PEPOSD). PEPOSD then systematically tests various error patterns (EPs) until a result that successfully passes the cyclic redundancy check (CRC) is obtained. This is similar but different from [6] which processes the OSD at the end of each iteration of the BP decoding.
• We conduct a thorough error analysis for both BP and PEPOSD. For BP, decoding failures are primarily attributed to the presence of stopping and trapping sets. In contrast, for OSD variants like PEPOSD, which rely on the outcomes of hard decoding, failures occur solely when the correct EP is not included in the queried set. This fundamental difference enables PEPOSD to accurately decode distinct codewords independently. Notably, as the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) increases, the intersection of errors between BP and PEPOSD diminishes, significantly enhancing the overall performance of the hybrid decoder.
• We propose two tailored hybrid decoding strategies, each suited for different SNR environments. The first, the ”switch plan,” simply alternates between decoding algorithms based on predefined conditions. The second, the ”soft plan,” innovatively transfers soft intermediate information from BP to PEPOSD, further optimizing performance. Additionally, we establish two criteria for terminating BP: the maximum iteration limit and the trapping iteration threshold, which signifies the point where the hard output of BP ceases to change over consecutive iterations

A. CRC-LDPC and BP Decoding
A CRC-LDPC code is characterized by its code length n, k-bit-length information bits, and l-bit-length CRC, denoted by [n, k + l]. We always use the parity-check matrix Hm×n to characterize the LDPC codes, m = n − k − l. The generator matrix G(k+l)×n can be derived from H.
LDPC codes are codes specified by a sparse matrix that mainly contains zeros and only a few ones. A bipartite graph with m check nodes and n variable nodes can be created using H.
For a specific LDPC code, the standard BP can exchange directed decoding messages along the edges of its Tanner graph. Also, because of the finite block length, BP can be only a suboptimal decoder. As space is limited, the detailed procedure will not be described here.
B. PEPOSD
In OSD, two permutations, λ1(sorting by the reliability of all bits) and λ2 (choosing linear independent bits as systematic bits), are performed over y and G before decoding. After these, the received vector y is reordered by y˜ = λ2(λ1(y)). Meanwhile, permutations and Gaussian elimination transform the generator matrix G into its systematic form G. Then, a number of codewords tested are compared to find the most likely estimate.
In traditional OSD, codeword estimates are tested in the increasing order of the Hamming weights of the EP. For example, in s-order OSD, codeword estimates with Hamming weight from 1 to s of the corresponding EPs are compared. After performing inverse permutations, the best result of the codeword estimates cˆ is chosen as the output.

III. HYBRID DECODER ARCHITECTURE
In this section, we will introduce the process of the proposed hybrid decoder of BP and PEPOSD, and analyze how this scheme takes advantages.

A. Decoder Architecture
In this paper, we propose the BP-PEPOSD hybrid decoder to further improve the performance for the present decoding algorithms for the short CRC-LDPC codes. The decoder process is depicted in Fig. 1, and the BP-PEPOSD algorithm can be described as follows.
1) After receiving the vector y, the BP decoder will continuously try to decode this vector until the result is correct as can pass the parity check and CRC check so the right result is obtained, or the conditions are met so the PEPOSD decoder is activated.
2) There are also two plans of how the data is input into PEPOSD from BP. In the switch plan (the dashed line in Fig. 1), these two decoders are used together just like in a switch. Thus, if BP fails, the PEPOSD decoder just utilize the received vector. In the soft plan (the dotted line in Fig. 1), the soft data in the last iteration of BP decoder is put into the PEPOSD decoder. Based on simulations, Section IV will reveal that the plans are suitable for high and low SNR respectively.

There are two proposed conditions to stop the BP decoder and activate the PEPOSD decoder.
• Condition 1: Maximum iteration condition. (tmax in Algorithm 1) This is a similar constraint to the maximum iteration of BP, which means that the BP runs as many iterations as the condition, and a valid result cannot yet be obtained. In general circumstances, the maximum iteration of BP for LDPC is set as about tmax = 100. As in the proposed scheme, PEPOSD can compensate for the failure of BP decoder, the number of maximum iterations can be set smaller.
• Condition 2: Trapping iteration condition to early stop (ES). (bmax in Algorithm 1) This restrains the maximum number of iterations where the hard result of the BP decoder isn’t changed. For example, if the hard decision of a 4-bit vector in BP is ’1 0 0 0’ in bmax = 20 consecutive iterations, then the condition is met. It’s also

because PEPOSD can make up for BP that this rule is brought in to cut down the meaningless decoding in BP.

B. Decoding Error Analysis

We give some definitions in the scheme. Event EB denotes for the decode error of the BP decoder, and event EP denotes for the decode error of the PEPOSD decoder. Thus, C = EB ∩ EP denotes their common error. For convenience, let B = EB \ EP and P = EP \ EB.
In [15], the asymptotic normalized average distributions of a class of combinatorial configurations in the LDPC code ensembles are evaluated. The trapping and stopping sets determine the height and point of onset of the error-floor in its performance curve. This is the vital cause of EB. However, there are two different major causes of EP . The first is that the right EP is out of the range that the probable EPs are preconfigured. The second is that the decoding error is an ML error.
There are two simple examples to illustrate the differences in error causes of these two decoding algorithms. SNR is set as 2.0 dB. BP with maximum iteration b = 10 and PEPOSD with (IW,HW) = (6, 2) are compared. There is a simple [6,3] LDPC code whose parity matrix is

V. CONCLUSIONS In this paper, we introduce the BP-PEPOSD hybrid decoder to enhance the performance of short and moderate CRCLDPC codes. In this decoder, BP will first decode the received vectors, end with decoding success, or be given up when Condition 1 or 2 is met, so PEPOSD will try to obtain a valid result from the received vector or soft information from BP. The analysis declares that as the SNR grows, the ratio of CEC will decline obviously, which is the reason why our scheme outperforms greatly better in the high SNR areas.
