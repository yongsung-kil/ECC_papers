Abstract—In this paper, a complex network approach is
presented to design the Bernoulli parity-check (BPC) codes
with enhanced belief propagation (BP) decoding performance.
By investigating the degree correlation between variable and
check nodes, we
propose
the normal graph configuration
model (NGCM) to adjust the assortativity coefficient of normal
graphs, and investigate the impact of disassortativity on decoding
performances. Numerical results demonstrate that, optimizing the
disassortativity of normal graphs can significantly enhance the
BP decoding performance in both the waterfall and error floor
regions. This improvement leads to lower bit-error-rate (BER),
frame-error-rate (FER), and decoding complexity. Our study
has significant practical and theoretical value, especially for the
interdisciplinary research of complex network and coding theory.

I. INTRODUCTION
The low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, which were
originally invented by Gallager in 1962 [1], are a class
of widely recognized capacity-approaching linear codes. In
contrast, the dual of LDPC codes, namely, the low-density
generator matrix (LDGM) codes, are not that good recognized
during the rediscovery of LDPC codes [2]. Recently, there
have been some new developments in research on LDGM
codes. In [3] and [4], it has been proved that the BGM code (a
class of LDGM codes) ensembles are capacity-achieving for
binary-input output-symmetric (BIOS) memoryless channels
in terms of bit-error-rate (BER) and frame-error-rate (FER).
However, the BGM codes have higher error floors caused by
the low weight codewords. To lower down the error floors,
the Bernoulli parity-check (BPC) codes were proposed in [5],
which can be seen as the systematic BGM codes with parity-
check bits known at the decoder. Indeed, if the parity-check
matrices are generated by sampling a Bernoulli process with
small success probability, the BPC codes are a class of LDPC
codes.
Complex network theory, which provides an advanced
theoretical framework for analyzing the information spreading
dynamics in networks [6], [7], can be utilized to study the
decoding dynamics on graphs, thereby establishing a bridge
between network science and coding theory [8]. On the one

hand, the normal graph of the LDPC/LDGM codes can be
regarded as a bipartite network, with edges connecting nodes
from different sets. On the other hand, the procedure of belief
propagation (BP) decoding algorithm employed on normal
graphs exchanges the extrinsic messages between variable and
check nodes. It is well-known that the network topology, such
as the shortest path length, clustering coefficient, and degree
distribution, influences the information spreading dynamics.
For instance, small-world networks, characterized by their
shorter average path length and higher clustering coefficient,
have advantages with respect to information dissemination
efficiency and speed [9]. Another important finding is that
scale-free networks [10], whose degree distribution following
power-law, can lower down the spreading threshold and
expand the spreading scale [11].
In this paper, we design the BPC codes with enhanced
BP decoding performance based on the complex network
theory. Specifically, we analyze the degree correlation between
variable and check nodes within normal graphs and propose
the normal graph configuration model (NGCM). Based on the
NGCM, we generate normal graphs with varying assortativity
coefficient and subsequently investigate the impact of disas-
sortativity on BP decoding performance for the BPC codes.
Our simulation results show that optimizing disassortativity
of normal graphs can enhance BP performance in both
the waterfall and error floor regions. The BER and FER
performance are improved, resulting in a coding gain of 0.6
dB in terms of BER and a coding gain of 0.4 dB in terms
of FER. Furthermore, the average number of BP iterations in
the waterfall region is significantly reduced, leading to a low
decoding complexity.

B. BGM Codes and BPC Codes
In this paper, we consider the systematic BGM codes and
their variants, i.e., BPC codes.
The systematic BGM codes transform the information
vector u ∈Fk
2 into (u, x) by x = uG ∈Fm
2 , where
G is a random matrix of size k × m with each element
Gi,j(0 ≤i ≤k −1, 0 ≤j ≤m −1) being generated
independently according to the Bernoulli distribution with
success probability Pr{Gi,j = 1} = ρ ≤1/2. We denote such
a Bernoulli random matrix by G(ρ). The generator matrix is
[I G(ρ)], where I is the identity matrix of order k. The code
rate R is defined as R = k/(k + m). For ρ ≪1/2, the BGM
code is a special class of LDGM codes. In this framework,
both channel 1 and channel 2 are noisy.
It should be noted that the BGM codes have higher error
floors caused by the low weight codewords. However, the
error floors can be lowered down by implementing the BPC
codes, which fall within the framework of the systematic BGM
codes with parity-check bits known at the decoder. Given an
information vector u ∈Fk
2, a BPC code is defined by uG = 0,
where G is a Bernoulli random matrix of size k × m. The
parity-check matrix of a BPC code is given by H = GT . The
code rate R satisfies R ≥(k −m)/k. For ρ ≪1/2, the BPC
code is a class of LDPC codes. In this framework, channel 1
is noisy and channel 2 is noiseless.

IV. NUMERICAL RESULTS
In this section, we conduct simulations on systematic BGM
codes and BPC codes for ρ ≪1/2. The minimum degree of
the variable nodes is set to 2. We assume that the codeword
is mapped to binary phase shift keying (BPSK) signals and
transmitted over additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) chan-
nels. The sum-product algorithm is employed for decoding,
where the maximum iteration number is set to be 50. We first
compare the BER and FER performance of these two types
of codes, and then investigate the impact of normal graph’s
disassortativity on the BP decoding performance for the BPC
codes.

B. Impact of Disassortativity on BPC Codes
Example 2: Consider the BPC code with k = 2048, m =
1024, ρ = 0.005. The original assortativity coefficient r,
calculated using Eq. (4), for this code is −0.45. To investigate
the impact of disassortativity on BP decoding performance, we
further increase the code’s disassortativity using Algorithm 1
for a specific target r∗. The BER and FER performance of
BPC codes with different target r∗are shown in Fig. 7 and
Fig. 8, respectively. It can be observed that, as r∗decreases
from −0.45 to −0.68, coding gain increases. For example, at
the BER of 10−4, coding gains are approximately 0.2 dB, 0.32
dB, and 0.6 dB for r∗= −0.55, −0.60, −0.68, respectively.
At the FER of 10−3, coding gains are 0.17 dB, 0.31 dB, and
0.4 dB, respectively.
The intuitive explanation for the coding gain is as follows.
Disassortativity tends to connect nodes with large degree
differences, which enhances the efficiency of transferring
extrinsic information. Simultaneously, it leads nodes with
high reliability (variable nodes with higher degrees and check
nodes with lower degrees) to cluster together, further reducing
the decoding error rate. However, excessive disassortativity
can reduce coding gains, as shown by the dashed lines
in Fig. 7 and Fig. 8. From the perspective of network
science theory, this can be explained by the hindrance of the
extrinsic message’s dissemination process due to imbalanced
connections between nodes, potentially leading to bridging
and clustering effects [14]. The subplots in Fig. 7 and Fig. 8
illustrate the relationships between BER/FER and r∗. Clearly,
there exists an optimal assortativity coefficient (−0.68) that
maximizes the coding gain.

V. CONCLUSIONS
We have proposed a complex network approach to design
BPC codes with enhanced BP decoding performance. The
bipartite normal graph was first transformed into a simple
network, and then its assortativity coefficient can be calculated.
By the proposed normal graph configuration model, we gen-
erated normal graphs with a specific assortativity coefficient
and investigated the impact of disassortativity on BP decoding
performance for BPC codes. Simulation results show that the
BER and FER performance can be enhanced by optimizing the
disassortativity of normal graphs. Furthermore, the decoding
complexity is significantly reduced in the waterfall region. A
future work is to explore the relationship between the optimal
assortativity coefficient and the sparsity of BPC codes.
