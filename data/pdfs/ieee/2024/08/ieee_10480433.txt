Exploiting Parity-Polytope Geometry in Approximate and Randomized Scheduled
ADMM-LP Decoding
Amirreza Asadzadeh , Member, IEEE, Anthony Ho , Member, IEEE, Frank R. Kschischang , Fellow, IEEE, and Stark C. Draper , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract— We present two strategies to reduce the complexity of the alternating direction method of multipliers when applied to linear programming (ADMM-LP) decoding of low-density parity-check codes. First, to address the high complexity of computing a projection onto the parity polytope, the complexity bottleneck of ADMM-LP decoding, we propose the sparse affine projection algorithm (SAPA). SAPA projects onto the affine hull of χ ≤ d nearby local codewords where the check degree is d and where χ can be significantly smaller than d. Unlike exact projection, SAPA does not require a water-filling process, and thus can be implemented with lower per-iteration complexity. Second, to reduce the number of effective iterations needed for ADMM-LP decoding, we propose a randomized layered scheduling framework. Rather than updating checks in roundrobin fashion in each iteration, more “problematic” checks have a higher probability of being updated. The probability mass function that governs the selection of which checks to update is based upon the location of replica vectors inside (or on) the parity polytope. The resultant decoder converges significantly faster under this randomized scheduling than under round-robin scheduling. This makes it well suited for use in applications that limit the number of iterations.
Index Terms— Low-density parity-check codes, alternating direction method of multipliers, linear-programming decoding, parity polytope.

When the objective function of LP decoding is augmented with a penalty term to encourage integer solutions, ADMM-LP decoding performance reaches the accuracy of messagepassing algorithms [3]. To reduce decoding complexity, one can attempt to reduce the per-iteration complexity or decrease the number of decoding iterations while maintaining decoding performance. In the first part of this paper, we consider lowering per-iteration complexity by modifying the parity polytope projection step. The first proposed algorithm for projection onto the parity polytope by Barman et al. [2] requires solving a quadratic program via a water-filling process. Later, Zhang and Siegel [5] reduced the projection complexity by efficiently identifying the target cut in the parity polytope. However, the resultant projection algorithm still involves a complex waterfilling routine that includes sorting and multiple comparisons with super-linear complexity.
Inspired by these works, a number of efforts have been made to simplify the projection step by generating look-up tables [6], [7], applying iterative projections [8], [9], or introducing hardware-compatible features to the algorithm [11], [12], [14], [15], [16]. More recently, two approximate projection algorithms (and enhanced variants) have been proposed: the even-vertex algorithm [17], [18], which projects onto the closest even-parity vertex to the point to be projected, and the line-segment algorithm [19], [20], which projects onto the line segment connecting the two closest even-parity vertices to the point to be projected. While these approximation algorithms have demonstrated promising performance, they require many additional iterations to converge. In this paper, we generalize the previous approximate algorithms, and propose SAPA, the “Sparse Affine Projection Algorithm.” SAPA projects onto the affine hull of a small number of vertices of the polytope. SAPA does not require a water-filling process (as is needed in exact projection) and has a computation complexity that scales linearly with the dimension of the polytope. SAPA maintains high decoding performance even when used in conjunction with iterationlimited ADMM-LP decoders. To validate why SAPA’s sparse approximation is well matched to the ADMM-LP decoding routine, we analyze the sparsity characteristics of exact parity polytope projection, and show that the exact projection is indeed very often sparse.
A parallel research thrust aims at reducing the number of decoding iterations. Among various approaches, layered ADMM-LP decoding [21], [23] with node-wise scheduling [20], [24], [25] is successful in reducing the required number of decoding iterations by changing the schedule of decoding updates. In standard ADMM-LP decoding, all of the check/variable nodes get updated simultaneously, while in layered ADMM-LP decoding, each node (as well as its neighboring nodes) is chosen to be updated in a specific order based on a sequential (round-robin) schedule. Moreover, node-wise scheduling adjusts the order of node updates in a layered decoding framework in a greedy fashion by tracking the difference of messages in consecutive iterations. Updatescheduling in all of these methods is deterministic, visiting each check node once in every decoding iteration. We conclude the discussion of alternate schedules by commenting that care needs to be taken when comparing these schedules. E.g., for a (dv, dc)-regular LDPC code the proposal in [20] would pass dc(dv + 1) messages, while the schedule we will propose will pass only 2dc messages hence consuming less energy per iteration.
Inspired by the existing works on layered ADMM-LP decoding and considering the fact that some nodes are more “problematic” and require more frequent updates in the decoding process, we propose a randomized schedule for layered ADMM-LP decoding. In this scheme, we introduce a probability mass function (PMF) π over check nodes, in which more satisfied check nodes are assigned a lower update probability. The probability mass assigned to a check node is derived from the location of its respective replica vector inside or on the parity polytope. In every decoding round of the randomized scheme, an index is sampled from the PMF π and the check node with that index and its neighboring variable nodes are updated. We show through numerical simulations that this randomized schedule outperforms existing schemes in terms of iteration-count reduction, making this schedule well suited for iteration-constrained decoders.

C. Sparse Affine Projection Algorithm
Inspired by the observations in Example 1, we propose another approximate algorithm. We again develop an affine projection routine, but this time only involve a subset of the d nearby even-parity vertices. More precisely, we constrain the sparsity (ℓ0-“norm”) of the coefficient vector ξ to a predetermined value, denoted by χ. By enforcing this constraint, the novel approximation routine projects the point onto the affine hull of {E1, . . . , Eχ}. The details of this approximate χ-Sparse Affine Projection Algorithm (χ-SAPA) is provided in Alg. 5. Depending on the LDPC code of interest, in most cases χ-SAPA outperforms LSA in terms of both the decoding performance and the convergence speed when χ > 2, while in the remaining cases it performs on par with LSA [27], [28].
For the case in Example 1, the outcome of 1-SAPA is equal to that of EVA while 2-SAPA results in the same projected point as LSA. Although 4-SAPA reproduces the poor approximation of APA since χ = d = 4, the output of 3-SAPA precisely matches the exact projection. Thus, this family of χ-SAPA algorithms can not only scale up and achieve the performance of the other approximate algorithms, including the exact projection, but it also avoids the complex waterfilling process required in the exact projection routine. This follows because affine projection is computationally simpler than convex projection.
It is worthwhile to mention that a fixed χ will be used for all χ-SAPA projections throughout the decoding process of a certain code C. For irregular LDPC codes, where the degree of nodes could be quite diverse, there might be some cases where the degree of a check node is less than the fixed χ of that code. In these cases, χ will be reduced to the degree of that check node by definition. The following theorem as well as a complementary lemma formalize some properties of the χ-SAPA projection algorithm.

IV. RANDOMIZED LAYERED SCHEDULING
A. Randomized Schedule
In both flooding-scheduled (FS) and horizontal layered (HL) decoding, all the nodes of the Tanner graph are visited at least once in each decoding iteration. However, as decoding progresses, some check nodes are already satisfied, so their updates can be skipped without affecting decoding convergence [29]. Inspired by this, we develop a randomized layered decoding scheme in which unsatisfied check nodes are updated more frequently than satisfied ones. More precisely, we define a probability mass function on [M ], denoted by π = (π1, π2, . . . , πM ), where πj is the probability that check j and its neighboring variable nodes are chosen to be updated. The probability that each check is updated should depend on how far each check is from being completely satisfied.
The randomized layered (RL) decoding scheme is described in Alg. 6. After a single standard iteration of HL decoding (lines 2–4, Alg. 3), decoding shifts to the randomized phase. In each decoding “round,” a single check index is drawn at random according to π. The corresponding check node and its neighboring variable nodes are updated. At the end of the round, the probability mass function π is modified slightly, as the state of the updated check will (most likely) have changed. In each round of RL decoding, only one check gets updated, whereas in a single iteration of HL decoding, all M checks get updated. Thus, we consider M rounds of RL decoding as one “effective iteration.” By doing so, we develop a fair comparison between the HL and RL decoding schedules in terms of number of iterations. After each effective iteration (equivalently M decoding rounds of RL decoding) syndrome checking can be performed as an early stopping criterion. It is important to note that a specified maximum number of decoding iterations is translated, under RL decoding, to a specified maximum number of allowed effective iterations.

VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper we have introduced the SAPA (sparse affine projection algorithm) family of approximate parity polytope projection algorithms for use in ADMM-LP decoding. These algorithms approximate the projection onto the convex hull of local codewords (the parity polytope) by projection onto the affine hull of the χ nearest local codewords, where χ can be significantly less than d. SAPA reduces per-projection complexity significantly and yet can, in some cases, produce the exact projection.
We have also introduced a randomized layered (RL) schedule for ADMM-LP decoding. In this schedule check nodes are updated randomly according to a probability distribution that gives priority to problematic nodes, nodes with replica vectors that are close to the center of the parity polytope.
Simulation results show that RL-scheduled ADMM-LP decoders are compatible with χ-SAPA polytope projections whenever a sufficient number of iterations is allowed. The RL-scheduled ADMM-LP decoding with χ-SAPA converges almost as quickly as belief propagation decoding at a significant complexity reduction when compared to the stateof-the-art ADMM-LP decoders.
In both SAPA and RL scheduling, static parameter values are used for the χ and ϕ. It would be interesting to explore whether these parameters can be adjusted dynamically during decoding to improve convergence speed and reduce decoding complexity. It would also be interesting to see whether grouplayered decoding [23] can further reduce the latency of RLscheduled ADMM-LP decoders.
The randomized layered framework can be adapted to other score metrics, e.g., the message-difference metric used for

node-wise scheduling [25]. Moreover, the probability mass function π could be conditioned on the most recently chosen check(s) to prevent potentially problematic cases, such as updating the same check consecutively, or cycling through a few checks repeatedly.
