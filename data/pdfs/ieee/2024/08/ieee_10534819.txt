Weighted Parity-Check Codes for Channels With State and Asymmetric Channels
Chih Wei Ling, Graduate Student Member, IEEE, Yanxiao Liu , Graduate Student Member, IEEE, and Cheuk Ting Li , Member, IEEE

Abstract— In this paper, we introduce a new class of codes, called weighted parity-check codes, where each parity-check bit has a weight that indicates its likelihood to be one (instead of fixing each parity-check bit to be zero). It is applicable to a wide range of settings, e.g. asymmetric channels, channels with state and/or cost constraints, and the Wyner-Ziv problem, and can provably achieve the capacity. For the channel with state (Gelfand-Pinsker) setting, the proposed coding scheme has two advantages. First, it achieves the capacity of any channel with state (e.g. asymmetric channels). Second, simulation results show that the proposed code achieves a smaller error rate compared to the nested linear codes. We also discuss a sparse construction where the belief propagation algorithm can be applied to improve the coding efficiency.
Index Terms— Nested linear codes, channels with state, information embedding, asymmetric channels.
I. INTRODUCTION
I N CONVENTIONAL (linear or nonlinear) code construction, the codebook is a set, and whether a bit sequence belongs to the codebook is a binary choice. In the recent work on code construction by the Poisson functional representation [1], [2], the codebook is instead a “fuzzy set”, where each bit sequence has a weight that corresponds to the likelihood that the sequence is selected. It was shown in [2] that this “weighted codebook” construction has several advantages (e.g. better theoretical guarantees for channels with state, which will be explained later). Nevertheless, the random weight assignment in [2] is unstructured, and does not allow efficient encoding and decoding algorithms.

In this paper, which is the complete version of [3],1 we present a general code construction based on this weighted codebook idea, but with a linear structure similar to conventional linear codes. Instead of fixing each parity-check bit to zero, we assign a weight to each parity-check bit that indicates its likelihood to be one. We call this weighted parity-check (WPC) codes. We will discuss its applications to channels with state and asymmetric channels.
In a channel with state [4], [6], [7], [8], the channel statistics depend on a state variable, which is not fully known and varies over the transmission. We review some works related to channels with state information available at the encoder noncausally (i.e., the Gelfand-Pinsker setting [4]). The problem of memory with stuck-at faults was studied by Kuznetsov and Tsybakov [7], where multicoding/subcodebooks is used to establish the capacity. Their result was generalized by Gelfand and Pinsker [4] and Heegard and El Gamal [9] to the discrete memoryless channel (DMC) with discrete memoryless (DM) state, where the capacity was characterized. Costa [10] proved the capacity theorem for the Gaussian channel with additive Gaussian state (i.e., “writing on dirty paper”). Refer to [11], [12], and [13] for finite-blocklength analysis of the GelfandPinsker setting.
Note that the coding schemes used in the direct part of the proofs of the aforementioned works are based on unstructured codes which are impractical. To realize a structured code construction, Zamir et al. [14] and Barron et al. [15] considered the nested linear codes for binary-Hamming information embedding [16], [17], and nested lattice codes for Gaussian-quadratic information embedding. Nested linear codes were studied previously by Wyner [18] for the SlepianWolf problem [19], and by Shamai et al. [20] and Pradhan and Ramchandran [21] for the Wyner-Ziv problem [5]. The connection between codes for the Wyner-Ziv problem and codes for channels with state was observed in [14], [22], and [15]. In [23], Martinian and Wainwright used a class of sparse graphical codes to generate practical nested linear codes. Nested constructions using polar codes were studied in [24].

The aforementioned structured codes are for symmetric channels. For structured codes for the general (symmetric/asymmetric) channel with state, Padakandla and Pradhan [25] utilized nested linear codes together with joint typicality encoding and decoding to construct coding schemes for channels with state and broadcast channels. Efficient coding scheme based on non-linear polar codes was proposed in [26]. Ghaddar et al. [27] proposed a method for constructing coding schemes for both the asymmetric channels and the Gelfand-Pinsker problem by leveraging existing codes for symmetric point-to-point channels.
All the aforementioned works use some variants of the binning/subcodebook construction, where each message is associated with a subcodebook of input sequences (either randomly generated, or as a coset of a linear code in [14] and [15]), and the encoder chooses an input sequence within the subcodebook close to the state sequence. The weighted codebook construction [2] eliminates the need of subcodebooks, and gives finite-blocklength and second-order error bounds sharper than previous finite-blocklength results in [11], [12], and [13]. An intuitive reason is that the subcodebook construction forces the encoder to choose a sequence within the subcodebook (a non-fuzzy set), which may result in having a sequence far from the state sequence. In the weighted codebook construction, we have a fuzzy set instead, so the encoder can trade-off between choosing a high-weight sequence (higher likelihood of being chosen by the decoder) and a low-weight sequence (which may be closer to the state sequence) depending on the state. The downside is that the weighted codebook in [2] is unstructured.
The weighted parity-check codes in this paper combine the advantages of the linear construction [14], [15] and the weighted codebook [2], giving a structured code construction that provably achieves the capacity, and has good practical performance. Experiment results show that our codes attain a smaller error rate compared to nested linear codes [14], [15]. Moreover, our codes can be applied on a sparse parity-check matrix in a manner similar to the low-density parity-check codes [28], [29], which allows the use of the belief propagation algorithm [30], [31], [32] to improve the coding efficiency.
Another application of our codes is to asymmetric channels (e.g. Z-channel), which arises in storage technologies such as flash memories [33], [34]. It was observed in [27] that a code for binary asymmetric channels can be obtained from a code for symmetric channels with state by setting the state sequence to zero. Our code can be applied to general (symmetric/asymmetric) channels with or without state, which is more general than [27] which applies only to the case where the distribution of the channel input conditional on the state is a binary symmetric channel.2 Compared to [25] which is also general, our scheme does not require joint typicality encoding and decoding. Instead, it admits an encoding and decoding scheme with a simple product structure where belief propagation can be applicable.
2The authors in [27] have generalized their work to any general input distribution in their more recent paper [35]. It would be interesting to investigate whether weighted parity-check bits can be applied to the approach in [35], though we leave this for future studies.

A. Other Related Works
Fuzzy codes are generalizations of conventional codes where the codebook is a fuzzy set [36], [37], [38]. The code in this paper can also be regarded as a fuzzy code. Nevertheless, this paper focuses on a concrete capacity-achieving coding scheme, whereas [36], [37], [38] are more about properties (e.g. distances) of general fuzzy codes in the context of coding theory, and have not discussed capacity-achieving properties. We also remark that the application of soft sets (generalization of fuzzy sets) to coding was studied in [39]. The construction used in this paper is unrelated to [36], [37], [38], and [39].
In decoding algorithms for linear codes for soft-information channels (e.g. AWGN), weighted parity-check information is sometimes utilized (e.g. [40]), where the weights come from the soft-information channel instead of being associated with the parity-check bit itself (as in this paper). In the analysis on Gallager codes in [41], parity-check bits were assumed to be observed with error for the sake of analytical tractability, though this soft parity-check assumption was only used in the analysis of decoding error instead of the code construction.
To design a capacity-achieving coding scheme for asymmetric channels, there are at least two paradigms and their variants in the literature [42]. A classic solution to the problem of coding over asymmetric setting was given by Gallager in [43], which concatenates a standard linear code with a non-linear mapping so that the input distribution and the generated codeword sequence to the channel are properly biased. However, a large extended alphabet size is required if the optimal non-uniform input distribution cannot be approximated by simple rational numbers [44]. To overcome the drawback of Gallager’s scheme, the integrated scheme is introduced [42], [44], [45]. The basic idea of the integrated scheme is to use a coding scheme that is simultaneously good for the lossless source coding to create a non-uniform distribution for the channel input, and for the channel coding to provide the reliability protection for the transmission over the channel [42]. A provably capacity-achieving scheme under this paradigm was first proposed in [44] in the context of polar codes, and the proposed scheme does not require an extended alphabet. Another realization of the paradigm in the context of sparse graph codes was given in [45] that allows the use of the belief propagation algorithm. Our paper provides an alternative approach to construct a code with any (uniform/non-uniform) input distribution, with a theoretical guarantee of its capacityachieving property.
We emphasize that the main contribution of this paper is the introduction of weights to linear codes, and how this modification can recover the nested linear codes [14], [15] as a special case, and achieve a smaller error rate compared to the nested linear codes, while being capacity-achieving for general (symmetric/asymmetric) channels with state and the WynerZiv setting. Therefore, the experiments in this paper will focus on comparing the proposed weighted construction with the original nested linear codes, in order to highlight the improvement brought by introducing the weights (while keeping other parameters of the codes equal). We only perform the experiment on the threshold linear option (12) for the parity bias PQ. The constant and linear option have significantly poorer performance. Intuitively, it is undesirable to have a small nonzero qi. Compared to the encoder, the decoder has a stronger belief on the codeword x, allowing it to ignore the small value of qi and decode to a codeword with the i-th parity-check bit (xˆHT )k+i = 1. Therefore, having a small nonzero qi makes it unlikely for the encoder to select 1 as the i-th parity-check bit, but not as unlikely for the decoder, resulting in the existence of codewords that cannot be selected by the encoder, but can be selected by the decoder. Therefore, a “threshold” method where each qi is either zero, one, or bounded away from zero and one is desirable. The performance of the threshold linear option is likely due to the combination of a hard threshold with nonuniform choices of the nonzero non-one qi’s.

Nevertheless, having a good performance for a small n shows that the WPC codes can be applied to practical delay-constrained communication settings where a small blocklength is desirable. We remark that the experiments in this paper focuses on the comparison between the proposed weighted construction and the unweighted nested linear codes, in order to show the improvement brought by introducing the weight alone. Sections IV and V show that the introduction of weights, while keeping other parameters of the codes equal, can reduce the error rate. We did not compare the low-density weighted parity-check codes to other approaches to channels with state, such as polar codes [24], [35], [44] and spatially-coupled codes [45], since such comparisons will be dominated by whether low-density parity-check codes or polar codes are better (or whether spatially-coupled codes are suitable) for the particular channel considered. Nevertheless, it will be interesting to study whether weighted parity-check bits can be applied to the approaches in [24], [35], [44], and [45], and whether the introduction of weights (while keeping other parameters equal) can offer improvements to these approaches. We leave these extensions to future studies.

VII. CONCLUSION AND DISCUSSIONS
In this paper, we introduced the weighted parity-check (WPC) codes, where weights are attached to the parity-check bits. It is applicable to channels with state [4], asymmetric channels, and the Wyner-Ziv problem [5]. We proved that the WPC codes are capacity-achieving for (symmetric or asymmetric) channels with state and the Wyner-Ziv problem. We performed experiments which show the reduction of error rate of the proposed WPC codes compared to the nested linear codes [14], [15] for channels with state. We also performed experiments on a sparse construction for the WPC codes where the belief propagation algorithm can be applied.
A potential future direction is to apply the WPC codes to multiuser channels, such as broadcast channels [48] and interference channels [49]. Another direction is to perform a theoretical analysis on the WPC codes with sparse parity-check matrices (recall that the theorems in this paper assume that the parity-check matrix is a random dense matrix). The key is to prove a version of Lemma 2 that applies to sparse matrices, which does not appear to be straightforward.
