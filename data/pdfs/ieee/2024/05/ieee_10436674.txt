Belief Propagation Decoding for Short-Length Codes Based on Sparse Tanner Graph
Zongyao Li , Yifei Shen , Member, IEEE, Yuqing Ren , Graduate Student Member, IEEE, Yongming Huang , Senior Member, IEEE, Xiaohu You , Fellow, IEEE, and Chuan Zhang , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract— In this letter, we present a novel approach to improve belief propagation (BP) decoding performance by sparsifying the Tanner graph. Our proposed method first constructs a low-weight parity-check (LWPC) matrix with increased rows based on the original parity-check matrix. Then, all 4-cycles in the LWPC matrix are eliminated by adding auxiliary variable nodes and corresponding parity-check rows, resulting in the generalized LWPC (G-LWPC) matrix. Compared to the original matrix, the sparsity is greatly improved without altering the encoding constraints. Simulation results show that BP decoding based on the G-LWPC matrix is particularly effective for short code lengths. For a (128, 20) polar code with 24-bit cyclic redundancy check, our proposed G-LWPC matrix reduces the density from 17.4% to 1.3%, and with 10 iterations, BP decoding can achieve the performance of successive cancellation list decoding with a list size of 32.
Index Terms— Belief propagation, Tanner graph, minimumweight codeword, low-weight parity-check, sparse graph.
I. INTRODUCTION
THE increasing diversity of channel coding standards in wireless communications has created an urgent need for co-architectural design in decoder hardware, making research on universal decoders a hot topic. Current universal channel decoders involves the message-passing decoding of Turbo codes, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, and
polar codes [1], [2], [3]. Emerging ultra-reliable low-latency communication (uRLLC) scenarios necessitate the design of short-length channel codes to meet the stringent latency con-
straints [4]. For such cases, conventional soft message-passing decoding is unable to achieve ultra-high reliability due to their
factor graph lacking sparsity, even for LDPC codes [5].

In this work, we aim to improve the error-correction performance of message-passing decoding algorithm for short code lengths and low-to-moderate code rates, which is precisely the regime specified in uRLLC [6]. Specifically, we focus on belief propagation (BP) decoding on Tanner graphs [7], as its generality and high parallelism make it feasible to implement a high-throughput and low-latency universal hardware decoder. However, the Tanner graph exhibits high density (low sparsity) for favored short-length codes, such as polar codes, Bose-Chaudhuri-Hocquenghem (BCH) codes, and Reed-Muller (RM) codes, as evidenced by the considerable number of 4-cycles. This low sparsity limits the error-correction performance of conventional BP decoding for those high-density parity-check (HDPC) codes [8].
Research on BP decoding for HDPC codes can be broadly classified into two categories. The first category explores highly reliable codewords by transforming different factor graphs [9], [10], [11], and the second category employs neural network to learn the parameters in soft-message propagation [12], [13]. Although increasing the number of parameters considered can lead to better performance improvement, it limits the generalizability of the decoder.
Most decoders proposed using these two techniques are based on the Tanner graph constructed directly from the original parity-check matrix. Bossert discovered that using the minimum-weight codeword of the dual code as the minimum-weight parity-check (MWPC) matrix can improve the decoding performance [14], [15]. Based on this finding, a pruned BP decoder was proposed in [16], which has fewer check nodes (CNs) and even better performance than using the full MWPC matrix. However, the Tanner graph still contains a large number of harmful 4-cycles, and the learning cost becomes unaffordable when the code length reaches 128.
We first propose a scheme to remove 4-cycles from the low-weight parity-check (LWPC) matrix, to improve the performance of plain BP decoding for short block codes. A similar idea has been used for Turbo codes, based on polynomials instead of matrices [17]. The removal of 4-cycles generally introduces numerous auxiliary variable nodes (VNs) [18], [19], [20], whose unknown soft messages can potentially diminish the benefits gained. To overcome this challenge, we carefully balance the size of the LWPC matrix to ensure an appropriate trade-off between complexity and performance. The numerical results show that BP decoding with the proposed sparse parity-check matrix achieves superior error-rate performance for short code lengths. The BP decoding on the proposed sparse Tanner graph has great potential for a universal decoder design.

II. PRELIMINARIES
A. Notations
In this letter, we denote a code with length N and information length K as an (N, K) code. Its standard parity-check matrix H has a size of M × N , where M = N − K, and can be transformed to a Tanner graph [7] that consists of M CNs (parity-check rows) and N VNs (coded bits columns), with the CNs, VNs, and edges included in C, V, and E, respectively. The density of the Tanner graph is defined as |E|/(M × N ). We assume that the codewords x are modulated by binary phase shift keying (BPSK) modulation and transmitted through additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels to obtain y.

B. Transformation of Parity-Check Matrix

For HDPC codes, the large number of short cycles in the

Tanner graph significantly impacts the performance of BP

decoding. While it is not possible to generate a large-girth

parity-check matrix for a given code, as is done in the design

of LDPC codes, it is still feasible to extract a redundant

parity-check matrix by increasing the number of rows and

columns without altering the encoding scheme constraints.

One effective approach is to utilize the minimum weight

codewords of the dual code as a parity-check matrix. This

can be seen as a linear row transformation originating from

the standard H, resulting in a significantly greater number of rows M ′ than M . For instance, in the case of the (32, 16) RM code, M ′ = 620 ≫ 16. Such MWPC-based decoding was first

proposed in 1986 [14] and later applied to iterative decoding

in 1998 [15]. Recently, MWPC has regained attention for

decoding short-length codes, with researchers pruning the full

MWPC matrix based on the bit reliability [21] or neural

performance and complexity reduction. Unfortunately, despite

III. PROPOSED SPARSITY-INCREASING SCHEME
In this section, we propose a two-step scheme for extracting the sparse Tanner graph given a specific code. First, we generate an LWPC matrix, and subsequently eliminate all of its 4-cycles by introducing auxiliary bits. The framework is shown in Fig. 1 and the detailed procedure is listed in Algorithm 1.

A. Low-Weight Parity-Checks
In previous MWPC-based decoding methods [16], [21], redundant parity-check rows are generated by pruning the full MWPC matrix, retaining 195 rows instead of 620 rows for the (32, 16) RM code [16]. In our scheme, we aim to minimize the number of redundant parity-check rows to avoid too many auxiliary VNs employed to break 4-cycles. Meanwhile, to utilize all the parity constraints, the matrix before the 4-cycles removal must keep the rank of the standard matrix H. Sometimes the full MWPC matrix is deficient to satisfy the rank constraint, so we need to extract parity-check rows with low weights beyond the minimum weight, and the resulting parity checks are referred to as LWPCs. Hereafter, the matrix made up of LWPCs is denoted as HLW.
The procedure for generating HLW is shown in the upper part of Fig. 1 and outlined in Step1 of Algorithm 1. The standard matrix H and the desired CN number nc that restricts complexity are used as inputs. Starting with a weight d
of 1, we use an iterative algorithm [23] based on Gaussian elimination to generate LWPCs with weight d, and all the generated LWPCs with weight d form a matrix Hd, which is stored in the overcomplete matrix HOC. To ensure the use of all parity constraints, the algorithm selects independent rows from HOC to form a full-rank matrix HFR, adds HFR to HLW, and removes HFR from HOC. Lower-weight rows are prioritized. If the current HOC is unable to fulfill the full-rank constraint for HFR construction, the weight d is incremented by one, and new LWPCs with the increased weight are generated and added to HOC. The process is repeated until an HLW with a CN number of nc is constructed.

IV. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
In this section, we present experimental results for BP decoding on the proposed sparse Tanner graph, using RM codes and 5G polar codes as two example codes. We first provide results for the widely studied (32, 16) RM code [16], [21] to compare with other MWPC-based methods and analysis the relationship between performance and sparsity. For 5G polar codes, the code length is set to 128. In addition to uRLLCpreferred low-rate codes [4], half-rate codes are also simulated to demonstrate the effectiveness of our scheme, resulting in information lengths 20, 40, and 64. Both downlink and uplink standard codes are considered, where 24-bit and 11-bit cyclic redundancy check (CRC) codes are concatenated and incorporated into the standard parity-check matrix, respectively.
Our BP decodings on HLW and HG-LW are abbreviated as BPLWPC and BPG-LWPC, respectively. They are based on the normalized min-sum algorithm with a damping factor of 0.7, I = 10 iterations for RM codes and low-rate polar codes, and I = 100 iterations for (128, 64) polar codes. For BPG-LWPC, flooding message-passing schedule is employed respectively for original CNs and auxiliary CNs, resulting in a coarselayered schedule. The complexity is evaluated by the matrix sparsity and decoding latency. Results for ordered statistics decoding with order 3 (OSD-3) are also provided as an estimation of the maximum likelihood (ML) performance.

In addition, BPG-LWPC has comparable decoding latency to CA-SCL decoding in most cases. The advantage of our BPG-LWPC for downlink low-rate cases results from the very low row weight of the sparse matrix HG-LWPC. When the code rate increases or CRC length decreases, the row weights are higher and the required auxiliary VNs increases, which limits the gain through the matrix sparsification. A feasible solution is to use BP list decoding, similar to SCL improving SC decoding [17].
V. CONCLUSION
In this work, we propose a two-step scheme to increase the sparsity of the parity-check matrix given specific channel codes. We test the effectiveness for RM codes and 5G polar codes, our decoding scheme shows better performance than traditional BP decoding. In addition, it can outperform CA-SCL decoding for short-length and low-rate 5G polar codes.
