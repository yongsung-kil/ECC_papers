A 128 Gb/s LDPC Decoder Using Partial Syndrome-based Dynamic Decoding Scheme for
Terahertz Wireless Multi-Media Networks

Tsung Han Wu, Ching Liang Yeh, Yi-Shan Huang, Shyh Jye Jou Institute of Electronics
National Yang Ming Chia Tung University HsinChu, Taiwan R.O.C. jasonwu0725@gmail.com

Abstract—This paper presents a low power and high

throughput multi-framed pipelined LDPC decoder architecture

based on a novel partial syndrome-based dynamic decoding

(PSDD) approach. The proposed PSDD can reduce clock cycle

to allow the LDPC decoder to be implemented with better

energy efficiency. We propose a high throughput sorting

method and implement the LDPC decoder with a pipelined

multi-frame VLSI architecture. The implementation results for

the IEEE 802.15.3d Thz standard shows that the proposed

design has a coding gain of

at the specified SNR of 18.1 dB

with 16 QAM modulation. Furthermore, the proposed design

can achieve a throughput rate of 128.5 Gbps with the 16nm

FinFET CMOS process, respectively.

Keywords—low-density parity check (LDPC) decoder, message-passing schedule, 6G, Terahertz, dynamic decoding
I. INTRODUCTION
LDPC code [1,2] has the low-density feature of the parity check matrices and the high degree of parallelism becomes an attractive coding scheme for error detection and error correction. The belief propagation algorithm (BP) [3], provides an important mathematical tool to decode over a factor-graph representation of a LDPC code.
In this paper, an LDPC decoder based on the IEEE 802.15.3d which is designed for date rates of up to 100 Gb/s and BER of 10 using Sub-THz band. We propose a novel dynamic schedule strategy, referred to as partial syndromebased dynamic decoding (PSDD) which combining the advantages of the layered schedule algorithm and the dynamic schedule algorithm. It allows the LDPC decoder dynamically updating the schedule based on the partial syndrome. This results in a reduction of the total clock cycle of the decoding process so as to reduce the power consumption. The proposed architecture combines the PSDD approach with existing multi-frame pipelining to provide a low power LDPC decoder with high throughput capability.

IV. HIGH THROUGHPUT SORTING ARCHITECTURE
For the LDPC decoder with PSDD algorithm, the bottleneck to increase the decoding rate is to find the 1st, 2nd, and 3rd minimum of the posterior probability and send them back to the variable node. Therefore, we propose a high throughput sorting architecture.
As explained in Section III, the row degree of H matrix defined by IEEE 802.15.3d is forty-five. That is, the check node processing in PSDD algorithm includes sorting the three most minimums (denoted by 6 '1, 6 '2 and 6 '3) out of the forty-five incoming messages and sign bits for each check node units (CNUs) based on Eqn. (1).
To achieve less latency time and hardware complexity, we adopt the divide-and-conquer method to find the minimums out of multiple inputs. Therefore, we first find the 15 mins out of the 45 and then 9 mins out of 15 and finally, 3 mins out of 15. We extend the rejection-based scheme [5], which is based on the tree structure so that non-useful inputs are rejected and the rest are propagated forward. In our proposed sorting method, we used 5 × 3 -MIN and 9 × 3 -MIN comparator circuits. With these comparator circuits, we can construct the sorting architecture to find the three minimums out of forty-five inputs. Fig. 4 shows the proposed sorting architecture, we use the 9 × 3-MIN blocks for both the first and the last stages, and 5 × 3-MIN blocks for the middle stage. For this method, the outputs, namely O1, O2, and O3, are in unknown order.

V. HARDWARE IMPLEMENTATION
The architecture presented in this section is based on the IEEE 802.15.3d with partial syndrome dynamic decoding presented in Section III.
A. Overall Decoder Architecture
The top-level block diagram of the LDPC decoder is shown in Fig. 5. To implement high throughput and optimal hardware utilization, we adopt the proposed multi-frame interleaved architecture [6] with three pipeline stages so the LDPC decoder can decode three frames simultaneously. Fig. 6. shows the layer accessing sequences. In a pipelined multi-frame design, the computing resources are shared among different LDPC decoding schemes. However, additional storage elements are required to be included for each LDPC frame. Therefore, there are three variable node registers (VNR) and each of them stores the information of 1440 variable nodes. We use 6 bits for word length so each VNR stores 8640 bits. To achieve high clock frequency, we use a register block to store the information instead of SRAM or register file. The notation F#L# in Fig. 6 indicates the LDPC frame and the layer ID, respectively.
B. Partial Syndrome Dynamic Decoding (PSDD) Shifter Network
The PSDD shifter network is shown in Fig. 7. At first, we use the sign bit of posteriori LLRs to make hard decision as shown in Eqn. (3). We can use XOR to get the syndrome (m), which is 96 bits with Syndrome Detection block. Then, we use the Partial Syndrome Unit to produce the partial syndrome (• -. Because we split [ into 4 layers, there are 4 Partial Syndrome Units and each of them would OR 24 bits to produce the partial syndrome. At the end of the PSDD shifter network, we circularly shift the posteriori LLRs according to the partial syndrome (• - in the shifter whose detail operation is shown in Fig. 7. The >>>24/48/72 blocks mean the circular shift of 24 bits to the right in each submatrix. As explained in Section III, the [ matrix defined by the 802.15.3d is constructed by 15 submatrices with dimension of 96 × 96. After the shifter, we generate the output which is stored in the VNR#1.

C. Implementation Results
The fixed-point bit-true simulation results by using C++ are shown in Fig. 8. We can use 6 bits quantization scheme to achieve a BER performance similar to that by using floating point, with negligible difference of 0.1 dB at the BER level of 10 . Furthermore, the coding gain is 10 k at the specified SNR of 18.1 dB with the fitting results.
Because there is not yet other proposed LDPC decoder for IEEE 802.15.3d, Table II includes an overview of designs with similar wireless communication standard. Although the degree of check node in IEEE 802.15.3d is the highest as compared to other existing designs, our proposed design can reach the highest clock frequency with three pipeline stages. This is because of the high-throughput sorting architecture capable of achieving such a high clock rate. Compared to the Layered algorithm [7], the proposed PSDD algorithm and the novelty of the architecture allow us to achieve the same performance with almost 1/4 power consumption and reduce more than 78 % of the core area. As compared to the IEEE 802.11ad/ay, the codeword length defined in 802.15.3d is much longer. This means we require more storage elements for variable nodes. However, our gate count is smaller than [7] with the proposed decoder architecture. Our proposed LDPC decoder can achieve a throughput rate of 128.5 Gbps with coding rate 14/15. The fabricated chip with self-test capability of the proposed design with the 16nm FinFET process is shown in Fig. 9.
VI. CONCLUSIONS
This paper presents a novel message passing schedule algorithm based on the IEEE 802.15.3d to reduce the power consumption. The chip measurement results verify that the design with PSDD algorithm and proposed high throughput sorting architecture can achieve a throughput rate of 128.5 Gbps with coding gain of 108@SNR 18.1 dB.
