Design of DNA Storage Coding Scheme With LDPC Codes and Interleaving
Jae-Won Kim , Member, IEEE, Jaeho Jeong , Graduate Student Member, IEEE, Hee-Youl Kwak , Member, IEEE, and Jong-Seon No , Fellow, IEEE

Abstract— In this paper, we propose a new coding scheme for DNA storage using low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes and interleaving techniques. While conventional coding schemes generally employ error correcting codes in both inter and intra-oligo directions, we show that inter-oligo LDPC codes, optimized by differential evolution, are sufficient in ensuring the reliability of DNA storage due to the powerful soft decoding of LDPC codes. In addition, we apply interleaving techniques for handling non-uniform error characteristics of DNA storage to enhance the decoding performance. Consequently, the proposed coding scheme reduces the required number of oligo reads for perfect recovery by 26.25% ∼ 38.5% compared to existing state-of-the-art coding schemes. Moreover, we develop an analytical DNA channel model in terms of non-uniform binary symmetric channels. This mathematical model allows us to demonstrate the superiority of the proposed coding scheme while isolating the experimental variation, as well as confirm the independent effects of LDPC codes and interleaving techniques.
Index Terms— DNA storage, inter-oligo coding, interleaving, low-density parity-check (LDPC) code, non-uniformity.

In DNA storage, DNA sequences made up of four bases (Adenine, Cytosine, Guanine, and Thymine, abbreviated as A, C, G, T , respectively) are artificially synthesized to store data [3], [4], [5], [6]. In order to increase the storage density, these DNA sequences are synthesized as singlestranded ones, called oligonucleotides or oligos. The process of retrieving data from DNA storage composes of polymerase chain reaction (PCR) amplification [7] and sequencing.
Since various types of errors such as substitution, insertion, and deletion errors are occurred at every stage of DNA data processing, lots of research works have been studied to cope with these errors to ensure the data reliability of DNA storage. For analysis on the error occurrence, it has been shown that they are largely dependent on some constraints imposed on DNA sequences [8], [9], [10], [11], [12], where the most commonly considered constraints are the homopolymer runlength and GC-content. In order to satisfy these constraints on DNA sequences, constrained coding schemes have been studied to reduce error rates [13], [14], [15]. In addition to conventional error correcting codes only having the substitution error correcting capability, edit error correcting codes were studied to correct insertion and deletion errors [16], [17].
By incorporating these techniques as well as several signal processing algorithms, DNA coding schemes with encoding and decoding methods have been researched to reliably store data. In the early stage of DNA storage, the rotating code was mainly utilized [18], [19]. Due to the simplicity and efficiency for satisfying the aforementioned constraints, the coding scheme using the rotating code with the blocking strategy was studied to handle several constraints and error rates [20]. In addition, numerous coding schemes have been developed using various types of error correcting codes for DNA storage: deletion error correcting codes [21], ReedSolomon (RS) codes [22], [23], [24], fountain codes [25], [26], [27], low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [28], [29], and convolutional codes [30]. More recently, new approaches of using machine learning based techniques such as the long short term memory (LSTM) [31] and a graph convolutional network with self-attention [32] have been explored.

Due to the powerful decoding performance of LDPC codes and effectiveness of interleaving techniques, we can ensure the reliability of DNA storage without using them. The detailed explanation, motivation, and main results for the proposed coding scheme are given as follows.
A. Inter-Oligo LDPC Codes for Error Correction
It was recently shown that decoding using soft information in FASTQ files [34] can improve the reliability (i.e., decoding performance) of DNA storage [26]. To be specific, they showed that the iterative soft decoding algorithm using quality-scores in FASTQ files and sequencing statistics outperforms the hard decoding algorithm [27] under the same encoding structure [25], where Luby transform (LT) codes for the inter-oligo direction and RS codes for the intra-oligo direction are employed. It is noticeable that soft LT decoding performed better although LT codes are originally designed for hard decoding. Therefore, it is reasonable to expect a further improvement by using error correcting codes designed for soft decoding. Viewed in this light, this paper utilizes LDPC codes for the inter-oligo direction, where LDPC codes are well-known for their strong error correction capability under soft decoding.
B. Intra-Oligo CRC Codes for Error Detection
In general, most of DNA storage coding schemes have utilized intra-oligo error correcting codes (e.g., RS codes [23], [25], [26], [27]). However, employing intra-oligo error correcting codes results in the increased computational complexity. In contrast to those works, we utilize CRC codes with the much lower decoding complexity for the intra-oligo direction. In fact, CRC codes are a preferable option over conventional intra-oligo codes (e.g., RS codes) when combined with inter-oligo LDPC codes. In the perspective of LDPC codes, undetected errors in index positions of oligos result in incorrect information for variable nodes, which is critical for the decoding of LDPC codes. However, the conventional intra-oligo codes exhibit a comparatively higher probability of undetected errors compared to CRC codes. Although CRC codes lack error correcting capabilities, they can detect errors with a high probability and then we can discard the oligo reads with incorrect index positions for decoding. In other words, CRC codes support LDPC codes by accurately identifying the variable node indices, showing a synergistic relationship between the CRC codes and LDPC codes.
C. Interleaving Techniques to Smooth Out Non-Uniform Error Characteristics
By comparing error statistics from sequencing results under the same sequencing conditions, non-uniform error characteristics in each base position are discussed. In order to handle this non-uniformity, we employ interleaving techniques while analytically investigating the positive effects of using them for DNA storage. Since DNA storage channels usually

show non-uniform error characteristics, there have been some studies utilizing interleaving in DNA storage [17], [28], [35]. However, they did not include an analysis of the positive effects of interleaving explicitly. Without interleaving techniques, the inter-oligo LDPC code at the position with the worst error rate becomes the primary bottleneck for decoding success of the entire system, leading to the overall performance degradation. By employing interleaving techniques, the performance degradation can be alleviated by distributing the error probabilities more evenly across all inter-oligo LDPC codes.
D. Reasons for Not Adopting Constrained Coding and Edit Error Correcting Codes
Despite of the effectiveness of constrained coding and edit error correcting codes, they inevitably generate additional parity bits and suffer from a rate-loss, which means that the amount of information data to be stored is reduced. The rate-loss induced by them is not negligible for practical applications of commercial devices that require very high code-rates such as over 0.9. In this perspective, there were some research works to overcome errors without using them [23], [26], [27], [29]. In these works, the constraints such as the homopolymer runlength and GC-content are not controlled by constrained coding. In addition, insertion and deletion errors are treated as long burst substitution errors or corrected using signal processing algorithms. These works show that it is possible to maintain the reliability of DNA storage without constrained coding or edit error correcting codes, where the redundancy bits needed for them can be replaced with parity bits for correcting substitution errors. In this paper, we also do not employ constrained coding and edit error correcting codes to avoid the rate-loss which is important in memory devices.
E. Performance Improvement
From the synthesis/sequencing experiments, we show that the proposed coding scheme provides an improved number of oligo reads for perfect recovery than those of [25], [26], and [27] under the same experimental conditions as well as coding parameters. Although there are a few works such as [29] suggesting the required number of oligo reads for perfect recovery, the experimental conditions are different. To provide a fair comparison between coding schemes, we only compare coding schemes having the same experimental conditions. Specifically, 26.25% ∼ 38.5% of gain is achieved in terms of the required number of oligo reads for perfect recovery with the reduced decoding complexity.
F. DNA Channel Modeling
Although we adopt the same experimental conditions, the experimental variation of sequencing results exists due to biochemical properties of DNA storage. To overcome this issue, we model the DNA channel as non-uniform binary symmetric channels (BSCs), allowing for a fair comparison of coding schemes in a mathematical channel model while isolating the experimental variation. II. ENCODING AND DECODING STRUCTURES
In this section, we explain the encoding and decoding structures of the proposed coding scheme. As mentioned before, our main objective is to improve the reliability of the DNA storage system, which is evaluated under the same experimental conditions for a fair comparison. Thus, we keep several parameters and conditions similar to those of the previous works [26], [27] while using different encoding and decoding schemes.
A. Encoding Structures
We first summarize the encoding structure used in [26] and [27]. There are 18000 oligos, where each oligo consists of 152 bases except sequencing primers: 1) 16 bases for LT seeds, 2) 8 bases for RS parities, and 3) 128 bases for payloads. For the intra-oligo code, the (38, 36) RS code over GF(28) is used for each oligo. Across 18000 oligos, the (18000, 16050) binary LT code is applied for inter-oligo coding without interleaving. Overall, there are 18000 RS codes in the intra-way and 256 LT codes in the inter-way. Further, they use two sequencing primers at both ends, that is, 1) for 5’ side: GT T C AG AGT T C T AC AGT CC G AC G AT C and 2) for 3’ side: T GG A AT T C T C GGGT GCC A AGG.
While keeping the above coding parameters as much as possible, we propose a new coding scheme using oligos with the length of 152, as shown in Fig. 1, in addition to the above two sequencing primers at both ends. Then, the total length of each oligo is 199 including sequencing primers. Since the previous coding scheme utilizes 16+8 = 24 bases for seed information and intra-oligo coding parities, we also allocate 24 bases for oligo-index and CRC positions. Specifically, 8 bases are allocated for index information and remaining 16 bases are used for CRC parities generated by the following polynomial: x 32 + x 26 + x 23 + x 22 + x 16 + x 12 + x 11 + x 10 + x 8 + x 7 + x 5 + x4 + x2 + x1 + 1. Unlike conventional coding schemes that

typically employ intra-oligo error correcting codes for index and payload positions, we solely employ CRC codes for error detection of index positions (not correction). This is because we expect that the proposed LDPC code with interleaving can guarantee the reliability provided that there are no errors in the index positions. The remaining 128 bases correspond to payload positions and the number of oligos to be synthesized is 17920, which is smaller than the number of oligos 18000 in the encoding structure [26], [27]. Thus, all coding parameters are matched except for the negligible difference of the code length between inter-oligo error correcting codes.
In the proposed coding scheme, two main methodologies are used: 1) LDPC codes and 2) interleaving techniques. LDPC codes have been widely used for communication systems such as 5G new radio channels as well as storage devices including HDDs and SSDs due to their outstanding error correcting capabilities using soft information [37], [38]. Similarly, interleaving techniques are also widely adopted to randomize burst errors and handle non-uniform error characteristics [39]. These methodologies are particularly beneficial for DNA storage channels, which provide soft information and exhibit non-uniform characteristics in terms of position errors. Note that position errors are determined only by whether base calls differ at the same positions, which means insertion and deletion errors are regarded as substitution errors for each position. Specifically, assume that the stitched oligo read (say R1) has the correct length 152. Then, we can find the synthesized oligo (say O1) corresponding to R1 based on the edit distance. By comparing each position in R1 and O1, we determine whether a position error occurs. Then, position error rates are calculated by the proportion of position errors in each position based on all stitched oligo reads with the correct length 152.
For inter-oligo error correcting codes, we design a protograph-based LDPC code using the differential evolution algorithm [40], which is known as an efficient method for designing capacity-approaching LDPC codes. Since the precise channel model for DNA storage with soft information cannot be established, we optimize the LDPC protograph under the uniform additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels, while adjusting the design parameters to be suitable for DNA storage. Before presenting the encoding structure in detail, we want to show the motivation for employing the interleaving technique with position error statistics. From position error statistics (data A) of [26], we first sort 152 base positions in the descending order of position error rates as in Fig. 1(a). To be specific, we obtain the sorted order {152, 151, . . . , 3, 4, 5, 6} for the original order {1, 2, . . . , 151, 152}. Since indices are the most important data of oligos, the last 24 sorted positions, having the highest reliability, are used for storing indices and CRC parities for all oligos. This sorting method is conducted to increase a possibility that users obtain the correct variable node indices of inter-oligo LDPC codes when recovering stored data.
Furthermore, we observe that the components of the most reliable 24 positions of three different sequencing results for one synthesized oligo pool [26], [27], [41] coincide except for

only one position. Since the orders are different for each result, we do not directly follow the ordering derived from data A in Fig. 1(a) but randomly mix the order of the last 24 positions to account for the possible biochemical variation. We also observe that the last 24 positions {2, 3, 4, . . . , 25} are consecutive, albeit in varying orders. Since 8 bases represent 16 bits and 216 > 17920, we choose 17920 index sequences among 216 sequences having the smallest homopolymer runlength in the perspective of the synthesized form. This selection is feasible due to the fact that the most reliable 24 base positions are consecutive.
The remaining base positions are assigned to the payloads. To mitigate the random effect of biological experiments, we group two base indices into one group to obtain 64 groups {G1, G2, . . . , G64}. Since the first two bases {152, 151} in Fig. 1(a) show much higher position error probabilities than the others (will be shown in Fig. 2), we group these two bases into G1. B. Decoding Structures
Since soft decoding of LDPC codes needs to derive log-likelihood ratios (LLRs) from channel information, we produce LLRs for each bit position using FASTQ files and conditional position error probabilities from sequencing statistics of the entire oligo reads. Since the LLR calculation method of stitched oligo reads follows the method in [26], we provide a brief explanation here. Note that stitched oligo reads with the correct length 152 are used for LLR calculation and decoding. First, we obtain conditional error probabilities of each base call for all positions based on edit distances between entire stitched oligo reads and synthesized oligo sequences. For each position, we determine the 12 conditional error probabilities P(synthesized base is b1|sequenced base is b2) for

b1 ̸= b2 and b1, b2 ∈ { A, C, G, T }. If b1 = b2, we set the probability to the corresponding quality-score from the FASTQ file. From these conditional error probabilities and quality-scores, we can calculate probabilities of four bases on a certain position and then obtain LLRs for all bit positions, considering the mapping between bases and bits (i.e.,{A, C, G, T } = {00, 01, 10, 11}, which aligns with the previous works [25], [26], [27]). For example, assume that the base call (sequenced base) is A and the quality-score corresponds to 80%. Then, we determine the probability of the base A (P(A)) as 80% and the other three probabilities as 20% × P(synthesized base is b1|sequenced base is A) for b1 ∈ {C, G, T }. For the first bit, we calculate the LLR as log (P(A) + P(C))/(P(G) + P(T )) .
The proposed decoding algorithm consists of three main stages as shown in Alg. 1. First, error detection is performed using CRC parity bits, where two sub-procedures are conducted for checking whether there are errors in index positions. We compare base calls from the FASTQ files for index positions with the predetermined values of index positions. Then, error detection is carried out using CRC codes. Second, LLRs of stitched oligo reads with correct index positions are merged if they have the same index values. Last, the parity-check matrix (PCM) is constructed based on index information and belief propagation (BP) decoding is performed for 256 LDPC codes. Note that these processes should be performed in consideration of the interleaving structures for encoding in Section II-A.
As demonstrated in [26], soft decoding using FASTQ files and sequencing statistics can effectively reduce the required number of sequencing reads. However, the decoding algorithm [26] requires BP decoding of LT codes multiple times depending on RS decoding results. III. MAIN RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
In this section, we compare the sequencing statistics of three sequencing results and decoding performance of four coding schemes under the same experimental conditions. From the synthesis/sequencing experiment, the effectiveness of our coding scheme is proved.
A. Sequencing Conditions and Statistics
To ensure a fair performance comparison under identical experimental conditions, we conduct the same PCR and Illumina sequencing procedures as previously described in [25], [26], and [27]. Briefly explaining, RP1/RPI1 primers, Q5 Hot Start High-Fidelity 2X Master Mix, and AMPure XP kit are used for PCR amplification and Illumina Miseq Reagent v3 kit is utilized for sequencing. Our main sequencing outputs are summarized as follows.
1) The ratio of Q30 is 97.37%. 2) PhiX spike-in is 30%. 3) The number of total reads including forward and reverse
directions is 17.9 millions. We utilize the stitching algorithm as the PEAR algorithm [42] like in [25], [26], and [27]. Further, the stitched oligo reads with the correct length 152 are only considered for statistics and decoding procedures. Similar to the previous works, we calculate sequencing statistics based on edit distances for the entire stitched oligo reads after the PEAR algorithm. For sequencing results, we first calculate PEAR passing rates, average numbers of position, insertion/deletion, substitution errors per base. Table I compares them for three sequencing results. Although the newly conducted experiment exhibits a larger number of insertion/deletion errors, it shows the less substitution errors, resulting in the less position errors, where position errors are more important in our decoding procedures. In addition, the experiment result shows a higher PEAR passing rate, indicating that more stitched oligo reads are available for decoding procedures under the same random sampling number. From sequencing results and synthesized oligo pools, we calculate position error probabilities based on edit distances. Specifically, Fig. 2 shows the average position error probabilities for 152 positions from two sequencing results (data A and this work). It is noted that the non-uniformity of oligo positions exists and the last position (152) shows a much higher position error probability than the others. This result highlights the importance of designing a coding scheme that takes into account the non-uniform position error characteristics. Although the experiments in [26] and this work share the same experimental conditions, the position error rates are different as shown in Fig. 2 due to the biochemical variation. Considering the biochemical variation, our approaches of payload grouping and uniform interleaving for LDPC codes are reasonable. We also find out that the most reliable 24 base positions do not coincide for two sequencing

results. To be specific, some base positions such as 2, 3, 4 in the sequencing result of this work show much higher position error probabilities even though they belong to the most reliable 24 base positions in data A. Therefore, for future studies, it might be more advantageous to randomly select the positions of indices and CRC parities among middle positions, which do not show the highest position error rate.
B. Required Sampling Number for Perfect Recovery
For oligo reads, we randomly select N pairs of forward and reverse reads and merge them using the PEAR algorithm. Among them, K stitched oligo reads with 152 bases are used for decoding. We compare four coding schemes ([25], [26], [27], and this work) and three sequencing results (data A, data B in [26], and this work). The number of decoding success for 50 random sampling trials is summarized in Table II. It shows that the proposed coding scheme succeeds all 50 trials with the random sampling number N = 59000 while other coding schemes succeed with N = 88000, 80000, and 86000 for data A and N = 96000, 86000, and 88000 for data B. In other words, our coding scheme reduces the required number of oligo reads for perfect recovery about 26.25% ∼ 38.5% compared to the state-of-the-art decoding algorithms. The three decoding algorithms in [25], [26], and [27] can be applied for data A and data B because those decoding algorithms are designed for the same encoding structure. Since the proposed encoding structure is different, our decoding algorithm cannot be performed with data A and data B while the other three decoding algorithms are not also applicable for the sequencing result of this work.
In order to demonstrate the effectiveness of discarding oligo reads having incorrect index positions for our decoding algorithm, we additionally conduct the random sampling experiment using Alg. 1 while excluding the error detection by intra-oligo CRC codes. As shown in Table II, the decoding performance is degraded, where the required number of oligo reads for perfect recovery is increased to 62000. As explained in Introduction, this performance degradation comes from the fact that incorrect information for variable nodes is critical for the decoding of LDPC codes. This result highlights the importance of discarding oligo reads using intra-oligo CRC codes which have the superior error detecting capabilities.
To maintain the consistent experimental conditions, we necessarily employ 32 CRC parity bits, which are excessive for error detection of index positions. It is possible to further enhance the decoding performance by replacing some of the CRC parity bits with error correction bits. Nevertheless, the result still demonstrates the effectiveness of the well-designed LDPC code with interleaving, quality scores, and sequencing statistics in improving decoding performance, without relying on intra-oligo error correcting codes. Interestingly, all decoding algorithms perform better on data A than data B although position error probabilities are lower in data B. Since error probabilities in Illumina sequencing are relatively low, it seems that the PEAR passing rate is more critical than other factors. Thus, it is necessary to investigate coding schemes that can enhance the PEAR passing rate for future work.

From [43], check node operations and their complexity for a check node with a degree dc in one BP decoding iteration are summarized as follows.
1) 2dc multiplications. 2) dc tanh operations. 3) dc tanh−1 operations. The maximum number of BP decoding iterations is 400 for both algorithms and it is noted that [26] performs BP decoding of 256 LT codes up to four times while the proposed algorithm performs BP decoding of 256 LDPC codes once. For the intra-oligo direction, [26] utilizes RS decoding and the proposed algorithm employs CRC decoding. Using Berlekamp Welch decoding, the time complexity of RS decoding is O(n3) while the time complexity of CRC decoding is O(n) for the code length n. From Table III, it is noted that the

proposed algorithm has the much lower decoding complexity compared to that of [26]. Thus, we demonstrate the superiority of the proposed coding scheme in both performance improvement and reduced complexity from Tables II and III.
For the complexity comparison, we do not include algorithms in [25] and [27] because they incorporate distinct operations based on hard decoding and numerous signal processing algorithms. Since the decoding complexity of hard decoding is much lower than that of soft decoding, it is expected that algorithms in [25] and [27] have the lower decoding complexity. However, soft decoding of error correcting codes has been widely adopted due to its superior decoding performance in communication systems and memory devices despite of the increased decoding complexity. Similarly, Table II demonstrates the superiority of soft decoding, suggesting a need for a transition from hard decoding to soft decoding even in the field of DNA storage.
IV. APPROXIMATE DNA STORAGE CHANNELS
Since the performance improvement is the mixture of the positive effects of the proposed LDPC codes and interleaving techniques, we cannot distinguish each independent effect on the decoding performance from Table II. Furthermore, one may wonder whether the improved performance of the proposed coding scheme comes from not only incorporation of LDPC codes and interleaving techniques but also the better sequencing statistics. In order to demonstrate the independent effects of two methodologies while compensating for the experimental variation, we derive non-uniform BSCs for DNA storage using sequencing statistics. By analyzing the FER results in the derived non-uniform BSCs, we prove the superiority of the optimized LDPC code and effectiveness of the interleaving technique by Monte Carlo simulations.

A. Modeling Non-Uniform BSCs for DNA Storage
Since two bits correspond to one base (i.e., {A, C, G, T } = {00, 01, 10, 11}), we have to derive the approximate channels in the perspective of bit channels. In this situation, we can take advantage of the fact that there is a similar trend between two different channel thresholds [36]. That is, we can approximate a certain channel as the well-known channel using the equal mutual information condition. From the base-tobit mapping and sequencing statistics from the entire oligo reads, we derive each bit channel as the BSC having the same mutual information. For each bit position, assume that Xi → Yi denotes the asymmetric bit transition channel that corresponds to sequencing statistics for the ith bit position. Then, let P(Xi = 0) = ai , P(Xi = 1) = bi , P((Yi = 1)|(Xi = 0)) = pi , P((Yi = 0)|(Xi = 1)) = qi , where these probabilities are calculated by finding the closest synthesized oligo for each stitched oligo read based on the edit distance. With mutual information I (Xi ; Yi ) = H (Yi ) − H (Yi |Xi ), we have the following equations:
P(Yi = 0) = ai (1 − pi ) + bi qi ,
P(Yi = 1) = ai pi + bi (1 − qi ),
H (Yi |Xi ) = −ai pi log2( pi ) + (1 − pi )log2(1 − pi ) − bi qi log2(qi ) + (1 − qi )log2(1 − qi ) ,
H (Yi ) = − P(Yi = 0)log2(P(Yi = 0)) + P(Yi = 1)log2(P(Yi = 1)) .
From above equations, we can obtain channel mutual information I (Xi ; Yi ). Since mutual information of the BSC with equal source probabilities is 1+ pi log2 pi +(1− pi )log2(1− pi ) for a crossover probability pi , we can derive non-uniform BSCs by calculating all pi ’s satisfying I (Xi ; Yi ) = 1 + pi log2 pi + (1 − pi )log2(1 − pi ). The derived non-uniform BSCs with pi ’s from the above procedures are shown in Fig. 3. These probabilities are generally proportional to the corresponding base position error rates in Fig. 2 and they are also affected by the mapping between bases and bits.
For example, p52 and p302 of data A in Fig. 3 are greater than the crossover probabilities of the previous bit positions because the corresponding base position error rates (26th and

151th base positions in Fig. 2) have the higher values. For our sequencing result, it is noted that the crossover probabilities in Fig. 3 fluctuate although the base position error rates in Fig. 2 are relatively stable. This phenomenon is related with the channel statistics and mapping between bases and bits. While the crossover probabilities of two adjacent bit positions corresponding to the same base position are quite different, the both odd and even bit positions show the similar crossover probabilities, respectively.
In Fig. 3, we observe that the bit channels are significantly non-uniform. To address this issue, we incorporate interleaving and will verify its effectiveness in improving FERs under the non-uniform BSCs in the next subsection.
B. Performance Evaluation in the Derived Channels
To demonstrate the effectiveness of our two methodologies, namely optimized LDPC codes and interleaving techniques, we consider three error correcting codes for comparison: 1) the LDPC code in [44], 2) the optimized LDPC code in this work from (1), and 3) the LT code in [26] and [27]. In particular, the LDPC code in [44] is known as the stateof-the-art LDPC code with the base size 4 × 40. Two LDPC codes have the same code length (17920) and rate (0.9) while the LT code has 18000 and 0.892. We first show FERs of these error correcting codes under the uniform AWGN channel to compare general soft decoding performance in Fig. 4. It is noted that the proposed LDPC code shows the superior decoding performance compared to the other codes. This result can be expected from their BP decoding thresholds because FER performances of LDPC codes are theoretically aligned with BP decoding thresholds. In specific, the BP decoding thresholds are 3.4 dB for the LDPC code in [44] and 3.34 dB for the proposed LDPC code. Since LT codes [26], [27] are designed for the hard decoding (erasure decoding), the FER performance is worse under the soft decoding (BP decoding).
In order to demonstrate the superiority of our coding scheme without the experimental variation, we conduct Monte Carlo simulations for two sequencing results (data A and this work) in Fig. 5, where each set of derived non-uniform BSCs is obtained from each sequencing statistics. Similar to the conventional channel model of error correcting codes, we conduct FER simulations while assuming that each bit of the codeword is transmitted once under one derived channel.

used for LDPC codes, the non-interleaved scenario means that only one type of the bit channel is associated with an LDPC codeword. We consider two extreme non-interleaved scenarios: 1) the best scenario, where only the bit channel with the best crossover probability is allocated for all bit channels and 2) the worst scenario, where only the bit channel with the worst crossover probability is allocated for all bit channels. The effectiveness of interleaving can be observed in Figs. 5(c) and 5(d). For both sequencing results, the worst channel case shows the inferior performance whereas the interleaving case shows similar results with the best channel case. As the overall decoding success is defined as the decoding success of all inter-oligo codewords, it is evident that the worst case performance plays a critical role in determining the overall decoding success. However, by incorporating interleaving, we can make the error distribution uniform for each inter-oligo LDPC code, which improves the overall decoding performance. Note that the performance of each inter-oligo LDPC code is the same as the performance of the interleaving case in Figs. 5(c) and 5(d). By introducing interleaving techniques to the proposed coding scheme, we make the performance of each inter-oligo LDPC code close to the performance of the best channel case.
In summary, we can conclude that the proposed methodologies are effective not only in the experiment (as shown in Table II) but also in the approximate channel model (as shown in Fig. 5), which is not varied by the experimental variation.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we proposed a new coding scheme for DNA storage using optimized LDPC codes by differential evolution and interleaving techniques to handle non-uniform position error characteristics of DNA sequences, where inter-oligo LDPC codes are utilized without intra-oligo error correcting codes. Under the same experimental conditions, we compared the proposed coding scheme with other coding schemes in the literature. The synthesis/sequencing experiment showed the superiority of our coding scheme for DNA storage, where the required number of oligo reads for perfect recovery is reduced about 26.26% ∼ 38.5% compared to existing stateof-the-art DNA storage coding schemes. Further, we derived non-uniform BSCs for DNA storage to analyze the independent effects of optimized LDPC codes and interleaving techniques in the mathematical model that is not affected by the biochemical variation. From the derived non-uniform BSCs, we figured out the above two methodologies are effective regardless of the sequencing statistics.
APPENDIX LDPC PROTOGRAPH CONSTRUCTION
As mentioned in Section II-A, we optimize the LDPC protograph with the size 4×40 under uniform AWGN channels through the differential evolution algorithm [40]. Since the size of the protograph is relatively large for the heuristic algorithm, we divide the 4 × 40 size protograph into eight 4 × 5 protographs and sequentially optimize the overall protograph as in [44]. Since the code-rate is very high, we heuristically determine some optimization parameters such as the difference between the maximum and minimum check node degrees. For each optimization stage, our optimization constraints are described as follows.
1) The number of generation G = 10000. 2) The number of candidates S = 500. 3) The max entry e = 4. 4) The sum of entries for each variable node is less than
or equal to 8. 5) The number of zero entry for each variable node is less
than 3. 6) Any cycles only consisting of degree-2 variable nodes
should be avoided.
The condition 3) is due to reduction of the search space and the condition 4) is due to avoiding short cycles. Further, we find that the condition 5) contributes to reducing FERs in the high code-rate region. The condition 6) is necessary for linear minimum distance growth [45]. In addition, we add following conditions for the 4×40 resulting protograph, which also contribute to reducing FERs heuristically in overall.
1) The average variable node degree is less than or equal to 4.7.
2) The difference between the maximum and minimum check node degrees is less than or equal to 7.
