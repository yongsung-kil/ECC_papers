Design of Concatenated Product Codes with Spatially-Coupled LDPC Codes
Yuqing Ren1, Yifei Shen1, Emmanuel Boutillon2, Alexios Balatsoukas-Stimming3, Andreas Burg1∗
1 Telecommunication Circuits Laboratory, EPFL, Switzerland, 2 Lab-STICC, Universite´ Bretagne Sud, France 3 Department of Electrical Engineering, Eindhoven University of Technology, Netherlands email: {yuqing.ren, andreas.burg}@epfl.ch

Abstract—In this paper, we introduce a novel coding scheme that concatenates product codes with spatially-coupled lowdensity parity-check (SC-LDPC) codes, termed PC-SC-LDPC codes, for next-generation wireless (and wireline) communications. These codes exhibit significant performance improvement in both waterfall and error-floor regions, compared to conventional schemes. We further correct residual errors through tailored interleaving strategies. Numerical results demonstrate that our approach can achieve a 1.2 dB gain at a bit error rate (BER) target of 10−8. Moreover, we offer a semi-analytical approach to estimate that the error floor of our designed PCSC-LDPC codes falls below 10−15.
Index Terms—Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, spatially-coupled LDPC (SC-LDPC) codes, product codes, concatenation codes, 6G wireless, optical fiber.
I. INTRODUCTION
While the roll-out of 5G networks is speeding along at a spectacular pace, 6G research has been vigorously launched across the world [1]. Among the most resource-consuming components in baseband processing, forward error correction (FEC) decoders are at risk to become a major bottleneck in 6G, since achieving a peak data rate of 100 Gbps 1 Tbps with an energy efficiency of 1 pJ/bit is difficult even in advanced technology nodes [2]. Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [3] have raised much attention over the past two decades and have already been applied to various communication and storage systems. Most prominently, owing to the extraordinary error-correcting performance and the inherently parallel processing under belief propagation (BP) decoding [4], LDPC codes were ratified as the data coding scheme in the 5G New Radio (NR) standard [5]–[7]. For practical LDPC codes, the property of BP thresholds close to the capacity becomes fully realized with long LDPC codes [8]. However, the complexity of BP decoding and corresponding hardware increases with increasing code length [9]. As indicated by the state-of-the-art 5G-NR LDPC decoder [9], the substantial memory overhead required to accommodate the maximum block length (Nmax = 26112) occupies a significant part of the decoder core area, greatly limiting the area efficiency of the decoder. The large area also makes it difficult to achieve high frequencies due to complex routing, capping peak throughput at approximately 20 Gbps.

Recently, spatially-coupled LDPC (SC-LDPC) codes (also called LDPC convolutional codes [10]) were proposed in [11] to superimpose LDPC block codes with a convolutional structure. This convolutional structure renders SC-LDPC codes mathematically proven to achieve the MAP threshold of their underlying LDPC block codes using BP decoding [11], which has attracted widespread interest for next-generation wireless and optical fiber communications [12]. In addition to achieving capacity, SC-LDPC codes offer several apparent advantages such as simple regular structures from underlying block codes, manageable encoding and decoding complexity, and appealing decoding performance. Moreover, unlike LDPC block codes as mentioned above, SC-LDPC codes can utilize the compact and regular structure of their underlying block codes to employ a low-complexity windowed decoder for decoding very long codewords with a sliding window approach [13]. Notably, advanced SC-LDPC decoders have reached peak throughput on the order of hundreds of Gbps [14], [15], showcasing the potential of windowed decoders to meet the stringent requirements of 6G. However, SC-LDPC codes suffer from performance degradation at high signal-to-noise ratios (SNRs) [16]. These failures are due to trapping sets (TSs) [17] in SC-LDPC codes, resulting in the error floor phenomenon. Despite numerous classical works in [18], [19] that proposed to optimize the SC-LDPC code construction by designing larger girth or eliminating small TSs, fully overcoming this error floor and improving performance remains a challenge.
It is noteworthy that industry standards for optical fiber communications (e.g., ITU-T G.709, 400G-ZR, and 800GZR) also set forth stringent demands akin to those of 6G wireless. For instance, 800G-ZR optical transport networks stipulate a target throughput of 800 Gbps and a bit error rate (BER) down to 10−15 to ensure reliable data transmission over long distances [20], [21]. Therefore, it is a valuable direction to investigate how the insights and experience gained from FEC in optical communications can be exploited for 6G. A pivotal factor in the success of FEC in current optical communications is the application of product codes, also called block turbo codes (BTCs) [22], which are constructed from short component codes and feature two-dimensional (2D) parity constraints for each information bit. II. PROPOSED CONCATENATED CODING SCHEME OF PC-SC-LDPC CODES
In this section, we introduce a novel concatenated coding scheme called PC-SC-LDPC codes designed for wireless communication systems. This approach splits FEC into an inner SC-LDPC code and outer product code. For the inner code, we execute global SDD BP decoding for the entire codeword to significantly diminish the error threshold in the low SNR region but still leave a handful of errors. Subsequently, for the outer code, a local iterative HDD decoding algorithm effectively corrects these residual errors to achieve a low error floor.

C. Concatenation of PC-SC-LDPC Codes
In our PC-SC-LDPC codes, we consider a SC-LDPC code with good sparse graph structure as inner code and put a product code constructed by BCH with explicit minimum distance as outer code. The authors of [25] demonstrate that concatenated codes need a trade-off between waterfall and error-floor regions for improved FEC performance. First, in the waterfall region, attributed to the sparsity of the Tanner graph and the gain from the convolutional structure, compared to standalone block LDPC codes, SC-LDPC (with similar-length underlying blocks) codes with windowed BP decoding are better suited to reduce the BER threshold below 10−3 after only a few iterations. In the waterfall region, the role of minimum distance is limited [26], since there exist lots of unconverged errors caused by insufficiently propagated messages. Yet, in the error-floor region, a large minimum distance of product codes would significantly lower the error floor. Hence, iterative HDD (such as iBDD shown in (4)) is even more reliable than SDD or soft-aided hybrid approaches [27], due to hard-

decision strictly following the parity-check constraints and reducing the risk of mapping to another valid codeword.

III. DECODING OF PC-SC-LDPC CODES
Algorithm 1 summarizes a two-phase hybrid decoding process for our PC-SC-LDPC codes. Initially, it employs windowed BP decoding with a window size of W , which loads received signals of W continuous sub-blocks. Following every IBP iterations, a newly estimated code block cˆt is produced, which continues until all n blocks are decoded (coupling length L = n). Next, a de-interleaver on CΨ generates CP, necessitating storage for an n×n binary matrix. Finally, iBDD decoding with an iteration count of IBDD is applied to correct residual errors, which executes row and column decoding in an alternating fashion.
It is noteworthy that there is no feedback loop between the two decoding phases for either encoding or decoding, as shown in Fig. 3. Namely, windowed BP decoding does not need to wait for feedback from the subsequent iBDD decoding, which allows for straightforward pipelining. This is critical to achieve very high throughput. Moreover, since the information length of underlying block codes in SC-LDPC is consistent with component BCH codes (i.e., k = n, usually on the order of several hundred bits), a BP decoder of this scale can relatively easily achieve a throughput of over 100 Gbps with an unrolled full-parallel architecture [29]. On the other hand, due to its simple hard decision logic, numerous iBDD decoders have already been reported in the literature to approach a nearly 1 Tbps throughput [21]. Note that in Algorithm 1, we omit early termination for both windowed BP and iBDD decoding due to our focus on the worst-case throughput.

Numerical results of Section IV demonstrate that our PCSC-LDPC codes demand only a few iterations to significantly outperform conventional approaches.

In this section, we evaluate the BER performance of our proposed PC-SC-LDPC codes. For this evaluation, we use an inner SC-LDPC code based on underlying blocks of an LDPC-(588, 518) code with w = 1; the outer codes (product) constructed by component BCH-(511, 493, 2) codes. To make k = n = 511, we use rate-matching to shorten 7 information bits. Therefore, this designed PC-SC-LDPC code CA, with an inner code rate of 88% and an outer code rate of 93%, has an overall total code rate of 82%.

B. Discussion 1: Interleaver Design
As explained in Section II-C, due to the same prototype matrix representation, the decoded cˆt for each sub-block has similar error patterns. If without any interleaving, i.e., the dimension of SC-LDPC codes overlaps completely with the row dimension of product codes, these error patterns will be stacked as a large error square (or a rectangle), as shown in Fig. 6. For product codes composed of BCH-(n, k, t) codes, once a (t + 1) × (t + 1) error array (i.e., stopping set) occurs, iBDD will inevitably fail. However, we can introduce an interleaver to reshape the error patterns and enhance the minimum distance of the entire concatenated code [25]. In Fig. 6, we propose a simple diagonal interleaver that allows each coded block ct to pass through as many BCH codewords as possible, effectively disrupting the 3 × 3 stopping set on the left. Note that all the PC-SC-LDPC performances in this paper are for this diagonal interleaver.

V. CONCLUSION
We introduce PC-SC-LDPC codes, a novel concatenated coding scheme to achieve significant performance in both waterfall and error-floor regions. The interleaver is also essential in our work to spread out potential decoding errors of the inner codes as much as possible. The error floors of our PCSC-LDPC codes can be effectively estimated by the stopping sets, which are below BER = 10−15.
