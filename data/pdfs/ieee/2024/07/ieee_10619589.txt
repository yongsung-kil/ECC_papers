Abstract—In this work, we propose a fully differentiable iterative decoder for quantum low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. The proposed algorithm is composed of classical belief propagation (BP) decoding stages and intermediate graph neural network (GNN) layers. Both components of the decoder are defined over the same sparse decoding graph enabling a seamless integration and scalability to large codes. The core idea is to use the GNN component between consecutive BP runs so that the knowledge from the previous BP run can be leveraged to better initialize the next BP run. This enables the proposed decoder to learn to compensate for sub-optimal BP decoding graphs that result from the design constraints of quantum LDPC codes. Since the entire decoder remains differentiable, gradient descent-based training is possible. We compare the error rate performance of the proposed decoder against various post-processing methods such as random perturbation, enhanced feedback, augmentation, and ordered-statistics decoding (OSD) and show that a carefully designed training process lowers the error-floor significantly. As a result, our proposed decoder outperforms the former three methods using significantly fewer post-processing attempts. The source code of our experiments is available online.
I. INTRODUCTION
Quantum low-density parity-check (QLDPC) codes are among the most promising types of error correction codes for fault-tolerant quantum computing. In recent years there have been numerous efforts to design QLDPC codes of high rate and high distance [1], [2]; even linear distance has been investigated in [3], [4]. Recent work from Bravyi et al. [5] shows how certain codes can be mapped to a bilayer hardware architecture, spurring further interest in the community and increasing the practical importance of low-complexity decoders.
In this work, we focus on decoding medium block-length and high-rate CSS QLDPC codes introduced in [6]. These codes are of particular interest due to their strong errorrate performance under maximum likelihood decoding and its approximations, such as ordered-statistics decoding (OSD). However, their performance is known to be suboptimal under plain belief propagation (BP) decoding, due to short loops and symmetric stabilizer trapping sets [7]–[9] present in the Tanner graph. The authors in [7] proposed to alleviate this issue by adding a small perturbation to the channel prior probabilities given as input to the BP decoder. An improved version is shown in [10], where the perturbation depends on the unsatisfied checks.
The goal of our work is to learn those perturbations using a GNN. The GNN follows the concept of [11] and acts as an intermediate layer between independent BP runs, as shown in

We call this intermediate layer a feedback GNN. The GNN operates on the same decoding graph as the BP decoder, facilitating a seamless integration with the BP decoding stages. Typically, machine learning for channel decoding is doomed by the curse of dimensionality, meaning that the training complexity scales exponentially with the codeword length [12]. However, we would like to emphasize that one advantage of the proposed feedback GNN structure is that the graph is explicitly provided and the main decoding task still relies on BP. II. QUANTUM LOW-DENSITY PARITY-CHECK CODES
In the following we restrict our attention to the i.i.d. depolarizing channel, in which each qubit independently and identically experiences a random X, Z, or Y = iXZ type of error, each with probability p/3 for some p ∈ [0, 1].
A. CSS Quantum Codes
Quantum Calderbank-Shor-Steane (CSS) [14], [15] codes are constructed from two classical codes CX = ker HX and

Rows of HX and HZ form the Xand Z-type stabilizer generators, while the X-type and Z-type logical operators are CZ \CX⊥ and CX \CZ⊥, respectively. All parity-check operations are binary operations in GF(2).
If CX is a blocklength N code, encoding KX information bits (denoted as [N, KX ] code), and CZ is an [N, KZ ] code, then the resulting quantum code is an N, KX + KZ − N code. The quantum minimum distance of this code is d = min{dX , dZ }, where dX = min{wt(x), x ∈ ker HZ \im HXT }, dZ = min{wt(x), x ∈ ker HX \im HZT }. wt(x) is the Hamming weight of the binary vector x.
An error is specified by N -bit vectors eX and eZ denoting the X and Z components of the error, respectively. A decoder takes the observed syndromes sX = HZ eX and sZ = HX eZ and constructs correction operations eˆX and eˆZ . Importantly, these do not need to precisely match the error for decoding to succeed. Instead, eˆX and eX may differ by an X-stabilizer, and eˆZ and eZ may differ by a Z-stabilizer, as these correction operations will return the system to the codespace without incurring any logical error. The requirement for successful correction can be compactly written as HX⊥(eˆX +eX ) = 0 and HZ⊥(eˆZ + eZ ) = 0, where the rows of HX⊥ and HZ⊥ generate CX and CZ respectively. This is more stringent than only requiring eˆ to have the correct syndrome, i.e., HZ eˆX = HZ eX and HX eˆZ = HX eZ , since the row span of HX is contained in the row span of HZ⊥ for a CSS code.
In the depolarizing model, the X and Z components of the CSS codes can be separately decoded using classical (binary) syndrome BP decoders. To address the correlation between X and Z errors, a quaternary BP decoder [16] was introduced to directly operate on the Tanner graph involving both the X and Z checks. However, due to the CSS code constraints, this Tanner graph inevitably contains cycles of length 4 [16]. This makes the decoding of QLDPC codes a challenging, yet from a research perspective attractive, task.
B. Existing Post-Processing Methods
One difficulty of decoding QLDPC codes using message passing methods is that the correction operator eˆ = (eˆX |eˆZ ) of the decoder is not assured to result in a zero syndrome s = (sX |sZ ) = 0. We call such cases “flagged” events as the decoder is aware of the existence of these types of errors. To overcome such flagged events, several post-processing methods have been proposed. One approach is to take the output of the BP decoder and use it to adjust the inputs to another round of BP decoding, in the hope that that round will lead to an unflagged error. The following is a summary of such methods, which can be applied repeatedly until the parity-checks are satisfied or the maximal number of attempts Na is reached:
• Random perturbation [7] randomly chooses unsatisfied (“frustrated”) checks and randomly perturbs the input LLRs for all the qubits involved in those checks.

• Enhanced feedback [10] randomly chooses one frustrated parity-check and one of its associated qubits. The channel prior of this qubit is adjusted such that an anticommuting/commuting error is more likely if the ground truth syndrome of the parity-check is 1 and 0, respectively.
• Matrix augmentation [17] randomly chooses and repeats some of the rows from the parity-check matrix, and uses this augmented matrix to initialize the message passing decoding in the next round.

B. Boxplus Loss
In order to train this GNN, we feed its output Λ′ to initialize another BP4 decoding stage, and let it run for 16 iterations. We record (ΓiX , ΓYi , ΓiZ ) as in Eq (6) at the end of iteration 8 to 16 and calculate λXi , λZi for all these iterations as in Eq (7), and for all X/Z-type checks calculate ⊞i′∈N (j)λiZ′/X , respectively.

These are the logits used to calculate a binary cross entropy loss for a given ground truth syndrome sj. This loss is minimized when HX eˆZ = HX eZ and HZ eˆX = HZ eX .
Note that the proposed boxplus loss is different from the sine loss introduced in [21]. It is also possible to extend this boxplus loss to take degeneracy into account. For CSS codes, no logical error happens if and only if HX⊥eˆX = HX⊥eX and HZ⊥eˆZ = HZ⊥eZ . And the CSS requirement implies HZ ⊂ HX⊥ and HX ⊂ HZ⊥. Therefore, we can simply enlarge the X and Z parity-check matrices into HZ⊥ and HX⊥ when doing the multi-loss calculation. When training the feedback GNN, we do not take degeneracy into account.
C. Training methods
• Step 1: Generate an easy dataset by storing the error patterns (eX , eZ ) that a 64-iteration flooding BP4 fails to decode. To target the low physical error region where the perturbationbased post-processing methods show an error floor, cf. Fig. 3(a), we only consider eX ∨ eZ (bit-wise OR) having Hamming weight up to 0.05N . We gather roughly a million such errors in a few hours.
• Step 2: Train a coarse GNN on the easy dataset by sandwiching it between two BP runs of 16 iterations. The output of iterations 8 to 16 of the latter run is used to calculate a boxplus multi-loss, as described in the last subsection. Training takes roughly ten minutes on a single NVIDIA RTX4090.
• Step 3: The coarse GNN, when embedded between BP layers, shows a severe error floor during inference, see Fig. 4. Therefore, we gather a hard dataset by storing error patterns that two 64-iteration BP runs sandwiching the coarse GNN fail to decode. We gathered roughly twenty thousand such samples in a few days.
• Step 4: We repeat the hard dataset by fifty times and mix it with the easy one to balance occurrence. We then train a refined GNN model on this mixed dataset by sandwiching it between two BP runs of 64 and 16 iterations each. Training one model takes roughly half an hour and we train ten such models and select the best one.
Both the coarse and refined GNN are trained by being embedded between two BP blocks, but during evaluation, it is reused for possibly more than one attempt. Let (64,16,16,16) denote the decoder consisting of 64 BP iterations followed by 3 × 16 BP iterations each with an intermediate GNN layer sharing the same weight. In Figure 4, one can see three attempts (64,16,16,16) are better than one attempt (64,64), though the latter involves more total BP runs but less GNN layers. Hence three attempts are deployed in the final decoder. The coarse GNN obtained in step 2 shows a severe error floor, while the refined GNN trained on the mixed dataset improves the error-floor behavior at low p.

The authors of [23] also employ a data-driven approach to learn a sequence of BP priors. However, the learned priors

of [23] are input-independent, while in our work the priors are computed from the samples through a learnable function, which conveys more prior information.
VI. CONCLUSION
We proposed an iterative decoding scheme for quantum LDPC codes based BP4 and GNN decoding stages. We train the GNN layers such that they learn to refine the channel input LLRs for subsequent BP decoding stages. We demonstrated that our feedback GNN significantly lowers the error floor on two medium blocksize QLDPC codes when carefully trained. The feedback GNN benefits from a computational latency of O(1) when nodes are updated in parallel (and assuming a fixed number of attempts). However, in practice the complexity overhead of the feedback GNN is similar to a 16-iteration BP due to the computationally complex matrix multiplications in the MLPs. Furthermore, we proposed a new loss based on the boxplus operation that calculates a more accurate reliability estimate of the check nodes.
When applying our approach to other QLDPC codes, a new dataset needs to be gathered every time. While creating an easy dataset and training a coarse GNN model on it only takes a few hours, our solution to the error-floor mitigation involves creating a hard dataset that takes a few days. Data augmentation approaches [23] can be adopted to speed up this process for quasi-cyclic codes. On the training side, the refined GNN trained on the mixed dataset may not be optimal when reused for later feedback attempts. We therefore believe training a different GNN for each feedback attempt, on the samples that the previous attempt fails to decode might alleviate the error floor. Finally, if simply perturbing the prior LLR is not enough, guided decimation-based approaches [24], [25] might be incorporated to further break the symmetry in the decoding problem.
