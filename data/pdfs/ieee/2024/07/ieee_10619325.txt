Abstract—In this paper we study a special family of LowDensity Parity-Check (LDPC) codes, called Primitive RateCompatible (PRC) LDPC codes, obtained by applying puncturing to some properly chosen simplex codes. We investigate their minimum distance properties and give insights on the design of PRC-LDPC codes for use in scenarios where constraints on hardware and software resources require maintaining a low level of complexity. We numerically assess the error rate performance of these codes in such a setting, showing that they also demonstrate good performance under belief propagation decoding, in addition to low decoding complexity.
Index Terms—LDPC codes, Minimum Distance, Simplex codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
In modern communications, balancing flexibility and performance is a crucial challenge. Rate-Compatible (RC) codes play a key role in this interplay, as they allow adjusting the code rate to adapt to channel conditions without changing code. Among them, RC Low-Density Parity-Check (RC-LDPC) codes [1] stand out as a promising option. Classical LDPC codes are renowned for their remarkable error-correction capability [2]. Yet, their rigidity with regard to code rate can become a limitation in applications that require fast adaptation to channel conditions. The emergence of RC-LDPC codes addresses such a limitation by offering a framework that reconciles the excellent error-correction properties of LDPC codes with the imperative need for rate flexibility. Primitive RC-LDPC (PRC-LDPC), obtained by puncturing simplex codes, were introduced in [3]–[5]. Each family of such codes is uniquely represented by a primitive polynomial. The design of PRCLDPC codes starts from simplex codes, thus from the choice of a proper primitive parity-check polynomial h(x). In order to obtain an LDPC code, h(x) must be relatively sparse, meaning that it has many more null coefficients than non-null coefficients, and the support vector of its coefficient vector must be a Golomb ruler. The main advantages of these rate adaptive codes over comparable solutions stand in their low encoding/decoding complexity, in the existence of convenient techniques to compute their minimum distance, and in the extremely low representation cost.

These benefits are also important in scenarios with stringent complexity constraints, for applications where resourcelimited devices must be employed, such as those commonly deployed in Internet of Things (IoT) applications. A commonly used solution to achieve fine rate granularity in these applications is that of puncturing a given starting code. When instead the code rate has to be kept unchanged, while varying the block length, for example for the purpose of meeting specific latency constraints, additional shortening operations are usually performed. Generally speaking, both puncturing and shortening operations change the parity-check matrix structure. The impact of these changes depends on the individual code family, but the performance that is generally obtained is hardly comparable with that achievable through the use of different codes individually designed for each target rate.
We tackle the challenge of rate-compatible code design in complexity-constrained scenarios by considering proper PRCLDPC codes. We show that, if a fixed code rate and an upper bound on the decoding complexity are given as design parameters, an ad-hoc LDPC code with proper characteristics (e.g., girth larger than 4, relatively low average column weight, good minimum distance, etc.) can be constructed by combining puncturing and shortening operations, with a limited impact on the structural properties of the code. Finally, the assessment of the error performance of codes with several different rates shows that the limitations on the decoding complexity do not prevent achieving good error correction performance.
The present paper is related to [6], where primitive rateless codes are analyzed. A fundamental difference with our approach lies in the fact that primitive rateless codes are not necessarily LDPC codes. Therefore, the analytical framework outlined in [6] does not consider the constraints imposed by the requirement of a sparse parity-check matrix. This paper also relates to our previous studies [3], [4], which provide an initial exploration of the theoretical basis of this work. In the present paper, we extend and enhance both the theoretical foundations and the numerical analysis. Finally, other works like [7]–[9] deal with the design of binary LDPC codes based on Golomb rulers. However, in all those papers the considered codes are quasi-cyclic, whereas the codes we consider are not. Quasi-cyclicity enforces the use of modular Golomb rulers, which require a different analysis with respect to the conventional Golomb rulers we consider. II. PRELIMINARIES
The rounding operation to the closest integer is denoted as [·]. A Golomb ruler is a sequence of non-negative integers characterized by the unique property that the difference between any two integers within the sequence is distinct. The Hamming weight of a vector is the number of nonzero elements it contains, and we simply refer to it as weight. Similarly, the weight of a polynomial is determined by the number of its non-zero coefficients. For any polynomial h(x) = h0 + h1x + . . . + hkxk, we establish an associated coefficient vector as h = (h0, . . . , hk). The Hamming weight of coefficient vector h is denoted as wh. The support vector of a vector h of length n is defined as supp(h) = (0 ≤ i ≤ n − 1|hi ̸= 0). The reciprocal of a polynomial h(x) is represented as h∗(x) = xkh(x−1). A polynomial of degree k in F2[x] is denoted as primitive if it is the minimal polynomial for a primitive element in F2k .

V. CONCLUSION
We have studied PRC-LDPC codes in terms of minimum distance and other properties. We have shown that their highly structured parity-check matrices make them proper for lowcomplexity applications. In fact, through numerical simulations, we have shown that puncturing and shortening operations have a limited impact on their error rate performance.
