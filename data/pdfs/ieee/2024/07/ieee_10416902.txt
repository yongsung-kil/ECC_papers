A Novel Search-Based Compute-in-Memory Minimum Values Generation Scheme for
Low-Complexity LDPC Min-Sum Decoding
Zhipeng Guo , Deming Zhang , Member, IEEE, Kaili Zhang, Mingyang Song, Yue Zhang , Senior Member, IEEE, and Lang Zeng , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—The generation of the minimum values (the first two minima and the index of the minimum) of numerous variableto-check messages (V2CM) is the major bottleneck in efficient min-sum decoding of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. This brief proposed a novel search-based compute-in-memory (CIM) based minimum values generation (MVG) scheme with two search strategies sharing two core circuits, which avoids the hardware-costly cascading of comparators and multiplexers necessary in conventional comparison-based schemes. The two shared core circuits include (1) the multi-bit content-addressable memory (MCAM) circuit for fast and concurrent search on stored operands (V2CMs) and (2) the search result evaluation (SRE) circuit to generate search result evaluation signals for the update of the minimum values. The two search strategies include (1) the sequential traversal search (STS) strategy and (2) the self-adaptive dichotomic search (SaDS) strategy for low- and high-precision decoding scenarios. Eventually, with the 14nm FinFET design kit, simulation results show that, in terms of area-delay complexity (ADC), the proposed MVG scheme achieves an average of 83% reduction in both low- and high-precision-scenarios, over the conventional comparison-based schemes.
Index Terms—Low-density parity-check, compute-in-memory, minimum values generation, min-sum algorithm.

Conventionally, the minimum values generation (MVG) scheme that computes the above three minimum values is based on costly cascading comparator units in a tree structure with various connection modules [7], [8], [9], [10], [11], which worsens as the codeword length of LDPC codes increases for better error correction performance. To reduce the hardware complexity, various single-minimum-based schemes [12], [13], [14], [15], [16], [17] are proposed to estimate rather than accurately compute the second minimum, which, however, induces certain error correction performance degradation. Therefore, inspired by the emerging compute-in-memory (CIM) technology and the concurrent search capability of content-addressable memory (CAM), we proposed a novel search-based CIM MVG scheme.

II. PROPOSED SEARCH-BASED CIM MVG SCHEME
As illustrated in Fig. 1, the proposed MVG scheme comprises two core circuits, including the multi-bit contentaddressable-memory (MCAM) circuit and the search result evaluation (SRE) circuit, shared by two search strategies, including the sequential traversal search (STS) strategy and the self-adaptive dichotomic search (SaDS) strategy for low- and high-data-precision decoding scenarios. The MCAM circuit realizes fast and concurrent search of input search data on stored operands (the MCAM cells), and the SRE circuit generates search result evaluation signals to update the minimum values.

A. NOR-MCAM Circuit
Fig. 2(a) shows the circuit schematic of the typical 10TNOR-based content-addressable memory (10T-NOR-CAM) cell, containing additional four transistors (N1, N2, N3, N4) for bit comparison between the memory bit (Q and QB) and the search bit (SL and SLB). The transistor pairs (N1, N2) and (N3, N4), respectively, constitute the pull-down paths of the bit-match-line (BML) which is connected to VDD via PMOS transistor P0. As shown in the truth table in Fig. 2(b), the bit comparison comprises the following two phases.
• Precharge phase: The search-lines SL (and SLB) are driven low to deactivate both pull-down paths, disconnecting the BML from the ground. Simultaneously, transistor P0 is driven high to precharge the BML VDD;
• Evaluation phase: the search-lines SL (and SLB) are driven according to the search bit ([SL, SLB] = [1, 0] for ‘1’, [0, 1] for ‘0’, and [0, 0] for ‘X’, which matches any bit, either ‘0’ or ‘1’). The match between the memory bit and search bit deactivates both pull-down paths so that BML maintains the precharged VDD. While the mismatch between them activates at least one of the pull-down paths (either N1 and N2 or N3 and N4), discharging BML (precharged to VDD) to GND.
As shown in Fig. 2(c), to perform word (multiple-bit) search operations, a NOR-based multiple-bit CAM (NOR-MCAM) cell is developed by concatenating the BMLs of multiple 10TNOR-CAM cells to form the NOR match-line (NOR-ML). As shown in the truth table of Fig. 2(d), a word match between the input search data and the memory data happens only when all w memory bits match the correspondent search bits.

B. Search Result Evaluation Circuit
The search result evaluation (SRE) circuit processes the dc parallel word search result signals WML[0:dc-1] from one column of the MCAM array to generate the counting signals GT0 and GT1 (whether the number of ‘1’ inputs in WML[0:dc-1] is greater-than-0 and greater-than-1), and the non-priority-encoder indexing signal NPE[0:K-1] (K = log2dc ) which gives the index of the only ‘1’ input when there’s only one ‘1’ input (indicated by GT0=1 and GT1=0).
As shown in Fig. 3, the SRE circuit employs the dynamiclogic-based implementation, to minimize the hardware cost without inducing delay degradation compared to static-logicbased implementation, since the precharge phase (“logic dead zone”) of dynamic logic circuits is concurrent with that of the MCAM circuit. Fig. 3(a) gives the general structure of dynamic-logic circuits, in which the inverse state (OUT) of the capacitive node (OUTB) gives the target logic expression. As shown in Fig. 3(g), the operations of dynamic logic circuits are composed of two phases. In the precharge phase, the capacitive node OUTB is charged to VDD (‘1’) via a precharge circuit (P0). In the evaluation phase, OUTB maintains the precharged VDD (logic ‘1’) or is discharged to GND (logic ‘0’) via NMOS-transistor-based pull-down network (PDN).
The major design challenge comes down to the transformation from indirect mathematical computations (especially GT1) to explicit Boolean equivalents, based on which the PDNs are built. The construction of GT0 and the GT1 & NPE complex circuits are detailed as follows.
1) PDN of the GT0 and NPE Circuits: The GT0 and NPE circuits intrinsically perform multiple input OR operations.

Therefore, as shown in Fig. 3(b) and Fig. 3(c), the PDNs are created by connecting multiple NMOS transistors parallelly, which are gated respectively by certain search result signals.
2) PDN of the GT1 Circuit: As illustrated in Fig. 3(d), the GT1’s PDN is composed of K (K= log2dc ) pull-down blocks (PDBs). Each PDB is composed of dc NMOS transistors gated respectively by dc inputs WML[0:dc-1] and separated into two serially connected groups, within each of which NMOS transistors are connected in a parallel manner. The grouping strategy in each PDB is based on the WML’s binary indexes. To be more specific, In the kth-PDB, the upper group (UG, connected to GT1B) of NMOS transistors are gated by WMLs whose binary indexes’ kth significant bit is ‘0’, and the lower group (LG, connected to GND) of NMOS transistors are gated by WMLs whose binary indexes’ kth significant bit is ‘1’.
The correctness can be validated through proof by contradiction. When more than one inputs (WMLs) are ‘1’, the indexes of at least two of them could be referred to as n1 and n2 (n1 = n2), and at least one bit (assumed as the kth significant bit) of their binary representation is different (otherwise n1 = n2). Then the kth-PDB will be activated since two NMOS gated by WML[n1] and WML[n2] belonging to two groups are turned on, connecting GT1B to GND, and eventually, GT1 turns VDD. In other cases when less than two inputs are VDD (‘1’), no pull-down paths are turned on since there’s only one group activated within each PDB. Therefore the eventual output GT1 maintains GND (‘0’).
3) The GT1 & NPE Complex Circuit: As illustrated in Fig. 3(e), the GT1 and NPE circuits can be integrated into the GT1 & NPE complex circuit to further minimize the area cost, since the PDB of NPE[k-1] (in Fig. 3(c)) is identical to the lower group of GT1’s kth-PDB (in Fig. 3(d)). As shown in Fig. 3(f), the intermediate capacitive node NPEB[k-1] could be discharged only through either (1) the internal path, or (2) the external path. When only one input (WMLs) is ‘1’, the GT1B maintains VDD, deactivating the external path; therefore, the intermediate nodes NPEB[0:K-1] can only be discharged through the internal path, the inverse state NPEB[0:K-1] of which gives the index of the sole match.

C. Sequential Traversal Search Strategy

The principle of the STS strategy is searching through the list of all possible values with the finite magnitude precision (w bits) sequentially, and the first search data that matches the memory data is the first minimum (Min1). Simultaneously, if the number of matches is greater than one, it represents that the first and the second minimum are the same (Min1=Min2), thus the second minimum (Min2) is also the current search data; otherwise (the number is equal to one), the search continues and the subsequent first search data that matches any of the memory data is the second minimum (Min2). The update control signals are given in the equations (1), where the Find_Min1 marks the find of Min1, Write_Min1 controls the update of the Min1 (Search_Data) and Index1 (NPE), and the Write_Min2 controls the update of Min2 (Search_Data). Fig. 4 clearly illustrated the minimum values generation processes of both cases (Fig. 4(a) for Min1 = Min2 and Fig. 4(b) for Min1 = Min2) when data magnitude precision w = 4. In the worst case, the process takes 2w search cycles; therefore, this strategy only applies to low-precision scenarios.

D. Self-Adaptive Dichotomic Search Strategy
To tackle high-data-precision decoding, the self-adaptive dichotomic search (SaDS) strategy is proposed based on dichotomy. As illustrated in Fig. 5, the computation comprises one initialization phase and two independent search phases for generating Min1 and Min2, respectively, each search phase comprising w cycles for determining w magnitude bits. Each cycle divides the currently possible range into two sub-ranges, with the most significant undetermined bit being ‘0’ or ‘1’ (the former (_. . . _0X. . . X)2, annotated in deep color, and the latter (_. . . _1X. . . X)2, annotated in shallow color), and attempts to search the former sub-range and determines the next search range according to the update results (the update bit signal).

III. VERIFICATION AND PERFORMANCE EVALUATION
In this section, based on a CMOS 14nm FinFET design kit, transient simulations have been performed for the functional verification of the core MCAM and SRE circuits, and the key performance evaluation and comparison of the proposed search-based MVG scheme with conventional comparisonbased schemes. The supply voltage VDD is set to 0.8V, and all transistors are with the default settings.
The verification adopts the magnitude bits width of w = 5 bits and the number of NOR-MCAM cells of 32. The NORMCAM cells are previously configured (cell[21] = (10001)2, cell[22] = (10011)2, and else are (10111)2). Fig. 6 gives the transient simulation waveform of three search cycles (in consequence of (10000)2, (10001)2, (10011)2), and the results are as expected.

1) Cycle 1: In the search of (10000)2, no NOR-MCAM cells match it so that GT0 = 0, GT1 = 0, and the index signal NPE is not needed;
2) Cycle 2: In the search of (1000X)2, only one memory data NOR-MCAM cell[21] ((10001)2) matches it so that GT0 = 1, GT1 = 0, and the required index signal NPE[0:4] are correctly given as (10101)2=(21)10 to locate the sole matched NOR-MCAM cell[21];
3) Cycle 3: In the search of (100XX)2, both NOR-MCAM cell[21] ((10001)2) and cell[22] ((10011)2) match it so that GT0 = 1, GT1 = 1, the signal NPE not needed.

IV. CONCLUSION
We have presented an efficient novel search-based CIM minimum values generation scheme with two search strategies sharing two core circuits for low- and high-magnitudeprecision decoding. The proposed MVG scheme largely reduces the area cost by avoiding costly comparators and multiplexors while maintaining comparable critical delay performances.
