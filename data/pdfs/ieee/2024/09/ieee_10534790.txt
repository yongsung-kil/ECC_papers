Improvement of SP Decoding Considering the Influence of Recording Patterns by Neural Network in SMR
Madoka Nishikawa 1, Yasuaki Nakamura 1, Yasushi Kanai 2, and Yoshihiro Okamoto 1
1Graduate School of Science and Engineering, Ehime University, Matsuyama 790-8577, Japan 2Department of Engineering, Niigata Institute of Technology, Kashiwazaki 945-1195, Japan

We study a low-density parity-check (LDPC) encoding and iterative decoding system for a shingled magnetic recording (SMR). In particular, we show the usefulness of applying a neural network in iterative decoding. We previously adopted the neural network to evaluate the log-likelihood ratio (LLR) related to row operations on the parity check matrix for the decoding target bit and improved the sum-product (SP) decoding. In this study, we propose to update the LLR considering the influence of noise depending on the recording pattern by providing the LLRs for the decoding target and its adjacent bits to the neural network in SP decoding. In addition, we explore the parameters to update the LLRs by applying the genetic algorithm (GA) to achieve more effective iterative decoding.
Index Terms— Low-density parity-check (LDPC) code, neural network, shingled magnetic recording (SMR), sum-product (SP) decoding.

I. INTRODUCTION

T HE explosive growth in the amount of data on a global scale requires the rapid addition of data centers and cloud storage. As various types of information storage coexist, hard disk drives (HDDs), which are characterized by their large capacity and low cost, are further expected as storage devices.
We have been studying low-density parity-check (LDPC) coding and iterative decoding in shingled magnetic recording (SMR) to realize HDDs beyond the areal density of 4 Tbits/in2 [1]. Then, we have also proposed a sum-product (SP) decoder using a neural network and realized efficient iterative decoding [2].
In this study, as a new approach to improve SP decoding, the neural network evaluates the log-likelihood ratio (LLR) related to the decoding target bit and its adjacent bits in row operations on the parity check matrix to consider the influence of noise depending on the recording patterns. Furthermore, we update the LLR using parameters explored by applying the genetic algorithm (GA) [3] to achieve more effective iterative decoding.

The input sequence is encoded by a 128/130 (0, 16/8) run-length-limited (RLL) [4] encoder and a (30, 3)-regular LDPC encoder [5]. After that, the recording sequence is recorded on a granular medium model [6]. The model sets the average grain size, the grain diameter dispersion, and the average nonmagnetic grain boundary to 5.0, 1.0, and 1.0 nm, respectively. III. SP DECODER USING NEURAL NETWORK
The SP decoding is performed based on a parity check
matrix and consists of row operations, parity checks, iterative
decoding checks, column operations, extrinsic value opera-
tions, and posterior value operations. Fig. 2 shows a block
diagram of the row operations in the SP decoder using the
neural network to estimate the influence of noise depending
on the recording pattern. The parity check matrix with a code
length of 4096 bytes, a column weight of 3, and a row weight
of 30 is employed, so three LLRs are calculated as shown in
the figure. Therefore, unlike the parity check matrix created
by Gallager’s construction method [5], the parity check matrix
we used does not have a run of “1” in not only the 2nd and
3rd sub-matrices but also the 1st sub-matrix to reduce the
correlation due to the PR channel. We use in the figure that γkn means the transition probability from the previous point to the current point k on the trellis diagram for the nth (n = 1–3) sub-matrix of LDPC code. Also, αkn and βkn mean the forward and backward probabilities on the point, respectively. Then, ln γkn is calculated from the SP decoder input and the nth LLR for prior information for each sub-matrix. Also, the
nth LLR for message nodes is calculated by row operations using ln γkn, ln αkn, and ln βkn based on the Max-log-MAP algorithm [8]. ln γkn is evaluated by the neural network and updated according to the evaluation results. We will explain the update method of ln γkn later.

IV. PERFORMANCE EVALUATION
Fig. 7 shows the BER performance for iglobal in the conventional system 2. The simulation parameters are set to SNRS = 21.5 dB and iglobal = 10. As can be seen from the figure, the error floor appears in isp ≥ 5. Therefore, we will focus on isp = 6 and aim to achieve error-free by applying the proposed system.
Fig. 8 shows the ln γk1 distribution before updating, that is, the iteration parameters are set to isp = 0 and iglobal = 0. Also, we set SNRS = 21.5 dB. Note that we use data in the 29th sector among 30 sectors where there is the cause of remaining errors, as shown in Fig. 7. Furthermore, Fig. 9 shows the ln γk1 distributions in Fig. 8 by discerning the respective recording patterns. In this study, we discern the recording patterns to “000” or “111,” “001” or “110,” “011” or “100,” and “010” or “101.” Also, Table II shows the number of ln γk1 for the respective recording pattern in Fig. 9. In those

figures, the symbols of the gray dot and red cross show the correct and erroneous ln γk1, respectively. As can be seen from those figures and the table, the recording patterns “010” or
“101” with magnetization reversal on both sides have the most
errors compared to the other recording patterns. From this
result, it can be seen that the influence of noise depending
on recording patterns is greatest for these recording patterns.
Therefore, we aim to reduce the influence of the noise by the
neural network shown in Fig. 3.
Next, we will focus on the decoding performance for record-
ing pattern “010” or “101” to confirm the effect of reducing
the noise influence depending on the recording pattern.
