A Relaxation Oscillator-Based Probabilistic 
Combinatorial Optimization Engine for Soft 
Decoding of LDPC Codes 
Evangelos Dikopoulos, Luke Wormald, Ying-Tuan Hsu, Wei Tang, Po-Shao Chen, Zhengya Zhang, Michael P. Flynn 
Electrical Engineering and Computer Science, University of Michigan  
Ann Arbor, Michigan, USA 
Email: vangelis@umich.edu 
Abstract— Drawing insights from quantum computing, 
oscillator-based computing leverages continuous-time operation 
and massive parallelism to accelerate challenging computational 
tasks. This work advances the field to demonstrate a 
Combinatorial Optimization Problem (COP) engine for 
efficient, robust, one-shot oscillator-based soft decoding of 
LDPC codes for the first time. We present a 28nm CMOS 
prototype that achieves a Frame, Bit Error Rate (FER, BER) of 
1.38 x 10-5, 1.25 x 10-6 respectively at 7dB SNR and an energy 
efficiency of 5.26 pJ/bit, which surpasses the normalized 
efficiencies of recent state-of-the-art decoders [1][2][3] by 11x, 
3x, 1.5x respectively. Tested with more than 100 million frame 
decodings, the prototype demonstrates consistent performance 
across a range of SNRs, supply voltages, and temperatures.

I. INTRODUCTION
Quantum-inspired architectures accelerate a broad range 
of computations thanks to direct mapping of optimization 
problems, flexible interconnect, continuous time operation, 
and massive parallelism [4][5][6]. Although quantum 
computers have shown computational supremacy for very 
specific computing tasks, CMOS architectures that emulate 
some aspects of quantum computing offer scalability and 
enormous energy savings. Oscillator-based computers replace 
quantum spin with phase, with 0 and 180 degrees representing 
0 and 1. The connectivity between oscillators defines the 
optimization problem objective function according to the 
problem graphs (e.g., Tanner Graph for LDPC Decoders). 
After initialization, the oscillator-compute elements interact 
and evolve rapidly towards a global extremum until 
convergence. The final phases represent the problem solution. 
This work demonstrates advancements in oscillator 
computing with 6-body interactions and arbitrary digital 
coupling, soft oscillator initialization, one-shot compute, and 
a robust and precise compute node featuring in-oscillator 
DACs. 
Recent work with the D-Wave quantum computer [7] and 
the Fujitsu digital annealer [8] implemented LDPC decoding 
as a combinatorial optimization problem, but they consume 
high power, up to kWs on a quantum computer. Oscillator-
based and similar computing systems introduced solvers for 
problems like max-cut and 3-SAT [4][5][6], but exhibit 
limitations in spin coupling, inefficient problem mapping, and 
hard (binary) solver initialization, which make them 
unsuitable for more complex optimization problems, like the 
soft decoding of LDPC codes. 
This work introduces a new architecture that directly maps 
a higher-order fully connected graph (like the Tanner graph of 
an LDPC code) to arbitrarily coupled relaxation oscillators 
(RXOs) that are precisely initialized to enable soft decoding. 
The proposed architecture natively implements k-body (k=6 
in our prototype design) interactions (Fig. 1) and can be 
adapted for other optimization problems with high 
complexity, like higher-order Ising machines or k-SAT 
graphs. While [7] uses the Chimera topology in D-Wave’s 
2000-qubit quantum annealer, it requires almost 5 physical 
qubits for every LDPC variable and consumes kWs of power. 
Our proposed direct mapping is optimal with only one 
physical spin per variable. [5] and [6] provide all-to-all 
connected spins, nevertheless with three and two-body 
connectivity, respectively. Also, the precise initialization of 
spins in this work showcases unprecedented oscillator control 
that allows for high-performance soft LDPC codes (Fig. 2).

II. PROPOSED LDPC SOLVER 
A. Optimization Problem Mapping 
 
We highlight the enhanced capabilities of our oscillator 
compute platform by demonstrating soft LDPC decoding. 
Inspired by Belief Propagation (BP), the dominant digital 
decoding algorithm, the proposed solver directly maps LDPC 
decoding as an optimization problem to coupled oscillators. 
The H matrix of the LDPC code is used for error correction by 
defining parity checks among received bits. In this work, it 
also describes the optimization function of the solver. 
Arbitrary coupling implements the H-matrix (Fig. 3), which 
defines XOR-based coupling among groups of k oscillators (k 
is the check node degree of the code) according to the sparse 
all-to-all connected graph. The proposed energy function 
features a stable state with minimum energy for every correct 
data frame with no parity check violations.  
 
Taking advantage of our solver’s unique ability to set 
arbitrary initial phases, we map the received, error-corrupted 
LDPC data frame to the initial oscillator phases (soft 
decoding). The reliability of a received bit (i.e., soft 
information) specifies its likelihood of being either a 0 or a 1. 
By initializing the spins with the best possible information, 
they are more likely to converge on the correct solution. 
Encoding soft information as the initial solver state increases 
both BER/FER performance and throughput significantly.  
 
Each oscillator’s instantaneous frequency changes based 
on the sum of its associated parity check violations. This 
allows the system to concurrently search for the ground state 
(lowest energy). Convergence is achieved when all the parity 
checks are satisfied and a corrected codeword is found. This 
architecture is easily adaptable to a wide range of other 
combinatorial optimization problems like Max-Cut or k-SAT. 
We initialize the RXO phases with soft inputs, instead of 
binary inputs as in hard decoding, to improve decoding 
performance. Once released, the feedback system computes 
parity checks in continuous time and adjusts the phases to 
minimize the system’s energy. The oscillator phases evolve 
until all parity checks are satisfied, at which point the phase 
difference between all compute nodes is either 0 or 180 
degrees. The final oscillator phases represent the decoded data 
frame.

B. RXO Core and Solver Macro 
A key innovation is the use of twin-capacitor RXOs 
(standalone proposed in [9]). The twin-capacitor relaxation 
oscillator alternately integrates current on one capacitor while 
resetting the other (Fig. 4). A comparator detects when the 
charging capacitor voltage exceeds a threshold and sets (or 
resets) a latch, which stores the oscillator’s binary state. The 
twin capacitor relaxation oscillator provides crucial 
advantages for oscillator computing, especially when 
compared to ring oscillators [4][6]: (i) the symmetric structure 
delivers an accurate duty cycle, (ii) manipulation of reference 
voltages enables accurate injection locking and coupling, (iii) 
current-source charging greatly reduces PVT sensitivity, (iv) 
the digital output is robust and doesn’t load the RXO, (v) we 
can directly set the state variables on the RXO capacitors. 
 
At initialization, we set the oscillator latch states and the 
capacitor voltages for unprecedented control of the initial 
phases. 3-bit Soft DACs (SDACs) set the capacitor voltage, 
which is equivalent to setting the phase. For each oscillator, a 
local digital circuit sums the number of parity check violations 
for each node (Fig. 4). The output of the summation circuit 
drives Feedback DACs (FDACs) that modulate the threshold 
of the comparator and consequently, the RXO’s frequency, 
across four distinct levels. The nominal frequency 
corresponds to zero parity check violations for an oscillator. 
We leverage digital signaling to avoid the limitations of 
conventional analog oscillator coupling (e.g., with resistors, as 
in [4][6]). After the array is initialized with soft information, 
it is released and the asynchronous digital feedback signals 
drive the FDACs and injection lock the spins (Fig. 6). 
 
We introduce a twin crossbar architecture (Fig. 5) that 
supports the bidirectional connectivity between oscillators and 
clauses and allows one-to-one variable-oscillator mapping. To 
demonstrate the architecture, we implemented a decoder for a 
regular (96, 48) code. The input crossbar maps RXO outputs 
to the 48 6-input XOR gates that implement parity checks. The 
output crossbar routes parity check outputs back to the array.

C. Sampling System and Digital Controller 
 
The sampling system (Fig. 7) enables robust and precise 
readout of the asynchronous array’s state at a compute cycle’s 
conclusion. The architecture comprises a Delay-locked Loop 
(DLL), a sampling controller, and output data registers. An 
error-sense circuit indicates the culmination of a compute 
cycle once all check node signals from the 48 XOR gates are 
de-asserted. The 1-bit flag from this circuit triggers sampling. 
The DLL generates six distinct output clock phases, enabling 
six samples of an oscillation period. The DLL phases route 
through a clock tree to the 96 6-bit registers, which sample 
continuously during computation. After the sampling trigger, 
the controller waits for all 6 DLL rising edges and then freezes 
the 96 registers for readout. The 0.1-300 MHz DLL frequency 
range supports a wide operating range.  
 
Fig. 7 DLL-based sampling system for robust oscillator array readout. 
To enable a self-contained solver, we integrate a digital 
controller (Fig. 8) that handles the configuration of the 
oscillator array, loading of initial soft values to the oscillators, 
management of operations, and retrieval of results from the 
sampling system. This controller generates control waveforms 
to initiate decoding, loop operations, pause states if needed, 
and terminate decoding upon completion. To minimize 
memory usage, we utilize an instruction-based processor, 
resulting in a smaller memory footprint. The entire decoding 
process can be accomplished using a sequence of up to 256 
instructions. A Quad-SPI (QSPI) interfaces with a host FPGA.

III. PROTOTYPE AND MEASUREMENT 
The solver is fabricated in 28nm CMOS (Fig. 9) and has a 
core area of 0.21 mm2. The prototype’s performance was 
evaluated with more than 100 million decoding cycles under 
various SNR levels (Fig. 10), for different temperatures, for 
different die, and supply voltages (Fig. 11). The prototype 
achieves a measured FER of 1.25 x 10-6  at a 7dB SNR, a 
convergence rate (% of times solver arrives at solution) of 
99.90%, and power consumption of 5.9 mW. The mean time-
to-solution (TTS) for an SNR of 7dB is 89 ns. The measured 
convergence rate (CR) varies less than 0.5% over a 
temperature range of -20° to 70° C, less than 3% over a supply 
range of 0.85-1 V, and less than 5% over 8 die. 
The system outperforms BP decoding, the standard in this 
field, in the low-SNR region (one and two orders of magnitude 
better FER and BER for 3dB SNR), is more power efficient 
than similar solver ICs (Table II), and has a smaller TTS. The 
solver surpasses the normalized energy efficiency of recent 
state-of-the-art BP-based decoders [1][2][3] (Table III). The 
proposed architecture is scalable and has the potential to be 
competitive and deliver better energy efficiency when adapted 
to the larger codes used in these dedicated digital decoders.

IV. CONCLUSION 
 
This work introduces a first-of-its-kind continuous-time 
probabilistic combinatorial optimization engine that directly 
maps a higher-order graph with 6-body interactions to a 
dynamical system of coupled oscillators, improving upon 
systems with two and three-body connectivity [4][5][6]. 
Direct one-on-one mapping of oscillators/variables alleviates 
inefficiencies of locally connected solvers, like the Chimera 
graph [7], while precise oscillator control enables high-
performance soft LDPC decoding.  
 
The proposed architecture tackles challenges of oscillator-
-computing, such as precision, throughput, variation, and 
iterative computing. A twin digital crossbar enables arbitrary, 
robust digital coupling for complex interactions, relaxation 
oscillators alleviate PVT sensitivity, while in-oscillator DACs 
allow soft decoding and precise coupling. The prototype 
surpasses the FER, and BER of Belief Propagation in the low-
SNR region. The sub-100ns time-to-solution combined with 
5.9mW of power dissipation enables improved normalized 
energy efficiency compared to state-of-the-art decoders.
