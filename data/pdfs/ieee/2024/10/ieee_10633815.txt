A Heterogeneous and Reconfigurable Decoder for the IEEE 1901 Standard
Yuxing Chen and Zhongfeng Wang , Fellow, IEEE

Abstract— The IEEE 1901 standard plays a crucial role in the extensive fields of smart grids, electric vehicles, and the Internet of Things. The forward error correction (FEC) codes specified in this standard include low-density parity-check convolutional codes (LDPC-CCs), Reed-Solomon (RS) codes, and RS convolutional concatenated (RSCC) codes. This work proposes a low-complexity decoder fully compliant with the standard. First, a heterogeneous scheme is introduced to LDPC-CC decoding. The new scheme assigns different data formats among processing elements (PEs), which reduces the overall storage size and enables a customized datapath down to the PE level. Then, to efficiently support diverse FEC demands in the standard, a reconfigurable architecture is thoroughly explored from both memory and datapath aspects. Leveraging these techniques, the first decoder compatible with the IEEE 1901 standard is developed and implemented with 55nm technology. Implementation results demonstrate that the proposed decoder satisfies the standard’s requirements while exhibiting low hardware complexity.
Index Terms— IEEE 1901, low-density parity-check convolutional codes, heterogeneous scheme, reconfigurable design.
I. INTRODUCTION
THE implementation of standard-compatible forward error correction (FEC) codecs has garnered considerable interest owing to their practical significance. For example, lowdensity parity-check block code (LDPC-BC) codecs for IEEE 802.11 standard [1], [2], polar codecs for 5G-NR standard [3], [4], low-density parity-check convolutional code (LDPC-CC) codecs for IEEE 1901 standard [5], [6].
The IEEE 1901 standard is developed by the IEEE Communications Society together with many corporations as participants (e.g., Broadcom, Intel, Texas Instruments, Panasonic) and plays a unique role in the broad areas of smart grids, electric vehicles, and the Internet of Things [7], [8]. III. HETEROGENEOUS LDPC-CC DECODER
In this section, first, the heterogeneous storage scheme will be proposed for the LDPC-CC decoder. Then, corresponding hardware architecture is designed.
A. Heterogeneous Storage Scheme
As shown in Fig. 6, the LDPC-CC decoder consists of APLLR storage, C2V storage, datapath, and control units. The datapath mainly contains PEs. The number of PEs N p is 10 in Fig. 6. First, a comprehensive analysis is conducted on the LDPC-CC decoder of the IEEE 1901 standard to identify the components that contribute significantly to the overall area consumption. The area breakdown analysis of the state-ofthe-art categorized memory-based (CMem-based) LDPC-CC decoder [9] is as follows. The datapath and control module occupies 16.2% and 0.8% of the total area, respectively. The AP-LLR memory and C2V memory occupy 51.9% and 31.1% of the overall area, respectively. In other words, the memory part consumes more than 80% of the total area. Therefore, design efforts should be primarily given to the reduction of memory size. Then, an experiment is performed to examine the distribution of AP-LLR message values among different PEs. The number of PEs and the bit-widths of messages are set the same as those in previous IEEE 1901 standard LDPC-CC decoders [6], [9], [10], where N p = 10, wL = 8, and wQ = 6. In other words, the number of PEs is set as 10, and the bit-widths of AP-LLR and C2V messages are 8 and 6. Probability density functions of absolute values for AP-LLR messages among different PEs are drawn in Fig. 7. The value range of AP-LLR messages exhibits significant variations across different PEs. As the PE index increases, both the peak value and the value range of AP-LLR messages

become larger. For PE 0, more than 99% of the absolute values

of AP-LLR messages range from 0 to 31, and less than 1%

of the absolute values range from 32 to 127. For PE 5, more

than 97% of the absolute values of AP-LLR messages range

from 0 to 63, and less than 3% of the absolute values range

from 64 to 127.

Inspired by this phenomenon, we propose a heterogeneous

storage scheme that strategically allocates different message

formats among PEs with the objective of minimizing the

overall storage size. The heterogeneous design follows two

rules. ➀ Due to the relatively small absolute value ranges of

AP-LLR messages in low-index PEs, it is possible to reduce

the integer bit-width of messages associated with those PEs.

➁ Besides, the fraction bit-width of messages in high-index

PEs can be reduced, the reason for which is as follows. The

final step of decoding, the hard-decision, is performed after the

calculation of the last PE and solely focuses on determining

whether the AP-LLR messages are positive or negative. As a

result, the resolution of messages in PEs with high indices

can be relaxed. In other words, the fraction bit-width can be

cut down. Similar results can be obtained for C2V messages.

A. Reconfigurable Memory
To minimize area consumption, the memory of the LDPC-CC decoder can be reused by the RS and CC decoders. Memory usage in different decoding modes is carefully designed to achieve optimal sharing.
The size of the memory in the RS decoder is 255 × 8 bits. In order to fit into the size of an AP-LLR bank, the number of words must ≤ 76, and the number of bits must ≤ 66. We propose a memory reshape method to resize the memory in the RS decoder into 64 × 32 bits. As shown in Fig. 14, the hardware overhead is three buffers and several multiplexers. Addr is the short form for address. The patterns of write addresses and write enable signals before and after memory reshaping are listed in Table IV. To write an RS codeword into the original RS memory, the 8-bit write address signal increments by one in each cycle, and the write enable signal maintains logic high. In each cycle, a byte of the RS codeword is written into the 255 × 8 bits memory. After memory reshaping, the input data is 1 byte, but a word of the memory is 4 bytes. Additional buffers are incorporated to aggregate every 4 bytes into a complete unit, which is subsequently written into the memory upon successful aggregation. Besides, three signals, the write word address signal, the write byte address signal, and the write enable signal, are used to control the write logic. The 6-bit write word address signal increments by one every four cycles. The write byte address is a 2-bit signal for

selecting the write position within a word (4 bytes). The write byte address signal follows a repeating pattern of 0, 1, 2, and 3. The write enable signal is set to logic high only at the end of every four cycles and during the last cycle. As shown in Fig. 14, inputs of the three buffers (buf 0, buf 1, buf 2) are connected to multiplexers controlled by select signals selbu f 0, selbu f 1, selbu f 2. The select signal selbu f i equals to (write byte addr == i), where i = 0, 1, 2. The output of buf 2 is connected to a multiplexer controlled by select signal selld , which is set as logic high only when the input data is the last RS symbol. For ease of design, the write word address and write byte address are set as the most significant 6 bits and least significant 2 bits of the 255-ary counter. Each buffer is implemented by an 8-bit D flip-flop. Read word address and read byte address signals behave similarly to the write addresses. However, the read enable signal maintains logic high. The read byte address signal acts as the select signal of the output multiplexer to choose the output 1-byte data from the 4-byte word.
The CC decoder has four memory banks. The size of each memory bank is 120 × 64 bits, larger than the size of an AP-LLR bank and less than the size of two AP-LLR banks. Therefore, at least two AP-LLR banks are needed to build a CC memory bank. A 56 × 64 bits upper bank and a 64 × 64 bits lower bank are used to build a CC memory bank by word expansion, as shown in Fig. 15. B. Reconfigurable Datapath
The Min1 Min2 Finder in the LDPC-CC decoder and the Min1 Finder in the CC decoder share similarities. However, the Min1 Min2 Finder operates on unsigned numbers, but the Min1 Finder operates on signed numbers. To solve this, we convert the metrics in the CC decoder from signed numbers to unsigned numbers. The channel LLRs are quantized into nL -bit signed numbers with n f fraction bits. Therefore, the resolution δ equals to 2−n f . Besides, −2nL −1 × δ is forbidden to achieve a balance between the quantity of positive and negative numbers, i.e., the value range is
(−2nL −1 + 1) × δ ≤ quan(Ik ) ≤ (2nL −1 − 1) × δ, (12)

where quan(·) denotes quantization operation. An offset is

added to the quantized channel LLR to convert it into a

nonnegative number Ik′ , as shown in (13),

Ik′ = quan(Ik ) + [(2nL −1 − 1) × δ].

(13)

Correspondingly, change Ik into Ik′ in (5) to (8). The negate operation of Ik′ is calculated by
neg(Ik′ ) = (2nL − 2) × δ − Ik′ = −quan(Ik ) + [(2nL −1 − 1) × δ]. (14)

The value ranges of Ik′ and neg(Ik′ ) lie in

0 ≤ Ik′ or neg(Ik′ ) ≤ (2nL − 2) × δ,

(15)

both of which are nonnegative numbers. The correctness of the Viterbi algorithm is preserved through this conversion, which can be explained as follows. The Viterbi decoding algorithm selects the maximum-likelihood path, which depends solely on the metric differences [21]. Since only an offset is introduced to the channel LLR, the relative value of Ik′ remains unaltered. This, along with (5) to (8), ensures that the metric differences remain consistent.
Through the proposed conversion, operations of the Min1 Finder in the CC decoder are performed on unsigned numbers. Since the Min1 Min2 Finder in the LDPC-CC decoder also operates on unsigned numbers, some circuitries can be shared to reduce hardware complexity. To achieve this, we propose a reconfigurable Min Finder, illustrated in Fig. 17(a). It is composed of four 16-to-2 Min1 Min2 Finders, one 4-to-1 Min1 Finder, and several multiplexers. The blue and orange arrows denote signals for the LDPC-CC decoder and CC decoder, respectively. The black arrows denote signals shared by both decoders. When the reconfigurable decoder is in LDPC-CC mode, four 16-to-2 Min1 Min2 Finders act as the Min1 Min2 Finders in PE 2 to 5, respectively. The last input of each 16-to-2 Min1 Min2 Finder is the maximum value, and the other inputs are 15 V2C magnitudes. When the reconfigurable decoder is in RSCC mode, 64 PMs are divided into four groups and delivered into the 16-to-2 Min1 Min2 Finders. The results of 16-to-2 Min1 Min2 Finders are fed into the 4-to-1 Min1 Finder to calculate the Min1 and Min1 index of the 64 PMs.
The LDPC-CC, CC, and RS decoders all include adders. Adders in the LDPC-CC and CC decoders handle binary numbers, but adders in the RS decoder handle finite field numbers.

The error-correction performance of the FEC specified in the standard is illustrated and compared in Fig. 20. At the BER of 10−4, the RSCC codes exhibit at least a 1.2 dB gain compared to the RS code. At the BER of 10−5, the rate-4/5 LDPC-CC achieves a 2.3 dB gain over the rate-239/255×6.7 RSCC code, where code rates of both codewords are around 0.8. This observation effectively confirms the design methodology employed in the standard. LDPC-CC, RSCC, RS codes are regarded as high, medium, low error correction performance codes, respectively, showcasing their versatility in meeting the needs of different scenarios.

VI. CONCLUSION
In this work, we propose a decoder supporting all FEC types in the IEEE 1901 standard. The LDPC-CC decoding part is optimized by the heterogeneous scheme. To effectively support the required FEC codewords, a reconfigurable method is introduced to the decoder from both memory and datapath aspects. The error rate performances of different FECs are analyzed in detail. Not only does the proposed decoder meet the demands of the standard, but it also possesses low hardware complexity.
