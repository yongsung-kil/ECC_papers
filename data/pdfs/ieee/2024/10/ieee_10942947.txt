AbstractŌĆöSpatially-coupled low-density parity-check (SCLDPC) codes are considered an important candidate for the upcoming 6G standardization, owing to their superior errorcorrection capability and practical decoding complexity. However, current high-throughput SC-LDPC decoders rely on unrolled architectures tailored to a certain code and thus lack the necessary rate compatibility for wireless communications. In this paper, we present a fully reconfigurable decoder architecture for SC-LDPC codes. This hardware architecture can decode virtually any SC-LDPC code that fits in the allocated memories. Based on simplified routing networks, our SC-LDPC decoder can deliver extensive code length and code rate support and achieve high throughput to satisfy the requirements of 6G.
Index TermsŌĆöLDPC codes, SC-LDPC codes, hardware implementation, next-generation, wireless communications, 6G.
I. INTRODUCTION
After the successful introduction of low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes in 5G New Radio (NR) [1], research is vigorously being done on the selection of next-generation channel coding for 6G [2], [3]. Beyond retaining key features of 5G-NR LDPC codes, such as rate compatibility and flexible code lengths and rates, next-generation channel coding must meet even more stringent demands, e.g., a peak date rate of 200 Gbps - 1 Tbps, an energy efficiency of ideally around 1 pJ/bit, and a frame error rate (FER) down to 10ŌłÆ7 [2]. Given the expected ultra-high data rates and diverse operating scenarios, the choice of the right codes and the development of corresponding efficient decoders will be critical for 6G wireless systems, which is a significant challenge.
Due to their capacity-achieving performance, spatiallycoupled LDPC (SC-LDPC) codes have been viewed as one of the most promising candidates for 6G wireless systems [4], [5]. By superimposing LDPC block codes with a convolutional structure, SC-LDPC codes can achieve the maximum a-posterior probability (MAP) threshold of underlying LDPC blocks using belief propagation (BP) decoding [6]. In ultrahigh data rate scenarios, SC-LDPC codes enable a flexible frame length extension by changing the coupling length [7]. Recently, the authors of [8] proposed a new SC-LDPC code family called edge-spreading Raptor-like (ESRL) LDPC codes, which provides a good solution for next-generation channel coding. Unlike other SC-LDPC codes [9]ŌĆō[12], ESRL

codes introduce a new protomatrix structure and focus on the design of the coupled matrix. While taking the performance benefits of SC-LDPC codes, ESRL codes also retain key properties of 5G-NR LDPC codes, such as rate compatibility, quasi-cyclic (QC) structure, and BP decoding. When all code configurations, computational complexity, and hardware architecture are equivalent, ESRL codes can outperform the error-correcting performance of 5G-NR LDPC codes by around 1 dB and provide higher processing parallelism [8].
In terms of hardware implementations, numerous classic QC LDPC decoder architectures with varying degrees of processing parallelism have been reported in the literature [13]ŌĆō [16]. Specifically for 5G-NR LDPC codes, these architectural strategies have rapidly converged into two main schemes: row-parallel and block-parallel architectures. For instance, the authors of [15] proposed a partial row-parallel architecture to alleviate the routing complexity for long 5G-NR LDPC codes, while the authors of [16] designed a block-parallel 5GNR LDPC decoder to balance flexibility and peak throughput while supporting all 5G-NR LDPC codes. For SC-LDPC codes, decoder implementations can adopt different strategies based on the coupling length. A full BP decoder [17] is suitable for small to medium coupling lengths, while a sliding window BP decoder [18]ŌĆō[20] is better suited for medium to large coupling lengths. It is noteworthy that [21] proposed an unrolled fully parallel window decoder architecture as a solution for ultra-high throughput SC-LDPC decoders. Subsequently, [19], [20] employed this architecture to achieve throughput on the order of several hundred Gbps on ASIC. However, such high throughput gains come at the cost of sacrificing decoder rate flexibility and relying on hard-wired routing. This limitation makes such architectures less suitable for wireless communication systems, which demand rate flexibility. Given the excellent performance of the aforementioned ESRL codes, a special SC-LDPC variant, developing a decoder architecture for ESRL codes that simultaneously achieves both rate flexibility and high throughput in hardware is a critical challenge to satisfy the 6G demands.

II. PRELIMINARIES A. LDPC Codes
A QC-LDPC code can be described by an m ├Ś n protomatrix B with the information length of k = n ŌłÆ m. Each entry can be extended by a Z ├Ś Z cyclic-shifting identity matrix (Z is the lifting size). Namely, this expansion transforms B into a parity-check matrix (PCM) H, where M = m ┬Ę Z, N = n ┬Ę Z, and K = N ŌłÆ M . From a graphical perspective, H can be further interpreted as a bipartite Tanner graph [22], consisting of a set of M check nodes (CNs) and a set of N variable nodes (VNs). For each CN, let Vc denote the set of the adjacent VNs for the c-th CN, and let Cv denote the set of neighbours of the v-th VN. The number of neighbours connecting to a VN or a CN is referred to as their column- and row-degree, denoted as dv and dc, respectively. For simplicity, we define the cth row of B, or the rows c ┬Ę Z to (c + 1) ┬Ę Z ŌłÆ 1 of H, as

the c-th layer. Let qv denote the posterior log-likelihood ratio (LLR) associated with the v-th VN, and Let rc,v denote the message from the c-th CN to the v-th VN. We also define the intermediate variable tv, corresponding to the message from the v-th VN at the current row. B. Edge-Spreading Raptor-Like LDPC Codes

ESRL codes are a new rate-compatible SC-LDPC code

family [8], characterized by a code profile defined through

IV. CONFIGURABLE HARDWARE ARCHITECTURE OF ESRL DECODER
A. High-Level Overview
Fig. 4 illustrates the high-level architecture of our configurable high-throughput decoder for ESRL codes. The timeinvariant nature and absence of parallel edges in ESRL codes allow for the decomposition of this decoder into W = L + Žē sub-decoders operating in parallel, using SLME decoding. Despite the relatively long overall code length of ESRL codes, the coupling results in a shorter underlying blocklength. Namely, each decoder can adopt a highly parallel architecture with moderate routing complexity. To exploit this property, we use a more advanced partial row-parallel architecture [15] to reduce the area of computation units. This architecture will further decompose the processing of each layer into multiple cycles, and each cycle deals with ZŌĆ▓ = Z/S rows, where S is referred to as folding degree.

In each sub-decoder (except the last one handling the tail), the gathering routing unit (GRU) collects n groups of Q-messages and then reorganizes them into dcmax groups of Q-messages, as the row-parallel architecture can process up to dcmax non-zero blocks per layer. A group of QC-LDPC shift networks (QSNs), each of size ZŌĆ▓, is implemented to execute cyclic shifting on the data. Subsequently, the row computation unit (RCU) executes (1a) to (1c) sequentially. Note that compared to the block-parallel architecture, the RCU utilizes a SMU to find the first minimum and second minimum simultaneously. Finally, the distribution routing unit (DRU) redistributes the dcmax groups of updated Q-messages into n groups of Q-messages and then writes them back to the Q-memory. The whole decoding process is orchestrated by a controller, which reads from a list of instructions in the instruction memory and coordinates the components of our ESRL decoder. This decoder is reconfigurable based on parameters such as the code profile {B, T, Q}, the lifting size Z, the iteration count I, and more.

Notably, a key advantage of our ESRL decoders is their rate flexibility, supporting a wide range of code rates from 0.87 to 0.34. This renders both the throughput and the area efficiency of the decoder dynamic.

V. CONCLUSION
In this work, we present a fully reconfigurable SC-LDPC decoder architecture for ESRL codes. Based on SLME decoding, the ESRL decoder can be decomposed into multiple individual sub-decoders running in parallel, leading to rate flexibility like classical LDPC block decoders and large parallelism. This hardware architecture can decode virtually any SC-LDPC code that fits in the allocated memories. Thanks to the regular routing for underlying short block lengths, our ESRL decoder with a partial row-parallel architecture can achieve high throughput across variable code rates. Implementation results demonstrate that ESRL codes outperform 5G-NR LDPC codes by around 1 dB under the same computational complexity and the corresponding decoder can work on a throughput of hundreds of Gbps, making ESRL codes a promising solution for 6G wireless systems.
