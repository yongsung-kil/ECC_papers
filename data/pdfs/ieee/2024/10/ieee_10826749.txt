Abstract—This paper presents a computer-aided search scheme based on simulated annealing methodology for regular quasicyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes, ensuring a minimum girth of at least 8. The approach utilizes the simulated annealing algorithm, leveraging the correlation between cycles and trapping sets, and analyzes their positioning in the Tanner graph to iteratively reduce trapping sets. This process ultimately results in the generation of high-girth QC-LDPC codes. A significant advantage of our method is that it greatly reduces the search time compared to existing approaches. Furthermore, our method generates QC-LDPC codes that demonstrate a diminution in the number of 8-cycles, concurrently achieving a reduction in detrimental trapping sets. Simulation results reveal that our method generates QC-LDPC codes with excellent bit error rate performance.
Index Terms—Girth, Low-density Parity Check (LDPC) codes, Quasi-cyclic (QC), Simulated annealing, Trapping sets
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, a type of linear error-correcting code originally introduced by Gallager [1], have garnered considerable attention for their exceptional error rate performance. In the domain of 5G communication, LDPC codes occupy a pivotal position as a channel coding scheme, primarily because of their renowned error correction capability and efficient decoding algorithms. LDPC codes are considered an essential component for the future wireless communication systems, given their ability to maintain communication quality at high data transmission speeds and reduced latency. These codes, being very close to the Shannon limit and easy to implement in hardware, are widely used in various communication systems, including digital, satellite, and wireless communications [2].
Quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes have gained increasing popularity among researchers owing to their capability to offer high throughput and low-complexity encoding and decoding [3], [4]. Specifically, QC-LDPC codes

rooted in Circulant Permutation Matrices (CPM) have attracted considerable attentions, largely because of their simplified analytical framework as compared to random LDPC codes [5], [6]. By leveraging linear feedback shift registers, QCLDPC codes emerge as a viable and promising solution across a range of applications. The performance of QC-LDPC codes is influenced by various factors, and numerous studies have focused on key aspects of the Tanner graph, including the girth value and the number of cycles [7]–[9]. Additionally, some studies have aimed to directly reduce the number of trapping sets [10]–[12], subsets of variable nodes that can cause decoding failures.
Previous methods have achieved improvements in minimal circulant size using simulated annealing (SA) [13]. In this paper, we propose an SA algorithm to construct QC-LDPC codes that exhibit improved trapping set distributions, thereby reducing the number of cycles and trapping sets, and ultimately achieving better performance. This method aims to optimize the coding structure, reduce the complexity of QC-LDPC code construction. At the same time, it mitigates the impact of short cycles and harmful trapping sets, improves the overall error correction ability of QC-LDPC codes.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section II introduces the basic concepts related to QC-LDPC codes and outlines the fundamental concept of trapping sets. Section III provides an overview of the construction of QC-LDPC codes using the SA algorithm. Section IV presents the experimental simulation results and performance comparisons. Finally, Section V concludes the paper, summarizing the main findings and contributions of this work.

B. Trapping Sets
Trapping sets are also one of the important factors affecting the performance of LDPC codes. In iterative decoding on the Binary Symmetric Channel (BSC), let W (s) represent the collection of variable nodes that hinder convergence during the decoding process. In the event that W (s) is not empty, it is designated as (a, b) type trapping sets. Here, “a” signifies the number of variable nodes encompassed by the trapping sets, while “b” signifies the number of check nodes possessing an odd degree within these trapping sets in the Tanner graph. Typically, trapping sets containing fewer variable nodes and odd-degree check nodes are deemed as small trapping sets. These small trapping sets exert a more profound influence on the code’s error rate performance.
The Tanner graph [14] of a trapping set pertains to a subgraph consisting of all nodes present within the trapping set and their corresponding connections. This subgraph specifically includes the variable nodes belonging to the trapping set, often illustrated as black circles. The nodes that are directly linked to these variable nodes are the check nodes, denoted by black squares for odd-degree nodes and white squares for even-degree nodes within the graph.

Concurrently, (6,0), (6,2), as well as partial (7,3) and (8,4) trapping sets are evolved from the (5,3) trapping set. They all involve different combinations of cycles. III. CONSTRUCTING QC-LDPC CODES USING SIMULATED ANNEALING
The construction method of the entire matrix can be summarized as iteratively proceeding from the second column to the last column, and performing specific operations on each element starting from the second row up to the last row in each of these columns. Constant zero-valued elements, such as pi,0 = p0,j, ∀i, j are excluded from interactions. For each column element, the simulated annealing algorithm is employed to identify the presence of trapping sets linked to cycles, enumerate them, and use this count as the objective function to eliminate specific trapping sets. The algorithm halts when further cost reduction becomes impossible by altering any individual value within the exponent matrix.
To detect cycles containing a specific variable node, such as node V, the search can be initiated from V and extended in both directions, horizontally as well as vertically, carefully noting the traversed variable and check nodes. The reappearance of node V during this traversal indicates its inclusion in a cycle. Following this, all paths that pass through and return to node V can be identified; these paths represent the complete set of cycle structures encompassing node V.
During the comprehensive search for all cycle configurations, it has been observed that certain cycles may span a

considerable number of information nodes, potentially encompassing the entire nodal network. To alleviate the complexity and workload of the cycle search procedure, nodes that have already undergone examination can be excluded from further consideration during the focused investigation of a specific node. This methodological approach effectively reduces the inherent challenges and intricacies of the process. Additionally, this strategic method facilitates a more streamlined identification of critical cycle structures, ultimately eliminating duplicative efforts and enabling a more efficient analysis and mitigation of trapping sets within error-correcting codes. By adopting this refined approach, the overall efficiency and accuracy of the cycle search process are enhanced.
In our proposed algorithm, we first ascertain the shift values for each vertex in the l − 1 column. Subsequently, we pinpoint the non-compliant cyclic shift values for each vertex in the succeeding column, using equation (4) as our guide. This methodical approach enables us to efficiently configure the QCLDPC code’s parity-check matrix, optimizing its performance by minimizing the presence of short cycles that can detract from the code’s error-correcting capabilities.
Given the cyclic structure characteristics of the QC-LDPC code, we can determine the shift values of cyclic sub-matrices column by column, paying attention only to the cyclic relationships between the current column and the previous columns. By eliminating shift values that meet the criteria of equation (4), we have assembled a set of candidate cyclic shift values. Furthermore, selecting combinations of cyclic shift values that violate equation (4) holds the potential to eliminate short cycles of length 2l. Importantly, any value chosen from this set has the potential to disrupt the cyclic connections between the current column and the preceding columns. In the search process, to prevent entrapment in local optimal solutions, the SA algorithm is employed to facilitate convergence towards the global optimal solution. The SA algorithm accepts a new solution based on the gain in the objective value. If the new solution positively contributes, it is accepted. Otherwise, Metropolis’ [18] criterion based on Boltzmann’s probability is applied. The acceptance probability P r is determined by equation (5):

P r = e−∆E/(kT ),

(5)

where ∆E represents the difference in objective function values between the current solution and the candidate solution, k is the Boltzmann constant, and T denotes the temperature.

Typically, nodes containing trapping sets exhibit decreased message reliability, thereby significantly impacting LDPC code performance. To enhance message reliability, we employ Tanner graph detection to eliminate trapping set structures. A smaller number of trapping sets around a node corresponds to a longer girth, indicating higher reliability in the node’s messages. Utilizing the proposed algorithm, we are able to obtain QC-LDPC codes with a minimum girth of at least 8. However, it is important to note that the algorithm faces considerable computational complexity, making it more suitable for scenarios involving smaller exponent matrices and avoidance of short girth instances.

The notable advantages of our proposed QC-LDPC code construction technique, particularly in terms of construction time, are underscored when compared to traditional QC-LDPC code construction methods. We have benchmarked our SA approach against other algorithms. It is worth noting that the SA method exhibits superior search speed and performance, enabling the generation of well-performing QC-LDPC codes even at shorter code lengths. By increasing the length of the exponent matrix of QC-LDPC codes, we are able to obtain relatively short QCLDPC codes that satisfy the desired performance criteria.

V. CONCLUSION
Based on the correlation between cycles and trapping sets, this paper proposes an effective method for constructing QCLDPC codes. The method optimizes the cycle structure and reduces the complexity of constructing QC-LDPC codes. The introduction of the SA algorithm plays a crucial role in effectively reducing trapping sets. This improvement mainly results from the fact that the SA algorithm successfully reduces the trapping sets in the Tanner graph. The simulation results further verify the effectiveness of this algorithm in improving error performance.
