Low-Latency High-Throughput Decoder of QC-LDPC Codes by Matrix Splitting

Abstract—As the continuously increasing demand of high data rate and lower transmission, many modern communication systems employ the LDPC coding scheme with ultra-high throughput decoder to achieve these challenging requirements. However, due to the implementation complexity of decoding algorithm, designing an ultra-high throughput decoder of LDPC codes still suffers many difficulties. Therefore, we propose a FPGAbased LDPC decoder with partial parallel decoding, which splits and virtually supplements the parity-check matrix to achieve higher throughput and lower latency. Our proposed FPGA-based decoder can achieve a throughput of 6.072Gbps in 10 iterations for the highest rate 5G-LDPC code.
Index Terms—high-throughput LDPC decoder, FPGA, partially parallel decoding, NMS algorithm, matrix splitting.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-Density parity-check (LDPC) codes were first introduced by Dr. Gallager in 1962 [1] and rediscovered by MacKay and others in 1996 [2]. They are a type of errorcorrecting codes (ECC) that can approach the Shannon limit. LDPC codes offer several advantages including inherent parallelism, lower hardware complexity, high throughput, and no error floor at high signal-to-noise ratios. LDPC codes are currently used in many digital communication systems such as WiMAX (802.16e) [3], WiFi (802.11n) [4], DVB-S2 [5], and 5G NR.
LDPC codes have various decoding algorithms, and the belief-propagation (BP) algorithm is the classic one of them [6]. However, the drawback of the BP algorithm is its high complexity, making hardware implementation for higher throughput more challenging. To address this, a simplified algorithm called the min sum (MS) algorithm was proposed [7]. To mitigate this performance loss caused by MS simplification, some modified algorithms like the offset min sum (OMS) and normalized min sum (NMS) were introduced [8]. These modified algorithms successfully improve the performance of the original MS algorithm, striking a better balance between algorithm complexity and performance.
With the increasing demand for higher data rate and lower delay in transmission applications, addressing the demand for ultra-high throughput and lower delay, several studies have proposed innovative decoder architectures. For instance, [9] introduces a flexible high throughput LDPC decoder that resolves pipeline conflicts efficiently and utilizes hardware resources effectively. This architecture incorporates both layered and flooding schedules to avoid stall cycles caused by pipeline

hazards, achieving a throughput of up to 4.92 Gbps. Additionally, [10] demonstrates the high-throughput and low-latency capabilities of GPU-based LDPC decoding for 5G and beyond. By sufficiently exploiting the parallel computing power of GPUs, this decoder achieves nearly 4 Gbps throughput on a Titan RTX GPU, providing a high-performance softwarebased solution suitable for virtualized radio access networks (vRAN).
Compared to recent state-of-the-art solutions, this FPGA prototype achieves a higher processing rate per hardware resource for most configurations. The architecture proposed in [11] targets FPGA devices, offering a highly parallel and flexible decoder for 5G-LDPC codes. This design supports frame parallelism and efficient data scheduling, significantly improving processing rates. [12] presents an FPGA-based LDPC accelerator integrated with a complete software platform, achieving over 1.6 Gbps decoding throughput. It is noteworthy that the decoding implementations mentioned above all focus on quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes, which have been gaining popularity in emerging communication standards. For QC-LDPC codes, a synchronous partially parallel (SPP) decoder structure has been proposed [13]. In this decoder, the check nodes and variable nodes simultaneously process data in parallel, allowing newly generated soft information to be used earlier in the iteration process, thus speeding up convergence.

B. SPP NMS decoding and FPGA Implementation

Considering the FPGA implementation, we use a synchronous partially parallel (SPP) decoding architecture based on NMS algorithm. After initializing qmn [k] and Qn, updating the C2V and V 2C information occupies most of the decoding time. The decoder needs to perform mb × Z C2V calculations and nb × Z V 2C calculations. The SPP NMS decoding leverages the block-structure of QC-LDPC codes, allowing parallel calculation of C2V and V 2C information within blocks mb and nb times, respectively, while using a pipeline structure to handle Z times serially in each block. According to the NMS algorithm, when C2V information is updaing, V 2C information cannot be accessed, and vice versa, leading to low efficient utilization of hardware resource. The SSP NMS decoding can significantly improves hardware utilization by updating C2V and V 2C information simultaneously, achieving lower decoding delay and higher throughput.
In this algorithm, one iteration is divided into Z clock cycles. During the Z clock cycles of the k-th iteration, mb check-node processing units (CNPUs) and nb variable-node processing units (VNPUs) work simultaneously to update C2V and V 2C information, respectively. Define rmn [k, b] as the C2V information starting in the b-th clock cycle of the k-th iteration and qmn [k, b] as the V 2C information starting in the same cycle. III. MATRIX SPLITTING OF LDPC DECODER

The decoder used in this design has a structure that is

serial within each block and parallel between different blocks.

Therefore, the data in each block is not read simultaneously,

and each block can be accessed using RAM to store its data.

Since each block of the matrix is a shifted unit matrix, RAM

addressing becomes simpler, and data can be stored directly

in the order of the shift values.

As the numbers of rows and columns in the base-matrix are

fixed, increasing the value of Z leads to longer blocklength

of codeword and larger decoding delay. However, due to the

fact that the single iteration time of this decoder is also

proportion to the value of Z, increasing Z does not lead

to higher throughput but larger decoding delay. To increase

throughput and reduce delay, a straightforward approach is

to transform the serial processing within blocks into partial

parallel processing. Since the R/Q-RAM can only read one

data item at a time, it becomes necessary to split the data

and store it in different R/Q-RAMs with smaller size. This

process could potentially disrupt the cyclic shifting structure

based on CPMs and affect the mapping relationships in the

NMS algorithm.

To ensure that the algorithm structure remains unchanged,

this paper proposes a method called matrix splitting. The basematrix can be splitted into s2 small matrices. The splitted matrix can also be viewed as a matrix divided into s2 blocks,

allowing the original serial schedule inner blocks and parallel

process inter blocks in the decoder architecture to be retained.

IV. IMPLEMENTATION AND DISCUSSIONS
The FPGA used in this design is the Virtex-7 series VC709 development board, with the model being Virtex7, XC7VX690T-2FFG1761C. The decoder employs a 6-bit quantization method. Due to the matrix splitting, Z becomes 32, resulting in relatively small memory depths. Using Block RAM directly would waste resources, so Distributed RAM is partially employed in the design.

V. CONCLUSION
This paper presents an implementation approach for a high-throughput LDPC code decoder, realized on an FPGA platform. Unlike typical decoders based on the NMS algorithm, this decoder employs a synchronous iterative decoding method, achieving decoding speeds twice that of conventional algorithms. In practice, it attains better performance within the same iteration time. Additionally, to significantly enhance the decoder’s throughput without altering its architecture, matrix splitting and element padding methods were employed.This paper analyzes the specific implementation of the NMS synchronous high-throughput decoder on the VC709 board, and also provides an analysis of the decoding timing and resource consumption of different FPGA decoders. In the end, the decoder achieves a throughput of 6.072 Gbps at a clock frequency of 253MHz.
