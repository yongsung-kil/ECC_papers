On Joint Neural Min-Sum Decoding and Quantization Optimization
Vladimir Kuzurman, Dmitry Artemasov, Kirill Andreev, Alexey Frolov Center for Next Generation Wireless and IoT Skolkovo Institute of Science and Technology Moscow, Russia
{vladimir.kuzurman, d.artemasov, k.andreev, al.frolov}@skoltech.ru

Abstract—Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are integral to modern communication systems, including the 5G standard, which supports a new service type known as massive machine type communications (mMTC). mMTC terminals, typically battery-powered and designed for short message transmissions, may face performance challenges with LDPC codes, which excel at longer lengths but show worse performance at shorter ones. Advanced decoders have been developed to address performance issues. One of the approaches enhances the belief propagation (BP)-based decoders by introducing trainable parameters, resulting in the neural BP decoders. To mitigate the energy consumption caused by the decoding complexity, message quantization is also employed in practice. This paper addresses both issues by proposing the collaborative utilization of a neural min-sum decoder to enhance decoding performance and a neural quantizer to reduce computational and memory demands. We present a joint training strategy that achieves superior performance compared to conventional methods.
Index Terms—Low-density parity-check codes, channel decoding, deep learning, quantization
I. INTRODUCTION
The 5G standard is designed to support diverse use cases, categorized into three primary areas: enhanced mobile broadband (eMBB), ultra-reliable low-latency communications (URLLC), and massive machine-type communications (mMTC). mMTC targets the Internet of Things (IoT), focusing on massive connectivity. IoT devices typically transmit small amounts of data and must operate for a long period of time autonomously. Such scenarios require the use of short codes with robust error-correcting capabilities and low-complexity decoders to reduce devices’ power consumption. In the 5G standard the low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are used for the data plane [1].
The LDPC codes, which are linear error correcting codes, were firstly introduced by Gallager [2] in the 1960’s. LDPC codes provide great performance with large block lengths, however, their performance tends to degrade significantly at shorter lengths. The sum-product algorithm (SP) [3] allows the LDPC codes to approach the Shannon limit over the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel. Despite its

effectiveness, the SP algorithm is not suitable for hardware implementation due to its high computational complexity.
As the solution to this problem, the check-node operation approximation can be used to reduce the complexity. The minsum (MS) algorithm approximates SP and achieves significant complexity reduction with only slight performance degradation [4]. For further MS enhancement, modifications such as the normalized min-sum and offset min-sum algorithms can be employed. The normalized min-sum algorithm scales the check node output by a constant factor, while the offset min-sum algorithm improves performance by subtracting a small offset from the minimum values used in the checknode calculations. These modified algorithms improve the decoding efficiency and error correction performance, which makes them more suitable for practical implementations where short code length and low latency are critical.
The application of machine learning, especially neural networks (NN), in communication systems has shown promising results across various domains [5], [6]. In the multiple-input multiple-output (MIMO) systems, neural networks have been employed for channel estimation [7], [8], beamforming [9], and interference suppression [10]. In signal processing, neural networks are utilized for tasks such as denoising [11], equalization [12], and modulation recognition [13], [14]. In coding theory neural networks have been explored to improve the performance of various decoding algorithms [15].
In the context of BP-based algorithms, neural networks allow to improve the traditional message-passing techniques by augmenting the classical decoding algorithms with trainable parameters or even replacing parts of the decoder by the trainable functions. These approaches aim to achieve better error correction performance, particularly in scenarios with strict latency and reliability requirements, such as in 5G communication systems.
To train the BP-based decoders, the Tanner graph [16] is represented in an unfolded form. This approach enables the optimization of trainable parameters at each decoding iteration. By leveraging the strengths of deep learning along with the robustness and sparsity of the traditional min-sum algorithm, these decoders can provide enhanced performance in terms of error correction and computational efficiency.

This approach replaces traditional node message updates with trainable functions, allowing the neural network to learn a generalized message passing algorithm over the graph representing the forward error correction code structure. Unlike other deep learning-based decoding methods, this GNN-based decoder scales efficiently to arbitrary block lengths and avoids the curse of dimensionality.
While the abovementioned studies introduce innovative approaches, they also increase computational complexity and memory requirements. To address these issues, message quantization is often employed. The authors [19] propose a modified quantized min-sum decoding algorithm that applies an adaptive clipping threshold to the variable-node messages based on the number of unsatisfied checks per iteration. Numerical results indicate that the proposed algorithm significantly improves performance compared to the MS algorithm under two-bit precision and achieves performance close to SP algorithm for certain short-length LDPC codes.
In this paper, we address the mentioned issues of complexity and performance, with a focus on short codes for IoT applications. We start with the Tanner-graph-based neural network decoder [20], which expands the traditional belief propagation graph into a layered structure that can be viewed as a sparse neural network. In this structure, the connections in the neural network correspond directly to the connections in the parity check matrix, ensuring that the neural network maintains the original sparsity of the LDPC code’s Tanner graph. To optimize the decoder performance, we integrate trainable parameters to check and variable node update equations. To ensure a more stable convergence process we consider introducing residual connections with trainable weights to the variable nodes. To reduce the computational complexity and memory requirements, we imply quantization. To enable the training of the neural quantizer, the differentiable soft staircase function [21] is integrated into the neural network decoder. The soft staircase function is differentiable, allowing effective application of backpropagation and optimization of the quantization levels during the training. In this paper we propose to perform training of the neural decoder and quantizer jointly, to tune the quantization levels to the optimized decoder messages distributions. Such an integrated approach allows us to outperform the traditional methods described in the literature.

II. SYSTEM MODEL
Let us describe the system model. We assume that the user intends to transmit a k-bit information word u ∈ {0, 1}k. The system utilizes the binary linear block code C with length n and dimension k. The notations H and G represent the parity-check and generator matrices of the code C, respectively. The information word u is encoded into the codeword c = (c1, . . . , cn) = uG ∈ {0, 1}n. Binary phase-shift keying (BPSK) modulation is then applied, resulting in the following mapping

C. Training procedure
We train the neural decoder jointly with the soft staircase neural quantizer on the [600,120] 5G LDPC code with 40 punctured bits for 20 decoding iterations. All the trainable parameters of the decoder are initialized from the U(0.05, 0.1) distribution. These parameters include: ωj→i and ωic→h j as scaling factors for messages between check and variable nodes; ωi(→ l−jt) for residual connection weights; and βj→i, the offset parameters in the check node update equations.

Such initialization strategy produces a set of initial quantization levels in which each level has a slight deviation from the uniform distribution, and drawing initial values from the normal distribution allows the quantizer not to fall into the local minima. For our experiments, we set the hidden size of the quantizer to K = 16, as this has resulted in better performance.
We follow a two-step training strategy that has proven to be most effective. We start the training procedure with the batch size of 128 codewords and the learning rate of 10−3. Then, after convergence of the first training stage, we lower the batch size to 32 and the learning rate to 10−4. The gradual decrease of these parameters allowed us to precisely fine-tune the neural decoder.
The quantized neural MS decoder was trained on a zerocodeword in the presence of AWGN. We note that the decoder performance does not depend on the transmitted codeword, what allows us to generate the training data infinitely without the risk of overfitting. During the training, each batch contained codewords corrupted by different noise realizations, with signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) sampled from U(−5.5, −5.0) dB range, which corresponds to the expected FER range of [2 · 10−2, 8 · 10−3]. The binary cross entropy (BCE) loss was utilized for training. It has shown better convergence rate, than both mean squared error (MSE) and a combination of BCE and MSE losses.

V. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we have addressed the performance and decoding complexity issues of short LDPC codes. To improve de-

coding performance, we employed the neural min-sum decoder with trainable scaling and offset coefficients. The convergence rate of the neural MS decoder was further enhanced using trainable residual connections. To tackle the complexity issue, we implemented a neural quantizer. The overparameterization technique and the gradual annealing of the soft staircase function allowed us to precisely fit the quantization levels to the decoder messages’ distributions during the joint training procedure. The achieved results demonstrate the potential of the developed method.
As further research, we plan to extend the ideas of joint neural decoding and quantization functions training to the area of finite alphabet iterative decoders.
