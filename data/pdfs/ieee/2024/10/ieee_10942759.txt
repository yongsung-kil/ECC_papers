Abstract—The slowdown in semiconductor technology advances presents challenges for next-generation communication systems. These systems require continuous improvements in error correction performance, throughput, and energy efficiency. Although transistor density continues to follow Moore’s law, there has been a notable decline in gains of interconnect delay and energy consumption. This raises two research challenges. First, data transfers contribute increasingly to the energy consumption for advanced technology nodes. Second, bridging the increasing gap between technology capabilities and demands in communication systems necessitates cross-layer optimizations. In this work, we address both issues by investigating low-density paritycheck (LDPC) and polar code decoder implementations. We quantify the energy consumption impact of different data transfer structures in the Tanner graph for high-throughput LDPC code decoder. Furthermore, we quantify the gains from cross-layer optimization only and the gains from technology advances only for high-throughput polar code decoders and compare them.
Index Terms—ASIC Implementation, 22 nm FD-SOI, 12 nm FinFET, LDPC Codes, Polar Codes
I. INTRODUCTION
Advancements in silicon technology following Moore’s law [1] have enabled significant improvements in wireless communications. Between 1995 and 2020, transistor scaling reduced feature sizes by around three orders of magnitude [2]. However, recent trends indicate fewer gains with advances in technology nodes. While transistor density continues to increase, improvements in interconnect delay, and energy consumption have stagnated [3].
Modern communication systems demand high error correction performance, high throughput, and low energy consumption. New application scenarios in 6G systems, such as virtual reality and ultra-high-definition video streaming, will require peak data rates of up to 1 Tbit/s [4]. This imposes stringent constraints on the energy consumption of the forward error correction (FEC) decoder. For instance, thermal design power constraints result in an energy budget as low as 1 pJ/bit [5].
In this context, we discuss two research challenges. First, data transfers contribute increasingly to the energy consumption for advanced technology nodes [3]. This is especially relevant for high-throughput LDPC code decoders [6]. Introducing locality in such decoders reduces wire lengths and, thus, improves the energy consumption [7], [8]. However, this locality conflicts with the high entropy required to achieve

good error correction performance [9]. In this paper, we present a novel methodology to quantify the energy cost of data transfers in LDPC decoder implementations. Second, bridging the increasing gap between technology capabilities and demands in communication systems necessitates crosslayer optimizations [4]. Cross-layer optimizations address code design, decoding algorithms, and architecture jointly to improve implementation efficiency. However, distinguishing and quantifying the individual contributions of cross-layer optimizations and technology advancements is challenging. In this paper, we address this challenge by implementing polar code decoders [10] in different technology nodes to isolate and compare these gains achieved through cross-layer optimizations and those resulting from technology advancements.
LDPC decoders rely on the exchange of messages within their Tanner graphs [11]. Achieving good error correction performance requires an irregularly structured Tanner graph [12]. Thus, the nodes in the Tanner graph are connected by pseudorandomly distributed edges. By varying the degree of randomness in the code design, we adjust locality in the structure of the Tanner graph. This enables the quantification of the energy cost of data transfers by comparing LDPC code decoder implementations with different degrees of randomness.
Polar codes enable low-complexity decoding with high locality using successive cancellation (SC) decoding. However, the error correction performance of SC decoding is limited. Successive cancellation list (SCL) decoding improves the error correction performance at the cost of increased complexity. To reduce the implementation costs of SCL decoders, numerous cross-layer optimizations were proposed, e. g., [13]–[16]. To quantify and compare gains from cross-layer optimizations and technology scaling separately, we implement different polar code decoders in two different technology nodes.
Thus, the contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
• We quantify the energy cost as a function of the degree of randomness in Tanner graphs by designing seven different LDPC codes and implementing the corresponding fully node-parallel, iterative decoder architectures.
• We quantify the gains achieved by cross-layer optimization only and the gains achieved by technology advances only at a fixed error correction performance and throughput. For this purpose, we analyze five different high-throughput SCL-based polar code decoders, each implemented in a 12 nm and a 22 nm technology node.

II. BACKGROUND
This section provides the necessary background on the accessed KPIs, the system model, LDPC and polar codes with their decoding, and the technology nodes used for implementation analysis.

A. Key Performance Indicators
In this paper, we assess two categories of KPIs to evaluate the trade-offs between communication performance and implementation efficiency:
1) Communication KPIs: The error correction capability of the code and decoding algorithm is an essential property to determine the reliability of data transmission. Other important metrics include flexibility in code rate, codeword length (N ), and number of iterations (I), allowing the decoder to handle diverse communication scenarios effectively. We focus on the frame error rate (FER) over signal-to-noise-ratio (SNR), which is given as Eb/N0 in dB.
2) Implementation KPIs: Numerous metrics are provided to quantify the complexity of the decoder implementations, which are listed in Table I. The derived metrics area efficiency, energy consumption per bit, and power density reflect the most crucial KPIs for high-throughput decoding.

B. System Model
The system model for data transmission applied for error correction performance evaluations is shown in Fig. 1. On the sender side, the source signals are encoded and binary phase shift keying (BPSK) modulated before being transmitted over an additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel. On the receiver side, the signals are demodulated to log-likelihood ratio (LLR) values and decoded to restore the originally transmitted data. The focus of this paper is on FEC decoder implementations, highlighted in blue in Fig. 1.

Irregularity in the degree distributions of VNs and CNs improves error correction performance, particularly in the waterfall region [12], [18]. Systematic encoding explicitly embeds the information word u within the codeword. Quasi-cyclic construction [19] arranges H into circulant sub-matrices, enabling efficient decoder implementation.
Decoding an LDPC code by belief propagation (BP) decoding relies on iterative message-passing over its Tanner graph. To overcome error propagation, which limits the error correction performance of SC decoding, SCL decoding [26] considers a maximum of L parallel decoding paths resulting from path splitting, i. e., considering both values for each information bit. SCL decoding approaches maximum likelihood (ML) performance with a sufficiently large list size L. Concatenating the code with a cyclic redundancy check (CRC) assists the selection of the decoder output, and is denoted by CRC-aided (CA)-SCL decoding.
Code design for SC decoding focuses on the channel polarization. However, especially in the short to medium code length regime, other criteria are also important. Designing symmetric polar codes with a rich automorphism group [24] enables automorphism ensemble decoding (AED) [27] and successive cancellation automorphism list (SCAL) decoding [28], which were shown to outperform SCL decoding. Furthermore, the distance spectrum of the code can be improved by pre-coding, e. g., polarization-adjusted convolutional (PAC) codes [29] or row-merged polar codes [30], to enhance the ML performance, which can be exploited by SCL decoding. It is important to note that fast simplified successive-cancellation list (Fast-SSCL) decoding [15] with specific decoders for subcodes can be applied in all SCL-based decoding algorithms.
E. Implementation Design Flow
To assess the implementation efficiency of the presented decoder architectures, we use the Synopsys tools Design Compiler and IC Compiler for synthesis and placement and routing (PAR), respectively. The designs are implemented in two different technologies:
• a 12 nm fin field-effect transistor (FinFET) technology with a super-low threshold voltage transistor library under worst case process, voltage and temperature (PVT) conditions for timing (0.72 V, 125 °C), and nominal case PVT conditions for power (0.8 V, 25 °C).
• a 22 nm fully depleted silicon on insulator (FD-SOI) technology with a super-low threshold voltage transistor library under worst case PVT conditions for timing (0.72 V, 125 °C), and nominal case PVT conditions for power (0.8 V, 25 °C).
Values for power consumption are calculated from post-PAR netlists with back-annotated wiring data.
III. COST OF RANDOMNESS IN TANNER GRAPHS
In this section, we quantify the energy cost as a function of the randomness of LDPC decoders. We design codes with varying degrees of locality and implement corresponding decoders in a 12 nm and a 22 nm technology node. The analysis covers both error correction performance and implementation complexity.

A. Setup
This subsection provides an overview of the performed investigations to quantify the impact of the randomness in the Tanner graph. Specifically, the code design, decoding algorithm, decoder architecture, and decoder implementation constraints are introduced.
1) Codes: Let L = {L1, L2, L4, . . . , L64} be an ensemble of seven protograph-based, irregular, quasi-cyclic, systematic (1536, 1280) LDPC codes, which are derived from a 4 × 24 base graph with lifting factor Z = 64. Furthermore, let Bk be the permutation matrix and Hk be the parity-check matrix of Lk ∈ L for all k ∈ {1, 2, 4, . . . , 64}. The elements bi(,kj) of Bk are elements of a predefined integer set Ωk = {i ∈ [0..63] | i mod k = 0}. These elements are chosen using the progressive edge growth (PEG) [31], which minimizes cycles in the code. Consequently, B1 may contain all integers in [0..63], B2 only integers in [0..63] that are divisible by two, B4 only integers in [0..63] that are divisible by four, and so on until B64, which may only contain zeros. In this way, we constrain the set of values Ωk that the lifting heuristic can draw from such that

In contrast to area efficiency, energy consumption is significantly impacted by randomness in the Tanner graph. As randomness increases, the total power consumption increases by 1.65×, regardless of the technology. For the 12 nm designs, this results in an increased energy consumption from 6.20 pJ/bit to 10.20 pJ/bit (see Fig. 6, red line). Consequently, power density increases from 1.42 W/mm2 to 2.43 W/mm2.
A detailed analysis of energy consumption is illustrated in Fig. 6 using bar charts. This shows that the components belonging to registers (Ereg), clock network (Eclk), and leakage (Eleak) are not influenced by the structure of the LDPC code. However, the energy consumption of the combinational logic (Ecomb) increases for increased randomness. In the 12 nm technology node, Ecomb is increased by a factor of 2.18 from L64 to L1. These results quantify the energy cost of randomness, allowing for the first time a quantitative trade-off between energy consumption and error correction performance.
As shown in Table II and Fig. 6, the area efficiency improves

by a factor of 1.7 when moving from a 22 nm to a 12 nm technology node due to increased transistor density. However, the improvements in energy consumption are negligible, as transistor power savings are counterbalanced by increased interconnect power. Together, this leads to an increase in power density in advanced technology nodes.
IV. CROSS-LAYER OPTIMIZATION VS. TECHNOLOGY ADVANCES
This section compares the gains achieved solely by cross-layer optimizations with those provided by technology progress only. For this purpose, the evolution of five SCLbased polar code decoders are investigated:
(a) a CA-Fast-SSCL-8 decoder as baseline [32], (b) Rate-1 node optimizations added to (a) [33], (c) SPC node optimizations added to (b) [34], (d) symmetric code design enabling SCAL-4 decoding in-
cluding the optimizations from (c) [28], (e) row-merged code with SCL-3 decoding including the
optimizations from (c) [30].
For a comprehensive description of each optimization step, we refer the reader to the respective literature. The following subsections describe the applied cross-layer methodology, and discuss the presented results.

V. CONCLUSION
This work demonstrates that randomness in Tanner graphs
of LDPC codes significantly affects energy consumption.
Furthermore, we have shown that cross-layer optimizations for
polar code decoders yield far greater gains in area efficiency
and energy consumption than those achieved through technol-
ogy scaling alone. As advances in semiconductor technology
slow down, implementation-aware code design and cross-
layer optimizations are essential for future communication
systems. However, additional power reduction strategies will
be required to meet stringent implementation constraints,
especially as power density is an increasingly dominant factor
in advanced technology nodes.
