Scalable High-Throughput and Low-Latency DVB-S2(x) LDPC Decoders on SIMD Devices
BERTRAND LE GAL
IRISA/Inria Lab., UMR CNRS 6074, University of Rennes, 35042 Rennes, France CORRESPONDING AUTHOR: B. LE GAL (e-mail: bertrand.le-gal@irisa.fr)
ABSTRACT Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are error correction codes (ECC) with near Shannon correction performances limit boosting the reliability of digital communication systems using them. Their efficiency goes hand in hand with their high computational complexity resulting in a computational bottleneck in physical layer processing. Solutions based on multicore and many-core architectures have been proposed to support the development of software-defined radio and virtualized radio access networks (vRANs). Many studies focused on the efficient parallelization of LDPC decoding algorithms. In this study, we propose an efficient SIMD parallelization strategy for DVB-S2(x) LDPC codes. It achieves throughputs from 7 Gbps to 12 Gbps on an INTEL Xeon Gold target when 10 layered decoding iterations are executed. Simultaneously, the latencies are lower than 400 μs. These performances are equivalent to FPGA-based solutions and overclass CPU and GPU related works by factors up to 5×.

I. INTRODUCTION
A FTER decades of dominance, specialized circuits such as ASICs and FPGAs are gradually being replaced by CPUs and GPUs in digital communication systems, owing to the flexibility offered by the latter. The use of purely software-based processing solutions also allows offloading and centralizing physical layer processing in data centers, to address scalability and security challenges [1], [2], [3]. However, these strategies face performance issues in terms of throughput and latency in communication systems. Despite the replication of computing units in multicore and manycore architectures, achieving performance levels equivalent to those obtained using ASIC/FPGA solutions remains a challenge for software designers. It is necessary to consider the choice of algorithms and their parallelization strategies in order to match the architectural capabilities of the device.
Accordingly, numerous research efforts have focused on the efficient implementation of error-correcting code decoding algorithms in programmable architectures. Several code families have been investigated: LDPC [4], [5], NB-LDPC [6], [7], polar codes [8], [9], [10], turbocodes [11], [12], and even Viterbi codes [13]. Recent works on the decoding of LDPC codes have been carried out in the

context of 5G [5], [14], [15], [16], and terrestrial wireless communications [17], [18]. Indeed, the decoding of LDPC codes represents a significant part of the computational complexity of the physical layer, and is generally considered a bottleneck from a system performance point of view. The proposed solutions exploit the properties of LDPC codes to achieve throughputs exceeding 1 Gbps on CPU or GPU targets [5], [14], [18], [19], [20]. Existing strategies cannot be directly applied to satellite communication standards such as DVB-S2(x). However, while the need for performant LDPC implementations has been revived by the demand for high-speed optical and spacial communications [21], [22]. This results from the specific features of the DVB codes to be processed (double-diagonal and irregular degrees). Although generic parallelization strategies are still applicable, they offer (a) high processing latencies [23], (b) low-throughput [24], [25] and (c) do not scale with current programmable architectures [20], [26].

The usual approach to decode LDPC codes is to use message-passing algorithms [30]. The most efficient decoding algorithm is the Sum Product Algorithm (SPA), which is typically used to evaluate the H’ matrix performance when designing them. However, the use of transcendental operations has driven researchers to propose approximations to reduce the computational complexity and allow designing energy- and silicon-efficient real-time decoders. The most widely used decoding algorithm is the MinSum (MS) algorithm [48] or one of its variants (OMS, NMS) [49]. The algorithmic formulation initially used for hardware architecture design was based on a flooding scheduling of computations, that exhibited a high degree of computational parallelism. It is also used in most software implementations of LDPC decoders on CPU and GPU targets [4].
Other formulations that accelerate the convergence speed of the decoding process, halving the number of required decoding iterations were proposed in [50]. Speed improvements were obtained at the expense of the computational parallelism limitations. In the context of QC-LDPC codes, formulations such as horizontal-layered-based (HLS) enables better control of hardware complexity with a memory footprint reduction. The formulation of the MS algorithm using HLS is presented in Algorithm 1. The computation intensive section of this algorithmic formulation (Kernel 2) consists of 3 levels of nested loops. The first level (Kernel 2) ensures that the decoder performs iter_max decoding iterations if no valid codeword is found. For each decoding iteration, Kernel 3 traverses a set of parity nodes (CN). Kernel 4.1 updates the internal state of the selected parity node on the fly. It computes the CN → VN messages (Lnm) of the dc(m) VNn neighbors to CNm from the VN eigenvalues and the previous CN → VN messages. Using these Lnm messages, the CNm parity is computed and the (min1, min2) internal values are updated. Finally, Kernel 4.2 generates CN → VN messages (Lmn) based on the CN state CN → VN messages (Lnm). Kernel 4.2 also directly updates the En values associated with the VN nodes.
The use of QC-LDPC codes from the hardware and software perspective enables the parallel execution of Kernel 3. The structure of the matrix ensures that Z CNs can be processed in parallel without data dependency. A considerable amount of work has been conducted on the implementation of this decoding algorithm on CPU and GPU targets [4], [20], [51]. However, not all QC-LDPC code families have a pure QC structure composed of single-identity matrices. For instance, DVB-S2x codes include a one-bit punctured Z × Z sub-matrix, whereas the DVB-S2 standard also includes double-to-quadruple Z × Z sub-matrices. These particular LDPC codes make it impossible to directly use the effective HLS algorithm proposed for the CPU/GPU decoding of QC-LDPC codes. Indeed, studies reported in the literature mainly use flooding formulations for GPU devices [24], [25] and/or layered formulations that exploit inter-frame parallelism [23], [26]. This latter parallelization strategy decodes several frames in parallel to achieve regular computational parallelism at the cost of a sharp increase (16× → 64×) in memory footprints. In the case of DVB-S2(x) codes with large frame sizes, this leads to rapid congestion of memory bandwidth, limiting solution scalability. This prevents such decoders from achieving the high throughput (≥ 10 Gbps) required for current optical satellite applications [52]. In addition, the parallel processing of tens or hundreds of frames involves latencies from several hundred milliseconds, and even up to seconds, which are often unacceptable. In the remainder of this paper, we present an approach dedicated to decoding DVB-S2(x) codes that

III. IMPLEMENTATION STRATEGY A. MULTICORE CPU OVERVIEW The processing power of multicore architectures has increased significantly over the last few decades, owing to the introduction of several levels of parallelism. First, several physical cores (P) coexist in CPU devices to accelerate application execution. Depending on the nature of the ARM and INTEL platforms considered, the number of physical cores can vary from a few units (P ≈ 4) to several dozens (P = 24 for Intel Xeon Gold 6248R) up to several hundred (288 cores for future INTEL Xeon 6). Second, the physical cores incorporate vector computing units also called SIMD units. These SIMD units enable parallel execution of homogeneous computations on multiple data sets. The widths of these SIMD units generally range from 128 bits to 512 bits. Therefore, they can perform several tens of scalar arithmetic and logical operations per instruction. For example, for data coded on 8 bits, the preferred data format for decoding LDPC codes [4] on software, SIMD units of width {128, 256, 512} bits can process F = {16, 32, 64} data in parallel. Moreover, because current processors are superscalar architectures, execution frontends can carry out several SIMD instructions (load, store, arithmetic or logic) per clock cycle if the required functional units are available. To take full advantage of these different parallelism levels, applications must be tailored to expose task and computation parallelism. The development of frugal data structures that offer reduced and/or regular memory access is essential for GPUs [5], [51]. These transformations enable the C/C++ compilers to use SIMD units automatically. However, the explicit use of SIMD intrinsics adapted to different architectures (INTEL/ARM) may be necessary when patterns to be accelerated are complex.
To optimize the performance of software implementations, special attention must be paid to memory management (footprint, regularity, and number of accesses). Although the amount of memory in a multicore system can be substantial, it is crucial to use the cache hierarchy efficiently to minimize data access times. L1 and L2 caches, which offer fast access times ranging from a few clock cycles to a few dozen cycles, have modest sizes ranging from a few tens to hundreds of kilobytes. Therefore, it is essential to minimize the size of the data in memory, align them to benefit from prefetch policies, and reduce the amount of temporary data as much as possible.
In the rest of this section, we present the parallelization strategy we implemented and the various optimizations we applied to maximize performance.

B. MAPPING OF LDPC DECODING ON MULTICORE In this section, we presented an LDPC decoding parallelization strategy and the applied optimization techniques used to efficiently manage DVB-S2(x). As in other related works [4], we selected the Offset Min-Sum algorithm because it offers efficient tradeoff between correction performance and computational complexity. However, contrary to [24], [25], we selected horizontal-layered-based scheduling (Algorithm 1) to halve the number of decoding iterations and to reduce the memory footprint by ≈ 40% as in [20], [51]. In the same way, contrary to [20], [26], we process only a single frame to avoid memory footprint increases and thus memory bandwidth issues, as in [14], [27]. As demonstrated in the experimental section, this strategy scales better on multicore devices. However, contrary to the choices made in [27] and [14], in which dedicated unrolled C/C++ codes were generated for each small-size or medium-size LDPC matrix (N ≤ 2304), we propose a generic formulation for the CN processing kernel. Indeed, applying source code unrolling to DVB-S2(x) LDPC codes produces large binary decoder programs, that involves many instruction cache misses at runtime. The unusability of the computation unrolling technique is related to the fact that the H matrices are too large in the DVB-S2(x) standards (16200 ≤ N ≤ 64800).
To achieve high throughput on CPU and GPU targets, programmers should pay attention to SIMD VN read-andstore operations. These values are unaligned and require vector rotations to produce aligned vectors of Z data to allow SIMD computations. These rotations depend on the shift values specified in the H matrix. A large part of the SIMD VN accesses (load/store) could be seamlessly executed (at least Z/F − 2), reason why in [14], [27], source codes are unrolled. This dedicated strategy removes most VN load penalties, as implied by more generic approaches. Note that the same procedure is required after CN execution, to store the updated VN accumulators in memory.
We found a generic way of accessing Z VN data in an interleaved manner, while halving temporal penalties. To achieve this, the storage method of VN in the main memory is modified. When Z VNs are loaded into the software decoder, the N LLRs associated with the VN nodes are grouped in memory into blocks of Z VNs as shown in Figure 2 (in the example, Z = 28). These groups of Z data are stored in a memory array of size Z/F × F, where F is the number of 8-bit data values that can be processed using the SIMD unit (in the Figure, F = 6). The F − (Z mod F) empty memory elements between VN groups were padded with zero values. This memory placement, whose memory footprint overcost is limited to N × (F − (Z mod F)) bytes, will ease memory access at runtime.
First, to execute a rotated load operation, two SIMD loads in the registers should be combined because of mask operation (ex. blendv), using a mask value [27]. This procedure was applied even if a single SIMD load was sufficient as illustrated in Figure 2. Assuming a shift rotation equals to 17 in H for the Z VN elements, the filling of

The VN elements loaded into the SIMD registers correspond to En in Algorithm 1. These values are equivalent to the sum of the CN → VN message values of VNn. To produce a CN → VN message, the CN → VN contribution from the previous iteration must be removed. The Lmn values stored in message memory are aligned with the padding elements between groups (Figure 3). Because they do not require rotation at runtime, their accesses are done using a single SIMD memory-load operation. To avoid polluting the cache memory of multicore architectures, the memory space freed up by the Lmn message read is directly reused to store the Lnm values during the CN evaluation.
Once the computation of the CN node is completed, the Lnm values are read back from the message memory and added to Lmn to generate updated En values. Unlike previous studies [14], [27], these En values were directly copied back into the VN memory without applying rotation, as shown in Figure 3. This choice eliminates the need for loading/storing and masking operations. This dynamic En storage strategy limits the penalties associated with rotated memory accesses to the loading phase only. In parallel with En memory store, the message memory space freed up by Lnm data is reused to store new Lmn messages, again limiting the memory footprint and bandwidth usage.

C. DVB-S2(X) CODE MANAGEMENT As previously mentioned, the LDPC codes of the DVBS2 standard are special quasi-cyclic codes. First, unlike the matrices present in other recent standards, they have Z × Z sub-matrices composed of multiple identity matrices (2 → 4), as shown in Figure 4, with the Z × Z sub-matrix at the top right corner. This particular structure prevents direct use of the layered decoding algorithm parallelization scheme presented above. Indeed, it is impossible to update
the same set of Z VNs using two CN → VN contributions at a time without a complex set of load/store operations as VNs are not shifted identically. This explains why studies in the literature have decided to use the flooding algorithm for decoding DVB-S2 codes [24], [25] or a layered inter-frame approach [20], [23], [26]. The latter approach is equivalent to a sequential decoding of F frames.

To overcome this limitation, we applied a strategy similar to that reported in [28], [53] for the FPGA-based LDPC decoder design. The main idea consists of updating partially the VN nodes at the end of the CN evaluation: a single CN → VN message is considered whereas the other messages are frozen. For instance, let us consider the first set of Z CNs shown in Figure 4. Calculating the Z parity nodes noted C0 requires four CN → VN messages (Lnm) from V0, V2 and V4. To support this strategy, two main modifications were applied to the previously described decoder implementation:
• The data storage of the rotated VN elements is different for odd and even iterations. Indeed, in the previous example (Figure 5), during even iterations at the end of C0 execution, V4 values are stored in the memory rotated by the red diagonal offset because the blue contribution is frozen. At the opposite, at the end of C0 execution during odd iterations, the blue rotation offset is applied. Consequently, the H1 matrix should be decomposed into two matrices: H1(odd) and H1(even) for even and odd iterations, respectively.
• The number of generated CN → VN messages is not equal to the number of CN → VN messages for a CN with multiple-diagonal elements. Indeed, even if for a CN with multiple-diagonals the kernel 4.1 always computes dc(m) CN → VN messages, the second kernel (4.2) produces fewer CN → VN messages, keeping in memory previous iteration values. Consequently, dedicated functions were developed to execute these CN kernels that should discard at the end some CN → VN message updates.

C. RELATIVE PERFORMANCE LEVELS 1) COMPARISON WITH MULTICORE IMPLEMENTATIONS
To highlight the strengths of the proposed low-latency implementation, we also report the results for the interframe decoders published in [20] and [26]. Both decoder implementations use an inter-frame strategy (i.e., each core decodes F frames in parallel, with F equals SIMD width in byte) to obtain independent and regular computational parallelism at runtime. This strategy increases latency owing to data buffering and generates huge memory footprints that are not cache-friendly when long LDPC codes are processed. The open source availability of related work decoders enabled us to perform identical tests on P1 → P3 platforms. Note that Aff3ct LDPC decoders [26] only process 16 bits values, halving the amount of data processed by each SIMD instruction. Figure 8 reports and compares the throughput and the latency of different decoder implementations. The highest F value was selected for each setup.
The scatter plot associated with single-core experiments presented for the P1 and P2 platforms show that the intraframe decoders proposed in this study deliver throughputs that are equivalent to those of inter-frame decoders, despite the greater complexity of memory accesses (VN). These similar performance levels reflect the better use of L1 and L2 caches, as the memory footprint of intra-frame decoders is F× lower than that of inter-frame decoders. Thus, more complex memory accesses and slightly lower computational parallelism are compensated by a lower number of cache misses. Latency, meanwhile, is considerably shorter than that of inter-frame decoders. The average latency was divided by F. The findings for the multicore configurations were similar even for the P2 platform with higher decoding throughputs. Finally, gains on the P3 platform were significantly higher. Owing to the low memory footprint of the intra-frame approach, which enables the full advantage of F = 64 (AVX512), an increase in throughput by a factor of 5 was achieved in the multicore configuration. Indeed, in this use case, inter-frame implementations are limited by the external memory bandwidth.

3) COMPARISON WITH FPGA IMPLEMENTATIONS
The proposed decoders were also compared with hardware decoders designed for DVB-S2(x) codes [28], [53] to estimate the feature differences. Indeed, hardware decoders can be deployed on FPGA targets and included in SDR or CloudRAN communication systems [55], [58]. The hardware architectures detailed in [28] are semi-parallel architectures that execute also an HL-MS based decoding algorithm. The architecture includes 180 dedicated processing elements that efficiently execute CN computations in parallel with a working frequency of 250 MHz. The reported decoding throughput was approximately 1500 Mbps on INTEL Stratix-V FPGA devices. As expected, despite the difference in operating frequencies, the throughput of hardware architectures is higher than that of software decoders when a single-core is used. However, when multiple cores are activated, the throughputs of software solutions becomes more relevant at a higher flexibility level. Directly comparing energy performances of both solutions is not possible, because these data are not provided in [28]. However, the rate should lie between 50× to 100× considering the work reported in [52].
