WiFaKey: Generating Cryptographic Keys From Face in the Wild

Xingbo Dong , Member, IEEE, Hui Zhang , Graduate Student Member, IEEE, Yen Lung Lai , Zhe Jin , Member, IEEE, Junduan Huang , Member, IEEE, Wenxiong Kang , Member, IEEE,
and Andrew Beng Jin Teoh , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract— Deriving a unique cryptographic key from biometric measurements is a challenging task due to the existing noise gap between the biometric measurements and error correction coding. Additionally, privacy and security concerns arise as biometric measurements are inherently linked to the user. Bio-cryptosystems represent a key branch of solutions aimed at addressing these issues. However, many existing bio-cryptosystems rely on handcrafted feature extractors and error correction codes (ECC), often leading to performance degradation. To address these challenges and improve the reliability of biometric measurements, we propose a novel biometric cryptosystem (BC) named WiFaKey, for generating cryptographic keys from face in unconstrained settings. Specifically, WiFaKey first introduces an adaptive random masking-driven feature transformation pipeline, AdaMTrans. AdaMTrans effectively quantizes and binarizes real-valued features and incorporates an adaptive random masking scheme to align the bit error rate (BER) with error correction requirements, thereby mitigating the noise gap. Besides, WiFaKey incorporates a supervised learning-based neural decoding scheme called neural-MS decoder, which delivers a more robust error correction performance with less iteration than nonlearning decoders, thereby alleviating the performance degradation. We evaluated WiFaKey using widely adopted face feature extractors on six large unconstrained and two constrained datasets. I. INTRODUCTION
BIOMETRICS offer reliable identity authentication by measuring unique traits like fingerprints, facial features, iris patterns, voice, and gait, converting these into digital data for analysis and comparison [1], [2]. However, directly using biometric measurements as a cryptographic key presents several challenges: 1) the variability and error rates in biometric measurements, such as iris codes with an error rate of 10%–30% [3] and face features with an error rate of around 30% (see Section IV-C), and 2) the stringent privacy and security policies required by regulations like the general data protection regulation (GDPR) [4].
Among existing works [5], [6], [7], biometric cryptosystem (BC) [8] is a prominent instrument system devised to generate cryptography keys from biometric measurements. BC can be further classified into key generation and key binding, where key generation BC directly generates a key from the biometric measurements such as fuzzy extractors [9], and key binding BC binds a key to a biometric datum such as fuzzy commitments [10] and fuzzy vaults [11]. Helper or auxiliary data are also generated by the BC and stored in the database simultaneously. Cryptographically, there should be minimal information leakage about original biometric measurements and keys from the helper data, as retrieving the key or the original biometric measurements would be computationally difficult based on the helper data. Instrumentally, biometric measurements of the correct person are required before the key can be released or reconstructed in BC.
Fuzzy commitment [10] is a typical representative BC scheme used in biometric systems to store and retrieve biometric measurements securely, which has been deployed with a variety of biometric modalities, including iris [12], face [13], fingerprints [14], etc. It generates a codeword from an error correction code (ECC) and computes the difference between the biometric vector and the codeword, which is hashed and stored as a commitment. 1) Performance Degradation: The fuzzy commitment scheme, which is typically applied to binary biometric measurements such as IrisCode [17], necessitates the conversion of real-valued biometric features into binary vectors. This conversion process inherently involves a loss of information, resulting in degraded performance [18], [19]. Furthermore, most fuzzy commitment schemes are designed and evaluated using controlled biometric datasets, which assume cooperative authentication scenarios [5]. However, in unconstrained environments, where biometric measurements are subject to variations and noise, significant performance degradation is observed [20]. Thus, when the fuzzy commitment scheme is deployed in unconstrained scenarios, the primary issue becomes measurement-induced performance degradation.
2) Noise Gap: The gap between the biometric measurement noise level and the ECC’s error tolerance capability is a critical issue when using fuzzy commitment in biometrics. The ideal scenario would feature zero intraclass variation and maximum interclass variation in the extracted biometric features, ensuring precise and accurate measurements. In this perfect scenario, the ECC would provide 100% error correction capability for the same user despite any noise in the measurements, while achieving 0% error correction for different users. However, this level of performance is practically unattainable due to inherent noise and variability in biometric measurements. For instance, in the case of binarized face features, intraclass samples often exhibit an error rate of approximately 30% (see Section IV-C). Given a Bose–Chaudhuri–Hocquenghem (BCH) code with parameters (n, k, t), where n = 511, the maximum number of correctable error bits t is 121 bits, and the corresponding secret key length k is only 10 bits, resulting in low secrecy utility. Alternatively, the error rate can be artificially increased by interleaving the binary vector with a constant binary vector, but this significantly increases the processing cost due to the multiple encoding–decoding steps required, which is impractical for real-time biometric authentication systems.

Our research is pioneering in proposing a random masking scheme that adaptively reduces measurement noise in biocryptosystems. Additionally, we are the first to integrate a supervised learning-based 5G NR LDPC min-sum (MS) neural decoding scheme into a bio-cryptosystem on unconstrained face datasets. Consequently, we tackled the performance degradation and noise gap problems from two directions by reducing the noise level in biometric measurements and improving the neural decoder of the LDPC. The proposed method can address the challenge and ultimately achieve a successful solution effectively. The main contributions of this article are as follows.
1) We propose a new fuzzy commitment BC system, named WiFaKey. The proposed fuzzy commitment system can handle unconstrained facial biometric measurements, which is more practical for real-world scenarios.
2) A pipeline called AdaMTrans is proposed for transforming features into binary bits suitable for LDPC coding. AdaMTrans utilizes quantization and binarization techniques, along with a random bit masking scheme, to effectively address the challenge of biometric intraclass variation while preserving LDPC error correction capabilities with minimal impact on accuracy.
3) A supervised learning-based 5G NR LDPC MS neural decoding scheme on the binary channel, namely neuralMS decoder, is proposed for the fuzzy commitment system. The neural-MS decoder improves error correction performance with less iteration than nonlearning decoders due to optimized parameters.
Our experiment simulates practical use cases with independent subjects (no identity overlaps the training and testing datasets) and fully validates the performance benefits of the proposed scheme. A. ECCs Enabled Fuzzy Commitment
1) Hadamard and Reed-Solomon ECC: An early application of fuzzy commitment to iris biometrics was demonstrated by Hao et al. [12] using a combination of two ECCs, Hadamard and Reed–Solomon (RS). For implementing the (64, 7) Hadamard ECC, which can correct at least 15 random bit errors and outputs a 7-bit word, the 2048-bit iris template was divided into 32 blocks of 64 bits each. The Reed–Solomon code (32, 20) was used to wipe out errors at the block level (burst), which can correct up to six incorrect 7-bit words. To obtain a 140-bit key, the 7-bit words used 32 input words to decode 20 output words. Rathgeb and Uhl [32] integrated a fuzzy commitment scheme into the iris recognition system and proposed a method to enhance accuracy. They employed Hadamard and Reed–Solomon codes for error correction and improved performance by optimizing the arrangement of iris codes based on global error distribution analysis.
2) Reed–Muller ECCs: The key recovery rate was found to drop drastically in [12] when tested on a challenging dataset. Bringer et al. [20] proposed an iterative soft decoding ECC as a combination of two Reed–Muller ECCs, (64, 7) and (32, 6), which significantly enhanced the accuracy of the fuzzy commitment scheme.
3) BCH Codes: In [33], BCH codes were employed to construct a fuzzy commitment scheme for face-ordered feature vectors. Nandakumar [34] applied a fuzzy commitment scheme with turbo codes for securing fingerprint minutiae patterns. Yang et al. [35] utilized a fuzzy commitment scheme

of biometric features into binary vectors (e.g., [14], [33], [36], [37]). This conversion can potentially lead to a loss of discriminability. In unconstrained settings, the variability within intraclass and interclass can be even more pronounced, underscoring the importance of robust error correction mechanisms [15], [16]. For instance, in face recognition, datasets such as face recognition grand challenge (FRGC) [39], FERET [31], PIE [30], and Extended Yale B [40] are commonly used in works like [18], [33], and [37]. As a result, fuzzy commitment schemes for unconstrained settings remain relatively underexplored.
Second, linear codes such as BCH, Hadamard, RS, LDPC, and their combinations have been extensively studied in the abovementioned literature. However, the error-correcting codes utilized in existing fuzzy commitment schemes for face recognition have not been optimized to their full potential (e.g., nonlearning LDPC in [14], [37], and [38]). Their error correction capability must be sufficiently robust to differentiate between intraclass and interclass variations [41]. Nevertheless, the noise gap, as discussed in Section I, often proves to be substantial. Leveraging known statistics about the differences in the considered biometrics can still prove invaluable in accommodating the high variability inherent in unconstrained scenarios.

The above task can be optimized along three dimensions, focusing on measurement accuracy and precision: 1) minimize the intraclass variations and maximize the interclass variations of the biometric features; 2) optimize the transformation function g(·) to preserve the discriminative information of the biometric features after binarization; and 3) optimize the error correction to achieve the highest error correction capability for intraclass and lower capability for interclass, ensuring reliable and accurate measurements for identity authentication. The first dimension has been well-explored, and tremendous feature extraction models have been proposed in the literature. However, the second dimension remains under-explored except for a few works such as [18] and [43]. These works mainly focus on transforming floating feature vectors into binary vectors, while the gap between BERs and ECC capability is overlooked. As for the optimization of the ECC, to our best knowledge, there is no existing work using a learning technique to optimize ECC for the bio-cryptosystem. This presents an opportunity for further research to enhance the precision and reliability of biometric measurements in cryptographic applications.
To bridge the gap between the BERs and ECC capability, we have designed a feature transformation function, AdaMTrans, and a learning-based neural-MS decoder on top of the widely used facial feature extractor. C. Learning-Based LDPC Neural Decoding
LDPC is a linear error correcting code [45], which can nearly reach the Shannon channel capacity and decode in linear time in the data length based on the iterative belief propagation (BP) algorithm. LDPC can be described with a sparse parity-check matrix H, which contains very few ones. The ones in each row from H specify which bits are associated in the corresponding parity equation. H can be used to create the generator matrix G, which encodes a message a with aG mod 2 based on matrix multiplication in modulo two arithmetic.

Except for the final layer (i.e., all the hidden layers), the remaining layers in the trellis are all of the size E, where E represents the number of edges in the Tanner graph. The hidden layer i corresponds to the ith iteration in the MS decoding process. Two sublayers, iv and ic, which stand for the updates of VN and CN, respectively, are included in each hidden layer. There are N neurons in the output layer. In the hidden layer i, sublayer iv outputs the message sent from the associated VN to CN along the edge, and sublayer ic outputs the message sent from the associated CN to VN along the edge (refer to [49] for more details).
The neural network is trained greedily in an iteration-byiteration manner to avoid the gradient vanishing issue [49]. Specifically, the network is optimized one layer at a time. Once a particular layer has been trained, its corresponding weights and biases are fixed for the remainder of the training process. This strategy ensures that the representations learned in earlier layers are preserved during the training of subsequent layers, thereby maintaining the integrity of the information propagated through the network. By doing so, the model avoids the gradual diminishment of gradients, which is a critical issue that arises when training deep networks using traditional backpropagation methods. In this greedy layer-wise training approach, the learning process is highly structured.

Training begins with a single-layer network, and as each layer completes its optimization phase, the network progressively evolves into a multilayer structure. This incremental growth ensures that each layer contributes effectively and consistently to the model’s learning process.
We adopt the base code BG2 defined in the 5G standard [50], which has 42 rows and 52 columns in the parity-check matrix H with 197 nonzero elements, and the baseline code rate of the BG2 codes is 0.2. However, the decoder is revised to be suitable for biometric measurements, making it distinct from [49] where the following holds.
1) The original neural-MS decoder is designed for the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel. In contrast, our decoder is designed to focus on the binary symmetric channel (BSC) for the binary operation in fuzzy commitment.
2) We adopt the lifting factor z = 10, therefore the code length is N = 52 × 10 and the message length is K = 10 × 10, which is appropriate for our features length.
The binary features b are divided into m [from (2)] blocks with a block size of 512. Each block is padded with eight zeros to fit the code length 520, and m codewords will be generated in the encoding process. In consequence, a key length of 100 × m can be achieved. In the implementation, the m subkeys are retrieved from m codewords and then concatenated to form the final key k. We follow the training details as discussed above to train the decoder greedily in an iteration-by-iteration manner.

Monte Carlo simulations statistically approximate the LDPC code’s performance metrics, such as the BER, under specific conditions like a given signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). The results can be averaged by conducting many simulations under varied conditions to yield a reliable estimate of the LDPC code’s error correction capability.

V. SECURITY ANALYSIS
The security risks considered in this article originate from compromising the stored commitment [14], [56]. Under this assumption, the adversary can try inverting the commitment back to the input face image. The successfully inverted face image can access the system as the corresponding enrolled subject. However, directly synthesizing an image based on a brute-force attack is infeasible due to the vast number of combinations of pixel values. Yet the adversary can invert the commitment to the image using learning-based reconstruction methods such as [62], [63], and [64]. These methods usually take the deep face features as the input and output reconstructed images. However, such reconstruction models cannot be learned directly because our proposed commitment depends on the input images x and the subject-specific random keys k. Therefore, the key k should be known first to achieve the reconstruction. However, the key in our scheme is secured using one-way hashing: the adversary needs to guess the key k by directly guessing k, which has a strength of 2100∗m, or guessing the intermediate feature b. Therefore, the security depends on the easier way of guessing k and b. We have formalized the following adversary model to provide a more rigorous security analysis.

A. Adversary Model
Given that fuzzy commitment schemes utilize ECC, it is essential to formally define our adversary model using information-theoretic measures, i.e., entropy, as defined by Dodis et al. [9]. We quantify the adversary advantage in terms of the leftover entropy, which captures the remaining randomness or uncertainty in the biometric feature b after the adversary has successfully decommitted the commitment δ. Since knowledge of b implies knowledge of k, this leftover entropy characterizes the randomness of the key that remains given that the adversary can recover the codeword c′ and find a collision such that c′ = c for successful decomitment of δ.
Within this model, we focus on BSCs, for our adversary model in LDPC decoding. This choice is motivated by three key reasons.
1) LDPC codes are highly efficient for BSCs because they can correct a high percentage of bit errors at low error rates, low decoding complexity, robustness against channel noise, and flexibility in design with various code rates. This provides significant advantages over other channel types and helps prevent overestimation of system security.
2) BSCs are a well-established model for communication channels that exhibit noise. They offer transparency when analyzing the impact of noise on the security of fuzzy commitment schemes using information-theoretic measures such as entropy and channel capacity.
3) BSCs are a valuable reference channel for comparing the security of different schemes, serving as a reliable benchmarking tool. Furthermore, a decoder that performs well on a BSC is likely to perform well in more complex and realistic channels, making BSCs crucial for evaluating the security of a system.
Based on the above reasons, it can be inferred that the adversary model utilized in our scheme is much more relaxed than the generic security model. This is because we impose more restrictive conditions on the noise in the biometric measurements, specifically, that the noise follows an independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) distribution. Furthermore, as the security of our scheme assumed the successful decoding and decommitment of δ, it is commonly believed that the decoding problem will be more complex and challenging in a generic channel.
B. Leftover Entropy Estimation

This is done by measuring entropy, which is key in designing and evaluating secure biometric systems. C. Unlinkability Evaluation In this section, we focus on the evaluation of unlinkability.
As we generate a random key for each user per enrollment, unlinkability, and cancellability can be naturally guaranteed. Once the stored commitment is compromised, a new instance can be generated using a different random key. Besides, using different keys for different enrollments ensures that no information will be leaked from a cross-matching attack, which attempts to infer any information by matching two commitments from two other applications.
We follow a protocol outlined by Gomez-Barrero et al. [65]. Using the neural-MS parameters, the experiments were executed with Magface and on the LFW, Agedb, and CFP datasets. The adversary can exploit the matching score distributions of the commitments to learn whether the commitment is from the same person. Here, we refer to the matching distance of the same subject in different applications as the mated distance. VI. DISCUSSION
A. Measurements Reliability
Key retrieval performance evaluates the system’s reliability on identity recognition in consistently retrieving the correct key from biometric features. High key retrieval performance indicates not only accuracy and but also the system’s dependability in generating the same key for the same input. To ensure reliable measurement, we assess the system across matched and nonmatched pairs from diverse datasets, gauging its consistency in performance under varying conditions. The experiments showed that, on average, 81.33% of samples were accurately reconstructed, surpassing SP by 0.4% and outperforming the counterpart MS decoder by 3.42%.
Security analysis involves reliability on security through the evaluation of leftover entropy and unlinkability. Leftover entropy measures the residual uncertainty in the keys after generation, ensuring the system’s robustness against attacks. Unlinkability, crucial for privacy, assesses the system’s ability to consistently prevent biometric data or keys from being linked to the same individual. Both metrics are evaluated to ensure the system’s reliability in maintaining security and protecting privacy across different scenarios. The analysis showed that our proposed scheme has achieved higher security bits and unlinkability measures.
B. Strengths and Limitations
The proposed WiFaKey achieved the reliable key measurements from two directions: 1) mitigate the impact of noise in the biometric original measurements by AdaMTrans and 2) improve the error correction capability by neural-MS decoder. Specifically:
1) Noise Mitigation: AdaMTrans is an adaptive random masking-driven feature transformation pipeline designed to quantize and binarize real-valued biometric features. This method enhances accuracy and reliability by transforming continuous feature values into a binary form, which is more resilient to variations and noise in biometric data. The transformation process ensures that noise from the features is consistently mitigated to the level LDPC can correct, which improves the reliability of feature comparison and matching. Therefore, AdaMTrans contributes to the observed performance boost.

2) Enhanced Error Correction: By integrating a deep face feature extractor with a neural-MS decoder, WiFaKey improves error correction capabilities. The neural-MS decoder is a supervised learning-based neural decoding scheme that learns from data to adjust its decoding strategy adaptively, providing robust mechanisms for processing noisy biometric signals. This capability is crucial for enhancing the accuracy of key retrieval and verification by effectively correcting errors introduced during biometric data acquisition and transformation.
In addition, the proposed system is designed with practical applications in mind for high utility, including financial services, Internet of Things (IoT) services, and public services. Its enhanced performance and robustness make it suitable for these critical domains.
Despite the strengths mentioned above, the proposed methodology has limitations. The key generation process is susceptible to variations in pose, hairstyle, lighting, and makeup variations. These factors introduce significant noise, which may impact the robustness of the generated cryptographic keys. Additionally, further research is crucial to quantifying the noise inherent in biometric measurements. Understanding the extent of this noise is essential to improving key retrieval performance and system reliability.
C. Future Works
Future efforts will enhance the feature extraction and transformation pipeline to effectively manage variations and noise in biometric measurements. Additionally, there will be a focus on developing new error correction algorithms to improve key retrieval accuracy and minimize FMRs. Another avenue of exploration will be integrating facial biometrics with other modalities, such as fingerprints and palmprints, further to strengthen the robustness and reliability of cryptographic key generation.
VII. CONCLUSION
This article presents WiFaKey, a novel fuzzy commitment scheme that combines a deep face feature extractor with a learning-based LDPC decoder. The integration of AdaMTrans for feature transformation further enhances the system’s capability to produce binary features suitable for LDPC coding. Our experiments demonstrate that WiFaKey outperforms existing methods on unconstrained face datasets, proving its effectiveness for various applications.
