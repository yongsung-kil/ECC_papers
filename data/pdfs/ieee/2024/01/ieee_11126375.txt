CellRejuvo: Rescuing the Aging of 3D NAND Flash Cells with
Dense-Sparse Cell Reprogramming

ABSTRACT
3D NAND flash memory is one of the most important storage tech-
nologies in modern computer systems because of its non-volatile
nature and excellent data access performance. However, it suffers
from aging and reliability issues due to its inherent property. In
contrast to the previous research that tried to recover the data with
additional encoding techniques, we propose a novel reprogramming
technique, called CellRejuvo, to improve the reliability of NAND
flash cells. To the best of our knowledge, CellRejuvo is the pioneer
for data recovery technique that cleverly leverages reprogramming
to alleviate cell aging, extending the lifespan of solid-state drives.
We implement CellRejuvo on a real 3D NAND flash-based SSD and
evaluate its capability on various realistic workloads. The extensive
experimental results show that CellRejuvo successfully reduces the
error rate of SSD by an average of 38.28% under various retention
times.

INTRODUCTION
3D NAND flash has gained an increasingly high position among
mainstream storage media due to its high performance, shock re-
sistance, and large storage capacity [15, 22, 27]. However, to meet
the growing demand for storage capacity, some trade-offs have to
be made. For example, the original NAND flash could store only
one bit per cell, but as the technology advanced, the number of
bits per cell increased. The flash architecture evolved from SLC to
MLC, then to TLC, and even QLC devices, which can store 4 bits
per cell. Although storing multiple bits per cell can provide larger

storage space, it reduces the margins between different states. The
shortened margins make the NAND flash become less tolerant of
the error effects caused by charge loss in NAND flash cells; in other
words, the reliability of NAND flash decreases.
To ensure data reliability of flash memory, there are two main
kinds of approaches for data recovery. Firstly, Error Correction
Code (ECC) can detect and correct a certain number of error bits
within a threshold of 120 error bits per 1K code word. The im-
plementation of ECC contains Bose–Chaudhuri–Hocquenghem
(BCH) and Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) encoding [10, 11].
BCH employs algebraic encoding to correct a fixed number of error
bits [21], whereas LDPC utilizes a probabilistic approach to cor-
rect a greater number of random error bits, albeit at the expense
of increased computational resources. ECC, on the other hand, is
exclusively utilized to rectify errors in read data and does not ad-
dress the inherent errors present in NAND flash cells. Secondly,
the read retry mechanism is another approach that adjusts the read
reference voltage during data retrieval to mitigate data errors [2].
When NAND flash storage devices read data, they utilize a read
reference voltage value to assess the state of data cells. If the error
counts of the result obtained from the read reference voltage exceed
the upper limit that ECC can correct, the storage device proceeds
to reattempt data retrieval using a different read reference voltage
offset. If the correct result cannot be obtained after a certain number
of retries, the storage device will report an error [19]. Note that in
practical applications, the extent of data errors and voltage distribu-
tion remains unknown. Therefore, the read retry mechanism often
requires multiple iterations to identify an optimal read reference
voltage value, resulting in the consumption of valuable reading
time and consequent reduction in read performance. Furthermore,
it is only used in the reading process and lacks the capability to
alter the voltage distribution state of cells within the flash memory,
nor can it mitigate the aging process inherent to NAND flash cell
storage.
Although the two approaches could recover a certain of data,
they have limitations and may cause some negative impact on sys-
tem performance. ECC cannot correct errors over a threshold of 120
error bits per 1K code word. Furthermore, its correction time will in-
crease as the number of error bits grows [8]. On the other hand, the
read retry mechanism may severely impact the read performance
when the flash memory requires more retries for error correction.
In this paper, we propose a novel data recovery technique that is
different from the previously mainstream data recovery approaches,
called CellRejuvo. It cleverly leverages reprogramming techniques
to alleviate cell aging resulting from charge loss while enhancing
data reliability. Note that the term “reprogram” mentioned in this

In 3D TLC NAND flash, each memory cell is divided into eight
different states to store three-bit information. Due to the different
manufacturing vendors, the coding rules are not the same because
of different Gray code encoding compositions. Figure 1 shows one
of the coding rules of the eight states, including Erase and A to G
states of 3D TLC NAND Flash. For instance, in this figure, the A
state indicates that the data in LSB, CSB, and MSB is 0, 1, and 1,
respectively.
In Figure 1, observing the junction of 1 and 0, the data stored in
the LSB page of 3D TLC NAND flash is determined by the threshold
voltages Va and Ve. In other words, when electrons are lost from
either the A state or the E state, it leads to an increase in errors in
the LSB page. The data in the CSB page is determined by Vb, Vd,
and Vf. Consequently, when electrons are lost from the B, D, or F
state, errors in the CSB page are exacerbated. Lastly, the data in the
MSB page is determined by Vc and Vg. Thus, electron loss from the
C or G state results in increased errors in the MSB page.
Based on our observations, there are three common error types
in 3D NAND flash: retention errors, read disturbance errors, and
program interference errors [1, 3]. Retention error arises due to
the gradual loss of electrons stored in the oxidized insulating layer
over time. Read disturbance error occurs when reading a specific
cell. This can potentially lead to interference with the surrounding
cells, resulting in changes in their charge states and subsequent
errors. Program interference error is similar to read disturbance
error, denoting errors from charge interference in surrounding cells
during the writing process to a specific cell [5]. Moreover, the tem-
perature is a significant aging factor for semiconductors, including
the 3D NAND flash in our study [6]. The relationship between tem-
perature and retention time follows the Arrhenius equation [26]
that uses high-temperature aging to simulate retention time, which
is also applied in our experiments. Furthermore, the accumulated
program/erase (P/E) cycle also causes more errors in 3D NAND
flash. Each time a programming or erasing operation is performed,
the memory cells are affected by the application of voltage and
physical stress, resulting in gradual wear and degeneration of the
cells. Consequently, this will make the aforementioned three error
types more serious. Among these error types, retention error is the
most influential factor in compromising the reliability of 3D NAND
flash.
In order to resolve the data retention issues on 3D NAND flash,
some approaches have been proposed by researchers. BeLDPC [28]
discusses a method that leverages the bit error characteristics re-
sulting from P/E cycles and retention times. This method designs
an LDPC encoding strategy to reduce the number of decoding itera-
tions. Additionally, Feng et al. [9] propose an approach involving an
analysis of the threshold voltage distribution in 3D TLC NAND flash
memory to optimize read reference voltage prediction. However,
both these ECC-based and read retry methods serve as remedial
measures for data retention. They lack the ability to influence the
voltage distribution within the NAND flash cell, thereby falling
short of addressing the root cause of the retention issue.

2.2
Motivation
Recently, many reprogramming techniques have been used for data
sanitization with the growth of the importance of security on 3D
NAND flash [7, 24]. Scrubbing [13] is one of the data-cleaning meth-
ods that reprograms the data to the highest electric potential state.
Moreover, Wang et al. [25] introduce a reprogramming solution for
data sanitization. It reprograms user data from the original state
to other states, which cleans the target data and improves the read
performance of 3D NAND flash.
However, the aforementioned reprogramming methods are mainly
focused on data destruction aspects. Upon our investigation, it is
possible to rethink the usage of reprogramming for data recovery.
Figure 3 shows our preliminary experiments on reprogramming
different types of page on the real-world 3D TLC NAND flash. The
three figures represent the data of the LSB page, CSB page, and
MSB page, respectively. The x-axis corresponds to the wordline
number within a block under 9,000 P/E cycles, while the y-axis
represents the Raw Bit Error Rate (RBER) measured under each
wordline. The gray points represent the RBER values obtained after
a 2-month device retention, whereas black points represent the
RBER measured after the device underwent a 2-month retention
period followed by reprogramming using the same pattern. It is
evident that data errors in the LSB and CSB pages increased sig-
nificantly after reprogramming. Surprisingly, errors in the MSB
page exhibited a remarkable reduction. This has motivated us to
explore the concept of reprogramming from a novel perspective:
“data recovery”.
In various studies, including our preliminary experiments, it has
been observed that the G state is the most susceptible to shifting.
In other words, the G state is also the most error-prone, resulting
in a higher incidence of errors [4]. These errors manifest primarily
on the MSB page. As shown in Figure 4, for a block with 0 P/E
cycle, after 12 months of retention, the G state experiences an offset
of -16. In contrast, the offsets for other states do not exceed -10
at most. Consequently, the proposed method primarily focuses on
reprogramming the error-prone G state to effectively reduce errors
on the MSB page.
To the best of our knowledge, this paper marks the initial endeavor
to employ reprogramming as a method for rescuing erroneous data,
focusing on implementation aspects. The technical challenges fall on
how to develop an intelligent reprogramming method that reduces
errors on MSB pages with a minimal impact on LSB and CSB pages.

CELLREJUVO: RESCUING 3D NAND FLASH
CELLS WITH REPROGRAMMING
We introduce the CellRejuvo strategy to address the data retention
challenges observed in 3D TLC NAND flash. In this section, we
begin by elucidating the write process of 3D NAND flash, which can
provide a foundational understanding of the CellRejuvo principle.
Subsequently, we delve into how CellRejuvo specifically targets the
reprogramming of the G state. Then, we propose three variations
of CellRejuvo based on the reprogrammed wordline density within
a block, ranging from dense to loose. These three reprogramming
methods are Dense Cell Rejuvenation Design (DCRD), Sparse Cell
Rejuvenation Design (SCRD) type-1, and type-2. Lastly, we present
the complete CellRejuvo workflow using an algorithmic approach
for explanation.
3.1
CellRejuvo
In 3D TLC NAND flash, a write operation involves three pages,
namely the LSB page, CSB page, and MSB page, which collectively
form a wordline of data. The flash controller determines the appro-
priate state for a given cell based on the various data arrangements
and combinations of these three pages. Additionally, in practice,
upon receiving data from the host, the flash controller undergoes
a scrambling process to ensure that the eight states occur at the
same frequency, thus preventing lateral charge migration.
As illustrated in the schematic diagram in the upper part of
Figure 5, each page has a size of 16 KB, equivalent to 131,072 bits.
To illustrate, let us consider the first five bits. Initially, cells 1, 2,
3, 4, and 5 are all in the Erase state (ER). After programming, as
illustrated in the lower part of Figure 5, their electric potentials are
adjusted to states D, G, C, F, and B, respectively. Once all the cells
within a wordline have their electric potentials programmed to the
specified positions, and a write operation concludes, the interior of
this wordline resembles the scenario depicted in Figure 1.

The example is illustrated in Figure 5. Upon analysis, it was deter-
mined that the G state positions corresponded to bit 1, bit 5, and so
on until bit 131,067 ( 2○). These positions were consequently set to
the data intended for reprogramming, which constituted G state
data (i.e., LSB-CSB-MSB being 1-0-1), while other positions were
configured with Erase (ER) state data (i.e., LSB-CSB-MSB being
1-1-1). As per this approach, only bit 1, bit 5, and so on until bit
131,067 of the data prepared for reprogramming would be retained,
with all other data bits set to ER state ( 3○). After the data for repro-
gramming was prepared, the programming operation ( 4○) could
proceed. As data was exclusively written to the G state positions,
cells with G state storage electric potentials were charged, while
other cells remained at their original voltage levels because they
were programmed in the ER state, meaning they would not be ac-
tively charged due to the programming operation. This is depicted
in the lower part of Figure 5, using the first five cells as an example.
Cells 1, 2, 3, 4, and 5 initially stored data in states D, G, C, F, and B,
respectively. After the reprogramming operation targeting the G
state, only cell 2, which originally stored G state data, was shifted
to the right, while the other cells remained unaffected.
Note that although cells storing other states may not be actively
charged during the reprogramming operation, they can still be
influenced by program interference from adjacent G state cells. As
illustrated in Figure 6, program interference effects can extend to
the cells surrounding those storing G state data, causing a slight
charge. Therefore, not only the cells that store G state can improve
the integrity of the data, but also the cells that store other states.

3.1.2
Sparse Cell Rejuvenation Design (SCRD). SCRD aims to re-
duce program interference among the cells to mitigate the short-
comings of DCRD that may increase LSB page errors. In contrast to
DCRD, SCRD does not involve reprogramming every wordline. In-
stead, it tries to distribute the reprogramming operations among the
wordline to strike a balance between data recovery and program
interference. We have evaluated two different levels of distribu-
tion strategy among flash cell and proposed two types of SCRD:
Type-1 SCRD and Type-2 SCRD. The main design idea of SCRD is
illustrated on the right side of Figure 8. In our real device testing,
each block comprises 96 layers, with every 4 wordlines forming a
single layer. Type-1 SCRD selects the second wordline from each
layer within the block for reprogramming. In contrast, type-2 SCRD
exclusively targets the wordlines in even-numbered layers for a
more dispersed reprogramming strategy. Figure 10 illustrates the

3.2
Cell Rejuvenation Algorithms
When CellRejuvo is enabled, we analyze the positions within a
wordline that inherently belong to the G state and how to pre-
pare the data for reprogramming the G state. This algorithm called
PrepareData corresponds to steps 1○, 2○, and 3○in process flow
Figure 5. The algorithm takes WL as its input, representing which
wordline requires data preparation. The output consists of a 16KB
PD used for reprogramming. The process begins by initializing
memory addresses and declaring four 16KB memory spaces for
PD, DataLSB, DataCSB, and DataMSB (line 1). Subsequently, NAND
flash page reading is initiated to retrieve the LSB, CSB, and MSB
pages for WLi, which are stored in DataLSB, DataCSB, and DataMSB,
respectively (lines 2-4). To identify the positions belonging to the
G state, or, in other words, to locate positions where LSB, CSB, and
MSB have the binary pattern 1-0-1, a simple bitwise operation is
performed. This operation involves taking the bitwise complement
(NOT operation) of DataLSB and DataMSB, followed by executing
the bitwise AND operation between these two datasets and DataCSB
(line 6). Finally, this process returns the prepared data PD for repro-
gramming the G state (line 8).
Algorithm 2 illustrates the complete workflow of CellRejuvo.
First, a 16KB memory space, referred to as PD, is initialized to store
the data for reprogramming the G state (line 1). Next, choose either
line 2, line 2-1, or line 2-2 based on the intended reprogramming
method. It can be referred to Figure 8. As the DCRD method, we
iterate through each WLi within the block (line 2). For SCRD type-1
reprogramming (line 2-1), we select every second wordline in each

Figure 14 shows the read latency while applying various repro-
gramming methods under different workloads in the late stage.
The y-axis is normalized to the Baseline. The experiment results
show that the proposed DCRD and SCRD results in a much shorter
read latency than the Baseline. This is because when read requests
need to access data with more than 120 error bits per 1KiB in the
NAND flash, the read retry mechanism is initiated. Read retry at-
tempts for sets of parameter provided by the manufacturer until
ECC correction is either unsuccessful or successful for a specific
parameter set, which results in heavy processing time. Although
our DCRD and SCRD may also encounter situations requiring read
retries, the experimental results revealed that the processing time of
read requests while applying the proposed Sparse Type-1 is 21.86%
faster than the Baseline on average and even up to 30.53% faster
than the Baseline on rsrch_1. However, in rsrch_0 and wdev_0,
because the addresses accessed by workloads happen to be in the
LSB pages of most blocks, DCRD will cause some errors to occur
in LSB pages, causing Dense to have slightly worse performance
under these two workloads. In summary, the proposed CellRejuvo
not only accelerates ECC decoding due to its outstanding error
reduction capabilities but also reduces the necessity for read retries,
resulting in a substantial enhancement in read performance.
5
CONCLUSION
In this paper, we propose a novel data recovery strategy called
CellRejuvo to rescue aging 3D TLC NAND flash cells. Differing
from time-consuming and constrained approaches like ECC and
read retry, our proposed CellRejuvo addresses the retention issue
at its root. The reprogramming method, traditionally employed for
data destruction, is leveraged here to achieve data recovery. To the
best of our knowledge, this paper is the pioneer to explore, using
real SSD device, the application of reprogramming techniques to
mitigate data errors. Specifically, CellRejuvo achieves the greatest
error reduction by reprogramming the most error-prone G state in
3D TLC NAND flash. We implemented CellRejuvo on a real SSD
device, and the experiments show that the method proposed in this
article can reduce errors by 38.28% compared to the Baseline on
average, and significantly improve read performance by 21.86% in
the late stage.
