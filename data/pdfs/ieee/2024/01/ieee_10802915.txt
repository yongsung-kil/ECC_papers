ABSTRACT In channel coding, reducing power consumption and improving energy efficiency are major challenges in sliding window decoding (SWD) architectures for spatially coupled low-density parity-check (SC-LDPC) codes. In contrast to the well-elaborated literature on energy-efficient decoder implementations of classical LDPC block codes (LDPC-BCs), there is little research on the aforementioned challenges for SC-LDPC codes. Thus, in this paper, we investigate a novel approach for energy-efficient implementation of very high-throughput SWD for SC-LDPC codes. First, our approach proposes an analogy to state-ofthe-art iteration control techniques for LDPC-BC decoders, by dynamically adapting the window size for the decoding of SC-LDPC codes. For this purpose, we derive new algorithms that sequentially activate and/or deactivate the processors inside the window, without loss in error correction performance. Second, we propose an architecture for very high-throughput decoder implementations. Furthermore, to meet the high throughput requirements and improve energy efficiency, we revisit the window-size adaption criteria and slightly relax the derived algorithms in terms of error correction capability. Implementation results of the new revisited full-adaptive decoder in a 12 nm technology show that, at a negligible loss in error correction performance, the proposed adaptive SWD approach improves the energy efficiency by a factor of 1.4 to 3.4 compared to the state-of-the-art in the 4 dB to 7 dB signal-to-noise-ratio (SNR) range. This improvement is further increased up to a factor of 6.5 at higher SNRs.

importance to the progress in data rates. More specifically, channel (de-)coding is a major component in the digital baseband processing chain to ensure the reliability and efficiency of communication systems [5]. Thus, the need for high transmission error detection and correction capabilities challenges the design and implementation of forward error correction (FEC) decoding under silicon implementation constraints [6], [7].
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [8] are an established class of FEC codes due to their channel-capacityapproaching performance for large code block sizes. LDPC codes are standardized and widely used in multiple practical applications like digital video broadcasting (DVB-S2X) for satellite transmission, 3rd Generation Partnership Project (3GPP) radio access technology for 5G networks (5GNR), and IEEE 802.11n Wireless local area network (LAN) and IEEE 802.16e Mobile worldwide interoperability for microwave access (WiMAX) for communications [5]. The inherent node-level parallelism of LDPC codes makes belief propagation (BP) decoding and its implementation-friendly normalized min-sum (NMS) version particularly interesting for very high throughput [9]. However, under silicon implementation constraints, i.e., fixed area and power budgets, two major factors limit the error correction performance and implementation efficiency of state-of-the-art high-throughput LDPC decoders:
1) Block size: To achieve good error correction performance, the Tanner graph [10] of the code must be large, of low density, designed with low locality and high irregularity. Thus, large code block sizes are mandatory, and short cycles must be avoided. However, energy-efficient silicon implementation requires locality [9] to reduce the cost of data transfers, the wiring complexity, and routing congestions. Additionally, high throughput decoder implementation requires high regularity to exploit large parallelism. Consequently, this imposes limitations on the code block sizes and the FEC decoding performance is compromised.
2) Iterations: LDPC code decoding requires a large number of iterations (> 20) to achieve high error correction capability and approach channel capacity. From a silicon implementation perspective, with a larger number of iterations, the latency increases, the throughput decreases, and the energy efficiency gets worse.
State-of-the-art very high-throughput decoders, e.g., [11] and [12], are limited to small block sizes, i.e., some thousand bits, and a small number of decoding iterations, i.e., ≤ 20, at the cost of a reduced error correction capability.
Spatially coupled LDPC (SC-LDPC) codes [13] exhibit locality in the Tanner graph by coupling small LDPC block codes (LDPC-BCs). SC-LDPC codes can be considered as a special class of LDPC-BCs, thus enabling the use of standard LDPC-BC decoding techniques. However, the code structure with its locality enables so-called sliding window
VOLUME 12, 2024

decoding (SWD) schemes. In these schemes, a window of a smaller block size is used for decoding. Since decoding is performed within this window, the block size limiting factor of conventional LDPC-BC decoders can be overcome.
Different SWD schemes for SC-LDPC codes are presented in the literature. The most straightforward decoding approach uses a standard conventional iterative block decoder inside a window [14]. To improve energy efficiency and reduce latency, iteration control (IC) methods for early termination from standard LDPC-BC decoding can be used in iterative SWD schemes. During decoding, the number of iterations, I , is constrained to a maximum number of iterations, Imax. Depending on the signal-to-noise-ratio (SNR), not all Imax iterations are required for successful decoding. Several techniques have been proposed to reduce I for SWD. These include individual deactivation and reactivation of processors [15], non-uniform schedules [16], stall detection [17], and termination based on a target (sub-block) for the leftmost symbols of a window [18]. These methods are parity check-based, e.g., [15], [19], [20], and [21], or loglikelihood ratio (LLR)-based, e.g., [16], [17], [18], and [22]. Parity check-based IC methods are typically applied due to their small implementation overhead. To further increase the throughput, iteration unrolling was investigated in [23] from an algorithmic perspective only, following the approach of [24].
The aforementioned iterative SWD schemes are still limited in data rates. Therefore, to achieve very high throughput, state-of-the-art SWD schemes of SC-LDPC codes pursue a different approach. Instead of using a LDPC-BC decoder for the complete window, decoding inside a window is performed by several processors where each processor performs a single decoding iteration on a small sub-block. These sub-blocks can be overlapping or non-overlapping [15]. In this way, the decoding is performed in parallel on sub-blocks of this window, and for one iteration only. Layered (non-overlapping) and compact (overlapping) SWD schedules were investigated in detail in [25]. By exploiting efficient spatial and functional parallelism and appropriate information exchange between the processors, [25] and [26] have shown virtual silicon implementations that exceed 100 Gbit/s.
Such SWD schemes showed the potential of SC-LDPC codes for very high throughput decoding and good error correction performance compared to LDPC-BCs. However, as opposed to the well-elaborated literature on energyefficient implementations of classical LDPC-BC decoders, power consumption, and energy efficiency are still major challenges in these SWD architectures. While there exist many state-of-the-art IC techniques for LDPC-BC decoders to reduce the power consumption [27], such techniques cannot be applied to very high throughput SWD schemes since processors are performing only one iteration.

C. LOW-DENSITY PARITY-CHECK CODES
A LDPC code is specified by a parity check matrix H ∈ F2(c−b)×c. The code length is c and the number of parity check equations is c − b. A valid codeword satisfies
the equation

H · u(t)T = 0.

(1)

To determine whether the received symbols, y(t), contain errors, the syndrome s is calculated. Therefore, the binary hard decision (HD) of the LLR values, i.e., the received codeword r(t), is multiplied by the parity check matrix

s = H · r(t)T .

(2)

A codeword is only valid if the syndrome of the received symbols is zero.
The Tanner graph of a LDPC code is a bipartite graph. Nodes represent variable nodes (VNs), which are columns of H, and check nodes (CNs), which are rows of H. Edges represent connections from VNs to CNs and vice versa, which are non-zero entries inside H.

E. HIGH THROUGHPUT DECODING Due to the structural characteristics of the SC-LDPC codes, the decoding can be performed with a sliding window W that diagonally moves, i.e., slides, from the upper left to the lower right of H[1,L]. Throughout the decoding process, the window W spans over a sub-matrix of H[1,L], which is a subset of VNs and CNs in the corresponding Tanner graph.
At each time step t, the SWD completes the decoding of one sub-block in a time Tp. For high-throughput decoding, this window processing time Tp should be less than one clock cycle, which is inversely proportional to the frequency of the decoder implementation. Thus, designing a decoder architecture that can handle large code block lengths c while minimizing Tp is challenging for high-throughput decoding. To address this challenge, state-of-the-art decoding methods divide the decoding window into smaller subwindows, which are then processed individually by dedicated processors in parallel. While there are multiple schemes to divide the window into sub-windows as detailed in [25], this paper focuses on row-wise layered decoding as we describe it next. However, the approach to be proposed is not limited to this decoding scheme.
F. ROW-WISE LAYERED DECODING A layer is defined as a row of ms + 1 submatrices, i.e., Hms , . . . , H0 , and consists of (c − b) rows of H[1,L]. A SC-LDPC code H[1,L] has L + ms layers, that are enumerated from 1, . . . , l, . . . , L + ms. Following this definition, by horizontally splitting the decoding window W into sub-windows, multiple non-overlapping layers are decoded in parallel using dedicated processors P inside W . Each processor p ∈ [1, . . . , P] processes only one row-layer in H[1,L], i.e., processes a single iteration involving one CN group and (ms +1) VN groups. In row-wise layered decoding, the sliding window is moved layer-wise from layer 1 to layer L + ms over H[1,L]. At every time step, the window slides one layer forward and starts the decoding again. Consequently, when the decoding of a full codeword is complete, each layer has been updated P times. Thus, the number of processors P, which determines the number of CN updates, is equivalent to the number of iterations.
Fig. 3 shows an example of row-wise layered SWD algorithm with ms = 1 and P = 3, where layers l, l − 2, and l − 4 are decoded in parallel at the time step t. With W sliding diagonally from left to right, in the rest of this paper, the bottom right layer inside W is denoted as the head layer, while the top left layer inside W is denoted as the tail layer.

G. ALGORITHMIC COMPLEXITY Designing energy-efficient high-throughput decoders is challenging under silicon implementation constraints [29]. In SWD of SC-LDPC codes, the throughput is limited by the iterative message exchanges between the CNs and VNs over the edges of the Tanner graph. After one decoding iteration, the calculated CN or VN updates are transferred to all other connected nodes via the edges. With each edge, a fixed number of calculations and data transfers is associated. Thus, the number of edges is a good metric to reflect the algorithmic complexity [30].
Using row-wise layered decoding for a SC-LDPC code with a parity check matrix H[1,L], the number of processed edges is given by dc · (c − b). By including the total number of iterations Imax , which is equivalent to Pmax for the non-overlapping row-wise layered decoding as explained in Section II-F, the complexity of state-of-the-art window decoders is evaluated by
C = dc · (c − b) · Imax = dc · (c − b) · Pmax . (6)
The complexity metric C measures the total number of processed and transferred edges for decoding a complete codeword [25].
With respect to Eqs. (4) and (5), reducing Pmax is equivalent to modifying the decoding window size. Thus, the objective of this work is to reduce the algorithmic complexity of the SWD algorithm while maintaining error correction performance. This is achieved by a novel approach that adaptively changes the size of the decoding window. Furthermore, we will exploit the SNR dependency on the

By dynamically adjusting the window size during the decoding process, these algorithms aim to omit unnecessary node updates while maintaining error correction

A. HALF-ADAPTIVE SLIDING WINDOW DECODING State-of-the-art SWD uses a fixed window size with a fixed number of processors Pmax. These processors are all active during the window decoding. However, in the HASWD algorithm, the window is initialized with a minimum window size that includes one active processor only. Then, depending on the syndrome fails, the window size might monotonically - increase to include up to Pmax active processors.
Within the window, the decoding process of a layer using a processor p checks if the syndrome for the current layer is zero. If this is the case, then, in principle, no further processing of this layer is necessary in the subsequent steps, which would save computations. Otherwise, this layer must be processed again after ms + 1 time steps. This (re-)processing is performed by the subsequent active processor p + 1.
Therefore, the HA-SWD algorithm (Algorithm 2) exploits the information of the fulfilled syndrome checks to successively increase the window size, starting with one processor, i.e., P = 1. Following the window decoding (line 4), if a syndrome check of the tail layer in the current window fails (line 5), we increase the window size in the next decoding step by activating an additional processor (line 7). Decoding will then be performed with the increased window size to include the ms left-connected layers of the layer that failed to decode correctly.

The window width P · (ms + 1) monotonically increases during the decoding from (ms + 1) and might get maximally to Pmax · (ms + 1). Thus, the HA-SWD algorithm approach performs fewer computations than the fixed SWD approach, where the window size is fixed to Pmax · (ms + 1).
B. FULL-ADAPTIVE SLIDING WINDOW DECODING The FA-SWD algorithm exploits the information of the fulfilled syndrome checks not only to increase the window size when required, similar to the HA-SWD algorithm, but also to decrease the window again to reduce unnecessary computations. The decoding starts with the minimum window size that includes one active processor only. Then, the window witnesses an increase in its size followed by multiple increases/decreases depending on the syndrome checks. At least one processor should be active.
If processor p corrects an error in an active layer l in a decoding window, and if subsequent layers (l + 1, l + 2, . . .) inside the window are also error-free, then no further processing of all these layers is required. This suggests

that it is possible to save unnecessary computations by omitting the reprocessing of these layers, which motivates the approach of reducing the sliding window size in subsequent decoding. However, as noted above, data dependencies require that preceding layers also should satisfy the syndrome check to exclude layer l from updating. Otherwise, the error correction performance will decrease, leading to an early error floor.
Enabling and disabling processors must not affect the error correction performance. For row-wise layered decoding, VNs are split into upper and lower parts (for ms = 1) or even smaller fragments (for ms > 1). To ensure the same error correction behavior as with fixed window size, a full node update is required in case of a syndrome check failure. Therefore, all connected layers of the layer with a syndrome failure must be updated again.

The FA-SWD algorithm (Algorithm 3) uses the same criterion as the HA-SWD algorithm to increase the window size. Once the decoding of the tail layer inside the window fails, an additional processor should be added to the window decoding in the next time step (lines 5-9). If no additional processor is activated, contrary to the HA-SWD algorithm, it is possible to check for decreasing the window size. For this, the syndromes of the ms right-connected layers to the tail layer in the current window [zi+1, . . . , zi+ms ], as well as the syndrome of their adjacent active layer [zi+ms+1] are evaluated (line 11). If all syndromes are satisfied, then it is allowed to exclude the last processor from further processing, i.e., decrease the window size (line 12). It is important to include the syndrome [zi+ms+1] check of the active layer just before (ahead) the tail layer, i.e., the adjacent-right active layer, to guarantee its (re-)decoding in case its syndrome fails. Following the decoding, the syndromes of all layers in the window are returned by Algorithm 1 (line 12) as [z1, z2, z3, z4, z5] corresponding to layers [l − 4, l − 3, l− 2, l − 1, l] respectively. If the syndrome z1 of the tail layer l − 4 is satisfied, there is no need to increase the window size. Furthermore, if the syndromes of the right-connected layer (z2 of layer l − 3) and the active layer ahead (z3 of layer l − 2) are both satisfied, then it is possible to deactivate processor #3 in the next decoding. Additionally, now by looking at the active layer l − 2, if the syndromes of the right-connected layer (z4 of layer l − 1) and the active layer ahead (z5 of layer l) are both satisfied, then it is also possible to deactivate processor #2 in the next decoding. This scenario will lead to a double decrease in the window size by deactivating two processors in the next time step. In this case, at time t + 1, the window is using only one processor to decode layer l + 1. Finally, to stress the importance of checking the active layer ahead, assume that all layers in the window are decoded correctly except for the head layer l. If we decide to deactivate processors #3 and #2, then at time t + 1, processor #1 will decode layer l + 1. This indicates that layer l will not be processed again and there will be no chance to be correctly decoded, which leads to a decrease in the error correction.
The FA-SWD algorithm further reduces the number of updates per layer compared to the HA-SWD algorithm. Depending on the syndrome failures, the window width might increase during the decoding to get maximally to Pmax · (ms+ 1), and decrease to get minimally to (ms + 1). Similar to the HA-SWD algorithm, the exact window-size adaptation depends on the SNR. We will show in the results that there is no impact of both algorithms on the error correction performance compared to state-of-the-art SWD using a fixed window size of Pmax.

Whereas for the FA-SWD algorithm, the window size can decrease by up to P − 1 processors before moving the decoding window. As a consequence, the potential gain in energy efficiency due to the window decrease is reduced.

A. DECODER The decoder architecture depicted in Fig. 8 consists of a pipeline of Pmax processors for the row-layered decoding schedule which allows adapting the decoding window size. All processors are instantiated individually and pipelined to ensure high throughput. The input LLR values, y(t), are fed into the first processor of the decoder pipeline, while the estimated information, uˆ (t), are output from the last processor in the decoder pipeline.
The black parts in Fig. 8 illustrate the data flow as the decoding window slides over the parity check matrix of the SC-LDPC code. Each processor operates on a layer inside the decoding window. Each processor in the pipeline has right APP (R-APP) and left APP (L-APP) values and extrinsic (EXTR) messages as both inputs and outputs. The R-APP output of each processor p is fed back into its own L-APP input via an intermediate register. The L-APP output of processor p is passed to the R-APP input of processor p + 1 via an intermediate register. Similarly, EXTR messages are passed from processor p to p + 1 through two intermediate registers. All outputs are connected to registers for synchronization and data flow management. This pipelined data transfer represents the sliding of the decoding

To support the window size adaptivity, additional logic, and registers are necessary, as shown by the red highlights in Fig. 8. The parity check output of each processor, i.e., the syndrome check result of each layer, is fed into a central controller. Processor 1 is always active for the initial parity check evaluation. The controller shown in Fig. 8 operates as a local controller for each processor, making decisions based on the parity check signal of the directly preceding processor. Based on the parity check signal of processor 1, the decision to enable processor 2 is made. The Re-HA-SWD algorithm describes the algorithmic behavior of the enable signal output of the controller for each processor. Consequently, correctly decoded tail layers are no longer updated, thereby fixing the HD of the left half of the tail layer, corresponding to H1. On successful decoding of the tail layer, the write left (write L) signal is activated for a single clock cycle, which allows to transfer the HDs of the L-APP values to the HD pipeline. The VNs of the right part of a layer (H0) are updated by the same processor again as the left part of the successive layer (H1). Thus, in the Re-HA-SWD algorithm, the write right (write R) signal is always disabled.
In very high-throughput architectures, all processors are instantiated, which is equivalent to the maximum window size of the sliding window. The window size is proportional to the number of processors inside this window, which in return is proportional to the power consumption. Thus, to minimize the power consumption of high throughput decoders, the number of active processors needs to be minimized. Therefore, the controller uses the parity check of the tail layer of the current window position. Only if this parity check fails, additional node updates are required. Thus, successive processors are enabled with a one-layer delay. Clock gating is applied to disable the processors that are not required which prohibits data toggling of successive processors.
2) REVISITED FULL-ADAPTIVE SLIDING WINDOW DECODING The Re-FA-SWD architecture extends the Re-HA-SWD architecture described above by adding a mechanism to disable already enabled processors. Suppose that layer l − 1 has been correctly decoded at time t − 1, and that processors 1 and 2 are enabled at time t, performing node updates at layers l and l − 2, respectively. After these node updates, all parity checks are evaluated resulting in successful parity check outputs for both processors that are passed to the controller. Consequently, updates using processor 2 are not required at the subsequent time step t + 1. Thus, the enable signal will disable the registers at the L-APP and EXTR outputs of processor 1, so that the registers connected to those of processor 1 will not propagate signals in the next clock cycle. As a result, the output of these registers at time t + 3 remains identical to that at time t + 2. This indicates that processor 2 is holding the same values and is not performing

The controller shown in Fig. 8 controls the activity of the processors and the HD bypass registers based on the parity check signals of the processors. As for the Re-HA-SWD algorithm, further processors are enabled by the enable signal output of the controller. Moreover, the processor updating the tail layer of the current window position can be disabled by the enable signal outputs of the controller. The write L and write R signals for each processor are activated in order to transfer the HDs of the L-APP and R-APP values, respectively, to the HD pipeline. The write L signal is activated for one clock cycle when the decoder successfully decodes the current tail layer for the first time, or if the processor will be disabled at the next time step. The write R signal is activated for one clock cycle if the processor will be disabled at the next time step.
The throughput for decoding is proportional to the frequency. Thus, we have to minimize the critical path. For the FA-SWD, communication between each processor is required, which results in a long critical path. To address this issue, the Re-FA-SWD algorithm allows for the enabling or disabling of a single processor at each time step. This approach limits communication to directly adjacent processors, enhancing the locality of the decoder architecture. Consequently, since the critical path is confined between two adjacent processors, high throughput decoding is feasible with the Re-FA-SWD algorithm.
C. PROCESSOR The processor is the fundamental building block of the SCLDPC decoder, responsible for performing CN and VN updates. Fig. 9 shows the processor architecture, which operates on one sub-matrix row of the parity check matrix of the SC-LDPC code using the NMS algorithm with a single iteration.
Each processor contains a fully parallel VN2VN unit that performs the node updates. The EXTR, L-APP, and R-APP values inputs are connected to the VN2VN unit. The first
VOLUME 12, 2024

and last sub-blocks of the SC-LDPC code require special processing that is realized by multiplexers, passing a neutral element and the terminations sequence, respectively. The outputs of the VN2VN unit are forwarded to the output of the processor.
The VN2VN unit, shown on the right side of Fig. 9, performs one iteration, i.e., one CN and one VN update, using the OVA schedule for faster convergence. Full parallelism is applied at both node- and edge-level. The VN2VN unit comprises three stages: VN functional unit output (VFUo), CN functional unit (CFU), and VN functional unit input (VFUi). The VFUo stage subtracts the exchanged messages, i.e., EXTR messages, from the a posteriori probability (APP) values, the CFU performs a minimum search and scales the extrinsic messages, and the VFUi sums the extrinsic messages to APP values. The Tanner graph, which connects the nodes, is represented by the CN-to-VN (CN2VN) and VN-to-CN (VN2CN) networks.
Decisions for enabling and/or disabling processors are based on partial syndrome checks, which are evaluated within the VN2VN unit. Each CFU performs a parity check, i.e., one parity check per row in the processed layers, and the parity output of the VN2VN unit is successful if all parity checks are correct. The parity signal is an additional output of the processor indicating the decoding success/failure of the currently updated layer.

At least 100 erroneous frames are simulated at each SNR point. Fig. 10 shows the BER performance of the proposed HASWD and FA-SWD algorithms for the SC-LDPC code with Z = 16, over the SNR range 2.5 dB to 4 dB. Results show that both algorithms have the same error correction performance over the full SNR range. The performance is also identical to that of SWD with fixed window size. This indicates that, once an active layer is identified as not correctly decoded, both adaptive SWD algorithms properly increase the window size by activating the next successive processor. This way, this same layer is decoded again with the newly activated processor to increase the probability of its correct decoding. In the worst case, both adaptive SWD algorithms must activate all Pmax processors, which is equivalent to the decoding with the fixed full-window
191154

size. Consequently, if a layer cannot be successfully decoded using HA-SWD and FA-SWD, its decoding also fails with fixed SWD. The same error correction performance for all three algorithms indicates that both adaptive SWD algorithms correctly decode a frame with at most Pmax processors in the worst case. It also shows that both algorithms successively adjust the window size without introducing losses in the error correction performance. The above results indicate that the error correction performance is preserved, without detailing the gain in the reduction of decoding operations. Thus, analyzing only the BER does not tell whether this identical performance is achieved with a smaller number of active processors than Pmax. This is important to highlight the window-size adaptation feature, i.e., by incrementally including additional processors without reaching Pmax as suggested by both HA-SWD and FA-SWD, or by decreasing back the window as additionally featured by FA-SWD. This is what we leave for the detailed discussion in the following section by looking at the difference between HA-SWD and FA-SWD in terms of the number of activated processors for each algorithm at different SNR points.
To better classify the results with respect to state-of-theart, the decoding performance of the LDPC-BC with block size (648, 540) is included in Fig. 10. This is selected as the codeword length is comparable to (640, 512) SC-LDPC subblock size. Similar to the maximum number of node updates, i.e., processors, used for adaptive SWD of the SC-LDPC code, the decoding of the LDPC-BC with IC is performed with a maximum of 8 updates per node, i.e., iterations. Results show that the SC-LDPC code with lifting factor Z = 16 outperforms the LDPC-BC by around 1.3 dB at a target BER operation point of 10−6, which is required for closeproximity communications. Even for infrastructure-type use applications with more strict BER requirements, the gain in error correction performance increases to more than 2 dB at a target BER of 10−10. This better performance in both, the waterfall and error floor, regions of the BER curve highlights the importance of SC-LDPC codes being able to cover a wide range of applications with extremely different BER requirements in contrast to using the LDPC-BC.
Fig. 10 additionally includes the performance of the decoding of two other SC-LDPC codes with different lifting factors Z ∈ {24, 32}. Due to the increased codeword length, increasing the lifting factor of the SC-LDPC from 16 to 24 (32) leads to an improved error correction performance by an additional 0.25 dB (0.38 dB) at BER of 10−10. Results also show that for the other two codes, there is a perfect match between the performance of both adaptive SWD algorithms and the fixed SWD. These simulation results suggest that the identical performance for all three decoding algorithms is not limited to the specific code under study with the lifting factor Z = 16. Rather, as proven by simulations, it is the same even for other SC-LDPC codes.

VII. DISCUSSIONS A. ANALOGY TO ITERATION CONTROL TECHNIQUES Table 5 provides a summary and highlights the analogy of our approach to the IC techniques for LDPC-BC decoders within a typical SNR operating range. IC has been shown to improve the energy efficiency of LDPC-BC decoders by 31.3 % (48.9 %) at 5 dB (6 dB) without any loss in the error correction performance. Similarly, the energy efficiency of SC-LDPC decoders improves by 29.2 %, 52.2 %, and 64.6 % at 4 dB, 5 dB, and 6 dB, respectively, through the adaption of the SWD window size using the Re-FA-SWD algorithm. However, compared to fixed SWD size, this improvement in energy efficiency comes at the cost of an increase in the error at very low BER targets (10−9 to 10−11).
Our results indicate that the adaptive SWD approach narrows the energy efficiency gap between energy optimized
VOLUME 12, 2024

unrolled LDPC-BC architectures and SC-LDPC decoders. For instance, at 6 dB, the state-of-the-art fixed SWD outperforms the LDPC-BC decoder in error correction performance by two orders of magnitude (3.1 × 10−9 to 1.8 × 10−11), albeit at 6.9 × worse energy efficiency. However, our adaptive approach reduces this gap between LDPC-BC and SC-LDPC decoders down to 2.4 × with negligible impact on the error correction performance.
B. LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK The primary goal of this work was to improve the energy efficiency of SWD algorithms for SC-LDPC code decoders while maximizing throughput. This improvement was successfully demonstrated through efficient hardware implementation, showing significant gains as a function of the SNR. However, a limitation of the current work is the absence of a rigorous theoretical analysis to support the negligible effect of the window size adaptation criteria on error correction performance. Currently, the negligible effect has only been validated through empirical evaluations conducted via Monte Carlo simulations. While this is also the case with IC in LDPC-BC decoding, the absence of a formal mathematical proof leaves a gap in fully understanding the underlying decision-making process and limits of the proposed approach. Additionally, the performance analysis of the proposed algorithms was performed for one code only with different lifting factors, which might limit the generalization of our findings.
Future research should address both of the aforementioned points and establish a formal theoretical foundation to mathematically prove and validate the observed benefits of the proposed adaptive SWD algorithms. Such an analysis would enhance the generalization of the proposed approach and provide deeper insights into adaptive decoding design principles, which trade off implementation complexity and error correction performance. Furthermore, extending the theoretical analysis helps in exploring the performance and

robustness of the approach to different codes and more diverse channel conditions.
Although this work is primarily focused on adapting the window size for the row-wise layered SWD schedule, the underlying concept can be extended in future to other decoding approaches, such as the column-wise layered SWD schedule, as well as, the respective compact SWD schedules. By extending and generalizing this work to encompass different scheduling techniques, the implementation gains, flexibility, and applicability of adaptive window size methods in various decoding scenarios could be further enhanced.
To achieve maximum throughput, we instantiate Pmax processors, each of which performs one full node update, i.e., one iteration, before the sliding window advances to the next spatial position. Consequently, the total number of updates per node is equal to the product of the number of iterations performed by each processor, which is assumed to be one in this work, and the number of processors updating the same spatial position as the window slides. Although the focus of this approach is on maximizing throughput, it may not be the optimal design principle for other applications with lower throughput requirements. In such cases, it may be advantageous to reduce the number of maximum available processors while increasing the number of iterations per processor. Future research could investigate the joint application of traditional IC techniques with the proposed adaptive window size approach. This exploration could provide deeper insights into the interplay between early termination strategies and dynamic window size adaptation. Additionally, it could quantify the benefits of this hybrid approach, particularly in terms of balancing error correction performance and implementation complexity.
VIII. CONCLUSION In this paper, we presented an adaptive SWD approach for SC-LDPC codes to improve the energy efficiency of high-throughput decoder implementations. In this approach, only a subset of available processors are activated on demand based on the evaluation of partial syndrome checks. The activation is based on the derived HA-SWD and FA-SWD algorithms that preserve the error correction performance. Then, we modified both algorithms by relaxing the processors’ activation and deactivation criteria for highthroughput decoding. The new Re-HA-SWD and Re-FASWD algorithms further reduce power consumption at a negligible cost in error correction performance. To prove the efficiency of our approach, we presented a full architecture
191160

for very high-throughput decoding including an activation controller for both modified algorithms. Implementation results in a 12 nm FinFET technology show SNR-dependent improvements in energy efficiency, which gets up to 6.5 × compared to implementations of state-of-the-art techniques. To the best of our knowledge, this energy-efficient adaptive approach for high throughput SWD implementations is unprecedented in the literature.
