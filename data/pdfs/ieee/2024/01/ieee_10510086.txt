AbstractŌĆöThe article introduces a novel method for finding low-weight words in linear block binary and ternary codes by leveraging the Number Geometry. The methodology involves employing a set of heuristics related to vector lengths, lattice densities, dual space operations, permutations of lattice basis, and the search for short vectors within it. Notably, this proposed method secured the top position in the international low-weight codeword search competition organized by prominent institutions, including the French National Center for Scientific Research (CNRS), Inria Paris, and the National Research Institute for Mathematics and Informatics in the Netherlands (CWI). In a remarkable display of its effectiveness, the method successfully discovered a codeword of weight 212 in the parity-check matrix of a block binary code with a rate of 0.5 and a code length of 1280. Importantly, this accomplishment was realized within a timeframe of 4,147,201 seconds, utilizing an Intel 7700K 64GB system with a 1070 8GB video card. The search specifically excluded automorphisms and other symmetry properties, such as cyclicity and quasicyclicity. Furthermore, when applied to the low-weight word search problem (weight 228) in the mentioned competition, our proposed method exhibited a remarkable acceleration, being 3172.9 times faster than the Brouwer-Zimmerman algorithm implemented in the MAGMA Version 2.22-3 package. Additionally, it surpassed the best standalone implementation with vectorization and parallelization by 899 times. This noteworthy speedup underscores the efficiency and competitiveness of the introduced approach in effectively addressing the intricate task of identifying low-weight codewords in linear block codes.

I. INTRODUCTION
In the realms of digital signal processing, noise-resistant coding, and contemporary cryptography, the imperative objectives include enhancing data security to thwart attacks from both quantum and classical computing devices, alongside elevating the efficiency and reliability of signal processing techniques and noise-resistant coding. These challenges prompted an exploration of effective methods, leading to the adoption of the mathematical framework known as the Geometry of Numbers. Despite its recent widespread acceptance beyond specialized circles, the mathematical methods derived from this framework have proven instrumental.
Significantly, Schnorr and RobinsonŌĆÖs work spotlighted vulnerabilities in asymmetric encryption systems, specifically

those relying on RSA number factorization ( [2], RivestShamir-Adleman), and discrete logarithm ECC ( [3]ŌĆō[5], elliptic curve cryptography), particularly with high key bit depths. The application of number geometry methods has facilitated the simplification of equalizers according to the maximum likelihood criterion, deploying spherical decoders for modulation constellations used in practical scenarios [6], [7]. Notably, these problems were previously deemed NPcomplete and practically unsolvable, even with relatively small problem dimensions.
The pursuit of a solution to the NP problem of finding a minimum-weight codeword is at the core of this article [8]. Herein, we propose a method that successfully addresses the challenge of searching for low-weight words in linear block codes. Importantly, this method achieves success without relying on cyclic, quasi-cyclic, or other symmetry properties inherent in block structures, which typically serve to reduce the complexity of this problem.

III. NUMBER GEOMETRY PROBLEM HEURISTICS
There exists a family of heuristic methods designed to expedite the estimation of code distance by a priori constructing a balanced tree or pruning branches in the tree. One group of heuristics is based on vector lengths and involves pruning branches in a search tree. These heuristics leverage the distance of lattice points from the Voronoi cell boundary of the nearest lattice point, as vectors closer to the boundary are more likely to be the shortest. Common distance-based heuristics include using metric spaces that accurately describe the non-spherical shape of Voronoi cells, such as Mahalanobis distances or the intersection body of cylinders. The procedure involves measuring the distance of a lattice point from the boundary of Voronoi cells using the selected metric.

1) Heuristics based on Optimal Pruning via Linear Programming: In some cases, linear programming methods are used to reduce the search for optimal Pruning. This is particularly effective for attacking asymmetric encryption systems lattice basis based on discrete logarithms (ECC) and prime factorization (RSA), [3]ŌĆō[5].
2) Heuristic Branch Pruning: This unit plays a crucial role in accelerating the method for finding a short lattice vector formed by a block code. As the Hamming weight decreases, the efficiency of discarding branches in the search tree increases. Lattice pre-reduction, rough branch Pruning functions, working in dual space, and density heuristics all contribute to optimizing specific areas and improving the statistical chances of obtaining the shortest vector, [25], [26], Neural Network Gaussian heuristics (predict radius of the hypersphere) [34].
3) Search Heuristics in Dual Space: Algorithms for finding the shortest vector can be applied in dual space (coset space or dual lattice). Operations such as pruning branches, sparse and high symmetry embedding, may be more efficient in this dual space, [27], [28].
4) Heuristics based on Random and Non-Random Permutations and Insertion: These heuristics involve random or structured permutations of the lattice basis, followed by lattice basis reduction using methods like LLL/BKZ. This helps improve the quality of the basis and speeds up the search for the shortest vector, [27], [31]ŌĆō[33].
5) Density Heuristic (Codes on the Graph Manifold): This heuristic estimates the density of lattice points around a vector. Regions with low density are more likely to contain extreme vectors. Strongest methods for finding the shortest vector are often based on this heuristic, [35].
6) Neural Network Heuristic: This heuristic use Deep Neural Networks to solve sub-lattice SBP- and CVP-problem instead (simultaneous) BKZ method, using nonlinear decision boundary functions, predict KFP branch properties (radius, etc) [34], [37]ŌĆō[40].

These heuristics collectively contribute to the efficiency and

speed of lattice-based cryptographic algorithms, making them

essential tools in the analysis and development of lattice-based

cryptographic systems. LetŌĆÖs delve into a detailed examination

Rounding heuristics based on reducing the problem of finding the shortest vector to the problem of finding the closest vector using the Babai algorithm is another family of heuristics [29]. These heuristics aim to determine how well a vector can be rounded to the nearest lattice point. A vector that is difficult to round is likely to be short. IV. CONCLUSION
The article presents an innovative methodology for discovering low-weight words in linear block codes through the application of Number Geometry principles. The approach incorporates heuristics related to vector lengths, lattice densities, dual space operations, permutations of lattice basis, and the identification of short vectors within it. This novel method achieved the top position in the international low-weight codeword problem competition, [1]. A noteworthy accomplishment of this methodology is the successful found of a word of weight 212. Importantly, this feat was achieved within a time frame of 4,147,201 seconds, utilizing an Intel 7700K 64GB system with a NVIDIA 1070 8 Gb video card. The search, which excluded automorphisms and symmetry properties such as cyclicity and quasicyclicity, exhibited a speed of 0.5 with a length of 1280. Furthermore, when applied to the low-weight word search problem (weight 228) from the competition, our proposed method demonstrated a remarkable acceleration of 3,172.9 times compared to the Brouwer-Zimmerman algorithm implemented in the MAGMA Version 2.22-3 package. It also outperformed the best standalone implementation with vectorization and parallelization by 899 times.
