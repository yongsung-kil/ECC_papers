Optimization of Finite-Length Protograph-Based SC-LDPC Anytime Codes

Xiaoxi Yu , Li Deng, Yong Liang Guan , Senior Member, IEEE, and Zhaojie Yang

Abstract— In this letter, we present an optimization method for designing Protograph-based spatially coupled low-density parity-check anytime (P-anytime) codes with outstanding finitelength performance. We first introduce two performance metrics, namely the delay-exponent and anytime mean parameters, to effectively capture the finite-length behavior of P-anytime codes. We then propose a multi-objective optimization algorithm to optimize these performance metrics and design novel P-anytime codes. Simulation results demonstrate that our proposed P-anytime codes achieve one to two orders of magnitude improvement in both bit erasure rate and bit error rate compared with prior-art anytime codes.
Index Terms— Anytime codes, spatially coupled LDPC codes, density evolution, finite-length analysis, multi-objective differential evolution (MODE).
I. INTRODUCTION
DELAY-SENSITIVE real-time applications typically require interactive and causal communications within strict delay constraints. For instance, in control-over-network systems, the system state information observed by remote observers needs to be periodically transmitted to the controller over a noisy channel. This allows the controller to make timely decisions and determine appropriate control actions. Anytime information theory [1] provides a solution for such applications. This theory requires the decoding error probabilities of each bit-stream block to decay exponentially with its corresponding decoding delay [2]. In other words, the reliability of a bit-stream block improves exponentially with the growth of its decoding window size. Codes that exhibit such performance are referred to as having anytime-reliable properties and are named as anytime codes.
Practical anytime codes have been developed for different channels and applications. A protograph-based lowdensity parity-check (LDPC) convolutional code that exhibits anytime-reliable properties has been proposed for the binary erasure channel (BEC) [3] and the binary-input additive white Gaussian noise (BI-AWGN) channel [4]. The aforementioned anytime codes have been further improved in [6] and [7]. Additionally, novel joint source-channel anytime coding (JSCAC) schemes have been developed in [8].
So far, the primary focus of anytime code designs has been on improving their delay-exponent. However, relying solely on optimizing the delay-exponent may not be the most effective approach for enhancing code performance in finite-length scenarios. This is because the delay-exponent is a metric used for asymptotic analysis, which assumes an infinite block length for the code. For instance, when transmitting over a BEC, anytime codes experience “erasure floors”, where their performance curves level off at large decoding delays [3], [4], [5], [6], [7]. Interestingly, it has been observed that two anytime codes with similar delay-exponent can exhibit significantly different erasure floors [6]. This observation suggests the existence of other performance metrics that can determine the finite-length performance of anytime codes.
To address high floor levels, retransmission schemes are proposed in [4] and [7]. However, no prior work has explored solving this issue through protograph design.1 Inspired by this, our letter designs optimized protographs for P-anytime codes with improved finite-length performance over prior art. Specifically, our contributions are as follows:
• We construct P-anytime codes and analyze their finitelength performance. Our analysis shows that the performance curves can be predicted using two bounds: the asymptotic decay line and the irreducible level.
• We derive the density evolution tailored for P-anytime codes and introduce two performance metrics: the delay-exponent and the anytime mean parameter. These metrics are able to accurately quantify the two bounds.
• We propose a multi-objective optimization algorithm based on differential evolution to simultaneously optimize both metrics. Subsequently, we construct P-anytime codes that exhibit excellent finite-length performance.

III. PROTOGRAPH-BASED ANYTIME (P-ANYTIME) CODES

A P-anytime code ensemble is constructed by spatially coupling a series of identical protographs. Each protograph is a small bipartite graph consisting of bc check nodes and bv variable nodes. A protograph can be also represented by a base matrix B of size bc ×bv, where each entry bj,i ≥ 0 denotes the number of edges connecting variable node vi (i = 1, . . . , bv) to check node cj (j = 1, . . . , bc). In Fig. 1(a), we show a protograph with bv = 3 and bc = 2. The white circle represents a punctured variable node, whose corresponding coded bits will not be transmitted. The black circles and the squares represent the transmitted variable nodes and the check nodes, respectively. V. PROTOGRAPH OPTIMIZATION ALGORITHM
Different from [13], which introduces a single-objective optimization method to determine the code with an optimal window mean parameter, we propose a multi-objective optimization problem (MOP) to search protographs of P-anytime codes with optimized values of α and γ. The proposed MOP can be expressed as minB {J1 = −α, J2 = −γ}, where J1 and J2 are the objective functions that need to be minimized.
In the proposed optimization algorithm (see Algorithm 1), we employ multi-objective differential evolution, which includes mutation, crossover, and selection stages, to evolve randomly generated component parts. The evolving component B is represented by an array x. Since MOPs have a series of optimal solutions, it is necessary to introduce the concept of
Pareto optimal solutions [15]. Definition 1: A solution x1 is said to dominate another
solution x2, if and only if ∀i ∈ {1, 2}, Ji(x1) ≤ Ji(x2) and ∃k ∈ {1, 2}, Jk(x1) < Jk(x2).
Definition 2: A solution x∗ is said to be a Pareto optimal solution if there does not exist another solution x such that x dominates x∗.
All Pareto optimal solutions constitute a Pareto optimal set, denoted as X∗, and the Pareto front Pf = {J(x∗) : x∗ ∈ X∗} is the mapping of X∗ in the objective space. Therefore, to solve a MOP, the goal is to find X∗ and Pf of the problem.
In Algorithm 1, the input parameters include the exponential coupling rate λ, the coupling width w, the preset decoding delay d, and the size of B. Differential evolution parameters are also specified, including the population size Np, maximum generations Gmax, mutation scale factor F ∈ [0, 1], and crossover constant pc ∈ [0, 1]. VII. CONCLUSION
In this letter, we proposed an optimization method to design good finite-length P-anytime codes. Specifically, we analyzed the finite-length performance of P-anytime codes and derived two performance metrics: the delay-exponent and the anytime mean parameter. Using these metrics, we proposed a multi-objective optimization algorithm to determine protographs that can have both large delay-exponent and large anytime mean parameters. Through simulations, we demonstrated that for a given exponential coupling rate λ (i.e., under the same coding complexity condition), our proposed P-anytime codes yield better finite-length performance than the prior-art anytime codes. This improvement holds true for both BEC and BI-AWGN channels, even with a block length as short as 40 bits.

We simulate these P-anytime codes with finite block lengths over a BEC in Fig. 3 and a BI-AWGN channel in Fig. 4. Recall that the reliability of anytime coding hinges on the performance of each individual block within an anytime-code sequence, our simulation results focus on block m5 of each code. This choice can mitigate the impact of irregularities at the beginning of the coupled chain, ensuring steady-state results. To observe the decoding delays up to dmax = 50, we simulate t = 5, . . . , dmax + 5. Each code has w = 60 (i.e., t < w), and we set M = 20 for C1λ and C2λ, M = 10 for C3λ, so as to ensure a uniform block length of 40 bits. As observed from these figures, the codes with λ = 0.1 (i.e., C10.1, C20.1, and C30.1) show similar performance decay rates at small decoding delays (d ≤ 5) due to their identical α. However, as d increases, codes with a relatively large γ, such as C20.1 and C30.1, outperform the benchmark C10.1 and do not have erasure floors until a bit erasure rate of 10−7. For the codes with λ = 0.3, although C30.3 has a slightly smaller α, its performance degradation can be negligible when d is relatively small. Furthermore, its larger γ allows it to outperform the benchmark C10.3 by approximately one order of magnitude when d is relatively large. The performance trends of the codes with λ = 0.5 are also consistent with the values of α and γ given in Table II, where the proposed optimized P-anytime codes achieve equal or better performance compared to the benchmark codes in the whole d region.
