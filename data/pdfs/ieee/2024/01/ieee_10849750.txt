Abstract—The use of Artificial Intelligence (AI) techniques 
and algorithms increases the last few years at all layers of 
wireless signal processing chain, paving the path towards 6th 
Generation (6G) networks. In this work, we focus on physical 
layer and we examine the use of Log – Likelihood Ratio Neural 
Network (LLRNnet), at the well – known decoding and 
demodulation receiver processes. Digital Video Broadcasting 
(DVB) – S.2 Satellite communications standard covers a variety 
of modulation and channel coding rates combinations at 
physical layer. Our work analyzes and compares Packet Error 
Rate (PER) performance of all these combinations for three 
decoding options: Neural Network (NN), log – MAP, max – log 
– MAP. Additionally, we examine 3 hidden layer sizes. Our 
analysis shows that for 16 simulation scenarios derived from the 
DVB-S.2 standard, NN and log – MAP exhibit nearly the same 
PER, verifying the use of NN as a potential decoding option with 
negligible loss, as compared to traditional options. Finally, the 
effect of modulation scheme, coding rate and NN size on PER is 
investigated. 
Keywords—AI, signal processing, physical layer, LLR neural 
network, log – MAP, max – log – MAP, DVB – S.2, PER, 6G. 
I. INTRODUCTION 
Recent 
advancements 
in 
5th 
Generation 
(5G) 
communications require changes to traditional physical layer 
components. In 5G systems, packet sizes are 100 times larger 
than those in 4th Generation (4G) systems and 5G applications 
impose strict delay requirements [1, 2]. As we move towards 
6G mobile communications technology, packet sizes are 
expected to grow exponentially. Additionally, 5G systems 
handle a significantly higher number of independent parallel 
streams compared to 4G. These factors have driven substantial 
modifications to the communication stack, particularly within 
physical layer. 
  In recent years, the rapid advancement of AI has sparked 
significant research interest in applying NNs to channel 
decoding [3, 4, 5], which represents the deployment of 
machine learning techniques for efficient universal soft 
demodulation in channel decoding operation. NNs, known for 
their ability to effectively approximate functions, are a natural 
and advantageous tool for training to replicate the 
functionality of demapping decoding algorithms. These 
algorithms can be either the impractical exact log – MAP, the 
less complex max – log – MAP, or other expert – based 
demapping ones. An LLRNnet offers an effective approach to 
achieve the desired demodulation performance [6]. For 
Quadrature Amplitude Modulation (QAM) constellations 
LLRNnet closely replicates the exact log – MAP algorithm, 
while significantly reducing computational complexity. 
Additionally, in [7] deep learning is applied to enhance the 
Belief Propagation (BP) algorithm for linear block codes. 
Subsequently, in [8] a combination of Convolutional Neural 
Networks (CNN) with BP algorithm is proposed. In [9] a 
shallow fully connected NN is utilized to assist the 
Normalized Minimum – Sum (NMS) algorithm by learning its 
normalization factor, significantly improving the performance 
of traditional NMS without adding complexity. Finally, in 
[10] the neural BP decoder is introduced, along with two 
training 
sample optimization algorithms 
to 
enhance 
robustness. 
  In this paper we propose a soft demodulation architecture 
with a NN designed to learn this desired soft demodulation 
scheme. The main process of the NN is to convert a complex 
symbol into real – valued LLRs of its transmitted bits. Thus, 
the main and original contributions of this work, are as 
follows:  
• 
In a holistic 5G/6G Communication model approach, 
we propose the use of a NN for decoding at the 
receiver side (LLRNnet). 
• 
Through simulation we optimize PER, considering 
different parameters of the simulation model. In fact, 
we examine all 78 combinations of modulation 
schemes and channel coding rates of DVB – S.2 
standard, along with the 3 NN hidden layer sizes. 
• 
Focusing on 16 PER performance improvement 
scenarios, we investigate two different cases: 1. 
Comparison in terms of decoding options (i.e. NN, 
log – MAP, max – log – MAP) and 2. Comparison 
in terms of modulation scheme, coding rate and NN 
hidden layer size. 
II. DVB – S.2 STANDARD 
DVB – S.2 standard has been developed to provide 
optimal modulation and channel coding rate combinations for 
various Satellite applications. It is optimized for broadband 
satellite applications, including Broadcast Services for digital 
multi – program television and High Definition Television 
(HDTV) [11]. It represents a highly flexible standard which 
supports a wide range of Satellite applications, with the 
following key features: Firstly, a versatile input stream adapter

another feature of this standard is a robust error correction-

combined with Bose Chaudhuri Hocquenghem (BCH) codes 
[11, 12, 13].  
LDPC codes can achieve extremely low error rates near 
channel capacity by using a low complexity iterative decoding 
algorithm. The outer BCH coding is used to correct sparse 
errors, which are introduced by the LDPC decoder. LDPC and 
BCH codes combination enables near error free performance 
between 0.7 dB and 1 dB from the Shannon limit depending 
on the transmission mode (Additive White Gaussian Noise – 
AWGN – channel and modulation constrained Shannon 
limit). Thirdly, another feature of this standard is a variety of 
code rates, ranging from 1/4 to 9/10, tailored to specific 
applications; 
four 
constellations 
providing 
spectral 
efficiencies from 2 bit/s/Hz to 5 bit/s/Hz, optimized for non – 
linear transponder operations; three spectrum shaping options 
with roll – off factors of 0.35, 0.25, and 0.20. Fourthly, 
Adaptive Coding and Modulation (ACM) capability, which 
permits frame – by – frame optimization of coding and 
modulation.  
III. SYSTEM MODEL  
We consider the communication system mode, as depicted 
in Figure 1 that is composed as stream processing loop. The 
first part of the for – loop simulates the system. The simulator 
encodes each frame using BCH and LDPC encoders as inner 
and outer codes, respectively. Subsequently, the encoded bits 
pass through an interleaver. Then, the modulator maps the 
interleaved bits to symbols from the predefined constellation. 
The modulated symbols pass through an AWGN channel and 
the demodulator uses an approximate log – likelihood 
algorithm to obtain soft bit estimates. The LDPC decoder 
decodes the deinterleaved soft bit values and generates hard 
decisions. Finally, the BCH decoder works on these hard 
decisions to create the final estimate of the received frame. 
The second part of the for – loop collects performance 
measurements such as Bit Error Rate (BER) or PER. 
We also assume that a modulated symbol b (a M – 
dimensional signal) is transmitted by the AWGN channel of 
Figure 1. On the transmitter side, the process of precoding 
exists precoding for transmission over Single Input Single 
Output (SISO) systems, mapping to orthogonal frequency 
division multiplexing (OFDM), digital – to – analogue 
conversion (DAC) and the assignment on a radio carrier. On 
the receiver side, the mobile channel may encapsulate, e.g., 
analogue – to – digital conversion (ADC), conversion to 
baseband, synchronization, filtering, OFDM demodulation 
and channel estimation/equalization with well – known 
methodologies from published literature, such as Minimum 
Mean Squared Error (MMSE) equalizing [14]. In this work we 
focus on the soft demodulation of the received symbols 
(LLRs) on the receiver side.

A. Scenarios performance comparison in terms of decoding 
options (NN, log – MAP, max – log – MAP) 
At Figure 2 we can see the PER performance of the 
simulated system vs Es/No ratio in dB for scenarios 1 and 2 
from Table 1. For each scenario we consider all three decoding 
options: NN, log – MAP, max – log – MAP. For both 
scenarios we observe that the PER performance of NN and log 
– MAP decoding algorithm is nearly the same, verifying the 
use of NN as a decoding option with negligible performance 
loss compared to traditional and well – known decoding 
options. As expected, for scenario 1 there is a performance 
gain of 0.02 dB at a PER of 10-1 when log – MAP or NN is 
used, compared to max – log – MAP. Similarly, for scenario 
2 the gain is smaller (nearly 0.015 dB). At Figure 3 scenarios 
3 and 4 from Table 1 are examined. As in Figure 2, for 
scenarios 3 and 4 there is a performance gain of nearly 0.02 
dB at a PER of 10-1 when NN and log – MAP decoding are 
used, compared to max – log – MAP. 
Figure 4 shows the PER performance of the simulated 
system for scenarios 5 and 6 from Table 1. Again, for both 
scenarios, we observe that the PER performance of NN and 
log – MAP decoding algorithm is similar. Furthermore, for 
scenario 5 there is a performance gain of 0.01 dB at a PER of 
10-1 when log – MAP or NN are used, compared to the other 
decoding option. Similarly, for scenario 6 the gain is slightly 
larger (nearly 0.015 dB). Figure 5 simulates scenarios 7 and 8. 
Like before, for scenario 7 there is a performance gain of 
0.016 dB at a PER of 5x10-1 when log – MAP or NN are used, 
compared to max – log – MAP. On the other hand, for scenario 
8 the gain is larger (nearly 0.03 dB). The reason for the larger 
gain is that a different modulation scheme is used. 
At Figure 6 we can see the PER performance of the 
simulated system for scenarios 9 and 10. For both scenarios 
we observe that the PER performance of NN and log – MAP 
is nearly the same. For scenario 9 we notice a performance 
gain of 0.025 dB at a PER of 10-1 when log – MAP or NN is 
used, compared to max – log – MAP. It must be mentioned 
that the corresponding gain at a PER of 10-2 is increased to 
0.04 dB. Subsequently, our analysis shows that for scenario 
10 the performance gain is 0.013 dB at a PER of 10-1 and 0.017 
at a PER of 10-2. 
Figure 7 presents the PER performance of the simulated 
system for scenarios 11 and 12. For scenario 11 we notice a 
performance gain of 0.012 dB at a PER of 10-2 when log – 
MAP or NN is used, compared to max – log – MAP. For 
scenario 10 the performance gain is 0.01 dB at a PER of 10-2 
and increases to 0.013 dB at a PER of 10-3. 
At Figure 8 the PER performance of scenarios 13 and 14 
is simulated. At scenario 13 there is a performance gain of 
0.025 dB at a PER of 10-3 when log – MAP or NN are used, 
compared to the other decoding option. Similarly, for scenario 
14 the gain is slightly larger (nearly 0.035 dB) at a PER of 10-
2. Figure 9 simulates scenarios 15 and 16. Like before, for 
scenario 15 there is a performance gain of 0.016 dB at a PER 
of 10-2 when log – MAP or NN are used, compared to max – 
log – MAP. The same is the observation for scenario 16.

VI. CONCLUSIONS 
6G mobile communications and AI represent two 
interrelated technologies the last few years: it is now clear that 
AI will replace many traditional signal processing elements at 
physical layer transmitter – receiver chain. Two of these 
elements 
are 
modulation/demodulation 
and 
channel 
encoding/decoding.  
DVB – S.2 Satellite communications standard specifies 
different combinations of modulation schemes and coding 
rates for BCH and LDPC coding. At the receiver physical 
layer, instead of using the traditional decoding algorithms of 
log – MAP and max – log – MAP, we investigate the 
possibility of decoding using a NN and particularly LLRNnet, 
as defined in [15].  
Our analysis investigates 78 simulation scenarios 
considering the different modulation schemes and coding 
rates of DVB – S.2 together with 3 NN hidden layer sizes. We 
concentrate only on 16 scenarios which exhibit NN PER 
performance improvement compared to the other two 
decoding options (log – MAP and max – log – MAP). Our 
analysis of the simulation results in terms of decoding options 
shows a PER performance improvement of up to 0.04 dB 
when NN or log – MAP is used compared to max – log – 
MAP. Thus, our approach verifies the use of NN as a decoding 
option with negligible performance loss compared to 
traditional decoding options. 
Our research also shows that increasing the NN hidden 
layer size exhibits PER performance improvement, whereas 
the smaller the coding rate the better the performance. A final 
remark is that an increase in coding gain, results in increase of 
performance gain.  
As future work we plan to investigate the effect of the NN 
hidden layers depth (i.e. the total number of layers) on the total
