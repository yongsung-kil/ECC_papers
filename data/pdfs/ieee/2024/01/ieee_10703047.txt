ABSTRACT The tremendous progress of 5G technology has transformed the landscape of the Internet of Things (IoT), allowing for fast data speeds, low delay, and widespread connection that is crucial for a variety of applications, including smart cities and industrial automation. In the context of 5G enabled IoT networks, colored noise introduces varying levels of interference across different frequency bands, which can significantly degrade the performance of 5G LDPC decoding. This paper presents a novel Deep learning approach for 5G channel LDPC code decoding tailored for next-generation IoT applications. The proposed method integrates an Iterative Normalized Min-Sum (NMS) algorithm with a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to enhance the performance of LDPC decoding in the presence of colored noise, a common interference in real-world communication channels. Through extensive simulations and analysis, our approach demonstrates a significant performance improvement, achieving a 3.8 dB enhancement at a Bit error rate of 10−6. This is achieved by accurately estimating and mitigating channel noise, thereby ensuring reliable data transmission for critical IoT applications. The findings indicate that our approach to decoding technique not only enhances error correction capabilities but also adapts to varying channel conditions, optimizing IoT network performance and efficiency. This research contributes a robust solution to the challenges posed by colored noise in 5G-enabled IoT networks, promoting the deployment of more reliable and efficient IoT systems.

II. INTRODUCTION TO KEY CONCEPTS A. 5G LDPC CODES Quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes have been selected as the channel coding scheme for data channels in the 5G New Radio (NR) specifications. This choice was made primarily to guarantee high throughput and low latency [11]. The QC LDPC codes utilize two fundamental base graph matrices (BGMs), Hb1 and Hb2, and incorporate 51 distinct lifting sizes zc to provide a diverse set of code rates [11]. Specifically, Hb1 dimension is 46 × 68, enabling it to support code rates from 1/3 to 8/9. Meanwhile, Hb2 of dimension 42 × 52, is designed to support code rates from 1/5 to 2/3. The base graph matrix Hb is used to construct the parity check matrix (PCM) H [19].
In the 5G standard, a binary (N , K ) LDPC code is defined by the null space of its Parity Check Matrix (PCM) H . The PCM has dimensions M ×N over the Galois Field GF(2). The

PCM can alternatively be represented visually as a bipartite Tanner graph [20], [21]. This representation consists of M parity or check nodes (CNs) that correspond to the rows of H , and N bit or variable nodes (VNs) that correspond to the columns of H . The decoding method for LDPC codes is performed iteratively using a message-passing algorithm on a bipartite Tanner graph [20].
For example, let’s examine a (6, 2) LDPC code. The parity check matrix H and its corresponding Tanner graph can be seen in Figure 1. The nodes are connected to one other by bidirectional edges, which are determined by the placements of ones in the PCM of the code. The channel logarithmic-likelihood ratio (LLR) of the s-th symbol xs in a code word is obtained from the s-th received channel symbol ys at vs, and it is represented as Lvs .

B. COLORED NOISE
In an elementary communication system model centered on channel coding, a message block vector m, comprising K bits, is encoded to generate a codeword c. This codeword is a N bit vector obtained by appending (N − K ) parity bits to the initial message block. Utilizing Binary Phase Shift Keying (BPSK) modulation, the modulator produces a modulated symbol vector x. This vector is then transmitted through the communication channel, resulting in the received signal vector y = x + η, where η represents the noise vector added by the channel, also consisting of N bits.
In environments where noise is correlated, such as in the case of colored noise, an Auto-regressive (AR) process of order 1 is frequently utilized to model the noise characteristics. In this AR(1) model, each noise value in the time series is a linear function of its immediate predecessor, combined with a white noise component. Consequently, this results in a noise spectrum that deviates from a flat (uniform) distribution, exhibiting frequency-dependent characteristics. The equation (4) illustrates that the correlation between any two noise values ηi and ηj decays exponentially with the distance |i − j| between their respective time indices. The structure of the covariance matrix reflects this exponential decay, ensuring that closer samples in time are more strongly correlated than those further apart. To elaborate:
1) Diagonal elements of T when (i = j): Tii = σn2 indicates that the variance of each noise sample ηi is σn2. This is because the diagonal elements represent the variance of the noise samples themselves, incorporating the full variance of the white noise component.
2) Off-Diagonal elements of T when (i ̸= j):
• The off-diagonal terms Tij illustrate how the correlation between two different noise samples decreases as the distance |i − j| increases. The correlation coefficient β raised to the power of the absolute difference |i − j| determines the strength of this correlation.
• If β is near 1, the correlations decay slowly, meaning that past noise values have a strong influence on future values, even if they are further apart.
• Conversely, if β is near 0, the correlations decay rapidly, resulting in noise samples ni and nj being almost independent when separated by a larger distance.
This framework shows how the covariance matrix captures both the variance of individual noise samples and the way correlations between different samples decrease with increasing separation, depending on the value of the correlation coefficient β.

D. CALCULATING LLR WHEN COLORED NOISE IS PRESENT IN A FADING CHANNEL
In the context of BPSK modulation, where the transmitted symbols xs can take values of either +1 or −1, the presence of a fading channel modifies the received signal. At a given time s, the received signal ys can be described by the equation:

ys = hs · xs + ns.

(11)

In this expression, hs represents the complex fading coefficient at time s, typically modeled as hs = |hs|ejθs , where |hs| is the amplitude and θs is the phase shift introduced by the channel. The term ns denotes the noise at time s, which is characterized as colored noise following an AR(1) process rather than white noise.
The complex nature of the fading coefficient hs influences both the magnitude and phase of the received signal, making the computation of the LLR more challenging compared to scenarios without fading.
When considering multiple time samples, the received signal vector y over N samples is given by:

F. BGM OF 5G NR LDPC CODES The BGM Hb [19] is used to define and construct the PCM H for the 5G LDPC code. The dimensions of H and Hb are M × N and mb × nb, respectively, with N = nb × zc and M = mb ×zc. The lifting size, denoted as zc, is used to expand the entries of Hb into H by employing a zc × zc square submatrix.
In Hb, there are three types of entries: ‘−1’, ‘0’, and non ‘−1’ values. These entries are expanded in H as follows:
• A ‘−1’ entry in Hb is replaced with a zero matrix of size zc × zc in H .
145676

• A ‘0’ entry in Hb is replaced with an identity matrix of size zc × zc in H .
• A non ‘−1’ entry in Hb is replaced with a circulant permutation matrix I (si,j) in H . This shift value si,j ranges from 1 to zc for all entries.
The subscripts i and j denote the row and column indices of the entries, respectively. The circulant permutation matrix I (si,j) is created by right-shifting each row of the identity matrix by si,j positions. The value of si,j is determined based on the chosen BGM and the set index iLS , as specified in Tables 5.3.2-2 and 5.3.2-3 of the 5G-NR standards 3GPP TS 38.212 [11]. In this study, BGM-1 is constructed using an LDPC code with dimensions 46 × 68 . The message block length is set at 1232 bits, the codeword length at 3808 bits, and the code rate at 1/3. A. A FLOW CHART FOR NMS-CNN DESIGN The flow chart for the NMS-CNN design process is depicted in Figure 3, beginning at the receiver side of the communication system. The process involves the following steps:
1) Signal Reception and LLR Calculation: Upon receiving the signal y, the Log-Likelihood Ratios (LLRs) of the symbols are calculated as Lvs .
2) NMS Decoder Input: The calculated LLR values for all symbols are then provided as input to the NMS decoder, which estimates the symbol vector x.
3) Channel Noise Estimation: Due to decoding errors, the estimated channel noise η does not exactly match the actual channel noise η. The estimated channel noise is given by η = η + e, where e is the noise error vector.
4) CNN Processing: The estimated channel noise η is fed into a trained CNN, which outputs ηc. The CNN leverages the correlation property of the channel noise

3) CONVOLUTION OPERATION IN CNNS
In CNNs, the convolution operation applied to colored noise data involves sliding a filter (kernel) across the input noise vector. For each filter position, a dot product is computed between the filter weights and the corresponding segment of data. Suppose a filter has weights [w1, w2, . . . , wk ] of size k, and at time t, it covers the segment [ηt−k+1, . . . , ηt ]. The convolution at this position is calculated as:

Ct = w1 · ηt−k+1 + w2 · ηt−k+2 + · · · + wk · ηt . (29)

The resulting value, Ct , contributes to one element of the feature map. After computing the convolution, the Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) activation function is applied to the output:

Ut = max(0, Ct ).

(30)

This combination of convolution and ReLU activation effectively highlights regions of autocorrelation in the time series. For an auto-regressive (AR) series where the correlation coefficient β is close to 1, successive values are similar and positive. If the filter weights are designed to detect this similarity (e.g., all positive weights), the convolution Ct at positions where ηt and ηt−1 are both positive will also be positive and significant, thus passing through the ReLU activation. As a result, Ut will be high in regions with strong positive autocorrelation, highlighting these areas in the feature map.

V. CONCLUSION The study presents an Deep learning - driven 5G channel LDPC code decoding approach for next generation IoT applications. The method integrates the NMS algorithm with a CNN to address colored noise interference, improving the reliability and performance of communication systems. The simulation results show a 3.8 dB enhancement at a BER of 10−6, demonstrating its efficacy in maintaining high-quality communication links in noisy environments. This design is suitable for diverse IoT applications, including smart cities, industrial automation, healthcare, and precision agriculture. Future research can further enhance the applicability and performance of the proposed NMS-CNN design, including real-world testing, optimizing energy efficiency, incorporating adaptive learning techniques, exploring other AI models, and integrating the method with emerging 6G features and requirements. Investigating the application of the decoding technique in multi-antenna systems and MIMO configurations can provide insights into its scalability and performance in more complex communication environments.
