ABSTRACT Error-correcting codes for insertion and deletion (ID) errors have attracted considerable attention because of their potential in future communication and storage systems. Existing schemes based on modern coding approaches approach the information-theoretical limits of an ID channel. However, a scheme that universally performs well across various channel impairments, such as Gaussian noise and bit-flipping noise, is yet to be established. In this study, we proposed a novel coding scheme for cascaded ID-noisy channels. The scheme manages the transmission of multiple codewords using a low-density paritycheck code. In the transmitter, a two-stage hierarchical interleaving with the codewords is used, whereas in the receiver, an efficient decoding strategy is used to sequentially estimate the original codewords with a decoding delay. We evaluated the performance of the proposed scheme through achievable information rates, asymptotic analysis, and numerical simulations. The results demonstrated that the proposed scheme achieved universal performance over ID-noisy channels with reduced complexity. Furthermore, to improve performance within the constraints of limited complexity and latency, we proposed an optimization method specifically for the proposed scheme.

I. INTRODUCTION Synchronization problems are increasingly becoming a concern in various systems. Communication and storage systems, including optical wireless communication [2], document exchange [3], and emerging storage technologies such as bitpatterned media [4], racetrack memories [5], and DNA data storage [6], are vulnerable to synchronization errors. These errors have attracted considerable attention in information and communication theory, particularly in the past decade.
Studies on synchronization errors were commenced in the 1960s with significant contributions from Gallager [7], Levenshtein [8], and Dobrushin [9]. Subsequently, studies have investigated various aspects of information theory and error-correction techniques for synchronization errors [10], [11], [12]. These errors are typically represented as channels with insertion and deletion (ID) errors. Two

approaches, namely algebraic/number-theoretic codes and modern capacity-approach codes, have been identified for correcting ID errors.
The algebraic/number-theoretic codes, exemplified by the Varshamov–Tenengolts codes [8], [13], [14], focus on correcting single or a few ID errors. By contrast, in the modern capacity-approach coding [15], [16], [17], [18], [19], [20], [21], relevant to our study, focuses on addressing randomly occurring ID errors. Promising results have been achieved using constructions based on sparse graph codes [15], [16], [17], [18], [19], [20], [21].
Concatenated coding schemes based on outer low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes and inner synchronization codes were proposed in [15], [16], [17], and [18]. Standalone coding schemes without an inner code were introduced in [19], [20], and [21]. In these schemes, a stochastic recovery mechanism based on maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimation is used. The decoder attempts to correct residual errors, usually using an iterative belief-propagation (BP) decoding algorithm. These algorithms are optionally performed iteratively and alternately to enhance decoding accuracy.
For concatenated coding schemes, in [15], the concatenation of outer nonbinary LDPC codes and inner watermark codes was investigated, whereas in [16] and [17], the concatenation of outer binary LDPC codes and inner marker codes was detailed. In the MAP detector, these inner codes identify the positions of drifts by leveraging the nonuniformity in the transmitted bits, which reduces the number of iterations between the detector and decoder. However, the inner codes result in rate loss without specific benefits for various channel impairments such as Gaussian noise and bit-flipping noise. In [18], the concatenation of outer binary LDPC codes and inner trellis codes was investigated. This method corrects ID errors that occur with high probability at low coding rates (below 0.2).
For standalone coding schemes, in [19] and [20], design and construction methods of binary irregular LDPC codes were proposed. These irregular codes achieve excellent performance under target channels. However, these irregular codes exhibit limitations in achieving universal performance against noise errors. Specifically, codes specialized for correcting ID errors render correcting noise errors difficult, and vice versa, because the optimal code profiles vary depending on whether ID or noise errors are dominant [20]. In [21], a coding scheme based on spatially coupled (SC) codes was investigated. Because of their universality, SC codes demonstrate excellent performance against combined ID and noise errors if long block lengths (over one million bits) can be used with a large number of iterations (over one thousand) between the detector and decoder, which increases complexity and reduces throughput.
To the best of our knowledge, coding schemes that universally achieve the theoretical limits of an ID noisy channel is yet to be achived. A comprehensive survey [11] highlights this phenomenon as one of the primary obstacles hindering the application of synchronization-error-correcting codes.
A. CONTRIBUTIONS In this study, we proposed a novel coding approach for an ID noisy channel. Instead of focusing on single-codeword transmission, we considered the transmission of multiple codewords. At the transmitter, we used a two-stage hierarchical interleaving (HI) technique to generate the channel input sequences from the codewords. At the receiver, we used a noniterative detection and decoding method called chained recovery (CR) to sequentially decode the original codewords. In this study, we devised a low-complexity recovery strategy and achieved excellent universal performance over IDnoisy channels. Compared with conventional schemes, the proposed approach demonstrated considerable improvements

The contributions of this study are as follows: • Novel coding approach: We introduced a coding
approach based on hierarchical interleaving (HI) and chained recovery (CR) schemes designed for noisy channels with insertion and deletion (ID) errors. The HI scheme rearranges multiple codewords and incorporates synchronization markers, ensuring minimal rate loss for large codeword sets. The CR scheme sequentially estimates original codewords using a noniterative detection and decoding strategy, using priorly decoded codewords for improved detection. • Universal performance: We proposed AIRs for the proposed system, demonstrating its ability to achieve universal performance over ID-noisy channels, including binary symmetric (BS) channels with ID errors and binary additive white Gaussian noise channels (BI-AWGN) with ID errors. • Optimization techniques for improved performance: We provided novel optimization techniques for the proposed scheme. These techniques enhance AIRs and simultaneously consider the limitations of complexity and latency, enabling superior performance within practical constraints. • Analysis of asymptotic and finite-length performance: We analyzed the asymptotic performance of low-density parity-check (LDPC) and spatially coupled (SC) codes in conjunction with the proposed scheme by using density evolution. Additionally, we showed the finite-length performance of these codes through numerical simulations. These results validate the universal performance of the proposed scheme and its applicability.

A. ID-NOISY CHANNELS Numerous channel models consider ID errors in combination with substitution errors [7], [15], errors caused by AWGN, erasures, or intersymbol interference [23]. In this study, we focused on the two major channel models, namely the Gallager channel model [7] and the Davey–Mackay channel model [15]. In the following, x = (x1, x2, . . . , xn) ∈ {0, 1}n denotes a binary input sequence of length n, and y = (y1, y2, . . . , yn′ ) denotes a corresponding output sequence of length n′, where n′ is a random variable depending on the number of insertions and deletions.
1) GALLAGER CHANNEL MODEL This channel is the concatenation of two subchannels. In the first subchannel, each input bit xk is deleted with a probability of pd , replaced by two random bits (insertions) with a probability of pi, or correctly transmitted with a probability of pt = 1 − pd − pi. Hereafter, we denote pi + pd as pid . In the second subchannel, the outputs from the first subchannel pass through a noisy channel, and the corresponding outputs are observed as y. In this study, we considered the two types of second subchannels. The first subchannel is the BS channel with a crossover probability of ps. The second subchannel is the BI-AWGN channel, in which the k-th output yk is given by yk = (1 − 2y¯k ) + zk , where y¯k denotes the k-th output of the first subchannel, and zk represents the zero-mean Gaussian noise with variance σ 2.
2) DAVEY–MACKAY CHANNEL MODEL In this channel, the input sequence x is considerable to a queue. Each queued bit xk awaiting transmission can undergo one of three possible events. An insertion event occurs with a probability pi, where a random bit is appended to the received sequence, and xk remains in the queue. A deletion event occurs with a probability pd , in which case xk is removed from the queue, and the subsequent bit xk+1 is enqueued. A transmission event occurs with a probability pt = 1 − pid . In this case, xk is flipped with a probability ps, and subsequently xk+1 is enqueued. The procedure finishes when xn leaves the queue, which results in the corresponding channel outputs y being obtained. To prevent an unlimited number of insertions, a maximum number of consecutive insertions, Imax , is imposed in practice.
3) STATE REPRESENTATION For ID-type channels, the received sequence y is typically formulated as the output of a hidden Markov model (HMM) [15], [16], [17], [18], [19], [20], [21]. Generally, a state representation, the so-called drift, is used to incorporate ID events. The drift sk is defined as an event in which both the bits {xi}i∈{1,2,...,k} are transmitted and {yi}i∈{1,2,...,k+sk } are received. Thus, sk = s indicates that the cumulative number of insertions minus the cumulative number of deletions before transmitting xk+1 is equal to s. B. INNER SYNCHRONIZATION AND OUTER DECODING
Here, we describe the standard bitwise MAP recovery scheme for ID-type channels. This scheme consists of two primary stages: MAP detection (synchronization) and outer channel decoding. Throughout this study, we assume the use of a linear block code, a soft input soft output (SISO) detector, and an iterative SISO decoder.
The MAP detection is implemented with the BCJR algorithm [22] (also known as the forward-backward algorithm for HMMs), functioning as a channel equalizer. This algorithm mitigates the effects of drifts (i.e., ID errors) in the received sequence and produces the APPs for each transmitted bit. The APP-LLR can be used as the soft input to the log-domain SISO decoder. The computational complexity of this algorithm for n timesteps is O(n|B|) because it is predominantly related to the number of edges in the trellis diagram.
Based on the APP-LLRs from the detector, the channel decoder finds a valid codeword that maximizes the bit-level APPs and produces extrinsic LLRs. These LLRs are used to extract novel estimates of the a priori probabilities p(xk ) to be used in the new detection round. In this study, a global iteration composed of detection, followed by a single iteration of outer decoding, was performed ℓgl times, after which the internal iterations in the decoding were executed (ℓlo − ℓgl ) times, where ℓgl ≤ ℓlo. In particular, when ℓgl = ℓlo, the scheduling is referred to as multipass decoding, whereas when ℓgl = 1, this is referred to as single-pass decoding. Generally, increasing ℓgl results in a performance gain at the expense of increased complexity and decreased throughput.
The MAP recovery scheme described was used to decode both the concatenated and standalone codes. In concatenated codes, the inner codes provide nonuniform input distributions (i.e., p(xk ) can be biased), which estimates the drift positions even in the initial MAP detection. For example, synchronization markers [16], [17] that are predetermined and periodically inserted into an outer codeword, provide perfect a priori information on their locations. Consequently, a reasonable performance can be attained with a small number of ℓgl, albeit at the cost of rate loss. By contrast, in standalone linear codes, the assumption is p(xk = 0) = p(xk = 1) (i.e., equiprobable) at the initial iteration and a trigger is not established for estimating drift positions. Therefore, numerous of ℓgl is recommended, where extrinsic LLRs from the decoder update a priori probabilities.

VI. CONCLUSION In this study, we proposed a novel coding scheme based on HI and CR schemes for ID-noisy channels. We addressed the challenge of achieving universal performance across various channel impairments, such as Gaussian noise and bit-flipping noise. In the proposed scheme, a two-stage HI is used at the transmitter, where multiple codewords in an LDPC code are rearranged appropriately to generate the transmitted sequences. In the receiver, an efficient decoding strategy based on the CR scheme is used to sequentially estimate the original codewords with a certain decoding delay, using previously decoded codewords to assist in the detection process. First, we evaluated the AIRs of the proposed scheme. The results demonstrated that the proposed scheme achieved universal performance over ID-noisy channels. We presented optimization methods for the proposed scheme to improve performance under the

constraints of limited complexity and latency. Additionally, we analyzed the asymptotic performance of LDPC and SC codes using density evolution. The finite-length performance of these codes was evaluated through numerical simulations, which confirmed the universal performance of the proposed scheme with a recovery strategy. Our findings highlight the effectiveness of the HI and CR schemes in achieving robust and efficient error correction for ID-noisy channels. The proposed coding scheme can enhance the reliability and performance of communication and storage systems in the presence of synchronization and noise errors. Future studies can investigate optimizations and extensions of the proposed scheme for specific application scenarios.
