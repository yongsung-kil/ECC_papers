Efficient Construction of Quasi-Cyclic LDPC Codes With Multiple Lifting Sizes
Huaan Li , Hengzhou Xu, Member, IEEE, Chao Chen , and Baoming Bai , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract— In this letter, we present an efficient method for constructing a family of quasi-cyclic low-density paritycheck (QC-LDPC) codes specified only by one single exponent matrix that can support multiple lifting sizes. Our method adopts the small-lifting-size-first design strategy and the resulting exponent matrix of larger lifting size has non-decreasing girth. Numerical results show that all constituent codes can perform as well as (outperform) the CCSDS (5G) LDPC codes over the AWGN channel with the iterative decoding algorithms. This fact demonstrates the feasibility and validity of our proposed method and makes it competitive in some applications, e.g., enhancing short and medium-length LDPC codes to support longer length.
Index Terms— LDPC, quasi-cyclic, multiple lifting size, girth.
I. INTRODUCTION
QUASI-CYCLIC LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes [1] are an important category of practical LDPC codes due to their better iterative decoding performance in short/medium length and efficient implementation. In the past decades, such codes have been progressively used in many wireless systems, e.g., CCSDS [2] and ongoing 5G communications [3], [4], [5].
Two popular methods for constructing QC-LDPC codes are the protograph-based (PTG-based) method and superposition (SP) construction, and the former has been shown to be one special case of the latter [6], [7]. Most of the existing standard codes belong to the SP-based QC-LDPC codes, including CCSDS LDPC codes [2] and 5G LDPC codes [3]. There are a number of letters tackling the SP construction of QC-LDPC codes from an algebraic point, see, e.g., [8], [9], [10], [11], [12]. And SP method can also be used to design the adjacent matrix of non-binary LDPC codes [13], [14]. SP construction generally involves three matrices and one process including base matrix, exponent matrix, circulant, and matrix dispersion. From a practical standpoint, a commonly used circulant is the circulant permutation matrix (CPM), where its size is called lifting size. Most investigated QC-LDPC codes are based on

Such property is of significance at least regarding the storage complexity [5].
At present, one approach for enhancing the existing 5G LDPC codes for future network is to extend the supported lifting sizes. It is worth mentioning that the design of 5G LDPC code exponent matrices is a large-lifting-sizefirst method, which poses a significant challenge to enhance such codes. Motivated by these facts, one efficient method from cycle perspective for constructing QC-LDPC codes with multiple lifting sizes is proposed in this work. Here the proposed method is a small-lifting-size-first method and thus can be followed to enhance the 5G LDPC codes to support larger lifting sizes and longer length. We finally consider CCSDS and 5G LDPC codes, and provide two design examples to demonstrate the feasibility and validity of the proposed method and designed algorithm. Note that, what our method adopts is a small-lifting-size-first design strategy, which can provide performance guarantee for the shorter constituent codes, to a certain extent. A protograph Gptg is a bipartite graph of relatively small size whose adjacency matrix Bptg = [bk,t] consists of nonnegative integers bk,t corresponding to bk,t parallel edges in Gptg [15]. During the PTG-based construction, the 1st step is to properly select Gptg. The 2nd step is to create L copies of Gptg and permute the edges of copies under some certain rules to connect them into a large bipartite graph, denoted by Gptg(L, L), whose adjacency matrix is denoted by Hptg(L, L). If Hptg(L, L) is sparse, its null space gives an LDPC code, called a PTG-based LDPC code. The 2nd step is commonly referred to the copy-and-permute operation [6], [7]. We see that this method is essentially to construct the Tanner graphs of LDPC codes of larger sizes by expanding a smaller welldesigned protograph with a factor. Moreover, if the used permutations are cyclic, then the resulting codes are QC-LDPC codes.
From the above brief description, we find that both methods include two key ingredients, a base and an expansion of the base. If we illustrate these two methods from the same point of view, either from the graph-theoretic or the algebraic point of view, we see that they are closely related, i.e., there exists a strong connection between the SP-construction and the PTGbased construction. With this PTG/SP connection, we can construct QC-LDPC codes uniformly from one point of view, e.g., algebraic.
The main factors that affect the performance of an LDPC code under the iterative decoding include common structural properties in its Tanner graph [16], e.g., degree distribution, cycle distribution, and girth. Short cycles, especially cycles of length 4, generally degrade the iterative decoding performance of an LDPC code, while the girth refers to the length of shortest cycles and is generally denoted by g. Then we can formulate the multiplicities of cycles in various lengths as one polynomial and get the cycle distribution C(X) = CgXg + Cg+2Xg+2 + Cg+4Xg+4 + · · · . In the following, by saying one cycle distribution is better, we mean that it has larger girth g and fewer short cycles. for any integer d (g1 ⩽ 2d < 2g1), it is certain that the number of Td in P1 is no less than that in P2. But the multiplicity of length-2d cycles in P1 is not sure to exceed that in P2 since the lifting size has become larger. Thus it’s necessary to introduce other metrics while designing P2 of larger lifting size from P1 with (7), e.g., cycle distribution.
We formulate our method as Algorithm 1. Here the search complexity mainly depends on the update of W, i.e., randomly or exhaustively. Evidently, complete coverage for W becomes prohibitively complex for larger a and the number of shift values expectantly adjusted in P1. And the conclusion in Proposition 1 illustrates the non-necessity of exhaustive search for W. That is to say, we can randomly update the weight coefficient matrix W and also obtain P2 with better cycle distribution. This will help greatly narrow down the search space. Thus Nsearch can be assigned from experience and the search is stopped based on the cycle distribution of P2.

In general, there is a trade-off on selecting proper lifting size and the size of base/exponent matrix when designing long QC-LDPC codes with desired code length [18]. Since for small base matrix and large code length, a large lifting size is necessary, which will make the code too structured and degrade the code performance. And if no metric can be referenced, large lifting size also will make the design of exponent matrix a challenging task directly based on the exhaustive search. However, construction for short- and medium-length QC-LDPC codes with good iterative decoding threshold has been very mature by combining the computeraided protograph EXIT [19] and algebraic method [7], [8]. Thus, our method provides an efficient way to construct long QC-LDPC codes based on short and medium length codes.

A. Example 1: CCSDS LDPC Codes
QC-LDPC codes recommended by CCSDS for the deep space communication [2] support code rates R ∈ {1/2, 2/3, 4/5} and information lengths K ∈ {1024, 4096, 16384}, respectively. These codes have their exclusive exponent matrices, i.e., 9 matrices in total. But such codes have one distinctive characteristic that the codes with same code rate have identical base matrices.
For simplicity, we directly take the case of R = 1/2 as the example and desire to design a single exponent matrix that can simultaneously support three codes of K ∈ {1024, 4096, 16384}, the relevant lifting sizes of which are 128, 512, and 2048, respectively. For the sake of fairness in terms of decoding threshold, we directly consider the 12 × 20 base matrix of R = 1/2 CCSDS codes. We first design an exponent matrix for (K, L) = (1024, 128), and then successively achieve the exponent matrices for (K, L) = (4096, 512) and (K, L) = (16384, 2048), respectively, by following Algorithm 1 with a = 4. Finally we obtain Pdes,1 = [pi,j ]0⩽i<12,0⩽j<20 as in Fig. 1. The 2048-fold CPM-dispersion of Pdes,1 results in the PCM of K = 16384. For K ∈ {4096, 1024} and corresponding lifting size L, the PCM is given by L-fold CPM-dispersion of P′ = [p′i,j ]0⩽i<12,0⩽j<20, where pi′,j = −1 if pi,j = −1, and p′i,j = pi,j (mod L) otherwise.
