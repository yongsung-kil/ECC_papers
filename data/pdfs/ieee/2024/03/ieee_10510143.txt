Small Elementary Trapping Sets Elimination of Girth-8 QC-LDPC Codes

Aleksei Ovinnikov, Aleksei Kharin, Konstantin Zavertkin, Sergei Ganin, Nikita Vinogradov Sphera Lab Limited Liability Company Ryazan, Russia
ovinnikov.a.a@labsphera.ru, kharin.a.v@labsphera.ru, zavertkin.k.n@labsphera.ru, ganin.s.v@labsphera.ru, vinogradov.n.s@labsphera.ru

Abstract—In this letter, we supplement a known technique to design quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes with girth 8 and variable node degrees 4. Using the technique of combining cycles of length 4 in the code’s Tanner graph small trapping sets are avoided. The simulation results are presented to demonstrate the superiority of the developed codes in comparison with similar codes in the literature by the criterion of minimum length with a slight loss in energy efficiency
Keywords—Quasi-Cyclic Low-Density Parity-Check codes, girth, normal graph, trapping set.
I. INTRODUCTION
To date, structured LDPC codes have found wide application in various communication and data transmission applications, information storage and bioinformatics. Such codes are usually obtained by cyclic lifting of the base matrix or protograph. As a result of this procedure, a parity check matrix or Tanner graph of Quasi-Cyclic (QC) LDPC code is synthesized [1] [10].
A large number of research papers concerning QC-LDPC codes are devoted to regular constructions for which the column degree of the parity check matrix is fixed and usually not large dv = 3, 4, ..., 6. Such restrictions on the dv parameter are important when it comes to hardware or software implementation of decoders whose performance is directly related to the density of the H-matrix. In addition, to obtain a decent energy coding gain (ECG), the girth of the Tanner graph is limited from below by the value gmin = 6. At the same time, a number of papers indicate [10], [11] that increasing the girth to eight is a good solution from the point of view of eliminating a number of harmful trapping sets that negatively affect the process of iterative decoding. In this paper, the emphasis is on a narrow family of regular QC-LDPC codes at dv = 4. In addition, the protograph should not contain vertices connected by parallel edges. In this case, it has dimensions [dv, dc] and is called fully connected, where dc is the weight of any check vertex, and z is the lifting factor. The QC-LDPC code parity check matrix consists of circulants (I(pij)) – square, diagonal, binary matrices whose shifts are described by the exponential matrix P = [pij], i ∈ [1, dv], j ∈ [1, dc]. Trapping sets (TSs) are often classified by their size a and the number of odd-degree check nodes b in their induced subgraph, and are said to belong to the (a, b) class. It is often the case that TSs with smaller a and b values are more harmful. It is also well-known that the most problematic TSs responsible for the error floor of variable-regular LDPC codes over the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel are leafless elementary TSs (LETSs) [13], [14]. They are characterized by two additional properties:
1. Each variable node in the LETSs is connected to at least two degree-2 check nodes.

Let’s consider the format of the compact graph representation of the LETSs, which will be used later in the text. In the literature, it is called a normal graph, but in comparison with the original definition given in the source [14], an even more convenient and visual representation is possible. An empty variable node has no connections with unsatisfied checks. Figure 1 shows an example of converting a bipartite graph to normal and vice versa.
In addition to the method of compact and visual graphical representation of individual LETS, the presence or absence of relationships between different classes of such objects is important. A convenient way to represent such dependencies is child-parent with some pre-designated transaction. As a universal transaction, one should use the dot-extension of an arbitrary LETS by one new node with m edges, where m ∈ [2, b]. An example of the extension of a modified normal graph LETS (4, 8) is shown in Figure 2 for a cycle of length 8.

Theorem 1. In a protograph QC-LDPC code with g = 8 and dv = 4, if there is no Type-A 8-cycle then the code is free of LETSs (8, 0) and (8, 2) respectively.
Proof. Type A cycles give maximum variability when the graph is lifted with new nodes and form the LETSs theorems specified in the condition. To prove sufficiency, it is necessary to consider the process of lifting the cycle 8 Type B by a sequential set of variable nodes. Figure 5 shows the process of obtaining the LETSs theorem specified in the condition from a single cycle.
Let’s take a closer look at LETS (7, 4), which is a common ancestor for target trapping sets, shown in Figure 6. We will introduce a unique sequence number for each vertex of the Tanner graph that is part of the LETS (7,4), and also number the row indexes of the base matrix from zero to three. It should be additionally noted that any variable node in a fully connected graph is connected to all checks.
Let the check nodes with numbers 2, 9, 12 and 18 have the following indices: 0, 2, 2, 1. In this case, for nodes with numbers 7 and 14, 2 different states are possible, namely 7 ↔ (3, 1), 14 ↔ (0, 3). Given the full connectivity of the graph, the total number of combinations is limited to three: 7 ↔ 3, 14 ↔ 0(I); 7 ↔ 1, 14 ↔ 0(II); 7 ↔ 1, 14 ↔ 3(III). Let’s consider each of them sequentially and in more detail.

(I) Assign possible block row indexes to the check nodes with ordinal numbers 8 and 13. Each of them can be assigned a value of 1 or 2, because 0 and 3 are already occupied. Let 8 ↔ 1, 13 ↔ 2. In that case 4 ↔ 3, 10 ↔ 1, 6 ↔ 0, and 16 ↔ 3 without alternatives. With this arrangement, a Type A cycle is formed along the variable nodes 3, 19, 15 and 11, which contradicts the condition of necessity. The castling of indexes 8 ↔ 2, 12 ↔ 1 leads to a somewhat more complicated, but solvable situation. If the index 0 is assigned to the check node 16, then another Type A cycle is formed for code vertices 11, 15, 19, 17. Therefore, 16 ↔ 3, 6 ↔ 0, 10 ↔ 1, 4 ↔ 3 without alternatives. However, in this case, the code vertex 15 turns out to be connected through the same check with nodes 1 and 11, which cannot be in fully connected graphs with the weight of the circulant 1. Thus, for case (I), all possible indexing is considered and none of them is acceptable for the existence of LETS (7, 4). Let’s move on to the next step. (II) Assign possible block row indexes to the test vertices with ordinal numbers 8 and 13. Each of them can be assigned values 2 or 3, because 0 and 1 are already occupied. Let 8 ↔ 3, 13 ↔ 2, then when 4 ↔ 1, 10 ↔ 3, and none of the remaining options for indexing check nodes with numbers 6 and 16 is unacceptable due to the formation of a circulant with a weight of 2, which is not present in the QC-LDPC class under consideration. The castling of assignment indices 8 ↔ 2, 13 ↔ 3 leads to an unambiguous comparison of the numbers of test vertices and indices remaining in the graph, namely, 16 ↔ 0, 6 ↔ 3, 4 ↔ 1, 10 ↔ 3. We get a forbidden situation for the code node 15. It remains to consider the final version of indexing vertices 7 and 14. (III) The logic of indexing the test vertices with ordinal numbers 8 and 13 remains unchanged. Let 8 ↔ 2, 13 ↔ 0, then for 16 ↔ 3, 6 ↔ 0, 10 ↔ 1, 4 ↔ 3, which leads to the formation of a cycle of length 8 Type A over variable nodes 1, 5, 11, 17. And finally, after the index permutation 8 ↔ 0, 13 ↔ 2 with 4 ↔ 1, we get a Type A cycle over code vertices 1, 3, 11, 5. If 4 ↔ 3, 10 ↔ 1, 6 ↔ 0, 16 ↔ 3, then we get a code that is unacceptable for existence in this class the top. As a result of the analysis, all possible combinations of check nodes are considered and none of the options allows to obtain LETS (7, 4) if the necessary condition of the theorem is met. Changing the indexing of the check nodes of the outer loop cannot affect the final result due to the symmetry of the graph shown in Figure 6. Due to the fact that, for objective reasons, the existence of LETS (7, 4) in the absence of cycles of length 8 Type A is impossible, as well as the fact that the corresponding graph is a direct ancestor for LETS (8, 0) and (8, 2), they also do not exist. I.e., the formulation of the theorem is true.

V. CONCLUSION
The paper considers a class of regular QC-LDPC codes with a girth of 8 and a column weight of dv = 4. A modification has been proposed for them, which makes it possible to eliminate a number of harmful LETS according to Theorem 1. The quantitative and qualitative result of the described innovation is a significant reduction in the minimum size of the circulant size compared to the performance of [11] as well as the ECG in relation to codes with a girth of 8 without additional optimizations. Thus, in practice, this modification can be useful for high-speed regular QC-LDPC codes.
