Non-Iterative MSNN Decoder for Image Transmission over AWGN Channel using LDPC

Abstract—The iterative nature of existing Low-Density ParityCheck (LDPC) decoding algorithms poses a significant obstacle to achieving fast reconstruction of received images with a low error rate. These decoders rely on probabilistic values, which necessitates an iterative approach. In this study, a novel approach to decoding LDPC codes that involves using a multilayer shallow neural network (MSNN) is proposed. By leveraging machine learning algorithms, the MSNN decoder avoids the iterative nature of existing decoders. LDPC construction Mackay Neel (MN) LDPC construction and Quasi Cyclic (QC) has been adopted to transmit the image over Additive White Gaussian Noise (AWGN). The performance of MSNN decoder against existing algorithms such as Bit-Flipping (BF), Belief Propagation (BP), and Sum-Product Algorithm (SPA) has compared. The experiments demonstrate that the MSNN decoder outperforms existing decoders by approximately 102 at 3 dB Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR). Evaluated the reconstructed image using Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR) and Entropy for both SPA and MSNN decoders. The results show that the MSNN decoder performs exceptionally well, even under qualitative analysis of the reconstructed image. Overall, the findings suggest that the proposed MSNN decoder offers a promising solution for fast and accurate reconstruction of received images with low error rates.
Index Terms—Image Transmission, LDPC Decoder, MacKayNeel, Multilayer-Shallow Neural Network, Quasi Cyclic, SumProduct Algorithm.

F. Training of MSNN
The process begins by converting non-binary numbers into a binary message stream of length m, which is then encoded using the QC-LDPC Generator matrix to create a code word. Next, the BPSK modulation technique is adopted to modulate the code word, resulting in a BPSK-NRZ signal that is transmitted over the AWGN channel with varying SNR values. On the receiving end, the signal is demodulated and LLR values are calculated using (4). To train the MSNN network, an input data set and a target data set are created, and the network adjusts its weights and biases after training for the target defined by binary values.
The training samples contained image pixel values ranging from 0 to 255, which are represented by 8-bit. This process occurred as we separated the RGB planes and converted the image to grayscale. Given this information, during the neural network training process, the input set initially ranged from 0 to 255 values, which were converted into 8-bit streams starting from zero. Subsequently, these streams were expanded to 16-bit lengths by padding zeros to align with an 8 × 16 parity check matrix. After constructing a code word, a 32-bit stream was generated and transmitted over the AWGN channel using binary phase shift keying (BPSK) modulation. Upon reception, the BPSK demodulated signal was used to calculate the log-likelihood ratio (LLR), which was then employed to reconstruct the input set. For the target set, a 16-bit binary stream was utilized corresponding to each pixel value. In terms of signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) considerations during training, values of 0, 2, 5, and 7 dBs were employed. The distributions of the input and target sets were allocated as follows: 70% for training, 15% for testing, and 15% for validation checks.

V. CONCLUSION This study presents a comprehensive analysis of the statistical performance measures of LDPC decoders, Bit-Flipping, MP-BP, MP-SPA, and MSNN and their respective performance indicators. Initially, the error performance curve was hypothesized for all decoders over AWGN channel for 10000 frames.

The results indicate that the BER for the MSNN decoder is comparatively inferior to that of other decoders by a factor of 10−3 at 3dB and 10−1 at 7dB.
Moreover, quantitative and statistical evaluations of the reconstructed images were carried out to assess the image reproduction quality via MSNN and MP-SPA at 9dB SNR when the original image was cloned. The statistical analysis revealed that the entropy and PSNR of the reconstructed image obtained through MSNN resembled the original image, and at 9dB, the information held was explicitly identical to that of the original image. These findings support the quantitative investigation results. The PSNR further demonstrated that the ideal value for a lossy compressed image is roughly 30dB to 50dB, and this was achieved at 7dB.
Overall, the proposed MSNN decoder performed excellently in all experiments, indicating a low degree of BER and favorable pioneering investigations. These results provide valuable insights into the performance of the two decoders and their respective statistical measures. The multilayer shallow neural network decoder exhibits superior noise immunity compared to other LDPC decoders due to several key factors. These include its ability to perform non-linear mappings with just one hidden layer, enabling it to handle complex relationships between input and output data during LDPC decoding. Additionally, its learnable parameters, such as weights and biases, can be adjusted during training to minimize decoding errors, making it adaptable to varying noise levels and channel conditions. Moreover, its parallel processing capability allows it to process multiple inputs simultaneously, speeding up the decoding process significantly compared to traditional sequential algorithms. The hidden layer of the neural network acts as a feature extractor, automatically learning relevant features from the received signal to improve decoding accuracy by filtering out irrelevant noise. Furthermore, its generalization ability enables it to perform well across various noise conditions and
