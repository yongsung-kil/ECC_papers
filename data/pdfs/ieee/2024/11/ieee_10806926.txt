Explicit Constructions of Girth-Eight QC-LDPC
Codes: A Unified Framework Motivated by TDGS
Guohua Zhang∗, Yulin Hu†, Defeng Ren∗, and Yi Fang‡ ∗School of Communications and Information Engineering, Xi’an University of Posts and Telecommunications,
Xi’an, China, zhangghcast@163.com, rdf xidian@126.com †Electronic Information School, Wuhan University, Wuhan, China, yulin.hu@whu.edu.cn ‡School of Information Engineering, Guangdong University of Technology, Guangzhou, China, fangyi@gdut.edu.cn

Abstract—A new search method named two-dimensional greedy search (TDGS) is proposed to progressively determine each element within an exponent matrix, so as to generate quasicyclic (QC) low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes without 4cycles and 6-cycles. By applying four different two-dimensional scanning orders to the TDGS, a design framework is formulated to unify several existing and novel explicit constructions for short (3, L)-regular QC-LDPC codes without 4-cycles and 6-cycles. Through equivalence analysis between the exponent matrices greedily found by the TDGS and directly defined by explicit constructions, novel girth properties of (and connections between) these existing explicit constructions are also revealed.
Index Terms—cycle, greedy search, low-density parity-check code, quasi-cyclic, unified framework.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are a powerful channel coding scheme that can approach theoretical limit performance by using low-complexity iterative decoding algorithms. Quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes [1], as an extraordinary category of LDPC codes facilitating hardware implementation, have attracted increasing attention from theoretical research [2], [3] and engineering applications [4]. Reducing short cycles and increasing girth (length of the shortest cycles) is an important way to improve performance of QC-LDPC codes [5]–[9]. According to whether one uses computer search, the methods of improving girth can be roughly divided into two categories: search-based methods [10]–[13] and explicit methods [5], [14], [15]. Explicit methods use mathematical formulae to define exponent matrices which satisfy girth requirement; however, the design routes of existing explicit methods vary widely [16]–[20], and lack a unified framework, especially for small column weights.
In this paper, a new search approach called two-dimensional greedy search (TDGS) is proposed to generate exponent matrices of QC-LDPC codes without cycles of length up to six. Motivated by the exponent matrices empirically found by the TDGS with four special scanning orders, a design framework for constructing column-weight-three QC-LDPC codes is presented to unify several explicit schemes (including two well-known methods [14], [15] and one new method). All these schemes from the proposed framework can explicitly provide column-weight-three QC-LDPC codes which possess reasonably small lengths and are free of cycles of lengths

up to six. Besides, novel girth properties of these explicit schemes as well as connections between them are revealed. These short QC-LDPC codes can play a crucial role in lowlatency communication scenarios and serve as good starting points for generating long QC-LDPC codes.
II. PRELIMINARIES
A (J, L)-regular LDPC code is defined as the null space of a sparse binary parity-check matrix (PCM) having J (resp., L) ones in each column (resp., row). The PCM of a (J, L)-regular QC-LDPC code is composed of P × P circulants. In this article, we only consider the most commonly used circulant, that is, circulant permutation matrix (CPM). A CPM associated with an integer e (called an exponent) is a binary P ×P matrix, in which each row is a cyclically right-shifted version of the previous row and the first row is an all-zero row except the e (mod P )-th position. Therefore, the PCM H of a (J, L)regular QC-LDPC code is completely determined by a (J, L) exponent matrix E and a CPM size P , and hence it can be represented by H(E, P ).
Two exponent matrices with the same CPM size are called equivalent, if their Tanner graphs are identical to each other upon permutations of check nodes and/or variable nodes. According to the above-mentioned concept of equivalence and Theorem 2.1 in [1], the following lemmas are easily obtained.
Lemma 1: The following operations yield a matrix equivalent to E: (i) Exchanging two rows/columns of E; (ii) Adding a fixed integer to each element in a row/column of E.
Lemma 2: Let x, y and z be arbitrary integers. Let a, b and c be integers such that a < b < c and 2b = a + c. Let P be an integer larger than c−a. If E0 equals [x, y, z; a+x, b+y, c+z] or its transpose, then there exist 8-cycles within H(E0, P ).
It should be pointed out that because there is a submatrix [0 0 0; 0 1 2] or its transpose in each exponent matrix for all constructions of this paper, there exist 8-cycles for these QC-LDPC codes due to Lemma 2.

By employing TDGS with four specific scanning orders, as many types of exponent matrices for (3, L)-regular QC-LDPC codes with girth eight can be designed based on a unified framework. Specifically, four types of exponent matrices are empirically found in this section and theoretically analyzed in Sections IV and V.

V. EQUIVALENCE ANALYSIS BETWEEN TDGS AND EXPLICIT CONSTRUCTIONS
A. Equivalence Analysis of Construction I
The connection between Construction I and TDGS is revealed by the following theorem.
Theorem 2: For any L, the two exponent matrices generated by TDGS and Construction I are exactly the same.
Proof : According to the greedy search method, it is obvious that the first row is an all-zero row, and the second row is [0, 1, · · · , L−1]. Now, we consider the third row by induction. First, it is evident that e(2, 0) = 0. Next, we examine the minimum value of e(2, 1). Obviously, if e(2, 1) ∈ {0, 1}, then 4-cycles occur. Similarly, if e(2, 1) = i where i ∈ {2, · · · , L− 1}, then there are 6-cycles expressed by [e(0, 1) − e(2, 1)] + [e(2, 0) − e(1, 0)] + [e(1, i) − e(0, i)] = 0 (mod P ) where P takes any positive number. On the other hand, e(2, 1) = L will not cause 4-cycles nor 6-cycles. Therefore, the greedy search yields e(2, 1) = L. Assuming that the choice of e(2, n − 1) accords with Construction I, where 2 ≤ n ≤ L − 1, we now prove that the choice of e(2, n) also accords with Construction I.
(1a): the 6-cycle expressed by e(2, i) − 0 + 0 − e(1, j) + e(1, n) − e(2, n) = 0 cannot exist, which means e(2, n) ≠ e(2, i) − e(1, j) + e(1, n), where 0 ≤ j ≤ L − 1, j ≠ i and j ̸= n. (1b): the 4-cycle expressed by e(2, i)−e(1, i)+e(1, n)− e(2, n) = 0 cannot exist, which indicates e(2, n) ≠ e(2, i) − e(1, i) + e(1, n). (1c): the 4-cycle expressed by e(2, i) − 0 + 0 − e(2, n) = 0 cannot exist, which means e(2, n) ̸= e(2, i). Combining (1a), (1b) and (1c), we have
e(2, n) ̸= e(2, i) + e(1, n) − {e(1, 0), · · · , e(1, L − 1)}. (7)

Corollary 2: For the QC-LDPC code generated by Construction III, girth is smaller than eight for any P ≤ e(2, L − 1).
From Corollary 2, Lemma 5 and Eq. (2) in [15], we conclude that Pmin = PLB = e(2, L − 1) + 1; that is to say, girth for Construction III is eight if and only if P ≥ e(2, L − 1) + 1.
D. Equivalence Analysis of Construction IV
The following conjecture has been verified for 4 ≤ L ≤ 30. Conjecture 2: For any L, the two exponent matrices generated by TDGS and Construction IV are exactly the same.
VI. RELATIONS BETWEEN CONSTRUCTION I AND CONSTRUCTION II (III)
A. Equivalence of Constructions I and II
The exponent matrix for Construction II gradually becomes that in Construction I, via the following equivalent transformations: (i) subtracting the second row from each row (elementby-element subtraction) of the exponent matrix, (ii) adding the fixed value L−1 to each element in the first and third rows, and (iii) swapping the first and second rows. Therefore, according to Lemma 1, Constructions II and I yield equivalent exponent matrices for any L.
B. Dual of Constructions I and III
Thanks to Theorems 2 and 3, we have fully revealed that the two apparently completely different methods, Construction I and Construction III, can be regarded as dual constructions to each other, from the perspective of TDGS with two scanning orders (i.e., RbRS and CbCS, both along one direction).
VII. CONCLUSION
Regarding QC-LDPC codes with girth eight, a new search method called TDGS is proposed, from which a design framework is formulated to unify four explicit constructions of (3, L)-regular codes, including two well-known methods and one new method. The main contributions are as follows: (i) Constructions I and II are proved to be equivalent; (ii) Constructions I and III, are proved to be dual constructions to each other, in the sense that they can be generated via TDGS with two scanning orders (i.e., row-by-row search and columnby-column search, both along a single direction); (iii) Novel girth properties of Constructions I and III are discovered; (iv) A new method (Construct IV) is discovered, which leads to the same (in the worst case) or shorter (for many cases) CPM sizes compared to the state-of-the-art method, i.e., Construction III.
