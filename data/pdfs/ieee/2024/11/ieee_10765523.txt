FPGA Implementation of QC-LDPC Decoder with Rapid Cyclic Shifter for CV-QKD Systems

Abstract—In continuous variable quantum key distribution (CV-QKD) systems, low-density parity check (LDPC) decoders have gained widespread adoption due to their robust error correction capabilities and good adaptability to parallel processing hardware designs. We introduce a syndrome-based normalized min-sum algorithm (S-NMSA) and implement the decoder on FPGAs for high throughput and minimal resource consumption. The decoder utilizes a semi-parallel architecture. The proposed rapid cyclic shifter (RCS) can complete the shift of any dimension matrix in one clock cycle and realizes a significant reduction in hardware resource consumption. The semi-parallel decoding structure attains a 68.5 Mbps processing throughput on the condition that the system clock of the quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC decoder is 250 Mhz.
Index Terms—CV-QKD, QC-LDPC, NMSA, RCS, FPGA
I. INTRODUCTION
Quantum key distribution (QKD) [1] is a quantum information technology that ensures unconditional security, a feat achieved through the utilization of the principles of immeasurability and quantum unclonability. Compared to discrete variable quantum key distribution (DV-QKD) systems [2], continuous variable quantum key distribution (CV-QKD) systems [3] are less expensive to deploy and offer higher potential key rates over short distances. Currently, the performance of the post-processing procedure in CV-QKD systems is a constraining factor for the development of the systems. This is because the extremely low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) conditions in which the CV-QKD systems is located place higher demands on the data processing speed for information reconciliation (IR).
Error correcting codes (ECCs) is used by both communicating parties during IR to effectively detect and correct errors in the key so that both parties end up holding identical keys. Robert Gallager initially presented low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [4] in 1962. LDPC codes are well known for their decoding ability close to the Shannon limit. Nevertheless, the implementation of the LDPC decoder is a challenge that should not be underestimated if the requirements of IR for low latency and high throughput of error-correcting codes are to be met [5].

In contrast to central processing unit (CPUs) and graphics processing units (GPUs) with instruction-based architectures, field-programmable gate arrays (FPGAs) offer higher data parallel processing power than GPUs, as well as lower power consumption and greater ease of integration. The FPGA-based implementation of the LDPC code decoder is a crucial step in the development of a CV-QKD system. In FPGA implementation of a quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC decoder, a programmable circular-shift network QSN [6] is designed and implemented in order to transfer the decoded messages correctly during the iterative decoding process. The QSN has a flexible structure and supports predefined cyclic shifts of arbitrary size, but the disadvantage is that the hardware resource consumption is still high.
This paper introduces a syndromes-based normalized minsum decoding algorithm (S-NMSA), integrated with the IR of the CV-QKD systems. Additionally, we delineates the realization of a decoder tailored for high throughput and minimal resource consumption implemented on a FPGA. In order to address the issue of repeatedly calculating the memory location index, this paper presents a check matrix preprocessing method. This method aims to reduce the timeconsuming process of obtaining the location index and to reduce the decoding complexity. A rapid cyclic shifter (RCS) architecture is proposed, aimed at efficiently executing cyclic shifting.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. In Section II, we describe the PCM and the decoding algorithm of LDPC codes. Subsequently, the overall system architecture of the QCLDPC decoder is described in Section III. Section IV presents the experimental results of QC-LDPC decoder. Finally, in Section V we summarize and conclude our work.

B. LDPC Codes Decoding Algorithm
The normalized min-sum algorithm (NMSA) [8] is proposed to neutralize the implementation complexity of the belifpropagation (BP) algorithm and the inaccuracy of the min-sum algorithm algorithm (MSA). In the reverse-multiple reconciliation scheme of the CV-QKD systems, Bob (the communication receiver) computes the syndromes S = Hu using the binary key sequence u and the PCM H. Alice (the communication transmitter) gets the key sequence u by decoding based on the syndromes S, if decoding is successful then u = u . B. Preprocessing of the QC base PCM
In order to simplify the design of the decoder and reduce the hardware complexity, this paper preprocesses the Hb to obtain a regular matrix Hb before decoding. The preprocessing of the Hb includes the following steps:
1) Extract the non-negative values from Hb to obtain a numeric matrix, referred to as the extracted matrix. This matrix contains values that represent the offsets for the cyclic right shifts of the block CPM. These numerical values are utilized by the RCS module to execute cyclic shifts on LLRs.
2) Get the addresses and values from the extracted matrices. Create the offset matrices Of f set1 and Of f set2 from the values in the extracted matrix. The number of rows and columns corresponding to the numeric values create the address matrices Addr1 and Addr2, which are essential for determining the addresses of the LLRs in the memory module.
3) Regularize the offset matrices and address matrices. Specifically, use ”Z” to fill the offset matrices and ”0” to fill the address matrices.

The VNCU employs a hierarchical addition tree structure as shown in Fig. 4. The inputs are Dv sets of Lc2v and one inherent LLR provided by the channel. The outputs are Dv sets of Lv2c and one posterior LLR corresponding to the variable node, which will be used as the basis of the hard decision to get the decoded codeword. Additionally, after each layer of additional calculations, the bit width of the result needs to be expanded by one bit to avoid overflow.
The minimum value structure shown in the Fig. 5 is used by the check node calculation unit (CNCU) to complete the sign product computation and the minimum (Lv2cmin) and the subminimum (Lv2cmin−minor) find operations. The inputs are Dc sets of Lv2c and the outputs are Dc sets of Lc2v corresponding to each input. The minimum value structure initially compares all the input values directly to identify the minimum and the subminimum among them. Subsequently, it assesses whether the value of an input Lv2c is the minimum. If not, its corresponding output the value of Lc2v is assigned minimum; otherwise, the value of Lc2v is assigned subminimum.
D. Rapid Cycling Shifter
In the proposed QC-LDPC decoder architecture, we propose a flexibly configurable RCS that supports the circular shifting of square Z × Z matrices with arbitrary offsets. As previously described, Lv2c is computed in the order of the columns of H and Lc2v is computed in the order of the rows of H, the RCS needs to convert the LLRs to the corresponding read order before the calculation process. Fig.6 shows the operation strategy of the RCS with a Z × Z CPM and an offset of 4.
The original matrix is first decomposed into parts that need to be shifted left and parts that need to be shifted right. The offsets for the left and right shifts are then determined based on the given matrix shift values. Secondly, the leftward and rightward shift matrices are added to obtain the circular shift matrix. The principle of merging two shifted matrices is that during shifting, only key parts are retained, while the rest are zeroed, leading to the final left-shifted and right-shifted matrices. The RCS is capable of simultaneously shifting and selecting matrices in both directions in a single clock cycle, greatly reducing the time required to shift matrix data sequentially. Its flexibility ensures parallel processing of multiple

matrix circular shift operations, thereby minimizing matrix shift complexity due to differing offsets.

IV. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
This section focuses on the optimization performance verification of the proposed decoder architecture. On one hand, the optimized performance of the rapid cyclic shifter is verified. On the other hand, the error correction performance of the decoding is verified.

A. Performance Optimization Results
The proposed rapid cyclic shifter is designed to enhance the throughput of QC-LDPC decoders by reducing the time required for iterative decoding. The designed RCS is implemented on Xilinx Vertex-7 FPGA platform, in which the shifter supports any shift value for any matrix size. Table II compares the network size and the look-up-tables (LUTs) and the clock frequency of the RCS with the IBN[11] and the FPGA-based implementation of QSN. As show in TABLE II, the proposed shifter structure can save up to 65% in the elimination of hardware resources. Compared to the barrel shifter in the current state-of-the-art LDPC decoder, the implemented RCS is capable of 128-bit data in one clock cycle, whereas literature [10] requires six clock cycles.

B. SNR Performance

Our proposed QC-LDPC decoder has been implemented and tested on Xilinx Vertex-7 FPGA platform. The experiments utilize QC-LDPC codes compliant with IEEE 802.11n, featuring a 1944-bit length and a 1/2 code rate.
