Abstract—We demonstrate an LDPC encoder/decoder architecture with a maximum throughput of 2229 Mbps. Implemented on an FPGA, the receiver sensitivity achieves -56 dBm@2Gbps BPSK (decoded BER < 1E-7), with a decoding gain exceeding 8 dB.
Keywords—LDPC, coherent optical communication, FPGA, CCSDS.
I. INTRODUCTION
With the rapid advancement of laser communication technology, the demand for high-capacity and stable transmission systems has sharply increased. In this context, coherent communication systems have shown more significant attraction and importance in the field of inter satellite communication due to their excellent sensitivity advantages [1]-[3]. This system not only meets the growing demand for data transmission, but also effectively addresses signal attenuation issues in long-distance transmission, ensuring communication quality and efficiency, making it an important development direction in this field.
Due to the performance of LDPC codes approaching the Shannon limit, the system is able to achieve error-free transmission at higher signal-to-noise ratios (SNR) [4]-[5]. The low bit error rate flat layer characteristics of LDPC codes can ensure that the system can maintain a low bit error rate even at low signal-to-noise ratios, thereby enhancing the reliability of the system. This is particularly important for application scenarios that require high reliability, such as deep space communication, fiber optic communication, etc [6]-[7].
Traditionally, the Normalized Min-Sum Algorithm (NMSA) is used for decoding LDPC codes, which typically involves constant-cycle weights and disregards potential memory conflicts. Although these decoders achieve relatively high clock frequencies, they still struggle to reach Gbps-level throughput [8].

In this paper, we demonstrated an LDPC encoder/decoder architecture with a maximum throughput of 2229 Mbps, operating at a clock frequency of 285 MHz. This architecture demonstrates extremely high receiving sensitivity. Implemented on an FPGA, the receiver sensitivity achieves 56 dBm at 2Gbps when the BPSK bit error rate is less than 1E-7, with a decoding gain exceeding 8 dB. We believe that this achievement demonstrates significant performance advantages of the architecture in coherent optical communication applications.
II. SYSTEM MODEL
A. LDPC Encoder/Decoder Design
The Consultative Committee for Space Data Systems (CCSDS) has defined an LDPC code with a rate of 0.875, designated as C2, which has dimensions of (8176, 7154). The parity-check matrix and the parity part of the generator matrix are composed of cyclic submatrices of size 511x511. The first row of the submatrix is defined in the CCSDS standard, and the remaining row vectors are obtained by cyclic right shifts of the first row [9].
LDPC decoding methods can be broadly categorized into two types: hard-decision decoding and soft-decision decoding. Soft-decision decoding utilizes posterior probability information and iterative operations to enable LDPC code performance to approach the Shannon limit, making it widely used. Soft-decision decoding involves numerous multiplications in the probability domain, leading to high computational complexity and significant resource consumption when implemented in hardware. By representing probability information using likelihood ratios, the Belief Propagation (BP) algorithm in the log domain can be derived, transforming many multiplication operations into addition operations. Although this simplification results in some loss of accuracy and impacts decoding performance, optimization can be achieved in practical applications by selecting an appropriate α value, typically between 0.6 and 0.9. B. LDPC Encoder/Decoder Architecture
In this paper, the encoder architecture is designed to achieve low encoding complexity by dividing the information sequence into 14 segments, as shown in Fig. 1. Each segment of the information sequence is multiplied by the corresponding cyclic submatrix, with the encoding structure depicted in the figure. First, the first row of , is stored in a 511-bit cyclic shift register, and the output register is initialized to zero. For each information bit, it is ANDed with the elements in the shift register, and the result is XORed with the values in the output register. The result is then stored back in the output register. This process is repeated until all information bits have been processed, after which the encoded parity bits are output. This architecture effectively conserves resources and enhances the throughput of encoding.

The processing flowchart of LDPC decoder is shown in Fig. 2. The decoder mainly consists of three RAM blocks, three array processing units (VNU, CNU, LLR initialization unit), other auxiliary units, and control modules. Firstly, write the initial LLR information received by the channel into the LLR RAM to complete the information initialization; Subsequently, a round of iteration begins: the VNU node processes the previous LLR and Mcv information to generate the variable node information Mvc for this round of iteration, which is transmitted to the CNU through the iteration information storage RAM. After processing, CNU generates the verification node information for this iteration and sends it back to the iteration information storage RAM to update LLR. The updated new information of LLR is written back to two blocks of RAM, completing one iteration. Then repeat the iteration process until the set number of iterations is reached, and the iteration ends. The decoder outputs the decoded result. Additionally, it significantly improves the decoding throughput and facilitates easier convergence of the implementation clock.

III. EXPERIMENTAL SETUP
A. Overall system
The experimental setup is illustrated in Fig. 3. The FPGA at the transmission end generates data and performs LDPC encoding. The encoded signal is modulated into BPSK signals at various rates, transmitted through an optical transmitter module, and received by a conventional heterodyne coherent receiver. A variable optical attenuator (VOA) is used to adjust the received optical power (ROP), and a 50:50 optical splitter (OS) is used to monitor the ROP. In the real-time processing flow at the FPGA-based (Xilinx 7VX690T) receiver, the received signal first undergoes digital signal processing for frame synchronization and parsing. Then, in the LDPC decoding module, the proposed design is analyzed and compared in terms of latency and resource consumption. Finally, the bit error rate (BER) is calculated and recorded.

V. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we present an LDPC encoder/decoder architecture with a maximum throughput of 2229 Mbps, operating at a clock frequency of 200 MHz. This architecture exhibits extremely high receiving sensitivity. When implemented on FPGA and with a BPSK bit error rate less than 1E-7, the receiver sensitivity reaches -56 dBm at 2 Gbps, and the decoding gain exceeds 8 dB. In the encoder section, resource savings of over 80% can be achieved while significantly improving throughput. On the decoder side, LUTs and registers can save over 62% of resources, with only a 15% reduction in throughput. We believe that this achievement demonstrates the significant performance advantages of the architecture in coherent optical communication applications.
