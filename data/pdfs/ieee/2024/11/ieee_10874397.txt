LDPC Decoding Based on Hybrid Stochastic Computing

Yali Li
School of Artificial Intelligence Beijing University of Posts and Telecommunications
Beijing, China ylli@bupt.edu.cn

Zhisong Bie
School of Artificial Intelligence Beijing University of Posts and Telecommunications
Beijing, China zhisongbie@bupt.edu.cn

Abstract—Compared with traditional computing methods, stochastic computing (SC) is simpler in circuit implementation. The Belief Propagation (BP) or Min-Sum (MS) algorithms used in traditional Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) decoding require a large amount of circuits for the calculation of check nodes (CNs). In this paper, decoding is completed on a decoding structure that combines the advantages of the Offset Min-Sum (OMS) algorithm and SC. CN information is calculated in the probability domain, and variable node (VN) information is calculated in the Log Likelihood Ratio (LLR) domain. It is proposed to use the gold sequence combined with a synchronizer to generate a positively correlated sequence, and combine the Noise-Dependent Scaling (NDS) algorithm to obtain a positively correlated random number generator to complete the conversion from LLR to a positively correlated bit stream. The positively correlated bit stream is input into an AND gate to find the minimum value of the input bit streams. Simulation results show that the bit error rate curve of the proposed method is close to the OMS algorithm.
Keywords—stochastic computing, gold sequence, synchronizer
I. INTRODUCTION
In 1962, Gallager proposed Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) code. However, due to the extremely high encoding complexity of LDPC code and the high requirements for hardware, they were considered impractical codes at that time. In 1996, Mackay re-studied LDPC and found that LDPC code have excellent performance close to the Shannon limit, as well as the advantages of low decoding complexity and parallel decoding, which can reduce decoding delay and is suitable for occasions with high real-time requirements. In 5G NR, Quasi Cyclic Low Density Parity Check (QC-LDPC) code are used as the channel coding scheme. Its parity check matrix has structural characteristics and lower decoding complexity. In semantic communication systems, QC-LDPC codes are also used as channel coding. However, for 6G, which is expected to use semantic communication systems, the performance indicators need to be improved by 10 to 100 times compared to 5G.
For traditional LDPC code, the wiring between variable node (VN) and check node (CN) is very complex and requires a large amount of resources. The reason for this problem is that in the parity check matrix of LDPC code, the position of '1' is random. For the fully parallel structure, this problem will be more serious, resulting in wiring congestion and interconnection problems. Wiring will consume a lot of hardware area and reduce the utilization efficiency of silicon wafers. Therefore, the implementation of decoders is often

very complex, and a trade-off between hardware resources and performance is often needed. The Offset Min-Sum (OMS) and Normalized Min-Sum (NMS) algorithms based on partial parallel structures are more commonly used decoding structures, but hardware complexity and routing congestion are still urgent problems to be solved. In order to improve hardware efficiency and reduce hardware complexity, stochastic computing (SC) and LDPC are combined. Bit stream information is used instead of Log Likelihood Ratio (LLR) information, greatly reducing wiring resources. At the same time, both VNs and CNs in the decoder can be implemented using logic gates, which can significantly reduce the overhead of hardware resources.
SC was first proposed in 1969 [1] as a low-cost alternative to traditional binary computing. The design of the arithmetic unit of SC is very simple, which was one of its main advantages in the early days. Despite having strong fault tolerance and a simple structure, due to its long calculation time and relatively low accuracy, SC was once regarded as a less practical technology. However, with the development of technology, the uncertainty of circuit behavior increases, which requires us to understand and use probabilities for computing more deeply and effectively.
II. BACKGROUND
LDPC decoding algorithms can mainly be divided into two categories: one is hard decision algorithms, and the other is soft decision algorithms. Soft decision algorithms have better performance and lower bit error rate, and have been widely used. The main representatives of soft decision algorithms are the BP algorithm and the Min-Sum (MS) algorithm. The BP algorithm decodes by passing information between VNs and CNs until the maximum number of iterations is reached or hard decisions can be made on VNs information after passing parity checks to obtain the decoding result. According to the form of transmitted information, the BP algorithm can be divided into a probabilistic BP algorithm and a LLR BP algorithm [2]. In the probabilistic BP algorithm, the transmitted information is posterior probability, involving a large number of multiplications. Later, after simplification, the transmitted posterior probability information is changed to LLR information, and multiplication operations are transformed into addition operations, greatly reducing the decoding complexity.
The biggest difference between the MS algorithm and the BP algorithm is in the update calculation of CNs. The MS algorithm uses the method of comparing the minimum data

In the decoding algorithm, during iterations, information is transmitted along the edges connected between VN and CN in the Tanner graph. After multiple iterations, when the VN information is stable, the output can be made for decision to obtain the decoding result. In the current mainstream decoding algorithms such as LLR BP, NMS, and OMS algorithms, the information transmitted in the Tanner graph is all LLR information. In the LDPC decoding algorithm based on probability computing, the information transmitted between VN and CN is converted into Bernoulli '0' and '1' bit streams. The number of '1's in the '0' and '1' bit streams represents the probability confidence. When the node information of VN and CN is transmitted in the form of binary bit streams, the information calculation of VN and CN can be calculated using simple logic gate circuits. Compared with the calculation of node information in LLR form, it greatly reduces the circuit area and circuit power consumption. In [6], Internal Memory (IM) and Edge Memory (EM) are combined to strengthen information interaction and generate more random bits. The working mechanisms of EM and IM are the same. EM is located on each edge, and IM is located on each sub-node. When VN is not in a deadlock state, new random bits are generated to update the memory. However, when VN is in a deadlock state, the memory will not be updated, and random bits are selected from the memory as the output of VN. In [7], a new re-randomization structure Tracking Forecast Memory (TFM) is proposed to replace EM. When VN is locked, TFM uses the previously tracked probability and random number comparison to generate bit, optimizing the EM structure. Compared with EM, it saves 60% of the hardware area. In [8], MajorityBased Tracking Forecast Memory (MTFM) is proposed. The original allocation of one TFM on each edge is replaced by all edges of one VN sharing one MTFM. Only when all edges are in an update state will the TFM in MTFM update the probability value it tracks. The MTFM method reduces the number of re-randomization units used. Compared with TFM, it saves 50% of the hardware area.

In SC, to generate positively correlated random sequences, a correlated random number generator (RNG) needs to be used to generate random numbers, or a synchronizer can be introduced to enhance the correlation between sequences[11]. The purpose of the synchronizer is to pair '1' and '0' in two input stochastic number streams as dynamically as possible to increase positive correlation. The synchronization method selected here is as follows: when two input stochastic streams X and Y are the same, the synchronizer directly passes them to the output. That is, the two output sequences are respectively equal to the input X and Y at this time. When X and Y are different, the unpaired bits are first saved, and then the saved bits and the complement of the current position are paired at subsequent unpaired positions to dynamically adjust the input sequences. The two output sequences will have higher correlation compared to the input. In general, compared with the traditional stochastic decoding algorithms, the method we proposed abandons the probability tracking unit. The probability tracking unit has always been a key research direction in the study of stochastic LDPC decoding. Initially, the combination of EM and IM required placing RAM on each edge and each node of the VNs in the Tanner graph. Later, when using TFM and MTFM, a large amount of circuit resources were needed for bit storage, and the circuit diagrams of the VNs with high complexity were very complicated. If one wants to use stochastic calculations to compute the information of VNs, both the complexity of circuit design and the utilization of circuit resources will be significantly increased. Therefore, in the proposed algorithm, the VN part no longer uses stochastic calculations but directly conducts calculations in the LLR domain. For the calculation of check nodes, the LLR is converted into a fully correlated bit stream, and the bit stream can directly enter an AND gate for amplitude value calculation, eliminating the need to use the semibroadcast XOR gate circuits used by traditional check nodes. For the LDPC codes of BG1, the maximum degree of check nodes is 19. Directly using an 18-input AND gate can complete the calculation. If using XOR gates to calculate the

IV. CONCLUSIONS
A new method for generating random bit streams is proposed. The re-randomization module is no longer used. Based on the decoding architecture that combines the advantages of stochastic computing and the OMS algorithm, the row-parallel decoding method is used to complete LDPC decoding. Bit error rate curve simulation is carried out under the parameters of K = 8448 and R = 1/3. The results show that the bit error rate performance is close to that of the OMS algorithm. Although there is a certain reduction in bit error performance, the circuit resources are reduced significantly. It has the advantages of low power consumption and small area and is applicable in scenarios where the requirement for bit error performance is not high.
