HEncode: A Highly Modularized and Efficient FPGA QC-LDPC Encoder Using High Level Synthesis
Jiawei Yang1, Shaohua Wang1, Xiangrui Yang1, Yifan Zhang1, Qiang Cao1, Jie Yao1, Xiaodi Tan2, Xiao Lin2 1Wuhan National Laboratory for Optoelectronics, Huazhong University of Science & Technology, Wuhan, China 2College of Photonic and Electronic Engineering, Fujian Normal University, Fujian, China {wadeyoung, m202273497, yxr620, xenon zhang, caoqiang, jackyao}@hust.edu.cn, {xtan, xiaolin}@fjnu.edu.cn

Abstract—QC-LDPC (Quasi Cyclic Low-Density ParityCheck) codes, as a regular block-based code, have been prevalently adopted in communication and storage fields to ensure high reliability and bandwidth of data channels. However, existing Field-Programmable Gate Array (FPGA) QC-LDPC encoders designed by RTL experts are generally dedicated to specialized LDPC codes and hardware platforms without flexibility and scalability. Recently, High-Level Synthesis (HLS) was introduced to compile a high-level encoding logic into Register Transfer Level (RTL) implementations, which are low performance and hardware efficiency due to the overlarge HLS-to-RTL design space, especially for large-scale FPGA hardware.
This paper proposes a highly modularized and efficient FPGA QC-LDPC encoder, HEncoder, to fully leverage HLS to achieve high bandwidth, flexibility in both code parameters, and hardware efficiency. Firstly, HEncode presents an efficient Encode Block (EB) fully exploiting the FPGA LUT characteristic. Second, HEncode designs a low-level subword-encoding pipeline using multiple EBs and subword-parallel Encode Units (EU). Third, HEncoder designs an encode module with a pipelined data stream consecutively passing Input, EU array, and Output to balance bandwidths of accessing and encoding words. Finally, HEncode develops a design space analyzer to automatically determine the encoder parameters under constrained conditions to achieve high bandwidth. We implemented and evaluated HEncode on the Xilinx U50. The results show that compared to existing encoders, HEncode gains an increase of approximately 154.5× in the peak throughput and about 5.89× in hardware efficiency to achieve the encoding throughput of 922.66 Gbps.
Index Terms—QC-LPDC, FPGA, HLS, Encoder
I. INTRODUCTION
LDPC codes have been broadly used in communications such as 5G and the latest IEEE 802.11 series [1] and highdensity storage, e.g., Solid-State Drive and holographic storage [2]. QC-LDPC is a regular LDPC code with scalable code-construction and high parallelism friendly for hardware implementation without compromising error correction ability. Emerging applications have been demanding highthroughput QC-LDPC hardware encoders. For instance, the IEEE 802.11ay standard theoretically requires a maximum transmission rate of 176 Gbps [3]. FPGA is widely used in the implementation of QC-LDPC encoders because of its programmability and high performance. Most LDPC hardware encoders use Hardware Description Language (HDL) languages to develop RTL implementation upon FPGA. The low-level development process is generally long and errorprone. Recently, HLS has been widely used to transform high-level C/C++ codes to low-level RTL implementation [4],

improving the hardware development efficiency. However, due to the significant semantic gap between high-level algorithm abstract and low-level hardware detail as well as the large space design for large-scale FPGA hardware, the existing HLS-based encoder implementation is suboptimal with low throughput and hardware efficiency.
To fully leverage HLS to achieve high bandwidth in largescale FPGA hardware, we propose a highly modularized and efficient FPGA QC-LDPC encoder. Firstly, HEncode achieved LUT-level optimization within the Encode Block (EB), implementing full connectivity logically, which enhanced the parallelism of encoding. Subsequently, HEncode implemented a low-level subword-encoding pipeline and three types of parallel encoding structures based on the EB, further improving efficiency. Lastly, to generate the optimal encoder under various constraints, a resource usage model and a performance model were developed for HEncode. Based on these, a design space analyzer was developed to facilitate parameter tuning. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
• We propose an Encode Block structure based on LUTlevel optimization, which achieves full logical connectivity within the EB with standardized characteristics.
• We implemented a multi-level scheduling system for the HEncode, realizing pipeline designs based on EB and Units, and parallel designs based on EB and HBM ports, enhancing the parallelism of the encoding process.
• We designed a universal design space analyzer engine based on resource utilization and performance models, generating and optimizing hardware configurations.
• We implement and validate the effectiveness of HEncode on Xilinx U50 in terms of performance, flexibility, scalability, and efficiency.
II. BACKGROUND
A. QC-LDPC
LDPC codes belong to the category of linear block codes, and in actual computations, they usually employ vector matrix multiplication (VMM) under GF(2) to generate check bits. LDPC codes come in various types and variations, one of the most widely used being QC-LDPC. The generation matrix of a QC-LDPC is usually composed of multiple circulant submatrices. Multiple sub-matrices are combined in the column direction to form a super row and in the row direction to form a super column. The super columns and super rows composed of

Consequently, we employ the conventional vectormatrix multiplication to design the encoder. B. HLS and FPGA
HLS translates behavioral descriptions, often in C/C++, into hardware, expediting development and verification for flexible, large-scale encoder designs like QC-LDPC [5]. FPGAs are pivotal in this context due to their reconfigurability, low power, and low latency. Traditional FPGA-based encoder designs, however, have not fully exploited the potential of FPGA hardware, such as the 4-6 input LUT structure. Furthermore, FPGAs paired with High Bandwidth Memory (HBM) are ideal for high-concurrency tasks like QC-LDPC encoding, yet design considerations for fan-in and fan-out between modules are crucial to mitigate increased latency and power consumption. C. Existing Research And Motivation
Existing QC-LDPC encoder designs, often optimized for specific code types, achieve high throughput rates (1–100 Gbps) with limited hardware resources (2k–20k LUTs) using techniques like Richardson-Urbanke (RU) and Lower TriangularUpper (LU) decomposition [6]–[8]. However, these optimizations limit encoder applicability and introduce additional costs. General VMM-based designs offer broader code compatibility but at the expense of increased hardware usage (20k-30k LUTs) and lower throughput (0.3–6 Gbps) [4], [9], [10].To address these limitations, we propose the development of a new QC-LDPC encoder architecture that leverages HLS and FPGA technology for high-throughput and adjustability.
III. DESIGN
A. HEncode Overview
This paper proposes a multi-level, scalable, and efficient encoder design using HLS. Through the use of HLS compilation directives, guidance is provided for the overall encoder logic and hints on some hardware details. Overall, HEncode consists of multiple Encode Modules, which encode in parallel. Each Encode Module comprises a DataLoad Unit(DLU), a DataSend Unit(DSU), and an Encode Unit Array. As shown in Fig.1, the encoding process is divided into several steps: ➀: The DLU reads data from the HBM. ➁: The DLU forwards the data bits to the DSU and distributes the data bits to the Input Buffers of various Encode Units. ➂: The Encode Unit Array completes the encoding of the codeword according to the encoding algorithm and writes the calculated check bits into the Output Buffer. ➃: The DSU concatenates the check bit results and data bits from different Encode Units. ➄: The DSU writes the concatenated codeword back to the HBM. B. Encode Block
We have designed an HEncoder Encode Block(EB) optimized for LUTs, utilizing HLS to achieve full connectivity logically and implementing shift cycling operations through physical connections. Input 3 serves as an input port for either zero or data from the Temp buffer, with the input data also depending on the current computation round. C. Multi-Parameter Scheduling
In the section III-B, we obtained a basic EB. However, due to the limited hardware scale of a single EB, the encoding throughput is constrained. Therefore, we consider improving the overall throughput of the encoder by reusing EB.
1) Low-Level Subword-encoding Pipeline in the Super Column Direction
The HEncoder organizes EBs in a head-to-tail ring structure to handle sub-matrices in the super column direction. This arrangement ensures that the XOR calculations required by the QC-LDPC encoding process are evenly distributed across each EB, thereby preventing fan-in issues that would arise from directly connecting all EBs to the output. In this structure, each EB is responsible for processing a portion of the sub-matrices within a super column. As the blocks progress through the ring, they perform their computations and pass their results to the next block in the sequence. This allows for a continuous flow of data and operations, with the final block in each super column directly connecting to the output.
2) Parallelism in Super Row Direction and Encode Unit Parallel processing in the super row direction leverages the lack of data dependency between super-rows, allowing straightforward replication of EBs. The design involves determining the replication factor and splitting the loop into parallel and serial parts for efficient check bit generation. This, combined with the super-column pipeline, speeds up codeword encoding. The aggregation of multiple EBs with Input and Output buffers is termed an Encode Unit. Parallelism between Encode Units enables simultaneous encoding of multiple codewords, requiring only the reuse parameter determination. 3) Dataload Unit, Datasend Unit, and Inter-Unit Pipeline The Dataload and Datasend Units manage data input and output, buffering EB operations to mitigate fan-in and fanout issues and reduce control complexity. These Units are connected to HBM through AXI interfaces Forming an Encode Module with a DataLoad Unit, a Datasend Unit, and an Encode Unit Array enables task-level pipelining. This module implements a three-stage pipeline for efficient data flow: “data reading, encoding computation, and data writing back.”

Additionally, we can control the throughput of the Dataload and Datasend Units by setting the interface bit widths, WL and WS, respectively. D. Design Space Analyzer
To optimize the encoder’s architecture for various code rates, lengths, throughput requirements, and FPGA chips with varying resources, a multi-dimensional design space analyzer engine is developed. The analyzer takes into account hardware constraints like the number of LUTs and encoding throughput. It outputs key encoder parameters, including ROW NUM(NR), COL NUM(NC ), UNIT NUM(NU ), WL, WS, and NM (Number of modules), along with associated hardware consumption and throughput. This approach avoids the labor-intensive task of manually testing numerous parameter combinations.

IV. EXPERIMENTS

A. Experimental Configuration

We are utilizing the Xilinx hardware development platform, Vitis, to implement our encoder. Our experimental setup

includes a server equipped with two Intel Xeon E5-2670 processors and 128GB of memory. The FPGA hardware is a Xilinx Alveo U50 data center accelerator card, equipped with 8GB of HBM and a total bandwidth of 316 GB/s. The FPGA is connected to the server via a PCIe Gen3 x16 interface. With the assistance of HLS, this design can be conveniently ported

to any other FPGA platform. We are running the experiments on the official firmware (Xilinx u50 gen3x16 xdma 201920 3), with Vitis version 2020.2 and XRT version 2.8.743. B. Encoder Flexibility
In this section, we will test the flexibility of the HEncoder. 1) Code Length We employ the QC-LDPC encoding rules specified under
the IEEE 802.16 standard. Tests are conducted with a codeword lengths of 768 to 2304 bits, and a code rate of 1/2. We used a moderately scaled design: NR=2, NC =2, and NU =3. The encoding throughput test results are shown in Fig.3. It can be observed that as the codeword length increases, the encoding throughput almost linearly increases with a linear regression parameter exceeding 0.99.

C. Models Accuracy
To test the accuracy of resource utilization model and performance model against actual conditions, we randomly selected three different parameter combinations for testing.(A:NR=2 NC =4 NU =3 NM =1, B:NR=4 NC =3 NU =2 NM =1, C:NR=3 NC =3 NU =2 NM =1) It is important to note that there is no comparative object for throughput; it can only be compared with actual performance. LUT usage has a system estimate provided by HLS. The evaluation results are depicted in fig.7. It can be observed that our throughput estimation results deviated from actual conditions by +16% to +39%, and the estimated results for LUT usage deviated by +23% to +47%.
V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we first implemented a modularized, fully parallel Encode Block. Furthermore, we developed a flexible, adjustable encoding framework. Finally, to adapt to different constraints and optimize encoder configuration, we designed

a design space analyzer engine. Compared to traditional en-
coders, our approach offers higher parallelism, better flexibil-
ity, and greater hardware efficiency.
