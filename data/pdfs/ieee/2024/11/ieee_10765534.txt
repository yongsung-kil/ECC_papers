Abstract—In this paper, we propose a QC-LDPC decoder architecture with low complexity as well as excellent decoding performance in CV-QKD systems, which employs the syndromeassisted layered belief propagation decoding algorithm. To minimize the complexity of hardware implementation and resource consumption in iterative decoding, we use fixed-point numbers instead of floating-point numbers to update node messages. Additionally, two-segment nonuniform lookup tables are employed to implement Ψ(x) efficiently. Besides, the layered syndromeassisted early termination (LS-ET) scheme is introduced, resulting in the reduction of the number of decoding iterations while maintaining superior frame error rate (FER) performance. The experimental results indicate that the QC-LDPC decoder implemented on an FPGA is capable of achieving throughput of 50.8 Mbps when the code length is 26112 and code rate is 22/68.
Index Terms—QC-LDPC, CV-QKD, FPGA, syndrome, BP.
I. INTRODUCTION
Owing to the development of quantum computers, various current cryptographic algorithms will no longer be able to guarantee the security of information transmission. Consequently, quantum key distribution (QKD) becomes one of the feasible techniques to solve this problem [1] [2].
Two main variants of QKD protocols exist: continuousvariable (CV) QKD and discrete-variable (DV) QKD, which are used for generating symmetric random secret keys over the quantum channel [2]. Compared to DV-QKD, CV-QKD employs high-sensitivity, low-noise heterodyne or homodyne detectors to measure the corresponding coherent states [3]. This approach facilitates efficient, stable, and cost-effective distribution of quantum keys. The post-processing procedure

is relatively sophisticated in the development of the CV-QKD system, involving four main steps: basis sifting, identity authentication, information reconciliation (IR), and privacy amplification (PA). During IR, error correction codes (ECCs) [4] are employed to rectify errors resulting from quantum channel disturbances and potential eavesdropping. Low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes, known for approaching the Shannon limit in performance, are commonly used for error correction in IR [5].
Currently, the speed of the post-processing in high-speed CV-QKD systems is limited by the throughput of IR [6]. The necessity for lengthy codes and multiple iterative operations inherent to IR renders the throughputs of ECCs in the central processing units (CPUs) severely constrained. One effective approach to enhancing throughput is leveraging the graphics processing units (GPUs) acceleration. Reference [7] and [8] demonstrate that acceleration using GPU increased the throughputs of IR from 9.17 Mbps to 30.39 Mbps. Field programmable gate arrays (FPGAs) offer distinct advantages over CPUs and GPUs, especially in LDPC decoder design. FPGAs can use pipeline parallelism and data parallelism to accomplish more concurrent processing than GPUs and consume less power than CPUs and GPUs [9].
The survey presented in [10] demonstrates that the majority of current decoders employ the min-sum algorithm (MSA). Although the MSA is more hardware-friendly, it will cause large loss of decoding efficiency compared to the belief propagation (BP) algorithm. In addition, the layered BP (L-BP) schedule requires only one type of computational node units (CNUs) when implemented based on FPGA in comparison to the flooding schedule. Besides, the early termination (ET) scheme

However, to our knowledge, the existing ET schemes cannot reduce average iteration count of failed decoding iterations since they neglect to assess the error-correcting status [11]. Against this background, aiming to obtain a decoder module with good performance while reducing the number of invalid decoding iterations, the LDPC code utilizing the L-BP algorithm based the layered syndrome-assisted early termination (LS-ET) is adopted.
The main contributions of this paper are described as follows. Firstly, a syndrome-assisted L-BP decoder is implemented to accommodate the characteristics of FPGAs. Secondly, in order to minimize hardware resource consumption and simplify implementation, we employ fixed-point numbers of type (1,6,12) for the quantization of log-likelihood ratios (LLRs) and node messages. Besides, two segmented look-up tables (LUTs) are utilized to implement Ψ(x). Thirdly, we implement LS-ET module using syndromic variation rates to reduce invalid decoding iteration of the IR. The result demonstrates that quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC decoder can achieve throughputs of 50.8 Mbps in accordance with the 5G new radio (5G NR) standard.
This paper is organized as follows. Section II presents QCLDPC codes in the 5G NR standard and L-BP algorithm. Then, Section III describes design of the proposed QC-LDPC decoder. We provide results of simulation and implementation in Section IV. In conclusion, Section V provides the summary.

The implementation of Ψ(x) on FPGAs incurs massive resource consumption and high latency. Consequently, it is necessary to employ the segmented LUT to implement Ψ(x).

D. The layered syndrome-assisted early termination

When the Decision module is finished, the LS-ET is activated. The structure of the LS-ET is illustrated in Fig.5. The layered offset data and layered position data read from P CM M EM are initially input into the LS-ET based on the layer structure. Subsequently, the decoding codewords u are split into n segments, with n corresponding to the column number in the base matrix. The codewords corresponding to the position data Ha are selected and then offset to the left by the barrel shifter. All codewords that have undergone a shift are subjected to a layer syndrome Sˆ(k,l) following the bitwise permutation. Simultaneously, the local syndrome Sl of Syn mem is written to LS-ET in the form of layer. Subsequently, the syndrome variation can be denoted by

will assess the performance by analyzing the throughput, the

average number of iterations.
