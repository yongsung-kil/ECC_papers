Iterative Decoding of One-Step Majority Logic Decodable Codes Based on Belief Propagation
Rainer Lucas, Member, IEEE, Marc P. C. Fossorier, Member, IEEE, Yu Kou, and Shu Lin, Fellow, IEEE

Abstract—Recently, the belief propagation (BP) algorithm has received a lot of attention in the coding community, mostly due to its near-optimum decoding for low-density parity check (LDPC) codes and its connection to turbo decoding. In this paper, we investigate the performance achieved by the BP algorithm for decoding one-step majority logic decodable (OSMLD) codes. The BP algorithm is expressed in terms of likelihood ratios rather than probabilities, as conventionally presented. The proposed algorithm fits better the decoding of OSMLD codes with respect to its numerical stability due to the fact that the weights of their check sums are often much higher than that of the corresponding LDPC codes. Although it has been believed that OSMLD codes are far inferior to LDPC codes, we show that for medium code lengths (say between 200–1000 bits), the BP decoding of OSMLD codes can significantly outperform BP decoding of their equivalent LDPC codes. The reasons for this behavior are elaborated.
Index Terms—A posteriori decoding, belief-propagation decoding, iterative decoding, majority-logic decoding, soft-decision decoding.

The error performances achieved by iterative decoding

differences. On one hand, a larger number of check sums per

bit results in a larger number of independent estimates per bit

as well as possibly a better distance profile. On the other hand,

the smaller the Hamming weight of the check sums, the smaller

the influence and propagation of errors.

In this paper, APP and BP iterative decodings of both classes

of codes are studied. The organization of the paper is as fol-

lows. In Section II, some fundamentals about OSMLD codes

are given. BP decoding is then considered in Section III, and

a decoding algorithm based on likelihood ratios is proposed.

In Section IV, BP decoding of OSMLD codes is investigated

for difference set cyclic (DSC) codes. Simulations show the im-

provements achieved by BP decoding of DSC codes compared

with iterative APP decoding. It is also shown that for the code

lengths considered, BP decoding of DSC codes outperforms BP

decoding of LDPC codes of same length and dimension. Finally,

concluding remarks are given in Section V.

III. BP DECODING

BP decoding was recently investigated in [5]–[9], mostly for decoding turbo codes and LDPC codes. BP decoding corresponds to the probabilistic solution to iterative decoding based on orthogonal parity checks [4]. Although not optimum due to the presence of loops in the graph representation of the parity check set considered, BP provides an efficient decoding method if these loops remain quite long, as for LDPC codes [2], [7] or more generally, orthogonal parity checks of relatively low weights. Hence, BP decoding shows some similarities to iterative APP decoding [3] as follows.

• BP decoding is based on symbol-by-symbol iterative softin/soft-out decoding.
• BP decoding is based on orthogonal parity check sets.

IV. BP DECODING OF DSC CODES
In this section, we investigate three aspects of BP decoding of DSC codes. First, for a relatively short code, we compare the error performance of APP and BP iterative decodings with that of maximum-likelihood decoding (MLD). Then, iterative APP and BP decodings are further compared for longer DSC codes. Finally, BP decoding of these longer DSC codes is compared to BP decoding of LDPC codes of the same length and dimension. For all examples, the computational complexity of the decodings is estimated by the required number of floating point multiplications and floating point additions. Let us consider the (73,45,10) DSC code. In Fig. 2, the bit- and block-error rates achieved for iterative APP decoding and BP decoding are compared to the order-3 reprocessing of [11]. For this code, the error performance degradation for order-3 reprocessing compared to that of MLD is negligible (i.e., the two curves are on top of each other). We observe that BP decoding performs slightly better than iterative APP decoding. However, even BP decoding does not achieve MLD. In Fig. 3, the performance of iterative APP decoding according to [3] for the (273,191,18) DSC code is compared to BP decoding presented in Section III. We observe that BP decoding gains 0.25 dB in comparison to iterative APP decoding at a bit-error rate (BER) of 10 . In terms of word-error rate (WER), the gain of BP decoding is about 0.3 dB at 10 . At a BER of 10 /at a WER of 10 , we observe from Fig. 4 a gain of 0.4 dB in BER and a gain of 0.5 dB in WER for BP decoding of the (1057,813,34) DSC code. We also observe that the iterative BP decoding converges

much slower than iterative APP decoding since 50 iterations

were needed to achieve convergence of the BP decoding for

both DSC codes, against 15 iterations for APP decoding of the

(273,191,18) DSC code and 8 iterations for APP decoding of

the (1057,813,34) DSC code. This fact can explain the relatively

larger coding gain achieved by BP decoding over APP decoding

for the (1057,813,34) DSC code since, in general, the larger the

correlation between iterations, the faster the convergence. Based

on these results, we conclude that the coding gain of iterative BP

decoding over iterative APP decoding is achieved at the expense

of a larger computational complexity since both the numbers of

iterations and real operations per iteration increase significantly.

At a BER of 10 , the DSC code is about 0.8 dB superior to the LDPC code with four check sums and about 1 dB superior to the LDPC code with three check sums. Note that the latter is subject to an error floor due to its relatively poor minimum Hamming distance. Qualitatively, the same remarks hold when comparing iterative BP decoding of the (1057,813,34) DSC code with iterative BP decoding of the two constructed (1057,813) LDPC codes, as observed from Fig. 6. However, the gain achieved by the DSC is less compared to that of the length-273 codes. At a BER of 10 , the DSC code is about 0.4 dB superior to both LDPC codes. This reduction of coding gain can be partially explained by the fact that as the code length increases, LDPC codes remain good codes (they are asymptotically good codes [7]), while in general, codes constructed based on algebraic structures tend to become bad as the code length increases.
Since both the DSC and LDPC codes considered have a relatively good Hamming distance, the observation that BP

decoding of the DSC codes outperforms BP decoding of the LDPC codes of the same length can be explained by the fact that the DSC codes have more orthogonal parity check sums per coordinate. Although the graph representations of these codes are rich in cycles of length 6, it seems more advantageous to keep all available check sums and maintain the balanced structure of the graph rather than to eliminate some check sums to reduce the number of cycles at the expense of the graph symmetry. As a result, each bit receives in a similar fashion more independent information at each iteration. In addition, the fast average convergence of the BP decoding ensures that at sufficiently high signal-to-noise ratio, the influence of cycles does not significantly influence the error performance. Note, however, that LDPC codes can be constructed for any and . This is a much harder task to achieve for good OSMLD codes.
Another significant advantage of OSMLD codes over LPDC codes is their simple encoding based on linear feedback shift registers since many OSMLD codes are cyclic codes [12].
Finally, it is interesting to point out that the error performances of iterative BP decoding represented in the figures above can be further enhanced by about 0.1–0.2 dB if the likelihood ratios are scaled properly in the iterative process [17].

V. CONCLUSION

In this paper, iterative decoding based on both APP and

BP has been investigated for LDPC codes and OSMLD codes

of medium lengths (

). The decoding algorithms

have been expressed in terms of log-likelihood ratios instead

of probabilities. This formulation reduces the influence of

numerical problems, particularly for OSMLD codes due to the

large Hamming weights of their associated parity check sums.
