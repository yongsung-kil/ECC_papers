A Method for Convergence Analysis of Iterative Probabilistic Decoding
Miodrag J. Mihaljevic´ and Jovan Dj. Golic´

Abstract—A novel analytical approach to performance evaluation of soft-decoding algorithms for binary linear block codes based on probabilistic iterative error correction is presented. A convergence condition establishing the critical noise rate below which the expected bit-error probability tends to zero is theoretically derived. It explains the capability of iterative probabilistic decoding of binary linear block codes with sparse parity-check matrices to correct, with probability close to one, error patterns with the number of errors (far) beyond half the code minimum distance. Systematic experiments conducted on truncated simplex codes seem to agree well with the convergence condition. The method may also be interesting for the theoretical analysis of the so-called turbo codes.
Index Terms—Convergence condition, iterative error-correction decoding, linear block codes.

In this correspondence, attention is focused on nonalgebraic soft-decoding procedures based on iterative error correction. A number of recently published results shed more light into the iterative decoding techniques and their applications (e.g., see [1], [8], [10], [9], and [14]). On the other hand, decoding of linear block codes beyond half the minimum code distance is also considered in the literature on its own (e.g., see [2]). A soft-decoding procedure based on posterior probability threshold decoding [11] and iterative principle is presented in [4, pp. 152–153]. In [5], two multistep decoding algorithms for the so-called low-density parity-check codes are proposed and discussed. Essentially using the underlying ideas from [5] and [4, pp. 152–153], a number of iterative error-correction decoding algorithms have been developed and analyzed in [12], [13], [16], [17], and [3], noting that the algorithms in [12], [13], [16], and [3] are presented in crypto-oriented forms.
However, only few analytical results related to the convergence conditions for a successful iterative error-correction decoding have been established so far (e.g., see [17] for probabilistic decoding and [16] for majority-logic decoding). Note that the result from [17] gives only an equivalent representation of a successful iterative probabilistic decoding process rather than a condition for success in terms of the problem parameters. The main objective of this correspondence is to derive such a condition. This is achieved by considering the self-composition of the expected bit-error rate as an approximate model for the iterative probabilistic decoding process. Preliminaries are presented in Section II and a convergence analysis of the expected bit-error probability is conducted in Section III. Section IV is devoted to experiments by computer simulations whose main objective was to test the theoretically established (approximate) convergence condition. Conclusions are given in Section V and mathematical proofs are provided in the Appendix.

III. CONVERGENCE ANALYSIS
In this section we present and discuss a model for the IPDA iterative process and conduct a convergence analysis of this model.
A. Approximate Model
 Without loss of generality, assume that p 0:5, where p is the
channel error/noise probability. The initial error probability used in the IPDA does not have to match p exactly: it may be close to p or larger. Experiments show that the error probability vector typically quickly converges to the all-zero one, except possibly for a few coordinates, even if the number of errors is not reduced. This corresponds to the fixed points of the vectorial function defined by (2) according to which the error probability vector is repeatedly computed. Note that the average value of the bit-error probabilities after each iteration (which drops to a quantity close to zero) represents the conditional bit-error rate only after the first iteration, and in subsequent iterations is just an approximation.
If the number of the parity checks used is sufficiently large given p, then the number of errors is reduced, but not necessarily to zero. All the errors can then be corrected by repeating the algorithm for a small number of times (rounds), each time resetting the error probabilities to p, and by applying a simple (low-complexity) information set decoding technique, specific to the code considered, at the end. Note that the channel capacity bound provides a necessary condition for a successful
0 (1 error correction, that is, n > k= H2(p)), where H2 is the binary
entropy function. Direct analysis of the algorithm convergence to the original code-
word would consist of characterizing all the error patterns such that all or most of errors are corrected. As this does not seem to be tractable, one may take an indirect approach by considering the convergence of a simplified IPDA (SIPDA) in which in each iteration the prior probabilities of error for all the codeword bits are assumed to be the same and equal to the conditional bit-error rate computed in the previous iteration. Experiments reported in [13] show that although the IPDA is more effective than the SIPDA, the advantage is not too significant.
So, one may investigate the convergence to zero of the corresponding sequence of the conditional bit-error rates in the SIPDA. However, it turns out that this sequence is not a self-composition, that is, the conditional bit-error rate after the jth iteration step is not a function of the
0 conditional bit-error rate after the (j 1)th iteration step. This makes
the convergence analysis very difficult. The main point of this correspondence is to take the simplification one step further and analyze the convergence of the self-composition of the expected bit-error rate instead. This model for the IPDA iterative process appears to be a very good approximation according to experiments.

The parity checks correspond to polynomial multiples of the parity-

applicable to h of relatively low weight. In this case, every parity check

has the same weight. If the weight of h is not low, then one should use

other techniques (e.g., see [6]).

B. Algorithm
We implemented a version of the IPDA with resetting where the error probabilities are all reset to the initial value p if the average residual bit-error probability, that is, the conditional bit-error rate drops below a threshold " or if the number of iteration steps reaches jmax. The sequence of iteration steps between two successive resets is called a round. The algorithm stops if all the parity checks are satisfied (i.e., if the estimated codeword is a codeword) or if the number of rounds reaches rmax. The values adopted in the experiments were " = 0:03, jmax = 10, and rmax = 10. Since the estimated codeword can contain a small number of residual errors, a simple information set decoding technique, specific to the code considered, has to be applied at the end to correct all the errors.
For the particular codes in question, the information set decoding technique consists of searching for an error-free sliding window of k

successive bits. In fact, in the conducted experiments, the maximum number of examined sliding windows was limited to only 10. For every assumed sliding window (information set), the whole codeword is recovered by the linear recurrence (backward and forward), and the reconstructed codeword is chosen as one with the minimum Hamming distance to the received codeword. If the error correction is successful, then this minimum distance should be consistent with the expected number of errors, np. A decoding experiment is considered successful if the reconstructed and the original codewords are identical.
Note that the computational complexity of each iteration step of the IPDA is linear both in n and in the average number of the parity checks per bit used. The computational complexity of the information set decoding technique employed is relatively negligible. Altogether, the decoding complexity can be significantly smaller than 2k, which is the complexity of the minimum distance decoding.
C. Experimental Results
The main objectives of the decoding experiments were to obtain an empirical estimate of the critical noise probability, above which the complete algorithm (IPDA with resetting and simple information set decoding) is not successful, and to compare this estimate with the theoretical approximation of the critical noise probability calculated according to Theorem 1, for each parity-check polynomial and each parity-check set used.
Since the number of the parity checks depends on a particular bit in a codeword (due to the way the parity checks were generated), J(w) in (14) is approximated as the average number of the parity checks of weight w per bit, and the theoretical critical noise probability is then numerically obtained according to Theorem 1. The empirical critical noise probability is obtained by averaging over 20 samples of noisy codewords, where each sample is obtained from a different codeword by introducing random errors whose number was gradually increased until the IPDA was no longer successful. For each sample, the maximum number of errors that could successfully be corrected divided by the codeword length was taken as an estimate of the critical noise probability.
The following three groups of experiments were realized. In all of them we used the same set of six low-rate binary linear block codes (5000; k), where k = 32; 64 and for each k three primitive paritycheck polynomials of weights 3; 5; and 7 were examined. In the first group, the parity checks were orthogonal on each bit and were generated from the parity-check polynomial multiples (obtained by repeated
 squaring) of degree m n=10 = 500. In the second group, the de-
gree of the parity-check polynomial multiples was not restricted to 500, and in the third group, apart from that, the orthogonality condition was not respected either. The experimental results are presented in the following self-explanatory tables.
In addition to the reasons given in the beginning of this section, the difference between the empirical and theoretical critical noise probabilities in Tables I–III is also due to the fact that the number of the parity checks used in the experiments depends on a particular bit in a codeword. The experiments show that the theoretical critical noise probability provides a (relatively tight) upper bound on the empirical one, even if the parity checks are not orthogonal. Note that for the SIPDA, this upper bound theoretically follows from the fact that there are no complementations in the first iteration step. More precisely, this fact is a direct consequence of equation (4) and Theorem 1, and implies that the bit-error probabilities are not changed by the SIPDA.
Interestingly, it appears that the greater the weight of the paritycheck polynomial, the smaller the difference between the two noise probabilities. In any case, the decoding experiments confirm that the IPDA is a fast decoding algorithm that can for large k correct many error patterns of weight far beyond half the code minimum distance

V. CONCLUSIONS
Iterative parity-check-based probabilistic decoding algorithms for binary linear block codes are investigated. A convergence condition for the self-composition of the expected bit-error probability to tend to zero is analytically derived. Systematic experiments conducted on truncated simplex codes seem to support the convergence condition, at least as a relatively tight upper bound on the critical noise probability. Extension to a more general case of linearly independent, not necessarily orthogonal parity checks is an interesting open problem. The developed method may also be useful for the theoretical analysis of turbo codes.
