I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check block codes were first introduced by Gallager in the early 1960’s [1]. He also studied an iterative decoding algorithm, which seemed to be extremely promising for achieving low-error probabilities at high data rates at a reasonable cost.
During the last few years, iterative decoding has attracted great interest [2]–[4]. In particular, a recent simulation of iterative decoding of long low-density parity-check block codes [5], [6] shows that, analogous to the so-called turbo codes, a performance close to the Shannon limit has been reached for this class of codes.
In this correspondence we consider the convolutional version of low-density parity-check codes. As in the case of conventional block and convolutional codes, we found that they perform better than block codes of the same length. Furthermore, these codes have a more regular structure which makes the implementation of the encoder and decoder simpler. The decoder can also be implemented by using several processors in a pipeline structure and in this way accelerate the decoding process.
A mathematical performance analysis of iterative decoding is difficult due to statistical dependence. A successful attempt analyzing iterative decoding of low-density parity-check block codes was made by V. Zyablov and M. Pinsker [7]. With their analysis, they proved that the number of corrected errors grows linearly with the block length. In practice, a more interesting parameter is the bit-error rate (BER) achieved by the decoder. Unfortunately, most of the available publications concerning the BER, as far as the authors know, are based on simulations.
This correspondence is organized as follows: In Section II the basic principles of low-density parity-check block codes and a method for iterative decoding of such codes are summarized. For simplicity, we
will restrict ourselves to the rate R = 1=2 binary codes. Based on the
definitions given in Section II, we make an asymptotic study of the behavior of the BER for this class of codes in Section III. In Section IV, we introduce the time-varying periodical binary convolutional codes and discuss the implementation of the encoder. In Section V, we analyze the iterative decoding of low-density convolutional codes in the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel and present a pipeline implementation of the decoder. Section VI illustrates the performance of the iterative decoding of low-density convolutional codes with different memory lengths.

VI. SIMULATION RESULTS AND CONCLUSIONS
A theoretical analysis of iterative decoding is difficult due to statistical dependence. Therefore, in order to get an idea of the BER performances of these codes, the pipeline decoder was implemented in software for simulations in the AWGN channel. The simulation of the BER for (M; 3(2); 5) codes with memories M = 129; 257; 513; 1025; 2049; and 4097 are shown in Fig. 7. In this figure is also shown the performance of low-density parity-check block codes with the same rate and length N = 126; 252; 504; 1008, from [1] and N = 19839 and 65389 from [5]. We see that the convolutional codes outperform the block codes of the same length. An important parameter for the decoder is the number of processors I. Figs. 8 and 9 show the BER dependence on I when decoding the same codes.
In this correspondence we have introduced a new class of codes, which differs both from the class of Gallager’s low-density paritycheck block codes and the class of turbo codes. Further investigations on these codes, carried out by K. Engdahl and K. Sh Zigangirov [8]–[10], after the submition of this correspondence, shows that these codes have some additional advantages.
We noted earlier that these codes, in contrast to the decoding of block codes, can be decoded easily with a pipeline decoder implementation. Also, they performed better than a block code of the same complexity. Furthermore, since the minimum distance of low-density codes grows linearly with the code memory [1] the biterror probability does not have the error floor effect found when decoding the turbo codes at large signal-to-noise ratios.
Finally, our preliminary investigation also shows that, at least for small interleaver size, low-density convolutional codes outperform

turbo codes, but this comparison needs a more carefull study.

The Golay Convolutional Code—Some Application Aspects
Sven and Christian Weiß, Student Member, IEEE

Abstract—The Golay convolutional code and related block codes are investigated from an application point of view. We calculate the distance spectrum of the Golay convolutional code and the weight distributions of the block codes using the transfer-matrix method. The performance of the considered codes is shown and discussed. Moreover, the codes are applied as component codes of parallel concatenated codes which are iteratively decoded.
Index Terms—Convolutional code, Golay code, iterative decoding, parallel concatenation, tail-biting.

I. INTRODUCTION

Recently, Calderbank, Forney, and Vardy investigated tail-biting

trellises for block codes [1]. Among other things, they have shown

that the extended (24; 12) Golay code can be represented by a

minimal tail-biting trellis which has 16 states and 12 sections. Since

this trellis also describes a convolutional code (CC), an interesting

16-state rate-1=2 periodically time-varying convolutional code with

free distance dfree = 8 is obtained. It is named Golay convolutional
code CG [1] due to its close relation to the Golay block code. From

the Heller bound we know that no CC of rate 1=2 can have a larger

free distance than 8. In fact, the best time-invariant rate-1=2 CC has

dfree = 7. Moreover, CG is self-dual and the weight of its codewords

is divisible by four, i.e., the distance spectrum is doubly-even.

The aim of this correspondence is to investigate the Golay CC

and related block codes from an application point of view. It is

divided into two parts. Section II deals with the Golay CC. A

minimal systematic encoder is presented. Afterwards, the distance

spectrum of CG is calculated applying the transfer-matrix method. Simulation results for CG itself and as a component code in a parallel

concatenation scheme are shown and compared to the performance

of the best time-invariant CC of same rate and complexity.

In Section III, tail-biting block codes related to the Golay CC are

discussed. Especially, we focus on the extended (24; 12) Golay code,

the (32; 16) code as well as the (40; 20) and the (48; 24) double

circulant code, respectively. Moreover, it is shown how to encode

these codes in systematic form using a convolutional encoder with

feedback. This is important if the considered block codes are used for

parallel concatenation. Again, the transfer-matrix method is applied

to calculate the weight distributions of the codes. Simulation results

for both single block codes and concatenated codes will be presented.
