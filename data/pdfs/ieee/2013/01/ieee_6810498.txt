SOFT-INPUT SOFT-OUTPUT LINEAR PROGRAMMING DECODING FOR SPREAD SPECTRUM UNDERWATER ACOUSTIC COMMUNICATIONS
Erica L. Daly
The University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering
1406 W. Green St. Urbana, IL 61801 edaly@illinois.edu

ABSTRACT
This paper presents the soft-input soft-output (SISO) linear programming (LP) decoder. It is shown that the soft information gleaned from a pseudo-codeword solution to the LP optimization is not only useful, but that it can be used to form a superior channel estimate compared to the output from a SISO belief propagation (BP) decoder in certain situations.
Index Terms— LP decoding, short block length codes, underwater communications, stealthy communications

A method for extracting usable soft information from an LP decoder was presented in [8]. This paper continues to explore this concept. It is demonstrated that valuable soft information may be gleaned from the codeword or pseudocodeword output from the LP decoder. This information is delivered to the channel estimator to improve its estimate. It is shown that, in certain situations, an iterative receiver that updates its channel estimate using soft information from the SISO LP decoder outperforms a similar iterative receiver using a SISO BP decoder.

1. INTRODUCTION

2. STEALTHY UNDERWATER COMMUNICATIONS

Efficiently and accurately communicating short packets of data is a challenge. However, it may be advantageous to do so in certain situations such as what is encountered with low probability of detection spread spectrum underwater acoustic communications. In this case, if the channel is to be considered stationary for the entire duration of the packet, the packet must be short. In the large block length regime, capacity may be approached by protecting the data with a LDPC code and decoding it with a belief propagation (BP) decoder. However, in the short block length regime, the performance of the BP decoder is difficult to ensure analytically due to its sensitivity to short cycles [1].

When communicating through the ocean, acoustic communications are preferred over electromagnetic communications, because electromagnetic waves are dissipated due to the conductive nature of sea water. In contrast, acoustic signals at a few tens of kHz may propagate for several kilometers [9]. Underwater acoustic signals present several unique challenges. One is that the speed of sound is only 1.5 km/s, which leads to a high latency and a long delay spread. Delay spreads in excess of 5 ms have been reported [9]. Another challenge is mitigating the effects of the undulating ocean surface on the signal. Long transmissions suffer from a significant, unknown Doppler spread [9].

Linear programming (LP) decoders have been proposed as an alternative to BP decoders. These decoders relax the decoding problem into that of a linear program. LP decoders have a performance that is competitive with BP decoders [2] and a similar complexity [3]. They have the advantage of proven performance bounds as well as a guarantee that any integer-valued output is the ML codeword [4]. It has been shown that joint equalization and decoding may be accomplished within the LP framework by formulating the equalization task as a linear program [5, 6, 7], which suggests that soft information might be extracted from the linear programming decoder.

A critical capability for stealthy undersea missions is communications that are difficult to detect. A good candidate for low probability of detection underwater acoustic communications is direct sequence spread spectrum (DSSS) signals [10]. These signals are constructed by modulating the data symbols with a chipping sequence, which is a sequence of symbols at a rate that is an integer multiple of the symbol rate. Such signals may be transmitted at very low powers and recovered due to the processing gain achieved by correlating the received signal with the chipping sequence [10].

3. LP DECODING
LP decoding transforms the problem of finding the maximum likelihood (ML) estimate of x given the code structure into a constrained linear optimization problem. In order to describe the ith symbol estimate xˇi with only positive variables so that the problem may be formulated as a linear program, define a variable representing the estimate of the ith code bit cˇi and a second variable cˇ−i = 1 − cˇi. Then xˇi equals cˇi − cˇi−.
The solution to the LP optimization is constrained to lie within a polytope defined by a relaxation of the parity check constraints that define the structure of the code. The mth parity constraint may require that the binary sum of the M bits in the set Sm be equal to zero, which is equivalent to requiring that the sum of the M bits in the set Sm be even. 4. SISO LP DECODING

At iteration k of the receiver, the SISO LP decoder extracts soft information x˜(k) from the solution xˇ(k) to the LP problem. It is imagined that xˇ(k) is an observation of x¯, the mean
of the “true” soft output given the input, that is quantized to
the nearest constraint polytope vertex. The additional uncer-
tainty that this quantization introduces is accounted for in the covariance of x˜(k).

Although x¯(k) is not directly observed, it may be mod-
eled as a uniform distribution over a region determined by
the code polytope. This region is a pyramid oriented along the line between the origin and xˇ(k) and bounded by the
element-wise constraint −1 ≤ x ≤ 1. It may be bounded
further by observing that the receiver is expected to decrease
the entropy h¯ of its output after each iteration as it improves its estimate of the channel. A scalar α(k) is found so that the entropy associated with the soft symbol α(k)xˇ(k) is equal to the entropy associated with x˜(k−1). The x¯(k) region is the section of the pyramid that is outside a radius equal to α(k) times the magnitude of xˇ(k). For convenience, a Gaussian
distribution capturing the aggregate properties of the code is used to approximate the uniform distribution over the x¯(k)
region.

Three alternative receivers are simulated for comparison with the SISO LP decoder system. One is an iterative receiver using a SISO BP decoder. The second employs a decision feedback equalizer and a standard LP decoder. This receiver uses the soft outputs from the equalizer to improve the channel estimate. After several iterations of the equalizer, a single pass of the decoder is run. The third system does not use an error correcting code and uses the soft output of the equalizer to estimate the channel. In order for the comparison with the coded system to be fair, the 95 uncoded bits are spread with a 220 chip spreading sequence so that the transmissions are the same length for all three cases. Up to one million trials of each experiment were run, with the simulation stopping after encountering one hundred block errors.

5. SIMULATION SETUP
A block of 95 message bits b is encoded into 100 code bits c using a regular LDPC code with a weight of 2. The code is constructed to have as few extremely short cycles as possible, but there are necessarily significant numbers of short cycles in a code of this type. The fact that the receiver with a SISO LP decoder outperforms the receiver with a DFE and a hard decision LP decoder suggests that soft information output by the SISO LP decoder is useful.
Although the LP decoder does not initially perform as well as the BP decoder, its soft output generates a more accurate channel estimate by the tenth iteration of the receiver. This is likely due to convergence issues making the BP decoder more prone to large errors in its symbol estimate. This demonstrates that it is not only possible to extract useful soft information from a LP decoder, but that a SISO LP decoder is an attractive alternative to a SISO BP decoder in certain situations. It may also be an attractive choice compared with the uncoded system, as the stealthiness gained by transmitting at 1 dB less power may be worth the extra processing required to decode the error correcting code.

7. CONCLUSION
The SISO LP decoder was presented. It was demonstrated that an iterative receiver using a SISO LP decoder may outperform one using a SISO BP decoder given a high rate, short block length LDPC code. The SISO LP decoder is superior in this situation, because it does not suffer from convergence issues the BP decoder encounters arising from short cycles inherent in this type of code.
It is interesting that there are important and realistic situations where the SISO LP decoder presented in this paper

outperforms the SISO BP decoder. It is left to future work to formalize and improve the method for extracting soft information from the LP decoder.
