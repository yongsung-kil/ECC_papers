PG-LDPCC Codes in Turbo Equalizer Systems - A Study on the InÀuence of Design Parameters of
Terminated Convolutional Protographs

Patrick Grosa and Gerhard Fettweis Vodafone Chair Mobile Communications Dresden University of Technology (TU Dresden), 01062 Dresden, Germany Emails: {grosa, fettweis}@ifn.et.tu-dresden.de

Abstract—This paper deals with the study of parameter variations of protograph-based low-density parity-check convolutional codes (PG-LDPCCC) for a transmission system incorporating linear mapping and the turbo equalization approach. LDPCCCs in general raised a lot of attention recently due to their very good error correction performance. In this work the focus lies on codes derived from protographs due to the opportunity to use a tool called density evolution for the analysis. In order to produce convolutional variants two different parameters (syndrome former memory ms, termination length L) play an important role. Throughout this paper, the inÀuence of these parameters is analyzed by means of EXIT-Charts and Monte-Carlo-Simulations and their inÀuences on the unwrapped graph are discussed. Although both parameters have an impact on the shape of transfer curves, and therefore on the overall performance, the memory has a more dominant effect on the shape, especially at the start of the iterative process when a-prioi information is low. However in order to reduce the rate loss, which depends on both parameters, for a ¿xed permutation size and ms, the termination length L must be increased which in turn increases the code length.
Index Terms—LDPC Convolutional Codes, Turbo Equalizer, Protographs, Parameter Study, Syndrome Former Memory, Termination Length, Density Evolution, EXIT-Charts
I. INTRODUCTION
The demand for high data rate services has increased in recent years. Especially, rate requirements for communication systems within a short range, e.g. the transmission of FullHDVideo between a DVD player and an LCD, show a dramatic raise in the rate needed. A promising technique to meet this rate requirement is the ultra wide-band communication. However, such systems need to utilize a huge part of the spectrum available. With the knowledge of the sampling theorem in mind a high temporal resolution is needed to capture the energy of the signal transmitted. In addition, this high sampling rate helps to distinguish the number of delayed versions of the original signal from multiple paths. In order to overcome the issue of multipath propagation or even exploiting the energy that is received at the receiver from multiple paths the turbo equalization is a suitable approach. It is shown, e.g. in [1], [2], [3], that a proper choice of the channel code can boost the overall performance of the system. In previous publications it is shown that low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes that are optimized for the AWGNchannel also perform very well for channels with a small

number of relevant taps and therefore in the presence of low inter-symbol-interference (ISI) [4] . Nevertheless, this is not case when the ISI is increasing. For that reason, small modi¿cations of the underlying protographs can improve the performance as shown in [5]. In lights of that, the use of LDPC convolutional codes [6] based on protographs seem a reasonable approach. Since these codes offer another degree of freedom by introducing several parameters, their inÀuence on the code’s behavior needs to be studied. This work concentrates on the syndrome former memory and the termination length. The remainder of this paper is structured as follows. Section II de¿nes the considered system model. Protographs in the variant as template for block and convolutional codes are explained together with the methods used for the analysis of the protograph ensembles. The analysis of the protographs is presented in section IV. The results anticipated by the density evolution analysis are validated by means of Monte-Carlo-Simulations. Finally, the work is concluded in section VI.

the need for Monte-Carlo simulations. PGs are bipartite graphs

G(VP , CP , EP ) that consists of two distinct sets of vertices (|VP | = NP , |CP | = MP ) and a set EP of edges that connects
the two sets. Although PGs are very related to Tanner-Graph

IV. ANALYSIS
In the following section the techniques used for the analysis are introduced. A common method for the analysis of a system incorporating the turbo principle is the so-called EXIT-Chart, which will be explained in detail in the next subsection. Although it is possible to use this common tool also for the codes proposed, a more accurate and less time consuming approach is used. This method is based on the idea of tracking of statistical measures through the decoding process and is named density evolution.

A. EXIT-Chart analysis of the turbo receiver
The basic idea of a system enabling the turbo principle is the continuous exchange of information between different components in order to improve the performance of the overall system. This is done by exploiting several side information embedded in these components. The channel decoder has additional information of the relation between several bits of a code word thanks to the parity-check relations de¿ned by a parity-check matrix (for the LDPC case). In the system suggested, the soft-equalizer does not know anything about this relation but can produce information on the bits based on its knowledge of the distortions made by the channel and the mapping rules. However, the equalizer might be able to use the additional information on the bits generated by the channel decoder to improve the equalization process. In order to determine how the system’s overall performance changes during this process certain conditions of the side information (channel, mapping, channel code) will be ¿xed. Then, the information gain produced by a prede¿ned information is measured. This is done for every component of interest. As a metric the averaged mutual information (MI) regarding the code word is used, where the additional information entering the component is called a-priori and the gain of MI is called extrinsic, denoted by Iamod and Iemod, respectively. Here mod can represent either the decoder (D) or the soft-equalizer (E). Since the a-priori MI can vary from [0..1] the corresponding extrinsic MI can be measured for every value, which yields to a transfer characteristic of each component. B. EXIT function by means of density evolution

Here, we use a more appropriate way to obtain the EXIT function of the decoder by means of density evolution. First, introduced for unstructured LDPC codes in [16], it was adapted later also for protographs. In this approach it is not necessary to create a suf¿ciently large number of realizations of the decoding process. In short, a distinct input probability density function (pdf) for each variable node of the protograph is assumed (unpunctured nodes have an probability of 1 for LLR = 0). These pdfs are passed along the edges to the check nodes and combined regarding their operation and the results are fed back to the variable node, where they are also combined with the input pdfs. These results are passed to the check nodes, where the procedure starts over again. In this way the decoding process can be tracked. With that knowledge it is even possible to determine the pdf of the a-priori LLRs and the pdf of the extrinsic LLRs after decoding. Hence, we can use this knowledge to compute the a-priori MI and the extrinsic MI by (6).

E. Trade-off between code parameters and code length
The curve shaping behavior of both parameters, the syndrome former memory and termination length, is based on their modi¿cations on the convolutional protographs. In both cases the number of connections and the node types that connect these edges are the same as for the original protograph, except for both ends of the convolutional protograph. There, the number of connections is limited to the connections of the partial base matrices. And, by de¿nition, the number of edges incide at a check node at the ends is smaller or equal to the number of edges incident at a check node in the rest of the protograph. The consequence of this structure is that variable nodes at both ends of the graph are stronger protected than the rest, since a smaller number of variable nodes is involved in a check equation. As shown in [17] these better secured nodes are used to bootstrap the decoding process, i.e. the knowledge of the reliability at the ends is propagated from these ends trough the graph. VI. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper the basic parameters as the syndrome former memory and the termination length of terminated PGLDPCCCs are studied. Both parameters have an impact on the code’s performance, and therefore on the system for a particular channel characteristic, especially for the case of a small to medium a-priori information. In that way it may helps to boost the bootstrapping phase of the turbo equalization process. However, the changes of the syndrome former memory have a larger inÀuence on the code’s behavior and performance. Additionally, the impact of these protograph parameter variations is strongly inÀuenced by the underlying protograph. Finally, a smaller rate loss for a desired error correction performance and a ¿xed permutation size can only be achieved by a longer code, since the length of the codes and

the ratio between inner nodes and end nodes are determined
by the parameters studied.
