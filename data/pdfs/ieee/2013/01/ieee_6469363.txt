Design of LDPC Codes for Hybrid

10Gbps/100Gbps Optical Systems with Optional

Differential Modulation

Doris Pflueger, Gerhard Bauch Institute of Information Processing Universita¨t der Bundeswehr Mu¨nchen Email: {doris.pflueger, gerhard.bauch}@unibw.de
Abstract—For 100Gbit/s fiber optical long-haul transmission systems, digital signal processing and forward error correction (FEC) coding play an increasingly important role. A major problem for actually desired coherent detection is the occurrence of phase slips caused by imperfect carrier phase recovery. Differential modulation can help to overcome the problem of phase slips on the expense of a performance degradation referred to as differential penalty. On the other hand, differential modulation allows to apply an iterative detection scheme, where extrinsic information is exchanged among FEC decoder and differential detector.
In this paper, we address the fundamental question, under which conditions with respect to code rate and complexity it is possible to design irregular low density parity check (LDPC) codes which allow to compensate or even overcompensate for the differential penalty by turbo iterations. The optimum LDPC codes are in general different for coherent and differential detection.
However, e.g. in hybrid 100Gbit/s channels with adjacent 10Gbit/s channels differential detection is preferred due to the high phase slip probability. In contrast, pure 100Gbit/s channels typically do not require differential decoding. Therefore, the operators wish to switch between coherent and differential transmission depending on the current channel conditions without changing the FEC code.
The main contribution of the paper is the extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) chart based design and analysis of LDPC codes which provide the best performance in both differential and nondifferential detection.
I. INTRODUCTION
In next generation fiber optical long-haul transmission systems, extremely high data rates of 100 Gbit/s and beyond need to be transmitted reliably over long distances of 1000 km and more. Unlike in wireless communications, automatic repeat request (ARQ) protocols cannot be applied in optical communications. Therefore, very low bit error rates (BERs) of about 10−15 need to be guaranteed. In order to meet those challenging performance targets, digital signal processing, higher order modulation methods and forward error correction (FEC) coding play an increasingly important role. Digital signal processing stages in the detector perform equalization as well as carrier recovery and timing synchronisation tasks. This has to be paid for by a higher computational complexity.
For high performance transmission with higher order modulation schemes, coherent detection is desirable due to the higher sensitivity. However, accurate carrier phase estimation is still a challenge in the non-linear optical channel. In

A common method in order to overcome the detrimental effect of cycle slips is the application of differential modulation, where the information is contained in the phase difference of successive transmit symbols rather than in the absolute phase of individual symbols. Hence, for a phase estimation burst with constant phase error over several received symbols, the phase difference of successive symbols remains unchanged and, consequently, the information can still be recovered.
The drawback of differential modulation is a performance degradation compared to perfect coherent detection. This degradation is called the differential penalty. The probability of cycle slips increases with decreasing channel quality. Wavelength-division multiplex transmission with only polarization-division multiplexed-quadrature phase shift keying (QPSK) channels typically does not suffer from nonlinear phase noise and differential decoding is not required. in contrast a worst case scenario in wavelength-division multiplex transmission with varying channel quality is a hybrid system where a 100 Gbit/s polarization-division multiplexed QPSK channel is surrounded by 10 Gbit/s On-Off-keying channels which cause strong nonlinear channel cross-talk. In such a scenario, the operator may wish to switch on differential detection.
The impact of the differential penalty depends on the applied differential detector. One possibility for soft-output differential demodulation is provided by the trellis based algorithm by L. Bahl, J.Cocke, F.Jelinek and J.Raviv (BCJR algorithm) [2]. In contrast to other differential detection methods, the BCJR algorithm algorithm takes into account not only two successive received symbols but all received symbols in a block for computation of the soft output value of each symbol. It provides exact log likelihood ratios (LLRs) to the successive FEC decoder.

Such iterative schemes have the potential to reduce or even overcome the differential penalty. In contrast to differential modulation, non-differential modulation does not allow performance gains by iterations between FEC decoder and demodulator in case of Gray mapping and only limited gains in case of other mappings.
In fact, it has been shown in [3], that differential modulation with turbo detection can even overcompensate the differential penalty. The authors in [3] used a convolutional code for forward error correction, which is not suitable for providing the extremely low BERs required in optical communications. On the other hand, close to capacity performing channel codes such as low density parity check (LDPC) codes or turbo codes behave significantly different in a turbo scheme. Often, schemes with strong FEC codes do not benefit from an outer turbo loop to the differential detector. extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) charts [4] are a powerful tool for illustration of this behaviour. Moreover, EXIT charts can be used in order to design FEC codes which are adapted to the differential detector and allow to make iterations between FEC decoder and differential detector effective [5],[6]. For LDPC codes, this adaptation is done via optimization of the degree distributions of variable nodes and check nodes in the Tanner graph which describes the LDPC code.
In this paper, we are interested in the fundamental question, under which conditions it is possible to design LDPC codes which allow to compensate or even overcompensate for the differential penalty by turbo iterations to the differential detector. In particular, we consider different code rates and decoding complexity restrictions, where we roughly assess the decoding complexity by the node degrees in the Tanner graph.
We first separately design LDPC codes which are optimized for coherent detection with non-differential detection and compare their performance to LDPC codes which are optimized for differential modulation with turbo detection. This allows to conclude whether the differential penalty can be overcome if both the differential and the non-differential scheme apply the best possible in general different - code given defined rate and complexity restrictions.
The main contribution of the paper is the design proposal for LDPC codes which provide the best performance in both differential and non-differential detection. This allows the operator to switch between differential and non-differential transmission depending on the current channel quality without changing the FEC coding scheme. We show that LDPC codes which are optimized for best performance in both differential and non-differential detection show only marginal or no performance degradation compared to the worse of the separately optimized codes.

II. CODE OPTIMIZATION USING EXIT CHARTS
To improve the performance of the transmission iterative schemes between an inner and and outer decoder are employed. In each loop the new generated extrinsic information is exchanged between the serial concatenated inner and outer code and used to further improve the decoding results.The gain of such schemes depends a lot on the used inner and outer decoder. For an LDPC in coherent transmission the variable nodes correspond to the inner decoder and the check nodes to the outer decoder, see Figure 2 part (a). To include differential detection, an outer loop from the LDPC decoder to the differential demodulation is considered [7]. Then the inner decoder includes additionally the BCJR algorithm, while the outer decoder, the check nodes, remains unchanged, considering one iteration inside the LDPC code, see Figure 2 part (b). For these two cases different LDPC codes perform optimal. To analyse this EXIT charts provide a method to easily predict and visualize the success of iterating schemes for particular outer and inner decoder, as explained in [4], [8], [9].
The EXIT chart consists on two EXIT curves, one for each decoder. The EXIT curve shows the relation between the mutual information at the input of a decoder and the extrinsic mutual information at its output. Theoretically error free transmission is possible, when the EXIT chart is open, that means the curve of the inner decoder lies always above the curve of the outer decoder, so that there is an open tunnel between them ending in the upper left corner at mutual information 1. While the curve of the outer decoder remains constant for different channel SNR the curve for the inner decoder rises with increasing SNR. Thus for sufficiently high SNR the EXIT chart is open. In this SNR region, the so called waterfall region, the bit error rate has an almost vertical descend. The point of interest is the turbo cliff, i.e. the lowest

A. Irregular LDPC Codes and Degree Distributions

An LDPC code, defined by a sparse parity check matrix

H, can be described by a Tanner graph, which allows to

use message passing for decoding. The Tanner graph consists

of check and variable nodes,corresponding to the rows and

columns of H respectively. The edges in the graph correspond

to ’1’ in the parity check matrix. A variable node treats the

information received along all incoming edges as a repetition

code. The information received by the check nodes is treated

as a single parity check code. For a detailed explanation see

for example [10], [9], [8].

The check node degree is the number of edges connected to

one check node. And the variable node degree is the number of

edges ending in one variable node. A code is called regular, if

all variable nodes and all check nodes have the same degrees,

otherwise the code is called irregular code.

The check edge degree distribution is a vector ρ, where each

component ρi corresponds to the fraction of edges connected to check nodes of degree i of all edges. In the same way the

variable edge degree distribution λ is the vector that contains

all of fractions edges connecting in variable nodes of a certain

degree of all edges. For λ and ρ the following conditions must

III. OPTIMIZATION PROBLEMS
In this section we answer the question, if a differential scheme with iterative detection can overcome the differential penalty. If the differential penalty can be overcome by the iterative scheme depends on the SNR at the turbo cliff of the coherent and the differential scheme, i.e. the SNR at which the EXIT chart opens. SN RC refers to the SNR at the turbo cliff of the coherent case and SN RD refers to the SNR of the turbo cliff of the differential case, see Figure 3.
There are two cases as shown in Figure 3: (a) SN RC ≤ SN RD and (b) SN RC > SN RD. Before the waterfall region, that is before the tunnel in the EXIT chart is open, the LDPC code with differential detection performs worse than the one with coherent detection due to the differential penalty. In case (a), the waterfall region for the coherent code starts for smaller SNR than that of the differentially modulated code. Thus the advantage of the coherent case is actually increased over differential detection. Once the differential decoder also passes into the waterfall region it would need a much steeper decent than the coherent case to overcome the differential penalty. This is not possible, supposing strong LDPC codes are used with an almost perfect vertical decay. Thus only the turbo cliffs of the descents are taken into account in our analysis, while the slope of the curve is neglected. This means in case SN RC ≤ SN RD the differential penalty cannot be overcome. In case (b) the waterfall region of the LDPC decoder with BCJR algorithm starts at a lower SNR than that of the coherent detector. Thus the distance between both decoders is diminished and the penalty might be overcome. Therefore, a code that can overcome the differential penalty satisfies SN RC > SN RD. V. CONCLUSION
To overcome the differential penalty via a turbo loop between the differential demodulation and the LDPC decoder, the EXIT chart of the differential scheme has to open up at a lower SNR than the coherent scheme. An optimization problem is introduced that allows to compare the performance of the optimal LDPC code for the coherent scheme with the performance of the optimal LDPC code for the differential scheme for a given rate and comparable complexity. The optimization has been performed for the check edge degree distribution. It has been shown that for LDPC codes with low variable node degree the differential penalty can be compensated, while in the other cases the coherent scheme exceeds the differential one. The optimization problem is also used to search for codes that perform well in both, the coherent and the differential case to allow to switch between both cases depending on the channel conditions. With optimized check edge degree distributions the combined case performs about the same as the worst of the coherent and differential case, depending on the restrictions on the complexity. In future work also the variable edge degree distribution will be included to get a complete overview.
