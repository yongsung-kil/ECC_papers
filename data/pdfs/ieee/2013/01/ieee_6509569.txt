A 6.72-Gb/s, 8pJ/bit/iteration WPAN LDPC Decoder in 65nm CMOS

Zhixiang CHEN, Xiao PENG, Xiongxin ZHAO, Leona OKAMURA, Dajiang ZHOU and Satoshi GOTO Graduate School of Information, Production and Systems, Waseda University, Japan

Abstract â€“ An LDPC decoder in 65nm CMOS targeting WPAN (IEEE 802.15.3c) is presented with measurement results. A modified-PCM based message permutation strategy with compatible data flow is proposed to solve the network problem raised by high parallelism LDPC decoding. Compared to the state-of-art, decoder chip achieves 17.7%, 33.5% and 49% improvements in chip density, gate count and energy efficiency, respectively.
I Introduction
High data rate (5.775-Gb/s) defined in WPAN [1] challenges design of LDPC decoder with high decoding parallelism, where a main problem is on implementation of a permutation network (PN) between processing nodes. High-complexity PN results in routing congestion, big logic overhead, and low energy efficiency.
Previous work on WPAN LDPC decoder can be found in [2]-[4], where various techniques are proposed to reduce the number of logic gates or multiplexers for implementing PN.
II. Technical Novelties and Decoder Architecture
Conventionally, both message updating and permutation are based on the standard provided parity check matrix. (PCM) [2]-[4] For our proposed decoder, PN is designed based on a modified PCM with enhanced regularity. Combining with a compatible data flow, 16 permutation patterns for 4 code rates are resolved by a simple PN which contains no logic gates or multiplexers but only signal wires.
Decoder data flow and architecture are given in Fig. 1. 672 a posterior probability (APP) messages in 32 column blocks are processed parallelly by 21 macro layer processing engines. (MLPE) Registers are used for storing all kinds of messages to provide enough memory bandwidth. Two PNs, namely pre-PN and post-PN are interfaced with MLPE and APP registers for message permutation.
Shown in Fig. 2, virtual sub-blocks are added into original PCM to enhance regularities, i.e., row split and macro block (MB) cyclic shift features. Based on these features, two PNs are constructed. Each PN contains three stages, all of which are of fixed-patterns and implemented by connecting wires only, as shown in Fig. 3. After each macro-layer updating, APP messages in every macro block are cyclically shifted by one sub-block in post-PN, which makes it available to use a same pre-PN for next consecutive macro layer because of PCM regularities, i.e. MB cyclic shift. Similarly, macro layers in different code rates are resolved by stage 2 in Pre-PN. Thus, two PNs are shared by all 16 macro layers.
Shown in Fig. 4, for each MLPE, both 32 temporary and APP message updaters are divided into 4 groups for code-rate-depended configurations, e.g. two, one and one groups are assigned for row one, two and three in each macro layer of rate 5/8, respectively. A similar configuration

is also applied on a binary tree based min sorter for check message updating. For added virtual sub-block, a message bypass path is enabled with multiplexers controlled by a pre-set message active look up table. (LUT) A frame level pipelining is applied to improve the critical path, resulting in a doubled amount of message registers being implemented. Normalized min-sum algorithm with factor 0.75 is used. A more detailed description on this design is available in [5].
III. Implementation and Measurement
The design is implemented using a 65nm LVT CMOS process. The chip die photo and its specifications are shown in Fig. 5. A PLL macro is used to generate on-chip high-speed clock. Asynchronous input and output FIFOs are used for data signaling between different clock domains. The decoder core occupies 430.8K equivalent gates, where 96.8K is register overhead due to pipelining. Compared to the design in [2] (without pipeline), our proposed message permutation strategy contributes to a gate count reduction of 334K (pipeline overhead excluded), and an improved routing result in terms of chip density from 73.3% to 86.3%.
Chip is tested via an FPGA based evaluation system shown in Fig. 6. Binary phase shift keying (BPSK) modulated signals with additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) are generated by an FPGA, interfaced with ASIC chip. A test at a dedicated clock speed is declared success after one million code frames which are decoded and checked as correct. At a 1.2V supply, it achieves a maximum clock speed of 450MHz. At 1.2V, 400MHz and 10 iterations, it delivers a throughput of 6.72-Gb/s and dissipates a power of 537.6mW resulting in an energy efficiency 8.0pJ/bit/iteration. As a low profile, at 1.0V, 200MHz and 5 iterations, a same throughput is achieved at energy efficiency 6.2pJ/bit/iteration with maximum performance degradation 0.75dB at BER 10-5, as observed in Fig. 7.
