Technologies for the next generation of digital terrestrial television broadcasting
-Decoding method of LDPC codes in dual-polarized MIMO transmission-

Shingo Asakura, Kenichi Murayama, Takuya Shitomi, and Kazuhiko Shibuya

Abstract— NHK is conducting research on the next generation of digital terrestrial broadcasting to enable large-volume content services such as Super Hi-Vision (8K). In a previous study on dual-polarized MIMO transmission, we used low density parity check (LDPC) code for forward error correction (FEC) and developed a multi-dimensional interleaving scheme that reduces deterioration caused by the difference between the channel responses of both polarizations. In this study, we developed a decoding algorithm for LDPC code that iteratively uses the channel responses of dual-polarized MIMO transmission for the log likelihood ratio (LLR) calculation, and tested it in a computer simulation.
Index Terms—Low Density Parity Check (LDPC), Log Likelihood Ratio (LLR), Dual-polarized MIMO, Ultra-multilevel OFDM
I. INTRODUCTION
The NHK Science and technology research laboratories (STRL) are conducting research on large-capacity transmission technology for next-generation digital terrestrial broadcasting systems that will provide large volume content services such as Super Hi-Vision (SHV). As part of this development, we proposed large-capacity transmission technologies using ultra-multilevel (e.g., 1024QAM or 4096QAM) orthogonal frequency-division multiplexing (OFDM) and dual-polarized multiple-input multiple-output (MIMO) [1][2]. In our previous studies, the received powers of the horizontal polarized and vertical polarized waves were different because of their different transmission characteristics, and this degraded the BER characteristics. To alleviate this problem, we devised a low density parity check (LDPC) code

In this paper, we propose an LDPC decoding method using the channel response of dual polarized MIMO transmission to further improve the BER performance. Distortion of the channel response causes a power difference between carrier symbols, and this degrades the BER characteristics. We use the channel response to estimate the noise variance not only for the average value of all carrier symbols in the OFDM signal but also for the value of each carrier symbol, and we apply this in the LDPC decoding scheme. The proposed method is based on iterative computation of the LLR in the sum-product decoding process [4], and the LLR iterative computation takes into account the noise variance of each carrier symbol. These measures make the LLR computation more accurate and improve the decoding performance. We report on our verification of the proposed method in various simulated situations.

There are two reasons for that. First, the information about the difference in received power is included in the decoding process. Pseudo LLRs are more accurate with the noise variance estimation of each carrier symbol than with the average noise variance of all carrier symbols in the OFDM signal. This means the proposed method will outperform the conventional method if the CNRs of all carrier symbols in both polarizations are not on the same level.
Second, a complex carrier modulation scheme will make carrier symbols in an OFDM symbol obscure. Fig. 8 shows the signal arrangement of 64QAM and 4096QAM on the I-Q figure. In Fig. 8(a), there are four signal points (called “surrounded signal points”) that are surrounded by others (called “edge signal points”). In general, the surrounded signal points easily generate an error bit if the QAM scheme is applied. The ratio of surrounded signals to all signal points is 36/64 = 56.3%. On the other hand, in Fig. 8(b), there are many more surrounded signal points in 4096QAM than in 64QAM, and the ratio in this case is 3844/4096 = 93.8%. These values show that a complex carrier modulation scheme degrades the BER performance. Note that the proposed method generates two replica symbols, and the pseudo LLRs are iteratively calculated with them. If either of them is identical to the received carrier symbol, the pseudo LLRs will be calculated more accurately. Moreover, the pseudo LLRs make replica symbols more accurately. The iterative process uses two symbols side by side and alleviates the degradation in the BER if there are many surrounded signal points.
For this reason, it is clear that the LDPC decoding method performs well when the technologies of dual-polarized MIMO and the ultra-multilevel OFDM are used.

IV. CONCLUSION
We developed an LDPC code decoding method of using the channel responses of dual-polarized MIMO transmission for computing the LLR iteratively. The method generates the replica symbols from the tentative decision words and replaces the relevant bit data by 0 or 1. After that, the pseudo LLR is iteratively computed with the received signal and its noise variance, and the received data is decoded accurately. We conducted a computer simulation with various channel responses of dual-polarized MIMO transmission and found that the proposed method is superior to the conventional method. In the future, we will incorporate our method in a prototype modulator and demodulator and confirm its effect in a field experiment.
