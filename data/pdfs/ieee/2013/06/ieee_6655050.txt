Design of Rate-Compatible Efficiently-Encodable Generalized LDPC Codes

Tingjun Xie, Stephen G. Wilson Charles L. Brown Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering,
University of Virginia, Charlottesville, VA, U.S.A E-mail: {tx4c, sgw}@virginia.edu

Abstract—We study rate-compatible (RC) puncturing algorithms for efficiently-encodable (EE) generalized LDPC (GLDPC) codes of short-to-moderate blocklength. EE-GLDPC codes are systematic codes and well-suited for puncturing, thus they are able to address a rate constraint issue, which is an obvious disadvantage of traditional GLDPC codes. In this work, we propose systematic puncturing algorithms for EE-GLDPC codes. Simulation results show that the systematic puncturing has considerable performance improvement over random puncturing. In addition, the punctured codes, like their mother codes, have the advantage of good error floor performance, which can be a very important feature in practical applications. Finally, it is also demonstrated that, compared with LDPC codes, GLDPC codes have a better error floor performance, but suffer from a decoding threshold loss at high puncturing rates; nonetheless the tradeoff between error floor and decoding threshold loss can be conveniently implemented via the method of doping.
Keywords: Generalized LDPC codes, error floor, ratecompatible
I. INTRODUCTION
In last two decades, we have witnessed the renaissance of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1], due to their remarkable performance under iterative decoding. In the Tanner graph of LDPC codes, the check node (CN) imposes a single parity-check (SPC) constraint on the adjacent variable nodes (VNs). As a more general form of LDPC codes, Generalized LDPC (GLDPC) codes [2] can be obtained by using some linear code C(n, k) other than a SPC code as the check node constraint, where C(n, k) is commonly referred as component code or super check node (SCN), and the corresponding check matrix adjacency matrix. When carefully designed, GLDPC codes can show many potential advantages, such as good minimum distance [3], low error floor [4] and fast convergence speed. In this work, we call a GLDPC code “strict-sense” if it uses a linear code C(n, k) other than SPC code for all its CNs [3], and denote them by “sGLDPC codes”; if C(n, k) is adopted together with other SPC codes, they are called hybrid GLDPC codes, denoted by “hGLDPC codes” [5] [6]. Throughout the paper, “CN” is used as a generic term referring to either SPC or SCN.
As a subclass of GLDPC codes, efficiently-encodable GLDPC (EE-GLDPC) codes [7]–[9], enjoy linear-time encoding complexity. Similar to irregular repeat-accumulate (IRA) LDPC codes [10] and efficiently-encodable rate-compatible (EERC) LDPC codes [11], EE-GLDPC codes are systematic codes; when EE-GLDPC codes is encoded, an iterative erasure

decoder begins encoding with only the information bits, and the value of parity bits are determined progressively during the iterations until the whole codeword is recovered. EE codes can be punctured all the way up to rate 1 without causing any stopping sets in the punctured set itself. Due to these advantages, EE-GLDPC codes are naturally suitable for puncturing and are able to address the rate constraint issue of ordinary GLDPC codes, i.e. the dedicated sGLDPC codes are constrained to relatively lower rate.
Rate-compatible (RC) codes are commonly used in timevarying channels such as mobile and wireless communications due to its flexibility and efficiency. In rate compatible codes, codes of various rates are nested together and the higher-rate codes can be obtained by puncturing parity bits in lower-rate codes, i.e. the higher-rate codes are “embedded” in lower-rate codes, thus suitable for Type-II hybrid automatic repeat request (HARQ). Research on RC-LDPC codes can be found in [11]– [17]. In [17], the authors investigate the optimal puncturing distribution based on an asymptotic analysis and show that it will result in little loss in decoding threshold over a range of code rates. When the code length is finite, the notion of l-step recoverable (l-SR) nodes is important; a node is called l-SR if it will be recovered after the l-th iteration when the punctured codeword is encoded by a erasure decoder. In [15], Ha et al propose an greedy “group-and-sort” algorithm which tries to minimize the number of iterations required to recover the whole codeword for a given rate, by sequentially maximizing the size of l-SR node group for l = 1, 2.... A modified version of [15] appears in [12], where the cost functions of VNs and CNs are defined to further enhance puncturing performance. A puncturing method for protograph-based LDPC codes is proposed in [13], where the notion of “puncturing score” is introduced to speed up convergence, which is in accordance with the principle adopted in [15]. Finally, an optimized puncturing scheme for IRA-LDPC codes is devised by Yue et al [14].
While the RC-LDPC codes are receiving wide-spread investigations, there are few studies on RC-GLDPC codes. Compared with LDPC codes, GLDPC codes use more complicated component codes as check node constraint, therefore it is not easy to design a straightforward yet efficient puncturing algorithm for GLDPC codes. In this paper, we demonstrate that for EE-GLDPC codes, we can design good puncturing patterns to improve the performance of punctured codes rather

II. PUNCTURING ISSUES UNIQUE TO SCNS
Previous studies on the sequential puncturing of finitelength LDPC codes can be found in [11]–[15]. By sequential puncturing we mean puncturing the bits one by one from mother code rate Rm to the highest rate Rh, or unpuncturing from Rh to Rm [14]. In the study on puncturing LDPC codes, an important notion is l-step-recoverable (l-SR) codes [15]. If an iterative decoder is used to implement encoding, in this case all unpunctured bits are known, then a punctured parity bit (or equivalently, a VN in the Tanner graph) is identified as an l-SR node if it can be recovered after l iterations. Let Gl denote the set of l-SR nodes, and G0 be the unpunctured nodes. When designing a puncturing algorithm for LDPC codes, it is desirable that the puncturing pattern have a smaller maximum value of l, i.e. the number of iterations required to recover the whole codeword. We denote this number by L. Simulation shows that this criterion is also applicable for EEGLDPC codes. Before we present the puncturing algorithm for EE-GLDPC codes, we will discuss several issues unique to GLDPC codes.
Assume a linear code C(n, k) with minimum Hamming distance dmin is used as the component code of GLDPC code. Let E be the set of erased bits in the C(n, k), and |E| be the cardinality of E. If the maximum likelihood (ML) decoder is used, then we have the following results:
1) |E| < dmin: all |E| erasures can be fully recovered. 2) dmin ≤ |E| ≤ (n − k): all |E| erasures may or may not
be fully recovered. 3) |E| > (n − k): a portion of erasures could possibly be
recovered, but not all of the erasures.
A justification of these results is given below. Let E be the complementary set of E, and col(E) be the columns of paritycheck matrix corresponding to E.
Fact 1: an erased bit v in E receives no extrinsic information if and only if col(v) is in the vector space spanned by col({E \{v}}).
Fact 2: an unerased bit v in E receives no extrinsic information if and only if col(v) is in the vector space spanned by col({E }).
Since a SCN C(n, k) can recover up to (n − k) erasures, under AWGN channel another issue with GLDPC codes is the reliability of extrinsic messages from the SCN. We can show that the reliability of the extrinsic messages decreases as the

number of erasures increases. As an example, assume Hamming (15, 11) code is used as the component node, the EXIT curves [18] of erased information bit(s) for |E| = 1, 2, 3, 4 are drawn in Fig. 1. The EXIT curves of one erased bit for SPC(5, 4), SPC(6, 5) and SPC(7, 6) are also shown in Fig. 1.
Based on this result, it is advisable to puncture in the way that bits in Gl be uniformly connected to as many SCNs as possible rather than being clustered in a small group of SCNs, although in both cases all the bits in Gl can be recovered after l iterations.
In view of this, for GLDPC codes, we define the “deficiency of a CN” as the number of punctured nodes connected to it, denoted by τ . A CN is called deficient iff τ > 0. Let T = (τ1, τ2, ...τM ) be the deficiency vector of a punctured codeword, where M is the number of CNs in the adjacency matrix H and τi is the deficiency of CN i. In next subsection we will see that T serves as an approximate and efficient auxiliary measure on the goodness of puncturing pattern.
Finally, for GLDPC codes we no longer have the result in LDPC codes on the relationship between the reliability of a recovered punctured node and the number of unpunctured VNs in its recovery tree [15]. Again this is due to the decoding behavior difference of SPC and C(n, k): for LDPC codes the “tanh” update rule on SPC guarantees that the extrinsic message emitted from SPC is always less reliable than apriori messages, but this is not the case with GLDPC codes. For example in Fig. 1, when |E| = 1 it is possible that IE,C > IA,C as IA,C → 1. Though for GLDPC codes there are cases that the extrinsic message is more reliable than the a-priori messages, in practice due to the number of erasures in each CN and the actual range of IA,C , together with the message dependence caused by the finite girth of the code, it is still important to minimize L during the sequential puncturing.

Due to the dynamics of iterative decoding and the intersecting EXIT curves of various CNs, we find it very difficult determining the optimal scoring system mathematically, hence the heuristic approach is adopted instead: for punctured bit p ∈ GL assuming binary value Xp = {0, 1}, let Qp be the random variable representing the a-posteriori probability (APP) of p after the L-th iteration. We define Ip = I(Xp, Qp), which is the mutual information (MI) between Xp and Qp, and I = {I1, I2, ..., I|GL|} be the vector of MIs of the punctured bits in GL. The following four criteria, in order of descending importance, are used for determining the best candidate VN v.
1) L is the smallest after v is punctured. 2) W is the smallest after v is punctured. 3) the mean of I is the largest after v is punctured. 4) the variance of I is the smallest after v is punctured.
In practice, I is still difficult to calculate, so we adopt an additional assumption that, during Lth iteration, CNs take the messages of some specific quality IA as their inputs, and output the recovery messages to the nodes in GL, by such simplification I can be calculated easily. The puncturing process for hGLDPC codes is essentially the same with sGLDPC codes, except that the rules are modified to accommodate the structure of hGLDPC codes. The puncturing algorithm stops when all parity bits are punctured, where we obtain a set of RC codes from R = Rm to Rh = 1. The third criterion is very useful at the beginning of puncturing: criterion 3 will prefer SCNs over SPCs because they are more powerful, resulting in a better puncturing performance, as verified by simulations.

However, Cs begins to show serious waterfall performance degradation, especially at R = 0.9; there is about 0.7 dB loss compared with EERCLDPC code at BER=10−5. We argue this is due to the inherent weakness of sGLDPC code at very high code rates.
Assume a sGLDPC code and a LDPC code has the same recovery time L at very high rates (R ≥ 0.9). Since most of parity bits have been punctured, by some simple analysis we know that on average each SCN recovers (n − k) parity bits in each iteration. A C(n, k) SCN with deficiency τ = (n − k) suffers greatly from performance degradation; for example a Hamming (15, 11) component code with τ = 4 has an EXIT curve nearly the same with SPC(8, 7), such a sGLDPC code at R ≥ 0.9 is essentially equivalent to a heavily-punctured LDPC code with CN degree 8 and dv around 2, which implies a poor decoding threshold; on the contrary, the EERC-LDPC code has dvmax = 6 and a large portion of SPC(6, 5) checks, both of which greatly help improving the decoding threshold. As shown in Fig. 7, we designed a R = 0.467 EERC and IRA-LDPC codes with dvmax = 6; IRA-LDPC codes are unpunctured from Rh = 1 using the method in [14]; at puncturing rate R = 0.9, the average APP quality of parity bits in GL, measured in terms of MI, are drawn for EERC-LDPC, IRA-LDPC code and Cs. We can see the quality of parity bits of Cs are significantly inferior to LDPC codes, verifying the analysis above.
Finally, hGLDPC codes can be regarded as SCN-doped LDPC codes. The allowed higher degree VNs in hGLDPC codes can overcome the drawback of sGLDPC codes at high code rates, as shown in Fig. 6. In practice, the doping degree, i.e. the number of SCNs in hGLDPC codes, can be easily and smoothly adjusted to encompass both advantages of sGLDPC and LDPC codes.

VI. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
EE-GLDPC codes are systematic and suitable for punctur-
ing, and a good puncturing algorithm can to a large degree
eliminate the rate constraint issue of traditional sGLDPC
codes. In this paper, we study the rate-compatible punctur-
ing for EE-GLDPC codes: two greedy systematic puncturing
algorithms are devised to provide good puncturing patterns for
EE-sGLDPC and EE-hGLDPC codes, and it is shown that it
can significantly improve the code performance compared with
random puncturing. Moreover, rate-compatible EE-GLDPC
codes show advantages over EERC-LDPC and IRA-LDPC
codes, especially at high SNRs, over a wide range of code
rates. Future work includes the study of optimized puncturing
of EE doubly-generalized LDPC codes, developing an across-
the-board puncturing principle for EE codes.
