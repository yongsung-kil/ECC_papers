Enhancing Iterative Decoding of Cyclic LDPC Codes Using Their Automorphism Groups
Chao Chen, Baoming Bai, Member, IEEE, Xinquan Yang, Li Li, and Yang Yang

Abstract—For cyclic LDPC codes, we propose to use their automorphism groups to improve the iterative decoding performance. The basic idea is to construct nonequivalent paritycheck matrices via column permutations. Three types of iterative decoders are devised to take advantage of the code’s automorphism group. In this paper we focus on cyclic LDPC codes defined by a circulant parity-check matrix and consider two known subgroups of the automorphism group of a cyclic code. For the large class of idempotent-based cyclic LDPC codes in the literature, we show that the two subgroups only provide equivalent parity-check matrices and thus cannot be harnessed for iterative decoding. Towards exploiting the automorphism group of a code, we propose a new class of cyclic LDPC codes based on pseudo-cyclic MDS codes with two information symbols, for which nonequivalent parity-check matrices are obtained. Simulation results show that for our constructed codes of short lengths, the automorphism group can significantly enhance the iterative decoding performance.
Index Terms—Cyclic low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, circulant, iterative decoding, the automorphism group of a code, pseudo-cyclic maximum-distance-separable (MDS) codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
W ITH the advent of Turbo codes [1] and the rediscovery of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [2], iterative decoding has attracted a lot of attention in recent years. With a reasonable complexity, iterative decoding achieves very good performance for these codes. In view of this, some researchers have adapted this decoding to classical codes.
Most classical codes are defined by high-density paritycheck (HDPC) matrices, whose Tanner graphs have a large number of short cycles. For this reason, iterative decoding often performs rather poorly for these codes. To mitigate the deleterious effect of short cycles, Jiang and Narayanan [3] and Kothiyal et al. [4] proposed adaptive versions of iterative decoding, respectively. As a result, the performance was greatly improved. However, due to the required matrix reduction after each iteration, a significant increase in decoding complexity was incurred.
On the other hand, classical codes are known to have a very rich algebraic structure. To overcome the adverse effect

The idea was first suggested by Jiang and Narayanan in [5] for decoding Reed-Solomon (RS) codes. Subsequently, Halford and Chugg [6] extended this work and presented the Random Redundant Iterative Decoding (RRD) algorithm. A parallel work was due to Hehn et al. [7], [8], who proposed the Multiple-Bases Belief-Propagation (MBBP) decoding algorithm. The main difference between RRD and MBBP is the way of using the code’s automorphism group. Specifically, RRD works in a serial fashion on some permuted received vectors, while MBBP operates in a parallel manner on a collection of column-permuted parity-check matrices. Recently, Dimnik and Be’ery [9] proposed the modified RRD (mRRD) algorithm, which shares some features of both RRD and MBBP. Simulations showed that mRRD achieves a much lower complexity.
Empirically, the automorphism group aided iterative decoding seems more suitable for short HDPC codes [8]. With the increase of code length, the performance gain becomes less significant. To address this issue, Ouzan and Be’ery [10] proposed moderate-density parity-check (MDPC) codes, whose parity-check matrices have a moderate number of one’s. They showed that with automorphism group aided iterative decoding, MDPC codes of moderate lengths can perform better than HDPC codes at similar rates.
As a related earlier work [11], Andrews et al. pointed out that iterative decoders based on different parity-check matrices often respond in different manners. Therefore, one can build multiple decoders, and hopefully, there exists one that decodes correctly. Recently, the idea was further developed by Declercq for decoding dense or locally-dense parity-check codes [12]. The author introduced the concept of “decoder diversity” and constructed multiple nonbinary decoders based on the observation that a binary code can have different representations in terms of nonbinary parity-check matrices [13].
In this paper, we are interested in applying automorphism group aided iterative decoding techniques to LDPC codes. In particular, we will focus mainly on how and to what extent the code structure can improve the iterative decoding performance. Since the automorphism group of a general LDPC code is not readily determined, we shall confine ourselves to cyclic LDPC codes. For a cyclic code, two particular subgroups of the automorphism group are well known [14]. Based on a given parity-check matrix, the two subgroups are partitioned into a number of equivalence classes. By doing so, we want to know how many nonequivalent parity-check matrices can be used for iterative decoding.

As a shortcoming, the proposed construction fails to provide high-rate cyclic LDPC codes of short lengths. For future work, we may consider how to construct such codes that can be assisted by the automorphism group.
For the existing LDPC codes in communications standards, iterative decoding performs well by itself. Moreover, a majority of these codes have a great deal of algebraic structure (e.g., quasi-cyclic designs). It would be valuable to investigate how to improve the performance by identifying and exploiting the automorphism group.
Recently, the automorphism group was introduced to improve the performance of linear programming (LP) decoding for HDPC codes [35]. With the constructed codes, our work may facilitate investigating the automorphism group aided LP decoding for LDPC codes.
Finally, we wish to reiterate that code structure is an asset to soft-decision decoding. It would be interesting to develop new decoding methods that can exploit some code structures.
