Error Rate Estimation of Low-Density Parity-Check Codes Decoded by Quantized Soft-Decision Iterative Algorithms
Hua Xiao, Amir H. Banihashemi, and Mehdi Karimi, Student Member, IEEE

Abstract—This paper describes a combinatorial approach to estimate the error rate performance of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes decoded by (quantized) soft-decision iterative decoding algorithms. The method is based on efficient enumeration of input vectors with small distances to a reference vector whose elements are selected to be the most reliable values from the input alphabet. Several techniques, including modified cycle enumeration, and the efficient derivation of problematic inputs for finer quantizers from those of coarser ones are employed to reduce the complexity of the enumeration. The error rate estimate is derived by testing the input vectors of small distances followed by estimating the contribution of larger distance vectors. We demonstrate by a number of examples that the proposed method provides accurate estimates of error rate with computational complexity much lower than that of Monte Carlo simulations, especially at the error floor region.
Index Terms—Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, finitelength LDPC codes, quantization, iterative decoding, softdecision decoding algorithms, error floor, error rate estimation.
I. INTRODUCTION
A SYMPTOTIC analysis of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, such as density evolution [15] and extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) chart [21], is usually based on the assumption that the code block length is infinity and the corresponding Tanner graph (TG) is cycle-free. However, in practice, one has to always deal with finite-length codes whose TGs contain many small cycles. For finite-length codes, firstly the performance deviates significantly from the asymptotic results of infinite block length [6], and secondly the concentration results break down and the performance of a given code can be much different from the ensemble average, particularly at the low error rate (error floor) region [6]. In many applications, such as optical communications and storage devices, error rates in the order of 10−12 to 10−20 are required. Since such error rates are far from the reach of

The average performance of ensembles of regular finitelength LDPC codes were studied in [6], where the decoding failures of the belief propagation (BP) algorithm over a binary erasure channel (BEC) were related to graphical objects called stopping sets. No general results, similar to those of [6], are available for other channels and other decoding algorithms. It is known that the performance of LDPC codes at the error floor region under iterative decoding algorithms is closely related to graphical structures in the TG, called trapping sets [16]. Several contributions [16], [5], [4], [17], [19], [20], [3], [7], [18], [10], [11], [12] have been made to estimate the performance of a given LDPC code based on identifying and enumerating dominant trapping sets in the TG. For an updated list of references on search techniques for finding dominant trapping sets of LDPC codes, the reader is referred to [12]. Richardson [16] proposed a semi-analytical technique which predicts the performance of a given code in the error floor region on the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel. Cole et al. [5] devised a three-step algorithm for estimating the frame error rate (FER) floors of soft-decision LDPC decoders on the AWGN channel using importance sampling. Importance sampling was also used in [2] and [14] to estimate the error floor of LDPC codes. Estimation of the FER of Gallager’s algorithms A (GA) and B (GB) for certain categories of LDPC codes was also presented in [4], [17]. Inspired by statistical physics, the authors of [19], [20], [3] estimated the FER of the min-sum (MS) algorithm for a given code using the concept of “instanton”, which is the most probable configuration of the noise to cause a decoding error. In [7], the error floor of structured LDPC codes was estimated based on graphical structures called absorbing sets and by using importance sampling. Schlegel and Zhang [18] estimated the error floor of regular LDPC codes based on a linear dynamic model for absorbing sets.
The structure of trapping sets for different codes and different iterative decoding algorithms is complex and unknown in general [22]. Heuristics are thus used to identify and enumerate them [16]-[17]. In an earlier paper [22], we proposed a method to estimate the performance of LDPC codes decoded by hard-decision iterative decoding algorithms over a binary symmetric channel (BSC). The following fact shows that the estimates of [22] are the hard-decision parallel of Estimate (3), where the alphabet size is two and the Euclidean distance is replaced by the Hamming distance.

C. Modified Cycle Enumeration Algorithm and Modified Estimation Equation
Cycle enumeration proposed in [23] has been shown to be a powerful tool in estimating the error rate of hard-decision iterative algorithms which can reduce the computational complexity by several orders of magnitude compared to direct enumeration. Instead of exhaustive search, cycle enumeration focuses on input vectors whose error locations correspond to small cycles in the underlying TG, which are believed to be problematic structures for iterative decoding algorithms.3
Let Cl denote the set of variable nodes in cycles of length l, l = g, g + 2, ... (g is the girth of the graph). In cycle enumeration [23], cycles of different lengths are enumerated, starting from those in Cg. For a q-bit decoder, for each cycle, input vectors are generated by setting the vector elements equal to 2q−1 − 1 in all positions except those involved in the cycle. For the positions involved in the cycle, we consider all the permutations associated with the 2q − 1 possible values

(2q − 1)l/2 test vectors. Note that, the (2q − 1)l/2 test vectors correspond to different values of d. By passing the test vectors
to the decoder, we obtain vectors in sets Ed for different distances.
Since we are interested in sets Ed with small values of d, we do not need to pass all the (2q − 1)l/2 vectors to the decoder. In fact, instead of testing all possible vectors corresponding to
one cycle set for a given l and then going to the next larger cycle set (with length l + 2), we take a different approach called modified cycle enumeration algorithm. This approach
is summarized in Algorithm 1.
In the modified cycle enumeration algorithm, the input
vectors are generated based on their distance to R in increasing
order. For a given distance, we enumerate all possible vectors
corresponding to cycles under consideration. We first generate cycle sets of different lengths, e.g., Cg, Cg+2, · · · . We use the set Tk {Cg, Cg+2, · · · , Cg+2k}, k = 0, 1, · · · , as possible locations where the input value is less than the maximum value of 2q−1 − 1. Such locations are called error locations, and the corresponding input values are referred to as error patterns.4
We then create another list PLk, which stores error patterns corresponding to the elements in the set Tk in increasing order of the distance. For a given distance d ∈ Dk, PLk stores error patterns of distance d with the size of the support set (support size) equal to g/2,· · · , (g + 2k)/2. The number of input vectors increases exponentially with the value of q as |V| = (2q − 1)n. Although the modified
cycle enumeration algorithm (Algorithm 1) greatly reduces
the search space, the computational complexity is still high for
many practical cases where the block length and the value of q
are large. Due to the relatively low computational complexity
for the cases with small q (e.g., 2, 3), we would like to obtain
the estimates for larger values of q based on the estimates we
already have for smaller values of q so that we do not need
to repeat Algorithm 1 for different values of q.
Consider the application of Algorithm 1 to a q1-bit decoder. The algorithm returns vector sets Edq,1l, d ∈ Dq1, l ∈ L, where Dq1 is the set of distances and L is the set of locations. To obtain estimates for a q2-bit decoder (q2 > q1), we use L as the location set. Since cycles in the set L are found to be
problematic structures for the q1-bit decoder, one would expect that with high probability, they are problematic structures for the q2-bit decoder as well. For each location l ∈ L, error patterns corresponding to l for the q2-bit decoder are generated based on vectors in sets Edq,1l, d ∈ Dq1 . Each quantization interval in the q1-bit case is divided into several intervals in the q2-bit case, which means that, each integer r ∈ Aq1 corresponds to several integers in Aq2 . For example, it took about 13 and 20 hours to obtain the FER estimates reported in Fig. 7 for the (1008, 504) and (2000, 1000) codes, on a desktop computer with 2GHz Core 2 Duo CPU and 2 GB RAM, respectively.6 Thus, it is of great practical interest to find more efficient and accurate methods of estimating the sets Ed for small values of d, particularly for larger block lengths, and codes with larger variable degrees or with highly irregular degree distributions.
It is noteworthy that different components of this work or their variants can be mixed and matched with other techniques to provide custom-made solutions for error rate estimation of different codes decoded by different iterative decoding algorithms over different channels. These components are: (a) the estimation technique, summarized in (4); (b) the modified cycle enumeration method of Section II-C and the corresponding estimation equation (6); and (c) the efficient estimation of the FER of q-bit decoders with large q based on the results for smaller values of q. For example, if one develops a more efficient and accurate method of estimating the sets Ed for small values of d (input vectors that are close to the most reliable vector and cause decoder failure), one can still use the components (a) and (c) to obtain an efficient and accurate estimate of the FER.
Finally, one should note that our estimation technique is based on a number of assumptions that are mainly verified through experimental results. We thus can provide no guarantee that the same technique can always work for every combination of code/channel/decoding algorithm/quantization scheme. We however hope that our results motivate further research in this area.

IV. CONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE WORKS
In this paper, we proposed an algorithm to estimate the performance of a given finite-length LDPC code decoded by a q-bit (soft-decision) iterative decoder. The proposed method can be applied in principle to any symmetric quantized version of different soft-decision decoding algorithms. We showed by a number of examples that the proposed method can provide accurate FER estimates for the whole SNR region of practical interest. The computational complexity of the proposed method can be much less than that of the Monte Carlo simulation, especially at the low FER region

Amir H. Banihashemi (S’90-A’98-M’03-SM’04) received the B.A.Sc. degree in electrical engineering from Isfahan University of Technology, Isfahan, Iran in 1988, and the M.A.Sc. degree in communications engineering from the University of Tehran, Tehran, Iran, in 1991, with the highest academic rank in both classes.
From 1991 to 1994, he was with the Electrical Engineering Research Center and the Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, Isfahan University of Technology. During 1994-1997, he was with the Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, University of Waterloo, Waterloo, Ontario, Canada, for his Ph.D. degree. He held two Ontario Graduate Scholarships for international students during this period. In 1997, he joined the Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, University of Toronto, Toronto, Ontario, Canada, where he worked as a Natural Sciences and Engineering Research Council of Canada (NSERC) Postdoctoral Fellow. He joined the Faculty of Engineering at Carleton University in 1998, where at present he is a Professor in the Department of Systems and Computer Engineering. His research interests include coding and information theory, digital and wireless communications, network coding, theory and implementation of communication algorithms, and compressed sensing and sampling. He has published more than 150 papers in refereed journals and conferences. Dr. Banihashemi served as an Associate Editor for the IEEE TRANSACTIONS ON COMMUNICATIONS from 2003 to 2009. He is the Director of the Broadband Communications and Wireless Systems (BCWS) Centre at Carleton University. He has been involved in many international conferences as chair, member of technical program committee, and member of organizing committee. These include co-chair for the Communication Theory Symposium of Globecom’07, TPC co-chair of the Information Theory Workshop (ITW) 2007, member of the organizing committee for the International Symposium on Information Theory (ISIT) 2008, and co-chair of the Canadian Workshop on Information Theory (CWIT) 2009. Dr. Banihashemi is a recipient of Carleton’s Research Achievement Award in 2006 and 2012, and Faculty Graduate Mentoring Award in 2011. He is also awarded one of the NSERC’s one hundred 2008 Discovery Accelerator Supplements (DAS).

Mehdi Karimi (S’10) received the B.A.Sc. degree and M.A.Sc degrees both in electrical engineering from Isfahan University of Technology, Isfahan,Iran in 2003 and 2005, respectively. From 2005 to 2008, he was with the Information and Communication Technology Institute, Isfahan University of Technology. Since 2008, he has been pursuing his Ph.D. at Carleton University, Canada. His research interests include graphical modeling, iterative coding, statistical signal processing and wireless communications.
