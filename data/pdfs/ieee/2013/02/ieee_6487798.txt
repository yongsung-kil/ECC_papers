24.3 Ultra-Wide Body-Bias Range LDPC Decoder in 28nm UTBB FDSOI Technology
Philippe Flatresse1, Bastien Giraud2, Jean-Philippe Noel1, Bertrand Pelloux-Prayer1, Fabien Giner1, Deepak-Kumar Arora3, Franck Arnaud1, Nicolas Planes1, Julien Le Coz1, Olivier Thomas2, Sylvain Engels1, Giorgio Cesana1, Robin Wilson1, Pascal Urard1
1STMicroelectronics, Crolles, France, 2CEA-LETI-MINATEC, Grenoble, France, 3STMicroelectronics, Greater Noida, India
This paper presents an IEEE 802.11n Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) decoder implemented in 28nm Ultra-Thin Body and BOX Fully Depleted SOI (UTBB FDSOI), and demonstrates the performance gains of this circuit vs. 28nm LP high-κ metal-gate CMOS bulk technology. It also introduces extended body bias (BB) design techniques to take advantage of specific features of the UTBB technology to overcome the +/-300mV BB range limitation of conventional bulk technologies [1].
A chip embedding a family of LDPC decoders has been fabricated in both 28nm LP bulk and 28nm UTBB production processes from STMicroelectronics [2]. The 28nm UTBB process is a gate-first high-κ metal-gate technology implementing fully depleted transistors thanks to an ultra-thin silicon body (7nm) and BOX (25nm), giving them an excellent electrostatic control that enables gate lengths down to 24nm, low DIBL (80mV/V), and best-in-class threshold voltage variability (Avt=1.1mV/μm). Thanks to the thin BOX, this technology also offers an extended body factor (exceeding 85mV/V), exploited in the LDPC design. The chip has been designed using standard tools and design flows. 28nm UTBB libraries were directly ported from 28nm LP Bulk and re-characterized for timing/power. Design synthesis constraints and verification steps were adjusted to take advantage of the large expected performance gain and to enable the additional wide voltage range. No additional design steps were required, demonstrating the ease of design integration in this planar technology.
There are 3 instances of the LDPC decoder in the circuit (chip micrograph in Fig. 24.3.7). The decoder supports the 12 modes required by the 802.11n wireless standard: 4 code rates (1/2, 2/3, 3/4, 5/6) over 3 possible block sizes (648b, 1296b and 1944b) [3]. Each decoder’s area is 1.1mm², including 2 types of power switches (UTBB standard and DTMOS), on-chip process monitoring and functional-mode auto-test hardware, as shown in Fig. 24.3.1. These decoders achieve the full functionality of a min-sum 802.11n LDPC decoder with a throughput of 1.03Gb/s. The first two instances have the same standard-cell implementation for a fair comparison. The first instance (LDPC_CW) is low standby power-oriented and uses a conventional well (CW) approach to enable forward BB (FBB) and reverse BB (RBB), both in bulk and UTBB. The second instance (LDPC_FW) is high-performance-oriented and demonstrates a new well configuration consisting of flipping well types (Fig. 24.3.2) [4]. This flip-well (FW) approach is only possible in UTBB, due to the buried oxide ensuring total dielectric isolation of the device. It maintains the p-well/n-well diode in reverse mode, which extends the FBB range, thus maximizing FBB effect. The third instance (LDPC_CW with SRAM) uses the same BB as LDPC_CW, with an SRAM-based LDPC architecture. There are 14 4Kb single-port SRAM (6T0.152μm²) macros, all biased (array and periphery) in the same way as the standard cells.
All 3 instances are fully functional over the full supply voltage range. Fig. 24.3.2 illustrates the range of BB voltages that we can apply in UTBB technology. The BB range in the bulk technology is limited to -300mV in RBB due to GIDL, while FBB is limited to +300mV because of source-drain junction leakage and latch-up risk at higher voltage and temperature. The UTBB technology enables an extended body bias range from -3V (RBB) to +300mV (FBB) in the CW implementation, while the LDPC_FW implementation enables BB ranges from -300mV (RBB) to +3V (FBB). This provides designers with a new lever for energy-efficiency optimization, performance boosting, ultra-low-voltage functionality and leakage reduction.
Fig. 24.3.3 shows silicon measurements of operating frequency gain in the UTBB vs. the bulk implementation, over the full VDD operating range at 25°C. While bulk designs only operate from 600mV to 1.5V, UTBB designs operate from 410mV to 1.5V for all instances, including the scenario with SRAMs without BB. When a FBB of 1V is applied, LDPC_FW can operate down to 350mV. At nominal supply voltage (1V), all the 3 UTBB instances reach at least 295MHz with no

BB, while the bulk instance only reaches 206MHz (with a FBB of 300mV). UTBB offers a +43% speed increase, thanks to the better electrostatic control offered by the technology. Owing to the FW design technique, wide FBB is made possible in UTBB. The LDPC_FW decoder, measured at a FBB of 1V, shows an additional gain of 21% vs. UTBB with no BB, and 73% frequency gain vs. bulk for a FBB of 300mV, which corresponds to at least two technology nodes in terms of speed improvement (assuming an average +30% speed improvement per node according to the ITRS [5]). In overdrive (1.3V), the 3 UTBB instances continue to outperform bulk performance by 29%, while the LDPC_FW decoder shows a gain of 46%, with a FBB of 1V. The maximum LDPC_FW frequency measured on the fastest dice reached 534MHz at VDD 1.5V, with a FBB of 1V.
At low voltage, UTBB outperforms bulk technology demonstrating the performance benefits of improved electrostatic control devices. Bulk implementations only reach 80MHz at VDD=0.7V, while the UTBB LDPC_CW and LDPC_FW designs reach 147MHz (+83% vs. bulk) with no FBB, and LDPC_FW reaches 214MHz (+168% vs. bulk) with 1V BB. The results demonstrate the design performance gain (speed and power) obtained through the use of 28nm UTBB technology vs. 28nm LP bulk and which is competitive vs. recent next-node introductions (Fig. 24.3.3).
Fig. 24.3.4 shows normalized leakage power vs. Vbb at VDD=0.6V and at 25°C, on the full RBB and FBB ranges for bulk and UTBB circuits (LDPC_CW and LDPC_FW instances). LDPC_CW achieves a 10× leakage reduction at a RBB of -1V vs. no BB, while bulk only achieves a leakage 2× reduction. On the other hand, a performance-oriented design like LDPC_FW is able to achieve a 2× leakage reduction, with a lower absolute value than bulk.
Fig. 24.3.5 highlights the energy efficiency of the UTBB technology at 25°C. A given operating frequency can be achieved with a 200mV lower VDD in UTBB when no BB is applied compared to a bulk FBB of 300mV. Furthermore, an additional 100mV of VDD reduction can be achieved in UTBB by applying a 1V FBB, leading to a 49% reduction in total power vs. bulk. Similarly, at constant power consumption, the frequency achieved by the UTBB design is 35% higher than bulk.
In UTBB technology, when the designer is looking for the optimal energy point at a given frequency, the wider range of FBB enables one go further than the techniques available in bulk. Fig. 24.3.6 shows the optimal VDD/Vbb values to achieve the lowest Energy-Delay Product (EDP). This metric enables designers to select the best tradeoff in delay and energy for a given operating point. We are able to decrease minimum bulk EDP (1V/0.3V) by 43% with LPDC_FW (0.7V/0.5V). We also achieve a power reduction of 24% on LDPC_FW by changing the VDD/Vbb couple from 0.8V/0V to 0.7V/1V.
This paper reports at 28nm UTBB VLSI circuit that is fully functional from 410mV to 1.5V, including SRAMs. It also demonstrates a straightforward migration path from bulk to UTBB for circuit design. Thanks to the flip-well design technique, we achieved up to 49% power reduction or 35% speed gain vs. bulk for performance-oriented designs (LDPC_FW). We also demonstrated that the same technology and library offers a 10× leakage reduction vs. bulk for low standby power applications (LDPC_CW with or without SRAMs).
