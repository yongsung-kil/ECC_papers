24.1 A 10.4pJ/b (32, 8) LDPC Decoder with Time-Domain Analog and Digital Mixed-Signal Processing
Daisuke Miyashita1, Ryo Yamaki2, Kazunori Hashiyoshi3, Hiroyuki Kobayashi1, Shouhei Kousai1, Yukihito Oowaki1, Yasuo Unekawa1
1Toshiba, Kawasaki, Japan, 2Toshiba, Yokohama, Japan, 3Toshiba Microelectoronics, Kawasaki, Japan
Analog computation is potentially more efficient in certain arithmetic operations since a single wire can represent multiple bits of information, while digital systems retain advantages, for example, in logical operations. However, the use of conventional voltage-domain analog computation [1, 2] is limited due to its poor scalability, design complexity, and the overhead of interface circuits (i.e. ADC/DAC) to a surrounding digital system. Therefore, an alternate technique is required for exploiting the efficiency of analog computation. In this paper, we propose time-domain analog and digital mixed (TDMixed) signal processing, wherein time instead of voltage is utilized as the analog signal. To verify the validity of the TDMixed signal processing, we implement an (32, 8) low-density parity-check (LDPC) decoder in 65nm CMOS. The decoder achieves power and area efficiencies of 10.4pJ/b and 6.1Gb/s/mm2, respectively.
Figure 24.1.1 shows the principle of the proposed TDMixed signal processing. In the time domain, data is represented by the time when the voltage transition occurs. The simplified mechanisms for converting digital-domain data and timedomain data with a digital-to-time converter (DTC) and a time-to-digital converter (TDC) are illustrated in the lower part of the figure. The DTC is realized by selecting a between delayed or non-delayed CLK signal according to a digital input signal. The TDC is realized with flip-flops, which output digitized data according to whether the input’ leads or lags the CLK’ (lower right of Fig. 24.1.1). Since time is an analog quantity, a single wire has infinite information in a cycle, in principle. This is more efficient than a digital representation in which a single wire has only one bit of information. Comparing to conventional voltage-domain analog and mixed-signal techniques [1, 2], TDMixed signal processing has significant advantages as follows: Area and power overhead for data conversion is reduced by using the proposed DTC and TDC units instead of a typically large and power-hungry DAC/ADC for voltage-domain analog techniques. Indeed, the proposed 5b TDC consumes only 3.0fJ/conversion-step by simulation, which is 1.5× better than the 4.4fJ/conversion-step of the best ADCs reported in ISSCC [3], and the TDC occupies only 107 gates (154μm2 in 65nm CMOS). Furthermore, since the DTC/TDC is composed of only logic gates, the TDMixed signal processing circuit is scalable and can be implemented with a conventional digital IC design flow.
One of the promising applications of TDMixed signal processing is an LDPC decoder. LDPC codes are attractive due to their excellent error correction performance. They have been adopted for a wide range of applications, such as NAND flash memories [4] and wireless communication standards [5-6]. In an LDPC decoder, probability messages with 5b resolution for each bit constituting a codeword are iteratively processed and communicated. The large number of wires as well as the gates have a crucial impact on the power and area efficiency [5]. Similar to the pulse-width message encoding proposed in [7], which represents information by the number of clock cycles a wire is held high, TDMixed signal processing can be applied to reduce gate count and wiring in a MIN-SUM LDPC decoder. The proposed TDMixed signal processing significantly mitigates the wiring issue because each probability message is conveyed in a single wire. The main calculations in the MIN-SUM algorithm for LDPC decoding are the minimum (MIN) and summation (SUM) functions. As shown in Fig. 24.1.2, an OR gate finds the minimum value by passing the first signal to arrive of the input time-domain signals. Cascading DTCs realize summation in time domain. Therefore, excluding data converters, only one and zero gates are required for MIN and SUM functions, respectively. Note that other calculations such as multiplication are also executable in the time domain.
Figure 24.1.3 illustrates the overall architecture of the implemented (32, 8) LDPC decoder leveraging TDMixed signal processing. It is mainly composed of variable node function units (VNUs) and check node function units (CNUs). The calculations in the VNUs and the CNUs are partitioned into the time domain and the digital domain, considering the efficiency. The MIN function in the CNU and the SUM functions in the VNU are implemented in the time domain, while absolute

value (ABS) and XOR functions in the CNU are executed in the digital domain. The architecture also permits messages between VNUs and CNUs to be transferred in the time domain, reducing wire count and routing congestion.
To reduce the overhead for data conversion, we propose an n-stage sub-ranging TDC (SR-TDC). As shown in Fig. 24.1.4, the n-stage SR-TDC is composed only of n flip-flops (FFs) instead of 2n-1 FFs for a conventional TDC. The time chart shows an example of 3b SR-TDC operation. At the first stage, the input time Tmeas is compared with T/2, where T is the full range, and the MSB data of D[2] is produced. Although D[1] could be directly obtained by comparing (Tmeas-D[2]×T/2) with T/4, this cannot be implemented since time cannot be subtracted [6]. Instead, by adding T/4+D[2]×T/4+Twait (=D[2]×T/2+(1-D[2])×T/4+Twait) to the both sides, where Twait is added to compensate for the delay of the FF, the comparison of (Tmeas+D[2]×T/4+Twait) and (T/2+D[2]×T/4+Twait) is implemented. Note that adding T/2 to both sides can also produce D[1] but with higher power consumption since the added delay is larger by T/4 when D[2] is zero. The lower bits are obtained in the same manner. The area and simulated power for the proposed 5b SR-TDC are reduced by 58% and 38%, respectively, compared to a conventional TDC.
The variable delay circuits (VDs) are composed of unit delay cells with Tdel delay (DELs), NORs, and inverters. When D[2] is low, node B stays low and a rising edge of the input signal passes through 2 NORs. On the other hand, when D[2] is high, the rising edge passes through 2 NORs and 2 DELs. As a result, according to whether D[2] is high or low, the delay varies by 2×Tdel, which defines T/4. The DTC is also realized by cascading the VDs having binary-weighted numbers of DELs. Employing the same cells for VDs in the DTC and the TDC cancels Tdel variations due to PVT variations. The value of Tdel is designed taking into account mismatch due to local variation so that the effective resolution does not deteriorate.
The test chip was implemented in 65nm CMOS utilizing a standard digital IC design flow. Fig. 24.1.5 shows measurement results for BER under an AWGN channel model. The measured results match with the simulation well and are not affected by PVT conditions.
Figure 24.1.6 shows the performance summary and a comparison with state-ofthe-art LDPC decoders. Comparing with [1], which implemented the all-analog decoder for the same code as ours, the proposed decoder improves power and area efficiency by 9× and 43×, respectively, even when the area of [1] is normalized to 65nm. Although the code length is different, the proposed design is also more efficient than the decoders reported in [5, 6] in terms of efficiency normalized to throughput. Fig. 24.1.7 shows the die photo and the area reduction offered by the TDMixed signal processing. The LDPC decoder occupies 0.063mm2. The area for the calculations of MIN, SUM, ABS, and XOR is 93% smaller than a digital implementation. Including the overhead of the converters, it is still 38% smaller.
