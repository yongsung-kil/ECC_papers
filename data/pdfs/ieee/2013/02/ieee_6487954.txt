An Improved Technique to Find the Trapping Sets
of the Irregular LDPC Codes
Kuntal Deka, A. Rajesh and P. K. Bora
Indian Institute of Technology Guwahati, India, email:{kuntal, rajesha and prabin}@iitg.ac.in.
Abstract—This paper presents an efficient technique to find
the dominant trapping sets of the irregular low density parity
check (LDPC) codes. This technique is based on the approach of
progressively finding the larger trapping sets from the smaller
ones. The proposed technique is applied to some of the most
commonly studied irregular codes and it is found to obtain more
number of trapping sets compared to those of the techniques
using the same approach and others as well.
Index Terms—Low density parity check (LDPC) codes, cycles,
trapping sets, absorbing sets, fully absorbing sets.
I. INTRODUCTION
The error floor problem of the low density parity check
(LDPC) codes refers to the sudden decrease in the slope of
the bit error rate (BER) and the frame error rate (FER) curves
that occurs at moderate to high signal-to-noise ratios (SNR).
The irregular LDPC codes are normally designed using the
degree distributions provided in [1]. These degree distributions
are optimized for the infinite block lengths using a tool called
density evolution with an objective to achieve the capacity.
Although the finite length irregular codes perform very well
in the waterfall region, they suffer from the error floor in the
high SNR region.
The error floor of the LDPC codes for an additive white
Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel is attributed to the subgraphs
of the Tanner graph associated with the so-called trapping sets
[2]. The definition of the trapping sets [2, 3] is not concrete and
a large number subgraphs which are not actually detrimental
may come under the umbrella of “trapping sets”. Zhang et
al. [4] have come up with the notion of a subset of the
trapping sets known as absorbing sets. The absorbing sets are
properly combinatoric in nature and are found to be the most
detrimental trapping sets.
In the works related to the error floor of the LDPC codes,
the most crucial part is to find a list of the dominant trapping
sets. A list of dominant trapping sets can be helpful in three
ways. First, using the knowledge of the detrimental trapping
sets, the parity check matrix of a particular LDPC code can
be modified to avoid the presence of some of the dominant
trapping sets in it. For example, the authors in [5, 6] have
used edge swapping between different copies of the Tanner
graph to remove some of the dominant trapping sets. The
authors in [7] have proposed a modified progressive edge
growth (PEG) [8] algorithm which uses a list of trapping
sets during the construction of the parity check matrix. The
second application of the list of the dominant trapping sets
is the decoder modification. Exploiting the knowledge of the
dominant trapping sets, Han and Ryan [3] have proposed three
decoders to lower the error floor. Observing the structures of
the most detrimental absorbing sets, Zhang et al. [4] have
devised a postprocessing technique to improve the high SNR
performance of a particular structured code. Thirdly, the low
error rate performance of the LDPC codes can be estimated by
using a list of the dominant trapping sets. Different types of
importance sampling methods [9, 10, 11] have been proposed
to estimate the high SNR performance by biasing the noise
towards the dominant trapping sets. From the above discus-
sion, it can be said that devising an efficient technique to find
out the dominant trapping sets of a particular LDPC code is
a very important task.
In this paper, we propose an efficient and easily imple-
mentable technique to find the dominant trapping sets of the
irregular LDPC codes. The rest of the paper is organized as
follows. Section II provides some definitions to be used in
the remaining part of the paper. In Section III, we present a
literature survey on the different types of methods for finding
the trapping sets. In Section IV, we discuss the proposed
technique. Section V presents some numerical results and
Section VI concludes the paper.
II. DEFINITIONS AND NOTATIONS
To understand and analyze the message passing decoding
algorithms more conveniently, usually an 𝑚× 𝑛parity check
matrix of an LDPC code is mapped to a bipartite graph
commonly known as the Tanner graph. In particular, let the
bipartite graph of an 𝑚×𝑛parity check matrix 𝐻be denoted
by 𝐺𝐻(𝑉, 𝐹, 𝐸), where 𝑉is the set of 𝑛nodes (known
as variable nodes (VN)) corresponding to the columns of
𝐻, 𝐹is the set of 𝑚nodes (known as check nodes (CN))
corresponding to the rows of 𝐻and 𝐸is the set of edges, i.e.
𝐸= {(𝑝, 𝑞) : 𝑝∈𝑉, 𝑞∈𝐹}. The number of edges coming
out of a particular node is known as the degree of that node.
For any subset 𝑆of 𝑉, let ℰ(𝑆) and 𝒪(𝑆) denote the
even-degree and odd-degree CNs respectively present in the
subgraph induced by the VNs in 𝑆.

if ∣𝑆∣= 𝑎and ∣𝒪(𝑆)∣= 𝑏.
Although a trapping set denotes a set of VNs, we will
use the term “trapping set” for both the set of VNs and the
subgraph induced by the VNs interchangeably.
Definition 2: An (𝑎, 𝑏) trapping set 𝑆is called an absorbing
set (AS) if all the VNs in 𝑆are connected to strictly more

III. LITERATURE SURVEY ON DIFFERENT METHODS OF
FINDING THE TRAPPING SETS OF THE LDPC CODES
The enumeration of the trapping sets of the LDPC codes has
two different principles: first is just to estimate the number of
the trapping sets and the second is to first identify the trapping
sets (i.e. the VNs in the trapping set) and then to count them.
The works in [14, 15] use the first principle. We focus on the
identification of the trapping sets because a list of dominant
trapping sets can be useful in many ways as discussed in
Section I.
Several methods have been proposed so far to identify the
dominant trapping sets of an LDPC code. These methods can
be classified into two broad categories: simulation based and
graph search based. In the simulation based methods, the
set of bits in which the decoder gets failed is considered
as a trapping set. In [11], a deterministic error impulse is
applied to some specific bits and the resultant vector is fed
to the decoder. The set of the erroneous bits at the decoder
output after the maximum number of iterations is considered
as a trapping set and the process is repeated several times to
collect more number of trapping sets. In [9], the importance
sampling technique is used by biasing the noise densities
corresponding to the bits involved in the smallest cycles and
the resulting decoder failures are accumulated as trapping sets.
The advantage of the simulation based techniques is that the
trapping sets in which the decoder actually gets failed are
found out. However, this approach is less likely to produce
an exhaustive list of the dominant trapping sets and also, the
time required for finding the trapping sets is very high.
In the graph search approach, specific subgraphs of the
Tanner graph are found out which correspond to the trap-
pings sets. These techniques may be further classified into
two groups: brute force and hierarchical. In [16] and [17],
the authors have proposed several brute force algorithms to
exhaustively find the trapping sets and the absorbing sets
respectively. However, the complexity of these algorithms is
very high and these algorithms are capable of finding only
the small error prone substructures of the small codes. In
the hierarchical techniques, smaller trapping sets are expanded
by adding some specific VNs to obtain larger trapping sets.
Recently, the authors of [12] and [13] have considered this
approach and they have found almost all the trapping sets
as much as the exhaustive methods can yield in a very less
amount of time. The algorithm proposed in [12] has been
applied to the regular codes of degree 3, whereas the algorithm
in [13] is more general and applicable to any code. In this
paper, we also adopt the hierarchical approach. We generalize
the technique proposed in [12] to apply it for the irregular
codes.
IV. PROPOSED TECHNIQUE
In the hierarchical approach of finding the trapping sets,
the smaller trapping sets are recursively enlarged by adding
some specific VNs to obtain the larger trapping sets. A larger
trapping set obtained from a smaller trapping set is called the
successor of the smaller one and the smaller one is called the
predecessor of the larger one [12].
There are numerous possible expansions from a particular
predecessor trapping set to obtain successor trapping sets of
different sizes [12, 13]. The authors in [12] have studied the
regular codes of VN-degree 3 and considered the expansion
by adding at most 2 VNs to the predecessor trapping sets.
In particular, to obtain the (𝑎, 𝑏) trappings sets, two kinds of
predecessor trapping sets (𝑎−1, 𝑏+ 1) and (𝑎−2, 𝑏) are
considered. We also consider the expansion by adding at most
2 VNs, i.e. for (𝑎, 𝑏) trappings sets, predecessor trapping sets
of the forms (𝑎−1, 𝑏′) and (𝑎−2, 𝑏′′) for some 𝑏′ and 𝑏′′,
are considered. For the irregular codes, the patterns of the
VN/VNs to be added to the predecessor trapping set may be
diverse because of the different kinds of possible degrees of
the VNs. In other words, one may have to consider several
types of (𝑎−1, 𝑏′) and (𝑎−2, 𝑏′′) trapping sets for different
possible values of 𝑏′ and 𝑏′′ in order to find out (𝑎, 𝑏) trapping
sets of an irregular code.
The trapping sets of the irregular codes are usually formed
by the low-degree VNs, in particular, mostly by the degree-2
VNs [13]. These degree-2 VNs, although improve the waterfall
region performance of the irregular codes compared to those
of the regular counterparts, are mainly responsible for the
development of the error floor. The low-degree VNs contribute
more to the error floor as they are more vulnerable because
of the reception of less number of extrinsic messages during
the decoding process. As the low degree-VNs are more likely
to be part of the trapping sets, we consider the expansion by
adding only the degree-2 and degree-3 VNs.

sets. These are presented below.

It should be noted that with the expansion by a maximum
of two VNs of degree at most 3, two more configurations are
possible. First is the expansion of an (𝑎−2, 𝑏+ 2) trapping
set to an (𝑎, 𝑏) trapping set by adding two VNs of degree 2
sharing one CN among each other and two odd degree CNs
of the (𝑎−2, 𝑏+2) trapping set. However, this expansion can
be realized by the concatenation of the expansions provided
in the case (1) and case (2) sequentially. The second possible
situation is the expansion of an (𝑎−2, 𝑏+ 1) trapping set
to an (𝑎, 𝑏) trapping set by adding a degree-2 and a degree-
3 VN, such that these two VNs share one CN among each
other and two odd degree CNs of the (𝑎−2, 𝑏+ 1) trapping
set. In this case also, the expansion can be realized by the
concatenation of the expansions mentioned in the case (1) and
(3) sequentially.
The trapping sets formed by a single cycle, sometimes,
may not be obtained by the expansions of the above four
predecessor trapping sets. Hence, we consider the trapping sets
formed by a single cycle as the fifth source of (𝑎, 𝑏) trapping
sets.
5) Length-2𝑎cycles:
Any length-2𝑎cycle containing 𝑏odd-degree CNs is an
(𝑎, 𝑏) trapping set.
To illustrate the five types of sources for a particular class of
trapping set, several possible configurations of (10,2) trapping
sets of the IEEE 802.11n code [18] with block length 𝑛= 648
and rate 𝑅= 1/2 are shown in Figure 5. Figure 5(a) and 5(b)
show the expansion of two different types of (9,2) trapping
sets. Figure 5(c) illustrates the expansion of a (9,4) trapping
set. The (10,2) trapping set in Figure 5(d) can be obtained
from a (9,3) predecessor trapping set. Figure 5(e) shows the
expansion of a (8,2) trapping set. Finally, Figure 5(f) shows a
(10,2) trapping set formed by a single cycle of length 20.
The process of obtaining larger trapping sets from the
smaller ones is initiated from some smallest predecessor
trapping sets. These initial most predecessor trapping sets
usually include the degree-2 and degree-3 VNs (which do
not contain cycles) and the cycles of the size equal to the
girth. Then, the process of finding out larger trapping sets
from the smaller ones is progressively carried out to obtain all
the dominant trapping sets of a particular code.
Consideration of only the five types of sources mentioned in
the above is found to be sufficient to find out all the dominant
trapping sets for several commonly studied irregular codes.
These numerical results are discussed in the next section.
