Abstract—EXtrinsic Information Transfer (EXIT) chart is a graphical tool for analysis of low density parity check (LDPC) and other codes with iterative decoding. In this paper, we propose the design of LDPC codes for coded modulation schemes by application of linear programming with conditions derived from EXIT charts. Points on the EXIT curve for bit and check node operations are first computed either using analysis or by simulation. The entire EXIT curve is then obtained by linear interpolation. Finally, the EXIT curves are optimized to obtain the maximum possible rate for the code under a given threshold condition. To demonstrate the design procedure, we consider the cases of M-PAM and 8-PSK over the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel. The optimization method presented is versatile, and can be extended to other constellations and situations easily.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are being widely used in many existing communication systems. Several properties of LDPC codes are crucial for this wide usage. Firstly, the analysis of LDPC codes is on established theoretical grounds and design follows from the analysis for the class of symmetric channels, which occur in many communication scenarios. Secondly, the implementation of LDPC encoders and decoders in VLSI and software has proven to be simple and flexible. Finally, even in asymmetric channel scenarios, coding systems using LDPC codes as components have been designed and accepted in some communication standards. However, the optimization of LDPC codes in asymmetric channels continues to have some room for improvement. In this article, we present results of one such effort in the design of LDPC codes for higher order constellations such as M -PAM for M = 4, 8 and 8-PSK.
Two schemes typically proposed for use in higher order constellations are MultiLevel Coding (MLC) [1] and BitInterleaved Coded Modulation (BICM) [2]. MLC uses multiple binary codes and offers different protection to bits at different levels. MLC is known to be a capacity-achieving strategy, and designing optimal codes for MLC involves joint optimization of component code rates and threshold [3]. The optimal decoder for MLC is the multistage decoder that suffers from high latency, but Parallel Independent Decoding (PID) is

It has been shown that when employed with Gray constellation mapping, the performance loss of BICM compared to MLC/PID is small [3]. Several other interesting modifications of the basic BICM and MLC idea have been proposed in the literature. In [4], the authors have considered a system that combines the good characteristics of BICM and MLC. See [4] for references to other works in this direction.
Designing codes for BICM or MLC is not straight forward because the equivalent binary channels at each level are not symmetric. Authors of [3] introduced iid adapters to impose symmetry on the overall channel, and used differential evolution for obtaining optimal degree distributions for both MLC and BICM. In [5], optimization based on Gaussian approximation and EXIT chart [6] was proposed for 8-PSK. In [4], Gaussian approximation and linear programming were used in a combined MLC/BICM code design.
In this paper, our focus will be on optimizing LDPC codes for BICM. We use linear programming for optimization, which is simpler and more flexible than differential evolution particularly for larger constellations. We use EXIT charts for deriving constraints and avoid an explicit Gaussian approximation. We demonstrate the design with some examples, simulation results and comparisons.

The optimization problem is highly non-convex and non-linear, and direct solutions are typically by powerful but complex methods such as simulated annealing or differential evolution. A popular simplification is by the Gaussian approximation, which assumes that pi is Gaussian and DE simply tracks the mean of pi. An EXIT chart for the decoder consists of two curves - one for the bit-node update and one for the check-node update. Each curve is a plot that maps extrinsic information coming out of the node versus a priori information going into the node. The bit-node curve is a plot of IE,V (y-axis) as function of IA,V (x-axis), and the check-node curve is a plot of IE,C (xaxis) as a function of IA,C (y-axis). Note that the check-node curve is on a switched axis. On the EXIT chart, a decoding trajectory starts at IE,V (IA,V = 0) and then hops between the two curves to attempt to reach the (IE,V = 1, IA,V = 1) point. So for decoding to converge at a given noise level σ, the bit-node curve should always lie above check-node curve in the EXIT chart. This is a useful condition that is exploited in the optimization of LDPC degree distributions.
For more details on EXIT charts, see [6], [8].

III. OPTIMIZATION OF LDPC CODES FOR BICM
The optimization of LDPC codes for Bit-Interleaved Coded Modulation (BICM) systems is complicated by the fact that the equivalent binary-input channels are not symmetric. A binaryinput channel is called symmetric if its output y and transition probability satisfy p(y|0) = p(−y|1). Without symmetry, application of DE [9] becomes infeasible because the all-zero codeword is not sufficient as input. To overcome this problem, we adopt the method of iid adapters proposed in [3].

EXIT chart is a graphical tool that provides an intuitive picture of the message passing decoder [6]. The idea is to track the mutual information between the extrinsic LLR and the corresponding codeword bit at each iteration, instead of the entire PDF. The bit-node and check-node update steps of an iteration are approximated as processes that transfer their input extrinsic mutual information from the previous step into improved extrinsic mutual information output.
