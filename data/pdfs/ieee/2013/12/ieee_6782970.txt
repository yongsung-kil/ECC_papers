High-Throughput Dual-Shift Stochastic-Detection Quasi-Cyclic LDPC Decoder

Melvin Heng Li Lim
School of Electrical and Electronic Engineering, Nanyang Technological University, Singapore LIMH0097@ntu.edu.sg

Wang Ling Goh
School of Electrical and Electronic Engineering, Nanyang Technological University, Singapore EWLGOH@ntu.edu.sg

Abstract—Driven by its renowned near-capacity performance over a wide spectrum of channels, the capitalization of quasicyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes is irrefutably the focal point of research and solution to virtually all communication systems of the next generation. Steered towards non-volatile memory (NVM) storage applications, we introduce a modernistic approach to LDPC decoding in this paper, known as the dual-shift stochastic-detection (DSSD). Weaving two novel ideas: dual-shift cyclic generation and stochastic-detection of local minima, our proposed DSSD decoder achieves throughput gains (~ 300%) by minimizing its overall computational delay and maximizing its operational frequency. Along with the amalgamation of our spearheading mirror-paradigm, the DSSD QC-LDPC decoder acquires yet another dimension of gain in throughput while relinquishing a cluster of its address generation counters, which elicits a wide expanse for its application.
Keywords-dual-shift stochastic-detection (DSSD); FPGA; highthroughput decoder; low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes; quasicyclic (QC) codes; Reed-Solomon (RS) codes
I. INTRODUCTION
The renaissance of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes by MacKay in 1996 [1] has kindled a reorientation of bearings in modern channel coding. First founded by Gallager in 1962 [2], LDPC codes are conventionally described in mathematical means by sparse parity-check matrices and represented graphically by bipartite graphs, also known as Tanner graphs [3], with two sets of nodes delineated by check-nodes and variable-nodes, respectively. In view of escalating demands for high-speed applications, many communication systems are hopping on the bandwagon of utilizing ultra-high throughput channel codes to satisfy data rate requirements and coding efficiencies. One such example would be the 802.15.3c standard, which is targeted at data rates of gigabits per second (Gbps). When placed in juxtaposition against Turbo codes, yet another near-capacity performing variant, LDPC codes transcend with lower hardware complexity and outstanding innate potential for parallelism [4]–[6], which are essential to the realization of high decoding throughput. Thus, LDPC codes are preferred over convolutional codes and Turbo codes, and have been widely adopted by several communication standards such as 802.11n, DVB-S2, 802.15.3c (WPAN) [7] and 802.16e (WiMax) [8].

Nonetheless, the actualization of a high-throughput LDPC decoder is a herculean task due to the complexity of its decoding algorithm. Throughput efficiency (TEFF), which in general can be calculated as

Taking a page from the fundamentals of generic hardware design, throughput conundrums can be dealt with via the reduction of algorithm complexity and/or the introduction of concurrency. The former, commonly referred to as turbo-decoding message passing (TDMP) or layered decoding [9], realizes a compact decoder through its rapid decoding convergence, but lacks the throughput to move beyond niche applications. On the contrary, the latter incorporates conventional two-phase message passing (TPMP) [10] and parallelized message relays, yielding exceptional throughput at the expense of die area. While the combination of both techniques appears to be propitious, that intent is often encumbered by prohibitive routing and memory constraints.

In this paper, an innovative dual-shift stochastic-detection (DSSD) technique is proposed to ameliorate throughput efficiencies of quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC decoders. Designed to tackle TPMP bottlenecks with partial-parallel architectures, DSSD mitigates not only critical path delays, but also computational resources in the decoder circuitry. Moreover, in conjunction with our pioneering mirror-paradigm reported previously in [11], DSSD offers remarkable decoding throughput performances with minimal area increment.

The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section II presents a brief review of existing comparator architectures and the bottlenecks encountered during LDPC decoding. The fundamentals of our proposed DSSD QC-LDPC decoder are expounded in Section III, followed by its implementation and analysis in Section IV. Last but not least, the conclusions are drawn in Section V.

II. DECODING BOTTLENECKS
In hardware per se, one of the most effective means of improving throughput efficiency of a QC-LDPC decoder is to diminish tOCD by insinuating concurrency within the decoding algorithm. While the use of partial-parallel architectures easily abates decoding latency, the cumbersome procurement of local minimum and sub-minimum limits fMAX. Attempts to salvage this deadlock have rendered the implementation of tree comparators based on the "divide and conquer" principle. Exemplified in Fig. 1, these tree comparators derive global minimum by dividing the entire cluster of messages into a tree-like hierarchy, where each branch attends only to the computation of its local minimum. Even though the various tree comparators effectively abate tOCD by logarithmic factors, bulk of the computational delay remains constituted by the replication of the parity-check matrix, better known as H-matrix, of a QC-LDPC code. Furthermore, the cascaded pair-wise nature of these tree comparators induces substantial critical path delays and an accretion of electronic components in the decoder circuitry, taking a toll on both fMAX and silicon area. Consequently, the resultant QC-LDPC decoder may not satisfy the specifications of futuristic communication systems. Thus, the optimization of sub-modular comparison units is only half the solution.
III. DUAL-SHIFT STOCHASTIC-DETECTION
DSSD, as suggested by its name, revolves around two basic principles: dual-shift cyclic generation and stochastic-detection of local minima. Dual-shift cyclic generation resolves the aforementioned bottlenecks by introducing bi-directional cyclic shifting of circulant generators during the replication of the Hmatrix of a QC-LDPC code. While the conventional cyclic shifting of circulant generators described in Fig. 2 occurs once per clock cycle in one direction, it requires a total of CS clock cycles to reproduce the entire H-matrix. With a two-fold increase in parallelizable threads, the corresponding QC-LDPC decoder acquires robust data processing capabilities, and hence, possesses the potential to double its resultant throughput. Nonetheless, this gain in throughput comes at the expense of chip area; the ability to process twice the amount of data also implies that the number of sub-modular computational units required is doubled. Likewise, a hike in address generation counters and memories to support extensive parallel threads is inevitable. Thus, it is paramount that the sub-modular computational complexities are kept to a minimum to prevent routing congestions and interconnect complications.
On that account, the second postulate of DSSD features a stochastic-detection of local minima that contravenes the implementation of cascaded comparators. Essentially, all soft messages in hardware are subjected to fixed-point quantization. Due to its finite precision, the number of possible values for

In other words, instead of performing bitwise comparisons within the cluster of soft messages, it becomes possible to speculate the values of local minima by verifying the existence of specific binary sequences. In this paper, since a 6-bit uniform quantization scheme of one signbit, two integer-bits, and three fractional-bits, is adopted in the QC-LDPC decoder, the search for local minima narrows down to the set of binary sequences ܏ below a threshold of 0.5, such that ܏ = {"000000", "000001", "000010", "000011"}. Apart from dealing away with heavily cascaded circuitries, our proposed stochastic-detection comparator elucidated in Fig. 4 minimizes critical path delays as a result of its simplicity and parallelism. Therefore, the computational delay of minimum and sub-minimum computation is drastically reduced from a logarithmic factor of row weights to a single clock cycle. In addition to the improvement of fMAX, logical components inherently consume less hardware resources as opposed to cascaded designs, and thus advocates practicality in actual fabrication.
IV. IMPLEMENTATION RESULTS
In order to validate the concept behind the DSSD, we implement our proposed QC-LDPC decoder on a Virtex-6 ML-605 FPGA board based on a Xilinx ISE 13.1 platform for analysis. Designed for NVM storage applications, the chosen rate-0.9 4489-bit mirror-paradigm Reed-Solomon (MPRS) based QC-LDPC code illustrated in Fig. 5 lays a firm foundation for further studies of DSSD on longer codewords with akin code rates. More importantly, the selected variant holds an exclusive mirror-paradigm feature, which offsets the escalation of address generation counters that arise from the dual-shift cyclic generation, reported in our previous work.

Correspondingly, based on the partial-parallel architecture presented in Fig. 6, our DSSD QC-LDPC decoder is assessed on error-correcting capability, throughput, and resource utilization. The procurement of error-correcting performances involves a hardware Monte Carlo simulator that assumes a Box-Muller AWGN channel module and a syndrome detector with NITR capped at 10 iterations. Both the conventional cascaded-comparison and our DSSD QC-LDPC decoder are each evaluated for their respective bit-error rates (BERs) and frame-error rates (FERs) using the TPMP decoding algorithm. Besides these fixed-point studies, the floating-point decoding model, undertaken by a MATLAB variant, provides a benchmark to complete our investigations. Simulation results in Fig. 7 suggest that both cascaded-comparison and DSSD decoder perform well within the 0.2 dB region of the ideal MATLAB decoder. In spite of the threshold assumption in our proposed DSSD, the trade-off in error-correcting performance is almost negligible (< 5%).

V. CONCLUSION
A modernistic DSSD approach to LDPC decoding has been demonstrated in this paper. Apart from reducing tOCD, DSSD mitigates critical path delays and routing complexities of the decoder circuitry, which engenders a substantial improvement in fMAX. As a result, our proposed DSSD decoder outrivals other existing decoders with outstanding throughput efficiency (up to 300%) over a reasonable die area, and minimal decoding performance loss.
