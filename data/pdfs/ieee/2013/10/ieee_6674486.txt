ABSTRACT

In this paper, a new theoretical model that describes the impact of the approximation error on the decisions taken by LDPC decoders is discussed. In particular, the theoretical model extends previous results and reconstructs the mechanism, by means of which the approximation error alters the decisions of the decoding algorithm, with respect to the decisions taken by the optimal decoding algorithm, namely Log Sum-Product. We focus on the most popular algorithm for LDPC decoding, namely Min-Sum and its also popular modifications, normalized and offset Min-Sum. The model is applied to all of these decoding algorithms, which are actually approximations of the Log Sum-Product. Moreover a method that exploits the output of the proposed model in order to estimate the decoding performance is also proposed. Finally, experimental results prove the validity of both the proposed model and the method, demonstrating the usefulness of this contribution towards achieving accurate decoding behavior prediction without relying on time-consuming simulations.

1. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity check (LDPC) codes, developed by Gallager [1] and rediscovered by Mackay a few decades later [2], have become popular within last decade and have already been adopted in a variety of standards ranging from satellite video (DVB-S2) to 10GBase-T Ethernet (802.3an), 802.11n, 802.11ac and 802.16e. Their favorable computational complexity and the decoding performance, that closely approaches the Shannon limit [3], are expected to increase their exploitation within coming years.
One of the main advantages of the LCPC codes, is the parallel nature of the LDPC decoding algorithms. Message-passing algorithms are the most popular ones among LDPC decoding algorithms. The easy parallelization of a message-passing decoding algorithm implementation on hardware, makes the LDPC codes suitable for high-throughput implementations, where a high degree of parallelism is required [4]. Due to this feature, the LDPC codes have overcome the turbo codes on high-throughput systems.

However, it is not possible to predict the decoding performance of an LDPC code decoded using a particular decoding algorithm, without actually simulating it. The only way to estimate the decoding performance without simulations is by exploiting the density evolution technique [5]. The density evolution characterizes the code taking into consideration the variable node degree distribution of its parity check matrix. The main drawback of this technique is the assumption that the Tanner graph of the code is cycle free. In real life, the existence of cycles on the Tanner graph of an LDPC code, is the main reason for the correlation of exchanged messages and the existence of the trapping sets, the activation of which on message-passing decoding process results to the error-floor effect [6], from which the LDPC codes suffer.
Since simulation is the only reliable method to reveal the decoding performance of the designed decoder, the determination of the finite word lengths required to represent the decoder inner data is a tedious and time consuming procedure, which requires extensive simulations. The traditional trade off between hardware complexity and decoding performance arises for the case of LDPC decoder design also. Word length determination is the first of a series of steps that lead from a given algorithm to an appropriate VLSI implementation. Targeting at the objective of reducing the corresponding design time, analytical models that relate word lengths to LDPC decoding performance are sought.
In previous works [7] [8], the authors analyzed the mechanism that changes the decision of the decoding algorithm and proposed a semi-analytical model that computes the probability of the algorithm decision change due to approximating the sub-optimal Log Sum-Product (Log-SP) LDPC decoding algorithm. In this paper, a technique is proposed to exploit the decision change probability in order to estimate the decoding performance of an approximation of a decoding algorithm, when
* the decoding performance of the original algorithm and * the decision change probability due to the particular
approximation
are known. Moreover, the proposed model in [8], is further enriched with analytical components targeting to a fully analytical model.
The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 presents the proposed method to estimate the decoding performance of the decoding algorithm approximation. The next Section 3 overviews the mechanism by means of which roundoff affects the decisions produced by the algorithm at each iteration, and presents the enrichment of the proposed semi-analytical. In Section 4 the performance of the proposed method and model are evaluated through simulation results, while Section 5 concludes the paper.

3. THE IMPACT OF AN ALGORITHM APPROXIMATION ON DECODING DECISIONS
3.1. Change mechanism for algorithm decisions
The constraints that should be satisfied in order to change the hard decision of the decoding algorithm, due to a particular modification on its implementation have already analyzed [7][8].

Let mˆ djec denote the decision message of the algorithm, for the case that the modification is applied. The approximation error that the modification introduces to the decision message is denoted by e, and it is computed as follows,

e = mdec − mˆ dec ,

(6)

where mdec is the so-called original decision message computed assuming the original decoding algorithm. To simplify notation mdec will be denoted for the remainder of the paper as m. An example of what we consider as a modification in an algorithm implementation is the replacement of Log-SP Φ(x) function, in case of the Min-Sum algorithm, by a much simpler-to-implement expression. This simplification induces an approximation error, which is here modeled as by (6).
The relationships that describe mathematically the conditions that should be satisfied in order to change the decision of the algorithm due to algorithm modifications, also describe the mechanism which leads to this change. An additive noise model is assumed for the approximation error. Since the error is additive, two possible scenarios are distinguished that describe the change of the decision of the algorithm due to the approximation error:
• in case that the original decision message is positive then the approximation error should be negative with absolute value greater than decision message’s absolute value, i.e.;

m < 0 ∧ e > −m ⇒

e > 0 ∧ −e < m < 0 ,

(7)

• in case that decision message is negative, the approximation error should be positive and its absolute value should be greater than decision message’s absolute value, i.e.;

m > 0 ∧ e < −m ⇒

e < 0 ∧ 0 < m < −e .

(8)

These two scenarios are the only cases where the decision of the algorithm is altered due to the approximation. Other values of the approximation error e, relative to m, do not cause a change of the decision. Eqs. (7) and (8) form a mathematical description of the cartesian plane regions where decision changes occur, as Fig. 1 indicates. The particular model is exploited in the following section to compute the probability of an altered decision due to the modification of the decoding algorithm implementation.

4. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
4.1. Evaluation of the proposed model
In order to validate the proposed theoretical model for all the cases of approximation error, appropriate simulations take place. Particularly, two decoders are simulated at each simulation, the first one stands for the original decoding algorithm and implements infinite accuracy Log Sum-Product decoding algorithm, that double-precision floating-point matlab code provides, while the second one, standing for the modified decoding algorithm, implements infinite accuracy original [9], normalized [10] and offset [5] Min-Sum decoding, correspondingly. Therefore, three pairs of decoders are simulated.
At each simulation, the same received symbols are decoded by both decoders of each pair. Each decoder executes up to ten decoding iterations. The used code is an irregular rate 1/2 (2016, 1512) LDPC code. The decisions taken by both decoders are compared and the ratio of different decisions is determined.
The parameters (μm, σm) and (μe, σe) of the proposed model, that correspond to the original decision messages and the approximation error mean values and standard deviation respectively, depend on the parameters of the used LDPC code (variable and check node degrees) and the Min-Sum decoding approximation. These parameters are experimentally determined. A large ensemble of the exchanged messages has been recorded during decoding simulations. Recorded samples were further processed in order to derive the joint pdf fm,e(m, e) and the parameters of the proposed model through fitting methods.
Figs. 2–3 indicate the results of the fitting procedure. Blue points indicate the recorded samples, while colored surface stands for the pdf provided by the proposed model. An overview of these results is presented on the diagrams of Figs. 4–8. The accuracy of the proposed model is satisfactory, particularly for the case of the original and the normalized Min-Sum decoding algorithms. For the case of the offset Min-Sum algorithm, the approximation error

does not follow a continuous distribution, because of the offset factor. However, the proposed bivariate gaussian model assumes a continuous distribution, both for the decision message and the approximation error variables. This assumption fails for the case of the approximation error of the offset Min-Sum, resulting to a reduced accuracy of the proposed model, while is satisfied for the case of the original and the normalized Min-Sum decoding algorithms.
4.2. Evaluation of the proposed method
In order to validate the method proposed in Section 2, the following simulation scenarios were realized. The Log-SP decoding algorithm was used on the first simulation in order to decode a (2016, 1512) LDPC code, executing a fixed number of five decoding iterations. The same code was decoded using respectively the normalized and the offset Min-Sum decoding algorithms, executing the same fixed number of decoding iterations, on the second and the third simulated scenarios. The normalization and the offset factor, for the case of the normalized and the offset Min-Sum algorithm, were respectively 0.3 and 2. The decoding performance of each decoding algorithm determined in terms of BER performance.
Assuming Log-SP the initial version of the considered decoding algorithm and considering the other two algorithms as modifications of the first one, we determined experimentally, as described in Section 3, the probabilities of the events that the decision of the normalized and the offset Min-Sum algorithm differs from the corresponding one of the first decoding algorithm. The determined probabilities were used by (1) in order to derive the BER performance of the two modified versions of the initial algorithm, regarding the BER performance of the last one.
The results are depicted on Figs. 9 and 10 for the case of normalized and offset Min-Sum respectively. It is illustrated that the proposed method predicts the decoding performance of the modified decoding algorithm very close to its simulated BER performance. 5. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper a prior empirical-theoretical model is refined, based on the observation that the bivariate gaussian model can describe the joint pdf of the decision message m and the approximation error e. Specifically the model estimates the probability of altering the decision of the Log-SP decoding algorithm due to the approximation error induced by modifying the algorithm implementation for the case of the Min-Sum decoding algorithm and its variants, normalized and offset Min-Sum. The joint pdf of the decision messages exchanged during the infinite accuracy Log-SP decoding and the approximation error due to its Min-Sum approximation, is modeled by a bivariate two-dimensional normal distribution. Further current research focusses on analytical models for distribution parameters, determined experimentally in this work, for a particular combination of code, decoding algorithm and decoding iteration.
The proposed model can be used as a tool that computes the expected degradation of the decoding performance for a particular implementation of the decoder, without the need of time-consuming simulations, in order to determine the approximation error effects. Such an approach is the proposed here method, that exploits the decision change probability of an algorithm modification, in order to estimate its decoding performance, given the decoding performance of its original implementation. Both the proposed method and model aspire to contribute to the reduction of the design time, avoiding the extended time-consuming simulations required in order to determine design parameters such as internal word lengths.
