Performance Analysis of LDPC Convolutional Codes
Zahoor Ahmed Baloch, Ayaz Hussain, and Noor Hussain Balochistan University of Engineering and Technology Khuzdar, Pakistan
email: zahoor@buetk.edu.pk engr_ayaz@yahoo.com fezan79@gmail.com

Abstract: Low Density Parity Check Convolutional Codes (LDPC-CCs) have the advantage of simple encoding and decoding. ASIC or FPGA implementation of LDPC-CCs have been shown to have very good complexity-performance trade-off. In this paper, we present a class of LDPC-CC and iterative algorithm to decode these codes. The construction technique of our code is simple and easy, compared to other LDPC-CCs, which reduces hardware complexity. The simulation results show that the performance of our proposed codes is better than the LDPC Block codes with the same decoding complexity per information bit. Our code also outperforms its counterpart LDPC-CCs.

Key words: Low Density Parity Check Codes, Convolutional Codes, Iterative encoding and decoding,

I.

Introduction

Low density parity check codes were firstly introduced in 1963[1]. After their introduction, the LDPC codes could not get proper attentions of the researchers due to their decoding complexity, rather were quite neglected for a long time. The idea of iterative decoding animated the immortality of LDPC codes [2]. The simulation of iterative decoding of long LDPCs have been shown to achieve near capacity performance for binary transmission over noisy channels [3]. LDPC Block codes (LDPC-BCs), however, require splitting of the data to be transmitted into frames, which can be a disadvantage in some applications [4].
Convolutional codes, on the other hand, have no such requirement, and are hence well suited for continuous transmission and streaming applications. In [5] the authors proposed the construction of periodic time varying convolutional codes with LDPC matrices. A set of time invariant LDPC convolutional codes are described in [6]. The codes in [5] were obtained by random construction techniques whereas those in [6] were essentially algebraic constructions. In [7] the authors have presented regular LDPC-CC in which the number of non-zero elements in each row and columns are uniform, whereas in [8] the authors proposed irregular LDPC-CCs.

In this paper, we present a convolutional version of LDPC codes. Comparing with LDPC-BCs it is found that our proposed code performs better than block codes of the same length. Furthermore, our code has a more regular and easy structure to design which reduces the hardware complexity and makes the implementation of the encoder simpler. Our proposed code also outperforms his brethren LDPC-CCs. For example our proposed code gets a better performance of about 1.2 dB and 0.2dB at a bit error rate of as compare to LDPC-BC and LDPC-CC of [9], respectively. The decoder can also be implemented by using several processors in a pipeline structure making the decoding process accelerated.

The rest of the paper is organized as follows. In Section II, we briefly review the LDPC-CCs. In Section III, we present a new technique for construction of LDPC-CC. Encoding and decoding of LDPC-CCs are explained in Sections IV and V respectively. Simulation results are presented in Section VI and conclusion in Section VII.

In [11] Tanner explained a very useful technique to illustrate a code over bipartite graph. A bipartite graph comprises of two codes, i.e. the symbol nodes corresponding to the columns of H and constraint nodes corresponding to the rows of H. An edge connects a symbol node to a constraint node if the entry in the corresponding column and row of H is a one. Figure 3 shows the Tanner graph corresponding to the parity check matrix of above example. There is a one to one correspondence between a parity check matrix and its Tanner graph representation. LDPC codes by virtue of their sparse parity check matrices have sparse Tanner graph representations. The Tanner graph provides a convenient setting to describe iterative decoding algorithms.

We assign a vector for the messages on graph and index the messages (edges). The vector contains the messages on the edges. It contains the messages (in LLR) from symbol nodes (Variable nodes) to check nodes (Function node). The length of this vector equals to the number of edges in corresponding graph. At first, all elements of the vector are zeros. Each one in matrix (15) corresponds to an edge. Thus we count (makes index) the ones of the first line from left to right, then second line and so on. Figure 4 shows the correspondence between the ones in matrix (15) and edge number in the graph. To calculate the length, we count the number of primitive matrix, H, in matrix (15). Assume L to be the length of input vector, i.e. number of rows in (15) then, division of L by the number of rows of H determines the number of primitive H in (15). Multiplying the result to the number of ones in H, we get total number of ones in (15) which is equal to the number of edges.
