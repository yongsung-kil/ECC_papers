Gallager B Decoder on Noisy Hardware
S. M. Sadegh Tabatabaei Yazdi, Hyungmin Cho, and Lara Dolecek, Member, IEEE

Abstract—Conventional communications theory assumes that the data transmission is noisy but the processing at the receiver is entirely error-free. Such assumptions may have to be revisited for advanced (silicon) technologies in which hardware failures are a major concern at the system-level. Hence, it is important to characterize the performance of a communication system with both noisy processing components and noisy data transmission.
Coding systems based on low-density parity check (LDPC) codes are widely used for a variety of applications. In this paper, we focus on probabilistic analysis of the LDPC Gallager B decoder built out of faulty components. Using the density evolution technique, we find approximations for the optimal threshold of the decoder and the symbol error rate (SER) of the decoded sequence as functions of both the channel error rate and error rates of the decoder components, for both binary and non-binary regular LDPC codes. Furthermore, we study the convergence of the output SER and the decoding threshold of the decoder for different ranges of error rates. We verify our results using MATLAB simulations and hardware emulation of noisy decoders.
Results presented in this paper can serve as systematic design guidelines in resource allocation for noisy decoders. Informed resource allocation is of particular relevance to emerging data storage and processing applications that need to maintain high levels of reliability despite hardware errors in advanced technologies.
Index Terms—Error analysis, Gallager B algorithm, faulty hardware, LDPC codes, non-binary LDPC codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
T HE goal of communication theory is to design and analyze communication systems, which are capable of efficiently and reliably transmitting and receiving information in the presence of noise. In this paper, we study the effects of noisy hardware on the output of communication systems. For future (silicon CMOS) technologies, there are significant concerns about errors in the underlying hardware. Such errors may be caused by several factors: radiation effects due to alpha particles and neutrons, cross-talk, and power-supply noise (when the supply voltage is lowered to reduce power, the tolerable voltage margins decrease and circuits become more susceptible to voltage variations) [1], [15], [13], [24], [38]. Von Neumann [33] was one of the first to model errors in logic devices by a stochastic process. The faulty hardware of von Neumann is based on transient errors where every gate or a component of a Boolean network fails at every usage with some fixed probability. The failures of a component are independent over uses, and the hardware components also fail independently of each other. Von Neumann proposed an architecture to combat such noise in hardware. His architecture was able to compute a Boolean function with some small but fixed probability of error. Furthermore, it comprised O(N log N )1 noisy gates, where N was the number of gates in the noisefree network.
A fundamental result in communication theory states that for any erroneous channel and sufficiently long block codes, communication with arbitrarily small probability of error is possible [26]. The discrepancy between this statement and von Neumann’s result that only implies a small fixed overall probability of error, is due to the implicit assumption of error-free decoding in a conventional communication system, whereas in computation theory a la von Neumann [33], all components of the computation are allowed to be faulty.
The bound on the number of gates by von Neumann was proved to be tight in later works of Dobrushin and Ortyukov [9] and Ga´cs and Ga´l [10] who found functions that need Ω(N log N )2 gates in noisy hardware, while their noise-free implementation needs N gates. Pippenger [22] showed that a variety of Boolean functions may be computed reliably by noisy networks requiring only a constant multiplicative redundancy. Recently, Wilhelm and Bruck [34] and Zhou and Bruck [39] have considered the minimal implementation of logic switches of certain error probability by using faulty Boolean gates.
Given a fixed amount of unreliable computational resources (e.g., noisy Boolean gates) and an algorithm for computing a Boolean function, the objective is to allocate computational resources to different tasks of the algorithm in order to minimize unreliability in computing the Boolean function. In this approach, one needs to compute the sensitivity of the error in the final output to the error in the output of each implementation block of the algorithm. One then assigns computational resources of higher reliability to more sensitive tasks and resources of lower reliability to less sensitive tasks. This approach has been successfully applied to probabilistic applications such as data mining, image recognition, and case synthesis [6], [18], [19], [2], [36].

C. Analysis of the noisy Gallager B decoder
In this section we analyze the performance of the Gallager B noisy decoder. Varshney [31] extended the celebrated results of Richardson and Urbanke [25] to the noisy processing case. He showed that, as in the noise-free case, it suffices to study the performance of the decoder under the assumption of a cycle-free Tanner graph. This assumption implies that the messages at different iterations of the decoding process and at different edges of the Tanner graph are statistically independent. Varshney also showed that under general symmetry conditions, again as in the noise-free case, the error behavior is independent of the chosen codeword. Therefore, in this section we analyze the performance of a noisy decoder under the assumption of a cycle-free Tanner graph for the all-zeros transmitted codeword.

Next we discuss some practical implications of Theorem 5 and Theorem 6. As Theorem 5 indicates, for a general q-ary decoder and dv ≥ 4, the final SER up to the first order of approximation is only a function of pMAJ, and for the binary decoder and dv ≥ 6, the final SER is only a function of pMAJ up to the second order. For a small variable node degree, the results also indicate that the final SER is much more sensitive to pMAJ than to pXOR.
These observations have two consequences. First, since the final SER is more sensitive to the error in variable nodes, it is more beneficial to assign more reliable processors to the computation at variable nodes. Second, since the final SER for large variable node degrees, is only related to the error in check nodes through the terms of O(p2XOR) for a general field decoder, and O(pX3OR) for a binary decoder, implementing less accurate processors for check node computations does not affect the performance of the decoder in a critical way. For decoders with a small variable node degree, the same conclusion is still valid since the coefficient of pMAJ is much larger than the coefficient of pXOR.
As a general phenomenon, the final SER for a general field decoder, and in particular for a binary decoder, is more sensitive to the error in check nodes when the variable nodes have lower degree. For a noisy binary decoder the sensitivity increases by increasing the degree of check nodes. In general, our analysis suggests that codes with a higher degree of variable nodes and a lower degree of check nodes are less sensitive to the error in check nodes and are suitable for noisy hardware implementation. Finally, notice that the analysis of the final SER for general field decoders does not reveal any dependencies in terms of the field size q. However, depending on the implementation of the finite field operations for different values of the field size, processing errors pMAJ and pXOR may be a function of the field size. These dependencies are not considered in the current work and are a subject of future studies.
