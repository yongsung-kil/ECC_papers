Construction of Structured Regular LDPC Codes: A Design-Theoretic Approach
H. Falsafain and M. Esmaeili, Member, IEEE

Abstract—A new combinatorial technique for constructing girth-6 structured binary regular low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes based on special types of t-designs is given. A very large number of well-known t-designs can be used by this method for code construction. Based on this method, a t-(v, k, λ) design D = (X, B) can be exploited for code construction if it satisfies the following three conditions: 1) |B1 ∩ B2| ≤ t for any two blocks B1, B2 ∈ B and B1 = B2; 2) λ > 1; and 3) k > t. Though the technique works for any t-design satisfying these conditions, we focus only on the utilization of simple triple systems, super-simple BIBDs, Steiner systems, and large sets (LSs) of t-designs. We also construct binary and non-binary girth-6 QC-LDPC codes from the t-designs satisfying these conditions by using matrix dispersion method. Experimental results show that the constructed non-binary QCLDPC codes can provide good practical performance under iterative decoding using the fast Fourier transform based qary sum-product algorithm (FFT-QSPA) and they can achieve acceptable coding gains over random-like codes of comparable parameters decoded with sum-product algorithm (SPA).
Index Terms—LDPC codes, combinatorial designs, Steiner systems, super-simple BIBDs, matrix dispersion.
I. INTRODUCTION
I T is well-known that LDPC codes are capable of operating very close to the channel capacity for a large number of channels. Indeed, by the aid of LDPC codes modern datatransmission and data-storage systems are able to achieve performances very close to the Shannon’s information-theoretic limits [1]-[5]. Traditionally, an LDPC code is defined to be a linear code having a sparse parity-check matrix H. These codes were discovered first by Gallager in the early 1960s [6], [7]. Unfortunately, this remarkable invention was almost disregarded by coding researchers until late 1990’s and early 2000’s when LDPC codes rediscovered by MacKay, Neal, and others [1]-[5],[8], [9]. Since this rediscovery, a great deal of effort has been devoted to designing and constructing efficient LDPC codes. From code construction perspective,

LDPC codes are classified into two main categories: computergenerated pseudo-random (random-like) LDPC codes and well-structured (structured) LDPC codes. The performance of structured LDPC codes is not generally as good as random-like ones, but such codes are much less complex than random-like ones as far as decoding/encoding operations are concerned.
By now, it is a well-known fact that the presence of short cycles in the Tanner graph [10] of an LDPC code can degrade the decoder’s performance [11]. Therefore, for a given LDPC code to perform well with iterative decoding, short cycles should be avoided in its Tanner graph, especially those of length 4. A parity-check matrix H is said to satisfy the rowcolumn (RC)-constraint if for any two rows (or any two columns) of H there is at most one position wherein both of them are non-zero. If the RC-constraint holds for H, then it can readily be seen that the Tanner graph associated with H is free of 4-cycles. Hence, the code has girth at least 6. A binary (γ, ρ)-regular LDPC code is defined as the null space of a sparse parity-check matrix over F2 with constant columnweight γ and constant row-weight ρ. If the parity-check matrix of a (γ, ρ)-regular LDPC code satisfies the RC-constraint, then the code has minimum distance at least γ + 1 [12], [9].
Structured LDPC codes are constructed based on combinatorial and algebraic tools; for example see [9],[13]-[22]. Finite fields are one of the most commonly used algebraic structures used in constructing structured LDPC codes. On the other hand, Steiner triple systems, projective planes, affine planes, unital designs, oval designs, and Bose-BIBDs are some of the combinatorial designs which already have been used for constructing structured LDPC codes [13]-[21]. As can be seen, all of these families of t-designs are indeed special types of Steiner 2-designs (BIBDs with parameter λ = 1). More generally, Johnson and Weller employed partial geometries for code construction [20]. Furthermore, recently, Falsafain and Esmaeili presented a method for designing LDPC codes using Steiner systems with parameter t > 2 and their residual designs [22]. This code design approach can be considered as a generalization of the well-known method which uses the point-block incidence matrix of a Steiner 2-design for code construction.

For a t-(v, k, λ) design (X, B) to be exploitable by this method it suffices that the following three conditions are satisfied: 1) |B1 ∩ B2| ≤ t for any two blocks B1, B2 ∈ B and B1 = B2; 2) λ > 1; and 3) k > t. It can be shown that these inequalities actually hold for various types of tdesigns; so they can be utilized for constructing LDPC codes. In particular, we use simple triple systems (should not be confused with Steiner triple systems), super-simple BIBDs, Steiner systems (and their residual designs), and large sets of t-designs for code construction.

Section 4 presents four utilizable families of t-designs and discusses the structural properties of their resulting LDPC codes. More precisely, the first step is to construct a parity-check matrix over F2 by using Theorem 3. The next step is to replace
the non-zero entries of this parity-check matrix by non-zero elements from Fq in a structured manner. Finally, the last step is to disperse each entry in the resulting matrix (over Fq) into a (q − 1) × (q − 1) matrix over Fq using the two-dimensional (q−1)-fold matrix dispersion. Note that each non-zero entry is dispersed into a (q − 1) × (q − 1) circulant permutation matrix over Fq and each zero entry is dispersed into a (q−1)×(q−1)
zero matrix.
Suppose q is a prime power. Let Fq be the finite field of order q, and let α be a primitive element of Fq. For each non-zero element αi, 0 ≤ i ≤ q − 2, the location vector of αi, denoted z(αi), is defined to be a (q − 1)-tuple (z0, z1, . . . , zq−2) over Fq (whose components are associated with the q − 1 non-zero elements of Fq) such that its (i + 1)-th component zi is set to αi and all the other components are set to zero. The location vector z(0) of the zero element of Fq is defined as the all-zero (q − 1)-tuple (0, 0, . . . , 0).
For any non-zero element αi ∈ Fq, 0 ≤ i ≤ q − 2, the twodimensional (q − 1)-fold matrix dispersion of αi (or simply matrix dispersion of αi), denoted A(αi), is defined to be a (q − 1) × (q − 1) circulant permutation matrix over Fq, whose r-th row, 1 ≤ r ≤ q − 1, is the location vector of αi+r−1. The two-dimensional (q − 1)-fold matrix dispersion A(0) of the zero element of Fq is defined as the (q − 1) × (q − 1) all-zero

structural characteristics of an LDPC code derived from a tdesign are directly linked to the intrinsic properties of the employed design. For example, the presence of some specific configurations (e.g., Pasch configuration) in the employed design can degrade the structural properties of the resulting LDPC code. Generally, for an arbitrary t-design, studying these structures and analyzing the effect of each of them on the performance of its resulting LDPC code is very difficult and almost impossible [14]. So it is hard to obtain a clear and thorough understanding of the behavior of the performance curve in the high-SNR region (the proposed method does not guarantee a good performance in the high-SNR region). However, simulation studies can reveal valuable information about the general behavior of the performance curve and about the phenomena that can occur in error-floor region (e.g., oscillation). In fact, in the case of combinatorially constructed LDPC codes, most of the studies regarding the behavior of the performance curve are based on computer-aided simulations (though there exist some analytical studies, for example [14]).

VII. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we first presented a new general combinatorial method for constructing structured 4-cycle free binary regular LDPC codes. Based on this method, an extremely large number of t-designs can be utilized for code construction. In particular, we concentrated on the exploitation of simple TSs, super-simple BIBDs, Steiner systems, and t-designs derived from LSs with k = t + 1. Several new classes of LDPC codes with a wide range of code rates and block lengths were introduced from utilization of these t-designs. We also described how to construct binary and non-binary girth-6 QCLDPC codes using appropriate t-designs together with the matrix dispersion technique. Simulation results confirm that the constructed non-binary QC-LDPC codes can yield good practical performances under iterative decoding using FFTQSPA.

His research interests include errorcorrecting codes and combinatorics, with emphasis on combinatorial designs and LDPC codes.
Morteza Esmaeili received the M.Sc. degree in Mathematics in 1988 from Teacher Training University of Tehran, Iran, and the Ph.D degree in Mathematics (Coding Theory) in 1996 from Carleton University, Ottawa, Canada. The following two years he was a postdoctoral fellow in the Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering at the University of Waterloo, Waterloo, Canada. Since September 1998 He has been with the Isfahan University of Technology as a faculty member where he is now a Professor at the Department of Mathematical Sciences. He joined the Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering at the University of Victoria, Victoria, B.C., Canada, as an Adjunct Professor in July 2009. His current research interest include coding and information theory, cryptography and combinatorics.
