A High Throughput Configurable Parallel Encoder Architecture for Quasi-Cyclic Low-Density Parity-Check Codes

Alaa Aldin AL HARIRI, Fabrice MONTEIRO, Loïc SIÉLER, Abbas DANDACHE Laboratoire LCOMS, Université de Lorraine 7 rue Marconi, 57070 Metz, France
{alla-aldin.al-hariri, fabrice.monteiro, loic.sieler, abbas.dandache}@univ-lorraine.fr

Abstract—In this paper, we are proposing a new architecture for fast encoding of Quasi-Cyclic Low-Density Parity Codes (QCLDPC). QC-LDPC codes are becoming more and more popular in a wide range of applications, including data transmission (WiMAX, DVB-S2) in telecommunication systems, increasing the need for effective encoder architectures. In our approach, support for a large subset of QC-LDPC codes is provided thanks to the configurability of the architecture at synthesis level. High levels of parallelism can be reached, and hence high throughput achieved, due to the modular encoder architecture that takes advantage of the highly regular structure of QC-LDPC parity check matrices.
The architectural design has been validated through implementation on an Altera Stratix II FPGA of different encoders related to DVB-T2 and DVB-S2. Very high data rates (up to 28.9 GB/s) have been achieved with still acceptable hardware consumption (about 32k logic elements) proving the effectiveness of the approach.
Index Terms—Error correcting codes, QC-LDPC, parallel and configurable encoder architectures, FPGA implementation.
I. INTRODUCTION
Modern communication systems requirements concerning performance, cost and dependability are higher and higher: transmitting ever increasing volumes of data in shorter and shorter delays on diverse physical media without data loss or corruption, providing support for multiple transmission standards, being versatile are only some of the many challenges to be met. Error correcting codes (ECC) are since long being used to protect the data from the corruption induced by the transmission disturbances. Effective use of shared media such as wireless with non-extensible bandwith raises the need for more efficient codes that are also unfortunately much more complex. With the ever increasing demand for higher data transmission rates (and data volumes), it becomes harder and harder to design an effective communication system. It is notably true concerning the ECC encoder and decoder subsystems, that require novel architectures. For a long while, the evolution of silicon techology allowed the “classical” approach to improve performance: increasing operation frequency, reducing device dimensions and integrating more functionnalities in the same circuit. New technological and design constraints (higher sensitivity of circuits to disturbances, low power requirements and other) have shifted the need for much more massively parallel architectures.

In this context of ECC, LDPC codes are currently the most promising coding technique to achieve the Shannon capacities. LDPC codes achieve 0.04dB of the Shannon’s limit [1] and LDPC is finding use in practical applications such as 10GBase-T Ethernet (sending data at 10 Gbits/s over noisy CAT6 cables) and many other applications. LDPC codes are also part of the WiFi-802.11 standard as an optional part of 802.11n and 802.11ac, in the high throughput (HT) PHY specification [2].
Globaly, methods for constructing LDPC codes can be broadly classified into two categories: graph-theoreticbased (GTB) methods and algebraic methods. In this paper, we are interested in the GTB approach, as it has lower errorfloors and offers the possibility to decode LDPC using iterative message-passing algorithms. Several architectures have been proposed for the LDPC encoder [3], [4]. However, the complexity of the general LDPC code encoders is still much too high.
In order to lower the complexity of encoding, many research works have focused their efforts in reducing the complexity of the encoding matrices using specially formed matrices such as lower triangular [5] or semi-random generated matrices [6]. The encoding process of standard LDPC codes requires transforming a parity check matrix (H) into an equivalent systematic form, which can be accomplished by Gaussian elimination [7]. Gaussian elimination however requires large amountsof memory and intense calculation [7].
There are by now more than 200 standardized LDPC codes [8]. However, most of the proposed architectures support just a single type of LDPC codes or at most, a family of codes sharing a fixed block length and code rate.
In this paper, we are proposing an alternative architecture for a configurable parallel encoder, compatible with a wide range of standardized QC-LDPC along with different block length. The details of the proposed architecture are discussed in the next section.

III. PROPOSED ENCODER ARCHITECTURE
We are addressing a parametrable LDPC encoder architecture, for which the parameters are: the size of the sub-matrix A, the code rate, and the block size that must be scalable to fit different LDPC code block sizes. There are three main parts in the architecture: the calculation window, the controller and the memory, as shown in Fig 1. We have reduced the hardware overhead of the encoder using a partial parallel encoder strategy, based on the use of a calculation window. The length of the window is equal to i (size of sub-matrix A) and the width is w, provided that imodw = 0. This window is used to calculate j hdi,jdj. It is implemented using XORs and shift registers. The count of XORs is equal to i whereas, the input of these XORs varies according to the width w of the window. The window will shift all over the entire matrix H to calculate the parity of this frame.
The controller is the management unit of overall architecture. It allows to select the coding rate, the size of sub-matrix A and width of calculation the window. It controls the memory read/write mod and also allows to control the shift amplitude in the shift registers, from 1 to multiple bit shifts.
Memory is like a buffer that allows us to store the received frame. During encoding, the memory size should be equal to at least twice the block length. However, the ideal scenario to store the current frame and the next frame is under condition that the size calculation window is equal to the square. In this case, we only need 1-bit shift register whose size is given by the expression (i × i). The encoding time depends on size of frame, clock frequency, code rates and size of window. In each step we repeat the calculation using the same hardware resources until the overall encoding is completed.

The parity sub-matrices are not stored in the encoder memory like other encoder architecture [4], [13], only input data are stored. In Table I, a comparison between this encoder architecture with three previously mentioned LDPC hardware encoders implemented on FPGA platforms is presented. The present encoder has reasonably high performance compared to the other FPGA implementations [3], [4], [13] and could reach up to 29 Gbps with acceptable hardware overheads [13]. The given architecture achieves higher throughput, between 3 and 6 Gbps. Compared to ours, the design in [13] is not flexible in regards to code rate or block size. The design [4] has, for a fixed code rate, a stable throughput regardless of data length. The throughput is however lower than with our design. Actually, the architecture we propose achieves a speedup of 3 and 20 times compared to [13] and [4], respectively. Furthermore, our design requires less memory (twice the block lenght) compared with [4] (a fixed amount of about 3.9 Mbits).

V. CONCLUSION
A new architecture for a configurable QC-LDPC encoder
using a semi-random H-matrix has been presented. This
method allowed us to effectively manage HW and memories
requirements. To verify our design, a flexible encoder was
implemented for the DVB-S2 standard and another for DVB-
T2. The two designs were synthesized and implemented on
Altera StratixII. The FPGA encoder had a throughput up to
29 Gbps for DVB-S2 and up to 19.5 Gbps for the second
encoder. A speedup of 3–20 times is provided compared prior
designs. The flexibility of the architecture for the block length
and code rate makes our encoder compatible with a wide range
of applications and for different standards of LDPC.
