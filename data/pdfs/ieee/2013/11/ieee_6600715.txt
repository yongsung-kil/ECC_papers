Low Density Parity Check Codes with Non-Equiprobable Symbols
Bowen Dai, Student Member, IEEE, and Lei Wei, Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—In this letter, we analyze how to decode LDPC codes with non-equiprobable binary symbols. For such symbols, we need to modify the conventional Tanner graph, because the message passing from check nodes to information bits and to parity check bits are diverse. In other words, in conventional Tanner graph, the message passing along two directions are identical, while in our case, the message along the forward direction and backward direction are different. A method to optimize signaling constellation which maximizes the channel mutual information is presented as well. In the numerical section, symbols with prior probabilities (0.3, 0.7) could gain 0.72 dB in performance if we replace equal space constellation with optimal constellation. Several cases of short LDPC codes (N, K = 1024, 512) are explored and gain almost 0.4 dB. The simulation shows that additional gain of only 0.2 dB could be attainable, if optimal source coding is achievable.
Index Terms—LDPC codes, non-equiprobable symbols, unequal energy allocation.

This letter makes several contributions. (a) We show how to choose the optimal constellations for LDPC codes optimizing mutual information. (b) We review message passing for LDPC codes with non-equiprobable symbols, which was already addressed, for example, in [9]. (c) We simulate LDPC codes using non-equiprobable signalling and compare them with equiprobable counterparts and prior work in [8].
This letter is organized as follows. Section II describes signal and system. We perform constellation optimization to maximize capacity for non-equiprobable symbols in Section III. Section IV introduces decoding procedure for LDPC codes with non-equiprobable symbols. Section V presents numerical results. Section VI gives conclusion and future works.
II. SIGNAL AND SYSTEM

I. INTRODUCTION
O PTIMAL digital communication systems with equiprobable symbols have been well analyzed and applied widely in practice, including wireless communications. Good summaries of these works can be found in many books, e.g. [1]. Recently, these optimal settings have been extended in various directions to accommodate practical needs. Source coding (for example, CELP encoder and decoder for speech signals in [2]) has been commonly applied so that at the output of the source coder the symbols approach equiprobable. However, the assumption of equal symbol probabilities at the output of the source coder is not always satisfied. In many practical applications, due to the sub-optimality of the compression scheme, the bit stream often exhibits a certain amount of redundancy [2], [3]. With these redundancies, the symbols are not equiprobable [4]. Recently, several papers [5] - [7] have a novel and direct approach to the original problem of optimizing the system with non-equiprobable symbols. On the other hand, the performance of LDPC codes and the design of LDPC codes with equiprobable symbols has also been studied intensively. Some works on LDPC and Turbo codes for non-equiprobable symbols can also be found in [8], [9]. In [10], it is also considered how to match up prior probabilities of information symbols and parity check symbols using transformation layers.

Let pi,1 = P (vi = 0), pi,2 = P (vi = 1), where P (.) denotes probability. {vi} is then mapped to binary constellation {di} ∈ {xi,1, xi,2} and corrupted by AWGN noise {ni} with zero mean and variance of N0/2, i.e., yi = di + ni. Traditionally, (i.e., equip√robable case), we have pi,1 = pi,2 = 0.5 and xi,1 = −xi,2 = Es, where Es is the average energy per transmitted symbol. In this letter, we focus on a special nonequiprobable case, i.e., pj,1 = p1 = pj,2 = p2, j = 1, · · · , K and p1 + p2 = 1.
We use (7,4) Hamming code to illustrate difference. In Fig. 1(a) we show the conventional Tanner graph with V nodes and check nodes, C. For equiprobable symbols, each edge can
be input and output of the parity check node. For example,
edge of C1 to v6 can be viewed as output of C1 with inputs v2, v3, v4; at the same time, edge of v6 to C1 can also viewed as input of C1 with outputs to v2, v3, v4 respectively. For non-equiprobable symbols, due to mismatches in prior
probabilities, we can only select one parity check bit as output,
i.e., each edge can only be either input or output, not both. For example, in Fig. 1(b), let p1,1 = p2,1 = p3,1 = p4,1 = 0.3, then we have p6,1 = 0.468. If we further view v4 as output, then for p2,1 = p3,1 = 0.3 and p6,1 = 0.468, we obtain p4,1 = 0.495, which is contradicted to p4,1 = 0.3.
To deal with non-equiprobable cases, we need to work out three tasks, (1) optimal constellations {xi,1, xi,2}; (2) message passing from information bits to parity check bits; (3) message
passing from parity check bits to each information bit.
Therefore, we will focus on short LDPC codes in this paper.
Task (1) will be resolved in next section and tasks (2) and (3)
will be addressed in Section IV.

VI. CONCLUSIONS AND DISCUSSIONS
In this letter, we reviewed how to decode LDPC codes with non-equiprobable symbols. We showed that the message passing from information bits and parity check bits to check nodes are different and formulated a method to optimize signaling constellation which maximizes channel capacity. In Section V, for symbols with prior probabilities (p1 = 0.3, p2 = 0.7), we can gain 0.72 dB in performance if optimal c√onste√llation ( Esp1/p2, − Esp2/p1) is used instead of (− Es, Es). We further simulated several cases of short LDPC codes and gained almost 0.4 dB. The simulation showed if we can achieve optimal source coding, then further additional gain of 0.2 dB could be achievable. In future, we would like to optimize codes using density evolution method and compare to many known best codes, including non-uniform Turbo codes.
