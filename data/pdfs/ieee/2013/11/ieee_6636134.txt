Large Scale LP Decoding with Low Complexity
Guoqiang Zhang, Richard Heusdens, and W. Bastiaan Kleijn

Abstract—Linear program (LP) decoding has become increasingly popular for error-correcting codes due to its simplicity and promising performance. Low-complexity and efficient iterative algorithms for LP decoding are of great importance for practical applications. In this paper we focus on solving the binary LP decoding problem by using the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM). Our main contribution is that we propose a linear-complexity algorithm for the projection onto a parity polytope (having a computational complexity of O(d), where d is the check-node degree), as compared to recent work [1], which has a computational complexity of O(d log d). In particular, we show that the projection onto the parity polytope can be transformed to a projection onto a simplex.
Index Terms—Distributed optimization, LP decoding, alternating direction method of multipliers.
I. INTRODUCTION
L INEAR codes have gained significant attention in the last two decades due to the near-Shannon performance of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [2]. For instance, in [3], a family of LDPC codes performs within 0.0045 dB of the capacity of the channel (as the block length goes to infinity). The most popular decoding method is belief propagation (BP) [4]. However, the exact convergence analysis of belief propagation remains challenging for loopy graphic models which represent linear codes.
An alternative decoding scheme is to draw upon convex optimization. In [5], Feldman et al. showed that for binary codes used over a memoryless binary-input output-symmetric channel, the maximum likelihood (ML) decoding problem can be relaxed to a linear program (LP). By doing so, the decoding of the linear block codes is transformed to a convex optimization problem. Empirical studies on LDPC codes [5], [1] demonstrate that the bit-error-rate performance of LP decoding is competitive with that of belief propagation decoding.
Since the work of [5], research interest has moved to the design of efficient optimization algorithms for solving LP decoding that is competitive to belief propagation decoding. Standard LP solvers are natural methods for LP decoding. For instance, the interior-point [6] and revised simplex [7] approachs have been adapted for LP decoding. An alternative approach is to design specialized algorithms that directly exploit the sparse geometry of the linear codes [8].
Recently, Barman et al. have proposed an iterative algorithm for LP decoding based on the alternating direction method

The optimization for each parity check constraint can be realized by the Euclidean projection onto an associated parity polytope [1]. The authors proposed an algorithm of complexity O(d log d) for the projection onto each parity polytope, where d is the associated check-node degree. In particular, the projection involves two sorting operations which require a significant fraction of the computational effort. In this paper, we simplify the projection by removing the two sorting operations.
Formally, we reconsider the projection onto the parity polytope. We show that the projection can be transformed to a projection onto a simplex, which is well studied and admits a simple projection algorithm [9]. As a result, the computational complexity is O(d) as compared to O(d log d) of the algorithm in [1], which is better suited for decoding large scale codes.
While our work was submitted for publication, we noticed that Zhang and Siegel (see [10]) have also attempted to simplify the projection onto the parity polytope. However, the algorithm in [10] still needs one sorting operation, which is computationally expensive. In this work, we show that by transforming the projection to a projection onto a simplex, sorting operations can be avoided.
II. LINEAR PROGRAM DECODING
In this section, we first introduce maximum-likelihood (ML) decoding. We then briefly describe the LP decoding problem [5] for approximating the ML solution. The notation used in the paper is in line with that of [1] for consistency.
Suppose a binary linear code C is defined by a m×n paritycheck matrix H with n codeword symbols. The code C can be fully characterized by a factor graph G. In particular, let I = {1, 2, . . . , n} and J = {1, 2, . . . , m} denote the set of variable nodes and the set of check nodes, respectively. The graph G consists of all the check and variable nodes with edges between I and J as specified by nonzero elements of H. We use N (i) to denote the set of check nodes incident to a variable node i ∈ I. Similarly, we let N (j) be the set of variable nodes incident to j ∈ J . As mentioned in the introduction, we use dj to denote the degree of check-node j ∈ J , i.e., dj = |N (j)|. For a regular LDPC code, we have dj = d for all j ∈ J .
A valid binary codeword y ∈ C satisfies all the parity-check constraints. We now describe the constraints mathematically. For each j ∈ J , we construct a dj × n matrix P j that selects the dj components of y involved in the jth check node. Consequently, the parity-check constraint for j ∈ J can be expressed as P jy ∈ Pdj , where Pdj = e ∈ {0, 1}dj | e 1 is even defines the “local codeword” ensemble of the length- dj single parity-check code.

V. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS

We tested Algorithm 1 by computer simulations using C

programming language, which we referred to as Alg1. for

brevity. From Section IV, we know that the complexity of

Alg1. is of order O(d). This is mainly because the two sub-

routines “partial sorting” and “linearProject2simplex” have

expected complexities linear in d. For comparison purposes,

we modified Alg1. by replacing the above two subroutines

with two new subroutines “sorting” and “project2simplex”,

respectively. Each of the two new subroutines involves a

standard sorting operation [9]. We denote the new algorithm

as Alg1mod. for simplicity. It is immediate the new algorithm

has computational complexity O(d log(d)), which is the same

as that of the algorithm in [1].

VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we have reconsidered the Euclidean projection onto the parity polytope, which is a key step of the ADMMbased distributed algorithm for large-scale LP decoding. We have proposed a new low-complexity method for the projection. We showed that the projection can be transformed to a projection onto a simplex, which can be realized in linear complexity in terms of the dimensionality of the simplex. Experimental results confirm the high efficiency of the new method.
