750Mb/s 17pJ/b 90nm CMOS (120,75) TS-LDPC Min-Sum Based Analog Decoder

Alireza Rabbani Abolfazli, Yousef R. Shayan and Glenn E.R. Cowan Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering Concordia University, Montreal, Quebec
Email: {a rabban, yshayan, gcowan}@ece.concordia.ca

Abstract—Circuit and IC implementation of a (120, 75) MinSum based Turbo-Structured LDPC analog decoder in CMOS 90nm technology is presented. This is the highest throughput and one of the longest codes implemented to date using analog techniques. At a Bit Error Rate of 10−5, the measured performance is within 0.2dB of modeled performance using floatingpoint arithmetic. The chip was tested at a throughput of 750Mb/s. This improves the throughput of analog decoders by a factor of 57. The power dissipation of the core is 13 mW resulting in 17pJ/b energy efficiency. The core area is 1.38mm2. The fabricated MSbased TS-LDPC analog decoder has BER performance nearly identical to theory without compromising energy efficiency.
Keywords—LDPC code, Decoder, Analog VLSI
I. INTRODUCTION
LDPC codes are a class of linear block codes that have sparse parity check matrices (H). Due to their superior BER performance these codes are adopted as the channel code in numerous communication systems with various applications, such as wireless communication, magnetic recording channels and fiber optic transmission.
The graphical representation of an LDPC code using a bipartite graph is known as a Tanner graph. The Tanner graph associated with the parity check matrix is comprised of two disjoint sets of nodes: variable nodes (VAR) and check nodes (CHK) nodes, corresponding to the columns and rows of the H matrix, respectively. VAR nodes are connected to CHK nodes through the edges. The connection structure is defined by the parity check matrix. Each VAR node represents a coded bit and each CHK node represents a parity check equation. Connections represent bi-directional signals.
In this work we consider a class of structured regular LDPC codes, called Turbo-Structured LDPC (TS-LDPC) codes. TSLDPC codes outperform random LDPC codes and have much lower error floor at high Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR). LDPC codes can be decoded using iterative soft-decision decoding algorithms such as the Sum-Product (SP) and the Min-Sum (MS) algorithms. However, the MS algorithm is used in this work since the complexity of CHK nodes and VAR nodes is much lower than for the SP algorithm. We have already shown that TS-LDPC codes can be decoded using the MS algorithm without any degradation in the error performance [1].
SP and MS decoding algorithms can be performed by analog or digital circuitry. Analog decoders are preferred in many communication systems due to their potential for higher speed, single-codeword latency, lower power dissipation and smaller chip area compared to their digital counterparts. Unlike

those for the SP algorithm, analog circuits that are designed to perform MS iterative decoding algorithms can be biased in weak, moderate or strong inversion regions, allowing for the trading off of speed versus power for the decoder. Additionally, analog implementations map the Tanner graph onto a collection of interconnected analog building blocks, thereby exploiting parallelism in order to give sufficient throughput. Finally, soft-decision digital decoders require Analog-to-Digital Converters (ADC) with multi-bit resolution. Analog decoders can directly process analog channel samples, eliminating these ADCs, replacing them with 1-bit ADCs at the output of the decoder.
Most of the previous analog decoders were based on short length codes [2]–[5]. Moreover, the only MS-based analog LDPC decoder chip under performs the theory by roughly 2dB at an SNR of 10−5 [3]. In this work, a medium length (120, 75) MS-based TS-LDPC analog decoder is designed and fabricated. It is among the longest codes implemented to date using analog techniques and the first TS-based decoder of either digital or analog implementation. This work improves the speed of analog decoding by a factor of 57 and achieves a throughput of 750Mb/s. This sets a new state-of-the-art for analog decoding.

III. IMPLEMENTATION OF A (120,75) TS-LDPC DECODER
The Tanner graph of the (120, 75) TS-LDPC code is shown in Fig. 1. For clarity, not all edges within the Interleaver are drawn explicitly. The code depicted in Fig. 1 has 120 VAR nodes and 45 CHK nodes. Each VAR node has a degree of 3 (i.e., it connects to 3 CHK nodes) and each CHK node has a degree of 8. Connections in Fig. 1 are bidirectional connections and are implemented using two separate wires. In this work, current-mode signals between nodes represent LLRs utilizing a scaling of 155nA per LLR value (I=M×155nA). A positive LLR is represented by a current which enters to the input of a module. Based on this definition a current mirror/buffer can be used as a block with gain of -1.

A. Structure of variable nodes
Fig. 2 shows VAR node 1 and its connections to CHK nodes 1, 2 and 3. VAR nodes use single-input, multi-output (1:n) current buffers in order to compute output signal currents. The LLR values from the channel (I10) and messages obtained from the CHK nodes 1, 2 and 3 ( I ) CH1,2,3→V1 ) are added using Kirchhoff’s Current Law and the output messages (IV1→CH1,2,3 ) are generated. The sum of input currents, IV1 , is converted to a voltage by the parasitic capacitor, Cp. The decoded bit (c1) is computed from the polarity of IV1 . Switches at the inputs of the VAR node, are closed to initiate decoding and opened in order to reset the analog decoder for the next codeword. These switches are controlled by an off-chip control voltage VSW .

B. Structure of check nodes
CHK nodes update their messages based on (1). To compute the outgoing message of CHK node j to VAR node i, each of the inputs of the CHK node is decomposed into its magnitude and sign. The signs of all the received signals are multiplied except the sign of the signal received from VAR node i. The minimum of the magnitudes of all the received signals except the signal from VAR node i is determined. The message that is sent to VAR node i is composed from the product of the signs and the minimum of the magnitudes.

Fig. 2: Variable node structure with signals between the 1st variable node and check nodes 1,2 and 3.
The structure of the CHK node 1 is shown in Fig. 3. It consists of 8 of each of the following: Magnitude/Sign Extractor Unit (MSEU), 7-output current mirror, 7-input minimizer, Magnitude/Sign Combiner Unit (MSCU), inverter, and 7-input XOR gate. The output current directed to a particular VAR node is computed based on the input currents coming from the other 7 VAR nodes. The polarity (sign) of the input currents is multiplied by the XOR gate to obtain the sign of the output, while the 7-way minimum of the input magnitudes determines the magnitude of the output.
In [3], it has been observed that the designed minimizer circuit contributes significantly to error performance degradation at high SNR. In this work, the 7-input minimizer is designed based on the one proposed in [6]. Its accuracy is improved by adjusting bias current and transistor sizes. Diodeconnected transistor stacks are added to the circuit to reduce latency and increase its accuracy by providing additional paths for current. Transistors are biased in moderate inversion to make a trade-off between power consumption and speed of the circuit. As shown in Fig. 3, eight 7-input minimizers are required to implement a check node. To implement 7-input minimizers different design strategies such as cascading 2input minimizers, combinations of 2-input and 4-input minimizers and combination of 3-input and 5-input minimizer have been studied. The sizes of the transistors are chosen for optimum balance between accuracy, speed and robustness against mismatch. It has been illustrated that the modeled (120,75) TS-LDPC decoder is robust against mismatch of up to 20% [1]. MonteCarlo simulation over 100 samples showed that mismatches of the implemented VAR node and CHK node modules are less than the targeted mismatch of 20%.
IV. (120,75) TS-LDPC DECODER CHIP
Fig. 4 shows the top-level design of the decoder chip. The input-stage provides analog LLR values to the analog-decoder block. The output-stage captures and serially transmits the estimated codeword to an off-chip module. For each decoding cycle, the chip receives LLR values calculated based on 120 noisy channel samples. To facilitate testing, LLRs are sent to the chip as 6-bit digital values. 5 bits control the inputs of a binary-weighted current-output DAC while the 6th bit selects the direction of the current. The received 720 LLR bits are saved in a memory block. For simultaneous loading and decoding cycles, a 720-bit latch is used in parallel with the memory block. By closing the switches controlled by VSW , the analog processor starts decoding. Currents representing LLRs start flowing between VAR nodes and CHK nodes. Over time, the analog continuous-time currents approach steady state. At this stage, the estimated codeword c is stored in the outputstage block. At the end of each decoding cycle, following a decoding time, Td, switches are opened to reset the analog decoder module.
At higher SNR, decoding can be concluded for most of

the codewords before the maximum settling time is reached. A unique feature of this analog decoder is the generation of an Early Termination Signal (ETS) raised if all parity-check equations are satisfied allowing a decoding decision to be made before Td has elapsed. This signal can be used to improve the energy efficiency of the decoder chip by moving the analog heart of the decoder to sleep mode and wait for the next codeword. This leads to a reduction in power consumption. ETS can be used as an on-chip Frame Error Declaration as well. If a decoded codeword does not satisfy the parity check equation, then ETS is not activated during decoding time (Td) representing an error in the codeword frame. In addition, ETS gives us the opportunity to measure the average decoding time.
This analog decoder was fabricated in TSMC 90nm technology. Its die micrograph is shown in Fig. 5. The chip has an area of 1.8×1.8=3.24mm2 while the analog decoder core uses a rectangle of 1.38mm2. Ignoring regions void of circuitry, the area of DACs, VAR nodes and CHK nodes is only 0.75mm2. In this work, digital circuitry uses 1.2V while the analog part uses 1.1V and 0.85V supplies. The reference current required for the DACs is provided by an off-chip current source.
V. MEASUREMENT RESULTS
The IC was tested using a Teradyne FLEX tester, which was programmed to generate all control signals and apply them to the chip. The vector of LLRs was produced using Matlab and sent to the chip by the tester. In order to find BER, the measured output data were compared to the expected values and the number of errors was generated by the tester.
The BER measurement results for different decoding times, Td, are shown in Fig. 6. When Td is 15μs the measured BER outperforms Matlab simulations at SNR 3. For Td=500ns and the tester-limited minimum of 100ns, measured performance is 0.1dB and 0.2dB worse, respectively than simulation. Power dissipation of the analog core is 13mW. Due to the tester speed limitations, LLRs were loaded before decoding took place. Theoretical information throughput performance is estimated from decoding time, assuming consecutive codewords could be decoded each Td. This implies a maximum measurable information throughput of 750Mb/s and a minimum energy efficiency of 17pJ/b.

VI. CONCLUSION
This work presents the first implementation of a TSLDPC decoder. The Min-Sum algorithm is used to perform decoding on the designed (120,75) TS-LDPC code. Due to the advantages of the MS algorithm and analog circuitry, the MSbased analog decoder was implemented. This decoder is one of the longest length codes implemented to date using analog techniques. The designed chip was fabricated using CMOS 90nm technology. The decoder IC was tested with a decoding time of 100ns, which results in throughput of 750Mb/s. The measurements show that the error performance of the chip is comparable with theory and the SNR of the chip differs only by 0.2dB at a BER of 10−5. The power consumption of the decoder heart of the chip was 13mW corresponding to an energy efficiency of 17pJ/b. The measurement results show that the fabricated chip is the best-in-class analog decoder. This decoder improves the speed of the analog decoders by a factor 57.
