Robust Rate-Compatible Punctured LDPC Convolutional Codes
Hua Zhou, Student Member, IEEE, David G. M. Mitchell, Member, IEEE, Norbert Goertz, Senior Member, IEEE, and Daniel J. Costello, Jr., Life Fellow, IEEE

Abstract—A family of robust rate-compatible (RC) punctured low-density parity-check convolutional codes (LDPC-CCs) is derived from a time-invariant LDPC-CC mother code by periodically puncturing encoded bits (variable nodes) with respect to several criteria: (1) ensuring the recoverability of punctured variable nodes, (2) minimizing the number of completely punctured cycle trapping sets (CPCTSs), and (3) minimizing the number of punctured variable nodes involved in short cycles. The influence of (1) and (3) on iterative decoding performance is felt most strongly in the waterfall region of the bit-error-rate (BER) curve, while (2) has a larger effect in the error floor, or high signalto-noise ratio (SNR), region. We show that the length of the puncturing period is an important parameter when designing high rate punctured codes and, moreover, that extending the puncturing period can improve the decoding performance and extend the range of compatible rates. As examples, we obtain families of RC LDPC-CCs from several time-invariant LDPCCC mother codes with monomial and binomial entries in their polynomial syndrome former matrices.
Index Terms—Rate-compatible codes (RCCs), low-density parity-check convolutional codes (LDPC-CCs), spatially-coupled codes, punctured codes, cycle enumerators, cycle trapping sets.
I. INTRODUCTION
R ATE-COMPATIBLE (RC) channel codes, a form of variable rate coding, are of great interest since they can adapt to the changing conditions of time-varying channels while allowing transceivers to employ the same encoder/decoder pair. As competitors to RC turbo codes, RC low-density parity-check block codes (LDPC-BCs) have recently been investigated using code modifying techniques such as nulling or shortening, extending, puncturing, and combining [1] - [12].

Other methods of puncturing LDPC-BCs can be found in [7]–[9]. To maintain a constant code block length, Casado et al. Costello et al. [13] have shown that LDPC convolutional codes (LDPC-CCs) [14] can be implemented without an increase in computational complexity compared to corresponding LDPC-BCs. In addition, it has recently been shown that terminated LDPC-CC ensembles, also known as spatially coupled LDPC codes, have substantially better belief propagation (BP) decoding thresholds compared to corresponding block, or uncoupled, code ensembles [15], [16]. Therefore, extending the concept of conventional RC punctured convolutional codes [17] to RC punctured LDPC-CCs is of practical interest. Conventional RC punctured convolutional codes are obtained by periodically puncturing encoded bits chosen with respect to a distance spectrum criterion [17]. For small to moderate syndrome former memories, the distance spectra of LDPCCCs can be precisely computed by the BEAST algorithm [18], or estimated using the techniques proposed in [19]; however, it is infeasible to precisely compute the distance spectra of practically interesting LDPC-CCs with large syndrome former memories. Moreover, the distance spectrum is primarily important for maximum likelihood (ML) decoding, whereas when using (sub-optimal) iterative decoding techniques, such as BP decoding, graph-based properties, such as short cycles in the Tanner graph and trapping sets [20], are more important. Hence we derive families of RC LDPC-CCs by periodically puncturing encoded bits based on the graphical structure of a time-invariant LDPC convolutional mother code.

In [5], the concept of a recovery tree was introduced to

determine the number of iterations required to ‘recover’ a punctured variable node vt(j) for LDPC-BCs. The recovery tree
is obtained using a two-step process: (1) connect the punctured variable node vt(j) to all of the neighboring check nodes in N (vt(j)); and (2) then connect each check node c(tk) ∈ N (vt(j)) to the variable nodes N (ct(k)) \ {vt(j)}. IV. RC PUNCTURED LDPC-CCS
In this section, we present a method to construct RC punctured LDPC-CCs by analyzing several properties of the Tanner graphs of punctured LDPC-CCs. The advantages of this method compared to a distance spectrum based technique is that each of the properties can be precisely and efficiently calculated, even for codes with large syndrome former memories, and that these graph-based properties are directly related to the performance of iterative decoding.
All of the LDPC-CCs considered in this paper are nonsystematic. Hence, in order to correctly decode codewords back to the original message, in addition to decoding the unpunctured variable nodes, we also need to recover the punctured variable nodes. As a result, when counting bits in error to obtain bit-error-rates (BERs), both punctured and unpunctured nodes must be included.
