Locally-Connected Viterbi Decoder Architectures and their VLSI Implementation for LDPC and Convolutional codes
Ahmed Refaey∗, Se´bastien Roy†, Isabelle Laroche‡, and Benoit Gosselin‡
∗Dept. of Electrical and Computer Engineering, University of Western Ontario, London, Canada. †Dept. of Electrical and Computer Engineering, Universite´ de Sherbrooke, Sherbrooke, Canada.
‡Dept. of Electrical and Computer Engineering, Universite´ Laval, Que´bec, Canada.

Abstract—The applicability of the Viterbi add-compare-select (ACS) functional block to both convolutional and LDPC codes in various parallel implementations is investigated. To this end, a trellis representation for arbitrary LDPC codes must first be established. Then, a high-level architecture for a Viterbi-algorithmbased unified decoder is proposed. An in-depth exploration of the crucial path metrics (i.e ACS) functional block is then presented, where various locally-connected parallel structures at different speed-area points are explored. Some implementation results are provided, showing that the proposed structures offer high throughput, low latency, and a wide spectrum of speed-area trade-off point, depending on the specific topology that is chosen.
I. INTRODUCTION
Recently, many efficient convolutional and turbo decoder architectures based on the Viterbi algorithm have been extensively investigated by many researchers. However, designing a flexible decoder to support LDPC as well as convolutional codes based on a unified Viterbi algorithm is very challenging. Consequently, in this paper we aim to provide a solution to propose a combined decoder supporting these two types of codes with a small additional overhead based on partially sharing the resources of the Viterbi decoder.
As a matter of fact, a few researchers have tried to treat LDPC codes as turbo codes, based on two convolutional code components, and apply a turbo-like message-passing algorithm to LDPC codes. For example in [1], Mansour and Shanbhag introduced an efficient turbo message passing algorithm for architecture-aware LDPC codes. One year later in [2], Hocevar was able to propose a layered decoding algorithm which treats the parity-check matrix as horizontal layers and passes the soft information between layers to improve the performance. Subsequently, Chakrabarti and Zhu demonstrated the supercode-based LDPC construction and decoding [3]. Furthermore, Zhang and Fossorier studied the shuffled BP algorithm to achieve faster decoding convergence. As well, Lu and Moura sectionalized the Tanner graph into several trees, and then they were able to apply a turbo-like decoding algorithm in each tree to achieve faster convergence.
Indeed, some previous hardware implementations have focused on serial butterfly architectures [4] [5], parallel butterfly architectures [6] and the so-called reconfigurable Adaptive

Viterbi Algorithm (AVA) architecture [7]. Little work has been done on hardware implementations of the systolic array architecture proposed by Chang and Yao [8]. One such implementation is presented by Guo et al, but its focus is on a CMOS lowpower design exploiting an adaptive architecture of the Viterbi decoder [9]. Our paper constitutes a comparative architecture study focusing on the hardware implementation of three different locally-connected (systolic) array architectures initially designed for a massively parallel SIMD (Single Instruction, Multiple Data) computer architecture [10]. Speed and area are the two main parameters of interest when choosing an architecture. The three proposed architectures offer a wide spectrum of area-speed trade-off points [11].
In this paper, we propose an approach to decode the two families of code by means of the Viterbi algorithm. In Section II, the graphical representation of the convolutional codes with the necessary notations and definitions used throughout this paper are introduced. Given that architecturally the Viterbi algorithm is a trellis based algorithm, the regular trellis diagram representation for the H matrix of LDPC codes has been proposed in section III. In Section IV, a brief overview over the well known implementation issues and architectural aspects of convolutional decoder using the Viterbi algorithm has been introduced. Furthermore, a general architecture for a combined decoder of the two mentioned codes is proposed. Finally, the conclusion is given in Section V.
II. CONVOLUTIONAL CODES
First introduced by Elias in 1955 [13], binary convolutional codes are one of the most popular forms of binary error correcting codes, present in numerous applications. They were referred to in Elias as convolutional parity-check symbols codes. Their most popular decoding method to date is the Viterbi algorithm [14].

III. TRELLIS REPRESENTATION OF LDPC CODES
In 1974, Bahl, Coke and Raviv described a technique for constructing trellises for block codes, uncovering an important relation between block codes and convolutional codes [15]. In fact, this work did not present an algorithm, but merely showed that a trellis can be constructed. In 1978, Wolf developed a similar trellis [16], which provides a basis for the maximum likelihood (ML) soft-decision decoding of block codes using the MAP algorithm [15]. Wolf’s method yields a trellis which represents a superset of codewords which must be modified by deletion of branches and states in order to obtain the trellis for the code.
A trellis definition and method for constructing a trellis for any LDPC code, which is conceptually the same as linear block codes, from its parity-check matrix will be presented in the following subsections. Once the trellis has been constructed, the well-known Viterbi algorithm can be applied. The trellis complexity of the decoding algorithm will then be evaluated.
A. Trellis Construction
There are several ways a generic (n, k) linear block code C can be represented by a trellis structure (whereas a binary LDPC code is a linear block code specified by a very sparse binary parity-check matrix). However, the original construction introduced by Bahl et al. [15] yields a trellis which uniquely minimizes both the edge count and the vertex count at each depth. Because of this dual minimal characteristic, the socalled BCJR trellis offers significant advantages over other trellises for the use of the Viterbi or MAP algorithms [12].

IV. HARDWARE IMPLEMENTATION
Figure 3 shows the diagram for a generic architecture. Modules represented with a solid line are common to all three architectures while modules represented with a dotted line are different. The branch metrics processing are done at the symbol level and the processing is slightly different for the convolutional and LDPC codes (due to the different code structures). However, the trace-back unit works on a block of symbols and it is shared for the two codes. Furthermore, once the trellis is obtained for both codes, its the same ACS step performed as a pseudo vector-matrix operation. Consequently, the path metrics unit is a shared resource between the two codes. This section explores three parallel locally-connected topologies of processing elements to implement the path metrics (add-compare select) block, which is where the bulk of the work is performed. Without loss of generality, all figures are for a K = 2 and 4-state Viterbi decoder.

B. Square architecture
While area efficient, the linear architecture is not time efficient. The square architecture utilizes a matrix of N × N PEs to quickly compute the surviving path metrics. Figure 6 shows the diagram for this architecture. It can be seen that besides the higher number of PEs, the main difference lies within a PE itself. The comparator and memory element are switched. This is to ensure that the additions occur in one clock cycle while the comparisons, and thus selection of the surviving path, are done in the next clock cycle. Propagation of the current path metrics pk is also done within the same clock cycle, so no memory element is necessary. To ensure that all branch metrics are received within one clock cycle, N × N branch metric computation PEs are also needed, as shown in Figure 7. The PEs have the same design as those used in the linear architecture. Again, a ROM is used to store the branch weights.

C. Division-Fusion architecture
This third architecture offers a good compromise between the area-efficient linear architecture and the time-efficient square architecture. It is in fact a generalization of the first two methods. By using M groups of N PEs, as shown in Figure 8 for M = 2 and N = 4, this architecture effectively splits the processing into M different linear arrays. When M = 1 we obtain the linear architecture, while when M = N we obtain the square architecture. Extra comparators are needed to select the surviving paths from the intermediary paths computed by the M arrays. Branch metric computation simply consists of using M copies of the module employed for the linear architecture. However, a single ROM can be used for all copies.

Blocks of x stages are decoded sequentially, where x must be longer than a predetermined truncation length δ. Generally,

In a convolutional encoder, the next state is always determined by the input bit. Thus, each link between two states corresponds to a specific input bit. The goal of decoding is to determine each bit associated with each link of the surviving path. In a strongly-connected trellis, each link in a stage corresponds to the transition between multiple states of the original trellis, so more than one input bit are associated with each link. A useful property of convolutional codes and their strongly-connected trellis is that irrespective of the starting state, the input binary code corresponds to the mirror value of the binary representation of the arriving state. Therefore, it is only necessary to output the mirror of every state in the surviving path as stored in the memory buffer. Decoding is then as straightforward as reading the surviving path indexes from the memory buffer and converting them to a serial form.

These synthesis results clearly show the differences, and possible compromises and trade-offs, between the various proposed architectures. The linear decoder has a very small area while the square decoder has a larger area. The divisionfusion decoder sits between the two, with each configuration having a different area. On the other hand, the square decoder has a faster clock speed than the linear decoder. The clock rates for the division-fusion decoders are in the same range as the other two. Latency is another difference between the decoders. While the linear decoder latency is dependent on the number of states in the decoder, which increases exponentially with the constraint length, the square architecture is dependent on the truncation length of the decoder, which only increases linearly with the constraint length. The division-fusion decoder is dependent on both the truncation length and the number of states, as well as the grouping factor. Therefore, it constitutes a compromise between the high latency of the linear architecture and the low latency of the square architecture, which allow a continuous range of trade-off points with varying degrees of parallelism.

VI. CONCLUSION
The Viterbi decoder is a notoriously difficult algorithm to
parallelize because of its high level of dependency between
components. With the strongly-connected trellis concept and
the proposed architectures, it was demonstrated that the Viterbi
decoder can be parallelized to a regular quasi-systolic form
containing mostly local connections. This allows high clock
rates and easy scaling. Furthermore, the proposed architectures
offer a wide spectrum of speed-area trade-off points. It was
also shown that this regular locally-connected form offers no
performance degradation when compared to a widely available
software implementation, while performance degradation for
the Modelsim simulated and on-chip implementations can
be attributed to the limited truncation length and fixed-point
precision.
