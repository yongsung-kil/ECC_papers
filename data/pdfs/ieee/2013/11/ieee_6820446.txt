A QC-LDPC Construction Algorithm for Increasing the Throughput of Layered Decoders

Jianjun Zhang, Mingke Dong, Ye Jin

School of Electronics Engineering and Computer Science,Peking University, Beijing 100876, China {zhangjianjun206, mingke.dong & jinye}@pku.edu.cn

Abstract: Pipelined layered decoder (PLD) of quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes is one of most popular high-throughput decoder in modern communication systems. However, the traditional QC-LDPC codes are not suitable for PLD and lead to memory access conflict problems (MACPs) that reduce the throughput of PLD. Therefore, an efficient layered block progressive edge-growth (EL-BPEG) algorithm is proposed to construct a class of QC-LDPC codes that can avoid MACPs and increase throughput. EL-BPEG appends check nodes, rather than variable nodes, progressively into Tanner Graph with strict concentrated check-node degrees. And it establishes edges according to the latency of the pipelines in PLD; thus, all idle clocks can be eliminated for the PLD with EL-BPEG codes. Result indicates that the throughput of PLD can be improved by 50.2% to 99.2% by using the EL-BPEG codes compared with the traditional QC-LDPC codes. Simulations show that the error performances of EL-BPEG codes are as well as the codes in standards.
Keywords: Quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes; Progressive edge-growth (PEG); Layered decoding; Memory access conflicts
1 Introduction
Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes have been adopted in many modern communications, and the high-throughput LDPC decoders are demanded for these communications. Layered LDPC decoding algorithm is a sequential schedule which convergences twice as fast as the traditional flooding schedule [1]-[2]. Block layered decoding based on Quasi-Cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) is very popular in practical applications [3]-[7]. A (m, n) QC-LDPC code is described by its parity matrix Hm├Śn or the mb├Śnb prototype matrix Hp whose elements are p├Śp square cyclic permutation matrices. m is the number of parity check equations; n is the code length; and p is the size of cyclic permutation matrices. A block row of Hp (p rows of the parity matrix) is treated as a layer, and the relevant check-node processes (CNP) are realized in parallel [3]. And the different layers are always organized in pipeline to increase throughput, and this decoder is named pipelined layered decoder (PLD) in this paper.
However, PLD causes two types of memory access conflict problems (MACPs). The type I of MACP ____________________________________ 978-1-4799-0077-0/13/$31.00 ┬®2013 IEEE

(MACP-I) is raised by the non-uniform check-node degree distribution [4]. The write stage of a layer will conflict with the previous layers when its degree is smaller than that of its previous layers. The type II of MACP (MACP-II) is introduced by the same a-posteriori probability (APP) messages that are needed by the successive layers in common [3]-[7]. The conflict is occurred when a layer needs to read the APP messages that have not been updated by the former layers.
For resolving the MACP-I, some idle clocks must be inserted into the pipeline, and this will decrease the throughput. Yang Sun resolves the MACP-II by inserting enough idle clocks [3]. Massimo rearranges the order of the messages input and output as well as the sequence of layers to reduce the idle clocks as much as possible [4]. Marchand splits the matrix to relax the pipeline conflicts at a cost of parallelism [5]. Bhatt updates messages approximately, and introduced mirror memory to reduce the latency with performance loss [6]. Jie Jin proposes a memory bypassing scheme which de-couples the read and write order of messages to reduce the number of memory access [7]. However, these methods are all limited by the positions of non-null elements in Hp, and they significantly need additional complexity.
In order to solve the MACP-I and MACP-II, this paper proposed a novel progressive edge-growth (PEG) [8] algorithm, referred to as efficient layered block-PEG (EL-BPEG) algorithm, to construct a class of QC-LDPC codes that can avoid the MACPs. Traditional PEG and Block-PEG (BPEG) algorithms [9]-[10] build Tanner graphs with large girth by progressively appending variable nodes and establishing edges between variable nodes and check nodes. However, PEG and BPEG algorithms are not suitable for arranging the positions of non-null elements layer by layer. Nevertheless, EL-BPEG algorithm progressively appends check nodes, rather than variable nodes, to easily arrange the positions of non-null elements between adjacent two layers and avoid MACP-II. It also restricts the check-node degree distribution to resolve MACP-I. Then it is proposed an efficient PLD (EPLD) for the EL-BPEG codes with less complexity. Results show that EPLD can improve the throughput by 50.2% to 99.2%.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews PLD and MACPs in PLD. Section 3 proposes EL-BPEG algorithm. Section 4 presents some results and designs EPLD. Finally, the conclusion is given in Section 5.

3.2 Discussion

The LDPC codes constructed by PEG algorithm have three types of check-node degrees for both regular and irregular codes in many cases. Although the PEG algorithm tends to produce codes with uniform check-node degrees, or codes with two consecutive non-null check-node degrees with its best effort [8], but there is no guarantee about it [9]. The step 2) in EL-BPEG algorithm calculates the concentrated check-node degree distribution and the previous degree is not greater than the later degree. Then in the followed process, the distribution is strictly ensured by the step 3)-h). This step can make PLD avoid MACP-I.

References [4] and [7] propose a scheduling method for decoupling the write and read sequences of uji by SMA in Fig. 2. The schedule finds the optimal layer order and the optimal decoupling order by a searching algorithm. However, the optimal layer order cannot support the solution of MACP-I. Moreover, the optimal layer order is not always sufficiently effective when all the latency of layered decoder occupies two cycles (LP=2) [7]. In fact, the latency is always greater than 4. Thus, it is very difficult to find an optimal layer order without any idle cycle. EL-BPEG algorithm resolves MACP-II based on the real latency LP in the step 3)-f). According to (4), the idle cycles can be reduced to zero as the condition

LP Ōēż (dci - ╬▓i) = ai

(14)

is satisfied [4].

The error performance of LDPC codes is related with the variable-node degree distribution. The optimal variable-node degree distribution can be confirmed by the DE [11] and EXIT [13] chart. The step 3)-d) of EL-BPEG algorithm establishes edges based on the smallest current degree ratio (rj). Because rj is not greater than one, this step can ensure the degrees of variable nodes be the same with the preset degree distribution finally. The error performance is also related with the girth. The step 3)-c) can make the girth of prototype matrix as large as possible [10]. Moreover, the step 3)-e) can ensure that the girth is larger than 4 [9]-[10]. Thus, EL-BPEG algorithm can obtain the QC-LDPC codes without loss of error performance.

5 Conclusions

This paper analyzed MACP-I and MACP-II in PLD firstly. Then it is proposed that the EL-BPEG algorithm to resolve these MACPs. EL-BPEG algorithm appends check nodes progressively rather than variable nodes. EL-BPEG algorithm can ensure the error performance by girth constraint and the variable-node degree distribution constraint. It avoids the MACP-I by

designing the concentrated check-node degree distribution strictly. It also resolves the MACP-II by limiting the positions of non-null elements of adjacent layers. The EL-BPEG codes are suitable for the efficient layered decoder without the MACPs and performance loss. The throughput of EPLD with EL-BPEG codes is improved by 22.5% to 45.7% for LP =2, and 50.2% to 99.2% for LP=5 compared with that of PLD.
