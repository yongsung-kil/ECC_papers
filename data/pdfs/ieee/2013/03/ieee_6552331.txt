I. 
INTRODUCTION 
Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes are linear block 
codes that provide near-capacity performance for many 
communication channel models [I]. LOPC codes have been a 
major focus of communications research and have been 
adopted in several digital communication standards, such as 
WiMAX (IEEE 802.16) [2], Oigital Video Broadcast (OVB-S2) 
satellite communications [3], I OGBase-T (IEEE 802.3an) [4], 
and ITU G.975.1 [5]. 
An LDPC code can be described by its parity-check matrix, 
H, that has a low density of non-zero elements. In [6], Tanner 
introduced an effective graphical representation for LOPC 
codes: a bipartite graph now known as a Tanner graph. The 
Tanner graph consists of two distinct groups of nodes: variable 
nodes and check nodes. There are edges connecting nodes, as 
per the H matrix's parity-check equations. For a code rate 
Rc = kin code, the Tanner graph contains n variable nodes 
that represent codeword bits and n - k check nodes that 
represent the code's parity-check equations. 
The most well-known iterative decoding method for LOPC 
codes is the Sum-Product Algorithm (SPA). The SPA 
iteratively computes a posteriori probability messages that are 
passed between the nodes in the Tanner graph. The 
performance of LDPC codes and the accuracy of iterative 
decoding methods improve as the codeword length n gets 
longer. However, when implementing an LDPC decoder, a lot 
of chip area is required as n increases, especially for the 
parallel implementations that are demanded to achieve multi­
Gb/s speeds required by emerging standards. The complexity 
of the interconnection network between nodes also becomes 
worse as n increases. Routing congestion leads to high chip 
area and low logic utilization. 
In 2003, Gaudet and Rapley demonstrated that stochastic 
computation can be used to efficiently implement iterative 
decoding over factor graphs [7]. Later Tehrani et al. refined the 
algorithm [8] for use in large, practical codes. Messages in 
stochastic decoders are represented by streams of randomly 
generated bits called Bernoulli sequences. The complex 
operations in conventional iterative decoding algorithms (e.g., 
SPA) can be simplified to bitwise operations using simple logic 
arrangements [7]. In [8], Tehrani et al. introduced the concept 
of Noise-Dependent Scaling (NDS), a critical aspect of 
stochastic decoding that made the concept feasible for use with 
large LDPC codes running over the Additive White Gaussian 
Noise (A WGN) channel. 
The Binary Symmetric Channel (BSC) is a common 
communications channel used in coding theory, and it is a 
prime example of a channel over which the performance of 
iterative decoding algorithms can be relatively easy to analyze. 
A BSC transmits only one of two symbols, 0 or I. The 
transmission is not perfect, and the receiver may receive the 
wrong bit. The probability of transmission error is denoted as 
the crossover probability E. To our understanding, there are no 
results in the literature relating to stochastic decoding over the 
BSC. In this paper, we extend results by Tehrani et al. by 
presenting a scaling method for stochastic LDPC decoders over 
the BSC. We investigate the use of a scaling factor, and 
determine that its optimal value is nearly constant over a wide 
range of crossover probabilities in the BSC. This outcome 
simplifies the conversion of received messages into the 
stochastic streams. We also compare the results with NDS, 
which was proposed for the A WGN channel model to 
introduce more switching activities in stochastic decoding [8]. 
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. In Section II, 
we describe stochastic decoding of LOPC codes. A scaling 
method for stochastic decoding over the BSC model is 
provided in Section III. Simulation results are given in Section 
IV, where we study the performance of a (1056, 528) LOPC

II. 
STOCHASTIC DECODING 
In stochastic decoders, messages along the edges between 
variable nodes and check nodes are represented by stochastic 
streams. Each bit of these stochastic streams is randomly 
generated with a probability of being equal to 1 of the 
probability to be encoded by the stream's message. A sequence 
of N bits of which m bits are equal to 1 represents a probability 
value of miN. For instance, a length 10 sequence with eight 1 s 
represents a probability of 0.8. The relationship between a 
probability value and a stochastic stream is not unique: since 
stochastic streams are randomly generated, different stochastic 
streams are possible to represent a given probability. 
Among others, stochastic computation has been used to 
decode: a (16,8) LDPC code [7], a (1024, 512) LDPC code [9], 
the (1056, 528) LOPC code in WiMAX standard [10], and the 
(2048, 1723) LDPC code in 100Base-T standard [11]. One of 
the advantages of stochastic decoding is its inherently low 
hardware utilization. At each time step, there is only one bit 
being computed for and transmitted over each edge. With this 
process, stochastic decoding reduces the number of physical 
wires required to exchange messages, and therefore alleviates 
routing congestion for longer codes.

Each decoding round in stochastic decoding is called a 
Decoding Cycle (DC). A DC includes the exchange of one bit 
for each graphical edge. One DC does not directly correspond 
to one iteration in the SPA [8]. The decoding process in 
stochastic decoders operates as follows. 
Step 1. Initialization. The received LLRs at each variable node 
are converted to the corresponding probabilities. Then, 
each probability, Pbis transformed to the corresponding 
stochastic streams by employing a random number 
generator and comparator [10]. Each Pi is fixed during 
the decoding operation and is compared with a random 
number R ,  which 0 :s: R :s: 1 and changes at every 
decoding cycle. The output bit of the comparator is 1 
when Pi > R, and 0 otherwise. 
Step 2. Variable to Check. In each decoding cycle, a variable 
node receives one bit from the channel, and the extrinsic 
bits from the check nodes. Variable node Vi propagates 
its outgoing I-bit message to the check node Cj based on 
the stochastic bit from the channel and the extrinsic bits 
received from all the other edges. 
Step 3. Check to Variable. Check nodes check the parities and 
send their I-bit messages back to the neighbors at each 
decoding cycle. The message sent from the check node 
Cj to the variable node Vi on the edge e is the XOR 
production of all messages received at node Cj on edges 
other than the edge e. 
Step 4. Codeword Test. Based on all the extrinsic bits from the 
check nodes and the stochastic bit received from the 
channel, the output of each variable node at the end of a 
decoding cycle is passed to an up/down counter to 
determine the codeword bit. 
After completing this exchange of bits between variable nodes 
and check nodes, variable nodes load in the next bit from the 
stochastic streams and start the next decoding cycle. The 
process is terminated when the maximum number of decoding 
cycles is exceeded or all the check nodes are satisfied.

of random switching activity, there is the possibility that the

Noise-Dependent Scaling (NDS) 
The main purpose of NOS is to increase the switching activity 
in stochastic streams. With NOS, received LLRs are scaled by 
a factor proportional to the noise power. NOS ensures a similar 
level of switching activity for different Signal-to-Noise Ratio 
(SNR) values. Furthermore, scaled LLRs are independent of 
channel noise and the decoder does not need to estimate the 
noise level of the channel. The down-scaled LLR results in that 
the probability, Pi, moves toward 0.5, which introduces more 
switching activity for the corresponding stochastic stream.

V. 
CONCLUSIONS 
We propose a scaling method on the probabilities which are 
used for transforming the received messages to stochastic 
streams in LOPC decoders. The scaled probability is a constant 
for different SNR values and works for any binary channel 
model. It can be applied to continuous-output channel models 
by making hard-decisions before transforming to stochastic 
streams. Compared to the conventional SPA, the proposed 
stochastic decoding method shows comparable performance. In 
addition to requiring less hardware complexity, the scaling 
method also provides lower error floor for stochastic decoding.
