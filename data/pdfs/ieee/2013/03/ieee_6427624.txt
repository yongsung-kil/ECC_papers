Two Informed Dynamic Scheduling Strategies for Iterative LDPC Decoders
Huang-Chang Lee, Yeong-Luh Ueng, Shan-Ming Yeh, and Wen-Yen Weng

Abstract—When residual belief-propagation (RBP), which is a kind of informed dynamic scheduling (IDS), is applied to lowdensity parity-check (LDPC) codes, the convergence speed in error-rate performance can be significantly improved. However, the RBP decoders presented in previous literature suffer from poor convergence error-rate performance due to the two phenomena explored in this paper. The first is the greedy-group phenomenon, which results in a small part of the decoding graph occupying most of the decoding resources. By limiting the number of updates for each edge message in the decoding graph, the proposed Quota-based RBP (Q-RBP) schedule can reduce the probability of greedy groups forming. The other phenomenon is the silent-variable-nodes issue, which is a condition where some variable nodes have no chance of contributing their intrinsic messages to the decoding process. As a result, we propose the Silent-Variable-Node-Free RBP (SVNF-RBP) schedule, which can force all variable nodes to contribute their intrinsic messages to the decoding process equally. Both the Q-RBP and the SVNFRBP provide appealing convergence speed and convergence error-rate performance compared to previous IDS decoders for both dedicated and punctured LDPC codes.
Index Terms—Belief propagation, informed dynamic schedule, error-control codes, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, punctured LDPC codes, residual belief propagation.
I. INTRODUCTION
R ECENTLY, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] have received increasing attention for their near capacity performance. When a belief-propagation (BP) algorithm is applied to the bipartite graph of an LDPC code, the decoding messages are iteratively exchanged between variable and check nodes. Flooding, which is also called two-phase message passing, is the most straightforward scheduling method, where all variable-to-check (V2C) messages are updated in the first phase, and then all check-to-variable (C2V) messages are updated in the second phase. If the updates can be sequentially scheduled such that information generated in the current iteration can be used to generate extrinsic messages in the same iteration, the convergence speed in error performance can be increased, even though the sequence of updates is

Even though the convergence speed of SSS is twice as fast as that of flooding, the convergence speed can be further increased if the decoding schedules are not predetermined and not fixed. This observation has lead to the investigation of the informed dynamic scheduling (IDS) strategies presented in [9] and [10]. The authors in [9] and [10] applied the residual belief propagation (RBP) algorithm presented in [8] to LDPC decoding, where the edge message with the maximum C2V residual is updated first. Although the error rate for RBP decreases very fast for the first few iterations, its convergence error-rate performance is worse than that of both flooding and SSS. In order to achieve a superior convergence performance, Nodewise RBP (NW-RBP), which not only updates the messages with the maximum C2V residual, but also all the messages generated by the same check node, was also proposed in [9] and [10].
Other methods, including Variable-to-Check RBP (VCRBP) and Informed Variable-to-Check RBP (IVC-RBP) were presented in [11], [12] and [13], respectively. Although these IDS decoders have attractive convergence error-rate performance, their convergence speed can not surpass that of RBP. In this paper, two related phenomena that explain why RBP suffers from poor convergence error-rate performance are observed, and two new dynamic scheduling strategies are proposed based on these observations. Both strategies not only retain the fast-convergence advantage of RBP, but also have a compatible convergence error-rate performance compared to that of NW-RBP and SSS, especially for codes having obvious trapping sets.
The first phenomenon observed in RBP is called the greedy group. RBP may instruct the decoder to update a small group of the edge messages in the code graph a large number of times, and ignore any update of other edge messages which may correct the bits in error. The update-hungry element is called the greedy group. Based on this observation, the proposed Quota-based RBP (Q-RBP) schedule limits the number of updates made to each edge message, and forces the messages that potentially form a greedy group to release the occupied computational resources to other edge messages. As the errors outside the greedy group can be corrected, Q-RBP achieves a much better convergence error-rate performance than RBP, but retains the same convergence speed as RBP.
The other phenomenon observed in RBP is called silent variable nodes, where certain variable nodes have no chance of contributing their intrinsic messages to the decoding process.

The value of U (S, IM ) not only depends on S and IM , but also on whether the decoded codeword is correct or not. When the decoded codeword is correct, the U (S, IM ) value will be low with a high probability, since the decoding process can be terminated early. However, when the decoded codeword is incorrect, the decoding process continues until the maximum number of iterations is reached, and the value of U (S, IM ) can be high. Based on our experiments, when the decoded codewords are correct, RBP and NWRBP have similar U (S, IM ) distributions. However, when the decoded codewords are incorrect, RBP frequently has a higher U (S, IM ) value than that of NW-RBP decoders. The higher U (S, IM ) value implies that RBP concentrates its decoding resources on a small part of the code graph. According to our experiments, when a received codeword cannot be correctly decoded using RBP, in most cases, there will be a small group of edges that have been repeatedly updated. This small group that occupies most of the decoding resources is defined as the greedy group.

The loops, or cycles, in the code graph do not necessarily create greedy groups. For example, greedy groups are not observed in flooding and SSS decoders, since all the messages have an equal chance of being updated. NW-RBP reduces the probability of the occurrence of a greedy group by updating all messages generated by the node that is connected to the one with the maximum residual. As a result, nodes outside the loop also generate new messages and new residuals, and the probability of the occurrence of a greedy group is lower. However, except for messages with the maximum residual, it cannot be guaranteed that other messages updated by NWRBP are also important. In other words, NW-RBP may update many messages that provide little contribution to the decoding performance. This observation has motivated us to devise better solutions for preventing the decoder from forming a greedy group in addition to maintaining the convergence speed of RBP.

According to (2), the C2V messages generated by these two check nodes, i.e., mc1→v1 and mc2→v3 contain the intrinsic information of v2. Obviously, it is impossible for an iterative LDPC decoder to achieve Maximum-Likelihood performance when some variable nodes provide no intrinsic message. Unfortunately, silent variable nodes can be found in many IDS decoders, such as RBP and NW-RBP. Fig. 3 shows the percentage of silent variable nodes in these IDS decoders, where the results for correctly-decoded and incorrectly-decoded codewords are shown individually.
For the incorrectly-decoded codewords, the percentage of silent variable nodes is the highest when using RBP, and stops decreasing after the first few iterations. In contrast, the percentage in both NW-RBP and Q-RBP continuously decreases as the number of iterations increases. This implies that both NW-RBP and Q-RBP can enable more and more variable nodes to contribute their intrinsic messages. The trend of these curves can be closely related to the error-rate performances, where the error rate cannot be decreased by using more RBP iterations, but can be continuously improved using NW-RBP and Q-RBP.
For the correctly-decoded codewords, the percentage of silent variable nodes when using either RBP or Q-RBP decreases faster than for NW-RBP. This result can be related to their convergence speed, where both RBP and Q-RBP can quickly achieve a lower error rate than NW-RBP for the first few iterations. If the percentage of silent variable nodes can be quickly reduced, and can finally enable all variable nodes to contribute their intrinsic messages, it is possible that a higher convergence speed and a better convergence errorrate performance can be simultaneously achieved. Although neither flooding nor SSS decoders suffer from silent variable nodes, their error-rate performance cannot surpass that of IDS decoders. Therefore, the update schedule for the messages is still important. Following these observations, a new IDS decoder, called the Silent-Variable-Node-Free RBP (SVNFRBP), has been implemented. SVNF-RBP can not only prevent the occurrence of silent variable nodes, but can also force the important messages to be updated first based on their residual values.

For time-varying channels, codes with different rates are desired. Instead of implementing many dedicated codes with different rates, punctured LDPC codes, which can provide a variety of rates using the same mother code, are attractive. In [14] and [15], the distributions of punctured bits for an infinite code length were presented, and showed that the convergence error-rate performance of punctured LDPC codes can be compatible to that of dedicated LDPC codes. With additional consideration for finite code lengths, many puncturing patterns have been proposed for practical LDPC codes [17]-[20].
The majority of the previous literature shows that punctured LDPC codes generally require a greater number of iterations to achieve a similar convergence error-rate performance compared to dedicated codes [16]-[18]. This result was introduced based on flooding or SSS decoders. In this section, we will show that the use of the proposed IDS decoders can reduce the exchanges of zero-value messages propagated from punctured nodes, and, hence, increase the convergence speed. In addition, we will demonstrate that punctured LDPC codes using the proposed IDS decoders can provide a higher convergence speed and a better convergence error-rate performance compared to dedicated LDPC codes using LBP decoders.

VII. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we have presented two innovative strategies, Q-RBP and SVNF-RBP, based on the observations of the greedy group and the silent variable node phenomena, respectively. Both of the proposed strategies can achieve a much better convergence error-rate performance compared to RBP without sacrificing convergence speed.
