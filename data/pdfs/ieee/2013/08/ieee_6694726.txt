2013 8th International Conference on Communications and Networking in China (CHINACOM)
Construction of Quasi-Cyclic LDPC Codes based on Euclidean Geometries*

Yuan-hua Liu, Mei-ling Zhang
School of Telecommunication and Information Engineering Xi’an University of Posts and Telecommunications Xi’an, China E-mail: yuanhliu@163.com

Abstract—A new method for constructing quasi-cyclic (QC) lowdensity parity-check (LDPC) codes based on the structural properties of Euclidean geometries is presented. The proposed method changes the incidence vectors of some lines in the original Euclidean geometry method to construct much more parity check matrices and results in a large class of QC-LDPC codes with girth of at least 6. Simulations show that some codes in this class have better performance and lower error floor than the original Euclidean geometry codes.
Keywords- Low-density parity-check codes; quasi-cyclic; Euclidean geometries
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have recently attracted much attention due to their capacity-approaching performance and low decoding complexity. LDPC codes decoded with iterative soft-input and soft-output decoding algorithm, such as sum-product algorithm (SPA), perform amazingly well. Long LDPC codes perform even a few thousandths of a decibel away from their theoretical Shannon limit[1].
As an attractive class of LDPC codes, quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes are well suitable for certain practical applications. They can easily be encoded using simple shift-registers with linear complexity. In addition, for practical lengths, it has been shown [2-8] that well-designed QC-LDPC codes can perform equally as well as random-like LDPC codes in terms of biterror probability, block-error probability, error floor, and rate of decoding convergence, correctively.
In this paper, we present a new method based on the structural properties of Euclidean geometries for constructing QC-LDPC codes with girth of at least 6. The proposed method changes the incidence vectors of some lines in the original Euclidean geometry method to produce a lot of new parity check matrices without 4-cycles. The distributions of cycles in the Tanner graphs of these new codes and the original code are the same. Some new codes have better performance and lower error floor than the original codes. In this paper, we apply the proposed method to the existing Euclidean geometry construction method presented in [7]. However, this new method can easily be extended to other LDPC construction methods which are based on points and lines in Euclidean

geometries.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. Section II presents a brief description of Euclidean geometries and their structural properties. A new method for constructing QCLDPC codes based on Euclidean geometries is proposed in Section III and the distributions of cycles in the Tanner graphs of these codes are analyzed. Simulation results of several original Euclidean geometry QC-LDPC codes and the new codes designed by the proposed method are compared in Section IV. Finally, Section V concludes the paper.

II. EUCLIDEAN GEOMETRIES
Let EG(m, ps ) be an m -dimensional Euclidean geometry over Galois field GF( ps ) , where p is a prime and m and s
are two positive integers [2]. This geometry consists of points and lines. A point is an m -tuple over GF( ps ) and hence there are pms points in EG(m, ps ) . The all-zero m -tuple is called the origin of the geometry. These pms points form an m dimensional vector space over GF( ps ) . Therefore, EG(m, ps ) is simply the m -dimensional vector space of all the m -tuples over GF( ps ) . A line in EG(m, ps ) is either a one-dimensional subspace of EG(m, ps ) or a coset of a onedimensional subspace. Therefore, a line in EG(m, ps ) consists of ps points and there are p(m−1)s ( pms −1) ( ps −1) lines in EG(m, ps ) .
An m -dimensional Euclidean geometry EG(m, ps ) has the following structural properties: 1) every line consists of ps
points; 2) any two lines are either parallel (i.e., they have no
point in common) or they intersect at one and only one point.

V. CONCLUSION
An algebraic method for the design of QC-LDPC codes based on the structural properties of Euclidean geometries has been proposed. The proposed method changes the incidence vectors of some lines in the original Euclidean geometry method to construct much more parity check matrices and results in a large class of QC-LDPC codes with girth of at least 6. The distributions of cycles in the Tanner graphs of these new codes and the original code are the same. Some codes in this class have better performance and lower error floor than the original codes.
