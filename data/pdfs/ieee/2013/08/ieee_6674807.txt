High Throughput Architecture for Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) Encoder

Silvia Anggraeni, Fawnizu Azmadi Hussin and Varun Jeoti
Electrical and Electronics Engineering Department Universiti Teknologi PETRONAS
Bandar Seri Iskandar, 31750 Tronoh, Perak, Malaysia silvia31804@yahoo.com, fawnizu@petronas.com.my and varun_jeoti@petronas.com.my

Abstract—This paper proposes a bit-wise matrix-vector multiplication in the optimization of a proposed low density parity check (LDPC) encoder. Investigation of this proposed architecture is done by implementing five code lengths using one IEEE 802.16e standard code rate. It is shown that the proposed architecture outperforms other works in terms of information throughput ranging from 0.235 to 8.83 times higher. In term of ratio of throughput per area, the proposed method exceeds other works in the range of 1.19 to 6.54 times higher.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low density parity check (LDPC) codes are considered as advanced codes for error correcting in the current communication and magnetic storage systems since they have an outstanding capability of error correcting and performance close to Shannon limit. An example of communication system which applied LDPC codes as an optional coding is IEEE 802.16e worldwide interoperability for microwave access (WiMAX) [1].
The standard complexity of encoding in LDPC codes takes time proportional to quadratic of the code length (N²) with a small constant factor [2]. In order to reduce the complexity of encoding, many communication systems apply structured LDPC codes like dual-diagonal LDPC codes in 802.16e standard, 802.11n standard [3] and the second generation of digital video broadcasting (DVB-S2) [4].
In this paper, we present architecture for LDPC encoder which can be applied for any code rate and code length. The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section II describes the proposed encoding process while section III illustrates the proposed encoder architecture. Section IV shows the implementation results and discussion and the last section is the conclusion.
II. ENCODING
A. Encoding Method
The purpose of encoding is to generate redundant or parity bits which are used to correct errors that occur in the channel. Our encoding utilizes the sparseness of parity check matrix (H) which is based on Neal method [2]. B. Proposed Bit-wise Matrix-Vector Multiplication Scheme
Our encoding applied in the proposed architecture is based on (5). Arithmetic operation utilized in (5) is a bit-wise multiplication since the sub-matrix P and the information bits s in our proposed architecture are binary bits. In order to simplify the binary multiplication in our architecture, we propose bit-wise matrix-vector multiplication (MVM) scheme as given below.

Proposed bit-wise MVM scheme: Step 1. Let the sub-matrix P and the vector s be binary matrix. Step 2. Represent nonzero of the sub-matrix P by column
location. The column location in this step will be used by the information bits s. The result is stored in P'. Step 3. Replace the nonzero in step 2 by values in the s and save the result in P''. Step 4. Do XOR operation in each row of P'' to get the parity bits p.

III. PROPOSED ENCODER ARCHITECTURE
The proposed parity generator is shown in Fig. 4 while Fig. 5 captures internal architecture of each part of parity block in Fig. 4. Both figures have labels of K/Div, p/Divp, t and t1 which indicate the lengths of bits used in the design. In this architecture, we apply encoding according to (5) and the proposed bit-wise MVM at section II is located in Col Part block of Fig. 5.
In the proposed architecture, we need to define division of the information bits s denoted by Div and division of the parity bits denoted by Divp. Div and Divp are integers. The lengths of t and t1 for the select mode of two multiplexers are defined by the value of Divp for multiplexer and the value of Div for multiplexer 1.
The information bits of K/Div are sent to each PART of PARITY block of Fig. 4 and sent to the Col Part block of Fig. 5 to do the proposed bit-wise MVM. Then, the outcomes of MVM are XOR-ed inside the Col Part block. The results of this operation are p/Divp bits of parity which will be saved in registers and will be sent to the multiplexer 1 of Fig. 5.
The multiplexer 1 selects which parity bits that will be compiled in the second XOR operation. After all parity bits are collected in this XOR block, the latest parity bits will be sent to the multiplexer of Fig. 4. The multiplexer selects Divp choices of the parity bits and determines the parity bits which will be generated in the architecture.

The information bits will be padded with the parity bits to produce the codeword c along with generation of the parity bits p. Therefore, there is no additional cycle for generating the codeword.
The number of clock cycles required in the proposed architecture is determined by the value of Div and Divp. If Div = 2 then the first part of parity bits are generated after 6 cycles with additional of 1 cycle for reset purpose. Each value of Div requires 3 cycles.
Meanwhile, if Divp = 2 then the first part of parity bits is produced within 7 cycles as explained above. The second part of parity bits is available 2 cycles after that. It means that the total cycles required for generating the parity bits p are 9 cycles.

IV. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
In this paper, code length (N) is the same as the codeword c. The same portion of length between information and parity bits is denoted by code rate R = 1/2. In order to investigate our proposed architecture, we have implemented IEEE 802.16e standard in the proposed encoder for code rate R = 1/2 and code length ranging from N = 576 to N = 2304.
Since we use Altera Stratix EP1S80F1508C5 device, we compare with works of [5], [6] and [7] which utilize the same device of field programmable gate array (FPGA). The comparison between these works is shown in Table I.
If we increase the value of Div and Divp in the proposed method, the cycles required to generate the codeword c will be increased. Therefore, the proposed architecture maintains the Div = 2 and the Divp = 2 for all code lengths which generate 9 cycles of the parity bits p.
Information throughput (IT) in Table I is achieved by calculating clock frequency × R × N ÷ CPC where CPC is clock cycles per codeword.
Derived from Table I, the clock frequency and logic element of the proposed architecture are in the middle between the highest and the lowest. The lowest clock frequency is the work of [7] while the lowest logic element is the work of [6].
The CPC of the proposed architecture is the same as [7] while the CPC of [5] and [6] are higher as the code length becomes longer.

The values of information throughput (IT) and the ratio between throughput/area are the highest for the proposed architecture. The values of IT increase as the code length becomes longer. The ratio of throughput/area is above 2 for all code lengths.
The percentage difference of IT and the ratio of IT/LE between the proposed architecture with other previous works are also shown in Table 1. We set the other works as the reference and we calculate the percentage difference between each IT and IT/area of other works with the proposed one.
It can be seen that the proposed method surpasses IT of other works ranging from 0.235 to 8.83 times higher since our method maintains 9-cycles for delay between input frames.
Since the arithmetic operation in hardware is XOR with three multiplexers, the clock frequency and the LE of the proposed architecture is in the middle position. The proposed method of the ratio of IT/area exceeds other works in the range of 1.19 to 6.54 times higher since our ITs are the highest and our LEs are in the middle position for all code lengths.
V. CONCLUSION
We have presented the proposed bit-wise MVM scheme and the proposed architecture for LDPC encoder. The clock cycle per codeword value for our architecture is the same for all code lengths.
