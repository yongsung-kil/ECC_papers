An Early Termination Strategy for Irregular LDPC Codes with Layered Decoding - Performance Evaluation and Implementation

Miroslav Marinkovic, Eckhard Grass, Milos Krstic
IHP, Im Technologiepark 25, D-15236 Frankfurt (Oder), Germany
marinkovic, grass, krstic@ihp-microelectronics.com

Abstract - Much attention has been paid to layered structures in the design of LDPC decoders. They promise fast convergence speed as well as reduced memory requirements. However, a correct stopping criterion (Hc checking) is difficult to implement in layered decoders, which results in unnecessary iterations. In this paper, we evaluate the error-rate performance of an hardware efficient early termination (ET) strategy under the normalized min-sum (NMS) algorithm and its improved versions. The irregular LDPC codes from IEEE 802.11n (WiFi) and IEEE 802.16e (WiMax) standards have been used for our investigation. From simulation results, it is shown that the conventional NMS algorithm with one optimal correction factor causes relatively high error floor. Therefore, we propose an improvement of the NMS algorithm by utilizing properties of degree-2 variable nodes. That improvement significantly lowers the error floor of the irregular LDPC codes with the ET strategy at high channel SNR values, which is confirmed by simulations. Finally, we employ the ET strategy in a WiMax decoder to reduce power consumption (around 50 % at a FER = 10-4).
Index Terms - LDPC codes, early-termination (ET) strategy, layered LDPC decoder, normalized min-sum (NMS) algorithm
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have generated much research interest because of their outstanding error correction performance and large degree of parallelism of the decoding algorithm. Many practical applications such as IEEE 802.16e (WiMax) [1] and IEEE 802.11n (WiFi) [2] have adopted irregular LDPC codes due to their better performance in comparison to regular ones.
The sum-product (SP) algorithm and the min-sum (MS) algorithm are two well-known iterative decoding algorithms for LDPC codes. Although the MS algorithm is suboptimal in terms of decoding performance, it has a few advantages over the SP algorithm such as lower hardware complexity and less sensitivity of decoding performance under finite word-length implementations. Moreover, the modified MS algorithms such as normalized MS or offset MS [3], [4] using additional correction factors have been preferred for many practical applications even though they exhibit small performance degradation over the SP algorithm.
Both the SP and the MS algorithms were originally formulated in the form of a two-phase message passing scheme. Recently, another message passing scheme known as layered decoding [5] or turbo-decoding message passing scheme [6] has been proposed. Its main advantages over the standard two-phase message passing are faster convergence and reduced memory requirements.

In order to detect that a correct codeword is found, an early termination strategy can be used. Otherwise, the iterative decoding process will not stop until the maximum number of iterations is reached which causes unnecessary calculations. From a hardware implementation point of view, those calculations lead to decreased decoder throughput and increased power consumption. Therefore, to address this problem, the implementation of an efficient ET strategy is necessary.
The common and mathematically exact method of ET is Hc checking. This means we stop iterative decoding precisely without introducing any loss of error-rate performance by checking whether all parity-check equations are satisfied. However, in the layered decoder (especially for larger LDPC codes) implementation of Hc checking is inefficient because of high hardware complexity.
Another popular ET strategy is known as hard-decision aided (HDA) criterion which was originally invented for Turbo codes [7] and applied in the chip implementation of a WiMax decoder in [8]. For the HDA criterion, if there is no difference between the decoded message words produced at any two consecutive iterations, the decoding process is terminated. However, there is no guaranty that a correct code word is found. In other words, misjudgments can occur producing the error floor at higher signal-to-noise-ratio (SNR). To mitigate this effect, an additional threshold for magnitudes of a-posteriori probability (APP) values is added, leading to an error-rate performance improvement [9]. The price paid due to introducing this additional constraint is a higher hardware complexity as well as an increase of the average decoding iteration number.
In this paper, we follow a different idea in order to improve error-rate performance of early-terminated layered LDPC decoders. We combine the strength of an improved NMS algorithm with an ET strategy based on the HDA criterion adapted to layered decoding. The ET strategy considered in this paper takes into account an essential feature of layered decoding: each iteration is a concatenation of several layer elaborations or sub-iterations. It is based on the sign comparison between the updated APP values and the current APP after processing each layer. Moreover, this ET strategy in layered decoders (we will use the name s-APP ET strategy) offers a simple method to tradeoff the number of decoding subiterations for the error-rate performance. A similar approach has been recently used in [10] for the implementation of a layered decoder for a regular LDPC code in China Multimedia Mobile Broadcasting (CMMB) standard. II. LAYERED MIN-SUM DECODING WITH EARLY TERMINATION STRATEGY
In contrast to conventional two-phase decoding scheduling, layered decoding follows a different scheduling scheme where subsets of check nodes are updated sequentially and passed over to the variable nodes immediately. The layered algorithm assumes that the rows of the parity-check matrix H are grouped into L layers.
As we have already mentioned, in the proposed s-APP ET strategy, we compare the signs of the updated APP messages with the current ones and check whether the signs are changed. The ET strategy requires that L* consecutive layers satisfy the previous constraint. Then, the decoder stops and delivers the obtained codeword. The parameter L* needs to be at least L (total number of layers of parity-check matrix) in order to elaborate all variable nodes. Let Qmn denote the variable node Log-Likelihood Ratio (LLR) message sent from variable node n to the check node m. Rmn denotes the check node LLR message sent from the check node m to the variable node n, and APPn denotes a-posteriori probability ratio (APP) for variable node n. III. IMPROVED MIN-SUM ALGORITHM

III-A. I-NMS algorithm
The causes of the performance loss shown in Fig. 1 have been analyzed. We have found by simulations that the NMS algorithm with the s-APP ET strategy often delivers LDPC blocks with only 1, 2 or 3 bit errors due to the false detection of the codeword. In such cases the decoding process is getting stuck in some kind of a steady-state, where no sign changes of the APP messages are detected, but the probability of the codeword misdetection is still relatively high. We want to break such steady states by attenuating the check-node messages entering low-degree variable nodes less than those entering high-degree variable nodes. We have found by simulations that a significant performance gain can be achieved using two normalization factors (depending on the variable node degree (VN_degree)) in equation (2). A similar idea was proposed in [14]. They investigated the error-floor lowering of irregular LDPC codes with floating-point precision and the optimal parity-check (Hc) ET strategy. As a general guideline, it was suggested to use three normalization factors (s1 = 1.0 for messages to variable nodes of degree-2, s2 = 0.5 for messages to variable nodes of degree-20, and s3 = 0.77 for all other messages). Here, we preserve the normalization factor s1 = 1.0, but we use another optimal normalization factor (depending on the code structure) for all other messages and apply this method to irregular LDPC code with the s-APP ET strategy. For example, for the LDPC (1944, 972) code, we found by simulations that the optimal combination is s1 = 1.0 and s2 = 0.8125. Therefore, the equation (2) can be reformulated:

III-B. 2-NMS algorithm
In [13], the authors proposed an improved NMS algorithm (we will use the name 2-NMS algorithm) using two different normalization factors applied to the minimum value and the second minimum value of Qmn messages. The optimal

normalization factors were found in [13] for the LDPC (2304, 1152) and the LDPC (1944, 972) codes taken from WiMax and WiFi standards, respectively. Those two normalization factors are set to be s1 = 0.75 (for the minimum message) and s2 = 0.875 (for the second minimum message) for both LDPC codes. In addition to the I-NMS, in the next chapter we will evaluate the performance of the 2-NMS algorithm for layered decoding with the s-APP ET strategy.

V. IMPLEMENTATION OVERHEAD AND POWER SAVING
We have designed a layered LDPC decoder of 2304-bit length with the s-APP ET strategy for the WiMax standard. Then, the decoder has been synthesized using a STMicroelectronics 40nm CMOS standard cell library. The overall architecture of the decoder is shown in Fig. 7. Since each layer of the parity-check matrix consists of 96 rows and with one processing unit per row, the same number of layered processing units is employed working in parallel. In addition, the changes in the sign bit of the APP messages are monitored continuously in the ET unit.
In order to support pipeline processing, the APP and check node messages are stored in two dual-port memory blocks denoted as APP and CHECK data, respectively. We employ special DPREG (dual-port register file) memories which are very efficient in terms of area and power and can operate at higher clock frequencies compared to a traditional dual-port memory.

Figure 5. Comparison of decoding performances of a length-1944 irregular LDPC code, rate-3/4
The presented simulation results are related to the longest LDPC codes from WiMax and WiFi standards. However, we have also simulated some shorter codes; results obtained are very similar pointing to a significant performance improvement achieved by the I-NMS algorithm.

Figure 8. ET unit block diagram
The block diagram of the ET unit is shown in Fig. 8. It consists of one DPREG memory, one 96-bit comparator and the ET controller. Once the termination criterion is met, the decoder is informed to terminate further processing and outputs the hard-decision values of the APP messages. Otherwise, the decoding operations would be performed iteratively until the maximum number of iterations is reached.
The decoder chip area obtained after synthesis is 0.53 mm2. The hardware overhead of the ET unit is 2.7 % of the entire design, which is very favorable. Moreover the ET strategy applied to the decoder design provides a good potential for decoder power reduction. At higher SNR regions, the decoding usually needs fewer iteration to converge than the maximum. We have estimated the power consumption of our decoder using the power analysis tool (Synopsys Prime Time PX) at Eb/N0 = 2.1 dB, where the error correction performances are FER = 10-4 and BER = 10-6. The average number of iterations is around 6 which is much smaller than the maximum number of decoding iterations (15) used for the performance evaluation. The average power consumption of our decoder has been estimated with and without the ET strategy, at the clock frequency of 400 MHz. The estimated power consumption with the ET strategy is around 104 mW which is a substantial reduction (around 50 %) over the decoder without the ET strategy, which consumes around 210 mW of power.
VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we have investigated an ET strategy (s-APP ET) in layered LDPC decoders based on sign comparison between the updated APP values and the current APP after processing each layer. In spite of a simple hardware implementation, we have presented that this ET strategy under the conventional NMS algorithm with one optimal normalization factor leads to an increased error-floor that significantly reduces decoding performance. This problem is caused by LDPC frames which contain 1, 2 or 3 bit errors due to the misdetection of the codeword. We have proposed an improvement of the NMS algorithm (I-NMS) taking into account the existence of degree-2 variable nodes in the paritycheck matrix for the irregular WiMax and WiFi codes. The I-NMS algorithm applies no normalization for the checknode messages entering variable nodes of degree-2 and an optimal normalization factor for all other check-node messages.
