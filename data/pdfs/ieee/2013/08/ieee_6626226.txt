Yongmin Jung, Yunho Jung, Senior Member, IEEE, Seongjoo Lee, Senior Member, IEEE, and Jaeseok Kim, Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract — This paper proposes a cyclic shift decomposition (CSD) algorithm to perform multi-way cyclic shifts with low complexity in the quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QCLDPC) decoder. The proposed algorithm decomposes the cyclic shift into a common cyclic shift and a private cyclic shift. Based on the proposed CSD algorithm, a low-complexity multi-way and reconfigurable cyclic shift network (CSN) for QC-LDPC codes is proposed. The proposed CSN is composed of the shared component, which performs the common cyclic shift, and the repeated component, which performs the private cyclic shift. Each component can support reconfigurability for given QCLDPC codes. By introducing the single-path shared component, only the complexity of the multi-path repeated component increases linearly as the number of multi-way paths increases. A complexity analysis of each component is also proposed. Based on the complexity analysis, the proposed CSN can perform multi-way and reconfigurable cyclic shifts with low complexity in the QC-LDPC decoder. The implementation results show that the areas of the proposed four-way CSN are 0.227 mm2 and 0.276 mm2 for the IEEE 802.11n/ac and IEEE 802.16e QC-LDPC codes, respectively, with 130 nm CMOS technology. The area saving per each-way is from 13.8% to 86.5% compared with previously presented works1.
Index Terms — cyclic shift decomposition, cyclic shift network, IEEE 802.11n/ac, IEEE 802.16e, multi-way cyclic shifts, quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check code, reconfigurability.

Recently quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes, which are defined by a sparse parity check matrix, have received much attention as one of the most attractive error

Because of the excellent performance in error correction, QC-LDPC codes are used as forward error correction codes in several wireless communication applications, such as Wi-Fi and WiMAX [3]-[5]. Since these applications support high data rates ranging from tens of Mbps up to several Gbps as well as various code rates and block lengths in channel coding, the major issues in QC-LDPC decoder design are highthroughput as well as reconfigurability [6], [7]. The throughput T of QC-LDPC decoder is computed as T = (Fclk × Lblk) / (NCPI × Niter), where Fclk and Lblk mean the clock frequency and the block length, respectively. NCPI and Niter denote the required number of clock cycles per iteration and the required number of iterations for decoding, respectively. Lblk is predetermined in the standards of applications, and Niter depends on a decoding algorithm. Fclk is also restricted by the system clock frequency in application-specified integrated circuit implementations. Thus, high throughput can be achieved by reducing NCPI. To reduce NCPI, a parallel architecture should be employed in QC-LDPC decoder design. The parallel architecture, however, significantly increases the interconnection complexity between variable node units (VNUs) and check node units (CNUs). To reduce the interconnection complexity in the parallel architecture, a broadcasting scheme has been introduced [8], [9]. Since one VNU (CNU) delivers different messages to neighboring CNUs (VNUs) in the message-passing algorithm without the broadcasting scheme, the interconnection complexity is very high. In contrast, since one VNU (CNU) delivers identical messages to neighboring CNUs (VNUs) in the message-passing algorithm with the broadcasting scheme, the interconnection complexity can be reduced. The broadcasting scheme can be applied to both SP and MS algorithms [8]. Since the broadcasting scheme does not change the desired messages of the message-passing algorithm, there is no performance degradation caused by the broadcasting scheme.
A parity check matrix of the QC-LDPC codes is divided into Z × Z square sub-matrices. Each sub-matrix is a zero matrix or a cyclic-shifted identity matrix. To support various code rates and block lengths, the various parity check matrices, which are composed of various sizes of sub-matrices, are

Due to the nature of the cyclic shift in the sub-matrix, Z VNUs and Z CNUs constitute one VNU group and one CNU group, respectively, and the interconnections between the VNU group and the CNU group can be implemented by a Z × Z cyclic shift network (CSN). To support the high data rate in Wi-Fi and WiMAX applications, the QC-LDPC decoder based on the parallel architecture needs multiple Z × Z CSNs, which can perform multiple cyclic shifts simultaneously. In addition, including the maximum sub-matrix size ZM × ZM, the CSN should be able to perform the cyclic shift for any Z × Z sub-matrices defined in the standards of applications. In other words, the ZM × ZM CSN has to support reconfigurability.
In many previously presented works, the reconfigurable CSNs based on the Benes network [10] or barrel shifter [11] were introduced [9], [12]-[21]. The Benes network-based CSN [9] was employed when the broadcasting scheme is applied to the messages-passing algorithm. Another Benes network-based CSN [12] can support the reconfigurability. However, the hardware complexity of the presented cyclic shift algorithm is high. The CSN [13] used the look-up table (LUT), which stores the control signals of Benes networks, to support the reconfigurability. However, the large sub-matrix increases the hardware overhead of the LUT. The barrel shifter-based CSNs used an additional network to support reconfigurability [14]-[16]. Another barrel shifter-based CSN [17], which also uses the additional multiplexer (MUX) to support reconfigurability, was introduced for WiMAX application. The low-complexity CSN based on the barrel shifter, which includes the efficient control signal generator, has been introduced [18]. The reconfigurable CSN [19] used additional resources called self-routing bits. Another reconfigurable CSN composed of three networks was presented [20]. The previously proposed CSNs [9], [12]-[20] are reconfigurable, but do not support the multi-way cyclic shifts. Repeating these CSNs for the multi-way cyclic shifts significantly increases the hardware complexity of the entire CSNs in the parallel architecture. The CSN [21] supported two-way cyclic shifts only for sub-matrices with sizes of 2 ≤ Z ≤ ZM / 2. As another CSN, two-stage CSN was introduced for non-binary LDPC codes [22]. This CSN supported the parallel processing by using the property of non-binary LDPC codes. Thus, it is hard to apply this CSN to the binary QC-LDPC codes of the Wi-Fi and WiMAX applications [3]-[5].
This paper focuses on the methodology for a state-of-the-art CSN design, which can support multi-way and reconfigurable cyclic shifts with low complexity in high-speed QC-LDPC decoder, and presents the implementation results of the lowcomplexity multi-way and reconfigurable CSN. This paper has two major contributions. First, in order to support multiway cyclic shifts with low complexity, this paper proposes the cyclic shift decomposition (CSD) algorithm, which decomposes the cyclic shift into a common cyclic shift and a private cyclic shift. This algorithm derives a design process for the multi-way CSN. The decomposition of the cyclic shift

Second, the low-complexity multi-way and reconfigurable CSN is proposed. B. Design and Complexity Analysis of the Reconfigurable Shared Component The shared component is composed of several stages, as shown in Fig. 2. When the regular interval is P, the number of
stages of the shared component is (ZM 1) / P . Each stage
has a P-step cyclic shifter that requires few MUXs to support the reconfigurability.
To perform the cyclic shift for the different size submatrices defined in the standards, MUXs are added at each Pstep cyclic shifter. The required number of MUXs varies with the position of the stage. The required number of MUXs at the vth stage is determined by how many different size submatrices should be considered for the reconfigurability. The cyclic-shifted value performed on the output of the vth stage is v × P. The vth stage does not need to support the reconfigurability for some sub-matrices whose sizes are smaller than the v × P, because the output Z-message of the vth stage is not used at the repeated component when the CSN

is operated for these sub-matrices. Therefore, by supporting the reconfigurability for the particular sub-matrices whose sizes are larger than v × P at the vth stage, the number of MUXs of each stage can be minimized, and the shared component can be optimized for the given QC-LDPC codes.
Let NMUX,v(Kv, P, ΔZ) be the required number of MUXs at the vth stage, where Kv means the number of sub-matrices whose sizes are larger than v × P, and ΔZ denotes the regular interval between the sub-matrix sizes. For example, Fig. 3 shows the P-step cyclic shifter for different Kv in IEEE 802.11n/ac QC-LDPC codes. There are three sub-matrix sizes, i.e., 27, 54, and 81, and by definition, ΔZ is 27. P should be smaller than the minimum sub-matrix size because if P is larger than 27, then the range of p(i, j) is not reduced by the shared component when Z = 27. Under these conditions, Kv is
expressed as Kv  3  (v  P) / Z  , and NMUX,v(Kv, P, ΔZ) is

In addition, although the critical path delay of l-to-1 MUX is longer than that of 2-to-1 MUX, the critical path delay caused by the additional network can be removed by using the l-to-1 MUX. This fine cyclic shifter can be applied to both IEEE 802.11n/ac and IEEE 802.16e QC-LDPC codes. When P is smaller than the minimum sub-matrices size, (K – 1) × 2w-1 2-to-1 MUXs at the wth stage are replaced with 3-to-1 MUXs, for 2w-1 ≤ ΔZ, where K represents the number of different types of submatrices to be considered for reconfigurability. For 2w-1 > ΔZ, (K – 2) × ΔZ 4-to-1 MUXs and 2 × ΔZ 3-to-1 MUXs replace the same number of 2-to-1 MUXs at the wth stage. According to the complexity analyses of the selection network and the fine cyclic shift network, the proper P that minimizes the complexity of the repeated component can be decided.

D. Simple Control Signal Generation
The control signals for the proposed CSN can be simply generated from s(i, j) defined in the base parity check matrix, when P is a power of 2. For example, assume that P = 16, Z = 81, and s(i, j) is 51. In binary form, s(i, j) can be described as {b6 b5 b4 b3 b2 b1 b0} = {0110011}. The cyclic-shifted values of the output Z-messages of the shared component are 0, 16, 32, 48, 64, and 80 in ascending order. These Z-messages are mapped to the digits from 0 to 5 in the same order. The selection network has to select the Z-message whose cyclicshifted value is closest to and not greater than 51. According the proposed CSD algorithm, the proper Z-message can be selected by k(i, j). k(i, j) can be easily generated from the
log2 Z   log2 P most significant bits {b6 b5 b4} = {0 1 1}.
As a result, the selection network can easily select the proper Z-message whose cyclic-shifted value is 48.
The private cyclic shift value p(i, j) is 3. p(i, j) is used as the control signal of the fine cyclic shifter. The first and second stages of the fine cyclic shifter have to perform cyclic shifts and the other stages have to pass their input Z-messages to the
next stage. p(i, j) can be easily generated from the log2 P
least significant bits {b3 b2 b1 b0} = {0 0 1 1}. The cyclic shift is performed on the Z-message at the wth stage if bw-1 = 1, and the Z-message is passed to the next stage otherwise. For the
reconfigurability, the log2 K  bit control signals are
required to distinguish the sub-matrix sizes in two components, where K denotes the total number of different types of sub-matrices defined in the standards. Therefore, only
log2 Z   log2 K  control signals are required.

Table V and Table VI summarize the comparison of the implementation results for the proposed CSN and existing CSNs [12], [14]-[19], [21] for IEEE 802.16e and IEEE 802.11n/ac QC-LDPC codes, respectively. Since the existing CSNs do not support parallel processing, the each-way area as well as the multi-way area of the proposed CSN are discussed for a fair comparison. The proposed CSN is synthesized using 130 nm CMOS technology. The regular interval P of the shared component is 16, and 4-way process is assumed. The message bits are 6-bits and 8-bits for IEEE 802.16e and IEEE 802.11n/ac QC-LDPC codes, respectively. The proposed 4way CSN for IEEE 802.16e QC-LDPC code occupies 0.276 mm2 at 300 MHz clock frequency, and the area of each-way is 0.069 mm2. For a fair comparison, technology scaling is applied to the results of the related works which did not use 130 nm CMOS technology. The area saving per each-way is 13.8% to 86.5% compared to previous works using the same message bits. The proposed 4-way CSN for IEEE 802.11n/ac QC-LDPC codes occupies 0.227 mm2 at 400 MHz clock frequency, and the area of each-way is 0.057 mm2. The area saving per each-way is 40% compared to previous work using the same message bits.
When the regular interval P of the shared component is 16, the shared component has five stages and the repeated component also has five stages including 6-to-1 MUX of the

selection network. Although the 6-to-1 MUX used in the selection network increases the critical path delay of the proposed CSN, the increased delay does not significantly lower the clock frequency. The proposed CSN can be well operated at the high clock frequency about 300 MHz. Since the system clock frequencies of the Wi-Fi and WiMAX applications are usually less than 300 MHz, the proposed CSN can be well applied to the QC-LDPC decoder of these applications.
In conclusion, compared to previous works, the proposed CSN can support multi-way and reconfigurable cyclic shifts with low complexity in the parallel architecture-based highthroughput QC-LDPC decoder.
VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, a CSD algorithm was proposed to achieve low-complexity multi-way cyclic shifts in the QC-LDPC decoder. The architecture and implementation results of a lowcomplexity multi-way and reconfigurable CSN based on the CSD algorithm were also presented. By the complexity analysis, the proposed CSN can support multi-way and reconfigurable cyclic shifts with low complexity for the given QC-LDPC codes of Wi-Fi and WiMAX applications. In addition, control signals were simply generated. Consequently, the proposed CSN can be effectively applied to the high-throughput QC-LDPC decoder for Wi-Fi and WiMAX applications.
