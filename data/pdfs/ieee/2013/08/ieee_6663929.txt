PROBABILISTIC ANALYSIS OF CYCLES IN RANDOM TANNER GRAPHS
Xiaopeng Jiao, Jianjun Mu
School of Computer Science and Technology Xidian University, Xi’an, China

ABSTRACT
Cycles in bipartite graphs (also called Tanner graphs in channel coding field) are of particular interest in modern coding theory, especially in capacity-achieving low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes. In this paper, the expected number of cycles of various lengths in randomly constructed regular and irregular Tanner graphs are calculated. For a given degree distribution, the expected number of cycles in randomly constructed Tanner graphs has negligible changes with the size of Tanner graphs. Based on tree expanding, we propose a cycle counting algorithm (CCA) for Tanner graphs. Numerical results by counting the average number of short cycles over 200 random ensembles using the CCA give convincing supports to our analysis.
Index Terms— LDPC code, cycles, Tanner graph, probabilistic analysis
1. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, one of the remarkable channel coding technologies, are widely deployed in modern communication and storage systems, such as DVBS2, Wimax, and CCSDS. It has been proved that using the belief propagation (BP) decoding, irregular LDPC codes are very close to the Shannon limit at infinite block lengths [1]. However, for practical block lengths, LDPC codes are shown to have error floors [2]. The BP decoding is performed based on bipartite graphs (also called Tanner graphs [3]). It has been shown that the problematic substructures of Tanner graphs are responsible for the error floors, such as stopping sets [4], trapping sets [2]. Both stopping sets and trapping sets are closely related to the cycles of the Tanner graph [5][6].
Many construction algorithms of LDPC codes use cycles as an optimization parameter [7][8]. In [9], the performance of LDPC codes are estimated by enumerating the short cycles. Asvadi et al. proposed to use cyclic liftings to improve the LDPC codes by breaking short cycles involved in the base code [10]. Short cycles can also be used to narrow the search space when searching the dominant trapping sets [5][6].

Counting and enumerating cycles in a Tanner graph are motivated by the research of LDPC codes. In [11], Halford et al. proposed an algorithm for counting short cycles with length g, g + 2, and g + 4, where g is the girth of the Tanner graph. This method transforms the problem of counting short cycles in a Tanner graph into the problem of counting a special class of walks called lollipop walks. In [12], Karimi et al. proposed a message-passing algorithm for counting short cycles of length g, g + 2, · · · , 2g − 2. Both the computational complexity and the space complexity of the algorithm in [12] are lower than the one presented in [11]. Moreover, numerical results in [12] showed that the changes of the number of short cycles with respect to the block length are negligible. This phenomenon is closely relate to the results in [13] as follows. For d-regular bipartite graphs, as the number of nodes goes to infinity, the distribution of short cycles of length ci tends to independent Poisson distributions with average µi = (d − 1)ci /ci. However, this result can not be used directly for Tanner graphs since they are not regular bipartite graphs.
In this paper, we analyze the expected number of cycles in random irregular Tanner graphs. We show that, as the block length goes to infinite, the expected number of cycles is related to the degree distribution and the length of the cycle. Note that our derivation can be seen as a generalization of [14] for counting cycles in random regular Tanner graphs (see the Appendix in [14]).
2. PRELIMINARIES
A bipartite graph G = (V, E) is a graph whose vertices V can be divided into two disjoint sets U and W such that every edge connects a vertex in U to one in W . For Tanner graphs of LDPC codes, U and W are referred to as variable nodes and check nodes, respectively. The number of variable nodes |U | = n is the block length of the code. The number of check nodes |W | = m is the number of parity check equations. The length of cycles in Tanner graphs must be even. For LDPC codes, m is always less than n.

The cycle counting algorithm (CCA) will be used in Section V to provide numerical results for the probabilistic analysis of cycles. The main idea of the CCA is the tree expanding of nodes in Tanner graphs. Fig. 2(b) illustrates the expanding tree of variable node v1 in the Tanner graph shown in Fig. 2(a). All the levels whose indexes are odd numbers consist of variable nodes, and all the levels whose indexes are even numbers consist of check nodes. A cycle occurs if two positions in the expanding tree represent the same node in the Tanner graph. For example, in Fig. 2(b) a cycle of length 4 expanded as v1 − c2 − v5 − c5 − v1.
