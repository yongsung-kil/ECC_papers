New High Rate LDPC codes for radio applications
Ankita Pramanik, Gangamma Patil, L. Borman
Central Research Laboratory, Bharat Electronics Limited Bangalore 560013, India
ankitapramanik@bel.co.in, gangammapatil@bel.co.in, lotiswarborman@bel.co.in

Abstract— Low-density parity-check (LDPC) code is linear-block error-correcting code defined by sparse parity-check matrix. LDPC codes have found wide application in various fields like satellite transmission, recording in magnetic discs etc. because of their capability to reach near Shannon limit performance. This paper presents 3 new high rates - 0.81, 0.84 and 0.89 of length 2512, 1843 and 2619 respectively, LDPC codes for radio application. The codes are constructed using shifted identity matrix and a lower staircase matrix. The complexity of encoding of these codes is low and decoding can be done by belief propagation. Simulation results of the codes show good bit error rate performance with min-sum decoding algorithm.
Keywords— High rate, irregular, lower staircase matrix, LDPC.
I. INTRODUCTION
Radio systems transmit data from source to receiver through air. The data after transmission may become erroneous by the addition of the channel noise and the external and internal system noise. Incorporation of Forward Error Correcting (FEC) codes in the data to be transmitted increases the reliability of the data at the receiving end by detection and/or correction of these errors. The level of ‘protection’ against these errors depends on the FEC chosen. Different types of FEC are available such as simple linear block codes, convolutional codes, turbo codes, low density parity check codes (LDPC), etc. LDPC was originally proposed in 1962 by Robert Gallager [1] in his PH.D thesis. These codes can achieve performance nearing the Shannon Limit. But such LDPC codes were difficult to implement in that decade as they had a complex encoding and decoding algorithm which needed a rigorous hardware implementation. So, the FEC implementers at that time were not interested in LDPC. But in the late 90’s LDPC codes were rediscovered by Mackay and Neal [2], Wiberg [3] and independently by Luby [4], [5]. Current scenario in field of hardware like the power of the processor, processing speed etc. is conducive for LDPC implementation. The decoding algorithms of LDPC codes can be parallelized and numerous applications making use of this feature is available. Due to their performance being very close to capacity curve, LDPC codes are presently used in DVB-S2, DVB-T, 10 G Base - T, 802.11n and 802.16e and also in magnetic recording discs. LDPC give very good performance even in fading channels. LDPC codes when applied in radio will give better error protection compared to the presently used FEC.

LDPC codes belong to the family of linear block codes. LDPC codes have very few 1’s in the parity check matrix H i.e., the H matrix is sparse and so the codes are called low density. LDPC code can be described by the H matrix and also by Tanner graph [6]. If in the H matrix there are a fixed number of ones (weight) in every row and also column, then the code is called regular LDPC code. Gallager has documented the construction of regular LDPC codes. With further research, it was seen that LDPC code performance was enhanced by using irregular LDPC codes, i.e., codes in which the number of ones per row and column were not fixed or in other words non - uniform [7-9]. The LDPC codes available in the standard literature conform to a specified block length. In this paper we present three high rate LDPC codes for radio applications. The LDPC codes presented here are irregular LDPC codes. In the DVB-S2 [10] standards high rate LDPC codes are presented. But the block length of the codes are very high i.e., length = 16,200 and 64,800. In practical radio application, such a long length of data may not be present. In IEEE 802.16-2009 [11] standard, LDPC codes of four different code rates (R) i.e., 0.5, 0.67, 0.75 and 0.83 and of various length n, ranging from 576 to 2304 in steps of 4 X 24 = 96 have been presented. The format i.e., the arrangement of the identity matrices and their shifted format and the zero matrices, of the codes documented in the IEEE 802.16-2009 standard are similar. All the codes are constructed by scaling each entry of a base matrix Hbase, by a matrix of suitable dimension to get the H matrix for the code of desired length. All the Hbase matrices have 24 columns and the number of rows in the Hbase matrices, varies based on the code rates. So, the final codes can be encoded and decoded by a generalized technique for all the code rates and lengths. In [12] and [13] the authors have presented new LDPC codes of various rates and lengths for wireless applications. In this paper we present 3 high rate LDPC codes of various block lengths constructed for our radio application.
The paper is organized as follows. The system model is given in the second section. The third section of the paper gives a brief overview of the LDPC codes i.e., it describes the existing methods of LDPC code construction and also the existing methods of encoding and decoding of LDPC codes. The 3 new high rate H matrices along with their detailed structure and simulated BER results are presented in the fourth section. Section five summarizes the paper.

II. SYSTEM MODEL FOR LDPC ENCODED RADIO In the transmitter side of the radio the information data, which is binary for our application, is encoded using a LDPC encoder. The encoded data is then mapped to the BPSK constellation. After the modulation step, the data is then transmitted to the channel. Unlike other FEC application, the coded data is not passed through interleaver as the structure of LDPC codes is such that it gives burst-error protection. For simulation purposes we have considered AWGN channel only. At the receiver side, the received data is at first demodulated and then the demodulated signal is passed through the LDPC decoder. The output of the decoder is the receiver’s estimate of the transmitted data. The radio used for our application is UHF radio and has a data rate of 19.2kbps. III. LDPC CODES LDPC code is a powerful error correcting code that achieves performance close to the capacity curve given by Shannon. In LDPC codes, most of the entries in the parity check matrix H is zero and hardly a few entries are ‘1’ for binary codes and other symbols for non - binary codes. LDPC codes belong to the family of block codes. But they differ from the older block codes in the aspect of decoding the received codes. The legacy block codes are algebraically designed and are smaller in length so that they can be decoded by Maximum Likelihood (ML) decoding techniques. LDPC codes are decoded iteratively and so to get good performance they are of huge lengths. The LDPC code-words satisfy a set of linear equations [1] given by an m-by-n the H matrix. The number of rows of the H matrix gives the total number of equations to be solved and the number of columns gives the length n of the codeword. LDPC codes can also be represented by the equivalent bipartite or Tanner graph of the H matrix. Let us define few terms which will be used in this paper: Cycle: [13], [14], [15] A cycle of length 2t is a set of t check nodes and t variable nodes connected by edges such that a path exists that travels through every node in the set and connects each node to itself without covering an edge twice. Regular LDPC codes: A regular LDPC code represented as (wc,wr) [13] is one in which each column has fixed number wc 1’s and each row a ¿xed number, wr of 1’s. Irregular LDPC codes: An irregular LDPC code is one in which the number of non-zero entries – 1 for binary codes are different for every row and column. In irregular LDPC codes, the degrees of the variable and check nodes are given by polynomial equations. The polynomial function Ȝ(x) for representing the degree distribution of the variable node is given as follows [14], [15]:

IV. NEW H MATRICES
Three new H matrices are constructed for our high rate radio applications. The matrices constructed have rate, r of 0.8 < r < 0.9. The information block length of the matrices corresponds to the length of the data of our radio application. The codes are obtained by arranging random permutation of unit matrix of weight 3. Let the designed H matrix be of the size m X (k + m). The H matrices constructed are such that the rightmost m X m sub-matrix is of lower staircase format. So, the codes can be encoded by the following procedure:
1. Define the matrix A such that matrix A is formed of the first k columns of H matrix.
2. Denote the k length information vector as ‘info’ 3. Let, the parity vector be ‘par’ 4. Derive the first parity bit as
¦ par(1) = A1, j d j j
where j denotes the number on non-zero positions. 5. For counter i = 2: m derive the parity bits as
¦ par(i) = par(i −1) + Ai, j d j j
The encoding process can be easily implemented using an exclusive-OR gate and a flip-flop. Decoding of the code can be done with any of the existing decoding techniques for LDPC codes.
The figure 5 shows the H matrix for data length of 2041 and has a rate of 0.81. The data word after encoding with the constructed H matrix has a length of 2512.

V. CONCLUSIONS
LDPC codes are very powerful codes with enormous potential. Enormous amount of research activities are directed towards LDPC code design, analysis and implementation. In this paper we have presented 3 new LDPC codes of various rates and lengths constructed for our radio application. The codes are of irregular weight. These high rate codes can be encoded easily using an exclusive-OR gate and a flip-flop. The simulated BER performance is also satisfactory for our application.
