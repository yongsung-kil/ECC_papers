The systematic form of a class of regular structured LDPC codes

Abstract—In this paper, we propose a systematic form about a class of regular structured LDPC codes. This class of LDPC codes is quasi-cyclic code with variable node degree dv = 2 on the Tanner graph. The defining parity-check matrices are composed of circulant permutation matrices. By columns permutation, we can locate the information bits and paritycheck bits easily. Furthermore, the values of associated paritycheck bits can be calculated directly on the Tanner graph.
Keywords-efficient encoding, circulant matrices, LDPC codes, systematic form.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, invented by Gallager in 1962 [1], have attracted considerable attention in the coding community. Loosely speaking, LDPC codes can be classified into two classes−random LDPC codes and structured LDPC codes−according to their construction methods. The family of LDPC codes constructed by circulant permutation matrices is a category of structured LDPC codes. In [2] Fossorier has summarized three methods (sum, product, power) to construct the structured LDPC codes based on circulant permutation matrices. The resulting LDPC codes are quasi-cyclic codes whose Tanner graphs have no girth 4.
For random LDPC codes, the laking of efficient encoding algorithms prevents them from practical applications. However, for the quasi-cyclic LDPC code, Li el [3] have presented an efficient encoding method depending on a new constructed generator matrix from the parity-check matrix. Aided by the constructed generator matrix, the encoding complexity is linearly with respect to the number of paritycheck bits for serial encoding.
In this paper, we study on the systematic form about a special class of regular structured LDPC codes with variable node degree dv = 2. These codes are constructed by the product method described in [2]. After permutation the columns of the parity-check matrix to a special form, all the parity-check equations can be expressed by the elements of a codeword matrix, just as the two dimension single parity product code. Aided by the codeword matrix, the information bits and parity-check bits are distinguished and located. Moreover, the values of located parity-check bits

can be calculated directly on the codeword matrix as well as on the Tanner graph.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. Section II describes the details about the systematic form of a class of structured LDPC codes. Section III presents the efficient encoding algorithms based on the codeword matrix. Section IV shows an example about the proposed systematic form and Section V concludes this paper.

III. ENCODING ALGORITHM
The LDPC code described by H˜ can be encoded via the codeword matrix C. From Subsetion II-B, we know that there are n − 1 row constrains at k = 0, 1, · · · , n − 3, p − 2 which has only one parity-check bit each. Therefore, the associated parity-check bits can be calculated directly by XOR operation. Now, there is no unknown bit at the first n− 2 rows of C, that leads to one unknown bit for every diagonal constrain at k = p − n + 1, p − n + 2, · · · , p − 1. Therefore, the associated parity check bits can be calculated by XOR operation. The remaining parity check bits can be obtained by applying the row and diagonal constrain alteratively.

The encoding algorithm shows us that the encoding process is a serial encoding and its complexity is linearly with respect to the number of parity check bits. Only the columns permutation is needed before the encoding process. The encoding process can also be implemented on the Tanner graph described by H˜ , which makes the encoder circuit share the same silicon area with a decoder. The encoding process on Tanner graph will be demonstrated through an example.

V. CONCLUSION In this paper, we have proposed a systematic form for a class of regular structured LDPC codes with variable node degree dv = 2. Based on the systematic form of parity-check matrix, the encoding process can be implemented linearly with respect to the number of parity-check bits. Moreover, the encoding can be implemented on the Tanner graph where the parity-check variable nodes are located. This can be valuable in transceiver systems for sharing the same silicon area.
