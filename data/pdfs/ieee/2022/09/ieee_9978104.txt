Abstract— This paper proposes an optimized pipelined decoding architecture with seven processing stages for unrolled LDPC decoders. Pipelined design with seven register layers significantly increases the resulting clock frequency. Moreover, we investigate the optimal layout shape for unrolled decoders. This paper's fastest decoder is based on the IEEE 802.11n LDPC(1944,1620) parity-check matrix and achieves 2937 Gb/s of coded throughput after physical design. By optimizing the pipeline, floorplan, and employing a codeword length of 1944 bits, we increased the throughput by 241% compared to the previous fastest LDPC decoder presented in the literature. To the best of our knowledge, it is the fastest soft-decision decoder published so far. The standard min-sum approach is employed for decoding, and the proposed improvements consider changes only on the hardware level.
Keywords— LDPC, pipeline, chip layout
I. INTRODUCTION
Forward error correction (FEC) algorithms and their effective hardware realizations are one of the key research challenges in high-performance communication systems. All high-speed communication standards employ effective error-controlling and error-correction mechanisms to provide reliable and efficient data exchange. However, with an increased data rate, improved FEC architectures are needed to support the highest throughput. This paper demonstrates a fully-parallel lowdensity parity-check (LDPC) decoders redesigned on the hardware level, supporting throughput up to 2.94 Tb/s in 28 nm CMOS technology. The applied decoding method is a belief propagation (BP) min-sum algorithm with a flooding schedule which facilitates a fully-parallel hardware realization. LDPC

decoders that support 550 Gb/s [1], 588 Gb/s [2], 1000 Gb/s [3], and even up to 1280 Gb/s [4] have been thus far demonstrated in the literature. However, this paper shows uncomplicated hardware architecture with a processing throughput of 2937 Gb/s, almost three times higher than any other known soft-decision FEC decoder. The throughput in our implementation is increased due to three factors: (I) a redesigned decoding pipeline with seven processing stages, (II) specific placement of the unrolled iterations in the chip, and (III) increased codeword size as compared to [1]. We investigate IEEE 802.11n LDPC codewords of 640 and 1944 bits. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first 802.11n LDPC decoder that successfully demonstrates the usage of iteration unrolling for the IEEE 802.11n 1944-bit codeword in a chip implementation. The design reuses the parity matrix of 802.11n, but the throughput achieved here is targeted for optical cable communication systems rather than wireless devices. Also, fixed coderate and high power consumption target our implementation for stationary high-throughput server centers rather than handheld low-power mobile devices. The organization of the paper is as follows. Section II presents our architecture for high-speed LDPC decoders. The achieved results and a comparison with state-of-the-art are provided in section III. Conclusions are made in section IV.

A. Iteration unrolling
Iteration unrolling is a well-known technique [5] to increase throughput for iterative FEC decoders. Such a method could be applied to BP polar and LDPC decoders, as well as turbo decoders. It is an unavoidable methodology to reach the highest possible decoding throughput. As implied by the name, iteration unrolling is the act of unrolling the successive iterations of an iterative algorithm, and devoting a dedicated hardware implementation to each iteration. Figure 1 shows the concept of iteration unrolling in our implementation. On the input, between the iterations, and at the output, additional pipelining registers are added to improve the iteration's hardware placement and timing. Without these extra registers, the critical paths are often located in the signals carrying the

soft bit values between the iterations. Thus, registers between the iterations are inserted. All iterations work with soft-bit values, and at the end of the decoding, module "HD ext." extracts the data bits.
B. Extended decoding pipeline
Figure 2 depicts the proposed pipeline with seven stages for a single iteration. All input and output signals to the iteration hardware are routed via flip-flops (FFs). The min-search modules are activated after computing the extrinsic information for variable nodes (denoted as the "Ext." block in Fig. 2). In our case, the input and output to the min-search modules need to be driven by the registers. Moreover, the min operation takes two clock cycles and is divided into two processing stages. Such pipelined min-search architecture is needed to avoid clock frequency reduction. The min-search modules and the signals connecting the iterations were the primary sources of critical paths in the design. Thus, these two elements need to be designed with special care and must be pipelined. The min-search modules are based on a typical binary-tree comparator. For LDPC(648,540), the min-search modules need to find the first and the second minimum out of 22 values in two clock cycles. Thus, the binary min-search tree has ⌊log 22⌋ = 5 levels. The third level is divided by FFs to reduce the critical paths. The min-search modules for LDPC(1944,1620) are also based on 5-level binary comparison trees. In the case of the (1944,1620) code, the modules need to find the first and second minimum out of 20 values. Thus its implementation is almost identical to that in (648,540).
C. Optimized layout shape
After redesigning the pipeline on the VHDL level, there is still a possibility to achieve higher throughput by modifying the shape of iteration hardware in the floorplan. To show the importance of the correct layout plan, we floorplan LDPC(648,540) and LDPC(1944,1620) decoders in three variants. Firstly, we use the default settings for the chip-layout software, and we try to fit the logic to a square-shaped floorplan with an aspect ratio of 1:1.25, as shown in Fig. 3. After that, we experiment with placement strategies and layout shapes, as presented in Fig. 4 and Fig. 5. Both layouts may look the same and have only reversed XY orientations. However, there are significant differences between Fig. 4 and Fig. 5. We keep the orientation of the horizontal and vertical metal layers constant for both layouts, and also the placement of the logic cell remains in the same orientation (row orientation is unchanged). Thus, XY reversed floorplan significantly impacts the cell placement and routing. As a result, the designs achieve different clock frequencies. In our case, the horizontally placed decoder’s iterations (Fig. 5) gave the highest throughput. More results are discussed in section III.

III. RESULTS
This section discusses the obtained chip results. All investigations are made after the physical design in 28 nm technology (place&route) in the worst-case corner at 0.9V, 125°C, and the slowest process.
A. Increased throughput due to redesigned pipeline
We have already demonstrated the idea of iteration unrolling in LDPC decoders in [1], where we achieved 550 Gb/s for 802.11n LDPC(648,540) code. That design uses six unrolled iterations and a processing pipeline with only three stages in each iteration. In this paper, we demonstrate that the typically employed 2-, 3-, and 4-stage decoding pipeline within an iteration [1], [5]–[7] is insufficient to achieve high throughput at the layout level. Thus, we extend the pipeline of a single iteration to seven stages. The additional registers (FFs) in the pipeline (see Fig. 2) increases clock frequency from 850 MHz to 2183 MHz, assuming that the clock frequency is determined in the worst-case conditions at post-layout timing estimation. Thus, the pipeline limited to three or four stages is the main bottleneck of unrolled LDPC decoders based on the min-sum BP algorithm. Increasing the pipeline to eight stages will increase the clock frequency further, but the increase is not higher than 100 MHz when compared to the proposed 7-stage pipeline. In order to floorplan LDPC(1944,1620), we need to balance the number of pipelining registers to save the chip area and reduce power consumption. In the case of the LDPC(1944,1620) code, every additional stage in the pipeline costs 5832 registers when using 3-bit soft values representation. In the case of LDPC(648,540), we use 4-bit soft values representation, and 2592 registers are required on each pipeline stage. Moreover, each unrolled iteration duplicates the pipeline, and we implement five iterations in our design. Thus, in total, every additional pipeline stage costs 5832×5=29160 FFs in the case of LDPC(1944,1620), and it is essential to find the tradeoff between the area overhead and achieved throughput. Also, the physical design of the decoders with long codewords is much more complicated than the LDPC(648,540). The very high overhead of registers for deeply pipelined and fully unrolled decoders seems to be very expensive. However, such decoders use a highly complex metal network to connect all parity matrix processing nodes. Most of the overhead comes from the routing area, not the logic. For example, our chip for the LDPC(648,540) has a core utilization of 40% and an area of 5.5 mm2. In our previous implementation of the LDPC(648,540), where the typical 3-stage pipeline and iteration unrolling are employed, we achieved 550 Gb/s with a similar chip area and core utilization of only 30% [1]. Thus, all additional registers increase the clock frequency and the core utilization. The overhead in the chip area after the floorplan is not as significant as it is after the synthesis. B. Floorplan and placement optimization improvements
Table I summarizes the chip results for three floorplans strategies for LDPC(648,540) and LDPC(1944,1620). For both decoders, horizontally placed iterations (Fig. 5) gave the best results concerning clock frequency and throughput. It also increases the area and energy efficiency. Thus, the shape of the decoder’s iterations and their placement in the layout is important, and must be considered together with an algorithm, pipeline scheme, and technology. In our case, the manually guided floorplanning tool obtained 17% higher throughput than the same tool with the default floorplan aspect ratio.

C. Core utilization
All our designs presented in this paper (Table I) achieve very low core utilization. In the best case, the core utilization is only 42%. This is two times less than for other typical digital designs. Such significant area overhead is caused by a limited metal stack for CMOS technologies and the congestion of the parity matrix. If we try to shrink the layout size, the router moves signals to the highest metal layers. This leads to a complicated network of vias, and as an effect, we get a vast number of design rule check (DRC) errors due to violated metal spacing rules. Thus, we propose to route data signals in lower layers of the chip, where the metal paths are narrower and require fewer and smaller vias. As a result, the core utilization is not higher than 42%, but the design contains a very low number of DRC errors that can be easily fixed manually. By default, in the 28 nm processes available for us, we employ maximally up to 7 copper layers. We can add an additional thick aluminum layer on the top, but routing on this layer causes many DRC issues and cannot be used for typical signal routing.

D. BER performance
Although we reduce the quantization for LDPC(1944,1620) to 3 bits only, this design achieves similar BER performance as the LDPC(648,540) with 4 bits quantization due to the increased block length from 648 to 1944 bits (Fig. 6). Thus, we can apply quantization reductions and increase the design's parallelism by switching to a longer codeword. The pipelined LDPC(1944,1620) decoder processes 1944 bits in every clock cycle, while the (648,540) processes only 648 bits per clock. Thus, the very long codeword is critical for achieving very high throughputs. In general, reducing the quantization to 3 bits for the (1944,1620) decoder significantly degrades the BER performance (Fig. 7), and the loss is 0.5 - 0.7 dB. Thus, using at least four quantization bits is recommended for these types of LDPC decoders. In our case, we decided to reduce the

number of quantization bits due to ~30% reduced chip area and ~15% increased clock frequency. As a BER performance reference, we used the (648,540) decoders presented in [1], [4]. Thus we had the flexibility of reducing the quantization due to the longer codeword that compensates the loss. Fig. 8 compares the BER performance of the LDPC(1944,1620) decoder based on 4-bit quantization and four unrolled iterations, together with 3-bit quantization and five unrolled iterations. The second combination gives better BER performance at a similar chip area and is employed in our design.

Table II summarizes the performance of high-speed LDPC decoders from the literature and compares them with our LDPC(1944,1620) implementation. IV. CONCLUSION
This paper proposes uncomplicated modifications in the hardware so that the standard min-sum decoding algorithm can process 2937 Gb/s of encoded data when implemented in mature 28-nm CMOS technology. The presented LDPC decoder based on 802.11n (1944,1620) parity-check matrix is the fastest soft-decision decoder published so far. We do not consider any algorithmic improvements, and we show that an optimized VHDL and floorplan design are the key elements of this high-speed implementation. We start with the pipeline improvements and investigate the register placement in the VHDL. Later, we investigate the optimal floorplan shape and the usage of metal layers. We balance the number of quantization bits and the number of unrolled iterations. A
