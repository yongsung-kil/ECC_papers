Design of Protograph-Based LDPC Codes for Two-User Gaussian Multiple Access Channel
Xiaodan Li , Pingping Chen , Senior Member, IEEE, Yi Fang , Member, IEEE, and Zhaopeng Xie

Abstract—In this letter, we study the performance of protograph-based low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes for two-user Gaussian multiple access channel (GMAC). Considering the characteristic of a joint protograph of two users, we first derive a joint protograph-based extrinsic information transfer (JP-EXIT) chart to analyze the iterative user decoding behavior of two-user GMAC. Guided by the JP-EXIT, we further propose a new design scheme and construct protograph base matrices of different code rates for two users, respectively. Both theoretical analyses and simulation results show that the proposed protograph-based LDPC codes of low decoding threshold outperform the conventional irregular LDPC codes for both equal-power and unequal-power GMAC with different code rates.
Index Terms—Two-user GMAC, protograph-based LDPC code, joint decoding.
I. INTRODUCTION
G AUSSIAN multiple access channel (GMAC) is a wellstudied multiuser channel in information theory and communication theory [1]. The capacity region of two-user GMAC has been completely characterized. The corner points of two-user GMAC can be achieved by successive interference cancellation (SIC), while other points can be achieved by time-sharing, rate-splitting, or joint decoding [2], [3].
Powerful channel codes can be used to improve the performance of GMAC. From a practical channel coding perspective, [4] is the first work to optimize irregular low-densityparity-check (LDPC) codes for two-user GMAC by extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) chart analysis. To improve accuracy of the EXIT chart, a Gaussian mixture (GM) assumption was derived for the state-to-variable under fixed channel gains and quasi-static fading [5]. By tracking the variation of the GM components in the iterative decoding, the irregular LDPC codes were optimized for equal-power multiuser GMAC [6]. Furthermore, a low-complexity multiuser repetition-aided irregular repeat-accumulate (IRA) code was proposed to approach the capacity of equal-power GMAC [7].

As an extension, the LDPC code designed by the EXIT charts was presented for two-user unequal-power GMAC [8]. As a result, an irregular LDPC code ensemble with desired degree distribution and low decoding threshold can be obtained. Unlike in [4], where BPSK signals with the same amplitude are employed, two users adopt the two power levels alternatively to avoid ambiguity in two-user GMAC [9].
However, the optimized irregular LDPC codes suffers from complex hardware implementation and high error floor [10]. Protograph LDPC codes have shown excellent performance for point-to-point communications and data storage [11], [12]. This kind of LDPC codes is particularly attractive for lowcomplexity implementation for its linear encoding and decoding [10]. Thus, this letter proposes the design of the protograph LDPC codes for two-user GMAC. We first derive a joint protograph-based extrinsic information transfer chart (JP-EXIT) to characterize the joint user decoding behavior. Then, we present the design of the protograph matrices for two users by exploiting the characteristic of the joint protograph graph. The simulation results show that the proposed protograph LDPC codes significantly outperform the conventional irregular LDPC codes with different code rates for both equal-power and unequal-power channels.
The rest of this letter is organized as follows. Section II briefly reviews the GMAC model and protograph code. Section III proposes the JP-EXIT for two-user iterative decoding and designs the protograph matrices for two users. Section IV presents the simulation results. Section V concludes this letter.

The GMAC model consists of two users that need to convey information to a receiver. The length-K information bits u[k] of user k are encoded to v [k] of length L, k = 1, 2. Then, v [k] are BPSK modulated to the vector x [k]. At the receiver, the j-th output signal of the GMAC, j = 1, 2, . . . , L, is given by

C. Protograph-Based LDPC Codes
A protograph code contains the variable nodes, the check nodes and the connecting edges. Unlike conventional LDPC codes, parallel edges are allowed in a protograph. The adjacency matrix of a protograph is defined as a base matrix of M rows and N columns. The variable nodes and check nodes of the base matrix are denoted as vj , j = 1, 2, . . . , N and ci , i = 1, 2, . . . , M , respectively. Considering the edge connections, the EXIT analysis for protograph codes (PEXIT) was proposed to analyze the decoding threshold [12]. PEXIT calculates the mutual information for the LLR messages between variable nodes and check nodes in the protograph rather than degree distribution pairs of the LDPC codes.

Furthermore, degree-2 variable node can reduce the decoding threshold of protograph codes in the waterfall region, while it may lead to error floor in high SNR region [14]. Hence, the proportion of the degree-2 variable nodes should be controlled carefully. Moreover, in the traditional LDPC code, the degree of variable nodes are usually greater than one to help decoding. It is interesting to note that in joint two-user protograph, the degree of variable nodes of B[k] of each user is increased by one, where the connected state nodes provide the additional messages for channel observation and cancel the interference, as shown in Fig. 1. Based on these observations, we consider a general design for protograph with Mk ≥ 4, i.e., at least four check nodes to enlarge the optimization space. The design rule of the protograph codes is given as follows.
1) Given a desired code rate, and determine number of check and variable nodes.
2) Avoid the punctured node in the protograph to enhance recovery probability from the superimposed signals.
3) To reduce decoding threshold, we start by a search structure with one degree-1 and one degree-2 variable node, which satisfies the stability condition (15). Degree-1 nodes and degree-2 nodes can be converted to each other. Allow one high degree variable node to guarantee decoding convergence.
4) Initialize degree of other variable nodes with 3 to preserve the linear growth of minimum distance, and these degree can be slightly modified, i.e., increase or decrease by one.
