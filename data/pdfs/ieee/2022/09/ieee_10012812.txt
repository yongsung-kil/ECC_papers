Noise-Assisted List Decoding for 5G LDPC Codes
Jian Gao, Hao Wang, and Kuangda Tian HiSilicon, Huawei Technologies Co., Ltd., Beijing 100095, China Email: hunter.wanghao@huawei.com, {gaojian78, tiankuangda}@hisilicon.com

Abstract—Weak information signals can be detected with the assistance of stochastic perturbation, and nonlinear systems benefit from this phenomenon. Inspired by this, we propose a noiseassisted NMS list (Na-NMSL) algorithm for LDPC decoding. The algorithm consists of L independent NMS decoders, and the inputs of the NMS decoders are generated by adding artificial noise with different strengths to the received signal. When lifting size Z is small, list decoding is performed in the Na-NMSL decoder by utilizing the remaining hardware resources. Therefore, the Na-NMSL decoder enhances the decoding performance of NMS without additional hardware overhead. Simulation results show that the Na-NMSL decoder has a lower block error rate (BLER) and faster convergence than traditional NMS decoding.
Index Terms—LDPC, list decoding, 5G URLLC, stochastic perturbation.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] deliver capacity approaching performance under iterative message passing decoding [2]. As a special type of LDPC codes, quasi-cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes [3] possess the additional advantage of low-complexity hardware implementation of encoders and decoders, and hence have been selected as the coding scheme of the data channel in the 5th generation communication systems (5G) [4]. Three scenarios eMBB, URLLC, and mMTC are discussed in 5G. Among the three scenarios, the URLLC has demanding requirements on latency (1ms) and reliability (99.9999%) [5], [6]. As a result, short-length QC-LDPC codes and high-performance decoders are required.
The development of LDPC codes has drawn great attention in terms of their excellent error correcting capability. LDPC codes can be efficiently decoded by Belief-Propagation (BP) algorithm that is based on a Tanner graph [2]. The BP decoder has satisfactory decoding performance but at a cost of a high computational complexity. The Min-Sum (MS) algorithm is a simplified version of BP, which reduces the decoding complexity by approximating the update function of check nodes [7]. To compensate for the performance loss caused by the approximation, the normalized MS (NMS) algorithm is proposed in [8], which introduces a normalized factor to adjust the approximation result. However, these algorithms and their optimized versions do not consider the hardware architecture of the decoder. The mismatch between algorithms and architectures leads to low hardware resource utilization, especially for short-length codes used in 5G URLLC. In the existing QCLDPC decoder architectures, the number of process elements (PE) is mainly intended to match the maximum lifting size Zmax [9], [10]. This means that up to Zmax variable nodes

or check nodes can be processed simultaneously. In the 5G [4], the lifting size Z has 51 values and Zmax=384. When decoding short-length codes with Z < Zmax, there are idle hardware resources in the decoder. The Remaining hardware resources make it possible to perform list decoding, thereby obtaining gains of multiple paths.
Noise is generally considered to have a negative impact on the communication system, and countermeasures are taken into account to suppress it. On the other hand, introducing a suitable white noise is regarded as a beneficial behavior in some channel decoding algorithms and related theories were studied [11]–[13]. Noise-assisted decoding algorithms can be divided into two categories according to usage schemes. One type is list decoding, which obtains multiple decoding inputs and corresponding outputs simultaneously by adding noises with different strengths to a received signal. For example, the NaBPL [14] and Na-BPLF [15] algorithms for polar decoding. The other type is attempt decoding. If the initial decoding fails, the received signal is modified by adding an artificial noise, and then re-decoding is attempted. Such attempt decoding algorithms include R-ABP [16] for LDPC decoding and the like. However, the two types of decoding algorithms improve error correction performance by consuming complexity and latency, respectively.
In this paper, we focus on the 5G URLLC scenario. The features of this scenario are that short length codes with small Z are transmitted, and a high-performance decoder is required to meet the demanding latency and reliability specifications. In view of this situation, we propose a noise-assisted NMS list (Na-NMSL) decoding algorithm. The algorithm consists of L branches that work as independent NMS decoders. The L NMS decoders simultaneously decode the same transmitted codeword, utilizing the idle hardware resources left because the lifting parameter Z is small. The inputs of the NMS decoders are generated by adding virtually artificial noise with different strengths to the received signal. The idle hardware resources are used by the Na-NMSL decoder to complete list enhanced decoding. Therefore, the Na-NMSL decoding does not cause additional hardware overheads. In Na-NMSL decoder, the cyclic redundancy check (CRC) code is used to pick out the correct branch form the L candidates.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. The basic concepts of QC LDPC and the NMS algorithm are reviewed in section II. Section III introduces the details of the Na-NMSL decoder. In section IV, we presents the simulation results. Finally, section V concludes the paper.

A. QC-LDPC Codes in 5G NR
An (N, K) LDPC code can be defined by a sparse paritycheck matrix (PCM) H of M rows by N columns with M > N − K. The QC-LDPC code is a specific type whose PCM is obtained by expanding the base graph (BG) with lifting size Z and cyclic shift factor τ . Each “1” entry in the BG is replaced with a Z × Z identity matrix which is circularly shifted by τi,j, where i and j indicate the i-th row and j-th column of the BG, respectively. For a specific LDPC code, all valid codewords are constrained to an irregular N -dimensional space. The transmitted codeword is affected by channel noise, so that the received signal may appear anywhere in the N-dimensional space, as shown in the Fig. 1. For a valid codeword x, its corresponding error correction space is specified. If the received signal y corresponding to the transmitted codeword x falls into the error correction region, y will be successfully decoded into the codeword x. Otherwise, another codeword or an invalid codeword will be determined, resulting in decoding failure. We spontaneously add an appropriate random noise to the received signal. This operation may pull the received signal back to the error correction region, leading to successful decoding.

The purpose of introducing artificial noise is not to cancel the channel noise, but to counteract the oscillation error of the decoder through stochastic perturbation. Therefore, the artificial noise should be much smaller compared to the channel noise in order to have a positive effect on the decoder without introducing additional transmission errors. However, the artificial noise should not be too small that it has no effect on the decoded output, which is meaning less. In the Na-NMSL decoder, an appropriate artificial noise is essential. In addition, the artificial noise of the first branch is always set to 0. This measure is to ensures that the error correction performance of the Na-NMSL decoder is not worse than that of the traditional NMS, even in cases where the artificial noise is inappropriate.

After each iteration, the early detection is performed. If ∃ si = 0 for ∀ i, the NMSi pauses decoding, and xi output from the NMSi is checked by the CRC module. The Na-NMSL decoding terminates and is determined successful, if xi passes the CRC. In this case, the output of the Na-NMSL decoder xˆ = xi. Conversely, if xi does not pass the CRC, the NMSi continues iterative decoding, and the CRC module waits for another notification that the early detection is passed. If all branches fail to pass the early detection and CRC after the preset maximum iteration number T is reached, it is determined that Na-NMSL decoding fails. In addition, if multiple branches pass the early detection and CRC at the same time, the passed xi with smallest artificial noise power is selected as the output of the Na-NMSL decoder.
D. Potential and Analysis
This paper focuses on the phenomenon that stochastic perturbation and list decoding can bring gains in LDPC decoding, so only a primary Na-NMSL algorithm is introduced. In fact, the Na-NMSL algorithm has the potential to further improve performance and hardware architecture matching.
1) In this paper, we identify the artificial noise through simulations, which may lead to the artificial noise falling into local optimality. Advanced reinforcement learning and genetic algorithm are effective techniques to solve this problem.
2) The Na-NMSL decoder is flexible and configurable. In 5G NR, Z has 51 values. If Z and L are in a one-to-one correspondence, the control algorithm of the decoder is complex. The partial parallelism is a solution that reduces complexity, where one L corresponds to multiple Z. The partially parallel scheme can achieve a tradeoff between control complexity and performance.

3) The pass-and-stop EDTC is adopted in this paper. Such EDTC may result in the case that some branches do not complete decoding. To obtain the contribution of all branches, the Na-NMSL decoder can be configured to terminate after all branches have finished. However, this configuration leads to an increase in the number of iterations.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we analyzed the reason why stochastic
perturbation is beneficial to channel decoding, and propose
a Na-NMSL decoding algorithm. The algorithm uses the
idle resources of the hardware architecture to complete list
decoding, which not only improves the decoding performance,
but also does not cause additional hardware overhead.
