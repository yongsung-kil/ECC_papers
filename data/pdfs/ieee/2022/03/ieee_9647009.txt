High-Throughput LDPC-CC Decoders Based on Storage, Arithmetic, and Control Improvements
Yuxing Chen , Hangxuan Cui , and Zhongfeng Wang , Fellow, IEEE

Abstract—This brief presents high-throughput low-density parity-check convolutional code (LDPC-CC) decoders in full compliance with the IEEE 1901 standard. The decoding architecture is improved from storage, arithmetic, and control aspects. First, to address the throughput bottleneck caused by memory bandwidth, we propose two methods, register-based and categorized memory-based (CMem-based) storage schemes. Then, the arithmetic improvement is extensively exploited for better area. Besides, the control unit is well-designed to reduce the hardware complexity. Equipped with these techniques, efficient LDPC-CC decoders for IEEE 1901 standard are developed and implemented with 55nm technology. Implementation results demonstrate that the proposed decoders can achieve more than twice the throughput of existing decoders. Furthermore, the proposed CMem-based decoder improves the area efficiency by 84.5%.
Index Terms—IEEE 1901, low-density parity-check convolutional codes (LDPC-CC), high throughput, storage categorization.
I. INTRODUCTION
L OW-DENSITY parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] are of two categories, LDPC block codes (LDPC-BCs) and LDPC convolutional codes (LDPC-CCs). The LDPC-BC, invented by Gallagar in 1960s, owing to its outstanding error correction performance, has been widely adopted by communication standards, e.g., IEEE 802.11, IEEE 802.15, DVB-S2, and 5G New Radio [2]–[5]. As the convolutional counterpart of the LDPCBC, the LDPC-CC was introduced by Felstrom and Zigangirov in 1999 [6]. The encoder of the LDPC-CC only requires simple registers, XOR logics, and multiplexers (muxes). Besides, the constraint length for LDPC-CC, which limits the maximum range of correlated bits, is usually shorter than the code length of LDPC-BC. This property ensures that LDPC-CCs usually possess a lower decoding complexity than LDPC-BCs.
The first decoding algorithm for LDPC-CC is based on the flooding schedule [6], similar to that in the LDPC-BC. Then, compared to the flooding schedule, the on-demand variable

Nevertheless, the layered schedule needs less storage than OVA, which ensures it the state-of-the-art decoding algorithm. The IEEE 1901 standard (1901 Std) [9] adopts LDPC-CC as an optional high-performance error correction code for the power line communication (PLC). The first LDPC-CC decoder for the 1901 Std is with layered schedule and memory-based first-in-first-out (FIFO) storage [10]. The memory access patterns in memory-based LDPC-CC decoders complying with 1901 Std are analyzed, the results of which verify that with proper window size the memory banks can be integrated to reduce power [11]. Due to the constraint of the memory bandwidth, existing 1901 Std layered decoders take three cycles to process each layer, which is time-consuming. In the field of LDPC codes, high-throughput decoders for wireless communication have been widely explored [12]–[15]. However, high-throughput decoders for PLC are relatively underexplored.
In this brief, we aim at designing high-throughput LDPCCC decoders fully compatible with 1901 Std for PLC, from three aspects of improvements.
• Storage improvement: To handle the constraint of the memory bandwidth, we propose two solutions, register-based (Reg-based) and categorized memorybased (CMem-based) storage schemes, both of which can achieve three times the bandwidth of the traditional storage scheme.
• Arithmetic improvement: We simplify the first processing element (PE) by exploiting the feature of the initialization step for hardware complexity reduction.
• Control improvement: Because of the synchronization of memory accesses, a global controller manages the entire decoding flow without the usage of local controllers. Besides, the design and improvement details of the address generator for CMem are provided. In addition, the judgment operations are simplified. The techniques for control improvement mainly target at area reduction.
The rest of the brief is organized as follows. Section II introduces the background. In Section III, the detailed storage, arithmetic, and control designs of the proposed decoders are described. Section IV presents implementation results. At last, Section V draws the conclusion.

B. The Integrated Storage Organization
The state-of-the-art integrated storage organization [11] for layered decoding algorithm is depicted in Fig. 1, where the solid arrows and the dashed arrows represent signals involved in the read and write operations, respectively. The circles are connection points. Circles with the same color and dash type denote the same physical point. The memory-based storage utilizes memory to implement the FIFO. Layered decoding requires the storage for AP-LLR and C2V messages. Thus, the memory-based storage is of two parts, AP-LLR memory (AM) and C2V memory (CM). In order to support the maximum symbol length (b = 5), the AM consists of five banks. If the symbol length is less than 5, certain banks can be disabled for power savings. The size of each bank in AM is l×(Np·wL). The CM owns one bank, the size of which is l×((Np−1)·wcp). The memory access patterns are described as follows. 1) During AM write cycles, each PE produces b new AP-LLRs. Total Np ×b new AP-LLRs are concatenated and written to the AM. 2) In AM read cycles, an (Np × wL)-bit concatenated old APLLR is read from a memory bank. Total Np × b old AP-LLRs are dispatched and distributed to the PEs. To realize the shift operation, the AP-LLR input ports of each PE are connected to a mux controlled by sh. When sh is high, the muxes shift the inputs right and the leftmost PE takes in channel LLRs. 3) For CM write cycles, (Np−1) new C2V messages generated by PE 0∼(Np − 2) are combined and written into the CM. The new C2V message produced by the last PE is not stored. 4) During CM read cycles, the concatenated C2V message from the CM is decomposed, shifted right, and passed to the corresponding PEs. For more details of the background, please refer to [6], [10], [11].

III. PROPOSED DECODING ARCHITECTURE

A. Storage Design

The AM bandwidth limits the throughput of the LDPC-CC

decoder in 1901 Std. To address this issue, two methods,

Reg-based and CMem-based storage schemes, are introduced.

Reg-based storage employs shift registers to store AP-LLRs.

The input (output) ports of the Reg-based storage are directly

to (from) the D-ports (Q-ports) of the registers. An example of the Reg-based storage of ZWts 0 (D) for a PE is shown in Fig. 2, where each square denotes a wL-bit register and the

number above each register denotes the index. The factors of

the delay terms determine the positions of the input and output ports. The total Reg-based storage of ZWts 0 (D) consists of Np components shown in Fig. 2 connected end to end. In each

cycle, 3 × Np needed AP-LLRs can be obtained from the Regbased storage of ZWts 0 (D), whereas only Np AP-LLRs can be obtained from an AM bank. Therefore, the storage bandwidth

triples by employing Reg-based storage scheme. The memory

categorization scheme for CMem-based storage is stated and

proved in Theorem 1.

Theorem 1: Read n data from addresses a0(t), a1(t), . . . , an−1(t) of a memory bank, t ∈ [0, +∞]. Condition 1: for a given t, (a0(t)%m) ∩ (a1(t)%m) ∩ · · · ∩ (an−1(t)%m) = ∅, m ≥ n, where % is the modulo operator. Condition 2: ∀t ∈ [0, +∞] meets condition 1. If the read

addresses satisfy condition 2, then the memory bank can be

divided properly into m partitioned banks, from which n data

is read out in each cycle.

Proof: Assume the depth of the original memory bank is l.

Denote the addresses in the original memory bank as {ri|i ∈ [0, l − 1]}. The data in the address set ϕj = {r0j , r1j , . . . , rljj−1} are allocated to the categorized memory bank (CMB) j, of

which lj is the depth, j ∈ [0, m − 1]. A possible classification

method is described below. Restore the word at address ri into
the CMB (ri modulo m), i.e., ri ∈ ϕ(ri%m). Data to be fetched are now in CMB (a0(t)%m), (a1(t)%m), . . . , (an−1(t)%m).

Since condition 2, n data are in n seperate CMBs and can

be popped out in a cycle without conflict.

The Min1 Min2 Finder calculates the first two minima and the index of the minimum from 15 inputs with a tree structure [17]. The pipeline register in the Min1 Min2 Finder is right before the last connection unit, details of which can be referred to [17].
In terms of PE level arithmetic, previous LDPC-CC decoders for 1901 Std use the same architecture for all PEs, except that the last PE includes extra hard decision unit. Based on the characteristics of the algorithm, we simplify the architecture of PE 0. Due to the zero-initialized Qcn→vi , all modules in the first stage could be eliminated. This technique achieves hardware complexity reduction. The designs of SM2TC, Neg, and TC2SM modules are based on [18]–[20].
C. Control Design
Since the window width l = 228 is divisible by 3, all memory access patterns among PEs are in synchronization [11]. Thus, we design a global controller to conquer all PEs. The global controller mainly consists of a frequency divider, a ts counter (cnt), shi signals, and address generators for AM, CM. V. CONCLUSION In this brief, we have proposed high-throughput LDPC decoders conforming to the 1901 Std. The VLSI designs for the proposed decoders are improved from storage, arithmetic, and control perspectives. First, to tackle the throughput bottleneck caused by memory bandwidth, two resolutions, Reg-based and CMem-based storage schemes, are presented. Besides, we carry out arithmetic improvement to lower the hardware complexity. In addition, the design and improvement details of the global controller are discussed. The implementation results show that, compared with existing 1901 Std decoders, not only do our proposed decoders have a significantly higher throughput, but they are also more area efficient.
