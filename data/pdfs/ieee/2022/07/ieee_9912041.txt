High-Throughput VLSI Architecture for LDPC Decoder Based on Low-Latency Decoding
Technique for Wireless Communication Systems

Kumari Suravi‚ÄÝ and Rahul Shrestha¬ß, Senior Member, IEEE
School of Computing and Electrical Engineering, Indian Institute of Technology Mandi ksuravi@qti.qualcomm.com‚ÄÝ; rahul shrestha@iitmandi.ac.in‚Ä°.

Abstract‚ÄîThis paper proposes hardware-friendly and lowlatency technique for min-sum based LDPC-decoding algorithm with flooding schedule. It enhances the data independency of LDPC decoding algorithm by mitigating dependency among the column slices. Parallel VLSI-architecture of LDPC decoder based on the proposed decoding algorithm has been presented here. Subsequently, performance analysis of such algorithm in AWGNchannel environment has shown that it delivers adequate BER of 10‚àí5 at 1.2 dB of channel-SNR. Furthermore, ASIC synthesis and post-layout simulation of our design are performed in 130 nm-CMOS technology-node. Thus, it occupies a core area of 6.02 mm2 and operates at maximum clock frequency of 217 MHz. The proposed LDPC decoder is capable of delivering a highthroughput of 36 Gbps per iteration. Eventually, aforementioned results are compared with the relevant contemporary works where our design achieved 85.64% higher throughput and 41.02% better energy-efficiency in comparison with the stateof-the-art implementation.
Index Terms‚ÄîWireless network, LDPC code, channel decoder, digital VLSI architectures, application-specific integrated-circuits (ASIC), and very-large scale integration (VLSI).
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) code is one of the most reliable and widely adopted codes due to its excellent near Shannon-limit error-correction performance [1]. Its lower decoding complexity is another salient feature that enables efficient decoder implementation. Aforementioned characteristics make LDPC codes to be widely adopted by the trending wireless standards like 5G-NR (fifth generation - new radio), IEEE 802.11ad (WiGig), IEEE 802.11n (WiFi), IEEE 802.16e (WiMAX) etc. [2]. These wireless standards demand highthroughput and energy-efficient LDPC decoders to meet their high-end specifications. For such LDPC decoding, min sum algorithm (MSA) with lower implementation complexity is preferred over original sum-product algorithm (SPA) [3]. They can be scheduled via two ways: flooding [2] and layered [4] based scheduling. Former is lesser memory intensive and possesses inherent parallelism due to data independency in comparison with the later one, at the cost of minor performance degradation [2].
Our work focuses on the flooding schedule based MSA and its VLSI architecture design. Recently, Milicevic et al. [5] presented frame-interleaved LDPC decoder-architecture based on novel interconnection partitioning scheme for MSA with flooding schedule. Subsequently, Lee et al. in [6] designed

layered schedule based LDPC decoder using the bit-serial first two minimum-value generator. Contemporary work of Usman et al. [7] suggested new fast-column message-passing schedule based decoder architecture for LDPC code. However, there still exists substantial scope to improve these significant parameters. Therefore, this paper proposes low-latency and hardware-friendly technique for the flooding schedulebased LDPC decoding algorithm. Its corresponding highthroughput and energy-efficient LDPC decoder-architecture has been presented in this paper. Additionally, performance analysis of the low-latency decoding algorithm is carried out in additive white-Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel environment. Eventually, ASIC design of the proposed decoder architecture has been performed in 130 nm-CMOS technology-node and its results are compared with the relevant start-of-the-art implementations.
II. PRE-REQUISITE
LDPC code is a linear block code that is characterized by sparse parity-check-matrix H ‚àà Cm√ón where m is the number of parity bits, n denotes the code-length, and (n‚àím)/n represents the code rate. Such H matrix can also be represented by a bipartite Tanner graph [3]. Its rows and columns represent check nodes (CNs) and variable nodes (VNs), respectively. Connection between these nodes denote the location of ones (i.e. 1s) in H matrix. Decoding of such LDPC codes is based on iterative message-passing technique in which the information transfers along the edges between CNs and VNs of the Tanner graph. In general, x VNs and y CNs are connected to ith CN and jth VN, respectively. Here, ith CN computes the message mcv that is transferred to connected VN. Subsequently, jth VN uses the received mcv for computing Lvc message which is transferred back to previous CN. Aforementioned message passing between the nodes can be performed via flooding or layered based scheduling. Further, LDPCdecoding algorithm is classified based on various computation methods of CN to VN messages (mcv). As discussed earlier, this paper focusses on MSA based LDPC decoding with flooding schedule and its conventional version is presented in Algorithm 1 where mcv computation is performed in step 3. Here, input channel logarithmic-likelihood-ratio (LLR) Qv is initialized to Lvc and mcv is computed using this value. Furthermore, VN computes Lvc based on mcv value in step 4

III. PROPOSED TECHNIQUE AND ARCHITECTURES

A. Hardware-Friendly LDPC Decoding-Technique

Conventional LDPC decoding is carried out by passing messages between check-node-units (CNUs) and variablenode-units (VNUs) [2]. In [5], Milicevic et al. presented a new LDPC decoder-architecture that combines both CN and VN processing into single unit, referred as CN+VN processing unit (CVPU). Here, each column is represented by a processing unit that computes minimums, mcv and Lvc values. Such computation of the minimums requires comparison between the successive columns where LLR of each processing unit is compared with the output of the previous one. Thus, computations performed for the column slices in CVPU are inter-dependent that surges the decoder latency and it linearly increases with the number of column slices in CVPU. On the other side, the proposed technique in our paper mitigates such snag and its schematic illustration is presented in Fig. 1 for H ‚àà C3√ó6 matrix (for the ease of understanding). Here, we eliminate the dependency among column slices where half of CNUs are separated from single CVPU. These CNUs are termed as min-block (MB) and the remaining CVPU is referred as LVC Block (LVC-B), as shown in Fig. 1. In the proposed technique, MB first computes two minimums (i.e. M in1 and M in2) and sign-bits (i.e. sgn) of LLRs, representing non-zero elements of each row in H matrix. D. Proposed OLB Micro-architecture
As shown in Fig. 4 (a), the proposed OLB comprises of 16 CCUs corresponding to the columns of B8√ó16 matrix. This work mitigates the dependencies of operations to eliminate the sequential processing of CNU which has been segregated into two parts. The first part has been performed by OMB (as presented in section III-C) and the second part is carried out by OLB. Specifically, suggested OLB-architecture eliminates dependencies between the columns of B-matrix (i.e. among the CCUs). Thus, all CCUs of OLB are independent that simultaneously perform their operations in a single clock cycle (i.e. computations of all mcv and Lvc values are computed in single clock-cycle). This enhances the throughput of our decoder architecture. Each CCU has been design using 42 LVC blocks, as presented in Fig. 4 (a). In every LVC block, one VN is connected to pc CNs where pc denotes number of nonnegative integers in each column of B matrix. Further, each LVC block comprises of 1 to 4 CNs which are connected to one VN, as shown in Fig. 4 (a). Since single VN is connected to maximum of four CNs, complexity of interconnect network is alleviated in our decoder design.
Using sm and r inputs, all CNs compute mcv values which are transferred to connected VN in the LVC block, which is fed with 2√ópc+1 inputs and subsequently generates pc+1 outputs. Proposed architecture of one of the 16 CCUs (i.e. CCU1) that encompasses 42 LVC blocks is shown in Fig. 4 (b). Here, micro-architecture of VN (i.e. VN1) consists of pc 2‚Äôscomplement-to-sign-magnitude (2C-SM) converter(s) at the output side and one sign-magnitude-to-2‚Äôs-complement (SM2C) converter at the input side, and pc+3 adders/subtractors. Consecutively, mcv values computed by CNs are added with input-LLR (Qv) to generate Lv values based on step 4 of Algorithm 1. Subsequently, C1 hard-decision bits from Lv is the final output (decoded bits) from our decoder. At the same time, each Lvc output-value is computed by subtracting mcv with Lv value, as shown in Fig. 4 (b). It is to be noted that the 2‚Äôs complement values are required for the algebraic computation and thereby, SM-2C converter has been used in the design of VN. Additionally, 2C-SM converters have been used for Lvc values because magnitude and sign bits must be separated for the computation of minimums in OMB. The reason begin, such Lvc values are fed back to OMB for the subsequent decoding iterations where minimum values are computed, as illustrated in Fig. 2.

IV. PERFORMANCE ANALYSIS, IMPLEMENTATION RESULTS, & COMPARISONS
Monte-Carlo simulation has been carried out to compute a bit-error-rate (BER) at every signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) for the performance analysis of LDPC decoding algorithms. Here, the LDPC code has a block-length of N =672 bit, coderate of 1/2, expansion factor of z=42, and it has been binary phase-shift-keying (BPSK) modulated as well as transmitted via AWGN channel.
