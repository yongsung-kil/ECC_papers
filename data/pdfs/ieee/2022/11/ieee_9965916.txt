Local Constraint-Based Ordered Statistics Decoding for Short Block Codes

Yiwen Wang, Jifan Liang, and Xiao Ma
School of Computer Science and Engineering, Sun Yat-sen University, Guangzhou 510006, P. R. China Guangdong Key Laboratory of Information Security Technology, Sun Yat-sen University, Guangzhou 510006, P. R. China
Email: wangyw93@mail2.sysu.edu.cn, liangjf56@mail2.sysu.edu.cn, maxiao@mail.sysu.edu.cn

Abstract—In this paper, we propose a new ordered statistics decoding (OSD) for linear block codes, which is referred to as local constraint-based OSD (LC-OSD). Distinguished from the conventional OSD, which chooses the most reliable basis (MRB) for re-encoding, the LC-OSD chooses an extended MRB on which local constraints are naturally imposed. A list of candidate codewords is then generated by performing a serial list Viterbi algorithm (SLVA) over the trellis specified with the local constraints. To terminate early the SLVA for complexity reduction, we present a simple criterion which monitors the ratio of the bound on the likelihood of the unexplored candidate codewords to the sum of the hard-decision vector’s likelihood and the up-to-date optimal candidate’s likelihood. Simulation results show that the LC-OSD can have a much less number of test patterns than that of the conventional OSD but cause negligible performance loss. Comparisons with other complexity-reduced OSDs are also conducted, showing the advantages of the LCOSD in terms of complexity.
Index Terms—List Viterbi decoding, ordered statistics decoding, short block codes, URLLC.

I. INTRODUCTION

W ITH the emergence of 5G, the ultra-reliable and low latency communication (URLLC), which focuses on

delay-sensitive applications and services, has attracted much

attention. This has in turn rekindled much interest in designing

channel codes with short block length [1] and their near

maximum likelihood decoding (MLD) algorithm such as the

ordered statistics decoding (OSD) algorithm [2]. The idea be-

hind the OSD is that errors typically occur in the least reliable

positions more often than in the most reliable positions. Thus,

by flipping a small number of bits in the most reliable ba-

sis (MRB) and re-encoding, a list of candidate codewords can

be generated. It has been proved in [2] that, for a linear block

code with minimum Hamming distance dmin, the OSD with the order of t = [dmin/4 − 1] can approach the MLD performance.

The main issue of the conventional OSD is that the order-

t OSD of a k-dimensional block code needs to generate a

list of candidate codewords with the size of

tk i=0 i

and

the overall complexity of the conventional OSD can be up

to O(kt). Several methods have been proposed to reduce the

complexity of the conventional OSD. The main approaches to

complexity-reduced OSD include skipping and/or discarding those unpromising or unlikely test patterns [3]–[5]. For this purpose, likelihood bounds and maximum likelihood (ML) conditions are analyzed and derived in [3]–[5]. These conditions can of course be used as criteria for early stopping. If so, the testing order can be optimized to reduce the average number of test patterns [6], [7], which can be implemented over a flipping pattern tree (FPT) similar to that presented in [8].
In this paper, we propose a new OSD for linear block codes, which is referred to as local constraint-based OSD (LC-OSD). Distinguished from the conventional OSD, which chooses the k most reliable positions as MRB for re-encoding, the LCOSD chooses the k + δ (δ ≥ 0) most reliable positions as an extended MRB. For δ > 0, any k + δ positions are linearly dependent and hence must satisfy some local constraints. By properly manipulating the parity-check matrix, the local constraints can be specified by a trellis. Then a serial list Viterbi algorithm (SLVA) [9] can be implemented to generate a list of candidate codewords. To terminate early the SLVA for complexity reduction, we present a simple criterion similar to that suggested in [10], which monitors the ratio of the bound on the likelihood of the unexplored candidate codewords to the sum of the hard-decision vector’s likelihood and the upto-date optimal candidate’s likelihood. More concretely, the SLVA terminates once the ratio reaches an off-line learned threshold. Simulation results show that the LC-OSD can have a much less number of test patterns than that of the conventional OSD but cause negligible performance loss. Comparisons with other complexity-reduced OSDs are also conducted, showing the advantages of the LC-OSD in terms of complexity.

B. Performance Comparison
In this subsection, we present simulation results to demonstrate the performance of the LC-OSD. The conventional OSD and the FPT-OSD are also implemented for comparison. All simulations are conducted by assuming BPSK modulation and AWGN channels.
1) Impact of Parameter of Stopping Criterion on the Performance:
Example 1: Consider a rate-1/2 (3,6) LDPC code C [128, 64]. The maximum list size lmax = 16384 and δ = 8. The performance of the LC-OSD with different α is shown in Fig. 1, where we observe that the LC-OSD with properly selected α exhibits almost the same frame-error rate (FER) compared to the conventional OSD. Table I shows the average number of test patterns for the LC-OSD with different α. We can see that the average number of test patterns increases with the increase of α. At low Eb/N0, more test patterns are required to recover the correct codeword since more errors are introduced by the channel.
Fig. 2 shows the performance of the FPT-OSD with different α. The average number of TEPs for the FPT-OSD with different α is shown in Table II. The FPT-OSD also exhibits almost the same FER compared to the conventional OSD

when α = 0.6, 0.7 and 0.8. Evidently, to minimize the average number of test patterns, we can choose different α for different Eb/N 0. For simplicity, to make a trade-off between performance and complexity, we choose α = 0.6 for the LCOSD and the FPT-OSD in the following comparison.
2) Impact of Length of Extended MRB on the Performance: Example 2: Consider a rate-1/2 regular (3,6) LDPC code C [128, 64]. Fig. 1 shows the performance of the LC-OSD with different δ. Here α = 0.6 and lmax = 16384. We can see that the LC-OSD exhibits almost the same FER compared to the conventional OSD when δ = 8 and δ = 12. The longer the MRB length, the more bits are constrained, and the more performance improvement is obtained accordingly. However, when δ exceeds 8, the performance improvement is saturated. The average number of test patterns decreases with the increase of δ and hence the increase of the complexity caused by the SLVA.

IV. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we have proposed a new OSD for linear block codes, which is referred to as LC-OSD. We selected an extended MRB for re-encoding, on which local constraints

are imposed. Two techniques were used in the LC-OSD: 1) the SLVA is performed over the trellis specified by a partial paritycheck matrix to generate a list of candidate codewords; 2) a stopping criterion, which monitors an upper bound on the likelihood of the unexplored candidate codewords, is presented to terminate the SLVA early.
We have analyzed the performance and complexity of the LC-OSD. The simulation results showed that: 1) the LC-OSD can achieve similar performance compared to the conventional OSD with a much less number of test patterns while selecting properly the length of the extended MRB and the parameter of stopping criterion; 2) the LC-OSD can be applied to any short linear block code; 3) the LC-OSD has a much less number of test patterns than other complexity-reduced OSDs but with negligible performance loss.
