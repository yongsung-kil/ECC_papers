A Semi Linear State Space Model for Error Floor Estimation of LDPC Codes Over the AWGN Channel

Ali Farsiabi and Amir H. Banihashemi , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract— In this paper, we propose a novel state-space model to represent the behavior of sum-product algorithm (SPA) in the vicinity of a trapping set (TS) of a low-density parity-check (LDPC) code over the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel in the error floor region. The proposed model takes into account the non-linear behavior of SPA in the initial iterations using a quadratic approximation, and dynamically adjusts the operating point of the model in accordance to the statistical properties of TS messages. This is in contrast to the existing linear state-space models which linearly approximate the non-linear behavior of SPA at around the operating point of zero in all iterations. Simulation results are provided to demonstrate the higher accuracy of the proposed model in estimating the error floor of LDPC codes compared to the linear state-space model. We also make connections to the semi-analytical code-dependent technique proposed by Richardson for the error floor estimation of LDPC codes, and demonstrate that the proposed state-space model not only is a fully-analytical code-independent counterpart to that method, but also has less complexity and, in some cases, more accuracy.
Index Terms— LDPC codes, error floor, error floor estimation, trapping sets (TS), elementary trapping sets (ETS), leafless elementary trapping sets (LETS), importance sampling, density evolution, state space model.

The error floor estimation/analysis techniques in the literature are generally divided into two main categories: the code-dependent and the code-independent methods. In the first category, the TSs are analysed as part of the Tanner graph of a specific code. In the second category, however, they are studied in isolation. The majority of works within the first category are based on importance sampling (IS) techniques, see, e.g., [1], [3], [4], [5], [7]. The works in the second category include the analysis of TSs under hard-decision decoding algorithms over the binary symmetric channel (BSC) based on critical number [42], [43], the error floor estimation of sum-product algorithm (SPA) over the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel based on a linear state-space model [2], [8], [11], introduction of absorbing set threshold for quantized min-sum algorithms (MSAs) [44], [45], the derivation of a lower bound for the error rate in quantized decoders [46], and the error floor estimation technique of [13] that is applicable to any saturating iterative message-passing decoder, symmetrically quantized or unquantized, over any memoryless binary-input output-symmetric channel.1 The fact that the harmfulness of TSs in the second category of techniques is estimated independent of the code is interesting as it implies that two isomorphic TSs in two different codes with the same degree distributions are equally harmful. Obviously, the two codes can have different error floors as they can differ in the multiplicities of the TSs. In general, the code-independent methods of error floor estimation are faster than the code-dependent ones, see, e.g., [13].
Among the code-independent techniques, the linear state-space model used in [2], [8], and [11] is of particular importance due to its analytical nature. In [2], Sun proposed a linear state-space model to analyze the dynamics of elementary TSs (ETSs)2 in the error floor region of LDPC codes over the AWGN channel. This model was based on the assumption that the decoder behavior outside an ETS can be determined by density evolution (DE) [47]. II. PRELIMINARIES
A. Notations
In this paper, matrices and vectors are denoted by boldfaced upper case and lower case letters, respectively. An exception is the LLR vectors that are denoted with the symbol Ł with different subscripts or superscripts. All the vectors are assumed to be column vectors. A list of the notations and symbols used in this paper is provided in Table I.

B. LDPC Codes and TSs
We consider the application of binary LDPC codes over the AWGN channel with noise variance σc2h using binary phaseshift-keying (BPSK) modulation in which the code bits 0 and 1 are mapped to +1 and −1 at the input of the channel, respectively. We consider the application of SPA for decoding, and without loss of generality, to analyze the error floor, we assume the all-zero codeword is transmitted.
It is well-known that the error floor of LDPC codes is due to the existence of certain substrucutures in the Tanner graph of the codes, collectively referred to as trapping sets (TSs). It is said that a TS belongs to (a, b) class if it has a VNs and b odd-degree CNs. Among the TSs, those with only degree1 and degree-2 CNs, known as elementary TSs (ETSs), are the most harmful ones [11], [12]. The CNs of a TS with even and odd degrees are referred to as missatisfied and unsatisfied, respectively. If the VNs of an ETS are connected to at least 2 missatisfied CNs, the ETS is called leafless elementary TS (LETS) [12]. Similar to most existing literature including [8], [11], in this work also, we only consider LETSs in estimating the error floor of LDPC codes. Moreover, similar to [8], [11], we focus on TSs that are stable, i.e., all the VNs of the TS are eventually in error as the number of iterations increases. This is referred to as TS failure.

IV. PROPOSED SEMI-LINEAR MODEL WITH DYNAMICAL OPERATING POINT
A. Main Ideas and Methodology
One of the main factors that affects the accuracy of the Taylor expansion of a non-linear function is the choice of operating point around which the function is approximated (expanded). A proper choice can significantly improve the accuracy of the approximation. For the case of the approximation of the missatisfied CN operation, which is of interest in this paper, the function inputs are random, and a good choice is not only dependent on the iteration number but also depends on individual missatisfied CNs within the TS. To simplify the method, in this paper, however, we ignore the latter dependency, and dynamically find the operating point for all missatisfied CNs of the TS at iteration as the weighted average of internal messages of the TS at iteration −1. In this process of averaging, it is essential to capture the most likely inputs that cause TS failures with a reasonable complexity. For this, we develop our state-space model with a condition on the noise vector, hence the term “conditional state-space model.”
The first order Taylor expansion of missatisfied CN functions results in a linear state-space model. In order to increase the accuracy of the approximations, we will use the second order Taylor expansion in the first few iterations and first order expansion for the remaining iterations. Thus, our model is called “semi-linear.” The reason for a higher order Taylor expansion at early iterations is that for those iterations, the extrinsic inputs to missatisfied CNs have not sufficiently grown yet, and as a result, their size is comparable to that of internal messages. This implies that the relative degree of non-linearity of missatisfied CNs at early iterations is higher.

C. Modifications to the Proposed Model
In this part, we study the asymptotic behavior of the model proposed in the previous subsection and explain its shortcomings in properly predicting the conditional failure probability of a TS. We then propose modifications to the model to address the shortcomings.
In general, our experimental results and those reported in other literature [11] show that when the decoder is trapped in a stable TS, the size of the internal messages of the TS grow with iterations. On the other hand, for a saturating decoder, the external messages entering the missatisfied CNs (through virtual VNs) also grow with iterations until they saturate at some value y0. D. Relationship Between Our Proposed Method and the Semi-Analytical Method of [1]
In a pioneering work [1], Richardson proposed a semianalytical code-dependent technique based on importance sampling for the error floor estimation of LDPC codes. In this method, the noise is biased in the direction of the TS by a certain (varying) amount, θ . Then, by using an orthogonal transform on the i.i.d Gaussian noise samples within the TS, the noise vector is represented with a new set of basis vectors, where one vector is in the direction of the TS. This allows for the bias value θ to be treated as a Gaussian random variable. Fixing θ , Monte Carlo (MC) simulation is then performed with respect to the other dimensions of the noise vector. The final estimate of the error floor is obtained by averaging the conditional failure rates over the Gaussian bias values, θ .
We note that the method of [1] for calculating the total failure probability by averaging over the bias value is equivalent to using Equation (22). The major difference, however, is that unlike our analytical approach in calculating Pe{S|θ }, the method of [1] estimates Pe{S|θ } for different values of θ using MC simulations.

VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we proposed a novel state-space model for the behavior of SPA over the AWGN channel in the vicinity of an ETS. We then used this model to evaluate the failure probability of ETSs of an LDPC code and to estimate the code’s error floor. Unlike the well-known linear state-space model of an ETS, the proposed model takes into account the inherent non-linearity of CN operations in the early iterations of SPA. Moreover, rather than using a Taylor expansion around zero to approximate the CN operations, the operating point of the expansion in the proposed model is nonzero and is chosen dynamically in each iteration. As a result, the proposed model is more accurate in estimating the error floor, particularly for cases where the clipping threshold of the decoder is rather large. It is important to note that the improved accuracy comes at negligible cost in complexity.
We demonstrated that the proposed semi-linear state-space model with dynamical operating point can be considered as a code-independent fully-analytical counterpart to the codedependent semi-analytical method of Richardson [1].6 We also showed that, in addition to having the advantage of being code-independent and fully-analytical, our method with extrapolation is both less complex and more accurate than the method of [1], particularly for cases where decoder messages have a large dynamic range.
