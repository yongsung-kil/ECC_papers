Parallel Decoding of PIC-LDPC Codes Aided by
Free-Ride Coding
Fanhui Meng∗, Qianfan Wang†, Suihua Cai‡, and Xiao Ma‡ ∗School of Systems Science and Engineering, Sun Yat-sen University, Guangzhou 510006, China †School of Electronics and Communication Engineering, Sun Yat-sen University, Guangzhou 510006, China ‡School of Computer Science and Engineering, Sun Yat-sen University, Guangzhou 510006, China

Abstract—In this paper, we apply free-ride coding, a recently proposed technique, to the partial information coupling (PIC) low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. Such an integration admits parallel decoding of PIC-LDPC codes, suggesting that high-speed decoders can be implemented. At the transmitter, the coupled information bits are periodically sampled and transmitted by the free-ride coding. At the receiver, these free-ride coded bits are first decoded and then used to decouple the whole PICLDPC coded chain into multiple shorter chains, each of which can be decoded separately and in parallel. This benefits from the fact that free-ride coded bits typically have higher reliability than others, as confirmed by the numerical results. Numerical results also show that the word error rate (WER) performance of the proposed codes is close to the genie-aided (GA) lower bound in the high SNR region.
Index Terms—Free-ride codes, high throughput, PIC-LDPC codes, transport block.
I. INTRODUCTION
The partial information coupling (PIC) technique was presented in [1–3] to improve the performance, where a few information bits between every two adjacent code blocks (CBs) are shared for encoding but transmitted only once by puncturing. Based on the PIC technique, a chained LDPC code, referred to as PIC-LDPC code, was presented [2], in which the feedforward and feed-back (FF-FB) decoding manner can spread reliable messages from the CBs at the two ends of the chain to other CBs. To reduce decoding latency and complexity, the PIC-LDPC code can be decoded by an iterative slidingwindow (SW) decoding algorithm. However, the SW decoding algorithm is subjected to low throughput since only one CB is decoded per sliding window.
Assuming that the decoder knows not only dummy blocks at the head and tail but also the coupled bits periodically. Then, a long PIC-LDPC coded chain can be decoupled into multiple shorter chains. Evidently, we can perform the decoding algorithm in parallel for each short chain, and the “known” bits of each short chain’s head and tail ensure a good performance as well. In addition, all CBs in each short chain can be decoded simultaneously. This motivates us to construct a new PIC-LDPC codes that can greatly enhance the throughput, by introducing periodically “known” bits (like doping bits for the spatially coupled LDPC codes [4–6]). However, it usually takes extra bandwidth or extra transmission power to transmit these doping bits. Fortunately, a new technique, referred to as

free-ride coding, was proposed in [7, 8] to transmit extra bits on the coded payload link without any cost of extra bandwidth or transmission power. Based on the free-ride coding, a few coupling LDPC codes, say, implicit globally-coupled LDPC codes, implicit product-LDPC codes and terminated spatiallycoupled LDPC codes, have been constructed [9–11].
Inspired by above works, in this paper, we propose a PICLDPC codes with free-ride coding, which can enhance the throughput of the original PIC-LDPC codes. At the transmitter, the coupled information bits are periodically sampled and transmitted by using the free-ride coding. At the receiver, after recovering the sampled information bits, the long PICLDPC coded chain is decoupled into multiple shorter chains. All shorter chains can be decoded in parallel and all CBs within a short chain can be delivered simultaneously. The numerical results show that the presented PIC-LDPC codes can outperform the benchmark LDPC codes. They also show that the word error rate (WER) performance of the proposed codes is close to the genie-aided (GA) lower bound in the high SNR region.

B. Free-Ride Codes
The free-ride codes, which were proposed in [7, 8], enable us to transmit a few extra bits over an existing coded transmission link without any cost of extra transmission energy or extra bandwidth. The basic idea is to transmit the extra bits over the original coded link in a superposition (XOR) manner, where the coded length keeps unchanged in comparison with the original coded link. Concretely, let u ∈ Fk2 be the payload data to be transmitted. Let Cp[n, k] be an LDPC code, referred to as the payload code. When there are no extra bits to be transmitted, u is encoded into a codeword v ∈ F2n such that vHT = 0 by the encoding algorithm of Cp[n, k], where H denotes the parity-check matrix of Cp[n, k]. After the codeword v is transmitted over a noisy channel, the receiver recovers the payload data u by performing the decoding algorithm of Cp[n, k] from a received sequence.

Now we assume that, besides the payload data, a few extra bits f of length ℓ ≪ k need to be transmitted. To implement it, one can build an extra coded link to transmit the extra bits, which requires extra bandwidth and transmission power. An alternative is to construct a new code [n, k + ℓ], which is inconvenient since it requires change the encoder for the payload transmission. Fortunately, the extra bits can be transmitted along with the payload data using the free-ride coding [7, 8] without any extra consumption of transmission power or bandwidth. Specifically, in this paper, we use ReedMuller (RM) codes to construct the free-ride codes Cf [n, ℓ]. Formally, let GRM denote a generator matrix of the RM code. The generator matrix Gf of the free-ride can be derived from
Gf HT = GRM.
We assume that the first m = n − k columns (denoted collectively by B) of H are linearly independent. In this case, the Gf can be obtained by
Gf = [GRM(B−1)T, 0ℓ×k].
Given the generator matrix Gf , the encoding and decoding process of free-ride coding are described as follows.
• Encoding: Firstly, by performing the encoding algorithm of Cp[n, k], the payload data u ∈ Fk2 is encoded into an LDPC codeword v ∈ F2n. Secondly, the extra bits f ∈ F2ℓ are encoded by the free-ride code Cf [n, ℓ], resulting in a free-ride codeword g = f Gf ∈ F2n. The transmitted codeword c can be calculated by
c = v + g,
where “+” is the addition over F2n. • Decoding: Upon receiving the noisy version y of c,
the receiver attempts to decode both the payload data u and extra bits f . This can be achieved by employing a successive cancellation decoder [8]. Firstly, the receiver utilizes the free-ride decoder to obtain the estimation f from the soft syndrome associated with y. Then, by removing the effect of estimated free-ride codeword

III. PIC-LDPC CODES WITH FREE-RIDE CODING
As presented in Sec. II-A, the WER performance of the LDPC code can be improved by using the PIC-LDPC code. However, the decoding throughput is relatively low when the SW decoding algorithm is performed, where only one CB is decoded for a given window. In this paper, we present a new PIC-LDPC code with parallel decoding by using the freeride coding. At the transmitter, coupled information bits are periodically selected and implicitly transmitted over the LDPC codewords. At the receiver, these selected coupled information bits are firstly decoded using the decoding algorithm of the free-ride coding. Then, the long PIC-LDPC coded chain is decoupled into multiple short chain and all the short chains can be decoded in parallel. In addition, all CBs within a short chain can be decoded simultaneously. These aforementioned decoding mechanism admits a high decoding throughput.

C. Lower Bound of PIC-LDPC Codes with Free-Ride Coding
To analyze the performance, we present the lower bound for the presented PIC-LDPC codes by using genie-aided (GA) approach [13]. Obviously, the performance of the presented PIC-LDPC codes can not be better than that in the case when the periodically sampled information bits are known for the decoder. This motivates us to present a GA lower bound by simulating the LDPC codes with known bits. Without loss of generality, we assume that ks = 2k′ coded bits are told by a genie to the decoder, and the remained n − ks coded bits need to be recovered.
Example 3: The mother code is a rate-1/2 (3,6)-regular LDPC code of length 1024, where the coupled information length k′ = 64. We present two GA lower bounds for the PIC-LDPC codes. The first one is the PIC-LDPC code with periodically known information bits, where the period is T = 8. The second one is the LDPC code with ks = 128 known bits. Fig. 6 shows the WER performances of the PICLDPC codes, the PIC-LDPC codes with GA and the LDPC code with ks = 128 known bits, where we can observe that the performance of the PIC-LDPC codes match well with the first lower bound and approach the second lower bound as the SNR increases.

V. CONCLUSIONS
This paper has presented a new PIC-LDPC code with free-ride coding, where the coupled information bits are periodically transmitted by using the free-ride coding. The long PIC-LDPC coded chain can be decoupled into multiple shorter chains after recovering these free-ride coded bits. This mechanism can admit high decoding throughput by performing the decoding in parallel. Our simulation results have shown that the PIC-LDPC codes with free-ride coding can outperform the original PIC-LDPC codes both in throughput and decoding complexity. They have also shown that the WER performance of the presented codes is close to the GA lower bounds in the high SNR region.
