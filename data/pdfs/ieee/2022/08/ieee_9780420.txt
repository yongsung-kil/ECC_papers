Construction of QC-LDPC Codes from Sidon Sequence Using Permutation and Segmentation

Lintao Zhang and Juhua Wang

Abstract— By utilizing a Sidon sequence, two new methods are proposed to construct quasi-cyclic (QC) low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes. The first method yields a QC-LDPC code without 4-cycles and the second method gives a QC-LDPC code without 4-cycles and 6-cycles. In the literature there are two methods which employ mathematical objects somewhat resembling a Sidon sequence to remove 4-cycles, and there are two explicit constructions which guarantee the absence of 4-cycles and 6-cycles. Compared with the former, our first method is much more flexible in selecting parameters while providing identical or better performance. Compared with the latter, our second method provides much better performance.
Index Terms— Circulant permutation matrix (CPM), cycle, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, quasi-cyclic (QC).
I. INTRODUCTION
L OW-DENSITY parity-check (LDPC) codes are a class of channel codes with excellent performance by using iterative decoding algorithms. As a special type of LDPC codes, quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes [1] possess the additional advantage of easy hardware implementation of encoders and decoders, and hence have found wide applications in a variety of communication systems [2], [3]. It is generally believed that short cycles should be avoided to achieve good decoding performance for QC-LDPC codes [4]–[12]. QCLDPC codes without small cycles are generally constructed based on complex mathematical tools, such as finite fields and BIBDs. Limited by the strict relationships among parameters in these tools, the typical lengths of the resulting QC-LDPC codes must be a multiple of a prime power, which restricts the application of QC-LDPC codes to some extent.
Based on a special sequence, referred to as a Sidon sequence, two novel classes of QC-LDPC codes without cycles of length four and without cycles of length up to six are proposed respectively in this letter, which have flexible lengths not limited to a multiple of a prime power. A difference set and a difference family are more or less related to a Sidon sequence, and they have been employed in two schemes [4], [5] to construct QC-LDPC codes without cycles of length four. Compared with the two schemes, the first new class possesses the flexibility in choosing rates and lengths, while yielding better or identical performance. On the other hand, compared with two existing explicit constructions [11], [12] which lead

II. PRELIMINARIES
A. QC-LDPC Codes and Cycles
A (J, L)-regular LDPC code is defined as the null space of a parity-check matrix (PCM) in which each column has J ones and each row has L ones. A QC-LDPC code is a special LDPC code whose PCM is an R×C array of P ×P circulants. A circulant is a binary square matrix where each row is a cyclic shift of the row above. Obviously, a circulant may be a circulant permutation matrix (CPM), a zero matrix (ZM) or a summation of several CPMs. For a QC-LDPC code whose PCM consists of CPMs and possibly ZMs, its PCM can be represented by the circulant size P and an R × C matrix E (referred to as an exponent matrix) composed of integers and a particular symbol ∞. The symbol ∞ corresponds to a P × P ZM, and an integer e corresponds to a P × P identity matrix with rows cyclically shifted to the right by e (mod P ) positions. Denote the PCM associated with E and P by H(E, P ). For a QC-LDPC code, a cycle of length 2l (denoted by 2l-cycle) can be expressed by an equation modulo P , where the left hand side (LHS) has 2l integers connected alternately with plus and minus signs and the right hand side (RHS) is zero [1].
B. Sidon Sequences
A Sidon sequence S over ZP , is a sequence consisting of N integers {s0, s1, · · · , sN−1} such that all the sums si + sj(i ≤ j) (mod P ) are different. Sidon sequences can be algebraically constructed [13] or randomly generated [6]. The following property of Sidon sequences is obvious.
Property 1: (i) all elements (modulo P ) within a Sidon sequence are different; (ii) [7] if a + b = c + d (mod P ) where a, b, c, d ∈ S, then either the two equations (a = c (mod P ), b = d (mod P )) hold true or the two equations (a = d (mod P ), b = c (mod P )) hold true.
According to the definitions in [4], [5], it is clear that (i) a difference set with a certain parameter is a special Sidon sequence; (ii) a difference set is a difference family with only one set; and (iii) each set in a difference family with a certain parameter is a Sidon sequence. Unlike the other two objects, a Sidon sequence allows a flexible choice of P .

C. Choice of Decimation
How to select a feasible tuple {d0, d1, · · · , dK−1} in Theorem 1 is discussed in this subsection.
Lemma 1: Let N ≥ 3 be an odd number. Represent N by a product of prime numbers, i.e., N = p0 · p1 · · · pn−1, where pi (0 ≤ i ≤ n − 1) is a prime number and 3 ≤ p0 ≤ p1 ≤ · · · ≤ pn−1. Then (i) if K = p0 − 1, there exists at least one K-tuple {d0, d1, · · · , dK−1} such that DC is satisfied; and (ii) if K = p0, there exists no K-tuple satisfying DC.
Proof: Obviously, the tuple {1, 2, · · · , p0 − 1} satisfies DC and hence (i) of Lemma 1 holds true. Next, we prove (ii) of Lemma 1. Suppose that {d0, d1, · · · , dp0−1} is a p0-tuple. Then {d0, d1, · · · , dp0−1}mod p0 must either include a zero entry, say di = 0 (mod p0), which is against gcd(di, N ) = 1 or include two identical entries, say di = dj (mod p0), which is against gcd(dj − di, N ) = 1. Therefore, it is impossible for a p0-tuple to meet DC.
Lemma 2: For an odd integer N ≥ 3, the three pairs {1, (N +1)/2}, {(N −1)/2, (N +1)/2} and {(N −1)/2, N − 1} all satisfy DC. In addition, the three series of pairs {2i, 2i+1} (2i+1 < N ), {2i, N − 2i} (2i < N ) and {N − 2i+1, N − 2i} (2i+1 < N ) satisfy DC as well.
Proof: The equation gcd(x, y) = gcd(x, y − x) where x and y are integers is utilized in the proof. We first consider the pair of {1, (N + 1)/2}. Since gcd(1, N ) = 1, gcd((N + 1)/2, N ) = gcd((N + 1)/2, N − 2[(N + 1)/2]) = 1 and gcd((N + 1)/2 − 1, N ) = gcd((N + 1)/2 − 1, N − 2[(N + 1)/2 − 1]) = 1, the pair {1, (N + 1)/2} satisfies DC. The other two pairs and the foregoing three series of pairs can be similarly proved.

VII. CONCLUSION
Novel methods are proposed to construct QC-LDPC codes without short cycles, via applying the operation of permutation and segmentation to a Sidon sequence. Comparison and analysis show that the new codes not only offer flexible circulant sizes, but also exhibit good performance. How to utilize the attribute of a Sidon sequence to eliminate 8-cycles or even small-size trapping sets is worthy of further investigation.
