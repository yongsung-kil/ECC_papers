Design of LUT-Based LDPC Decoders for Spin-Transfer Torque Magnetic Random Access Memory
Chatuporn Duangthong , Watid Phakphisut , and Pornchai Supnithi School of Engineering, King Mongkut’s Institute of Technology Ladkrabang, Bangkok 10520, Thailand

Recently, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have been proposed to improve the reliability of spin-transfer torque magnetic random access memory (STT-MRAM). While several research works focus on the design of LDPC codes to combat the process variation and thermal fluctuation of the STT-MRAM, we aim to tackle the high decoding latency issues by the novel design of a lookup table (LUT)-based LDPC decoder for the forward and backward (FB) processes. In this work, the channel quantizer and LUT operations of the LDPC decoder are jointly designed by minimizing the error probability estimated from the density evolution (DE) algorithm. The simulation results show that the proposed design method for a code rate of 0.9 outperforms the previous methods in terms of bit error rate (BER) performance. Furthermore, the proposed design can support the channel model of the STT-MRAM without an independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) channel adapter.
Index Terms— Lookup table (LUT) operations, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, spin-transfer torque magnetic random access memory (STT-MRAM).

I. INTRODUCTION
S PIN-TRANSFER torque magnetic random access memory (STT-MRAM) is one of the potential candidates to replace a dynamic random access memory (DRAM) [1] due to its nonvolatility and high read/write speed. However, the process variation and thermal fluctuations influence the reliability of STT-MRAM [2], [3]. As a result, several research works use low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes to improve the reliability of STT-MRAM. Li et al. [4] proposed an A-LDPC code for the asymmetric channel of STT-MRAM. Simulation results show that the A-LDPC code outperforms the existing error-correction codes for single-level cell (SLC) and multi-level cell (MLC) STT-MRAM. However, to achieve the powerful error-correction capability of the LDPC codes, they must be designed by considering the channel characteristics. In [5], the rate-compatible (RCP) LDPC codes have been proposed to improve the reliability of STT-MRAM. Since STT-MRAM exhibits an asymmetric channel, an independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) channel adapter is applied to transform the channel output. protograph EXIT (P-EXIT) chart and asymptotic weight enumerator (AWE) analysis are adopted to design the RCP-LDPC codes for STT-MRAM. The RCP-LDPC codes outperform the rate-compatible AR4JA protograph codes and the fixed-rate QC LDPC codes in terms of error rate performance and convergence speed.
In general, STT-MRAM has shown a significant advantage in read/write latency. Therefore, we consider the lowcomplexity LDPC decoder to simplify the hardware implementation. The LUT-based LDPC decoders [6]–[11] are one of the simplest decoders, which employ the table lookup operations instead of arithmetic operations. Meidlinger et al. [6] proposed the lookup table (LUT) operations and simplified arithmetic operations for variable nodes and check nodes.

The LUT operations are designed by maximizing mutual information. Furthermore, Meidlinger et al. [7] proposed the joint design of the LDPC code and LUT-based decoder. The simulation results show that the joint design of the LDPC code and LUT-based decoder outperforms the individual design. The information bottleneck method (IBM) has been proposed in [8] and [9] to design the LUT operations for check nodes and variable nodes. The IBM can be viewed as an alternative optimization approach based on maximizing the mutual information. Stark et al. [10] proposed the message alignment to arrange the LUT operations for irregular LDPC codes. The LUT-based decoder with message alignment provides better BER performance compared to the design without message alignment. Instead of mutual information, the density evolution (DE) technique [11] was proposed to design the LUT-based decoder. This method offers the joint design of the channel quantizer and LUT-based decoder. The simulation results show that DE technique outperforms the IBM [9] for 2-, 3-, and 4-bit quantizations.
To design the LUT-based LDPC decoder for STT-MRAM, there are two considerations from an implementation perspective: the decoding latency and the asymmetric channel. In [6]–[11], the LUT operations of check nodes and variable nodes are performed sequentially. This procedure requires the additional latency to compute all messages of the check nodes and variable nodes. Therefore, the main contribution of this work is to design the novel LUT-based LDPC decoder for the forward and backward (FB) processes to reduce the decoding latency. This process offers the semi-parallel computation of the log-likelihood ratio (LLR). We propose to use the DE technique to jointly design both the channel quantizer and LUT-based decoder. The simulation results show that the bit error rate (BER) performances of the FB process are identical to those of the sequential process. In addition, our design offers better BER performance than IBM. For an asymmetric channel issue, we will show that our proposed design can support the asymmetric channel of STT-MRAM. The proposed LUT-based decoder in STT-MRAM with and without i.i.d. channel adapter has the comparable identical performance.

II. CHANNEL MODEL OF STT-MRAM
A. Cascaded Channel Model
A magnetic tunneling junction (MTJ) and an nMOS transistor are two essential components of STT-MRAM [2], [3]. In the MTJ, a tunneling oxide layer is placed between two ferromagnetic layers. One ferromagnetic layer, called the reference layer, has a fixed magnetic direction, whereas the other, called the free layer, has a varied magnetization direction. When the switching current is applied to the MTJ, the magnetization direction of the free layer will be changed. If the magnetization directions of the free layer and reference layer are parallel (P), MTJ shows low resistance, corresponding to bit “0.” On the other hand, if the free layer and reference layer are anti-parallel (AP), MTJ presents high resistance, corresponding to bit “1.” Due to the process variation and thermal fluctuation, the required switching current is not constant, that is, writing bit “1” may require a greater amplitude of switching current than formal amplitude. In [12], the writing process of STT-MRAM is described by binary asymmetric channel (BAC). In the reading process, the read current is applied to MTJ. This process can flip the data bits stored in MTJ. For example, if the switching current with the write direction of bit “1” is applied, the stored bit can be flipped from “0” to “1.” In [12], the reading process can be modeled as the Z channel. To facilitate the channel model of STT-MRAM, the combining channel model of BAC and Z channel is applied. Let p0 and p1 be the cross probability of writing bit “0” and “1.” According to [12], the MTJ resistance of bit “0” and “1” can be represented by the Gaussian distribution of the two random variables R0 and R1, respectively. The cascaded channel of STT-MRAM is shown in Fig. 1. The parameters μ0 and σ0 are defined as the mean and standard deviation of R0 and μ1 and σ1 are defined as the mean and standard deviation of R1. In this work, we define the important parameters as follows [12]: p0 = 10−6, p1 = 1.02 × 10−4, μ0 = 1 k , μ1 = 2 k , and σ0/μ0 = σ1/μ1. We assume that the memory cell size is fixed, while the standard deviation of the resistance distribution is changed by varying σ0/μ0 (or σ1/μ1) to show the influence of different manufacturing quality.
B. I.I.D. Channel Adapter
As mentioned in the previous section, the STT-MRAM channel shows asymmetric channel, that is, p(Y = y| C = 0) = p(Y = −y|C = 1), where p(Y = y|C = 0) is the distribution function of channel output y ∈ Y given data bits C = 0 and p(Y = y|C = 1) is the distribution function of channel output y ∈ Y given data bits C = 1. In [5], since the STT-MRAM channel is asymmetric, an i.i.d. channel adapter is applied to convert an asymmetric channel to a symmetric channel. The i.i.d. channel adapter is composed of the binary random generator, exclusive-OR (XOR) operations, and multiplication operations. Before the codeword bits are written in the memory cells, the binary bits (bn) generated by the binary random generators are added to those codeword bits. For the readback signal, the LLR of the readback signal is multiplied by the binary signal. As a result, the symmetric channel is given, that is, p(Y = y|C = 0) = p(Y = −y|C = 1).

VI. CONCLUSION
In this work, we propose the design of the LUT-based LDPC decoder for STT-MRAM. The FB process of LUT operations is presented to reduce the latency of decoding, where the DE technique is adopted to design the LUT operations. We found that the sequential and FB processes of LUT operations provide the comparable identical BER performance. We also consider the design of the LUT-based LDPC decoder for STT-MRAM with and without an i.i.d. channel adapter. The simulation results confirm that our design supports the asymmetric channel of STT-MRAM.

Note that the sequential process of the LUT operations is only employed since the IBM offers the design rule of the sequential process. The simulation results show that the LUT operations designed by our DE technique outperform the IBM [9] at the waterfall region. For example, the BER performance gain of the 2-bit quantizer combined with the LUT-based decoder is about 1% at 10−6. We further investigate the design of an LUT-based LDPC decoder for STT-MRAM without an i.i.d. channel adapter. Fig. 6 shows the BER performance comparison of LUT operations designed for the STT-MRAM with and without an i.i.d. channel adapter. As a result, both designs provide the same BER performances for 2-, 3-, and 4-bit quantizers. It means that our proposed designs can support the asymmetric channel of STT-MRAM.
