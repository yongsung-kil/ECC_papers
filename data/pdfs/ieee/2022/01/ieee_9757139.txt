A 58.6/91.3 pJ/b Dual-Mode Belief-Propagation Decoder for LDPC and Polar Codes in the 5G Communications Standard
Bei-Sheng Su, Chia-Heng Lee, and Tzi-Dar Chiueh , Fellow, IEEE

Abstract—This letter presents the first belief propagation (BP) decoder IC implementation for the two forward error correction (FEC) codes in the 5G communication standard. The LDPC mode supports 5G BG2 with 128 lifting size, while the polar mode supports code length N = 1024. The 40-nm CMOS chip features BP module sharing, memory reuse, check node unit design, forwarding and layer pipelining, and dataflow rearrangement. Compared to two single-mode decoders, this dual-mode decoder saved 37% in the overall die area, 32% in the computation circuit area, and 41% in the memory area. The chip delivers throughputs of 2.38 and 1.85 Gb/s from 0.9-V Vdd with energy efficiencies of 58.6 and 91.3 pJ/b in the LDPC mode and the polar mode, respectively.
Index Terms—5G communications, belief propagation (BP), forward error correction (FEC) decoder, LDPC code, polar code.

I. INTRODUCTION
In 2018, LDPC codes and polar codes [1] were officially adopted as forward error correction (FEC) codes by the 3GPP in the 5G Release 15 standard. They are used in the enhanced mobile broadband (eMBB) scenario to achieve the high throughput and high-reliability requirements of the 5G systems. LDPC codes protect data channels, and polar codes are used for control channels. 5G receivers need to decode the polar-coded control information before decoding the LDPC code that protects the data information. The belief propagation (BP) decoding processes of the LDPC codes and polar codes are similar. As such, they can be decoded by a single BP decoder. There are similar concepts in previous works, e.g., [2] and [3], but the code length in [2] does not meet the 5G requirements. The work in [3] only considered memory sharing, and the processing components are still individually designed. Besides, its results are based on synthesis, not chip measurements. In summary, this letter is the first 5G dualmode decoder IC implementation with memory sharing, processing element sharing, and several hardware innovations.

The basic computational unit (BCU) formulas of the polar decoder implement the parallel updates of the L messages and the R messages in each stage. The LDPC layer decoder has two steps of computation: 1) the check node unit (CNU) and 2) the variable node unit (VNU). Note that most of the calculations are comparison and addition operations. Fig. 2 illustrates the concept of PE-sharing architecture. We designed 512 BCUs to support a single stage of polar codes (N = 1024) BP decoding. Each BCU consists of two comparators and four adders (enclosed by the red box) to process scaled min-sum and subsequent calculation. To accommodate the three to ten check node degrees in the LDPC BG2 mode of 5G, we combined four BCUs to construct one LDPC PE that can be configured as one VNU and one CNU in layered LDPC decoding. The LDPC PE supports the comparison of up to ten LLRs to find the minimum value, the second minimum value, the index of the minimum value, and the sign bits. The 512 BCUs could be configured as 128 LDPC PEs to support decoding of quasi-cyclic LDPC codes with 128 lifting size.
The memory usage in the two modes was also carefully designed to achieve maximum sharing. The LDPC mode memory usage is higher than that of the polar mode, while the polar mode memory throughput is higher than that of the LDPC mode. To reduce the complexity of finding the minimum and second minimum values in CNU, we proposed the reduced min-sum (RMS) method, shown in Fig. 4. In traditional CNU, each variable node must find the exact minimum and second minimum values from the 3–10 input LLR values. In the worst case, there will be ten LLRs, making the maximum finding circuit complicated and having long latency. Therefore, we proposed an approximation method that simplifies the circuit and saves latency and pipeline registers. Take Fig. 4, for example; if A1 is smaller than A5, A5 will be dropped in the first stage without considering the possibility of A5 being the second minimum value. In the second stage, the minimum and the second minimum values will be decided among the four winners from the first stage. If there are more than eight LLRs, another cycle will be needed to compare the result with the remaining LLRs. The proposed RMS method takes two cycles for eight or fewer LLR inputs, and it takes three cycles for nine or ten LLR inputs in CNU. The probability of the RMS method making an incorrect decision in the second minimum value is no more than 10% in all code rates. The block error rate (BLER) performance is degraded by less than 0.1 dB in SNR.

The LLR reorder architecture shown in Fig. 5(a) is a barrel shifter, which can transfer data during writing and reading. It can shift the eight LLR inputs by any number of positions. For N inputs, log2 N levels of multiplexers are stacked. The reorder circuit of the polar decoder is similar to that in the FFT circuit [4]. As shown in Fig. 5(b), the data locations will be bit reversed first to ensure that the wiring of each stage is the same, and the connections will be adjusted according to whether L propagation or R propagation is implemented.

a revised scheduling scheme based on the BG2 characteristic, as in Fig. 6. Layered LDPC decoding needs to finish one layer before it continues to the next layer, where both reading and writing stages need shifting. The forwarding mechanism adjusts the data order in advance and sends them to the PE directly without writing back to the memory, thus saving two cycles between layers. We also designed three pipelining strategies for three types of layers in the BG2 mode, thus increasing the throughput and saving energy, as shown in Fig. 7. The type-I layers have data dependency, while the type-II and type-III layers do not need to wait for the calculation of the previous layers to complete. The difference between type-II and type-III layers is that type-II layers are two consecutive layers without overlapping, while type-III layers are three consecutive layers without any overlap among them. Note that a maximum of three type-III layers can be processed as a group because the RMS architecture of the LDPC decoder supports the maximum calculation of three layers. With these two techniques, the energy is reduced by about 34%–41% in the LDPC mode, as shown in Fig. 8.
We adjusted the single-column structure mentioned in [5] by shuffling one of the LLR reorder units, as shown in Fig. 9. The inputs of reorder unit #1 no longer come from the PE outputs, which have many transients and are unstable before the PE operations are complete. By the proposed modification, spurious signal transitions are avoided, and the power and the critical path delay of the reorder unit are reduced by 15% and 20%, respectively.

C. Decoder Chip Architecture
After careful fixed-point simulation, we used 6-bit LLR value in the dual-mode decoder to strike a balance between circuit complexity and BLER performance. The BLER simulation results shown in Fig. 10 indicate almost no degradation in BLER when the decoder is in the polar mode. In the LDPC mode, depending on the code rate, the SNR degradation by the fixed-point decoder can be around 1 dB in the worst case.
Fig. 11 depicts the 5G dual-mode decoder IC’s architecture: sharable I/O buffer, LLR cache, general PE, CRC circuit, and nonsharable modules that include address generator unit (AGU) and LLR reorder unit. The CRC architecture is based on the parallel architecture of [6] to reduce latency. This IC supports all LDPC code rates in the 5G BG2 with up to 128 lifting size and a maximum polar code length of 1024. Its data flow is described as follows. First, the LLR vector is fed from off-chip to the LLR cache by the I/O LLR buffer. During iterations, the LLR reorder unit adjusts the LLR order according to the factor graph of either mode and moves the reordered LLRs to the PEs for BP processing or moves the reordered PE results back to LLR cache for data storage. At the end of each BP iteration, the parallelized CRC unit checks the hard decision outcomes for early termination. After termination, the LLR cache will output the soft decisions and hard decisions.

IV. CONCLUSION
Through the common PE design, we realized the CNU/VNU processing of LDPC decoding and the BCU of polar decoding in the proposed dual-mode FEC decoder architecture. Careful shared memory design reduced the overall memory area while maintaining the decoding speed. The shared design saved 37% of the chip area in total, with 32% PE area and 41% memory area being saved, respectively. The fabricated chip achieves an LDPC decoding speed of 2.38 Gb/s and a polar decoding speed of 1.85 Gb/s, as well as respective energy efficiency of 58.6 and 91.3 pJ/b. In conclusion, this letter is the first silicon integration of a dual-mode 5G FEC decoder IC. Moreover, it provides a promising solution to reducing the energy consumption and circuit area for 5G FEC decoding.
