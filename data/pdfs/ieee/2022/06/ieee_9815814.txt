550 Gbps Fully Parallel Fully Unrolled LDPC
Decoder in 28 nm CMOS Technology
A. Hasani∗, L. Lopacinski∗, G. Panic∗, and E. Grass∗†
∗IHP - Leibniz-Institut f¨ur innovative Mikroelektronik, Frankfurt (Oder), Germany
†Humboldt-Universit¨at zu Berlin, Institut f¨ur Informatik, Berlin, Germany
Abstract—A fully unrolled fully parallel decoding architecture
for Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes is proposed in this
paper. Specifically, the iterations of a min-sum decoding scheme
are also unrolled and each is implemented with a separate
dedicated hardware. The proposed architecture implemented in
a 28 nm CMOS technology is able to achieve the throughput
of 550 Gbps, occupying an area of 4.98mm2. These results are
accomplished with 4-bit quantization of the min-sum decoding
messages and six iterations in the case of a (648,540)-LDPC code
from IEEE 802.11n. The results show improvements compared
with the adaptive degeneration [1] and finite-alphabet [2] decod-
ing algorithms, in terms of both throughput and chip area.
I.
INTRODUCTION
Forward Error Correction (FEC) is one of the most vital
elements of communication systems providing a means for
reliable and effective data exchange. Data transmission via an
unreliable wireless link lacking FEC suffers from a significant
overhead due to the need for frequent retransmissions. Over the
years FEC methods have been constantly improved in terms
of Bit-Error Rate (BER) performance, throughput, energy
efficiency, etc. An important breakthrough in this way was the
discovery of Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes [3] and
their decoding with a Belief Propagation (BP)-based algorithm.
This combination provides high error-correction performance
and allows the design of ultra-high-speed decoders in a digital
CMOS technology. In particular, several successful and afford-
able implementations of these codes running at hundreds of
Gbps have thus far been presented [1], [2], [4], [5]. Today,
LDPC codes are employed in several well-known communi-
cation standards like DVB-T2, DVB-S2, DVB-C2, DVB-S,
DVB-C as well as IEEE 802.11n and 5G. Accordingly, the
employment of LDPC codes in next-generation communication
systems like beyond 5G and 6G is expected.
Authors in [5] proposed a decoding architecture for LDPC
codes unrolling the iterations of a BP algorithm, thus achieving
the throughput of 160 Gbps. It was also demonstrated that the
average energy consumption can be reduced by unrolling the
iterations due to the simplified control path. In general, BP
decoding of an LDPC code with iteration unrolling facilitates
a massively parallel decoding structure, capable of handling
hundreds of gigabits of data. In this paper, we present yet
another ultra-high-speed decoder architecture for an LDPC
code also based on the idea of iterations unrolling. The
proposed architecture proves a user data-rate of 458 Gbps for
a (648,540)-LDPC code belonging to IEEE 802.11n (Wi-Fi)
communication standard. The employed decoding algorithm is
a standard min-sum decoding procedure [6], [7], implemented
in a 28 nm CMOS technology. The central aspect targeted here
is reduction of the timing of critical paths, leading to clock and
thus data rate maximization.
The organization of the paper is as follows. Section II
briefly overviews some of the significant related works. Section
III presents a concise introduction of min-sum BP algorithm
with flood schedule. Afterwards, in section IV, the proposed
fully parallel fully unrolled decoding architecture is presented.
The achieved results and their analysis together with a com-
parison with state-of-the-art is provided in section V. Final
conclusions are made in section VI.
II.
RELATED WORKS
The concept of iteration unrolling for LDPC BP decoders
was firstly introduced in [5]. This idea is in general iden-
tical to the loop unrolling (loop unwinding) technique used
in programming languages. In the case of LDPC decoders,
unrolling the iterations and placing individual hardware for
each iteration instance significantly improves the decoding
throughput (up to 160 Gbps in 65 nm CMOS as reported in
[5]) and lowers the energy consumption due to the elimination
of control circuits. In particular, there is no need to control
the decoding layers and manipulate the parity equations in
such an unrolled decoder, as it is the case, for instance, in
semi-parallel architectures based on layered decoding sched-
ule [8]. Therefore all corresponding controlling signals and
modules can be removed. The disadvantages of this idea are,
however, high chip area occupation (12 mm2 in [5]), fixed
code rate, and the lack of early termination strategy. Such
an unrolled LDPC decoder perfectly fits for high throughput
optical communication, where the fixed code-rate is not a
significant limitation, and high complexity is compensated by
the high throughput demanded in this domain. For wireless
communication applications, the lack of variable code-rate
does not allow to implement link adaptation techniques, as well
as variable modulation and coding schemes, which is strongly
demanded.
Another LDPC decoder with fully unrolled architecture
was realized firstly in a TSMC 90 nm technology in [9] and
then in a 28 nm CMOS technology [2]. In the latter article,
a throughput of 588 Gbps in a typical case (25◦C and 1V
supply voltage) is claimed.

IV.
PROPOSED DECODING ARCHITECTURE
The details of the proposed and implemented fully parallel
fully unrolled min-sum decoding method are presented in the
following subsections.
A. Single Iteration Architecture
The proposed architecture for a single iteration of decoding
is shown in Fig. 2.
The block ”VTC computation” is an equivalence of (1). At
the beginning of first iteration where still no a-posteriori LLR
exists, VTC values are taken from the channel output y by
establishing the S1 path. However, from the next iterations, the
path S2 is established leading to computation of VTC messages
from a-posteriori LLRs from the previous iteration. The block
”Min find” is responsible for finding the first two minima and
location of the first minimum to provide them to the subsequent
block ”CTV computation”. This block is an equivalence of
(2). At the end of an iteration a-posteriori LLRs are computed
within the block ”A-posteriori LLRs”. The minimum search
modules needed in this algorithm for computing these check
nodes values in a min-sum decoding method use a typical
comparison tree architecture as discussed in [10].
The input data y representing soft bits are quantized to
fixed-point binary values with two’s complementary format.
This coding simplifies arithmetic computations such as ad-
dition and subtraction. The input to the min modules are,
however, converted into sign-magnitude format, since the min-
modules find the two inputs with minimum absolute value. The
reverse process is performed in the check nodes, where the
sign-magnitude format is converted back to two’s complement.
In our implementation, 4-bit quantization is used to represent
soft values, while the result of arithmetic computations are
provided with 5-bit precision. After an arithmetic computation
the data shrinks again to 4 bits by removing the least significant
bit (truncation). This extra one bit serves as a cautionary
bit preventing probable errors caused by saturation during an
arithmetic computation.
Each iteration according to this architecture takes 4 clock
cycles to complete its function. Nevertheless, this architecture
is well suited to pipelining; specifically, when iterations are
unrolled and an entirely distinct hardware is dedicated to each
iteration. Accordingly, the successive sequences of input data
could be processed in every clock cycles, and apart from an
initial latency the flow of decoding could become nonstop.

C. Chip Synthesis and Floor-planning
The Hardware Description Language (HDL) code defining
the design is synthesized to the netlist by means of an
industry standard synthesis tool. Then, the challenging stage
of floor-planning is carried out whose key steps are placing the
logic cells, clock-tree-insertion, design routing, and post-route
optimization measures. The main challenge faced during floor-
planning is to find clock constraints that allow to successfully
floor-plan the chip. In general, it is impossible to find the
optimal settings for performing floor-plan with respect to the
performance, area, and Design Rule Checking (DRC) viola-
tions. Therefore, we performed the floor-planning with the first
successful configuration set. In our experiments, we repeated
this stage nearly 20 times to achieve the reported results.
This indicates that designing and fabricating a fully unrolled
LDPC decoder is challenging due to congestion problems and
complex metal connection networks. This network is required
for implementing the PCM functionality in the hardware.
To achieve a good BER performance, the PCM uses long
cycles, bringing about routing difficulties. We can use seven
copper layers and one aluminum on the top in the 28 nm
process, but three top layers insert very large vias and are
not well optimized for standard signals’ routing. Also, placing
power strips in layers 7 and 8 causes connections problems
because it requires to get connected to metal layer 1, and a
relatively large area under the power stripes is blocked (large
vias). This causes a large number of DRCs which are difficult
to repair (metal cut and metal spacing violations).
V.
RESULTS AND ANALYSIS
All synthesis and floor-plan results presented in this section
are related to the worst-case corner, i.e., the temperature of
125 ◦C, and voltage of 0.9V in industrial 28 nm CMOS
technology. The worst-case corner refers to the extreme cir-
cumstances such as high ambient temperature, sharp voltage
drop, etc. that may occur when the fabricated chip is in use.
A. BER Performance
Fig. 4 depicts the BER performance of the implemented
decoder along with the performance of the ideal Sum-Product
Algorithm (SPA) and min-sum decoding algorithms with
floating-point values. The quantization of the originally real-
valued messages in a BP decoding algorithm results in a loss
of ∼0.25 dB compared to the unquantized SPA given that
the maximum number of iterations are the same in the two
cases. therefore, the selected quantization precision (4 bits) is
a reasonable trade-off between the hardware area and BER
performance. These simulations have been performed with
the assumption of Additive White Gaussian Noise (AWGN)
channel and binary Phase Shift-Keying (BPSK) modulation.
B. Synthesis and Floor-planning Results
The maximum clock frequency at the synthesis stage is
estimated to be 1.85 GHz, yielding the throughput of 1178
Gbps, assuming the worst-case corner. The chip area at this
stage is 1.346 mm2. After performing chip floor-planning, the
clock frequency and throughput are reduced to 850 GHz and
550 Gbps, respectively, while the chip area grows to 4.98mm2.
The implemented chip after floor-planning looks like what
is shown in Fig. 5 or Fig. 6. Fig. 5 depicts the placement of
unrolled iterations in a circular pattern. The soft bit values are
input to the chip from the upper left corner, and the decoded
hard-bit information is output from the upper right corner.
Thus, the data path follows a counterclockwise direction from
the upper left to the upper right corner of the chip. In the
middle of the chip, all shared signals are placed, to yield
similar delays among the shared signals for all iterations. We
presume that this is one of the optimal placements for this
design type. Fig. 6 although similar to Fig. 5 specifies the input
and output pins more clearly as a yellow strip from upper left
to upper right corner of the chip.

Since we have used the well-known PCM of a (648,540)-
LDPC code with coderate R = 5/6, the input soft bits
routed to the decoder require 2592 wire connections. This is
caused by 648 codeword bits multiplied by 4, since a 4-bit
quantization has been used for representation of soft values,
i.e., 648 × 4 = 2592. This high number of wires leads to an
extremely large routing overhead. Consequently, every pipeline
stage requires 2592 FFs. 30% core utilization in our design,
although improved with respect to others, indicates the extreme
order of congestion that exists due to the connections between
variable nodes and check nodes in the PCM as well as the
fully unrolled decoding architecture.
C. Floor-planning vs. Synthesis Results
A detailed comparison between the synthesis and floor-plan
results is also of importance. Synthesis estimations usually
neglect the influence of routing structures made in metal layers
of the chip. However, in case of fully unrolled and fully
parallel LDPC decoders, the overhead caused by the routing
is extraordinarily high and cannot be neglected. To achieve
a good BER performance, the PCM of LDPC codes uses
long cycles, which result in routing difficulties. This causes
long lines needed during routing which in turn introduce large
propagation delays and occupy large chip area. Thus, we
observed that the throughput of our decoder drops downto 550
Gbps after floor-plan, while the synthesis tool had reported
a throughput of 1178 Gbps. The chip area required for the
routing signal causes an overhead of 3.65mm2, while the logic
alone requires only 1.35 mm2. Moreover, energy efficiency
decreases by approximately 400% after floor-planning.
VI.
CONCLUSION
To the best of our knowledge, the fully parallel fully
unrolled LDPC decoder presented in this paper is the fastest
decoder prototyped in a 28 nm technology. The implemented
min-sum decoding method does not achieve a worse BER
performance with a standard IEEE 802.11n (648,540)-LDPC
code than the adaptive degeneration [1] and finite-alphabet [2]
methods, respectively with codewords of 60000 and 2048 bits.
The achieved throughput is also higher than the both works,
yet with a considerably smaller chip area.
