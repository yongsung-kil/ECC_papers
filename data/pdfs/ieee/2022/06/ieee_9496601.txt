A Low Bit-Width LDPC Min-Sum Decoding Scheme for NAND Flash
Lanlan Cui , Xiaojian Liu, Fei Wu , Member, IEEE, Zhonghai Lu , Senior Member, IEEE, and Changsheng Xie , Member, IEEE

Abstract—For NAND flash memory, designing a good low-density parity-check (LDPC) decoding algorithm could ensure data reliability. When the decoding algorithm is implemented in hardware, it is necessary to achieve an attractive tradeoff between implementation complexity and decoding performance. In this article, a novel low-bit-width decoding scheme is introduced. In this scheme, the quasi-cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) is used, and the row-layered normalized min-sum algorithm is improved by restricting the amplitude of minimum and second-minimum values in each check node (CN) updating. The simulation shows that our approach achieves a lower uncorrectable bit error rate (UBER) with a negligible increase in computational complexity, especially with low-precision input log-likelihood ratio (LLR).
Index Terms—Bit-width, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, min-sum decoding, NAND flash memory.
I. INTRODUCTION
With the development of memory technology, the capacity of NAND flash memory is getting larger. At the same time, the reliability of stored data is seriously reduced, producing a high raw bit error rate (RBER), which represents the primitive reliability state of NAND flash. Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] are used as the error correction codes for NAND flash memory, with their superior error correction capability. A commonly used LDPC decoding algorithm is the row-layered normalized min-sum (NMS) algorithm [2] which ensures low implementation complexity while maintaining good error correction capability. When this LDPC decoding algorithm is implemented in hardware, its error correction performance is reduced due to the limited bit-width. To implement the decoding algorithm in hardware, we need to achieve a good tradeoff between the bit-width of the message and error correction capability. Ideally, the bit-width of the decoder should be as small as possible without harming its error correction performance. Designing a suitable decoding algorithm with superior performance and easy implementation is crucial for NAND flash memory.

Due to the introduction of more variables, our method is more adaptable to different matrices and decoding frameworks. Simulation results show that our algorithm gets a lower bit-width compared with the standard algorithm. When the input bit-width is less than 5, the lower uncorrectable bit error rate (UBER) is obtained.
II. NAND FLASH AND LDPC CODE
Despite increased storage capacity, data errors are more likely to occur for current flash memory chips. Due to the shifting and widening of threshold voltage distribution, raw bit errors occur during the read operation [14]. To ensure data reliability, LDPC codes are employed for NAND flash memory.

After the LDPC decoding algorithm is designed, it needs to be implemented in hardware. Designing an excellent algorithm to balance the needs of low bit-width and high performance is essential.

III. PROPOSED SCHEME
This article presents a simple yet effective low bit-width minsum scheme, which can be effectively used for NAND flash memory.

This scheme can be applied for the main branches of min-sum algorithms, including the NMS algorithm, row-layered NMS algorithm, and column-layered min-sum algorithm. The proposed scheme can reduce the internal bit-width by at least 1 bit under the same input bit-width, and does not negatively affect the error correction capability. It is suitable for applications that require low-power consumption and large throughput, such as NAND flash memory.
Here, we take the row-layered NMS algorithm as the prototype to explain the proposed scheme. The operation in (3) needs to select the minimum and the second-minimum essentially. When the bit-width of the decoder is small, the selected two values are often equal. So that the amplitude of the information transmitted by the CN is the same. In addition, even if the two values are not equal, the difference between them may be decreased compared with the original level as the bit-width is small. For example, the minimum value and second-minimum values are 4 and 10 originally, and their difference is 6. Assume the set bit-width is 4 bits. To keep the value in the 4 bits range, the second-minimum value needs to be adjusted to 7, resulting in their difference being reduced to 3. Due to the above two phenomena, either the resolution of the information transmitted by CNU is reduced, or the VNU is saturated too quickly. Eventually, the error correction capability is declined. The method proposed in this article is to limit the minimum value and second-minimum value by different amplitudes. The detailed process is as follows.
Let M1m and M2m, respectively, denote the minimum and the second-minimum amplitudes of the message passed to the mth CN. We use P and Q, where, P ≤ Q, to perform the following limiting operations on M1m and M2m, respectively:

The selection of ranges P and Q is built on simulations through scanning within all possible values. Since we only need to simulate integers for hardware implementation, the cost of this search is not large. The improvement of our algorithm is carried out in the process of calculating CNU. Assume that the bit-width of input message is a, the upper bound of the LLR amplitude is L = 2a−1 − 1. We get the optimal (P, Q) through simulation. To facilitate understanding, we give an example here. Set a = 3, then L = 3. As P ≤ Q, all possible (P, Q) are (1, 2), (1, 3), and (2, 3). Assuming when (P, Q) = (1, 2), the performance difference compared with the floating-point minsum is the smallest, thus, (1, 2) is selected as the final parameter. For every LDPC code, the proper P and Q need to be found by the above process. P and Q are code dependent. Generally, the limit of the second-minimum value is around half of L, and the limit of the minimum value is around L/4. After the minimum value and the second-minimum value are updated to P and Q, they are substituted into (3) to calculate the CNU. Then, the LLR amplitude is limited according to the bit-width of the CNU.
It should be noted that only one position is marked as the minimum value position in our method. If the minimum value appears in several positions, we randomly select one as the only position. In our algorithm, when the LLR is a decimal, the decimal part is directly discarded to obtain a fixed-point LLR.
This method is simple and easy to implement, which can reduce the bit-width of CN output information, and slow down the saturation of VN at the later stage of the iteration, thus slowing the appearance of the error floor [15].

IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
To verify the effectiveness of the proposed scheme, we use different LDPC codes for simulation. For a high code rate, we choose two LDPC codes. The (37888, 33280) (puncture 784, shorten 240) QC-LDPC code is constructed to assure a very low error floor. Its code rate is 0.9, row weights are 33 and 34, and column weights are 4, 5, and 6. Its block-structured matrix is shown in Fig. 1. The (9984, 8448) (puncture 768) 5G-LDPC [17] has a better threshold but a high error floor. Its code rate is 0.92, row weight is 19, and column weights are 2, 3, and 4. We choose it to show the adaptability of our algorithm. On the other hand, for low code rate, (1768, 572) 5G-LDPC with code rate 0.32 and (2304, 1152) WiMAX [18] with code rate 0.5 are also used to evaluate the algorithm performance. Simulations are carried out assuming that the codewords are modulated using binary phase-shift keying (BPSK) and passed over the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel, which can be used to simulate NAND flash channel [19]. The maximum number of decoding iterations is set to 20. The initial information from the channel is uniformly quantized and does not exceed the maximum value defined by the bit-width. C. Comparison With Other Algorithms
We compare our algorithm to some former solutions, such as FAID [3], APP-SO [11], and vwMS [12]. Here, we use (9984,8448) 5G-LDPC with a high code rate, as its code length is short, which is convenient for observation. Considering the complexity of hardware implementation, we pay attention to the error correction performance under low bit-width, so the bit-width configuration of 3434 is used to compare the performance. We implement the scheme proposed in these studies for soft-decision decoding, and the results are shown in Fig. 6. Overall, the scheme proposed in this article has better error correction performance. The UBER performance of other algorithms is slightly worse because they are not effective in dealing with the matrix used in this article. The advantage of the APP-SO algorithm lies in its simplicity and low implementation complexity. FAID and vwMS algorithms are similar to our method in the complexity of parameter selection.
For the hardware implementation complexity, our algorithm adds two comparators [formulas (5) and (6)] after finding the minimum

and second-minimum values when performing row updates. Their bit-widths are multibits, decided by P and Q. As the row update is only a step of the LDPC decoding process, and the addition of two comparators only increases dozens of row operations, the overhead can be ignored. This algorithm reduces the bit-width of the VUN message and posterior probability, memory bits are greatly decreased.
V. CONCLUSION
In order to improve the data reliability of NAND flash memory, the LDPC decoding algorithm is applied. Due to the limited bitwidth in the hardware implementation, the accuracy of the LDPC decoding algorithm is reduced. We propose a new scheme that can maintain good decoding performance with a low bit-width, by limiting the amplitude of the minimum and the second-minimum values to different ranges. Simulation results demonstrate that the proposed scheme reduces the bit-width by at least 1 bit under the same input bit-width, and does not negatively affect the UBER. Especially, for 3 bits and 4 bits input messages, the UBER is declined for several orders of magnitude.
