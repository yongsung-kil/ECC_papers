SAPA: Sparse Affine Projection Algorithm in ADMM-LP Decoding of LDPC Codes
Amirreza Asadzadeh∗, Masoud Barakatain†, Stark. C. Draper∗, and Jeebak Mitra† ∗Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, University of Toronto, Toronto, ON, Canada,
Email: amirreza.asadzadeh@mail.utoronto.ca, stark.draper@utoronto.ca †Huawei Technologies, Ottawa, ON, Canada, Email: masoud.barakatain@huawei.com, jeebak.mitra@huawei.com

Abstract—We present a simplified alternating direction method of multipliers with linear programming (ADMM-LP) decoder for LDPC codes by developing a method of approximate projection onto the parity polytope. The algorithm projects onto the affine hull of the χ closest local codewords for each check of degree d, where χ can be much smaller than d. We name this SAPA, the “sparse affine projection algorithm”. In contrast to exact projection, SAPA does not require a water-filling step and thus can be implemented with lower per-iteration complexity. We present numerical results which demonstrate not only that SAPA’s performance, when used as part of an overall decoder, is close to that of exact projection, but also that the use of SAPA does not incur many additional iterations. This is in contrast to other approximate algorithms. Thus, SAPA is appropriate for use in limited-iteration decoders in high-throughput applications. Moreover, we analyze sparsity of polytope projections in the exact ADMM-LP decoding to explain the suitability of the proposed sparse approximation.
Index Terms—Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM), Low-Density Parity Check (LDPC) Codes, Parity Polytope Projection, Affine Projection Algorithm
I. INTRODUCTION
Linear programming (LP) decoding of low-density parity check (LDPC) codes, proposed by Feldman et al. [1], is an alternative to message passing decoding which benefits from many theoretical guarantees. Since solving the LP decoding problem using generic LP solvers is extremely complex, Barman et al. [2] proposed a distributed framework based on the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM). When the linear objective of LP decoding is augmented with a penalty to encourage integer solutions, “ADMM-LP” decoding performance reaches the accuracy of message passing algorithms [3]. However, the complexity of the penalized ADMM-LP decoder is not yet comparable to message passing decoders, due to the parity polytope projection step used in the decoding routine.
Zhang and Siegel [4] reduced the complexity of ADMMLP decoding via a cut-based efficient projection algorithm. Inspired by that, Wasson et al. [5] proposed a hardwarecompatible algorithm. Still, the projection step in [5] involved a complex water-filling routine that included sorting and multiple comparisons with super-linear complexity.
More recently, two approximate algorithms for parity polytope projections and their enhanced variants were proposed. These algorithms are called the even-vertex algorithm (EVA)

[6], [7] and the line-segment algorithm [8], [9]. They respectively project onto the closest vertex or the line connecting the two closest vertices of the polytope. While these approximation algorithms have demonstrated promising performance, they require many additional iterations to converge. However, in high-throughput applications such as optical communications, the number of iterations is severely limited. In this limited-iteration regime, these approximate algorithms lose their accuracy.
In this work, we generalize the prior approximate algorithms, and propose SAPA, the “Sparse Affine Projection Algorithm”. SAPA projects onto the affine hull of a small number of vertices of the polytope. SAPA doesn’t involve a water-filling process as in exact projection. And while SAPA has linear complexity, it maintains high performance even when used in conjunction with low-iteration ADMMLP decoders. We also analyze the sparsity characteristics of the exact parity polytope projection routine and validate why sparse approximation is well matched to the ADMM-LP decoding routine.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. In Section II we review fundamental background on ADMM-LP decoding and parity polytope projection, and provide a brief review of prior approximate algorithms. Then, in Section III, we propose our approximate algorithm, SAPA. In addition we provide a motivating example explaining the sparsity constraint we assert on the affine projection. In Section IV, we first discuss the sparsity properties of the exact projection routine, and then provide numerical results verifying SAPA’s high-accuracy, and low-complexity characteristics. In Section V, we conclude the paper. Throughout this paper, all vectors are written in bold format, e.g. x. In addition, the all-zero and all-one vectors of length d are respectively denoted by 0d and 1d.

C. Approximate Projection Algorithms
Projection onto the parity polytope is considered to be the most complex operation in the ADMM-LP decoding algorithm, as it requires sorting a vector of size d and finding the proper shift to yield a convex combination of the closest even-weight vertices of PPd. Hence, pursuing an approximate parity polytope projection algorithm for the the ADMMLP decoder that does not substantially degrade the overall decoding performance is of great interest. We now briefly describe previously introduced approximate algorithms for parity polytope projection.
• Even-Vertex Algorithm (EVA) [8] projects onto the closest even-vertex of the parity polytope, while ignoring all other d − 1 neighbouring even-vertices. The process is similar to lines 1-7 of Alg. 1 only by substituting the term “even” with “odd” in line 4.
• Hybrid Projection Algorithm (HPA) [6] utilizes a predefined schedule to alternate between the exact projection algorithm and EVA in consecutive iterations of the ADMM-LP decoding. It was shown empirically that the optimal mixing ratio (in terms of Frame Error Rate) between the exact projection and EVA is 1:1.

As an alternative and approximate solution, we consider projection onto the extended hyperplane of the probability simplex. Instead of projecting a d-dimensional vector v onto a convex hull of the d closest vertices of the polytope, we project onto the affine hull of those d vertices. While performing affine projection requires computing a sum over all entries, it doesn’t involve sorting the vector initially nor applying multiple comparisons to single out the right amount of shift. C. Sparse Affine Projection Algorithm
Inspired by the numerical analysis in the previous subsection, we propose another approximate algorithm. We again develop an affine projection routine, but this time only involve a subset of the d closest even-weight vertices. More precisely, we constrain the sparsity (ℓ0-norm) of the coefficient vector ξ to a pre-determined value, denoted by χ. By enforcing this constraint, the novel approximation routine projects the point onto the affine hull of χ closest even vertices, {E 1, · · · , E χ}, and often outperforms LSA with χ > 2. The details of this approximate χ-Sparse Affine Projection Algorithm (χ-SAPA) is provided in Alg. 3.
For the example in Section III-B, the outcome of 1-SAPA is equal to that of EVA while 2-SAPA results in the same projected point as LSA. Although 4-SAPA reproduces the poor approximation of APA since χ = d = 4, the output of approximate 3-SAPA matches precisely with that of the exact projection. Thus, this family of χ-SAPA algorithms not only can scale up and achieve the performance of the other approximate algorithms as well as the exact projection, but also avoids the complex water-filling process required in the exact projection routine. This follows because affine projection is computationally simpler than convex projection.
The family of χ-SAPA algorithms, unlike the exact projection routine, doesn’t require sorting, computing all possible shifts, and applying multiple comparisons in the water-filling process. Instead, it involves a partial sorting to find indices of the χ minimum values of the input vector (line 1 in Alg. 3) and computing the sum of the entries with those indices as the affine shift (line 2 in Alg. 3). In this subsection, we empirically analyze the sparsity of the coefficient vector ξ across ADMM-LP decoding iterations. The notion we use for measuring sparsity is the ℓ0-norm of the coefficient vector, i.e., the number of non-zero coefficients in the convex combination. To capture the statistics of the sparsity of such vectors, we record the ℓ0-norm of the coefficient vectors across all M checks and all iterations. Then, in order to track the dynamics of the sparsity, the average, the 80%quantile, and the 90%-quantile of the ℓ0-norm of these vectors over various iterations are computed. In addition, to determine the distribution of the sparsity of coefficient vectors among all checks with negative membership test results in a specific iteration, the cumulative distribution function (CDF) is plotted for different stages of decoding.
While we collected observations for all three LDPC codes under study, only three representative samples of successful decoding are included in Fig. 1, one for each of the codes C1C3. We did not consider any limit on the number of iterations in this subsection as this is irrelevant to our experiments. The plots on the left of Fig. 1 demonstrate the dynamics of the average and the quantiles of sparsity throughout the iterations of ADMM-LP decoding. The graphs on the right of Fig. 1 illustrate the cumulative distribution of the sparsity at different phases of decoding. For each of the samples, four phases were considered: one for early iterations, two for intermediate phases, and one for the final iteration prior to correct decoding.
As the plots suggest, in early iterations, more than 80% of the checks are projected to a convex combination of only three local codewords, regardless of the maximum checkdegree. Moreover, starting from halfway through decoding, using three local codewords is sufficient for more than 90% of the checks. In addition, the CDF plots suggest that in early iterations, a significant portion of the probability mass function is associated with highly sparse or highly dense coefficient vectors. In contrast, in intermediate to final iterations, most of the probability mass function is related to reasonably sparse coefficient vectors. The aggregation of these observations suggests that only a few of the closest local codewords (regardless of the code’s degree distribution) suffice for the exact projection of most check variables across most iterations. As a result, sparse projection algorithms can be considered as a potential candidate for an accurate, but simpler, projection algorithm to use in ADMM-LP decoding.

There, d and η respectively denote the dimension of the polytope, and the ℓ0-norm of the convex coefficients vector in the exact projection routine. By definition, η ≤ d. In addition, g(χ) is defined as min{χ, d−χ}, since finding the χ minimum values of an array of size d is equivalent to searching for the d − χ maximum values. For example, consider 3-SAPA, which is (one of) the best candidate(s) among the proposed approximate algorithms for codes C1 − C3. 3-SAPA requires only 3d − 3 comparisons, 8 additions, and 1 multiplication for d ≥ 6. Also note that the complexity of LSA is slightly higher than that of 2-SAPA, due to some additional comparisons. Therefore, one can verify that the per-iteration complexity of all χ-SAPA algorithms is O(d), i.e., linear in d.

V. CONCLUSION
We proposed a family of approximate algorithms for parity
polytope projection in ADMM-LP decoding. These algorithms
are based on projection onto the affine hull of the closest local
codewords for each check constraint. Moreover, it is shown for
different LDPC codes that projection onto the affine hull of the 3 to 5 closest vertices of the polytope (regardless of the
maximum check degree) suffices to maintain decoding performance. In addition, these χ-sparse affine projection algorithms
converge almost as fast as the exact projection algorithm with a proper choice of χ, while significantly reducing per-iteration
complexity when compared to the exact algorithm.
