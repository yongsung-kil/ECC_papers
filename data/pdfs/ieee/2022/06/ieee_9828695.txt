Improvement of L1 Signaling Protection Based on ATSC 3.0 LDPC Codes
Seok-Ki Ahn and Sung-Ik Park
Media research division, ETRI, Daejeon, South Korea {seokki.ahn, psi76}@etri.re.kr

Abstract— This paper proposes a new low-density parity-check (LDPC) coding scheme for physical-layer [layer-1 (L1)] signaling (L1 signaling) protection based on modulo lifting operation. The proposed LDPC coding scheme uses the same base matrices as those in ATSC 3.0, but adjusts the lifting size depending on the number of signaling bits. Since L1 signaling protection utilizes quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes, their exponent matrices are obtained from those in ATSC 3.0 by modulo lifting operation for the proposed scheme. Numerical results show that the proposed scheme can give better block error rate (BLER) performance than the existing ATSC 3.0 signaling protection because the proposed one can dramatically reduce the portions of information shortening and parity puncturing than those in ATSC 3.0.
Index Terms— ATSC 3.0, L1 signaling, LDPC codes, modulo lifting, mobile broadcasting.
I. INTRODUCTION
Advanced Television Systems Committee (ATSC) 3.0, which is the latest broadcast standard, has approved a physical-layer standard for the next generation digital terrestrial television systems in 2016 [1-2]. For both fixed and mobile broadcast receivers, ATSC 3.0 can support ultra-high-definition (UHD) services thanks to the advantages of its excellent spectral efficiency, robustness, and flexibility [3]. In this regard, one innovative physical-layer technology in ATSC 3.0 is a low-density parity-check (LDPC) code, which was first adopted in the DVB-S2 standard. Since then, its performance is continuously improved up to the latest broadcasting standard, ATSC 3.0 [4-6].
From the viewpoint of the physical-layer of broadcasting standards, not only the payload data but also layer-1 (L1) signaling should be properly decoded, which contains essential information for receiving payloads. Furthermore, L1 signaling should be more powerfully protected than payloads because payloads may require several levels of protection depending on the required service types [7]. Since the same LDPC codes protect both payloads and L1 signaling to minimize the hardware implementation complexity, severe information shortening and parity puncturing of LDPC codes are inevitable

Therefore, it sometimes makes L1 signaling performance be a bottleneck of the physical-layer, because payloads can utilize time diversity gain obtained by the time interleaver in ATSC 3.0. For this reason, ATSC 3.0 has adopted additional parity for enhancing the robustness of L1 signaling, however, L1 signaling protection still needs to be improved, considering the future broadcasting standards.
In the field of mobile communication, the 3rd generation partnership project (3GPP) first adopted LDPC codes as 5G error-correcting codes for the physical-layer data transmission [8]. In the 5G standards, there are various service scenarios and system requirements, and therefore 5G LDPC codes should support the extreme flexibility required for varying channel conditions and payload sizes. For this reason, 5G LDPC codes are designed to provide a high level of flexibility in terms of code rate and code length by using a modulo lifting operation, together with well-designed two base graphs. It means that 5G LDPC codes are a kind of QC-LDPC codes supporting various code lengths with a base matrix by using a wide range of lifting sizes. As a result, 5G LDPC codes can efficiently support payload sizes from 40 to 8448 with stable performance.
In this paper, we propose a new LDPC coding scheme based on modulo lifting operation like 5G LDPC codes to improve the robustness of L1 signaling protection. Based on the ATSC 3.0 LDPC codes, new QC-LDPC codes of smaller lifting sizes are utilized in the proposed scheme, under the restriction of using the same base graphs in ATSC 3.0. Section II briefly reviews L1 signaling protection, QC-LDPC codes, and modulo lifting operations. Then, the proposed L1 signaling protection scheme is explained in Section III. Then, performance and computational complexity gains are evaluated in Section IV. Finally, concluding remarks are given in Section V.

In this section, we first give an overview of L1 signaling protection. Then we provide a brief review of QC-LDPC codes together with modulo lifting operations, which can be employed for QC-LDPC codes.
A. L1 signaling protection

Since L1 signaling includes essential information for receiving payloads, it should be powerfully protected considering the wide range of reception SNR. In ATSC 3.0, there are seven L1 signaling protection modes consisting of different coding rates and modulation orders, using the length-16,200 LDPC codes of rate 3/15 or 6/15. Even though the LDPC codes in payloads are well-designed and have a powerful error correction capability, they cannot give a good enough performance for L1 signaling protection because of massive information shortening and parity puncturing required to support the small number of signaling bits k. Therefore, L1 signaling protection cannot fully utilize the excellent performance of the well-designed LDPC codes in ATSC 3.0.

III. L1-SIGNALING DESIGN BASED ON MODULO LIFTING
This section proposes a new LDPC coding scheme for L1 signaling protection by using the same base matrices and shortening and puncturing patterns in ATSC 3.0, but new lifting sizes and corresponding exponent matrices directly obtained from the exponent matrices in ATSC 3.0 with modulo lifting operation. It means that the proposed scheme supports short-length L1 signaling and long-length payload data based on common base matrices with different lifting sizes, similar to the 5G LDPC coding scheme.
In ATSC 3.0, two base matrices of rate 3/15 and 6/15 are commonly used for seven protection modes, together with well-designed patterns of information shortening and parity puncturing defined by each mode of L1-Basic and L1-Detail. The proposed scheme employs the same base matrices and shortening and puncturing patterns for seven protection modes, but uses new lifting sizes and exponent matrices.
At first, we explain how to choose a lifting size for a given number of signaling bits . In ATSC 3.0, the lifting size is fixed as 360 for all kinds of , however, in the proposed scheme, a lifting size is chosen according to so that the number of shortened bits is minimized. As possible lifting sizes, all divisors of 360 are permitted in the proposed scheme.
Since the proposed scheme may employ smaller lifting sizes than ATSC 3.0, the exponent matrix should be also changed according to the lifting size. In this regard, a modulo lifting is used for the proposed scheme. For example, when a lifting size is 60, the components of the exponent matrix are obtained by modulo operation of 60 to the components of the exponent matrix in ATSC 3.0. It means that the proposed scheme employs QC-LDPC codes whose base matrix is the same as that of payload transmission, and exponent matrices are obtained by modulo operation to that of ATSC 3.0 corresponding to the smaller lifting size.
Due to the limitation of the non-contiguous lifting size, the proposed scheme also needs information shortening for the required number of signaling bits, and parity puncturing to support the required number of parity bits. From the viewpoint of the shortening and puncturing patterns, the proposed scheme can use the same patterns as those in ATSC 3.0 because the patterns are defined in the column unit of the base matrix.

A. Performance evaluation
In order to verify the performance gain of the proposed scheme, the decoding performance of L1 signaling is evaluated over AWGN channels for various signaling bits. The performance is measured in terms of the block error rate (BLER) by extensive computer simulations, as shown in Fig.1. Note that the sum-product algorithm with 50 iterations is assumed.
As shown in Fig. 1, the performance gain of the proposed scheme is larger as the number of signaling bits is smaller. As a result, the SNR gain for achieving BLER = 10-2 reaches up to about 0.9 dB and 0.2 dB for k = 600 and k = 3000, respectively. Even though the exponent matrices used for the proposed scheme are not delicately optimized, the proposed scheme has better performances than those of ATSC 3.0 because the proposed one can use proper lifting sizes so that the portion of information shortening and parity puncturing is greatly reduced. Suppose the exponent matrices used in the proposed scheme can be jointly optimized considering payload transmission and L1 signaling protection, the performance gain will be increased without performance loss when transmitting data payloads.
