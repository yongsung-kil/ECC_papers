Ultra high speed 802.11n LDPC decoder with seven-stage pipeline in 28 nm CMOS

L. Lopacinski1, A. Hasani1,2, G. Panic1, N. Maletic1, O. Schrape1, J. Gutiérrez1, M. Krstic1,3, E. Grass1,4, R. Kraemer1,2 1 IHP – Leibniz-Institut für innovative Mikroelektronik, Frankfurt Oder, Germany 2 Brandenburgische Technische Universität Cottbus-Senftenberg, Cottbus, Germany 3 Universität Potsdam, Potsdam, Germany 4 Humboldt-Universität zu Berlin, Institut für Informatik, Berlin, Germany

Abstract— This paper reports our latest implementation results of a fully unrolled LDPC decoder prototyped in 28 nm CMOS technology. The decoder achieves 1218 Gbps coded throughput and consumes a 5.49 mm2 chip area. The standard min-sum decoding algorithm with four-bit quantization, five unrolled iterations, (648,540) parity matrix, and a seven-stage pipeline is employed. Such implementation achieves a higher data rate than adaptive degeneration and finite-alphabet decoding algorithms, requires less silicon than the solutions mentioned above, and is fully compliant with the IEEE 802.11n WLAN standard.
Keywords— LDPC, loop unrolling, flooding schedule
I. INTRODUCTION
Forward error correction (FEC) is one of the most demanding aspects of communication devices. It is an essential block in all high-speed communication chipsets, which provide reliable and effective data exchange. Without FEC, transmission via an unpredictable and unreliable wireless link would cause significant overhead due to frame loss. This loss causes frame retransmissions, which reduce the effective link capacity. Moreover, the frame length and repetition size strongly depend on the bit error rate (BER), and without FEC, this size would be minimal. Consequently, overhead caused by frame-headers and fragment selective retransmissions headers reduce the link capacity additionally. Therefore, efficient FEC is one of the most important aspects for engineers working on data link layer protocols for wireless communication. Over the last few decades, FEC algorithms have been significantly improved according to the error correction performance, hardware complexity, throughput, energy efficiency, and fault tolerance [1]. One of the breakthrough algorithms is the low-density parity-check code (LDPC) and the belief propagation-decoding scheme. This combination provides high error correction performance and allows realizing ultra-high-speed decoders in digital CMOS. LDPC employed with iteration unrolling [2] allows constructing massively parallel decoding structures, which handle hundreds of gigabits represented as soft bit values. Although LDPC codes were already introduced in 1962 [3], they initially did not take any significant interest, mainly due to inefficient hardware available at that time. LDPC codes became dominant after the year 2000, when the performance of digital electronics has exploded, and electronics circuits were able to support LDPC decoding easily. Moreover, the data rate of wired and wireless systems increased rapidly, and there was a need for efficient bit error correction mechanisms. LDPC very quickly replaced other FEC methods in many popular systems. For example, DVB-T2, DVB-S2, and DVBC2 standards use LDPC codes, and these codes have replaced the combination of convolutional and Reed-Solomon FEC introduced in the first versions of DVB-T, DVB-S, and DVBC. Also, in other systems, LDPC superseded the previous FEC generations, e.g., in wireless LAN, by introducing the 802.11n

standard, and in optical fiber communication [4]. LDPC became widespread, and many efforts have been addressed to improve its decoding throughput. One of the most exciting decoding architecture concepts was introduced in [2] by unrolling the iterative LDPC decoding procedure in the hardware. This allowed to achieve 160 Gbps in 65 nm CMOS and has shown the full potential of unrolled LDPC decoders. The design supported an extremely high data rate and demonstrated that the average energy consumption can be reduced by unrolling the iterations due to the simplified control path. Later, similar concepts were used, and LDPC decoders with decoding throughput up to 1000 Gbps in 16 nm FinFET CMOS were presented [5]. This paper shows yet another high-speed decoder for IEEE 802.11n with n = 648 and k = 540, which processes up to 1218 Gbps of encoded codeword bits, corresponding to the user data rate of 1015 Gbps (R = 0.8333). We do not consider algorithm optimizations in this work, and we use the standard min-sum decoding procedure only [6], [7]. All optimizations are performed on the RTL code, synthesis, and floorplan stages. We aim to realize a decoder that achieves the highest possible throughput in state-of-the-art 28 nm CMOS. Power and area optimizations are not considered, and the central aspect targeted here is to reduce the timing of critical paths, which corresponds to clock and data rate maximization. Although LDPC is well known and many massively parallel decoders exist, we could design a decoder that achieves one of the highest throughputs reported in the literature so far. We avoid complicated processing on the algorithm level. The standard min-sum approach is employed.
II. RELATED WORKS
This paper is closely related to the work presented in [2], where the authors have realized in hardware all LDPC decoding iterations in a single pipeline. This allowed achieving an extremely high data rate and super high-energy efficiency. Although the work presented in [2] proposed new trends in decoder implementations, this solution brings two problems. Firstly, the unrolled decoders are extremely large compared to standard layered-LDPC schemes. Secondly, it is challenging to support variable code-rate, which is demanded for wireless communication to support adaptive modulation and coding schemes on the fly. Due to technology node reduction, unrolled decoders' silicon area is no longer a braking issue, and the benefits of FEC unrolling can be used in practical implementations. Additionally, the problem of fixed code-rate can be easily solved by unrolling polar decoders, and by manipulating the positions and the number of frozen bits. Thus, we strongly believe that the idea of FEC unrolling in hardware is interesting and need to be continuously researched. We already tried to reflect the unrolling idea from [2] in 28 nm CMOS achieving 550 Gbps in [8]. In this paper, we redesign the decoding pipeline of LDPC codes, and we prove that the unrolled decoders can work even faster, but the pipelining

A. Step 1 - unrolled architecture
Fig. 1 depicts the top view of the decoder. In contrast to [8], we insert additional flip-flops for all signals exchanged between the iterations. Although all outputs from every module contain output registers, we insert additional flipflops to relax the timing between the iterations. Therefore, the floorplanning tool can more flexibly place the particular iteration-decoders in the chip. From the synthesis perspective, all the additional inserted flip-flops cause only area overhead and do not increase the clock frequency. The advantages of these flip-flops are visible only after floorplanning when the tool uses the additional timing margins for placing the hardware in the physical chip area. The lack of these registers was the primary source of clock reduction in the design presented in [8].
B. Step 2 – seven-stages decoding pipeline
After solving the timing issues between the iterations, improving the critical paths in the single iteration hardware was also possible. Therefore, we proposed processing with a 7-stages pipeline, instead of 2, 3, and 4 stages like employed in [2], [8], [11], [12]. Thus, our decoder processes 7 frames

Each iteration requires three values at the input (“Var. to check in,” „Soft In,” and „Channel Values Input,” as shown in Fig. 2). In our case, the data uses 4-bit quantization, and with a codeword length of 648 bits, 7776 input wires need to be routed as an input to every iteration. The same amount of signals needs to be placed on the output interface. “Var. to check in” signal is used to extract the extrinsic information from the soft values. In our case, we divide this computation into two clock cycles. Firstly, we only negate the U2-encoded value, and in the 2nd clock cycle, we add this value to respective soft values. After that, the variable node logic is run, and the resulting signals are converted to sign-magnitude representation. Such representation is preferred for minsearch modules. In the case of the WLAN (648,576) code, the modules need to find the minimum in a set of 22 values for each check node. This operation uses a standard binary comparison tree with ⌈log 22⌉ = 5 stages. The min-search tree finds the minimum in two clock cycles. With the min values, the check nodes can update their messages, and the soft bit values on the output are computed using the delayed channel values. After that, the next iteration can be started. One iteration takes exactly seven cycles, and therefore it is possible to achieve short critical paths. This is the critical element to achieve high clock frequency and the resulting throughput in our design.
In general, the architecture of our decoder is very similar to the solution published in [8], with the difference that the pipeline is optimized according to the timing results obtained after floorplanning, not after the synthesis as made in [8]. This allowed us to estimate the critical paths more precisely and define the pipeline with seven stages. This is the first unrolled LDPC decoder that uses such a long pipeline to the best of our knowledge.

C. Synthesis and floorplanning
Chip synthesis follows a standard methodology, where the maximum clock frequency is found, and the resulting design is stored into a netlist for further processing in floorplanning tools. At the synthesis stage, the design is processed as any other standard design, and no unique settings are applied. In contrast to [8], we changed the strategy for floorplanning. Instead of constraining the chip-die initially, we set the core size to 10 mm2, and we estimate the maximal clock frequency that can be achieved with such a design. Then, we measure the resulting core and constrain the area to 7 mm2. After that, we reduce the core area by 0.5 mm2 iteratively until the clock frequency and metal spacing violations are insignificant so that we can fix these issues in later processing. This allowed us to reduce the core area to 5.49 mm2 without any reduction in the clock frequency. We were able to increase the logic density, and as a result, we achieved almost tripled throughput compared to [8]. At the same time, the chip area is preserved on a comparable level. There is no visible area overhead from the additional pipeline registers due to the low logic density achieved in [8]. The main overhead area comes from the routing structures realized in the metal layers, not the logic elements. In our case, the core utilization is increased to 36%, as compared to [8], where 30% is achieved.

C. Comparison to the results from the literature
Table I compares our implementation to high-speed LDPC decoders published in the literature recently. We compare only the decoders realized in 28 nm and 16 nm technologies. Our implementation significantly outperforms all other solutions realized in comparable 28 nm technologies in almost every aspect. Only the solution in 16 nm [5] shows higher area efficiency (throughput/area). However, the data rate achieved by us is definitely the highest. The elementary modifications of the decoding pipeline allowed us to increase the clock frequency almost triple times, which leads to a linear increase in the decoding throughput by the same factor.
V. CONCLUSION
The decoder presented in this paper is probably the fastest LDPC decoder published so far, to the best of our knowledge. The most exciting aspect is utilizing the state-of-the-art minsum decoding scheme, a standard 802.11n parity matrix, and a short codeword length of 648 bits only. The achieved throughput is higher than in any other previous work, but the implementation area is preserved at a level of 5.49 mm2. Thus, this paper proves that there is still a prospect for uncomplicated decoding architectures. We can achieve high decoding throughput by avoiding all the complicated LDPC solutions introduced in recent years. A carefully designed pipeline for belief propagation with min-sum approximation allows achieving extremely high data rates and can outperform many other techniques intended for future ultrahigh-speed applications.
