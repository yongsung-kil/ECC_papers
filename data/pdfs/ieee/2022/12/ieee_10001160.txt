Mutual Information-Maximizing Quantized Layered
Min-Sum Decoding of QC-LDPC Codes

Abstract—In this paper, we propose a mutual informationmaximizing quantized layered min-sum (MIM-QLMS) decoder for quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes. Our proposed decoder operates similarly to a layered min-sum decoder with additional reconstruction and quantization operations by using single-input lookup tables (LUTs). In particular, we first develop the protograph-based MIM density evolution to design the LUTs, which may differ for each iteration and each edge in the protograph of the QC-LDPC codes. Furthermore, to minimize the memory requirement for storing the LUTs, we propose an optimization method to unify all LUTs into only four distinct LUTs, which can be used for all decoding iterations. To the best of our knowledge, the proposed MIM-QLMS decoders are the first class of layered finite alphabet iterative decoders (FAIDs) that are designed based on accurately tracking the probability distributions of the exchanged messages. Simulation results show that for 3bit (resp. 4-bit) exchanged message precision, the proposed MIMQLMS decoders can reasonably (resp. generally) outperform the state-of-the-art layered FAIDs and the layered normalized minsum decoder, in terms of both the error rate performance and the average number of iterations.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] have been widely applied to various practical systems, such as the fifth-generation (5G) communication systems and the data storage systems [2], [3], due to their capacity approaching performance [4]. Recently, the finite alphabet iterative decoders (FAIDs) [5]– [12] with non-uniform quantization schemes using coarsely quantized messages attract much attention due to their excellent error rate performance and low decoding complexity. These decoders exchange the messages represented by symbols from finite alphabets for decoding and adopt the lookup tables (LUTs) for the node operations, which aims to maximize the mutual information (MI) between the coded bits and the exchanged messages within the decoders. More specifically, the LUTs of the FAIDs in [5]–[8], [10] are designed based on discrete density evolution (DE) [4] and dynamic programming (DP) [13]. Furthermore, in [9] and [12], the non-uniform quantization scheme is implemented based on the information bottleneck (IB) method rather than DP to design the LUTs of the IB decoders. In addition, the state-of-the-art FAID [11] follows a reconstruction-computation-quantization (RCQ) paradigm and constructs the LUTs using the greedy hierarchical dynamic quantization scheme which has less computational complexity.

Most of the FAIDs, e.g., [5]–[8], [12], focus on decoding the LDPC codes with the flooding schedule [14]. Compared to the flooding schedule, the layered/shuffled schedules [15], [16] are preferred in many practical applications due to their fast convergence speed and efficient resource utilization. However, there are only a few works [9]–[11] considering the design of FAIDs with layered/shuffled schedules. For example, Mohr et al. only presented an overview of the layered IB decoder in [9] for decoding the regular LDPC codes without providing the design details. The shuffled mutual information-maximizing quantized min-sum (MIM-QMS) decoder in [10] can only handle the case that the quantized channel outputs have the same precision (bit width) as that of the exchanged messages within the decoder. This restricts the application scenarios of the shuffled MIM-QMS decoder. The layered RCQ decoder in [11] was initially designed based on a high-precision uniform channel quantization. Hence using the coarsely quantized channel outputs for decoding is highly possible to cause performance loss. Moreover, the design methods in [10] and [11] fail to accurately track the probability distributions of the exchanged messages, which may also result in performance degradation.
In this paper, we propose a mutual information-maximizing quantized layered min-sum (MIM-QLMS) decoder, which is essentially a type of FAIDs, for decoding quasi-cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes. The proposed MIM-QLMS decoder operates similarly to a layered min-sum (MS) decoder [16] with additional reconstruction and quantization operations by using single-input LUTs. Compared to the layered FAIDs in the literature, our proposed decoder is flexible in setting arbitrary precision for the exchanged messages and the channel outputs. Moreover, we design the MIM-QLMS decoders by accurately tracking the probability distributions of the exchanged messages. Specifically, we first propose the protograph-based MIM-DE (PMIM-DE) to design the LUTs associated to each iteration and each edge in the protograph for decoding the QCLDPC codes, referring to as the edge-specific design. We then propose an optimization method to unify all LUTs into only two reconstruction LUTs and two quantization LUTs, which can be used for all decoding iterations, referring to as codespecific design. Compared to the edge-specific design or those FAIDs in [9]–[11], the code-specific design can significantly reduce the memory requirement for storing LUTs and greatly simplify hardware implementation. II. PRELIMINARIES A. QC-LDPC Codes
QC-LDPC codes are a class of structured LDPC codes. The parity check matrix (PCM) H of a QC-LDPC code consists of an Mb × Nb array of circulants, where a circulant is a Z × Z matrix in which each row is cyclically shifted to the right by one position compared to its preceding row. Thus, H has M = MbZ rows and N = NbZ columns. If a circulant has exact one 1entry in each row, it is called a circulant permutation matrix. The most popular QC-LDPC codes have PCMs consisting of only zero matrices or circulant permutation matrices. Each PCM of this type can be equivalently represented by a base matrix B of size Mb × Nb, where each element in B corresponds to a circulant in the PCM: integers between 0 and Z −1 specifies the position of the 1-entry in the first row of a circulant permutation matrix, while ∞ corresponds to a zero matrix.

C. Decoding Framework of an MIM-QMS Decoder
The MIM-QMS decoder [8], [10] is a class of FAIDs, where
the messages exchanged within the decoder are represented
by symbols from finite alphabets instead of by LLR values.
In this subsection, we introduce the decoding framework of a
(qch, qm, qv)-bit MIM-QMS decoder. The three positive integers qch, qm, and qv are the bit widths of the quantized channel outputs, the exchanged messages, and the a posterior messages
computed at a VN, respectively. There are two main parts: VN
update and CN update.
1) VN Update: Consider the VN update at a degree-dv VN vn. Assume that L ∈ L = [2qch ] is the quantized channel output and Si ∈ S = [2qm ] is the C2V message coming from the i-th neighbouring CN, i ∈ {1, 2, . . . , dv}. As shown by Fig. 1, the VN update of the MIM-QMS decoder consists of
three steps, i.e., reconstruction, calculation, and quantization, which aims to compute the V2C message Ri ∈ R = [2qm ] for i ∈ {1, 2, . . . , dv} and send it back to the i-th neighbouring CN. In the reconstruction step, L and each Si are mapped to the computational messages (integers of bit width > qm) based on the single-input reconstruction LUTs φch and φv, respectively. After that, the calculation step is conducted by
performing additions and subtractions on the computational
messages. More specifically, we obtain the summation Qn, which is called a posterior message of the node vn and will be used for the hard decision of vn. The other outputs are called V2C computational messages. Finally, we quantize each V2C computational message into a V2C message in R based on a
single-input quantization LUT Γv. 2) CN Update: Consider the CN update at a degree-dc CN
cm. Assume that Rj ∈ R is the V2C message coming from the j-th neighbouring VN, j ∈ {1, 2, . . . , dc}. The CN update computes the C2V message Sj ∈ S and propagates it to the j-th neighbouring VN. III. EDGE-SPECIFIC DESIGN OF MIM-QLMS DECODER BASED ON PROTOGRAPH
In this section, we first propose the decoding architecture of our MIM-QLMS decoder based on reconstruction and quantization LUTs. Following this architecture, we then develop a technique, so-called the protograph-based MIM-DE (PMIMDE), to efficiently design the LUTs via accurately tracking the pmfs of the exchanged messages.
A. Decoding Architecture
Suppose that the decoding proceeds on the base matrix B introduced in Section II-A, where each row of B corresponds to a layer. Consider the protograph representation of B [2]. Denote the i-th CN and the j-th VN in the protograph by ui for i ∈ [Mb] and wj for j ∈ [Nb], respectively. Similarly, denote the index sets of neighbouring nodes of ui and wj by N (ui) and N (wj), respectively.
Fig. 2 demonstrates the architecture of the message update related to the node cm at the i-th layer of the t-th iteration, m ∈ [M ], t ∈ {1, . . . , Tmax}. We assume that i = m/Z , N (cm) = {n1, n2, . . . , ndm }, and jk = nk/Z for each k = 1, 2, . . . , dm. We remark that the reconstruction LUT φv may differ from each edge in the protograph at each iteration, as specified by the superscripts. This is the reason that we call the decoder edge-specific. The quantization LUT Γv is potentially distinct for each layer at each iteration. The reason for considering Γv as layer-specific rather than edgespecific will be explained later. Moreover, the reconstruction LUT φch, not shown in Fig. 2, is used to initialize a posterior messages Qn, ∀n ∈ [N ]. In the following, we illustrate how to design these LUTs based on the PMIM-DE under this decoding architecture.

V. SIMULATION RESULTS
In this section, we evaluate the performance of different decoders via Monte-Carlo simulations. Due to limited space, we only consider a rate-0.9028 QC-LDPC code, which is potential to be used for the flash memory system [3]. More simulation results of different LDPC codes will be presented in the full version. The simulated QC-LDPC code is constructed according to [18] from a base matrix of size 7 × 72 with each circulant of size 256 × 256, which has degree 4 for all VNs and degrees 41 and 42 for its CNs. Assume that each codeword is modulated by BPSK and transmitted over AWGN channels. The maximum allowed number of iterations Tmax is set to 10, and at least 100 error frames are collected at each simulated SNR. We denote the floating-point layered belief propagation (LBP) decoder by LBP(∞), and describe the fixed-point decoders by a three-tuple (qch, qm, qv). Note that the normalized factor for the LNMS decoder is optimized as 0.8. The FER and the average number of iterations of different decoders are illustrated in Fig. 4. We are more interested in the performance of the code-specific MIM-QLMS decoders, since they can achieve the least memory requirement for storing decoder parameters compared to both the edge-specific MIM-QLMS decoders and the RCQ decoders. We have the following observations on Fig. 4.
(i) For the same (qch, qm, qv), the code-specific MIM-QLMS decoders can slightly outperform the edge-specific MIM-QLMS decoders in terms of both FER and average number of iterations. This is mainly because at earlier decoding iterations, the magnitude of a posterior messages computed by the edge-specific MIM-QLMS decoder is much smaller than the maximum allowed magnitude (i.e., 2qv−1 − 1) represented by qv bits. In this case, the bit width qv for the VN update is not fully used. In contrast, the code-specific MIM-QLMS decoders are naturally optimized to make full use of the qv bits in the design process for computing the posterior messages.
(ii) Compared to the RCQ decoders, the code-specific MIMQLMS decoders can achieve lower FER and smaller average number of iterations, in particular for the case of qch = 3. For example, compared to the (3, 3, 7) RCQ decoder, the (3, 3, 7) code-specific MIM-QLMS decoder can improve the FER by at most 0.06 dB and reduce the average number of iterations by up to 10%. We remark that the constructed LUTs of the RCQ decoders are naturally with real-valued elements and require extra quantization for implementing the decoder with finite precision. VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we propose to design MIM-QLMS decoders for QC-LDPC codes. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first design for layered FAIDs based on accurately tracking the probability distributions of exchanged messages. In particular, we first propose an edge-specific design of the MIM-QLMS decoder, which associates each edge in the protagraph with a potentially different reconstruction/quantization LUT at each iteration. Then, we develop a code-specific design method by unifying the edge-specific reconstruction/quantization LUTs into only four distinct LUTs: φch, φv, Γch, and Γv, which are used for all decoding iterations. Compared to the edgespecific design or those FAIDs in [9]–[11], the code-specific design can reduce the memory requirement for storing LUTs by up to TmaxNB times and thus greatly simplifies the hardware implementation. Simulation results show that under the same

(qch, qm), the code-specific MIM-QLMS decoders perform the best in terms of FER and the average number of iterations,
compared to the edge-specific MIM-QLMS decoders, the state-
of-the-art RCQ decoders, and the LNMS decoder.
