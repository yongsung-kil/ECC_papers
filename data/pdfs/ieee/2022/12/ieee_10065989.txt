Sign Aided Adaptive Noisy Gradient Descent Bit-Flipping Algorithm for LDPC Codes
Keyue Deng, Yuxing Chen, Suwen Song*, and Zhongfeng Wang* School of Electronic Science and Engineering, Nanjing University, Nanjing, China Email: kkeyuedeng@163.com, yxing.chen@outlook.com, suwsong@sina.com, zfwang@nju.edu.cn

Abstract—The adjustment factor aided noisy gradient descent bit-flipping (ANGDBF) offers a significant improvement in error correction capability compared with other gradient descent bitflipping (GDBF) variants. However, there still exists a considerable performance gap between the ANGDBF algorithm and the belief propagation (BP) algorithm. This paper presents a sign aided adaptive noisy GDBF (SANGDBF) algorithm, in which the mean of noise perturbation shows an adaptive property, facilitating the decoding process to escape from the local maxima more effectively. Moreover, the sign of the correlation term is fully utilized to improve the reliability of the inversion function. Simulation results show that the SANGDBF algorithm can achieve about 0.5 dB coding gain compared with the ANGDBF algorithm with limited hardware overhead.
Index Terms—Low-density parity-check codes, gradient descent bit flipping, adaptive noise perturbation, iterative decoding.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes were introduced by Gallager in 1962 [1] and have been widely used in communications and NAND flash memories due to their near-capacity error correction capability. Among all decoding methods, the soft-information message passing methods such as beliefpropagation (BP) [2] and the approximate min-sum (MS) [3] algorithms provide the optimal performance on the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel. However, the high computation complexity limits the throughput of decoders. On the contrary, the hard-decision bit-flipping (BF) [4] algorithm, gradient descent BF (GDBF) algorithm [5] and its variants, such as tabu-list random-penalty GDBF (TRGDBF) [6], finegrained BF (FGBF) [7], counter random GDBF (CRGDBF) [8] and probabilistic GDBF with momentum (PGDBF-w/M) [9], have less computation complexity but lead to performance loss, since only binary messages are passed and calculated. To balance the error correction performance and implementation complexity, some soft-information-aided BF algorithms have been proposed.
One branch of the soft-information-aided BF family is weighted BF (WBF) [10]. Another famous branch is GDBF [11], the essence of which is optimizing a non-linear objective function. The GDBF demonstrates better performance than WBF, but it can easily be trapped in undesirable local maximum. Aiming at this problem, the noisy GDBF [12] and tabu-list NGDBF (TNGDBF) [13] algorithms are proposed. The former introduces a random perturbation into the inversion function. The latter further employs a tabu-list to

avoid repeated flips in several consecutive iterations. More recently, Dai et al. proposed the adjustment factor aided NGDBF (ANGDBF) [14] algorithm, where an adjustment factor is employed on each syndrome, promoting the error bits to be accurately flipped. Although the ANGDBF offers a significant improvement in error correction capability over other soft-information-aided BF algorithms, the performance gap between it and BP algorithm is still non-negligible.
In this paper, we propose a novel algorithm named sign aided adaptive NGDBF (SANGDBF) algorithm. The prior works apply the noise perturbation with zero mean and variance σ2 to the inversion function. In this work, the mean of noise perturbation can be adjusted based on the historical flipping information, which enables the decoder to always escape from the local maximum. Besides, the sign of the first term of the inversion function is considered in the mean updating rule. Numerical results show that the SANGDBF could exhibit not only better error correction performance but also faster convergence speed than the ANGDBF.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. LDPC codes and the ANGDBF algorithm are presented in Section II. In Section III, the SANGDBF is illustrated in detail. Section IV shows the simulation results, including error-rate performance and convergence comparisons. Finally, Section V delivers the conclusion.
II. PRELIMINARIES
A. Notations
A binary linear LDPC code is defined by an M × N parity check matrix H that consists of elements hij over Galois field GF (2), where i ∈ [0, M −1] and j ∈ [0, N −1]. The code rate R = 1−M/N . Besides, an LDPC code can also be represented by the Tanner graph, which contains two types of nodes, that is, variable nodes (VNs) and check nodes (CNs). There is a correspondence between the matrix H and the Tanner graph. Element hij equals one means that the i-th check node is connected to the j-th variable node. Let the set M(i) = {j : hij ≠ 0} denote the VNs that connect to the i-th CN and the set N (j) = {i : hij ≠ 0} denote the neighborhoods of the jth VN. For the regular LDPC, |N (j)| = dv and |M(i)| = dc, ∀i, j, where dv and dc represent the degrees of VNs and CNs respectively. By using the binary phase shift keying (BPSK) modulation, a length-N binary vector u is mapped to a bipolar sequence c, which is transmitted through AWGN channel. III. SIGN AIDED ADAPTIVE NOISY GRADIENT DESCENT BIT-FLIPPING ALGORITHM
For GDBF algorithm, repeated flip issue is the main cause of the decoding failure [6]. For the hard-decision GDBF, since only binary messages participate in calculating energy function, several unreliable bits with the same energy value will probably be flipped in one iteration. But for the soft-information-aided GDBF, mostly only one bit has the minimum energy value in each iteration. Therefore, the soft-information-aided GDBF algorithm suffers from a more serious repeated flip issue. Adding the noise perturbation into the inversion function partly alleviates the issue but is ineffective enough. The repeated flip issue is still likely to result in decoding failure. We collected 100 error frames of the ANGDBF algorithm and recorded their flipped locations in each iteration. A dv = 3, dc = 6, R = 0.5, N = 1008 code (identified as PEGReg504×1008 in MacKay’s encyclopedia) is used and Tmax is set to 100. The flipped locations of two frames, randomly selected from 100 collected error frames, are shown in Fig. 1. Repeated flips are observed in most error frames, especially after iteration 60. This means that the ANGDBF falls into the local maximum after about 60 iterations. In addition, we found that some bits may not be flipped continuously but will be flipped at intervals. In view of this situation, the method in [13] that prevents the same bit from being flipped between two adjacent iterations is useless. Actually, not just the flip of the previous iteration, the flip of several iterations ago should impact the current iteration.
In the ANGDBF algorithm, the mean of noise perturbation qj is fixed to zero, which means that bits with different flipping behavior are treated the same. Therefore, we propose to divide the noise perturbation qj into several types with different means according to the historical flipping information. To give them more chances to be flipped and hence improve the reliability of the inversion function, VNs are divided into two classes by the sign of correlation term. VNs satisfying sign(xjyj) = +1 belong to the class 1, and VNs satisfying sign(xjyj) = −1 belong to the class 2. The noise perturbation with a smaller mean should be added to the inversion function of class 1, in which most bits are never flipped. So far, 2l+1 kinds of noise perturbation are needed. After 2l mean values are derived for class 2 through simulation, the other 2l are obtained by subtracting a constant δ to reduce the complexity of parameter selection. Parameter settings will be given in Section IV. As shown in orange bars of Fig. 3, the percentage of error bits that have never been flipped decreases to around 40%, which indicates the effectiveness of the proposed method. IV. SIMULATION RESULTS A. Error-Rate Performance Comparison

The simulation was evaluated on the rate 1/2 PEGReg504×1008 code and PEGirReg504×1008 code which are used in previous relevant works, e.g. [12], [13], [14]. The optimal values of parameters are found based on analysis and Monte Carlo simulation. According to the settings in [14], we fix λ1 = −0.3, λ2 = 1, and θ = 0. The smaller value of l means that fewer hardware resources are consumed. Considering both decoding performance and hardware implementation, l = 2 is a good choice. Fig. 4 shows the bit-error-rate (BER) performances of several algorithms on the PEGReg504×1008 code. All BF-based algorithms are with Tmax = 100. The performance of MS algorithm with Tmax = 5 is also included. As can be seen, the advantage of the ANGDBF algorithm with adaptive mean over the ANGDBF algorithm is about 0.25 dB at BER = 10−6, where η={1.5, 1, 0.5, 0}. The SANGDBF algorithm with η={1.5, 1, 0.5, 0} and δ = 0.3 can achieve around 0.5 dB coding gain compared with the ANGDBF algorithm at BER = 10−6. The trend of the frame-error-rate (FER) for PEGReg504×1008 code is similar to that of the BER, which is not shown here for the sake of aesthetics. Fig. 5 indicates the error-rate vs. Eb/N0 with different algorithms for the PEGirReg504×1008 code, where the dashed lines represent the FER and the solid lines represent the BER. It is clearly observed that the SANGDBF algorithm still has the best decoding performance. And the advantage of the SANGDBF would be more obvious with the increase of Eb/N0.

Fig. 6 shows the value of f (x) vs. the iteration number at Eb/N0 = 4 dB with the same noisy received message on the PEGReg504×1008 code. Tmax is set to 150. The global maximum value of f (x), marked as the gray line, is fmax ≈ 1490.75. After 50 iterations, the f (x) of GDBF flattens out. Although the values of f (x) for NGDBF and ANGDBF continue to rise with the help of the random perturbation after 50 iterations, they are trapped in the local maximum after 60 iterations unfortunately. V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we propose an improved decoding algorithm, denoted as SANGDBF, for the LDPC soft-information-aided BF decoding. By introducing adaptability to the mean of noise perturbation, the ability to escape from the local maxima is enhanced. Besides, the sign of the correlation term is considered in the mean updating rule, which improves the reliability of the inversion function, providing a greater flipping chance to the error bits that have not been flipped. Simulation results

and analyses demonstrate that the SANGDBF algorithm is more promising than the ANGDBF algorithm, considering decoding performance, convergence speed, and computational complexity integrally.
