Joint MIMO Detection and LDPC Decoding Via Enhanced Belief Propagation for 5G-NR
Jing Qian∗, Sha Hu†, and Hao Wang∗ ∗Huawei Technologies Co., Ltd., Beijing, China †Huawei Lund Research Center, Lund, Sweden
Email: qianjing3@huawei.com, hu.sha@huawei.com, hunter.wanghao@huawei.com

Abstract—In this paper, we consider joint MIMO detection and LDPC decoding on a tripartite factor graph. Conventional method in this regard design a belief propagation (BP) detector that applies an matched filtering detection per layer by subtracting interferences from other layers, which is suboptimal. Further, the BP scheduling between detection and decoding is unoptimized, which makes the interaction less effective. To overcome these issues, we propose to enhance the BP based joint detection and decoding (BP-JDD) receiver with 4 different techniques: pre-filtering (PF), partial marginalization (PM), hybrid updating schedule (HUS) and damping. Among them, PF and PM can improve the BP detection under challenging conditions, such as correlated MIMO channels, high-order modulations, or high code-rates, while HUS and damping are effective to accelerate convergence and reduce the number of overall iterations for BPJDD to succeed. Combining these 4 techniques yields an enhanced BP-JDD design that can provide significant performance gains over traditional turbo receivers, and is also robust against channel variations.
I. INTRODUCTION
In 5G new radio (NR), low density parity check (LDPC) codes [1], [2] have been introduced as a new physical layer channel coding scheme. Due to its sparse structure, factor graph (FG) based decoding algorithms on a bipartite graph such as belief propagation (BP) can be applied [3]. On the other hand, multiple input multiple output (MIMO) detection can also be seen as a bipartite graph, which can be integrated into the graph of LDPC code to form an integrated tripartite graph [4] that comprises of variable nodes (VNs), observation nodes (ONs) and check nodes (CNs). We show an example in Fig. 1. On this graph, BP based joint detection and decoding (BP-JDD) can be implemented with a unified BP algorithm, where BP based MIMO detection involves ONs and VNs, and BP based LDPC decoding involves VNs and CNs.
With a traditional turbo receiver [5], the detection and decoding processes are treated as separate parts, and extrinsic information is exchanged only after each process is completed. With BP-JDD, however, the MIMO detector and the LDPC decoder are tightly coupled as a whole, and beliefs are propagated and updated frequently on the graph. In each BP-JDD iteration, beliefs from CNs are passed to VNs used as priors in MIMO detection, and vice-visa. Advantages of BP-JDD are twofold. Firstly, accurate beliefs can be obtained with fewer iterations due to frequent interactions between detection and decoding, which yields a lower processing latency and a less complexity than a turbo receiver. Secondly, with beliefs output from decoder as priori information, the MIMO detector can

be made much simpler than what is used in a turbo receiver, which typically is a quasi maximum likelihood detection (MLD) e.g., fixed-complexity sphere-decoding (FSD) [6] or QR decomposition based M-algorithm [7] in order to have a good detection performance.
Conventional designs of BP-JDD have been considered in e.g. [4], [8]–[11]. In [4] significant performance gains are observed for LDPC coded large scale MIMO systems compared to traditional turbo receiver. Later in [8], the authors apply a layered FG algorithm to non-binary LDPC coded MIMO [9] where the beliefs of VNs are updated sequentially, and it shows that the complexity is reduced with negligible performance losses. In [10], a BP-JDD receiver is designed to achieve better performance than a turbo receiver for LDPC coded sparse code multiple access system. In addition, different scheduling schemes and convergence behaviours of BP-JDD are analysed in [11] using extrinsic information transfer charts. Nevertheless, all these conventional designs perform well under wellconditioned MIMO channels, or with low code-rates and loworder modulations such as quadrature phase shift keying (QPSK). When channel becomes ill-conditioned, or with a high code-rate and a high-order modulation such as 64 quadrature amplitude modulation (64QAM), their performances degrade and can be even worse than a turbo receiver. This is due to the facts that they all apply a per-layer matched-filtering (MF) based MIMO detection based on interference cancellation (IC), which is suboptimal under these challenging conditions, and the BP scheduling between the detection and the decoding is unoptimized and not robust against channel variations.
To overcome these issues, we propose to enhance conventional BP-JDD designs with 4 techniques: pre-filtering (PF), partial marginalization (PM), hybrid updating scheme (HUS) and damping. Due to the nature of short cycles on the graph of MIMO detection, PF is necessary to reduce the weights from other VNs connected to the ON associated to a current VN. This can relieve the impacts from short cycles and also the error-propagation in IC. When channel is strongly correlated, PF can be less effective, and PM [12] can be applied, which utilizes the principle of subspace marginalization via IC. PM improves BP detection by jointly processing two or more ONs or VNs together. This increases the detection complexity, but it can be still much simpler than a conventional quasi-MLD, and the number of nodes to be jointly processed can be kept small, e.g., 2, to yield a good performance-complexity tradeoff. C. Hybrid Updating Scheme

Since detection and decoding are highly coupled in BP-JDD receiver, finding an optimal updating schedule of beliefs is crucial. In this section, we compare three different scheduling schemes: PUS, SUS and HUS.
With PUS [4], detection and decoding are running in parallel, and the tripartite graph in Fig. 1 can be split into two small graphs, i.e., one includes ONs and VNs for BP detection, and the other includes VNs and CNs for BP decoding. Beliefs are exchanged after each small graph finishes one iteration. PUS shares more similarity to a conventional turbo receiver, with each BP-JDD iteration consisting of one detection iteration for all channels in a block, and one decoding iteration. That is, the beliefs on VNs are computed only with updated LO2V in the detection stage, or updated LC2V in the decoding stage as in (13).
While with SUS [8], beliefs are updated at a per VN basis, and detection and decoding are running crossly. For instance, as in Fig. 1, the BP-JDD starts with detecting the first VN b1, which gets 4 beliefs LOq2V1 , (q = 1, 2, 3, 4) from BP detection, and they are summed up to obtain the belief Ldem,Vi as in (9), which is then propagated to CNs connected to b1. Afterwards, the BP decoding updates all beliefs as in (10) and send back to all VNs affected. Then, the BP-JDD moves to the second VN b2, and so on and forth, until reach the last VN, which completes an SUS iteration. As seen, SUS is more complex than PUS with a higher complexity and also processing latency.
Comparing PUS with SUS, when the BP detector is powerful and beliefs LO2V are with good qualities, SUS outperforms PUS and converges faster, since it exchanges beliefs between detection and decoding more timely and can prohibit error propagation. Otherwise, SUS is inferior to PUS since it only utilizes partial information of a complete decoding iteration, while PUS applies more accurate information from the decoder with running a complete decoding iteration. In general, SUS is expected to converge slower than PUS with less oscillations around the optimum solution, due to the fact that the beliefs from ONs and CNs are correcting each other and the beliefs change slower than PUS. On the contrary, PUS moves faster to the optimum point, but can cause large oscillations around the optimum, which leads to a decoding failure.

IV. NUMERICAL RESULTS
In this section, we evaluate the enhanced BP-JDD for LDPC coded OFDM-MIMO systems under 4×4 MIMO by comparing the block error rate (BLER) performance. A link level simulator is developed to support 5G-NR standards, and channel modelling with correlation is configured according to [2]. We use LMMSE as PF and initialize the beliefs on VNs with LLRs generated from LMMSE. The damping factors in Sec. III-D are set to δ1 = 0.6, δ2 = 0.8, γ1 = γ2 = 0.5 which are found through simulations and used for all simulations. We also compare different scheduling schemes with the same maximal number of BP-JDD iterations, which is 30. While with HUS, in the iterations with PUS it only runs BP decoding and skips BP detection for complexity-savings, with the iteration number set to 81, followed by the SUS with a maximal iteration number

In Fig. 4, it can be seen that with 64QAM and a 0.5 LDPC code, the proposed BP-JDD outperforms turbo LMMSE and turbo FSD receivers by 0.5dB and 0.3dB, respectively. With HUS, BP-HUS firstly runs the decoder 8 times and then runs BP-SUS for maximally 22 iterations, and it is shown to be superior to both BP-SUS and BP-PUS. Note that BP-HUS also has a less complexity compared to BP-SUS. Further with LMMSE based PF, BP-HUS-PF improves BP-HUS by around 0.2dB at 10% BLER.

V. SUMMARY
We have considered designing BP-JDD receiver on an integrated tripartite factor graph for joint MIMO detection and LDPC decoding, by proposing enhancements with 4 techniques: PF, PM, HUS and damping. PF is introduced to reduce the impacts of short cycles in BP detection, while PM aims to improve BP detection under correlated MIMO channels or with high-order modulation, and also to cope high LDPC code-rate where BP decoding only provides marginal decoding gains to assist MIMO detection. Further, by optimizing the BP scheduling between detection and decoding with HUS, BP-JDD is more efficient than conventional SUS and PUS scheduling. Furthermore, damping factors are also introduced to relieve the potential error propagation in IC process and improve the convergence of BP iterations.
