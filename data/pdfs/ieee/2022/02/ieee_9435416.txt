Reliability Ratio-Based Serial Algorithm of LDPC Decoder for Turbo Equalization Schemes
Sirawit Khittiwitchayakul , Watid Phakphisut , and Pornchai Supnithi
School of Engineering, King Mongkut’s Institute of Technology Ladkrabang, Bangkok 10520, Thailand
Serial decoding algorithms of low-density parity-check (LDPC) code converge efficiently with low errors. Previously, a serial decoding algorithm, named a shuffled belief-propagation (SBP), was applied in turbo equalization of bit-patterned magnetic recording (BPMR) systems. With the SBP algorithm, an LDPC decoder converged twice as fast as one using conventional BP algorithms. We further improved the convergence speed of SBP by updating the messages in an adaptive order, which played a flexible role throughout decoding. We proposed two adaptive-serial algorithms for LDPC codes in turbo equalization. One updated the messages using the extrinsic loglikelihood ratio (LLR) and the result of the parity-check equation checking. The second contained an additional rule that tracked the LLR sign changes in each iteration. Both algorithms converged faster and with lower bit error rates (BERs) than the SBP and previous adaptive-serial algorithms in a BPMR system with media noise.
Index Terms— Belief propagation, bit patterned media, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, serial algorithm, turbo equalization.

I. INTRODUCTION
L OW-DENSITY parity-check (LDPC) code [1] is currently the error-correcting code in magnetic recording system [2] since it can achieve the decoding performance close to the Shannon limit using a belief propagation algorithm [3]. The flooding belief propagation (FBP) algorithm is the most straightforward algorithm, which updates all variable nodes and check nodes simultaneously. Several non-simultaneous or serial algorithms [4]–[10] were addressed to improve the convergence speed and error performance. A layered belief propagation (LBP) algorithm was designed by Hocevar [4], where each check node and its connected variable nodes were updated serially in a pre-defined order. Another approach was the shuffled belief propagation (SBP) [5] which updated the variable node and its connected check nodes in a serial manner. The convergence speeds of LBP and SBP algorithms were twice as fast as those of the FBP algorithm.
Although the LBP and SBP algorithms showed effective convergence speeds, the acceleration gains were limited by the fixed (not adaptively scheduled) order. To further increase the convergence speed, the variable nodes or check nodes can be updated serially in an adaptive order [6]–[10]. The checkto-variable residual belief propagation (CV-RBP) [6] and its counterpart VC-RBP algorithms [7] adaptively updated the check nodes and variable nodes. A residual value, the absolute difference between the message generated in the current and previous updates, was used to generate the update order. An improved version of VC-RBP, the node-wise VC-RBP (NVC-RBP) algorithm, was described by Kim et al. [7]. Moreover, some further improved algorithms based on residual value were also described by Lee et al. [8] and Liu et al. [9]. Since the residual-based algorithms [6]–[9] require the residual values of all edges in the Tanner graph, the computational

complexity increases. Sun et al. [10] omitted the residual operations in their reliability-based-layered belief propagation (RBL-BP) algorithm. The received absolute LLRs from the channel were used to generate the variable node update orders instead of the residual values.
In the magnetic recording system, the LBP and SBP algorithms were studied in the turbo equalization of the bit-patterned magnetic recording (BPMR) system with media noise [11]: they achieved significant coding gains over the FBP algorithm. Liu et al. [12] added a stopping criterion to the SBP algorithm to reduce the number of updated variable nodes. The property of faster convergence speeds of the SBP algorithm was further confirmed in the BPMR system with multiple islands per read-head model [12].
Previous works [11], [12] used the SBP algorithm straightforwardly in the turbo equalization, and the adaptive-serial algorithms were not considered. Hence, we examined the bit error rates (BERs) of the RBL-BP algorithm [10] in the turbo equalization and observed that the extrinsic LLR of the LDPC decoder determined the update orders better than the LLR obtained from the Bahl–Cocke–Jelinek–Raviv (BCJR) detector. Therefore, we proposed two adaptive-serial algorithms based on the extrinsic LLR of the LDPC decoder in the turbo equalization. The first algorithm, the reliability ratio-based belief propagation (RR-BP) algorithm, selected the variable nodes to be updated according to its extrinsic LLR and the result of parity-check equation checking. The second, the improved RR-BP (IRR-BP) algorithm, tracked the variable nodes having the LLR sign changes in each iteration. Our simulations showed that RR-BP and IRR-BP algorithms, in a BPMR system with media noise, converged faster and lower BERs than the SBP, NVC-RBP, and RBL-BP algorithms.

VI. CONCLUSION
We proposed two algorithms, RR-BP and IRR-BP, in turbo equalization, in which the update criteria were introduced to arrange the variable nodes, updated in each iteration. Both RR-BP and IRR-BP converged faster, with lower BERs than previous serial algorithms. Our simulations on the BPMR system, with media noise, showed that the coding gains of the RR-BP were about 0.27 versus FBP, 0.14 versus SBP, and 0.1 dB versus RBL-BP. Similarly, the coding gains became 0.37, 0.24, and 0.2 dB when IRR-BP was applied.
