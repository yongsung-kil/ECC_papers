On the Construction of QC-LDPC Codes Based on Integer Sequence With Low Error Floor
Xiongfei Tao , Xin Chen , and Bifang Wang

Abstract— Quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes have efficient hardware implementation and excellent correcting performance. However, the existence of trapping sets can affect the decoding performance. In this letter, a novel class of QC-LDPC codes based on integer sequence with column weight 3 and girth at least 8 is proposed. If the numbers in the sequence are different, the QC-LDPC codes free of elementary trapping sets (a, b) with a ≤ 10 and b ≤ 3 can be constructed. The row weight of the parity check matrix can be arbitrary and we give a lower bound of the circulant permutation matrix (CPM) size. Simulation results show that the generated codes have good performance with low error floor over AWGN channels.
Index Terms— Quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes, integer sequence, elementary trapping set, girth.
I. INTRODUCTION
L OW-DENSITY Parity-Check (LDPC) code is a class of linear block code proposed by Gallager in 1962, which performs close to the Shannon limit [1]. As an important class of LDPC codes, Quasi-cyclic low-density paritycheck (QC-LDPC) codes have received much attention from researchers due to their linear efficient hardware coding implementation and excellent error performance. However, the phenomenon of error floor, an abrupt degradation in the frame error rate (FER) performance of an iterative decoder in the high signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) region, will lead to decoding failures. The error floor is mainly attributed to the presence of a kind of topology structure called trapping set [2]. And the cycles in the Tanner graph are the main factor for the existence of the trapping sets.
While designing QC-LDPC codes, many studies focused on eliminating small trapping sets. Diouf et al. improved the progressive-edge-growth (PEG) algorithm to obtain LDPC codes free of (5, 3) trapping sets and decreased the number of (6, 4) trapping sets [3]. Asvadi et al. proposed a lifting method to eliminate the constituent short cycles of LDPC codes so that the dominant trapping sets were removed [4]. Tao et al. constructed QC-LDPC codes free of some small trapping sets by controlling cycles [5]. Moreover, several

Hashemi et al. proposed an exhaustive counting algorithm for the ETSs of regular LDPC codes [7], which laid the foundation for the design of QC-LDPC codes without ETSs. Nguyen et al. established a database of ETSs called trapping set ontology (TSO) [8]. Amirzade et al. presented analytical lower bounds on the size of ETSs of regular LDPC codes [9], and an edge-coloring technique to characterize and avoid ETSs [10].
It is well established that the length of the shortest cycles in the Tanner graph, called girth, should be at least six to obtain good performance of QC-LDPC codes. Many studies regarded eliminating cycles of lengths less than 8 as a good strategy. A large number of construction methods of QC-LDPC codes with girth at least 8 have been proposed to eliminate small trapping sets in the Tanner graph [3], [5], [11]–[14]. In this letter, we present a construction for QC-LDPC codes with column weight 3 and girth g ≥ 8 based on integer sequence. Small (a, b) ETSs with a ≤ 10 and b ≤ 3 in the Tanner graph can be eliminated.
The remainder of this letter is organized as follows. Section II presents a novel construction of QC-LDPC codes free of small tapping sets with girths at least 8. In Section III, we demonstrate that the generated codes are free of (a, b) ETSs with a ≤ 10 and b ≤ 3. Section IV gives construction examples and compares their error performance with QC-LDPC codes constructed by other methods. Section V is the conclusion.

In mathematics, a Golomb ruler is a set of marks at integer
positions along an imaginary ruler such that no two pairs of marks are the same distance apart. A set of integers Am = {a1, a2, . . . , am}, a1 < a2 < . . . < am, is a Golomb ruler if and only if ∀ i, j, k, l ∈ {1, 2, . . . , m}, ai − aj = ak − al ∧ i = j ⇔ i = k ∧ j = l. In other words, for any different pair of numbers in a Golomb ruler set, the difference is unique.

III. SMALL TRAPPING SETS IN THE TANNER GRAPH
An (a, b) trapping set is defined as a set of a variable nodes and b check nodes with odd-degree in the subgraph induced by these variable nodes. If all check nodes have either degree one or two, a trapping set is called an elementary trapping set (ETS). Nguyen et al. used line-point representations to illustrate ETSs and conveniently establish their topological relations [8]. In order to distinguish (a, b) ETSs with nonisomorphic line-point representations, we use (a, b){i} to index each representation. In this line-point representation, check nodes correspond to points and variable nodes correspond to lines. Moreover, degree-2 check nodes are denoted by “◦” and degree-1 check nodes are denoted by “•”.
Theorem 3: If the numbers in SL are different, the generated codes are free of small (a, b) ETSs with a ≤ 10 and b ≤ 3.
Proof: Based on Theorem 1, the length of cycles in the parity check matrix H is at least 8, and an 8-cycle, i.e. (4, 4) ETS can only exist in the submatrix Hi. Since the column weight of QC-LDPC codes in this letter is three, any variable node in the Tanner graph is connected to three check nodes; thus, in the corresponding parity check matrix, two check nodes are located on the two block rows of the submatrix Hi, and another check node is located on one block row of the submatrix Di. For ease of description, we use Ci1 and Ci2 to represent the check nodes on the two block rows of the submatrix Hi respectively. And we use CD to represent the check nodes on the block rows of the submatrix Di.
The study by [8] indicated that larger ETSs can be obtained by adjoining variable nodes to smaller ETSs, and this process is called evolution.
A. (5, 3) Elementary Trapping Sets
Based on the research in [8], a girth-8 (3, k) LDPC code has only one form (5, 3) ETS, i.e., (5, 3){2} ETS, which is formed by adding one variable node to a (4, 4) ETS.

Fig. 2. Line-point representations of 8-cycles and (6, 4) ETSs with girth 8: (a) 8-cycles. (b) (6, 4){1} ETS. (c) (6, 4){2} ETS.
Fig. 2(a) shows a (4, 4) ETS formed by an 8-cycle on the two block rows of the submatrix Hi, and the four degree-1 check nodes are all CD. According to the structure of the submatrix Di, any two CD check nodes cannot be connected through a variable node, which means that no (5, 3) ETSs can be formed by adding one variable node to the (4, 4) ETS.
B. (7, 3) Elementary Trapping Sets
The study by [8] indicated that (7, 3) ETSs are direct successors of (5, 3) ETSs and (6, 4) ETSs. Since (5, 3) ETSs are removed from the Tanner graph, (7, 3) ETSs can only evolve from (6, 4) ETSs. The line-point representations of (6, 4) ETSs are illustrated in Fig. 2(b)-(c).
For the (6, 4){1} ETS shown in Fig. 2(b), it contains two 8-cycles. Without loss of generality, suppose the outer 8-cycle exists in Hi, so the inner 8-cycle L1 can only be obtained from both His and Dis, which will contradict Theorem 1. Therefore, (6, 4){1} ETSs do not exist in the Tanner graph.
For the (6, 4){2} ETS shown in Fig. 2(c), no variable nodes can be connected to C1 and C2 owing to the nonexistence of (5, 3) ETSs. The condition of girth eight prevents any variable nodes from being connected to C3 and C4. Furthermore, a variable node that is connected to C1 and C3, C1 and C4, C2 and C3, C2 and C4 violates the conclusion that 8-cycles can only exist in the submatrix His. Consequently, no (7, 3) ETSs can be formed by adding a variable node to the (6, 4){2} ETS.
Therefore, the generated codes are free of (7, 3) ETSs.
C. (9, 3) Elementary Trapping Sets
From [8], we can know that (9, 3) ETSs can only evolve from (8, 4) ETSs. Fig. 3(a)-(i) show the line-point representations of (8, 4) ETSs with girth 8 [15]. It can be seen that the (8, 4) ETS shown in Fig. 3(a) evolve from (5, 3){1} ETS and all the (8, 4) ETSs shown in Fig. 3(b)-(d) evolve from (6, 4){1} ETS. Since the generated QC-LDPC codes are free of (5, 3) and (6, 4){1} ETSs, (8, 4) ETSs shown in Fig. 3(a)-(d) do not exist.
We now consider Fig. 3(e). The 8-cycle L2 can only exist in the submatrix Hi. None of the three check nodes which are connected by dotted line can be CD, which will contradict the structure of the matrix H. Thus, the (8, 4) ETS shown in Fig. 3(e) does not exist.
For the (8, 4) ETSs shown in Fig. 3(f ) and (g), all degree-1 check nodes are CD. Since any two CD check nodes cannot be connected through a variable node; thus, no (9, 3) ETSs can be formed by adding a variable node to the (8, 4) ETSs.

to C1 and C2 or C2 and C4. A variable node that is connected to C2 and C3 will lead to a 10-cycle in the submatrix Hj, and it will violate the fact that the length of cycles in the submatrix His is 4, 8, 12, etc. Furthermore, the Tanner graph formed by adding a variable node connected to C1 and C3 evolve from the (8, 4) ETS shown in Fig. 3(h). It has been proved that the (8, 4) ETS shown in Fig. 3(h) is nonexistence. Therefore, no (9, 3) ETS can be formed by adding one variable to the (8, 4) ETS shown in Fig. 3(i).
A hierarchy of ETSs originating from the (4, 4) ETS for girth-8 and column-weight-three LDPC codes was presented in the Fig. 11 of [8]. Removal of the (5, 3), (7, 3) and (9, 3) ETSs leads to the spontaneous removal of their successors, i.e. (6, 0), (6, 2), (8, 0), (8, 2), (10, 2) ETSs, and so on.
Therefore, any (a, b) ETSs with a ≤ 10 and b ≤ 3 do not exist in the Tanner graph of the generated codes.
IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
Example 1(C1): For the QC-LDPC code with row weight L = 5, since the lower bound of N is the smallest prime number that is no smaller than 2L − 1, N = 11 is selected. According to the construction method in this letter, as long as the numbers in SL are different, the girth of the Tanner graph is at least 8. The sequence SL can be randomly selected from [0, 10] with five different integers, such as SL = (0, 2, 7, 8, 10). Based on (3), (4) and (5), we can obtain the parity check matrix H; thus, a length-275 (3, 5) regular QC-LDPC code C1 can be constructed.
Example 2(C2): For the QC-LDPC code with row weight L = 6, since N ≥ 2L − 1 and N is a prime number, N = 13 is selected, and the sequence SL can be randomly selected from [0, 12] with different six different integers, such as SL = (0, 1, 3, 7, 11, 12). Based on (3), (4) and (5), a length-468 (3, 6) regular QC-LDPC code C2 with girth 8 can be constructed.
The trapping sets search algorithm is used to find the elementary trapping sets in the Tanner graph of the two regular QC-LDPC codes C1 and C2. V. CONCLUSION
In this letter, we proposed a method to construct (3, L) QC-LDPC codes with girth at least 8 and free of small ETSs.
