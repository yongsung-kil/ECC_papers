Achievable-Rate-Aware Retention-Error Correction for Multi-Level-Cell NAND Flash Memory
Yingcheng Bu , Yi Fang , Member, IEEE, Guohua Zhang , and Jun Cheng , Member, IEEE

Abstract—Owing to the effect of data retention noise in multi-level-cell NAND flash memory, the initial threshold-voltage distributions and read voltages can no longer be used to accurately calculate log-likelihood ratios (LLRs) as the retention time increases, thus causing retention errors. To solve this problem, we first utilize the so-called “correction factors” to optimize the LLR accuracy by maximizing the achievable rate of a flash-memory system without introducing extra memory-sensing operations. We further prove that the optimization of the correction factors is a convex optimization problem and can be solved analytically. To obtain the optimal correction factors, we propose two retention-error correction schemes, referred to as offline maximum-achievable-rate correction (MARC) algorithm and online MARC algorithm, which enable the flash-memory controller to utilize the corrected LLRs that are stored in a look-up table and correct the inaccurate LLRs in real time, respectively. Motivated by the variation characteristics of the threshold-voltage distributions, we also propose an enhanced expectation–maximization (EM) algorithm to reestimate their corresponding parameters, and then adjust the read voltages. By combining the enhanced EM algorithm with the MARC algorithms, an enhanced EM-based correction strategy is developed to further boost the retention-error endurance of flash memory while avoiding excessive memory-sensing overhead. Theoretical analyses and simulation results illustrate the superiority of the proposed correction mehtods in terms of the robustness against retention errors.
Index Terms—Achievable rate, data retention noise, loglikelihood-ratio (LLR) accuracy, multi-level-cell flash memory.

This trend is driven by the manufacturing process scaling and the advent of techniques that can store more than one bit of data within a cell, such as multi-level cell (MLC), triple-level cell (TLC), and quadruple-level cell (QLC) techniques [5], [6]. However, the scaling down of flash-memory chips along with the application of MLC/TLC/QLC technique makes the flash memory prone to suffer from various types of circuit-level noises [7], which causes the degraded reliability issue. Among those noises, data retention noise is the principal source of errors in the programmed memory cells [8]–[11].
To compensate the raw bit-error rate (BER) caused by various noises of flash memory, the most common solution is to apply error-correction codes (ECCs) [12], [13]. Benefiting from excellent error-correction performance with low-complexity decoding, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have appeared to be an outstanding solution for emerging flash-memory devices [14]–[20]. For LDPC codes, the belief-propagation (BP) algorithm [21] is generally applied in the decoding process, which requires high-precision initial log-likelihood ratios (LLRs) to attain excellent decoding performance. However, due to the effect of retention noise, the initial threshold-voltage distribution parameters (i.e., mean and standard derivation) and read voltages can no longer be used to accurately calculate LLRs as the retention time increases [22], [23]. Thus, to improve the decoding performance of flash memory, it is indispensable to develop some retention-error correction schemes to mitigate the effect of retention noise.
A. Related Work
To attenuate the retention errors, some state-of-the-art techniques have been presented to periodically refresh (including read, decode, and reprogram) the stored data before reaching the maximum error-correction capability of the decoder [11], [24]–[26]. One of the major drawbacks for the refresh techniques is that the data must be frequently read and reprogrammed, which has an important impact on the lifetime and performance of flash memory. Meanwhile, other retention-error correction schemes that avoid the reprogram operations have been developed in [8] and [27]. In [8], a retention optimized reading (ROR) scheme has been proposed, where the errors are reduced by adaptively applying the optimal read-voltage levels for flash memory. Jeong et al. [27] have presented a flash defibrillator (FD) scheme that can recover the stored data by recharging the charge-loss cells. Unfortunately, the aforementioned techniques require additional memory-sensing operations (i.e., reread the flash-memory cells). For this reason, a

Another solution to overcome retention errors is to employ the flash-memory channel estimation algorithm to update the suboptimal voltage-distribution parameters, which consequently facilitates the calculation of more accurate LLR information. Typically, the threshold-voltage distribution of each state is modeled as a Gaussian distribution in these estimation algorithms [28]–[31]. To estimate voltage-distribution parameters without utilizing extra memory-sensing operations, a decision directed estimation (DDE) algorithm and an RABPassisted channel update (RABP-CU) algorithm have been presented in [30] and [28], respectively, both of which require to obtain the decoded data as the a-priori information in estimation process. In addition, an expectation–maximization (EM) algorithm has been proposed for the estimation of voltage distributions [31], which does not need to acquire the decoded codeword.
B. Motivation and Contributions
Although the above methods can reduce the retention errors for flash memory, they suffer from some limitations as follows.
1) Many retention-error correction techniques, such as the refresh scheme [11], [24]–[26], ROR scheme [8], and FD scheme [27], require additional memory-sensing operations (i.e., reread the flash-memory cells) to attenuate the retention errors, thus causing significant latency and energy consumption. Although the RABP decoding scheme [28] does not introduce extra read operations, it may be only applicable for MLC flash memory employing six read voltages, which restricts its practical application.
2) To update the suboptimal voltage-distribution parameters and calculate more accurate LLRs, DDE [30] and RABP-CU [28] schemes require to obtain the decoded data for estimation. However, the decoding accuracy of these schemes has a great impact on the estimation performance. As an alternative, the EM algorithm does not need the decoded data as the a-priori information [31]. Yet, the conventional EM algorithm only estimates the voltage distributions for a given number of program-and-erase (PE) cycles without considering the effect of retention errors. Moreover, it requires relatively more read voltages to obtain the accurate read-back threshold voltages, which leads to long memory-sensing latency.
In this article, our goal is to optimize the inaccurate LLR information induced by the retention noise so as to effectively reduce the retention errors in MLC flash memory, while avoiding the limitations of the existing retentionerror correction techniques. Our contributions are summarized as follows.
1) The concept of achievable rate is introduced to characterize the LLR accuracy in flash memory. To avoid introducing additional memory-sensing operations, we propose to employ correction factors to improve the inaccurate LLRs via maximizing the achievable rate of flash memory based on initial threshold-voltage distributions and read voltages, and further prove the convexity of optimizing these correction factors.

Fig. 1. Block diagram of the proposed MLC flash-memory system.
2) To acquire the optimal correction factors, we conceive offline and online maximum-achievable-rate correction (MARC) algorithms to meet diverse application requirements. The offline MARC algorithm can precalculate the corrected LLRs for different retention time by utilizing the optimal correction factors and then store them in a look-up table (LUT). On the contrary, the online MARC algorithm can optimize the inaccurate LLRs in real time.
3) Motivated by the variation characteristics of the voltage distributions, we present an enhanced EM algorithm to estimate the voltage distributions, which reduces the number of read voltages and computational complexity compared with the conventional EM algorithm [31]. To make the MARC algorithms effective for longer retention time, we propose an enhanced EM-based correction strategy that combines the enhanced EM algorithm and MARC algorithms.
4) Comprehensive simulations are performed to verify the superiority of the proposed schemes. The results demonstrate that the proposed MARC algorithms can effectively boost the retention-error endurance of flashmemory systems. It is also illustrated that the enhanced EM-based correction strategy can further improve the tolerance against retention errors.
The remainder of this article is organized as follows. Section II introduces the system model of MLC flash memory and its preliminary foundations. The concept of achievable rate of flash memory and the proposed MARC algorithms is presented in Section III. The enhanced EM-based retentionerror correction strategy is proposed in Section IV. Simulation results and conclusions are given in Sections V and VI, respectively.

As shown, the retention noise shifts the means of the voltage distributions of the programmed states s2, s3, and s4 to the negative direction and increases their variances. In contrast, the mean and variance of the voltage distribution of the erased state s1 are not affected by the retention noise [8], [28]. Moreover, the threshold-voltage distribution of a higher voltage programmed state changes faster than that of a lower voltage programmed state [8]. Therefore, with the increase of retention time, more overlaps occur between two adjacent states, causing more errors in flash memory.
Based on the principle of the conventional detection scheme [20], [22], [28], [31], we assume that the voltagedistribution parameters are only known when the retention time equals zero, and the read voltages are selected based on these initial voltage-distribution parameters [i.e., only P(st=0) and R(t=0) are known] in this article. Both voltage-distribution parameters and read voltages are fixed with the increase of retention time unless they are updated by conducting the channel estimation algorithm. Based on this assumption, we design the retention-error correction algorithms to optimize the LLR accuracy for reducing retention errors over different retention time.

D. Overview of the Proposed Design
The relationship between the proposed MARC algorithms and the enhanced EM-based correction strategy is illustrated in Fig. 1. In actual implementation, as the retention time increases, the flash controller first attempts to employ the proposed offline and online MARC algorithms to optimize the inaccurate LLRs based on the initial voltage-distribution parameters and read voltages. When the retention noise is severe, the retention errors caused by the initial voltagedistribution parameters and read voltages may exceed the correction capability of the MARC algorithms, thus causing decoding failure. To address this issue, the enhanced EM-based correction strategy combining the enhanced EM algorithm with the MARC algorithms is proposed. It uses the enhanced EM algorithm to estimate the voltage-distribution parameters and update the read voltages so as to make the MARC algorithms effective for longer retention time, hence, further improving the retention-error endurance of flash memory.

III. ACHIEVABLE-RATE-AWARE RETENTION-ERROR CORRECTION ALGORITHMS
Many existing retention-error correction techniques [8], [11], [24]–[27] require additional memory-sensing operations, causing significant latency and energy consumption. In addition, although the RABP decoding scheme [28] does not introduce extra read operations, it may be only applicable for

MLC flash memory employing six read voltages, thus restricting its practical application. To address the above drawbacks, we develop the offline and online MARC algorithms, which not only can alleviate the effect of retention noise without introducing extra memory-sensing operations but also are suitable for the flash-memory systems using different quantization schemes and memory-sensing precisions.

C. Offline and Online MARC Algorithms

According to the above discussion, we need to obtain a

large number of coded bits and corresponding LLRs to calcu-

late the optimal correction factors for the sake of improving

the inaccurate LLRs. To this end, we propose the offline and

online MARC algorithms, which meet different flash-memory

application requirements.

The basic idea of the offline MARC algorithm is to pre-

calculate the corrected LLRs for different retention time and

store them in an LUT, where the offline MARC algorithm can

be implemented when the system is idle. The retention time

is assumed to be known at read time in the offline MARC

algorithm, because it can be traced by using counters inside

the flash-memory controller [25]. When the offline MARC

algorithm is performed, it first offline collects a sufficiently

large number of coded bits c and their corresponding LLRs

L in order to calculate the histogram Njx,q (j ∈ {msb,lsb},

q = 1, 2, . . . , Q + 1, and x ∈ {0, 1}) through the Monte-Carlo

histogram method. Then, the optimal be calculated based on the histogram sponding corrected LLR is computed

The major objective of the online MARC algorithm is to calculate the correction factors for optimizing the inaccurate LLRs in real time. Therefore, it is required to obtain the coded bits and their corresponding LLRs to compute the Monte-Carlo histogram in real time. To this end, once the first-round decoding fails, we first exploit the hard-decision result (i.e., decoded codeword cˆ) as the estimate of coded bits. Subsequently, based on the decoded codeword cˆ and its corresponding inaccurate LLR sequence Lˆ , the online MARC algorithm calculates the approximated histogram Nˆ jx,q by using the Monte-Carlo histogram method, where Nˆ jx,q is the approximation of Njx,q since the decoded codeword contains some error bits. After that, the correction factor αˆ jq,t can be computed based on the approximated histogram, and thus, the corrected LLR can be calculated as αˆ jq,tLjq,t. Finally, the second-round decoding is performed based on the corrected LLR information. Through this way, the online MARC algorithm can reduce retention errors in real time. The details of the online MARC algorithm are outlined in Algorithm 2.
According to [28], the RABP scheme also requires to execute the second-round decoding if the first-round decoding fails. However, the RABP scheme particularly targets the MLC flash-memory systems using six levels of read voltages. In this scenario, the threshold voltages are divided into four data regions and three erasure regions (i.e., the overlapped regions between every two adjacent states), where most of errors occur in the erasure regions. Therefore, through a specific way to reassign the LLRs for the cells in three erasure regions, the RABP scheme can achieve a significant gain over the conventional detection scheme [20], [22], [28], [31]. However, for the flash-memory applications using other numbers of read voltages, such as 3 and 32, the LLR reassignment method in the RABP scheme does not work [28]. Consequently, the RABP scheme only targets the flash-memory applications using six read voltages, but may be not suitable for the flash-memory applications using other numbers of read voltages (e.g., 3 and 32). This is considered as a fundamental limitation of the RABP scheme and thus, restricts its application in practice. Nevertheless, the proposed MARC algorithms are suitable for the scenarios with different memory-sensing precisions.

D. Complexity Analysis
To analyze the complexity the proposed MARC algorithms, we present their computational/performance overheads and storage overheads in this part. We further discuss the tradeoff between the offline and online MARC algorithms.
1) Computational Overhead: As for the proposed offline MARC algorithm, its computational overhead may not be important since it is implemented offline when the system is in the idle state. For the proposed online MARC algorithm, it requires to execute the second-round decoding to correct the retention errors once the first-round decoding

fails, which commonly exists in many state-of-the-art counter-

parts [7], [28], [29]. Apart from the second-round decoding,

2) Storage Overhead: For the offline algorithm, its storage

overhead depends on the LUT size, which is determined by the number of corrected LLRs recorded in the LUT.2 These LUTs

can be typically stored within the dynamic random access

memory (DRAM) of flash controller [8], where the size of

DRAM accounts for a fixed fraction of SSD capacity (e.g.,

1 G storage is used as DRAM for a 512 G SSD).

With regard to the online MARC algorithm, its storage over-

head originates from calculating the Monte-Carlo histogram

based on the decoded codeword and its corresponding LLRs.

Hence, the storage overhead of calculating the Monte-Carlo

histogram contains the overheads of storing the decoded code-

word, its corresponding LLRs, and the obtained histogram,

where the decoded codeword comprises N bits, the corre-

sponding LLRs comprises N float values, and the Monte-Carlo

histogram comprises 4(Q + 1) integer values. As a result,

the storage overheads of the decoded codeword, its corre-

sponding LLRs, and the Monte-Carlo histogram are N/8 Byte,

N ×4 Byte = 4N Byte, and 2 Byte×4(Q+1) = 8(Q + 1) Byte,

respectively.

3) Tradeoff: The offline MARC algorithm can approach

the optimal performance (see Section V-A), but requires rel-

atively high storage overhead for the LUTs to store the

corrected LLRs. In contrast, although the online MARC algo-

rithm can optimize the inaccurate LLRs in real time with lower

storage overhead, its retention-error correction capability is

inferior to that of the offline MARC algorithm. Therefore, the

offline MARC algorithm is suitable for the applications with

high-performance requirement but not high demand on storage

efficiency, while the online MARC algorithm is suitable for

the applications with the requirements of real time and low

storage overhead.

IV. ENHANCED EM-BASED CORRECTION STRATEGY
As mentioned in the preceding section, the MARC algorithms improve the LLR accuracy based on the initial voltagedistribution parameters and read voltages. When the retention noise is severe (i.e., the retention time is large), those initial voltage-distribution parameters and read voltages lead to significant degradation on the LLR accuracy, thus causing a large number of retention errors. If the number of retention errors exceeds the correction capability of the MARC algorithms, the MARC algorithms become no longer effective. As such, we develop an enhanced EM algorithm to update the voltage-distribution parameters and use them to optimize the read voltages so as to obtain more accurate LLRs for correcting the retention errors. Furthermore, we find that the updated voltage-distribution parameters and read voltages can effectively enhance the correction capability of the MARC algorithms. Consequently, we put forward an enhanced EMbased correction strategy that combines the enhanced EM algorithm with the MARC algorithms to jointly optimize the inaccurate LLRs for longer retention time.
A. Selection of Read Voltages for Estimation
Before the estimation, the read-back threshold voltages need to be obtained. Obviously, it is desired to utilize less number of read voltages while ensuring the accuracy of voltagedistribution estimation. According to [23] and [28]–[30], the variance of the erased state is larger than that of three programmed states, which illustrates that the erased state occupies a relatively large threshold-voltage space, as shown in Fig. 2. For that reason, the conventional EM algorithm requires to employ a relatively large number of read voltages for the erased state to guarantee the accurate estimation of the parameters. E. Enhanced EM-Based Correction Strategy
In this part, we propose an enhanced EM-based correction strategy that combines the enhanced EM algorithm with the MARC algorithms to further improve the retention-error endurance while avoiding the excessive memory-sensing overhead. Actually, the enhanced EM algorithm requires additional memory-sensing operations to obtain the quantized threshold voltages. Hence, there exists a limitation for the proposed correction strategy. If the enhanced EM algorithm is performed frequently, the retention errors can be reduced significantly, but it inevitably introduces excessive overhead of memorysensing operations. To prevent the excessive memory-sensing overhead, we assume that the enhanced EM algorithm is only invoked when the current average achievable rate of flash memory is less than a predefined threshold η (i.e., C(t, Ps(t=0), R(t=0))/K < η, K = 2). Based on the updated voltage-distribution functions Ps(t=tupdate) (tupdate represents the time triggering the enhanced EM algorithm), we use the three read-voltage design methods in Section III-E to update the read-voltage levels. After that, we can execute the offline MARC algorithm or online MARC algorithm to further enhance the retention-error tolerance of flash memory based on the updated read voltages R(t=tupdate) and voltage-distribution functions Ps(t=tupdate).

The simulations are carried out by using MATLAB, which is a mathematical computing environment with powerful computational capability. We employ the conventional detection scheme in [20], [22], [28], and [31] as the baseline, which detects the data with fixed LLR values and read voltages over different retention time. In the simulations, we employ the regular column-weight-3 (CW-3) protograph LDPC code [37], [38] with a code rate of 6/7, which can be constructed by using a modified progressive edge growth (PEG) algorithm [39].8 The codeword length of regular CW3 photograph LDPC code is 4000. We also assume that the codeword is decoded by an iterative BP algorithm [40] and the maximum number of iterations is set to 25. VI. CONCLUSION
To alleviate the retention errors over the retention time, the concept of achievable rate has been introduced to facilitate the characterization of LLR accuracy in flash-memory systems. Based on this metric, we have utilized the correction factors to optimize the inaccurate LLR information by maximizing the achievable rate of flash memory and have illustrated that
