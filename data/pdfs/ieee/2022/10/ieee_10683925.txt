Abstract—This paper presents a class of cyclic partial geometries, called triplet partial geometries, which are constructed based on finite fields of characteristic 2. Using these partial geometries, constant-weight codes and LDPC codes with cyclic and quasi-cyclic structure are constructed.
I. INTRODUCTION
Lately, partial geometries (PaGs) have been shown to be effective mathematical tools in constructing well performing LDPC codes with distinct geometric and algebraic structures [1], [2]. This paper presents a class of PaGs with cyclic structure which are constructed based on finite fields of characteristic 2. Each line in a PaG in this class consists of 3 points. For this reason, the PaGs in this class are referred to as triplet partial geometries (T-PaG). Besides being used as an effective tool for construction of LDPC codes, PaGs can also be used to construct constant-weight (CW) codes. CW codes under certain constraints can also be used to construct LDPC codes whose Tanner graphs have girth at least 6. In this paper, cyclic T-PaGs will be used to construct cyclic and quasi-cyclic (QC) CW and LDPC codes.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. In Section II, a basic review of PaGs is given. Section III presents the construction of cyclic T-PaGs. In Section IV, several constructions of cyclic and QC-CW codes are presented. Section V presents a construction of nonbinary QC-LDPC codes based on a cyclic T-PaG. Section VI concludes the paper with some remarks.
II. REVIEW OF PARTIAL GEOMETRIES
A combinatorial system composed of a set of N points and a set of M lines forms a partial geometry (PaG) if the following conditions are satisfied for some fixed integers r ≥ 2, t ≥ 2, and c ≥ 1 [3]–[5]: (1) Any two points are on at most one line; (2) Each point is on r lines; (3) Each line passes through t points; and (4) If a point a is not on a line L, then there are exactly c lines, each passing through a and a point on L. We denote such a PaG by PaG(r, t, c). Reference [5] shows that PaG(r, t, c) contains N = t((t − 1)(r − 1) + c)/c points and M = r((t−1)(r −1)+c)/c lines. If two lines have a common point a, we say that they intersect at a; otherwise, they are said to be parallel. Two lines in PaG(r, t, c) are either parallel or intersect at one and only one point. The lines intersecting at

a point form an intersecting bundle of lines. The lines parallel to a line form a parallel bundle of lines.
Label the points in PaG(r, t, c) from 0 to N − 1 and the lines from 0 to M − 1. Form an M × N matrix H = [hi,j]0≤i<M,0≤j<N over GF(2) with rows and columns corresponding to the M lines and N points in PaG(r, t, c), respectively. The entry hi,j of H in the i-th row and jth column equals 1 if the j-th point is on the i-th line of PaG(r, t, c); otherwise, it equals 0. The matrix H is referred to as the line-point incidence matrix of PaG(r, t, c) with column weight r and row weight t. The rows of H are called the incidence vectors of the lines in PaG(r, t, c). Since two lines in PaG(r, t, c) are either parallel or intersect at one and only one point, no two rows in H have more than one location where they both have 1s. Such a structural property of H is referred to as row-column (RC) constraint [6], which ensures that its associated Tanner graph has girth at least 6. The transpose G = HT of H is an N × M matrix with rows and columns corresponding to the points and lines of PaG(r, t, c), respectively, which is referred to as the point-line incidence matrix of PaG(r, t, c). The rows of G are referred to as the incidence vectors of the points in PaG(r, t, c). Clearly, G satisfies the RC-constraint. PaG(r, t, c) is uniquely specified by either its line-point or its point-line incidence matrix. Both H and G can be used as the parity-check or generator matrix of a linear block code. If H (or G) is a sparse (or low-density) matrix, its null space gives an LDPC code.
If the cyclic-shift of the incidence vector of a line one place to the right is the incidence vector of another line, the partial geometry PaG(r, t, c) is a cyclic PaG. In this case, PaG(r, t, c) can be specified by a set of base-lines and the others lines can be obtained by cyclically shifting the base-lines. The row space (or null space) of H (or G) gives a cyclic (or QC) code.

A CW code Ccw(N, ω, λ) meeting the upper bound is called an optimal CW code. A CW code of length N is called a balanced code [8] if the weight of each codeword in the code is equal to N/2 . V. LDPC CODES ASSOCIATED WITH T-PAGS
For m ≥ 3, the matrices H and G are low-density matrices
and satisfy the RC-constraint. Hence, these matrices or their
submatrices can be used as parity-check matrices to generate
binary LDPC codes with girths at least 6.
In the following, we give a construction of nonbinary LDPC codes. Using the base-lines in B, we form a 3 × τ1 (or 3 × (τ2 + 1)) matrix B(3, τ1) (or B(3, τ2 + 1)) over GF(2m) in which the t-th column consists of the 3 points on the line (α0, αjt , αkt ) in B. The matrix B(3, τ1) (or B(3, τ2 + 1)) is called the base matrix of PaG(2m−1 − 1, 3, 3). Let αl be a point on a base-line in B. We form a circulant CPM(αl) of size (2m − 1) × (2m − 1) in which the leftmost column v has a single nonzero component at position l with value αl and all the other 2m − 2 columns are 2m − 2 successive downward cyclic-shifts of v. All the entries in CPM(αl) are the same point αl. We call this circulant CPM(αl) as the 2m-ary uniform circulant permutation matrix (CPM) dispersion of αl. The 2mary uniform CPM-dispersions of the 3 entries α0, αjt , and αkt in the t-th column of B(3, τ1) (or B(3, τ2+1)) (the 3 points on the line (α0, αjt , αkt )), give a column of 3 2m-ary uniform CPMs, CPM(α0), CPM(αjt ), CPM(αkt ), called the 2m-ary uniform CPM-dispersion of the t-th column of B(3, τ1) (or B(3, τ2 + 1)). The 2m-ary uniform CPM-dispersions of all the columns in B(3, τ1) (or B(3, τ2 + 1)) give a 3 × τ1 array Gunif,cpm(3, τ1) (or a 3 × (τ2 + 1) array Gunif,cpm(3, τ2 + 1)) of 2m-ary uniform CPMs of size (2m − 1) × (2m − 1). The array Gunif,cpm(3, τ1) (or Gunif,cpm(3, τ2 + 1)) is called the 2mary uniform CPM-dispersion of the base matrix B(3, τ1) (or B(3, τ2 + 1)) which is a 3(2m − 1) × τ1(2m − 1) (or 3(2m − 1) × (τ2 + 1)(2m − 1)) matrix over GF∗(2m). If we take the sum of the three 2m-ary uniform CPMs in each CPM-column-block of the array Gunif,cpm(3, τ1) (or Gunif,cpm(3, τ2 +1)), we obtain a row of 2m-array Gunif,circ(τ1) (or Gunif,circ(τ2 + 1)) of uniform circulants, each with weight 3. The subscript “circ” in Gunif,circ(τ1) (or Gunif,circ(τ2 + 1)) stands for “circulant”. For 0 ≤ t < τ1 (or 0 ≤ t < τ2 + 1), the t-th uniform circulant in Gunif,circ(τ1) (or Gunif,circ(τ2 + 1)) is called the circulant-composition of the 3 2m-ary uniform CPMs in the t-th CPM-column-block of the array Gunif,cpm(3, τ1) (or Gunif,cpm(3, τ2 + 1)). We call Gunif,circ(τ1) (or Gunif,circ(τ2 + 1)) the uniform circulant-composition of Gunif,cpm(3, τ1) (or Gunif,cpm(3, τ2 + 1)) which satisfies the RC-constraint. For any positive integer k less than or equal to τ1 (or τ2 + 1), we take k circulants from Gunif,circ(τ1) (or Gunif,circ(τ2 + 1)) to form a row Gunif,circ(k) of k 2m-ary uniform circulants. The null space over GF(2m) of Gunif,circ(k) gives a 2m-ary QC-LDPC code of length k(2m − 1).
If we replace all the nonzero entries by 1s in the arrays
constructed above, we obtain binary arrays. Based on these
binary arrays, we can construct binary QC-LDPC codes of
various lengths and rates. Furthermore, the rows of these
binary arrays of CPMs or circulants form QC-CW codes.
VI. CONCLUSION AND REMARK
This paper presented a class of cyclic PaGs, called cyclic
triplet PaGs. Their structural properties have been investigated,
based on which, methods for constructing cyclic and QC-CW
and LDPC codes were proposed. It was shown that the null
space of a CW code that satisfies the RC-constraint is an LDPC
code whose Tanner graph has girth at least 6. Besides triplet PaGs presented in this paper, there are other
PaGs with cyclic or QC structure [2]. Based on these PaGs,
cyclic and QC-CW and LDPC codes can be constructed.
