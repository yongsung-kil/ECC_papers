Abstract—LDPC codes have been widely applied in highdensity flash memories, e.g., TLC flash and QLC flash, to ensure data reliability. In order to reduce the read latency of highdensity flash memories, this paper proposes a prediction-based fine-grained LDPC reading method, named as PreLDPC. From a preliminary study, we observe that the ratio of cells that lie in error-prone areas (i.e., the areas between two adjacent cell states) is closely related to the final read level for successful decoding. Based on this observation, PreLDPC predicts the read level for LDPC reading, which could avoid excessive unnecessary read-retries. Furthermore, a fine-grained read method with fine sub-levels is used in the read-retry iteration for read latency reduction. From experimental results over real-world workloads on Disksim with SSD extensions, the effectiveness of PreLDPC on reducing read latency is verified in high-density flash memories.
Index Terms—High-density flash memory, LDPC codes, Performance optimization, Cell state ratio, Error-prone area
I. INTRODUCTION
High-density flash memories, e.g., TLC flash and QLC flash, have been already manufactured by industry to achieve large storage capacities. However, they are compromised by more serious data errors due to narrowed margins between threshold voltages states [1]. Due to the increased number of cell states, more read voltages are applied in LDPC flash reading to correct more errors [2]. This significantly increases the read latency and sacrifices flash read performance. What’s more, as the raw bit error rate (RBER) of data is unknown in advance, reads with LDPC codes (called LDPC read in this paper) adopt a read-retry manner to gradually increase the number of read voltages [3]. The read would first begin by sensing with the smallest number of read voltages ( this number is decided by LDPC read level). Sensed data would be transferred from flash to finish the decoding. Once decoding failed, a high read level with more read voltages would be iteratively applied until the successful decoding [4].

LDPC reading method, named as PreLDPC. We perform a preliminary study on the cell distributions after applying two read voltages within the adjacent area of states. Cells that lie between these two read voltages are called error-prone cells. It is observed that the ratio of error-prone cells in all cells of a page is proportionally related to the final read level. By exploiting this observation, we design a read level prediction method to obtain the final read level in advance. Thus, unnecessary read-retries can be avoided. Meanwhile, in order to further reduce the latency caused by the large number of read voltages, we design a fine-grained LDPC reading method in PreLDPC. By dividing one read level to be several sub-levels, LDPC reading can be performed in a smaller step. We evaluate the proposed PreLDPC method in Disksim with SSD extensions by using real-world workloads. Experimental results verify that PreLDPC can effectively decrease read latency of high-density flash memories.

In order to obtain the error-prone cell ratio, we need to count the number of cells in the error-prone area. As shown in Figure 3, cells in the area of V1 and V1.25, V1 and V1.5, V1 and V1.75 and V1 and V2, which represent the number of cells in error-prone area, can be determined after reading with level 1 and level 2. In order to obtain the predicted read level based on the ratio information, a correspondence table (corrtable) keeps the correspondence between ratio ranges and read level for seven read levels. After reading level 2, the predicted read level is obtained by computing cells ratio and looking up the corr-table, and the data is read with the predicted reading level.

B. The Fine-grained LDPC Reading Method

We define the sub-level read to be the read that only increases one extra read voltage based on already used read voltages. Figure 3 shows an example of sub-level for upper pages in TLC flash. Applying Level 1 to read flash involves four threshold voltages represented by the black solid lines, while applying Level 2 would involve all eight threshold voltages in the figure. We name three sub-levels between Level 1 and Level 2 as Level 1.25, 1.5 and 1.75, respectively. Sublevels are more appropriate to read the data whose RBER is between decoding capabilities of the adjacent levels.

C. The Design of PreLDPC
Our proposed prediction-based fine-grained LDPC reading method, PreLDPC, mainly contains three steps: 1) try to read with Level 1 and next sub-level; 2) obtain information on the number of error-prone area cells, use the information to predict the read level, and then use the predicted read level to read; 3) proceed with fine-grained LDPC reading based on the second step. In order to get the required information more quickly, We select the area between state P6 and P7 to represent the overall error-prone areas. And the error-prone cell ratio can be obtained from two sensings with Level 1 and the next sublevel.

III. EVALUATION
We use Disksim with SSD extensions to simulate a 64GB 3D TLC flash. Five workloads are chosen from MSR. We implement five methods listed as follows: Baseline is the existing progressive LDPC read method with seven read levels [3]. Fine-grained is the fine-grained LDPC reading method. Prediction is the LDPC read level prediction. PreLDPC is the proposed PreLDPC method that combines two designs of Prediction and Fine-grained. Ideal is the ideal case that the final read level is known in advance.
Experimental results of normalized read latency reduction for four methods compared with Baseline are shown in Figure 4. The orange bars in Figure 4 shows the read latency reduction of PreLDPC method in different trace and average situations. Compared with Baseline, PreLDPC method presents the most read latency reduction by 30% on average when P/E cycle is 4000 and retention is a month. That is because PreLDPC combines the advantages of two designs efficiently. When the read level required for the data is relatively high, PreLDPC quickly jump to the corresponding read level, reducing unnecessary decoding latency. And when the traditional method of reading level error correction ability is more than the data required, PreLDPC can use the fine-grained LDPC reading method to reduce unnecessary sensing latency.

IV. CONCLUSION
In order to reduce LDPC read latency on high-density flash memories, PreLDPC is proposed to predict the read levels and performs further reads in a fine-granularity manner once the predicted level fails. Experimental results on Disksim with SSD extensions have verified the effectiveness of PreLDPC on enhancing read performance of high-density flash memories.
