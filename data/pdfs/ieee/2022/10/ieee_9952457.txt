Selective Layered BP Decoding for low-latency NR LDPC Codes
Chanho Yoon, Woncheol Cho, and Young-Jo Ko Telecommunications & Media Research Laboratory
ETRI, Daejeon 34129, Republic of Korea chyoon@etri.re.kr

Abstract—In this paper, we propose selective layered BP decoding approach for decoding 3GPP NR rate compatible punctured LDPC codes that eliminates the ambiguity of BP decoding for information bit punctured codes. Conventional BP or layered BP decoding approach for NR LDPC codes does not perform well in frequency selective fading channels, and, with the sub iteration applying to the sub matrix portion, the proposed SL layered BP decoding improves the performance drastically. We show that faster decoding speed by applying the proposed scheme to the 3GPP NR LDPC code is achieved in terms of decoding latency.
Index Terms—LDPC, NR LDPC, Layered Decoding, Vertical Layered Decoding.
I. INTRODUCTION
R ECENTLY, low density parity check (LDPC) [1][2] channel coding specification for 3GPP has been released in non stand-alone version of new radio (NR) [3]. Since high throughput, low complexity, support of HARQ, flexibility, and latency are desired, QC-LDPC channel coding scheme for both uplink (PUSCH) and downlink (PDSCH) data channel is chosen. As an flexible LDPC design, which employs rate-compatible puncturing design, shortening, and HARQ enabling/supporting flexible codeword length code design, it is worth mentioning that NR LDPC codes supports various information bit and rate matching codeword sizes for eMBB, mMTC and URLLC use cases. In particular, rate-compatible punctured (RCP) LDPC code scheme is adopted, and, as a result of this scheme, the code offers lower overall coding rate compared to non-punctured rate-compatible schemes. This RCP LDPC scheme obviously offers additional coding gain that is more effective especially in high coding rate codewords.
Recently, layered belief propagation (BP) decoding approach for recovering the punctured LDPC codewords were introduced to achieve capacity approaching LDPC codes [4][5]. Previous works for decoding long RCP LDPC codewords are effective in AWGN only channel. However, if more than two columns of information bits are consecutively punctured, decoding performance degrades, especially in frequency selective fading channels. In this paper, we propose selective layered (SL) BP decoding to overcome the performance limits of applying conventional layered BP decoding to NR RCP LDPC code in frequency selective fading channel by selectively scheduling check/variable node updates during the punctured bit recovering phase.

II. 5G NR BASE GRAPH STRUCTURE AND CODEWORD
CONSTRUCTION AFTER RATE MATCHING
In this section, we first overview the NR LDPC parity check matrices H that are consisted of quasi-cyclic (QC) circular permutation sub-matrices I(Pi,j) of size Z ×Z, where i and j are the row and column indices of the element in the base matrix H. Pi,j indicates the right shift value of the circulant identity matrix, which is defined in the TS 38.212. The minimum value of lift size Z is chosen according to the information bit size and base graph selection, which is independent from the rate matched target codeword size c. Once groups of sub matrices I(Pi,j) of rectangular or square shape can be formed, the H can be partitioned into 5 sections, as shown in Fig. 1. It can be considered as an extended version of the QC-LDPC codes in IEEE 802.11n standard. In fact, a structure of an LDPC parity check matrix consisting A and B block matrices only can represent the core structure of the LDPC codes defined in the IEEE 802.11n standard.
In contrast to the relatively limited flexibility of adjusting channel coding rate in IEEE 802.11n LDPC (coding rate formation is limited by choosing the base parity matrix of rates R=1/2, 2/3, 3/4 or 5/6 and applying shortening techniques only), NR-LDPC facilitates RCP LDPC codes for various rate matching circumstances with incremental redundancy based hybrid automatic repeat request (IR-HARQ) support by applying extension to IEEE 802.11n structure with a Raptor codes-like single diagonal parity check matrix [6]. Puncturing this extended part of parity bits does not affect the generated systematic codeword belonging to matrix part A and B. In addition, shortening technique is also applicable for accommodating various information bit sizes. Depending on the choice of the base graph, the mother coding rate is fixed to either R=22/68 (base graph 1) or R=10/52 (base graph 2), respectively.
Another difference between the IEEE 802.11n LDPC and NR-LDPC codes is information bit puncturing during rate matching operation. The initial information bits part of NRLDPC for exactly up to two lift size, 2Z, are completely discarded, and never send to the decoder. The punctured information bits are easily recovered at the decoder due to relatively dense check/variable nodes allocated at the information bit puncturing region. III. SELECTIVE LAYERED BP DECODING FOR NR LDPC
CODES IN FREQUENCY SELECTIVE FADING CHANNELS
LDPC decoding based on belief propagation (BP) algorithm and layered BP algorithm are practiced commonly in many implementation and research areas. Layered BP decoding divides the Tanner graph of an LDPC code into smaller subgraphs and all their neighboring variable nodes. In effect, check nodes are divided into groups called layers and the check node along with variable node update within the layer group is performed. This is called sub-iterations, and this technique can lead faster convergence of BP decoding to LDPC codes.
For the conventional BP approach to recover punctured log likelihood ratios (LLRs) by updating the check node value is viable, because sum product algorithm (SPA) requires at least one of its neighboring available variable nodes with input LLRs. As shown, in Fig. 1, there is at least one available neighboring variable nodes at each row j for recovering the LLRs in the punctured region. However, performance of the conventional method is degraded noticeably at high SNR region, if more than one variable node with zero (punctured) LLR exists. For the term conventional uniform layered (UL) BP decoding approach refers to the decoding scheduling that does not skip the check node update procedure at row j that contains two check nodes in the punctured region. The performance gap between maximum decoding iteration trial of 10 and 20 is noticeable, while the gap between 20 and 50 are relatively narrow. For the SLBP, LDPC decoding iteration of 10 is enough to match the performance of ULBP with 20 decoding iterations.
On the other hand, performance gap between the ULBP and SLBP is enlarged when relatively higher coding rate and shorter codeword size is considered for SNR vs BLER evaluation. For the R = 256/512 case, ULBP shows a noticeable diversity order loss when LDPC decoding iteration is fixed to 10. The proposed SLBP scheme, with only 10 LDPC decoding iterations, shows nearly identical diversity order as ULBP with 50 LDPC decoding iterations. As for the AWGN only channel case, the proposed scheme saves decoding latency about half of that of ULBP. In addition, we observe error floors for ULBP schemes.

V. CONCLUSION
A simple selective layer BP decoding approach to recover punctured LDPC codes was proposed for 3GPP NR RCP LDPC codes. With minimal LDPC decoding iteration design, the proposed method helps to decode LDPC codes with low latency and higher throughput. With the proposed SLBP decoding method, error floor region is expected to be existed in a lower BLER region, compared to the conventional techniques.
