Modified Noisy Gradient Descent Bit-Flipping Decoding Algorithms for LDPC Codes
Yidong Li, Wai M. Tam and Francis C.M. Lau Department of Electronic and Information Engineering, The Hong Kong Polytechnic University, Hong Kong
Email: yidong.li@connect.polyu.hk and {wm.tam, francis-cm.lau}@polyu.edu.hk

Abstract—In this paper, we propose modified multi-bit noisy gradient descent bit flipping (M-NGDBF) algorithms for decoding low-density parity-check codes. To simplify the decoder design, we eliminate the use of Gaussian noise generators at the decoder and replace them with received signals after simple transformations. We then improve the convergence rate by removing the randomness in the M-NGDBF algorithm during the first few iterations. Subsequently, we construct a tabu-list to record bits that are flipped in the current iteration and allows these bits to be flipped in the next iteration only with a very small probability. Simulation results show that our proposed algorithms outperform the original M-NGDBF algorithm in terms of both bit error rate and convergence rate.
Index Terms—gradient descent bit flipping, LDPC codes, multi-bit flipping, noisy gradient descent bit flipping.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes were proposed about 50 years ago [1] and have been widely deployed in various digital communication standards over the past two decades. There are two main decoding algorithms for LDPC codes — soft decision belief propagation algorithms and hard decision bit flipping (BF) algorithms. Compared with the BP algorithms, the BF algorithms are simpler in terms of hardware complexity but performs poorer in terms of error rate. Recently, the Gradient Descent Bit Flipping (GDBF) algorithm has been proposed [2]. It is relatively more complex than other bit-flipping algorithms but can achieve better error performance. The GDBF algorithm treats the decoding process as maximizing an objective function with an iterative process. However, the algorithm can be easily trapped at the local maxima and causes the error-floor phenomenon. By introducing a perturbation signal into the GDBF algorithm, the noisy GDBF (NGDBF) algorithm is formed [3]. The perturbation signal enables the algorithm to jump out of a local maximum and hence improves the overall error performance. However, the perturbation signal is realized by random number generators. Also, in order to attain optimum error correction performance, a threshold in the NGDBF algorithm needs to be adjusted according to bit-energy-to-noise-power-densityratio value (Eb/N0). Both requirements will increase the complexity of the decoder.
In this paper, we aim to eliminate the use of random number generators and to remove the requirement of a Eb/N0dependent threshold. Section II briefly reviews the GDBF algorithm, the NGDBF algorithm and some other GDBF derivatives. Section III presents our proposed NGDBF algorithms. In

particular, we propose replacing Gaussian distributed perturbation signals with incoming channel signals after transformations. Section IV illustrates the steps to optimize the proposed NGDBF algorithms. It also compares the bit error performance and convergence speed of the proposed algorithms with the original one. Section V gives some concluding remarks.

II. REVIEW OF GDBF DECODING ALGORITHMS

A binary LDPC code can be characterized by an m × n parity check matrix H that comprises elements hij ∈ {0, 1} for i = 1, 2, . . . , m and j = 1, 2, . . . , n. The corresponding
LDPC codeword set C is given by C={c = (c1, c2, · · · , cn) ∈ {0, 1}n : cHT = 0} where (·)T denotes the transpose
operator. Moreover, the two sets N (i) = {j : hij = 1} and
M (j) = {i : hij = 1} define, respectively, the locations of 1s in the i-th row and the j-column of H.
We consider transmitting the code vector c using binary phase-shift keying (BPSK) modulation over an additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel. Hence, we map c to a
transmitted signal vector c˜ = (c˜1, c˜2, · · · , c˜n) ∈ {−1, +1}n using c˜k = (−1)ck ∀k. Denoting N0 as the noise power spectral density, we represent the noise vector by z = (z1, z2, · · · , zn) where zk ∀k follows a Gaussian distribution with zero mean and variance σ2 = N0/2. Then the noisy vector y = (y1, y2, · · · , yn) at the receiver can be written as y = c˜+ z. We suppose the receiver outputs a decision vector x = (x1, x2, · · · , xn) ∈ {−1, +1}n. For the i-th row (i = 1, 2, . . . , m) in H, we can compute the corresponding bipolar
syndrome component given by si = j∈N(i) xj = ±1. If si = +1, the i-th check constraint is satisfied; otherwise, it is not satisfied. When all check constraints are satisfied, i.e., si = +1 ∀i = 1, 2, . . . , m, x corresponds to a valid codeword.

In a BSC, the original MGDBF algorithm computes the Ek values ∀k as defined in (2) and flips the bits having the minimum Ek. (Note that multiple bits may have the same minimum Ek value). In [4],

a flipping probability is introduced for the bits having the

algorithm. The PGDBF algorithm helps the objective function

escape from the local maximum and hence improves the error

correction performance. The same article [4] also reveals that

in the first few decoding iterations, the randomness introduced

not only does not help, but also slows down the convergence

speed and degrades the decoding performance. In [5], a tabu-

list random-penalty GDBF (TRGDBF) algorithm is proposed

to eliminate the scenario where a bit oscillates between two

states. In the TRGDBF algorithm, the bits which are flipped

in the current iteration are put into the tabu-list and cannot be

flipped in the following iteration. In each iteration, only the

bits which are not in the tabu-list and have the minimum Ek

value will be flipped with a certain probability. By limiting

the oscillations, the error correction performance and the

convergence speed of TRGDBF are improved compared to the PGDBF algorithm.
III. PROPOSED NGDBF+ ALGORITHMS
In this section, we propose some modifications to the original M-NGDBF algorithm for decoding LDPC codes sent over an AWGN channel. We propose replacing the perturbation signals qk’s in the M-NGDBF algorithm with transformed received signals yk’s. The purpose is to eliminate the random number generators at the decoder and to make the hardware requirement lower. Based on the received messages yk’s, we generate a sequence of “random variables” lk (k = 1, 2, . . . , n) with the following transformations.
Transformation #1 : For k = 1, 2, . . . , n, if yk > 1, then lk = yk − 2; elseif yk < −1, then lk = yk + 2; else lk = yk.
Transformation #2 : For k = 1, 2, . . . , n, if lk > 0, then lk = lk − 1; else lk = lk + 1.
Transformation #3 : Given two fixed values p1 and p2, for k = 1, 2, . . . , n,
if p1 < lk < 1, then lk = lk + p2; elseif −1 < lk < −p1, then lk = lk − p2; else lk = lk. One sequence of lk (k = 1, 2, . . . , n) is obtained for each received signal vector y and is shifted cyclically to replace the perturbation signals qk’s in the M-NGDBF algorithm. As mentioned in [4], randomness introduced in the first few iterations slows down the convergence speed. Here in the first TGDBF iterations, we propose removing the influence of randomness by using the M-GDBF algorithm with a fixed threshold θGDBF to update the objective function. Afterwards, the M-NGDBF algorithm is used for the remaining iterations. We call this algorithm the “M-NGDBF+A” algorithm. As no perturbation signals are used during the first TGDBF iterations, the decoder can make use of the extra time duration and generate lk’s with fewer transformation units. We further propose using a “tabu-list” to record the bits flipped in the current iteration. Instead of forbidding these bits from being flipped in the next iteration, we allow them to flip only with a very small probability. For each of these bits, we remove the “randomness” in the corresponding inversion function and compare the inversion function with a very low threshold denoted as θlimit. Details of the decoding algorithm, called NGDBF+B algorithm, is described in Algorithm 1. Note that Counterk(t) = 1/0 indicates the k-th bit is flipped/not flipped in the t-th iteration.

V. CONCLUSION
Without sacrificing any error performance, we have replaced the random perturbation signals in the original NGDBF algorithm with channel signals after simple transformations. We have also proposed two modified NGDBF algorithms that have shown to outperform the original M-NGDBF algorithm. In particular, the proposed M-NGDBF+B algorithm outperforms the original M-NGDBF algorithm by 0.15 dB at a bit error rate of 10−5 and by up to 30.5% in terms convergence speed.
