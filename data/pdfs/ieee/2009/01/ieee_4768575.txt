A Unified Approach to the Construction of Binary and Nonbinary Quasi-Cyclic LDPC Codes Based
on Finite Fields
Shumei Song, Bo Zhou, Member, IEEE, Shu Lin, Life Fellow, IEEE, and Khaled Abdel-Ghaffar, Member, IEEE

Abstract— A unified approach for constructing binary and nonbinary quasi-cyclic LDPC codes under a single framework is presented. Six classes of binary and nonbinary quasi-cyclic LDPC codes are constructed based on primitive elements, additive subgroups, and cyclic subgroups of finite fields. Numerical results show that the codes constructed perform well over the AWGN channel with iterative decoding.
Index Terms— Base matrix, dispersion, finite field, LDPC codes, location-vector, quasi-cyclic.
I. INTRODUCTION
L DPC codes, first discovered by Gallager in 1962 [1], were rediscovered and shown to form a class of Shannon-limit approaching codes in the late 1990’s and early 2000’s [2]–[7]. Ever since their rediscovery, a great deal of research effort has been expended in the design and construction of these codes. However, most of the effort has been focused on the design and construction of binary LDPC codes, very little on the design and construction of nonbinary LDPC codes.
Nonbinary LDPC codes and their iterative decoding using the sum-product algorithm (SPA) were first investigated by Davey and MacKay in 1998 [8], [9]. Since their work, very little progress has been made in either construction or decoding of nonbinary LDPC codes. Most recently, some encouraging research works started to emerge in decoding [10], [11], code construction [12]–[14], and analysis [15],[16].
Many structured binary LDPC codes presented in the literature have a quasi-cyclic (QC) structure. Encoding of QCLDPC codes can be implemented using simple shift-registers with linear complexity [17] in contrast to the quadratic complexity involved in computer-generated random-like LDPC

In this paper, a unified approach for constructing both binary and nonbinary QC-LDPC codes under a single framework based on finite fields is presented. The constructed codes are shown to perform well over the AWGN channel using either the binary SPA [3], [4], [6], [7], [19], [20] or the Fast Fourier Transform based q-ary SPA (FFT-QSPA) given in [9]–[11]. The construction is based on a base matrix over some finite field that satisfies certain constraints. The elements in this matrix are then replaced by binary or nonbinary circulants to form parity-check matrices of binary or nonbinary QC-LDPC codes, respectively. It should be noted that some of the known constructions of QC-LDPC codes follow from this framework, e.g., [21], [22]. In [23] the same construction methods are used to yield binary codes. However, since the rows and columns of the circulants in the parity-check matrices in our constructions have weight one, constructions in which the rows and columns have weight two or larger, such as in [17], [24], [25], are not covered by our framework.
The organization of this paper is as follows: Section II gives a brief introduction to binary and nonbinary LDPC codes; Section III presents a general construction method of binary and nonbinary QC-LDPC codes based on the concepts of location-vectors, dispersion, and base matrices; Sections IV, V, and VI give techniques to construct such base matrices using primitive elements, additive subgroups, and cyclic subgroups of finite fields, respectively; and Section VII concludes the paper.
II. LDPC CODES
Let GF(q) be a finite field with q elements where q is a power of a prime. A q-ary regular LDPC code C is given by the null space over GF(q) of a sparse parity-check matrix H = [hi,j] over GF(q) that has the following structural properties: 1) each row has weight ρ; 2) each column has weight γ. We further impose the following additional structural property which is enforced in most LDPC constructions: 3) no two rows (or two columns) have more than one place where they both have nonzero components. VII. CONCLUSION
In this paper we have presented a general approach to the construction of binary and nonbinary QC-LDPC codes under a single framework. The proposed approach is based on dispersing a base matrix over a finite field that satisfies two specific row-constraints. This results in a sparse matrix whose associated Tanner graph has a girth of at least 6. Based on this general approach, three specific methods for constructing binary and nonbinary QC-LDPC codes have been presented. Three classes of binary QC-LDPC codes and three classes of nonbinary QC-LDPC codes have been constructed.

Shu Lin (S’62-M’65-SM’78-F’80-LF’00) received the B.S.E.E. degree from National Taiwan University, Taipei, Taiwan, in 1959, and the M.S. and Ph.D. degrees in electrical engineering from Rice University, Houston, TX, in 1964 and 1965, respectively.
In 1965, he joined the Faculty of the University of Hawaii, Honolulu, as an Assistant Professor of Electrical Engineering. He became an Associate Professor in 1969 and a Professor in 1973. In 1986, he joined Texas A&M University, College Station, as the Irma Runyon Chair Professor of Electrical Engineering. In 1987, he returned to the University of Hawaii. From 1978 to 1979, he was a Visiting Scientist at the IBM Thomas J. Watson Research Center, Yorktown Heights, NY, where he worked on error control protocols for data communication systems. He spent the academic year of 1996-1997 as a Visiting Professor at the Technical University of Munich, Munich, Germany. He retired from University of Hawaii in 1999 and he is currently an Adjunct Professor at University of California, Davis. He has published numerous technical papers in IEEE Transactions and other refereed journals. He is the author of the book, An Introduction to Error-Correcting Codes (Englewood Cliff, NJ: Prentice-Hall, 1970). He also co-authored (with D.J. Costello) the book, Error Control Coding: Fundamentals and Applications (Upper Saddle River, NJ: Prentice-Hall, 1st edition, 1982, 2nd edition, 2004), and (with T. Kasami, T. Fujiwara, and M. Fossorier) the book, Trellises and TrellisBased Decoding Algorithms, (Boston, MA: Kluwer Academic, 1998). His current research areas include algebraic coding theory, coded modulation, error control systems, and satellite communications. He has served as the Principle Investigator on 36 research grants. Dr. Lin is a Member of the IEEE Information Theory Society and the Communication Society. He served as the Associate Editor for Algebraic Coding Theory for the IEEE TRANSACTIONS ON INFORMATION THEORY from 1976 to 1978, the Program Co-Chair of the IEEE International Symposium of Information Theory held in Kobe, Japan, in June 1988, and a Co-Chair of the IEEE Information Theory Workshop held in Chengdu, China, October 2006. He was the President of the IEEE Information Theory Society in 1991. In 1996, he was a recipient of the Alexander von Humboldt Research Prize for U.S. Senior Scientists, a recipient of the IEEE Third-Millennium Medal, 2000, and a recipient of the IEEE Communications Society 2007 Stephen O. Rice Prize in the Field of Communication Theory.
