Construction of Low-Density Parity-Check Codes Based on Frequency-Hopping Sequences∗

ZHANG Guohua and WANG Xinmei
(State Key Laboratory of ISN, Xidian University, Xi’an 710071, China)

Abstract — Three classes of structured regular Lowdensity parity-check (LDPC) codes with girth at least 6 are constructed from frequency-hopping sequences. The parity-check matrices of these codes have uniform row and column weights, simple and explicit structure, as well as a large variety of candidate parameters. Simulation results show that the new codes perform very similar to the randomly constructed LDPC codes over Additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels.
Key words — Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes,
Girth, Iterative decoding, Frequency-hopping sequences.
I. Introduction
A binary regular Low-density parity-check (LDPC) code is defined as the null space of a sparse parity-check matrix H over GF(2) that has the following structural properties: (1) every row has weight ρ; (2) every column has weight γ; and (3) two rows (or two columns) have at most one 1-component in common. Such a matrix is said to be (γ, ρ)-regular and the corresponding code is called a (γ, ρ)-regular LDPC code. Property (3) of H ensures that the minimum distance of the code is at least γ +1, and that the corresponding Tanner graph has girth at least six[1,2].
Compared with randomly generated codes, structured LDPC codes usually have the advantage of less encoding or storage complexity. Many construction schemes for structured LDPC codes are based on complicated combinatorics such as finite geometries[1] and Balanced incomplete block design (BIBD)[2,3]. Other methods for LDPC codes construction, such as Mutually orthogonal squares (MOLS)[3], Kirkman triple systems (KTS)[4], oval design[5] and unital design[6] are also devised based on combinatorics. Besides these complex constructions with intense mathematical flavor, several common topics in communication domain, for example ReedSolomon codes and optical orthogonal codes, have lent themselves to the construction of LDPC codes[7−9]. In this paper we propose a novel construction procedure by Frequency-hopping (FH) sequences, one of the most familiar topics in spread spectrum communications.

Two classes of LDPC codes follow this general construction.
1. Construction AHSC-A Given any prime number p > 2, for every integer k (1 ≤ k ≤ p − 1), a sequence Xk = (X0k, X1k, · · · , Xpk−1) of length p can be constructed by Xik = (k · i) mod p. Then all the sequences Xk(1 ≤ k ≤ p − 1) compose a Prime sequence family (PSF)[11,12], which meets both CA and CC. The resultant LDPC code based on PSF is (γ, ρ)-regular for any ρ (1 ≤ ρ ≤ p − 1) and any γ (1 ≤ γ ≤ p). 2. Construction AHSC-B Given any finite field GF (q) (q, prime power), all elements can be denoted by

GF (q) = {α∞, α0, α1, · · · , αq−2}

where α is a primitive element of GF (q). For any element βk over GF (q), a sequence Xk = (X0k, X1k, · · · , Xqk−2) of length q − 1 can be constructed by Xik = βk + αi. All the sequences Xk (1 ≤ k ≤ q) compose a hopping sequences family, which satisfies CA and CC[12]. Direct application of AHSC (employ-
ing only a subset of the family) will result in a parity-check
matrix with constant column weight γ but varying row weight
ρ (ρ ∈ {ρ, ρ−1}), due to the obvious fact that every sequence Xk in the family excludes a unique element βk of GF (q). In this case, the parity-check matrix is irregular and its null space
gives a near-regular LDPC codes. If ρ is selected as q, the size
of this family, a (γ, q − 1)-regular LDPC code can be created
for any γ ≤ q − 1.

IV. Examples and Simulations
Example of AHSC-A: Given p = 53, PSF includes 52 FH sequences. Let g(x) = x + 1. Then the 53 frequency slots (0, 1, · · · 52) are mapped into (1, 2, · · · 53). Choose γ = 3, ρ = 52 and (i1, i2) = (1, 2). The resultant AHSC-A-LDPC code is a (3, 52)-regular (2756, 2599) code with rate 0.943.
Example of AHSC-B: Given GF (25), this family includes 32 FH sequences. Set γ = 15 and (i1, i2, · · · , iγ−1) = (1, 2, · · · , 14). Using the 32 FH sequences, we obtain a (15, 31)-regular (992, 771) AHSC-B-LDPC code with rate 0.777.
Example of SHSC: Given GF (26), this family includes 26(26 − 1) synchronous FH sequences. Choose ρ = 32, γ = 6, k1 = α∞, ki = αi−2(2 ≤ i ≤ ρ) and βi = αi−1(1 ≤ i ≤ γ). The resultant SHSC-LDPC code is a (6, 32)-regular (2048, 1723) code with rate 0.841.

V. Conclusion
Based on FH sequences that are needed in FHMA communication systems, we presented three families of structured binary LDPC codes. The proposed method can generate lowdensity parity-check matrices with constant column and row weight, simple and explicit structure, girth at least six, as well as flexible code parameters. Experimental results show that the proposed codes can achieve identical or comparable performance compared with randomly generated codes over AWGN channels. Quasi-cyclic LDPC codes are a special class of LDPC codes whose encoding can be efficiently implemented by shift-register circuits; the codes presented in this paper and several other existing families of codes such as those in Ref.[7], however, do not share the quasi-cyclic property. The further work is to develop efficient encoders for these novel codes.
