Abstract—This paper presents our investigation on performance enhancement due to the implementation of low density parity check (LDPC) codes on IEEE 802.11n system and its Application Specific Integrated Circuit design. Simulation result shows that in higher coding rate, LDPC codes gives 6 dB better performance compared to binary convolutional codes. Logic synthesis is succesfully done on 0.13 µm CMOS technology with low-power standard cell library.
I. Introduction
Recently, multimedia data becomes huge and one need a high-performance system to communicate it over wireless system. Multiple-input multiple-output (MIMO) with orthogonal frequency division multiplexing (OFDM) is a promising technique to support such communication due to its ability in maintaining the quality while providing high-throughput (HT) communication system. The wireless LAN standard of IEEE 802.11n uses this combination to gain 260 Mbps of throughput at physical layer for 20 MHz of bandwidth [1].
To enhance the error performance one should involve error correcting codes (ECC) in his system. Turbo codes and LDPC codes are well known as the most powerful ECC. Both of them can give performance close to Shannon limit on binary symetric channel (BSC) [2]. Both of them have some similarities. They are kind of sparce matrices and may use sum-product algorithm as decoding algorithm. However LDPC codes offer lower decoding complexity [3]. Further more, with a long code length and a large number of decoding iteration, LDPC codes can achieve performance as close as 0.0045 dB to Shannon limit on BSC with aditive white Gaussian noise (AWGN). [4].
Two types of ECC technique are adopted in IEEE 802.11n for forward error correction (FEC) encoder scheme. They are binary convolutional codes (BCC) and LDPC codes. The BCC are mandatory for all MCS configuration and LDPC codes are used optionally as high-performance ECC technique in HT system.
In this paper we report our investigation about performance enhancement by using LDPC codes for IEEE 802.11n instead of BCC. In our comparation, system with BCC as ECC are

decoded using soft-decision Viterbi algorithm. While system with LDPC are decoded by Min-sum algorithm. We compare the performance of both systems on AWGN channel. Then, to get closer to real situation, we compare them on smalloffice channel model of MIMO channel model B of task group IEEE 802.11n (B TGn). Information about MIMO channel models TGn can be found in [5]. In Application Specific Integrated Circuit (ASIC) design, we synthesized our LDPC decoder using Synopsys Design Compiler on 0.13 µm CMOS technology with low-power standard cell library.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. LDPC coding and Min-sum algorithm LDPC decoding for IEEE 802.11n is briefly reviewed in Section II. Computer simulation results for both LDPC and BCC are displayed in Section III. ASIC design of LDPC decoder are presented in Section IV and some conclusion are drawn in Section V.

III. Simulation Result
To verify the contribution of LDPC codes over BCC, we simulated both ECC schemes in MCS-30 and MCS-31 which are the 2 highest throughput of IEEE 802.11n. For both cases the LDPC codeword block length is 1944 bits. The simulations are done under AWGN channel and channel model B TGn. For simulations on channel model B TGn we investigated LDPC iteration 15 and 30. The simulation parameters are listed in Table II, while the rate dependent parameters of IEEE 802.11n for MCS-30 and MCS-31 with 4 spatial streams and 800 ns guard interval (GI) on 20 MHz are listed in Table III.
Simulation results of performance comparison in packet error rate (PER) between 2 ECCs of MCS-30 and MCS-31 on AWGN channel are shown in Fig. 4. In MCS-30 case where the coding rate is 3/4, LDPC code shows 1.8 dB better performance while in MCS-31 where the coding rate is 5/6 LDPC code gives 3.2 dB better performance compared to ones with BCC.
Simulation results of PER comparison between 2 ECCs of MCS-30 and MCS-31 on channel model B TGn are depicted in Fig. 5. LDPC code shows 3 dB better performance for MCS-30 while it gives 6 dB better performance for MCS-31 compared to ones with BCC. From these results, we can conclude that as the coding rate is higher and the channel is worse, LDPC code proofs its excelent power in correcting the errors.

Regarding number of LDPC iteration on last simulations. Since 15 times of LDPC iteration shows same performance as 30 one, but with half-time computation, we can conclude 15 times is optimum for LDPC iteration in this case.
IV. ASIC Design of Min-sum Algorithm LDPC Decoder
In this section, we describe hardware implementation of our LDPC decoder. To implement an LDPC decoder based on the Min-sum algorithm, we employed partially-parallel architecture [6], [7] and to represent internal data in the decoder we used 16-bits fixed point representation which consists of a sign bit, 11 bits integral part and 4 bits fractional part. The top level block diagram of our LDPC decoder is shown in Fig. 6 which is composed by a multiplier (MUL) unit, input buffer (IBUF) and output buffer (OBUF) units, λ−, α− and β− Memory units, Horizontal Processing Units (HPUs) and Vertical Processing Units (VPUs). Brief explanation of these units are as follow.
A. MUL unit
The MUL unit is a 16-bits multiplier which calculates the log likelihood ratio (LLR) λn = 2yi/σ2, where 2yi and σ2 represent the received word and the variance of the channel noise, respectively.
To reduce the delay time of the multiplier, we employed a four-stage pipelined structure which is composed of a threestage carry save adder (CSA) and a carry propagation adder (CPA).
B. IBUF, λ-Memory and OBUF units
Our LDPC decoder works simultaneously on three consecutive received words. While one received word is being iteratively decoded, the next received word is loaded into the decoder and the tentative LDPC code word of the previous received word is read out from the decoder.
The λ-Memory unit stores LLRs which are used for LDPC decoding and the IBUF unit buffers LLRs for the next received word. The OBUF unit buffers the tentative LDPC code word.

C. HPU
Our LDPC decoder has 108 HPUs. Each HPU carries out the row operation of the Min-Sum algorithm. The HPU finds the extrinsic LLR αmn and each extrinsic LLR is stored in the α-Memory unit. For code length n = 648, 1296, 1944, each HPU processes 6, 12, 18 rows in the parity check matrix H, respectively.
Normally, the row operation is done using the signed magnitude arithmetic. Therefore, each HPU has 2’s complement to signed magnitude converters at its input. Further, each HPU has eight 3-to-2 and seven 4-to-2 comparators to find the two

least input magnitude values and has an XOR tree to decide the sign bits.
D. VPU
Our LDPC decoder consists of 216 VPUs. Each VPU executes the column operation of the Min-sum algorithm. The VPU calculates the posteriori LLR βmn and each posteriori LLR is stored in the β-Memory unit.
For codeword length n = 648, 1296, 1944, each VPU processes 3, 6, 9 columns in the parity check matrix H, respectively.

V. Conclusions
We have investigated the performance of LDPC as ECC for IEEE 802.11n-based WLAN system and get 3.2dB and 6dB gain compared to BCC in high coding rate on AWGN channel and channel model B TGn, respectively. 15 times of iteration is the optimum for LDPC decoder. Logic synthesis result on 0.13 µm CMOS technology with low-power standard cell library shows that the proposed Min-sum algorithm-based LDPC decoder is acceptable for portable devices.

Like the HPU, the column operation is also done by using the 2’s complement arithmetic. Therefore, each VPU has signed magnitude to 2’s complement converters at the inputs and each VPU has a CSA tree and a two stage CPA to accumulate input values.
E. α-Memory and β-Memory units
The α-Memory and β-Memory units store the extrinsic LLR αmn and the posteriori LLR βmn, respectively. Both units have input and output multiplexers (MUXs) to support 12 combinations of LDPC codeword lengths (n) of 648, 1296, 1944 bits and code rates (R) of 1/2, 2/3, 3/4, 5/6 based on the IEEE 802.11n standard.
We implement this LDPC Decoder in Verilog Hardware Description Language and synthesize it using Synopsys Design Compiler (Ver.2006.06) using 0.13 µm CMOS technology with low-power standard cell library. Table IV summarizes the design statistic results of ASIC for the designed LPDC decoder, while Table V lists the design statistic results of ASIC for each unit in the LDPC decoder. From these tables we can see that most of the area are occupied by the memories. However total cell area is acceptable as a portable device.
