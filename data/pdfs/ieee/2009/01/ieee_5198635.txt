A Mutual Information Approach for Comparing LLR Metrics for Iterative Decoders
Jianwen Zhang, Marc A. Armand, and Pooi Yuen Kam Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering National University of Singapore, Singapore, 118622 Email: {elezhan, eleama, elekampy}@nus.edu.sg

Abstract— We develop an approach to compare different loglikelihood ratio (LLR) metrics for iterative soft decoding. We show that an LLR metric for a function of the received signals is a sufficient statistic to this function about the binary channel input. We also prove that when the function belongs to a set of specific mappings, the corresponding LLR metric can feed the maximal mutual information to the decoder. For decoding low density parity check codes with the belief-propagation decoder, we develop a method to estimate the minimal average number of iterations. The results are applied to compare the Gaussian metric in [1] and the two-symbol-observation-interval LLR metric in [2]. The latter is shown to be superior.
I. INTRODUCTION
Turbo and low density parity check (LDPC) codes with appropriate iterative decoders are shown to be capacity approaching in various channels [3]–[9]. These works are based on the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels and the assumption that the channel parameters are known to the receivers which is not always met in practical applications, such as the phase offset on noncoherent channels. In such channels, the accuracy of the initial Log-likelihood ratio (LLR) fed to iterative decoders depends on how the unknown channel parameters are estimated from the received signals. Different values of initial LLR may lead to significantly different behavior of the iterative decoders. Therefore, it is important to develop an approach to compare the performance of iterative decoders using different LLR metrics.
In this paper, we develop a systematic approach based on the mutual information (MI) to comparing different LLR metrics. We show that the LLR metric for a function of the received signals is a sufficient statistic to this function about the channel input in binary transmissions. We also prove that when the function is an information preserving (IP) mapping, the corresponding LLR metric can produce the maximal MI to the decoder. The IP mapping is defined later. When the belief-propagation decoder (BPD) for LDPC codes is applied, we develop a method to estimate the minimal average number of iterations required for a target bit error rate (BER) by the property of extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) chart.
Turbo- and LDPC-coded differential binary phase shift keying (DBPSK) with iterative decoders are applied in noncoherent AWGN channels in [1], [10], [11] and [2]. A Gaussian approximation metric (GM) and a two-symbol-observationinterval metric (TSOIM) for the initial LLR are proposed in [1] and [2], respectively. In [2], it is suggested that the

GM lost information compared with the TSOIM and only BER simulation results are used to compare these metrics. We apply our approach to comparing the GM and the TSOIM on noncoherent AWGN channels and show that the latter is superior in terms of the MI fed to the decoder and the minimal average number of iterations. We also find that an LDPC code using the TSOIM has better noise threshold than that using the GM.
This paper is arranged as follows. We develop the general approach in Section II. The comparison of the GM and the TSOIM by applying our approach is shown in Section III. We conclude this paper in Section IV.

The operations of the set of variable nodes and the set of check nodes are referred to as the variable node decoder (VND) and the check node decoder (CND), respectively. Denote by Iv,e(i) and Ic,e(i) the MI between the output messages of the VND and the transmitted codeword, and the MI between the output messages of the CND and the transmitted codeword, respectively, after iteration i. Viewing the output MI of each component decoder as a function of its input MI, the VND and the CND are characterized by their respective MI transfer functions. From Fig. 1, we can see that the output MI of the VND, Iv,e, is the input MI of the CND, denoted as Ic,a, and the output MI of the CND, Ic,e, is the input MI of the VND, denoted as Iv,a. Moreover, the VND takes outputs of the channel and the CND as its inputs while the CND always takes the output of VND as its only input. This implies that Tv(Iv,a), the function for the VND , is determined by the channel and the degree distribution of the variable nodes while the function for the CND is determined by the degree distribution of the check nodes only. The inverse MI transfer function of the CND, Tc−1(Ic,e), is of particular interest. The curves for these functions can be obtained by simulation and called EXIT curves of respective decoders [13]. By assuming that the soft messages from the VND and the CND are Gaussian distributed, the expressions of the EXIT curves are developed in [14]. B. Difference in the Minimal Average Number of Iterations
The method developed in Subsection II-B can be applied to the iterative decoding of infinite-length LDPC codes on (virtual) channels with the same I. By this method, we can estimate the MANI required to achieve the same target BER by using the TSOIM and the GM, respectively.
For example, if set the target BER pe = 4.8748 × 10−5, we have = 1 − J 2Q−1(pe) = 0.0005 bits. Assume that a rate 1/2, (3, 6) regular binary LDPC code is applied in a noncoherent AWGN channel using DBPSK, Es = 1. The minimal average numbers of iterations required to converge to the target BER for different noise standard deviations σ are shown in Fig. 6. It can be observed that fewer iterations are required for both metrics when values of σ are small. We also can see that using the GM requires more iterations than using the TSOIM for most of values of σ calculated. The reduction on K when using the TSOIM can compensate for the increase in computational load due to the more complex expression of the TSOIM. In addition, in high noise power regime, the decoder using the GM needs far more iterations than the decoder using the TSOIM or even does not converge at all. This is coincident with the TSOIM resulting a better noise threshold than the GM as in Subsection III-A.

IV. CONCLUSION
We develop an approach based on MI for comparing different LLR metrics of the iterative decoders. We show that the LLR metric for a function of the received signals is a sufficient statistic to this function about the binary channel input. We also prove that when this function is an IP mapping, the corresponding LLR metric feeds the maximal MI to the iterative decoder. From the property of the EXIT chart, we develop a method to estimate the minimal average number of iterations for a target BER when the BPD is applied to decode an LDPC code.
We apply our approach to compare the GM and the TSOIM on the noncoheretn AWGN channel. We find that the TSOIM can feed more MI to the decoder than the GM. We show that for a target BER, the decoder using the TSOIM needs fewer iterations than the decoding using the GM. Our results also suggest that LDPC codes using the TSOIM have better noise thresholds than those using the GM.
The results can be applied to the iterative decoding of turbo codes with minor changes on the distributions of the soft messages.
