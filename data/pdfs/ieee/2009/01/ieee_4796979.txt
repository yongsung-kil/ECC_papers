Abstract
In this paper, we propose a novel method with low complexity for constructing quasi-cyclic LDPC codes. The algorithm chooses every circulant permutation matrix sequentially, and makes sure that the current circulant permutation matrix forms no cycles of length 4 and 6 with the existent matrices, finally a family of (3, L) QC-LDPC codes with girth at least 8 is obtained. The main complexity of the algorithm is checking cycles of length 6, the number of which is no more than L2. Simulation results show that, when block length is 1008 and using the iterative belief propagation decoding, the LDPC code constructed by the proposed algorithm outperforms the random code about 0.1dB at the bit-error-rate of 10−5.
1. Introduction
Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes were discovered by Gallager in the 1960s [1], however, they became popular only after the rediscovery of them by Mackay [2].
Recently, several methods for constructing good families of LDPC codes have been proposed. Based on methods of constructions, LDPC codes can be divided into two categories: random or pseudorandom codes, and structured codes. In the first category, Gallager and Mackay gave their own construction method in [1] and [3] respectively, Chung optimized the degree distribution and obtained LDPC code with rate 0.5, the performance of which can be achieved within 0.0045dB of the Shannon limit at the bit-error-rate of

10−6 when block length is 107 [4]. Long random codes perform close to the Shannon limit, however, they don’t have sufficient structure to allow simple encoding, so researchers proposed the algebraic constructions of LDPC codes. This family of LDPC codes usually have the cyclic or quasicyclic structure, and can be encoded with simple feedback shift registers [5]. Based on the methods of algebraic, structured codes can be divided into three main categories. The first category is based on finite geometries [6], these codes are either cyclic or quasi-cyclic and contain no cycle of length 4, but the block length is restricted by the parameters of the finite field. The second category is based on block designs [7]. The third category is based on the circulant permutation matrices [8], [9], cycles of length 4 are not prevented if the circulant permutation matrices are simply random chosen. For the given desired girth g, random construction of (J, L) QC-LDPC codes is proposed [10], this construction method equals to the exhaustive search and the searching complexity is too high.
To reduce the construction complexity, we propose a novel construction method of (3, L) QC-LDPC codes with girth at least 8 in this paper. This algorithm generates every block sequentially, when generating the current block, makes sure that the current block forms no cycles of length 4 and 6 with the existent blocks. If the construction succeeds, the QC-LDPC code with girth at least 8 is obtained. Compared with the random constructions of (3, L) QC-LDPC codes with girth at least 8, the proposed algorithm avoids the exhaustive search and the complexity is extremely reduced.

3.2. Description of Progressive-Block Growth
From the LEMMA 1 and the LEMMA 2, we get a novel construction method of (3, L) QC-LDPC codes with girth at least 8 called Progreseive-Block Growth (PBG). For a given p to construct (3, L) QC-LDPC codes by PBG, we choose the first row of the parity-check matrix H From GF (p) with no constraints first. And then generate the elements in the incidence matrix D sequentially, the order is row by row, and the order in the same row is from the left to the right. When specifying the current D(i, j), the exclusion pool and candidate pool should be established using LEMMA 1 and LEMMA 2 first. After this, if the candidate pool is not empty, just randomly generate an element from the candidate pool to be the current D(i, j). If the candidate pool for any D(i, j) has no element, then the PBG algorithm terminates, the construction of (3, L) QC-LDPC codes fails. If the construction succeeds, which means the whole elements of the incidence matrix D has been specified, the paritycheck matrix H can be easily obtained by (5).
4 Complexity and performance simulations
The complexity of the PBG algorithm and random construction is analyzed first. The number of the cycles of length smaller than 8 to be checked is the main factor of the complexity from the two algorithms.
The fact that a cycle of length 6 consists of a long edge and two short edges has been summarized in section 3. For a parity-check matrix of (3, L) QC-LDPC code constructed by the random construction with the given p, there are L choices to specify the long edge, L choices to specify one of the two short edges, L − 1 choices to specify the other short edge so as to avoid the cases that three edges lie on the same column, here the other short edge has L − 1 not L − 2 choices because the cycles of length 4 can be considered as a particular case of length 6. To sum up, there are L2(L−1) cycles of length 4 and 6 in a matrix. If the construction succeed, the number of the parity-check matrices which need to be checked is between 1 and p3L , and if the construction fails, all the p3L possible parity-check matrices have to be checked. When checking a matrix, if this matrix has no cycles of length 4 and 6, the complexity of check this matrix is L2(L − 1), and if this matrix has cycles of length 4 and 6, the number of the cycles should be checked is between 1 and L2(L − 1), because if any cycles has been tested, we should not check the other cycles of this matrix any longer.

The parity-check matrices of Mackay’s LDPC codes are obtained from [12], and the LDPC codes based on finite geometries are generated according to [6]. The simulations adopt BPSK modulation, AWGN channel, and BP decoding algorithm with the maximum iterative number 100.

After downloading the parity-check matrices with length N = 504 and N = 1008 from [12], we construct (3, 6) QC-LDPC codes by the PBG algorithm for p = 84 and p = 168, and obtain two QC-LDPC codes with length 504 and 1008. 5 Conclusion
In this paper, we propose a low complexity construction method for (3, L) QC-LDPC codes based on circulant permutation matrix called Progressive-Block Growth. Compared with the random construction, the construction complexity of (3,L) QC-LDPC codes with girth at least 8 by this PBG algorithms is much reduced. Computer simulations show that the PBG-LDPC codes can not only outperform the random LDPC codes when the block length is short, but also can perform better than the the structured PG-LDPC codes.
