Convergence Analysis of Generalized Serial Message-Passing Schedules
Eran Sharon, Noam Presman, and Simon Litsyn, Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—Schedule is the order of passing messages between vertices of the bipartite graph defining an LDPC code in the process of decoding. Schedules affect the rate of decoding convergence. New efficient generalized serial schedules are described and analyzed, exhibiting significantly faster convergence compared to previously known schedules. For the proposed schedules, combinatorial and probabilistic analysis is presented, explaining the fast convergence observed in simulations. Using it, LDPC ensembles for which significantly better convergence rates can be achieved are identified. Specific code constructions from lifted graphs are further proposed, efficiently supporting the schedules. Examples based on regular LDPC codes are provided, in which the schedules achieve convergence speedup factors of up to 6 in comparison with the flooding schedule. Higher speedup factors are predicted by the analysis for irregular codes.
Index Terms—LDPC codes, iterative decoding, belief propagation, density evolution.

I. INTRODUCTION
T HE MOST attractive feature of LDPC codes is their ability to achieve a significant fraction of the channel capacity using message-passing decoding algorithms. These algorithms operate on the bipartite graph representation of the code by iteratively exchanging messages along the graph edges between variable and check nodes. As LDPC codes are maturing into commercial use, it becomes more important to develop efficient low complexity versions of these algorithms.
There are two main approaches to reducing the complexity of message-passing decoding algorithms. The first one is based on simplifying the computations involved in updating a message. This can be achieved by using low message resolution as done in Gallager A and B algorithms [12] or in various quantized versions of Belief Propagation (BP) decoding [26],[31]. In this case we also reduce the storage complexity of the decoder. Alternatively, sub-optimal message computation rules can be used, replacing the costly BP computations by simpler ones, as is done in the min-sum algorithm and its variants [11], [10], [35]. In both cases, the complexity reduction comes at the expense of degraded error correction capability. The second approach is based on reducing the expected number of messages that need to be computed until decoder converges, by using efficient message passing schedules. A message-passing schedule is the order of passing the messages along the graph edges. The scheduling determines the propagation speed of information over the code’s graph. Hence, it affects the decoder’s convergence rate and its computational complexity. In

The standard message-passing schedule is the flooding schedule [12],[15], where in each iteration all the variable nodes, and subsequently all the check nodes, pass new messages to their neighbors. Even though the flooding schedule is very popular, it is not efficient in terms of decoding complexity due to its slow convergence rate. Alternatively, faster converging schedules, which are based on a serial updating order have been considered in various papers [34], [14], [20], [28], [13], [36],[6]. Adaptive scheduling techniques, which change the message updating order based on the graph structure or the specific channel realization were also considered in several papers [21], [5], [17], [2].
Empirical results, indicating that the serial schedules converge twice as fast compared to the flooding schedule, were presented in various papers. In a previous work, we theoretically analyzed and quantified these observations using computation tree evolution analysis and Density Evolution (DE) analysis of serial schedules [30], [29]. It was shown that the serial schedules are expected to converge in half the number of flooding iterations when operating near the decoder’s threshold.
In cycle-free graphs one can define a schedule that converges in a single iteration [16]. Unfortunately, the codes based on such tree like graphs have poor performance due to small minimum distance [8]. Inspired by the fact that an optimal scheduling can be defined on a tree graph, several researchers suggested to emulate similar schedules on cyclic graphs by partitioning the graph into several spanning trees and performing locally optimal scheduling on these trees [19],[18],[24],[33]. These papers reported that the convergence is only twice as fast as the flooding schedule, similarly to the serial schedules. The paper is organized as follows. Background on LDPC codes and message-passing decoding is provided in Section II. Generalized serial schedules and code constructions that can efficiently support them are described in Section III. The rest of the paper is dedicated to theoretical convergence analysis via combinatorial and probabilistic approaches. Section IV includes combinatorial analysis of the schedules’ computation tree growth rate. It shows that the computation tree under generalized serial schedules can grow much faster, implying that they can propagate information faster on the code’s graph compared to previously known schedules. We further prove that the computation tree depth is asymptotically concentrated around its expected value. This indicates that the accelerated growth rate of the computation tree should be manifested in a similar acceleration in the schedule’s convergence rate. Based on the analysis we identify LDPC ensembles for which appropriate generalized serial schedules can significantly accelerate the convergence rate. We show some specific simple examples based on regular LDPC codes where speedup factors of up to 6 are obtained compared to the flooding schedule. In Section V we develop a protograph DE algorithm for general lifted schedules over lifted graphs, supported by an appropriate concentration theorem, justifying the analysis. Based on it we perform a probabilistic analysis of the asymptotic convergence rate of various generalized serial schedules. The analysis confirms that the acceleration factors of the computation tree growth rate, analyzed in Section IV, are manifested in similar convergence rate acceleration factors. Simulation results are presented in Section VI. Discussion and conclusions are provided in Section VII.

Serial scheduling can be done by serially updating variable nodes’ messages [34], [14], [17]. More specifically, the variable nodes are traversed in some order and for each variable node v ∈ V , all the check-to-variable messages are sent to the node v and then all the variable-to-check messages are sent from the node v. We will refer to such a schedule as a serial-C schedule.

IV. COMBINATORIAL CONVERGENCE ANALYSIS BASED ON
COMPUTATION TREE EVOLUTION
In this section we examine the evolution of the messagepassing algorithm’s computation tree under the proposed scheduling. We briefly review the results from [30] on serial schedules and then extend them to generalized serial schedules. We present the analysis for the serial-V type schedules. However the same analysis applies to the serial-C schedules as well. Consider a message passed from a variable node v to a check node c along the edge e = (v, c) of a given graph G during message-passing decoding. Depending on the iteration number, this message is a function of a tree spanned according to the code’s graph G from the edge e towards the variable node v and of the received channel observations for variables that are contained in the tree. We refer to this tree as the computation tree of e, and denote it by Te(l). Note that the computation tree for a graph with cycles may contain replicated nodes. For example, the computation tree of an arbitrary edge e after the first decoding iteration of a length 4 regular (2,4)-LDPC code under the flooding and the serialV1 schedules is shown on Figure 4. Through the rest of this section we will assume computation tree without cycles. Such an assumption is asymptotically justified. The reason is that at a given iteration, the computation tree of a given edge is cycle free with high probability, assuming the code is long enough, as proved in various concentration theorems [26], [30]. The computation tree of the flooding schedule is a balanced tree, and its depth is increased by 2 at each iteration. On the other hand, the computation tree of a serial schedule is not a balanced tree and its depth with respect to each of its leaves grows by at least 2 at each iteration. Thus, the computation tree of an edge under a serial schedule always contains the computation tree of the edge under the flooding schedule. This implies that a serial decoder is expected to converge faster (or at least not slower) compared to the flooding scheduled decoder. In [30] we refine this claim by analyzing exactly how fast the serial schedule’s computation tree grows compared to the flooding schedule computation tree.

The combinatorial acceleration factors of several regular LDPC ensembles are shown in Figure 10.
Analyzing the expression in Theorem 4.6 based on (IV-A), one can see that as the average node degrees become smaller the acceleration factors increase. Actually, any asymptotic combinatorial acceleration factor can be achieved by making both the average variable and check node degrees approach 2, and considering scheduling of sufficient depth d. Indeed, in the degenerate case of a (2, 2)-regular LDPC ensemble the combinatorial acceleration factor equals 2d, as the schedule’s sub-tree is composed of a single path. Obviously, this case is not interesting. However, this indicates that one can design low rate, long, irregular LDPC codes which exhibit very high convergence speedup factors under appropriate generalized serial scheduling. We leave the design and simulation of such codes for future research.
Another observation is that, for a given degree distribution, increasing the serial schedule’s depth can increase the acceleration factor only up to a certain limit (except for the degenerate case of a (2, 2)-regular LDPC ensemble). VII. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper we described efficient generalized serial schedules and analyzed their convergence rate. We showed that the proposed schedules can provide significant convergence speedup factors over the flooding schedule, which exceed the speedup factor of 2 provided by standard serial schedules. These can be translated into significantly reduced decoder logic complexity and power consumption, at no coding gain penalty. Such advantages can help in making LDPC based error correction coding schemes a commercially viable solution for emerging communication industry standards, which are characterized by high throughput and low complexity and
