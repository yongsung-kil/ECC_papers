Abstract‚ÄĒWe propose a symbol-based belief propagation (BP) algorithm for iterative soft-decision decoding of Reed-Solomon (RS) codes. Complexity reduction is achieved by using a fast Fourier transform (FFT)-based BP algorithm. Parity-check matrix adaptation based on the reliability of the codeword symbols is an essential step to make the BP algorithm effective on high-density parity-check matrices characteristic of RS codes. The matrix adaptation, as well as all other operations, is performed at symbol level such that bit-to-symbol and symbol-tobit conversions are avoided. A moderate coding gain over algebraic hard-decision decoding is achieved on additive white Gaussian noise channels.
The symbol-based algorithm presented in this paper addresses the main weakness of its bit-based counterpart, namely the prohibitively high complexity for practical applications that use long codes and large finite fields, albeit with less coding gain.
Keywords-belief propagation; iterative soft-decision decoding; low-density parity-check codes; Reed-Solomon codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
Reed-Solomon (RS) codes have been widely used in a variety of communication systems on both transmission and storage channels, and are expected to be included in several new and future standards, such as the future fourth generation mobile systems [1]. This widespread use of RS codes is due to the excellent performance of algebraic hard-decision decoding (HDD) algorithms, such as the Berlekamp-Massey and Euclidian algorithms in both random noise and in the presence of burst errors. However, improved performance can be realized by using soft-decision decoding (SDD) algorithms, albeit at a significant cost in complexity.
Over the years many SDD algorithms have been proposed in the literature trading-off coding gain for complexity [2]. Since the introduction of turbo codes [3] in 1993, the central theme in error control coding has shifted to iterative soft-input soft-output decoding. Recently, iterative SDD of RS codes has been the subject of a great deal of work [4]-[8]. Both the BahlCocker-Jelinek-Raviv (BCJR) [4] and belief propagation (BP) [5]-[8] algorithms have been employed. Because of the high complexity of the BCJR algorithm, BP-based algorithms are preferred. Strictly speaking, BP algorithms are only exact for tree graphs, i.e., cycle-free graphs, but simulation results have shown that they still work well on linear block codes with

sparse parity-check matrices, particularly if they do not have short cycles. However, the parity-check matrices of RS codes are not sparse; in fact they can be very dense. In order to employ BP algorithms, several schemes have been considered; most notably Jiang and Narayanan [7] proposed to diagonalize the submatrix corresponding to the least reliable bits of the equivalent binary parity-check matrix at each iteration before applying a BP algorithm. The error performance compares favorably with other known algorithms for both short and long RS codes. The main issue is the high complexity.
All of the above BP-based methods use binary image expansions of the RS codes and then deal with the binary linear block codes thus obtained; the algebraic structure of the RS codes is only partially utilized. However, many important channels, such as bandwidth-efficient channels, naturally associate with nonbinary channel alphabets, where soft information from the demodulator can be directly employed by the decoder avoiding symbol-to-bit or bit-to-symbol conversions. This suggests that algorithms based on symbol representation may be more efficient. As far as the BP algorithm is concerned, we employ an efficient fast Fourier transform (FFT)-based BP algorithm initially designed for lowdensity parity-check (LDPC) codes over the finite field GF(q) [9]. All the processing is performed at symbol level, i.e., using nonbinary elements over GF(q).
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. In Section II, we review BP algorithms for decoding nonbinary LDPC codes in the probability and the frequency-domains. The emphasis is on the reduced-complexity symbol-wise FFT-based BP algorithm. In Section III, we present the symbol-based BP algorithm for decoding RS codes and identify the difference with its bit-based counterpart [7]. In Section IV, we present numerical results in terms of the frame-error rate (FER) performance and complexity for some commonly used RS codes over the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel. Finally, we conclude the paper in Section V.

III. SYMBOL-BASED BELIEF PROPAGATION ALGORITHM
Many simulations have shown that BP algorithms work well on linear block codes with sparse parity-check matrices, particularly if they do not have short cycles. However, the parity-check matrices of RS codes are not sparse; in fact they can be very dense. In order to employ BP algorithms to decode RS codes effectively, we should make the parity-check matrices sparser. Because most hard-decision errors are made on the least reliable symbols, making the submatrix corresponding to these symbols of the parity-check matrix sparse is desirable such that the influence of these least reliable symbols during the iterative decoding is minimized. Because the parity-check matrix HS of an RS code is full rank [15], we can at least obtain an equivalent matrix of the form HS,D = [DM P] or HS,D = [P DM ], where
DM = diag(Di ), Di ‚Č† 0, ‚ąÄi =1, 2, , n ‚ąí k, is a diagonal matrix of size M = rank(HS ) = n ‚ąí k. In the following, we will discuss two methods of diagonalizing the submatrix corresponding to the least reliable symbols of the parity-check matrix of an RS code.

A. Gauss‚ÄďJordan Elimination over GF(q)
The Gauss‚ÄďJordan elimination performs elementary row operations on a matrix from top row to bottom row such that the matrix has the reduced echelon form, i.e., there are zeros both above and below each pivot, where the pivot is the nonzero entry of the matrix such that the row operations can work properly. Traditionally, all the operations are performed in the real number field. It can be extended to finite fields, where all the arithmetic operations are performed over GF(q). The complexity of the Gauss-Jordan elimination for matrix HS of size (n ‚ąí k) √ó n is O((n ‚ąí k)n2 ) .

NUMERICAL RESULTS For RS (31, 25) code on an AWGN channel with binary PSK (BPSK) signaling, the FER performance is shown in Fig. 2 and compared with the bit-based ABP and HDD under the same conditions. At FER=10-4, there is about 1.3 dB gain over HDD, but the performance falls short of the bit-based ABP by about 1.0 dB. At FER=10-6, the values are 1.6 dB and 0.9 dB, respectively. However, the complexity of the symbol-based ABP is low. For example, to achieve the same FER of 1.1√ó10‚ąí4 , bit-based ABP decoding takes 3.6 times longer than symbol-based ABP decoding. Similar ratio is obtained for a FER of 1.1√ó10‚ąí6 , of about 3 times. For five iterations, the performance of the symbol-based ABP decoding is only 0.2 dB inferior to the bit-based ABP as is shown in Fig. 3. However, the former is about 2.5 times faster. The performance of the bitbased ABP is sensitive to the number of iterations. The gain of the bit-based ABP over the symbol-based ABP can be minimal if the number of iterations is not chosen properly. On the other hand, the performance of the symbol-based ABP algorithm does not change much by increasing the number of iterations. There is less than 0.2 dB difference when the number of iterations changes from 5 to 20.

V. CONCLUSION
The reduced-complexity implementation of the symbolbased ABP algorithm presented in this paper is significantly faster than the standard bit-based version, and delivers a moderate coding gain over algebraic hard-decision decoding of RS codes. In spite of some performance loss with respect to the bit-based ABP, the proposed implementation is useful for practical applications that use long codes over large finite fields, and require short decoding latency. Since all the operations are performed at the symbol-level, bit-to-symbol and symbol-to-bit conversions are not needed and the matrix adaptation is performed in an efficient way that can be parallelized.
