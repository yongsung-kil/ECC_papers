Memory-Efficient and High-Throughput Decoding of Quasi-Cyclic LDPC Codes
Yongmei Dai, Student Member, IEEE, Zhiyuan Yan, Senior Member, IEEE, and Ning Chen, Student Member, IEEE

Abstract—We propose turbo-sum-product (TSP) and shuffledsum-product (SSP) decoding algorithms for quasi-cyclic lowdensity parity-check codes, which not only achieve faster convergence and better error performance than the sum-product algorithm, but also require less memory in partly parallel decoder architectures. Compared with the turbo decoding algorithm, our TSP algorithm saves the same amount of memory and may achieve a higher decoding throughput. The convergence behaviors of our TSP and SSP algorithms are also compared with those of the SP, turbo, and shuffled algorithms by their extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) charts.
Index Terms—LDPC, quasi-cyclic, turbo decoding, shuffled decoding, sum-product decoding, EXIT charts.

Based on the same approach, we also propose the SSP algorithm corresponding to the shuffled decoding algorithm. We focus on quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes in this paper since they have relatively good error performance [13] and very efficient partly parallel decoder architectures (see, e.g., [14]). Although our TSP and SSP algorithms are proposed for QC LDPC codes, they can be easily extended to decode general LDPC codes. Finally, we present simulation results as well as extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) charts analysis to compare all the algorithms.

I. INTRODUCTION
L OW-DENSITY parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] and their efficient hardware implementation have been receiving a lot of attention due to their near-capacity performance when decoded with the iterative sum-product (SP) algorithm [2]. Many variants and extensions of the SP algorithm, which change the message passing (MP) schedules to improve the performance of the SP algorithm, have also been proposed (see, e.g., [3]–[12]). For efficient hardware implementation, the turbo decoding [3] and shuffled decoding [5], [6] algorithms are of particular interest since both algorithms can lead to better error performance, faster convergence, and reduced memory requirement. However, the decoding throughput of both algorithms may be limited by multiple serial sub-iterations despite their faster convergence. Hence, we propose turbo-sum-product (TSP) and shuffled-sum-product (SSP) decoding algorithms. Each iteration of our TSP algorithm consists of two sequential sub-iterations. Despite its slower convergence than the turbo decoding algorithm, our TSP algorithm may achieve a better tradeoff between convergence and throughput, resulting in a higher decoding throughput than the turbo decoding algorithm. The implementation of our TSP algorithm can save the same amount of memory as that of the turbo decoding algorithm. II. TSP AND SSP DECODING OF QC LDPC CODES
In this section, we first compare the MP schedules of the SP, turbo, and group shuffled algorithms, and show that the turbo and group shuffled algorithms are both variants of the SP algorithm and are dual of each other. Then we focus on QC LDPC codes, and propose our TSP and SSP algorithms.
A. MP Schedules of Various Decoding Algorithms
In the turbo decoding [3], H is split into J submatrices row-wise, i.e., H = [H1T H2T · · · HTJ ]T . Each Hg defines one supercode Cg, and the original LDPC code C = C1 ∩ C2 · · · ∩ CJ . Each submatrix Hg (g = 1, 2, · · · , J) must also satisfy the constraint that there is at most one 1 in each column to save memory. Let C and V denote the sets of check and variable nodes respectively in the Tanner graph of C. The breakup of H partitions C into J disjoint subsets: C = C˜1 ∪ C˜2 ∪ · · · ∪ C˜J , where C˜g (g = 1, 2, · · · , J) denotes the set of check nodes corresponding to Hg. In the shuffled or group shuffled decoding [6], H is split into G submatrices column-wise, i.e., H = H¯ 1 H¯ 2 · · · H¯ G . Correspondingly, V is partitioned into G disjoint subsets: V = V˜1 ∪ V˜2 ∪ · · · ∪ V˜G, where V˜g (g = 1, 2, · · · , G) denotes the set of variable nodes corresponding to H¯ g.
As shown in Fig. 1(a), each iteration of the SP algorithm consists of two serial sub-iterations: first passes the messages from V to all C˜g’s (g = 1, 2, · · · , J) concurrently and then passes the messages from all C˜g’s (g = 1, 2, · · · , J) to V concurrently. In the turbo decoding, each iteration becomes J serial sub-iterations as shown in Fig. 1(b): first passes the messages from V to C˜1 and from C˜1 to V , then passes the messages from V to C˜2 and from C˜2 to V , and so on. In the turbo decoding algorithm, in order to save memory, each of the J submatrices must satisfy the constraint that there is at most one 1 in each column. For general LDPC codes, to satisfy this constraint may require a very large J. Hence, the turbo decoding algorithm is more suitable for codes with architecture aware structures [3]. In contrast, our TSP algorithm can be easily extended to decode general LDPC codes since we need to split H into two submatrices and only one submatrix needs to satisfy the constraint. Given a general LDPC code, we first perform row permutations on its H such that when H is split, H1 has as many 1’s as possible and has at most one 1 in each column. Note that the row permutations are performed to maximize the memory saving. After H is split into two submatrices, our TSP algorithm can be applied accordingly. The group shuffled decoding algorithm has a similar limitation, whereas our SSP algorithm can be easily extended to decode general LDPC codes.
The turbo decoding algorithm splits H into J submatrices, while our TSP algorithm splits H into two submatrices. In general, we may split H into 2 < J1 < J submatrices, among which J1 − 1 submatrices have only one 1 in each column, and one submatrix may have more than one 1 in each column. In this way, we may be able to achieve a better tradeoff between convergence and throughput for certain codes while maintaining the memory saving. Similar generalization is possible for our SSP algorithm.

As J increases, the convergence rate improvement of our TSP algorithm decreases. However, our TSP algorithm trades the convergence rate improvement for higher throughput. Note that decoding throughput is inversely proportional to both D and the clock period. Note that T0, T1, and T2 as well as the clock period ultimately depend on implementation details of the architectures. To illustrate the difference between decoding algorithms, we assume the clock period is the same in all architectures henceforth. Due to faster convergence, our TSP algorithm achieves higher throughput than the SP algorithm assuming T0 ≈ T2. Our TSP algorithm has fewer sub-iterations and hence may achieve a higher decoding throughput than the turbo algorithm. For the three SFT codes, the decoding throughput of our TSP algorithm is roughly 20%, 44%, and 72% higher than that of the turbo decoding algorithm assuming T1 ≈ T2.
