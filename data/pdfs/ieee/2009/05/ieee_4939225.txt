Doubly Generalized LDPC Codes over the AWGN Channel
Yige Wang, Student Member, IEEE, and Marc Fossorier, Fellow, IEEE

Abstract—In this paper, the design of doubly generalized low-density parity-check (DGLDPC) codes is proposed. This approach generalizes the structure of LDPC codes at both check and variable nodes. The performance of DGLDPC codes over the AWGN channel is analyzed using EXIT charts. Combined with differential evolution optimization, this analysis provides thresholds for DGLDPC codes that are better than that of LDPC and GLDPC codes with the same maximum variable degree. These theoretical thresholds are verified via simulations. Furthermore DGLDPC codes exhibit a lower error floor compared with their LDPC and GLDPC counterparts.
Index Terms—Differential evolution optimization, extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) charts, generalized low-density paritycheck (GLDPC) codes, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
L OW-DENSITY parity-check (LDPC) codes were originally proposed by Gallager [1] in 1960s. Due to the limited processing capabilities then, they did not receive much attention until being rediscovered by MacKay and Neal [2] in 1996. Tanner extended the work of Gallager using bipartite graph representations [3]. Based on the Tanner graph, a standard LDPC code is characterized by random connections between variable nodes and check nodes, which can be viewed as repetition codes and single parity check (SPC) codes, respectively. Tanner also proposed the use of short component codes as check nodes other than SPC codes. These codes are referred to as generalized LDPC (GLDPC) codes. Within this framework, many codes have been considered as check node component codes, such as Hamming codes [4][5], BCH codes [5][6], Reed-Solomon (RS) codes [6] and Hadamard codes [7]. Some hybrid constructions have also been studied. In [8], a hybrid structure mixing SPC codes and Hamming codes at the check nodes has been presented for the GilbertElliott channel. In [9], another hybrid construction referred to as doped LDPC code has been studied for the AWGN channel. All these constructions are based on strengthening the check node component codes at the expense of a rate loss. In [10], GLDPC codes referred to as molecular codes are constructed by allowing both variable and check nodes to represent codes more general than repetition and SPC codes, and regular molecular codes are mainly discussed. In this

paper, GLDPC codes more closely related to conventional LDPC codes than the codes of [10] and with more diversities in variable and check nodes are studied. We refer to them as doubly GLDPC (DGLDPC) codes.
For LDPC codes, both density evolution [11] and extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) charts [12]−[21] are effective ways to analyze the performance of a code. For the binary erasure channel (BEC), these two methods are equivalent. For the AWGN channel, density evolution is more accurate, while EXIT charts are easier to visualize and to evaluate. For DGLDPC codes, density evolution becomes often intractable, and only EXIT charts are left to analyze their performance and estimate their threshold. In [22]−[25], a concept termed information combining has been introduced. In particular a lower bound and an upper bound on the combined information are presented. In this paper, using the concept of information combining, we derived closed forms for some variable component codes of DGLDPC codes. For more general codes, we applied the method of [26], which has been derived for extrinsic mutual information at the output of a pseudo-MAP decoder over any binary input memoryless symmetric channel. For the AWGN channel, the general expression of [26] is accurate for SPC codes and it is also a good approximation for Hamming codes and simplex codes. While the results of [26] are directly applicable to check component codes, we generalize them to variable component codes in the process.
Based on EXIT charts, differential evolution (DE) is used in threshold optimization. DE is a parallel direct search technique featured by evolution of variable vectors [27]. This technique has been successfully applied to design irregular LDPC codes with excellent thresholds for the BEC [28], the AWGN channel [29], and the Rayleigh fading channel [30]. In [31], it has been used for threshold optimization of DGLDPC codes over the BEC. In this paper, we use this technique for threshold optimization of DGLDPC codes over the AWGN channel. From DE optimization of EXIT charts and simulation results, we observe that carefully chosen variable and check component codes can significantly improve the threshold of DGLDPC codes compared with that of LDPC and GLDPC codes with the same maximum variable degree.

C. Threshold Optimization Over the AWGN Channel Based on EXIT Charts
It has been shown that capacity-achieving sequences have to satisfy flatness condition [35] for the BEC and stability condition [29] for other channels. Therefore, to approach capacity for the BEC one must match the EXIT curve of the variable nodes exactly to that of the check nodes [18][20]. This curve-fitting technique also works well for BPSK signaling over an AWGN channel.
Based on EXIT charts, differential evolution (DE) is used in threshold optimization. This technique has been applied to design irregular LDPC codes with excellent thresholds in [28]−[30]. It is straightforward to apply DE to threshold optimization of DGLDPC codes. Detailed steps can be found in [42].
In DE, the thresholds of all the degree distribution candidates need to be updated at every generation. Since the variable EXIT curve changes with Eb/N0, we need to evaluate it at many different values of Eb/N0. Numerical simulations of IE (IA, Eb/N0, R) for variable nodes are also possible. However it is quite inflexible and time-consuming. The EXIT functions derived in Section IV-A and IV-B allow us to obtain IE (IA, Eb/N0, R) much faster.
