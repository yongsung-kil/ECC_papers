Regular LDPC Codes from Strongly Regular (α , β ) -Geometries

Li Xiuli Shan Zhengduo
School of Math. and Phys., Qingdao University of Science and Technology, Shandong 266061,China lixiuli2004@tom.com; shanzhengduo_11@163.com

ABSTRACT: By considering a class of combinatorial structures,
known as strongly regular (α , β ) -geometries, we define a class
of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. We derive bounds on minimum distance, rank and girth for the codes from strongly
regular (α , β ) -geometrie, which have not previously been
proposed for use with iterative decoding. In further, we present performance results for the class of codes.
KEYWORDS: Gallager codes; iterative decoding; low-density
parity-check (LDPC) codes; strongly regular (α , β ) -geometries
I. INTRODUCTION
The aim of this paper is to develop block codes suitable for iterative decoding using the sum-product algorithm. Block codes which are iteratively decoded were first presented by Gallager[1], and called low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, as a sparse parity-check matrix is essential for the decoding. LDPC codes have been the focus of intense research interest in recent years, when rediscovered in the wake of turbo codes, and shown to perform remarkably close to the Shannon limit. When applied to LDPC codes, the sum-product algorithm has a decoding complexity linear in the blocklength which makes very long codes feasible.
For a good LDPC code, it is required that the parity-check matrix is sparse. The girth of the Tanner graph of the code is also important. Other properties considered are the rank over GF (2) of the parity-check matrix and the minimum distance of the code. The properties required of LDPC codes make the field of combinatorial designs a particularly promising source for algebraic code constructions.
Vontobel and Tanner[2] have presented algebraic LDPC codes from combinatorial structures called generalized quadrangles. Johnson and Weller[3] developed their results and presented algebraic LDPC codes from combinatorial structures called partial geometries.
In this paper, we will consider a class of combinatorial structures called strongly regular (α , β ) -geometries. New classes of codes are derived, and the structure of strongly regular (α , β ) -geometries can be used to derive expressions for the minimum distance, girth and rank. In further, we will present the performance of the codes.

Graphical representations of designs are also a useful way

The length of the cycle is the number of edges it contains, and the girth of a graph is the length of its shortest cycle. An incidence structure can also be described by a point graph G , which has vertex set V=P and v= V vertices. An edge

connects two vertices if the corresponding points are incident with the same block B ∈ B , and we say that the vertices (and the corresponding points) are connected.
A graph G is said to be regular if each vertex is
connected to exactly k other vertices. Further, if any two
connected vertices of G are both connected together to exactly λ other vertices, and two unconnected vertices are
both connected to exactly μ vertices together, the graph is

called strongly regular and specified by the parameters (v, k, λ, μ) .

IV. PERFORMANCE
We compare the performance of LDPC codes from strongly regular (α , β ) -geometries with that of randomly constructed codes with the same code length in the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel, using the sumproduct decoding algorithm from [9].

Figure 1. Performance of LDPC codes in an AWGN channel using sum-product decoding
In Fig. 1, a (4,14)-regular LDPC code from strongly regular (1,5)-geometries is compared with a randomly

constructed (4,14)-regular LDPC code with the same length using a maximum of 100 iterations. Their code rates are 0.591 and 0.456 respectively. It shows that the BER of LDPC codes from strongly regular (α , β ) -geometries descends in advance although the code rate of strongly regular (α , β ) -geometries codes is higher than that of randomly constructed codes. This property shows that the LDPC codes from strongly regular (α , β ) -geometries perform well.
