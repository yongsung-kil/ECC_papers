AbstractвҖ”In this paper, we derive two algebraic methods for constructing regular low density parity check (LDPC) codes вҖ“ one based on elements of finite fields and the other directly based on cyclotomic cosets. We show that the constructed codes have high rates and are free of cycles of length four; consequently, they can be decoded using standard iterative decoding algorithms. In addition we compute the exact dimension and establish bounds on the minimum and stopping distances of the constructed codes.
Index TermsвҖ”LDPC Codes, Nonprimitive BCH Codes, Channel Coding, Performance and Iterative Decoding.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity check (LDPC) codes as an important class of modern coding theory have many applications. They are a capacity-approaching class of codes that were first described in a seminal work by Gallager [4] and extended later by Tanner [11]. A regular (ПҒ, О») LDPC code is measured by the weights of its columns ПҒ and rows О». Iterative decoding of LDPC and turbo codes highlighted the importance of these classes of codes for communication and storage channels. Furthermore, these codes are practical and have been used in many beneficial applications [2], [5]. In contrast to BCH and Reed-Solomon (RS) cyclic codes, LDPC cyclic codes with sparse parity check matrices are customarily constructed by a computer search. In practice, LDPC codes can achieve higher performance and better error correction capabilities than many other codes, because they have efficient iterative decoding algorithms, such as the product-sum algorithm [12], [7], [6], [5].
There have been several methods to construct regular and irregular LDPC codes including algebraic and random approaches, see for example [3], [10], and references therein. A survey of the previous work done on algebraic constructions of LDPC codes based on finite geometry, elements of finite fields, and RS codes is presented in [6]. A construction of LDPC codes based on binary narrow-sense primitive BCH codes is given in [13]. Furthermore, a good construction of LDPC codes should have a girth of the Tanner graph, of at least 6 [6], [5].
One way to measure the decoding performance of linear codes is by computing their minimum distance. The performance of low-density parity check codes under iterative

decoding can also be judged by measuring their stopping sets and stopping distance, which is the size of the smallest stopping set [9]. For any given parity check matrix H of an LDPC code C, one can obtain the Tanner graph G of this code and compute the stopping sets. BCH codes are decoded using the Berlekamp-Massey algorithm; on the other hand, LDPC codes are decoded using iterative decoding and Belief Propagation (BP) algorithms.

A low-density parity check code (or LDPC short) is a binary block code that has a parity check matrix H in which each row (and each column) is sparse. An LDPC code is called regular with parameters (ПҒ, О») if it has a sparse parity check matrix H in which each row has ПҒ nonzero entries and each column has О» nonzero entries.
A regular LDPC code defined by a parity check matrix H is said to satisfy the row-column condition if and only if any two rows (or, equivalently, any two columns) of H have at most one position of a nonzero entry in common. The row-column condition ensures that the Tanner graph does not have cycles of length four.
A Tanner graph of a binary code with a pa. rity check matrix H = (hij) is a graph with vertex set V вҲӘ C that has one vertex in V for each column of H and one vertex in C for each row in H, and there is an edge between two vertices i and j if and only if hij = 0. Thus, the Tanner graph is a bipartite graph. The vertices in V are called the variable nodes, and the vertices in C are called the check nodes. We refer to d(vi) and d(cj) as the degrees of variable node vi and check node cj respectively.
Definition 2 (Grith of a Tanner graph). The girth g of the Tanner graph is the length of its shortest cycle (minimum cycle).

A Tanner graph with large girth is desirable, as iterative decoding converges faster for graphs with large girth.

VI. CONCLUSION
We introduced two families of regular LDPC codes based on elements of finite fields and structures of cyclotomic cosets. We demonstrated a systematic method to present elements of the parity check matrix of nonprimitive BCH codes as vectors of certain length. In addition, we demonstrated that these constructed codes have high rates and a uniform structure that made it easy to derive their dimensions. Finally, a comparison between the performance of the constructed codes and other previous known codes is shown.
