Performance Analysis of Different Puncturing Schemes for Rate Compatible Block-type LDPC

Zheng Meng, Fei Zesong, Kuang Jingming, Chen Xiang, Sun Wei
RCDCT, Modern Communication Lab, Dept. of E.E. Beijing Institute of Technology Beijing, P.R. China {ZMRA, feizesong}@bit.edu.cn

AbstractвҖ”The LDPC can provide multiple rates without employing more parity check matrices through the using of Rate Compatible strategy, in which the idea of puncturing is used to get codes at higher rates. This paper mainly focused on Blocktype LDPC, for which three different puncturing algorithms are shown. One is the original scheme of puncturing based on the recovery step. Another one is proposed by directly using the former on Base check matrix. Besides, the idea of puncturing distribution on systematical B-LDPC also performs well. These puncturing schemes are analyzed and compared in the text, and based on the result, new puncturing schemes for B-LDPC in IEEE 802.16e standard are proposed.
Keywords- Block-type LDPC; puncturing; IEEE 802.16e
I. INTRODUCTION
LDPC code has been proved to be one of the best channel codes with its performance approaching the Shannon limit. Because of the advantage such as the parallelizable decoding algorithm and the self-detection capacity, it has been chosen as the Forward Error Correction (FEC) candidate of the 802.16e standard [1]. According to the standard the Block-type LDPC (B-LDPC) is employed, which can flexibly support different coding length through the use of blocks with different size. However, in practical communication system, the channel state may vary considerably, which means many flexible optional rates are always demanded. In traditional way, more rates available means more parity-check matrices, which will need lots of memory space and increase the complexity of the decoder. A typical method for achieving multiple rates without new parity-check matrices introduced is the Rate Matching, which can be described as two parts. One is to get the lower rates and the other for the higher. To get lower rates codes, previous work in [2] and [3] gives the method of extension of the parity-check matrix and information bits nulling. And for the latter, the idea of puncturing several bits from the code to be transmitted is always imposed, and it actually gives good performance [4] [5].
As the position of the bits deleted will absolutely affect the performance of the punctured code, those bits have to be chosen carefully, which is called puncturing distribution. In [6] an idea is proposed to puncture the code respectively with the recovery sequence in the decoding BP algorithm. But such a puncturing distribution scheme is of high computing

complexity. It proves the puncturing algorithm with the sequence of recovery can be directly applied to the base matrix of the B-LDPC [7]. And for more simplified application, [8] [9] gives the deleting sequence of blocks for certain systematic BLDPC code. These three algorithms above are all applicable to the practical puncturing distribution, and each with its own advantage and disadvantage. In this paper, the performance of these three schemes will be compared through simulation results.
The 802.16e standard gives four different rates: 1/2, 2/3, 3/4, 5/6, which means at least 4 different parity-check matrices may be used in the decoding process. Through the using of puncture, we hope to reduce the number of matrices needed while a wide range of coding rates can be provided and the performance wonвҖҷt affect great loss. According to the simulation result, two optional puncturing schemes are designed based on the standard, with which the objective above is achieved furthest.
The paper is organized as follows. Section в…Ў introduces the detail of the three puncturing distribution algorithm. In Section в…ў, the simulation result is shown and analyzed. And in Section в…Ј, the conclusion is given and the puncturing schemes are proposed for 802.16e LDPC rate matching.

A. Puncturing With the Recovery Step(RS)
As mentioned in the first part, to get the codes with higher rates, some bits of the code, which are shown as variable nodes in the Tanner graphs, have to be deleted. And the deleted nodes wonвҖҷt be transmitted to the decoder, so their prior information will be fixed to 0. With the iterative decoding process going on, the prior information of every node will be updated. If a variable node receives non-zero information from the neighboring check node, we note the variable node as вҖҳrecoveredвҖҷ. The scheme of puncturing with the sequence of recovery was proposed so that the nodes deleted will be recovered within fewer steps and we can get the better performance of the punctured code.
Once a variable node is deleted, its relative check nodes will have one of their neighbors punctured. If a check node has only one neighboring variable node deleted, itвҖҷs called вҖҳsurvived check nodeвҖҷ. By contrary, if more than two neighboring variable nodes deleted, itвҖҷs named вҖҳdead check nodeвҖҷ. Once a variable node connects to at least one survived check nodes, it can receive non-zero information from the check node in the first iteration and thus been recovered. Such a variable node is called one-step-recoverable (1-SR) node. These conceptions are shown in Fig.1. The filled and unfilled circles are undeleted and deleted variable nodes, respectively. The squares S are the survived check nodes, and the square D is the dead check node.

Figure 1. One-step recoverable node
As variable nodes sends non-zero messages to its neighboring check nodes, a 1-SR node may help one dead check node turning to a survived check node. And in the iterative process, the new survived check node makes other variable nodes be recovered. So with the iterative decoding process going on, all the deleted nodes receives a non-zero value, which means they are recovered. If a variable node is recovered in the k-th iteration, it is named k-SR node. Fig.2 shows the total recover trees of a 3-SR node. As all the variable nodes are partitioned into subsets labeled with the recover steps, G0 , G1 and Gk , the puncturing sequence is determined. The 1-SR nodes in G1 should be deleted first, and if the number doesnвҖҷt meet the demand of the target rate, nodes in G2 , G3 ,вҖҰ, Gk should be deleted. The nodes in G0 can never be deleted.
Most time only part of the variable nodes in a subset need to be deleted. So puncturing sequence within the subset is influential to the performance of punctured code. Reference [6] proved that over an Additive White Gaussian Channel (AWGN), the recovery error probability of a deleted variable nodes v вҲҲ Gk depends on the number of variable nodes S(v) on the recovery tree. Based on the criterion described above, the iterative grouping and sorting algorithm are proposed to determine the final order of puncturing, with grouping to divide all the variable nodes into subsets, and sorting to get the sequence within subsets.

C. Block Puncturing on Systematical code (BPS)
Systematic code consists of information part and parity check part, so all the bits to be punctured can be chosen from the parity bits, which can keep the information bits intact. Applied to the 802.16e B-LDPC, it means all punctured blocks are from the Hb2 . Though not optimized, it can also provide good performance close to the two schemes mentioned before, while avoiding the complex grouping and sorting computation to generate the order of deleting. WhatвҖҷs more, such a scheme is able to offer higher rate limit than the other two schemes. Assume a mb Г— nb base matrix, the deleting order of the node blocks should be fixed:

{kb + 2, kb + 4....kb + ((nb вҲ’ kb )div2) * 2,

(6)

kb + 3, kb + 5...., kb + ((nb вҲ’1вҲ’ kb )div2) * 2 +1}

Here kb = nb вҲ’ mb and div(i) means only the integer part of the division result will be used. The column weight of the nodes in node block kb +1 is all 3, which is higher than those of all the other parity bits. These parity bits could not be deleted, otherwise, only information part will be left which means no protection to the information bits. Thus/Therefore the highest rate can be reached is kb / kb +1 employing this puncturing method.

Seen from Fig 3 and Fig 5, the codes generated from the same code with different schemes demonstrate nearly the same BLER at lower rates such as 0.6 and 0.7, and it seems that the length of the codes which are generated from the same base check matrix doesnвҖҷt affect their performance loss because of puncturing. ItвҖҷs also shown that at rate 0.8 and BLER 0.01, the punctured codes with Block Puncturing demonstrate 0.3-0.4dB loss than that with the other two schemes. But in Fig 7, as all the codes are generated from code (576,432) at rate 0.75, the Block Puncturing scheme outperforms a little than the other two schemes at rates 5/6 and 0.9. It should be mentioned that from Fig 3 and Fig 4, we can see though the BLER may be worse, the Block Puncturing always gives out a better average BER result. It means that using Block Puncturing, some bits would be relatively more fallible than others because of the deleted parity bits.
According to the simulation results, the scheme of Recovery Step puncturing perform close to that of Block Recovery Step puncturing, but the latter is much simpler than the former on complexity, and can provide higher top limitation than the former. Punctured from rate 0.5 codes, the Block Recovery Step puncturing can give out better performance at higher rates than the Block Puncturing scheme, but itвҖҷs limited under rate 0.857, which means it canвҖҷt meet the demand of Rate Compatible LDPC designing. Base on the fact, we propose two rate matching schemes for 802.16e B-LDPC with the objective of best performance.
1. Use LDPC code at rate 0.5 to get all the target rates with two different puncturing schemes. To generate codes at rates 0.6, 0.7 and 0.8, the Block Recovery Step puncturing is imposed. And the Block Puncturing scheme is used to get codes higher than the limit of the former.

2. Use two different codes at rate 0.5 and 0.75 to get all the target rates, in which lower than 0.75 would be punctured from code at 0.5, and the others from the one at 0.75. Since the Block Puncturing exhibits the same performance as the Block Recovery Step puncturing at low rates punctured from rate 0.5, and outperforms a little than the latter when punctured from rate 0.75, only one puncturing algorithm is needed here.
IV. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we described three puncturing scheme aiming at B-LDPC, the RS, BRS and BPS. They are compared by simulation, whose results shows the existence of puncturing scheme for rate matching B-LDPC codes with excellent performance over a broad range of coding rates. The outcome of simulation indicates a fact that for B-LDPC, puncturing algorithm based on blocks works better rather than that based on bits. Finally, according to the features of 802.16e itself, we bring forward a rate matching scheme with puncturing for LDPC codes in this standard.
