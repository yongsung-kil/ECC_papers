Decoder Design for RS-Based LDPC Codes
Jin Sha, Member, IEEE, Jun Lin, Zhongfeng Wang, Senior Member, IEEE, Li Li, and Minglun Gao

Abstract—This brief studies very large-scale integration (VLSI) decoder architectures for RS-based low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, which are a special class of LDPC codes based on Reed–Solomon codes. The considered code ensemble is well known for its excellent error-correcting performance and has been selected as the forward error correction coding scheme for 10GBase-T systems. By exploiting the shift-structured properties hidden in the algebraically generated parity-check matrices, novel decoder architectures are developed with significant advantages of high level of parallel decoding, efficient usage of memory, and low complexity of interconnection. To demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed techniques, we completed a high-speed decoder design for a (2048, 1723) regular RS-LDPC code, which achieves 10-Gb/s throughput with only 820 000 gates. Furthermore, to support all possible RS-LDPC codes, two special cases in code construction are considered, and the corresponding extensions of the decoder architecture are investigated.
Index Terms—Error correction codes, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, parallel processing, very large-scale integration.
I. INTRODUCTION
S INCE the rediscovery of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] in the late 1990s [2] as a class of near-Shannonlimit codes, a great deal of research effort has been spent in the construction of these codes [3], [4] and the design of associated decoders [5]–[9]. In [3], the authors constructed a class of LDPC codes based on Reed–Solomon codes and called them RS-based LDPC codes. These codes have good minimum distances and girth properties and perform very well with iterative decoding. One code constructed in this way has been chosen as the channel coding scheme in the IEEE 802.3an 10GBase-T Ethernet standard [10].
The realization of a high-speed LDPC decoder implementation has been a big challenge and is a crucial issue that determines how well we can exploit the unmatched merits of the LDPC codes in practical applications. To implement the decoder directly in its inherent parallel manner may give rise to routing congestions, and the complex interconnection may take up more than half of the chip area [5]. For partially parallel decoder architectures, the major challenge is to avoid memory

II. REVIEW OF RS-BASED LDPC CODES
A. Review of LDPC Codes and Decoding Algorithms
An (N, K) (dv, dc) LDPC code has a Tanner graph with N variable nodes and M check nodes in which all the variable nodes have degree dv and all the check nodes have degree dc. The typical LDPC decoding algorithm is the sum–product (or belief propagation) algorithm [2]. After variable nodes are initialized with the channel information, the decoding messages are iteratively computed by all the variable nodes and check nodes and exchanged through the edges between the neighboring nodes.
The modified min-sum decoding algorithm [12], [13] is similar to the sum–product algorithm, with an approximation of the check node process. It has some advantages in implementation over the sum–product algorithm, such as less computation complexity and no requirement of knowledge about the signalto-noise ratio for additive white Gaussian noise channels. In the modified min-sum decoding algorithm, the check node processing units (CNUs) compute the check-to-variable messages Rcv as follows:

V. CONCLUSION
As one special class of algebraically generated codes, RS-based LDPC codes are well appreciated for their excellent error-correcting performance. Novel decoder architectures dedicated for this ensemble of LDPC codes have been presented in this brief. We have shown that the geometrical properties hidden in the code construction can be explored to optimize the decoder design. A high-speed decoder implementation for the (2048, 1723) (6, 32) RS-LDPC code is reported which achieves 10-Gb/s throughput with 820 000 gates. In brief, the proposed decoder architecture will make RS-based LDPC codes more competent in future communication systems.
