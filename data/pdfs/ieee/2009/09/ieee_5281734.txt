A Differential Binary Message-Passing LDPC Decoder
Nastaran Mobini, Student Member, IEEE, Amir H. Banihashemi, Senior Member, IEEE,
and Saied Hemati, Senior Member, IEEE
Abstract‚ÄĒIn this paper, we propose a binary message-passing
algorithm for decoding low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes.
The algorithm substantially improves the performance of purely
hard-decision iterative algorithms with a small increase in the
memory requirements and the computational complexity. We
associate a reliability value to each nonzero element of the
code‚Äôs parity-check matrix, and differentially modify this value
in each iteration based on the sum of the extrinsic binary
messages from the check nodes. For the tested random and finite-
geometry LDPC codes, the proposed algorithm can perform as
close as about 1 dB and 0.5 dB to belief propagation (BP) at the
error rates of interest, respectively. This is while, unlike BP, the
algorithm does not require the estimation of channel signal to
noise ratio. Low memory and computational requirements and
binary message-passing make the proposed algorithm attractive
for high-speed low-power applications.
Index Terms‚ÄĒLow-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, iter-
ative decoding, binary message-passing decoding, hard-decision
decoding, soft-decision decoding, successive relaxation.
I. INTRODUCTION AND MOTIVATION
L
OW-density parity-check codes [1] perform very well
when decoded with iterative decoding algorithms also
known as message-passing algorithms [2]. An iterative de-
coding algorithm is identified by its message alphabet A, the
operations performed in the variable nodes and the check
nodes of the Tanner graph [3], and the maximum number of
iterations ūĚźľmax.
The message alphabet can be as simple as binary, e.g.,
ūĚźī= {+1, ‚ąí1}, considered in this work, or can consist of
infinite uncountable number of symbols such as the set of
real numbers. If ūĚźīis binary, the algorithm is referred to as
hard-decision; otherwise, it is called soft-decision.
A proper choice of an LDPC code and its decoding algo-
rithm depends on the application and is mainly driven by the
performance/complexity considerations. From the implemen-
tation point of view, hard-decision algorithms are particularly
attractive due to their low complexity, high speed and low
power and area consumption in hardware. Examples of hard-
decision algorithms are the so-called Gallager algorithms A

based (MB) algorithms [6]. The downside of hard-decision
algorithms, however, is their relatively poor performance that
can be as far away as a few decibels from that of soft-decision
algorithms. At the other end of the performance/complexity
spectrum are belief propagation (BP) [2], [3], and its vari-
ants [7], [8], [9], [10]. These algorithms have very good
performance but are highly complex. Approximations of BP,
most notably min-sum (MS) and its modifications [11], [12],
hybrid soft-hard algorithms [13], [14], weighted bit-flipping
algorithms and their variants [15], [13], [16] ,[17] are in
between the two extremes, each striking a different balance
between the performance and the complexity.
The main idea of this paper is to combine the simplicity of
binary message-passing with the good performance of soft-
decision algorithms. Binary message-passing is particularly
attractive for VLSI implementations as it translates to a smaller
number of wires between the variable nodes and the check
nodes. This in turn simplifies the routing of the wires and can
significantly reduce the area and the power consumption of the
decoder [18]. To achieve a good performance with the binary
message-passing, we introduce memory to the algorithm by
storing and updating soft information at the variable side of
the Tanner graph. The soft information is initiated from the
received values at the output of the channel and is then updated
differentially at each iteration based on the binary messages
of the check nodes. We refer to the proposed algorithm as
‚Äúdifferential decoding with binary message passing," or DD-
BMP, in short.
The introduction of memory in DD-BMP is motivated
by the impressive performance of successive relaxation (SR)
applied to soft-decision decoding algorithms [19], [20]. In
SR, at each iteration, the iterative decoding trajectory, defined
by messages passed along the edges of the Tanner graph, is
changed only differentially with respect to the last iteration‚Äôs
trajectory. The memory is thus required in SR to store the state
of the system in the last iteration as a reference for the change
in the current iteration. In fact, DD-BMP can be considered
as an SR-type algorithm tailored to binary message-passing.
Compared to purely hard-decision algorithms, the proposed
scheme provides improvements of up to about 3 dB and
1 dB at the bit error rates of about 10‚ąí5 for the tested
randomly constructed and finite-geometry (FG) codes, respec-
tively. These results are as close as about 1 dB and 0.5 dB to
the performance of the floating-point implementation of BP
for the two categories of codes, respectively.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we propose a binary message-passing iterative
algorithm for decoding LDPC codes. The algorithm incor-
porates soft information by storing the quantized received
values and differentially updating them in each iteration based
on the binary messages from the check nodes. While the
binary message-passing is particularly attractive for its low
complexity and simple implementation (translating to lower
area and power consumption in VLSI chips), the incorporation
of soft information provides more coding gain and improves
the performance. The results for a large number of tested codes
indicate that the proposed algorithm and its variants provide an
attractive solution in terms of performance/complexity tradeoff
for a wide range of rates for both random and FG LDPC
codes. This tradeoff is between those of the two extremes
of purely hard and purely soft decision algorithms. The
proposed algorithm is particularly attractive for FG codes,
where performance as close as about 0.5 dB to that of belief
propagation can be achieved at much lower complexity.
