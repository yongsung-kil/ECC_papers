Soft Decision LSI Operating at 32 Gsample/s for LDPC FEC-based Optical Transmission Systems
T. Kobayashi, S. Kametani, K. Shimizu, K. Onohara, H. Tagami and T. Mizuochi
Information Technology R&D Center, Mitsubishi Electric Corporation, 5-1-1, Ofuna, Kamakura, 247-8501, Japan Kobayashi.Tatsuya@ah.MitsubishiElectric.co.jp
Abstract: We have developed a 2-bit 32 Gsample/s soft decision LSI for low-density parity-check code FEC at 100 Gb/s in 0.13 μm SiGe-BiCMOS which generates confidence bit with 25 mVpp sensitivity. ©2008 Optical Society of America
OCIS codes: (060.4510) Optical communications
1. Introduction Very high-speed optical transport systems, e.g. OTU4V, need increasingly powerful but practical forward error correction (FEC) to enhance the transmission range. Several types of powerful FEC have been developed to date for 10 Gb/s ~ 40 Gb/s systems. For example, the dual orthogonally interleaved BCH coding in ITU-T G.975.1 has a 6.9 dB Q limit at 10 Gb/s with 25% redundancy. OTN compatible enhanced FEC LSI has been developed with a 9.1 dB Q limit at 43 Gb/s [1].
Soft decision decoding is a promising way to improve error correction capability. Most current FEC LSIs for optical communications use using hard decision decoding. The difference in error correction performance between hard and soft decision decoding with infinite quantization bits and near-infinite redundancy is π/2 [2]: about 2 dB. With 25% redundancy, the difference is about 1.5 dB. Soft decision is performed by setting 2M-1 decision thresholds, M being the number of quantization bits. With M = 3, the two possible received signals, “one” and “zero”, lie in regions represented by a binary vector ranging from [011] to [111]. The left-most bit is the hard decision digit, and the other two bits indicate the probability of “one” or “zero”.
Puc et al. were the first to demonstrate soft decision by concatenating a Reed-Solomon code with a Viterbi convolutional code, for a net coding gain (NCG) of 10.3 dB at 2.5 Gb/s [3], but the redundancy was 113%. We have developed 3-bit soft decision and Block Turbo code LSIs [4]. The soft decision LSI in 0.18 μm SiGe BiCMOS with 120 GHz fT is a flush type 3-bit analogue-to-digital converter (ADC) operating at 12.4 Gb/s [5]. Combining these two LSIs yields a record Q-limit of 6.3 dB with 24% redundancy, but still at 12.4 Gb/s due to the difficulty in speeding up the soft decision LSI, especially the high sensitivity ADC circuitry. Only a few fast ADCs have been reported to date. Cheng et al. demonstrated a 3-bit 40 Gsample/s flush type ADC in 0.12 μm SiGe BiCMOS [6]. Schvan et al. demonstrated a 5-bit 22 Gsample/s flush type ADC in 0.13 μm SiGe BiCMOS [7], and 24 Gsample/s 6-bit ADC in 90 nm CMOS using a time interleaved architecture [8]. Adamczyk et al. demonstrated a 5-bit 12.5 Gsample/s flush type ADC in 0.25 μm SiGe BiCMOS [9]. However, no soft decision LSI with an ADC operating at over 12.4 Gsample/s has been reported to date. This is because the soft decision LSI needs not only a fast ADC but also the follow-up functions, i.e. a fast interface, a precision phase-locked loop, an M-bit encoder to create the confidence bits, and a fast deserializer.
We report here a newly developed 2-bit soft decision LSI operating at 32 Gsample/s. This is currently the fastest soft decision LSI to the best of our knowledge. By combining the four LSIs with the low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes we are also developing [10], there is the potential to realize an NCG of 9.4 dB at 126.4 Gb/s for optical transmission systems.
2. Circuit Design and Implementation To build a high-speed soft decision LSI, we took the following approach: (1) to use M = 2 quantization bits for good FEC gain with practical circuit size; (2) to operate at half-rate to relax the bandwidth requirement; and (3) to use 0.13 μm SiGe BiCMOS technology with a transit frequency fT of 200 GHz.
Fig.1 (a) shows that the 2-bit soft decision LSI has the following five circuits: (1) a fan-out buffer to distribute the input equally to eight outputs; (2) two sets of three deciders with different threshold levels; (3) 2-bit encoders to generate confidence bit from the outputs of the three deciders; (4) two 2:16 deserializers; and (5) phase-locked loop clock recovery. The incoming 32 Gsymbol/s signal is distributed by the cascaded-amplifier fan-out buffer, then decided by three deciders with different threshold voltages Vth(+1), Vth(0) and Vth(-1). Vth(0) is a central hard decision threshold which is automatically tracked for minimum bit errors by a feed-back loop. The upper and lower deciders provide confidence as to whether the hard-decision outputs are “0” or “1”. Their thresholds Vth(+1) and Vth(-1) are respectively placed in the lower tail of the distribution of the “ones” and the upper tail of the “zeroes”, as shown in Fig. 1(b). Since we operate at half rate, the two sets of deciders are placed in parallel. The three outputs from the deciders are encoded as a 2-bit binary vector ranging from [0,1] to [1,1], the left-most bit being a harddecision digit and the other, confidence digit indicating the probability of “zero” or “one”: “1” for high confidence

Fig. 3 is a photograph of the 9.7 mm x 6.9 mm chip, which consumes 14 W at +3.3 V. Since the chip needs about 570 I/O pads including ground and power supply, flip-chip bonding was used to mount the chip on the 30 mm x 29 mm x 2.15 mm custom package, which was made from a low temperature co-fired ceramic for its high frequency characteristics. The 32 Gsymbol/s data and the 16 GHz clock were input to the chip through transmission lines on the package. Although monitoring was via the transmission lines on the package, the parallel data was output through a ball grid array (BGA).

3. Evaluation of the soft decision LSI
Fig. 4 shows the LSI on its evaluation board. A 32 Gsymbol/s electrical NRZ test signal was input via GPPO connectors connected to surface feed-throughs on the ceramic package by wire bonds. The pattern length was 231-1
and the input amplitude ranged from 0 to 200 mVpp. A 16 GHz test clock was also input to the LSI with a fixed amplitude of 200 mVpp. The decision thresholds at the three Vth pins were from precisely set DC supplies. The demultiplexed 2 Gsymbol/s signals were measured with a BERTS.
The decision sensitivity is the most important parameter of the soft decision LSI. Even with the 32 Gsymbol/s
input signal with an eye opening of only 25 mVpp shown in Fig. 5 (a), the clear monitor output prescaled at 16 Gsymbol/s shown in Fig.5 (b) was obtained. 25 mVpp sensitivity is considered to be adequate when using LDPC codes. The soft decision operation was evaluated by changing the decision thresholds. Correct 2-bit output of the
hard decision and confidence bits was obtained at the demultiplexed output. We have also measured long-term BER

4. Discussion Fig. 6 shows a typical application of this LSI (conceptual drawing). An OTU4V mapped 100 GbE signal at 126.4 Gb/s is encoded for FEC and mapped onto a 31.6 Gsymbol/s (4 bits per symbol) polarization multiplexed DQPSK signal. The received signal is polarization demultiplexed and differentially decoded by a 1-bit delay interferometer. The regenerated 31.6 Gsymbol/s signal is input to the soft decision LSI. For such a high-speed signal, a simple but powerful FEC algorithm such as an LDPC concatenated with RS [10] is required. A Q-limit of 7.1 dB can be expected with only 20.5% redundancy, with no BER error floor thanks to the concatenated codes. The cyclic approximated δ-minimum algorithm drastically decreases the circuit complexity. Increasing the soft decision bits makes the error performance approach the ideal, but with increased circuit complexity. It is expected that 2-bit soft decision gives a performance degradation of only 0.4 dB [11].

5. Conclusion We have successfully developed a 2-bit 32 Gsample/s soft decision LSI for very high-speed optical transmission systems employing LDPC FEC. The LSI exhibits 25 mVpp sensitivity, which is adequate for 2-bit soft decision decoding. When combined with an LDPC FEC algorithm currently under development, the LSI has the potential to achieve a Q-limit of 7.1 dB with only 20.5% redundancy.
