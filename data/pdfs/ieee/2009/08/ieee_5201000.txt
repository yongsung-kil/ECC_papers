New Insights into Weighted Bit-Flipping Decoding

Xiaofu Wu, Cong Ling, Ming Jiang, Enyang Xu, Chunming Zhao, and Xiaohu You

Abstract—A natural relationship between weighted bit-flipping (WBF) decoding and belief-propagation-like (BP-like) decoding is explored. This understanding can help us develop WBF algorithms from BP-like algorithms. For min-sum decoding, one can find that its WBF algorithm is the algorithm proposed by Jiang et al. For BP decoding, we propose a new WBF algorithm and show its performance advantage. The proposed WBF algorithms are parallelized to achieve rapid convergence. Two efficient simulation-based procedures are proposed for the optimization of the associated thresholds.
Index Terms—Weighted bit-flipping decoding, beliefpropagation decoding, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes.

I. INTRODUCTION
I TERATIVE decoding of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes has received much attention in recent years. The standard belief-propagation (BP) decoding can achieve excellent performance but often with heavy implementation complexity. Bit-flipping (BF) decoding is simple, but it often incurs considerable performance loss compared to BP decoding. To bridge the performance gap between BF and BP decodings, weighted BF (WBF) decoding [1] and its variants [2]–[9] were proposed. Performance/complexity tradeoff was also achieved for many hybrid message-passing algorithms such as those in

IMWBF algorithm proposed in [5]. From belief-propagation (BP) decoding, one can derive a new WBF algorithm and simulations show its performance advantage over the IMWBF algorithm. Secondly, parallel versions of the WBF algorithms are proposed, and the associated thresholds are determined.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. The relationship between WBF and BP-like decodings is explored in Section II. Section III discuss the parallel versions of various WBF algorithms and the associated thresholds determination. Simulations results are reported in Section IV. Section V concludes the paper.

Let C be a regular (γ; ρ) LDPC code of block length N
and dimension K, which has a parity-check matrix H of M
rows, and N columns with exactly ρ “ones" in each row and γ “ones" in each column. Let Rc = K/N denote its code rate.
Consider now that the LDPC coded bits are BPSK modu-
lated, and further transmitted over an additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel. Let c = (c1, c2, · · · , cN ) denote a codeword of C. It is mapped to x = (x1, x2, · · · , xN ) by xn = 2cn − 1 before transmission. At the receiver, we get the received vector y = (y1, y2, · · · , yN ), where yn = xn + vn, n = 1, 2, · · · , N , and vn is the additive white Gaussian noise with zero mean and variance σ2 = (2RcEb/N0)−1. Let z = (z1, z2, · · · , zN ) be the binary hard-decision vector obtained from y, i.e., zn = 1 if yn ≥ 0 and zn = 0 otherwise.
We denote the set of bits that participate check m by N (m) = {n, hm,n = 1}. Similarly, We denote the set of checks in which bit n participates as M(n) = {m, hm,n = 1}.

D. Complexity Issue
As far as the computational complexity is concerned, the BP-based WBF algorithms appear less attractive than the minsum-based WBF algorithms.
In what follows, we focus on NBP-WBF and IMWBF algorithms due to their performance advantage. It is soon recognized that two WBF algorithms differ mainly in the preprocessing stage. Other than the preprocessing stage, the computation complexity per iteration for updating is just the same [14]. Hence, we focus on the preprocessing stage. For the NBP-WBF algoritm, it requires N real multiplications to obtain the log-likelihood ratio of the received signals with the help of a signal-to-noise ratio estimator. This, however, is not required for the IMWBF algorithm. To compute wn,m, the NBP-WBF algorithm requires real additions, along with the look-up tables for both Φ(·) and Φ−1(·). Recently, we have proposed a parallel WBF (PWBF) decoding algorithm in [14]. For conciseness, we refer readers to [14] for details. Since various WBF algorithms differ in the computation of the flipping function En, it is clear that the proposed parallel implementation framework can be adapted to all existing WBF algorithms. However, it remains open for how to determine the associated thresholds.
In what follows, k denotes the iteration counter. Imax denotes the maximum number of iterations. Fth(k) denotes the threshold at kth iteration to be optimized. Essentially, we have to determine the threshold vector Fth = (Fth[1], Fth[2], · · · , Fth[Imax]). δF denotes a vector of length Imax, which is initialized to [0, 0, · · · , 0, 1] and used to adjust Fth. δF << 1 denotes a left cyclic shift of δF by 1.

For most LDPC codes such as MN-LDPC and FG-LDPC codes, extensive simulations show that the IMWBF outperforms the LP-WBF[5]. However, for some LDPC codes, we show here that the LP-WBF can outperforms the IMWBF. For high-rate QC-LDPC codes constructed based on finite fields[13], our simulations show that the LP-WBF performs well and it outperforms the IMWBF in high SNRs.

V. CONCLUSION
We have explored a natural relationship between BP-like decoding and weighted bit-flipping decoding. This can help us develop a WBF algorithm from one type of BP-like decoding algorithm and vice versa. For the recently-proposed parallel implementation framework for WBF decoding, we propose two practical simulation-based approaches for determining the associated thresholds.
