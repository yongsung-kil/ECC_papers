Abstract—Low density generator matrix to construct lowdensity parity check (LDPC) codes is designed in this paper. The proposed structure significantly reduces the encoding complexity of LDPC codes. Codes in this class can be easily constructed by concatenating the parity-check matrix of array low-density parity-check (ALDPC) codes with the identity matrix and also contains no short cycle of length four. The results shown in this paper indicate that these codes can perform very well at high code rate (R ≥ 0.85) under iterative decoding with no serial or parallel concatenation of two codes.
Index Terms—Low density parity check (LDPC) codes, Low Density Generator Matrix (LDGM) codes, high rate, irregular, systematic, low complexity encoding
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are a class of linear block codes first introduced by Gallager [1] in his 1960 doctoral dissertation and rediscovered by MacKay and Neal [2]. These codes have been adopted in a wide range of applications [3]. The name of these codes comes from the characteristic of their parity-check matrix which contains only a few non-zero elements in comparison to the number of zero elements. Their main advantage is that they provide a performance which is very close to the theoretical limit under iterative decoding for a lot of different channels.
The main disadvantage of LDPC codes is high-complexity encoding. Many solutions that can solve this encoding problem have been introduced. T.Richardson and R.Urbanke [4] presented the techniques that can be reduce the encoding problem. They stated that LDPC codes can be encoded using the paritycheck matrix directly by transforming the parity-check matrix into upper triangular form and using back substitution to find its corresponding parity bits.
Structured LDPC codes can be used to significantly reduce the encoding problem, and some of the prominent structured LDPC codes are introduced here. A. Fan proposed a class of algebraically constructed array codes that are called array LDPC (ALDPC) codes [5] and which can be decoded with iterative decoding. Subsequent to Fan’s work, E.Eleftheriou and S.O¨ lc¸er [6] proposed a modified array LDPC (MAC) codes that can be easily encoded because their parity-check matrix is in upper triangular form.
J.Garcia-Frias and W.Zhong [7] investigated the use of systematic linear codes with low-density generator matrices (LDGM). Because of the sparseness of the generator matrix, the amount of processing required in the encoder is less than for standard LDPC codes. Although LDGM codes have these

advantages, the performance of LDGM codes is generally bad because of their minimum free distance. This problem is solved by concatenating two LDGM codes. Concatenated LDGM codes under iterative decoding were shown to achieve near theoretical limit performance.
Although low-rate LDPC codes have performance very close to the theoretical limit, in some applications such as digital subscriber line or magnetic storage, high-rate LDPC codes must be considered. In this paper, a class of high-rate LDPC codes with low-density generator matrix which guarantees low complexity encoding are introduced. The construction of parity-check matrices is based on ALDPC codes. The density of parity-check and generator matrix of the proposed codes are less than ALDPC codes and MAC codes but the performance at high rate are still very good.
The remainder of the paper is outlined as follows. Section II provides the necessary background and notation for the subsequent sections. Section III presents the code-design method. Section IV, the complexity of the proposed codes are discussed and the simulation results are given in section V. Finally, some conclusions are given in Section VI.

II. BACKGROUND
In this section, the principle of LDPC codes is brieftly introduced. As mentioned above, LDPC codes are a class of linear block codes so LDPC codes can be described in the same way as classical linear block codes. Dimension and length of LDPC codes are denoted by k and n respectively and the code rate of LDPC codes are denoted by R = k/n. The message and codeword are conventionally denoted by m = [m1, m2, . . . , mk] and c = [c1, c2, . . . , cn] respectively. The parities are denoted by u = [u1, u2, . . . , un−k], where the number of elements is equal to the number of parity bits. V. SIMULATION RESULTS
In this section, we will show the simulation results of the proposed codes with different block lengths and rates that were constructed by the instructions in the section III. The bit error rate and signal to noise ratio are denoted by BER and SNR respectively. Let (n, k) represent the block length and dimension of the code respectively. The sum-product algorithm (SPA) is employed as LDPC decoder with maximum iteration 20. These results are performed on a binary phase-shift keying (BPSK) modulated AWGN channel.
Fig. 1 shows the performance of the proposed code at length 529 and R ≈ 0.87 compared with the MAC code. Parameters which are used to construct HS are p = 23, q = 20 and j = 3. Parameters which are used to construct HM are p = 23, q = 23 and j = 3. The figure shows that the performance of the MAC code is approximately 0.25 dB better than the proposed code at SNR greater than 5.85 dB. The figure also shows that the performance of (529,462) ALDPC code which is very identical to (529,460) MAC codes. Parameters which are used to construct HA are p = 23, q = 23 and j = 3. The ALDPC codes can not be constructed at the same rate as the MAC codes because the ALDPC codes have dimension q2 − qj + j − 1.
Fig. 2 shows the performance of the proposed code at length 2209 and R ≈ 0.91 compared with the MAC code. Parameters which are used to construct HS are p = 47, q = 43 and j = 4. Parameters which are used to construct HM are p = 47, q = 47 and j = 4. The results show that the performance of MAC code are approximately 0.15 dB better than the proposed code.
