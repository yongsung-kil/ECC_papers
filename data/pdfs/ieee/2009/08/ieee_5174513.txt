Geometrically-Structured Maximum-Girth LDPC Block and Convolutional Codes

Morteza Esmaeili, Member, IEEE, and Mohammad Gholami

Abstract—Four classes of maximum-girth geometrically structured column-weight-two regular quasi-cyclic (QC) low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are introduced.
Two classes of these codes, referred to as Type-I and Type-II codes, are with row-weights 4 and 3, and maximum girths 16 and 24, respectively. The idea behind the construction of these two classes of codes, with rates at least 1/2 and 1/3, is slightly generalized to obtain two classes of variable-high-rate codes, referred to as Type-III1 and Type-III2 codes, with maximum girth 20 and 16, respectively. A low-complexity deterministic algorithm for constructing these four classes of codes is given. The algorithm generates maximum-girth Type-I and Type-II codes with almost arbitrary length n not less than 216 and 243, respectively.
The output of the algorithm substantially improves on some of the previously best known codes constructed using a randomized progressive edge-growth (RPEG) algorithm. For instance, we have rate-0.71 Type-III1 codes of lengths 308 and 728 with girths 10 and 12, respectively, versus the code lengths 385 and 840 obtained by the RPEG algorithm.
Simulation results on AWGN channel confirm that, from BER performance perspective, the constructed LDPC codes are superior to the column-weight-two LDPC codes constructed by the previously reported methods.
The generator matrix G(D) of the convolutional codes associated with Type-I and Type-II codes is given. The free distance dfree of such a convolutional code is equal to the minimum distance of the corresponding QC block code.
Index Terms—LDPC codes, LDPC convolutional codes, QC codes, circulant matrices, slope, maximum girth.
I. INTRODUCTION
L OW-DENSITY parity-check (LDPC) codes [1]-[2] are considered as a class of codes with near-optimum performance [3]-[6]. In terms of construction methods, these codes are divided into random-like codes, such as [4],[6], and structured codes, such as those given in [3],[7]-[18]. It has been reported that, unlike the long length codes, for medium length LDPC codes the structured codes can outperform the pseudorandom ones [19]. In the construction of structured LDPC codes there has been considerable interest on the design of quasi-cyclic (QC) codes, such as [3],[9],[10],[20]-[24], among others, as these codes can be encoded with simple shift registers [25]-[29].
Gallager [1] has shown that the minimum distance of column-weight-two LDPC codes increases logarithmically with code length, while this increment is linear for the codes with column-weight j ≥ 3. In spite of this weakness, when compared with LDPC codes with column-weight j ≥ 3, LDPC

codes with column-weight-two have their own advantages: their encoding and decoding operations have lower computation and storage complexity, and they have better block error statistics [30]-[32] when applied on partial response channels.
In this paper we are concerned with a geometric approach to the construction of four classes of regular (n, 2, k ≥ 3) QC LDPC codes using the slope concept introduced in [33]. Applying this method, H. Zhang and J.M.F. Moura [33]-[34] tried to construct codes having Tanner graphs [35] with large girths. They constructed (n, 2, 4) and (n, 2, 3) LDPC codes (referred to in this paper as Type-I and Type-II codes) with girths 16 and 20, respectively.
We show that the maximum girth achieved by Type-I and Type-II codes is 16 and 24, respectively. By analyzing the cycle structure of Tanner graph of these two types of codes, we provide a low-complexity algorithm for the construction of these two types of maximum-girth codes. According to the output of the algorithm, it appears that such maximum-girth (n, 2, 4) (resp. (n, 2, 3)) codes exist for almost all lengths not less than 216 (resp. 243). The rate of a Type-I (resp. Type-II) code is at least 1/2 (resp. 1/3).
To obtain slope-based column-weight-two QC LDPC codes with better rates, we provide a generalization of the method constructing Type-I and Type-II codes that generates two types of variable-high-rate codes, referred to as Type-III1 and TypeIII2 codes. The maximum girth achieved by the proposed Type-III1 and Type-III2 codes is 20 and 16, respectively. From performance on AWGN channel perspective, these codes outperform their random-like and algebraic [16] counterparts.
Cage graphs [36][37][38] that have column weight two have the maximum girth permissible with the least number of vertices possible. Though in some cases such codes have fewer number of variable nodes than the codes proposed in this paper but this is not always the case (see Table IV). We have a girth-20 length-108 row-weight-three Type-III1 code while the smallest code-length reported in [37] for girth 20 and row-weight 3 is 201.
Though a column-weight-two binary LDPC code may have poor performance, this is not the case with the non-binary codes. The non-binary column-weight-two LDPC codes are among the most promising non-binary codes and for q = 64, 128, 256, the best q-ary LDPC codes decoded with BP algorithm are the column-weight-two codes [39]-[40]. III. CYCLE STRUCTURE OF TYPE-I AND TYPE-II CODES A. Type-III cycles in Type-I codes

In this section we characterize a class of cycles, to be called Type-III cycles, in Type-I and Type-II CSGs. The importance of Type-III cycles lies on their crucial role on the girth of these CSGs. Thus, results of this section provide the ground for constructing codes with maximum girths 16 and 24. Theorems 2 and 3 are the main results of this section.
Let Si = S(Xi−1, Xi, s1) ∪ S(Xi−1, Xi, s2), 0 ≤ s1 < s2, be a 2-admissible section of a given Type-I or Type-II CSG G and Pi be a walk of odd length 2m + 1 in Si started from a vertex in Xi−1 and terminated at a vertex in Xi. We say Pi is a Type-I (Type-II) walk if it starts with an edge from S(Xi−1, Xi, s1) (resp. S(Xi−1, Xi, s2)) and that from any two consecutive edges of Pi one edge is in S(Xi−1, Xi, s1) and the other one in S(Xi−1, Xi, s2). A Type-I (Type-II) path in Si is a Type-I (Type-II) walk with distinct vertices.
Definition 2 (belt-like cycle): Let G be a CSG of length l and C a cycle in G. We say that C is a belt-like cycle if it does not divide the surface of the CSG into inside and outside regions. In other words, considering C as a directed cycle, C is a non-belt cycle if for each 1 ≤ i ≤ l the edges of C ∩ Si is partitioned into two equally sized sets with the property that under the direction of C all edges in one set are from Xi−1 to Xi and those in the other set are from Xi to Xi−1. Topologically, a non-belt cycle creates a closed region, known as the interior region of C, on the surface of the cylinder. A belt-like cycle C is called simple (non-simple) if it circles the cylinder only once (resp. at least twice).
Figures 2a and 2b represent a simple and a non-simple beltlike cycle, respectively, on a cylinder of length l = 3. The non-simple cycle in Figure 2b has circled the cylinder twice. From among the class of non-simple belt-like cycles we are concerned only with the cycles shown in Figure 2b, that is the belt-like cycles circling a cylinder of length 3 exactly twice. In the rest of the paper, unless otherwise stated, by a belt-like cycle we mean a simple belt-like cycle.

VI. CONVOLUTIONAL CODES
There is a close relationship between QC codes and convolutional codes [49], [50], [51]. Following the approach used in [16], in this section we construct LDPC convolutional codes associated with the Type-I and Type-II codes. For simplicity, however, we just consider the Type-I codes. In the context of convolutional codes a circulant block matrix Is, s ≥ 0, in the parity-check matrix of a CSG G is denoted by Ds if s > 0 and by 1 when s = 0, where D stands for the delay operation. Thus the matrix HI given by (1) changes to the matrix HI (D) given in (7).
HI (D) is the polynomial form parity-check matrix of the corresponding LDPC convolutional code. With this notation, the parity-check matrix of the QC Type-I and Type-II codes are over the ring F2[D]/(DN + 1) while HI (D) is over the rational field F2(D).
In this part we first determine the polynomial form generator matrix of Type-I LDPC convolutional codes associated with the Type-I CSGs. It is shown that the free distance dfree of a so constructed LDPC convolutional code is equal to the girth of the corresponding CSG if the CSG has maximum girth.

An efficient deterministic algorithm constructing these codes was given. As part of the algorithm output, a set of codes with rates between 0.33 and 0.83 was given. The output of the algorithm has improved some of the code-parameters obtained before by a randomized progressive edge-growth method. The introduced high-girth variable-rate column-weight-two LDPC codes together with the given efficient construction method have a good contribution to the construction of non-binary column-weight-two LDPC codes that are known as a class of good codes.
As, ultimately, higher column-weight (column-weight 3 or more) codes will form the candidates for many practical applications due to their superior distance properties, an extension of the method given in this paper has been applied to design codes with girth 18 and column-weight 3[52].
