Comments on Successive Relaxation for Decoding of LDPC Codes
Hua Xiao, Student Member, IEEE, and Amir H. Banihashemi, Senior Member, IEEE
Abstract—The application of successive relaxation (SR) to the
fixed-point problem associated with the iterative decoding of low-
density parity-check (LDPC) codes was proposed by Hemati et
al.. The simulation results presented by Hemati et al. for the SR
version of belief propagation (BP) in the likelihood ratio (LR)
domain and that of min-sum (MS) in the log-likelihood ratio
(LLR) domain are based on the assumption of all-zero codeword
transmission. This assumption however results in erroneous error
rates when SR is applied in the LR domain. Here, we correct the
simulation results reported by Hemati et al. for SR-BP in the LR
domain. Furthermore, we investigate the performance of SR-BP
and SR-MS in the LLR and LR domains, respectively. The results
for a binary input additive white Gaussian noise (BIAWGN)
channel show that for both BP and MS, the application of SR
in the two domains of LR and LLR results in different error
correcting performance. In particular, for the tested codes, it
is shown that among the four algorithms, SR-MS-LLR has the
best performance. It outperforms standard MS and BP by up
to about 0.6 dB and 0.3dB, respectively, offering an attractive
solution in terms of performance/complexity tradeoff.
Index Terms—Successive relaxation, low-density parity-check
(LDPC) codes, belief propagation (BP) algorithm, min-sum
(MS) algorithm, iterative decoding, message-passing algorithms,
successive substitution.
I. INTRODUCTION
I
NSPIRED by the dynamics of analog decoders, Hemati et
al. [2] proposed the application of the successive relaxation
(SR) method to the fixed-point problem of iterative decoding
of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1]. Based on the
results of [2], it is known that for LDPC codes, SR outper-
forms the widely popular method of successive substitution.
In [2], SR was applied to belief propagation (BP) in the
likelihood ratio (LR) domain and to min-sum (MS) in the
log-likelihood ratio (LLR) domain. The simulation results
presented in [2] are based on the transmission of the all-
zero codeword. Since SR-LR algorithms are not symmetric
(symmetry is defined in [5]), the simulation results presented
in [2] for SR-BP in the LR domain (SR-BP-LR) are incorrect.
Consequently, the result of [2] that SR-BP-LR is superior in
performance to SR-MS-LLR is false.
Here, we correct the simulation results of [2] for SR-BP-LR.
Furthermore, for the same codes, we report simulation results
for SR-BP-LLR and SR-MS-LR. The comparison among the
four algorithms leaves us with the conclusion that among the
four SR algorithms, SR-MS-LLR has the best performance.

same. SR-MS-LR performs the worst, still better or the same
as SS-BP. The fact that SR-MS-LLR has the best performance
and the simplest variable and check node operations among
the four SR algorithms, and that it also performs considerably
better than SS-MS and SS-BP, make SR-MS-LLR a prime
candidate for the iterative decoding of LDPC codes.
IV. CONCLUSIONS
The application of successive relaxation (SR) to belief prop-
agation (BP) and min-sum (MS) algorithms in LR and LLR
domains is studied. Our results for the tested codes show that
among the four variants of SR, SR-MS-LLR performs the best
and has the lowest complexity. This algorithm also provides
substantial improvement over standard BP and MS algorithms
