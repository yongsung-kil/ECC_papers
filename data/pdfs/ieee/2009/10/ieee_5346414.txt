Design of Irregular Quasi-Cyclic LDPC Codes Based on Euclidean
Geometries
Xueqin JIANG†, Moon Ho LEE†
† Department of Electronics and Information Engineering, Chonbuk National University, Korea E-mail: †{jiangxueqin,moonho}@chonbuk.com

Abstract This paper presents an approach to the construction of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes based on hyperplanes (μ-flats) of different dimensions in Euclidean geometries. Codes constructed by this method have quasicyclic and irregular structure. The degree distributions of these codes are optimized by the curve fitting approach in the extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) charts. By constraining the fraction of degree-2 nodes, we can lower the error floor at the cost of a small increase in the threshold SNR. Simulation results show that these codes perform very well at both of waterfall region and the error floor region with the iterative decoding. key words: Irregular QC LDPC codes, EXIT chart, Euclidean geometry, μ-flats, parallel bundles, Galois fields.
1. Introduction
Richardson et al. [1] showed that irregular Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes with carefully chosen node degree distributions perform very close to the Shannon limit on the erasure channel. Empirically, the same is true for other channels. Methods for the construction of regular QC LDPC codes based on Euclidean geometries have been proposed in [2]. By column(row) splitting, these codes can be extended into irregular LDPC codes with at most two different column(row) weights and regular distribution. However, the column(row)splitting may result in an extended code which is not quasi-cyclic. In this paper, we propose an approach to the construction of irregular QC LDPC codes with flexible column(row) weight distribution based on hyperplanes (μ-flats) of different dimensions in Euclidean geometries. The construction approach consists of two steps. First, construct a regular base matrix based on the μ-flats in Euclidean geometries and obtain an irregular base matrix B¯ by column(row) decomposition. Secondly, replace each ‘0’ of B¯ by a ‘−1’ and replace each ‘1’ in the mth row and nth column of B¯ with an integer a(m,n) in the range [0, q-1] and obtain a shift matrix S. Each a(m,n) is replaced with a q × q circulant permutation matrix which cyclically shifts the identity matrix to the right by a(m,n) positions and each ‘−1’ is replaced with a q × q zero matrix. Then, a QC parity check matrix denoted by H is obtained. We employ the EXIT chart technique to find an optimal degree distribution for our irregular QC LDPC codes.
This paper is organized as follows. The Design of the base matrix is introduced in Section 2. QC LDPC codes based on the base matrices are constructed in Section 3. Section 4 introduces an approach to find an optimization degree distribution for our codes. An example of our irregular LDPC code and its simulation result are given in Section 5. Section 6 concludes the paper.

3. Construction of The QC LDPC Codes

For a code to perform well with iterative decoding, its Tanner

graph must not contain too many short cycles. According to

Theorem 2.1, the girth of the base matrix B is 4 or 6. The

row(column) decomposition in base matrix B may not break

all the short cycles in B. We can break these short cycles in the base matrix B¯ by replacing ‘1’s by circulant permutation

matrices with appropriate shift values and replacing ‘0’s by

zero matrices [4]. Each cycle of length 2i in the base matrix B¯ corre-

sponds to a block-cycle of length 2i in H. It is stated in

[4] that in a QC LDPC code, the necessary and sufficient

condition for the existence of the block-cycle of length 2i in

H is

The paper [6] show that to approach channel capacity, one must match the VND and CND transfer curves. This approach is called curve fitting approach. We first plot the CND curve (20) as a constant curve and then we adjust (av,1, av,2, . . . av,D) to finding a VND curve (19) that lies above the CND curve.

Consequently, the irregular QC LDPC codes optimization include three steps as: (1) Find the optimal degree distribution pair of the Tanner graph of B¯ by using the curve fitting approach in the EXIT chart of B¯. (2) Reduce the fraction λ2 if the fraction of degree-2 variable nodes is higher than the optimal value [8]. (3) Prevent cycles of short length in the Tanner graph of H by carefully choose entries of the shift matrix S.

6. Conclusion
In this paper, an approach to the construction of irregular QC LDPC based on Euclidean geometries has been presented. Firstly, we design the regular base matrix B based on μ-flats of different dimensions in Euclidean geometries. Using the column decomposition and the row decomposition, we get the irregular base matrix B¯. Secondly, we optimize the entries of the shift matrix S to break the short cycles in the base matrix B¯ and construct the QC matrix H by replacing each entry of S with a circulant permutation matrix or a zero
