A Low-Complexity Rate-Compatible LDPC
Decoder
Kai Zhang and Xinming Huang Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering
Worcester Polytechnic Institute Worcester, MA 01609, USA {kzhang, xhuang}@wpi.edu

Abstract—In this paper, we present a rate-compatible LDPC decoder architecture which supports code rates between the rate of the mother code and 1. The rate-1/2 2304-bit Quasi-Cyclic (QC) LDPC codes with dual-diagonal parity check structure is selected from WiMax standard as the mother code and is punctured using speciſc puncturing patterns to obtain arbitrary rates. Parallel layered decoding architecture (PLDA) is employed to reduce chip area and improve the throughput. Simulation results show that the proposed punctured codes have comparable error correcting performances with the performances of dedicated codes from WiMax standard. The decoder is implemented on the platform of Xilinx XC2V8000. Compared with previous WiMax LDPC decoders synthesized by the same device, our lowcomplexity decoder consumes less FPGA resources: reduction of slices by 63%, ƀip-ƀops by 73%, look-up tables (LUTs) by 60% and RAMs by 30%.
Index Terms—VLSI design, LDPC codes, rate compatibility, layered decoding, puncturing scheme
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, discovered by Gallager [1], have been investigated extensively in recent years since they were rediscovered by [2] because of their powerful error performance and inherent parallelism for hardware implementation. Recently, there is a growing interest in ratecompatible (RC) LDPC codes and their applications. On timevarying channels, it is desirable to adjust the FEC code rate according to the channel state information (CSI). The concept of RC LDPC codes was ſrst proposed in [3] by puncturing the parity bits of a low-rate randomly constructed LDPC code (called the mother code). The punctured codes showed good error performance, but the complicated puncturing algorithm and large block size were not practical for hardware implementation. Later, ſnite-length puncturing patterns were proposed in [4] by introducing the deſnition of k-step recovery (kSR) variable node. Based on the k-SR theory, several studies were presented on the puncturing schemes for quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes with dual-diagonal parity structure [5], [6]. Nevertheless, the hardware design of RC LDPC decoder has not yet been well studied.
In this paper, we investigate the puncturing schemes for RC LDPC codes and their hardware implementations. For QC LDPC codes with dual-diagonal parity structure, an efſcient puncturing scheme is selected which includes the weight3 vertical submatrix in the puncturing block. As a case study using the rate-1/2 WiMax LDPC code, we show that

the selected puncturing scheme results in the bit-error-rate (BER) performance degradation of less than 0.2dB compared with dedicated WiMax codes at four different code rates. Subsequently, we present a low-complexity LDPC decoder using parallel layered decoding architecture (PLDA) for the aforementioned puncturing scheme [7]. The decoder is implemented on the platform of Xilinx XC2V8000. Compared with previous WiMax LDPC decoders synthesized by the same device, our low-complexity decoder consumes less FPGA resources: reduction of slices by 63%, ƀip-ƀops by 73%, lookup tables (LUTs) by 60% and RAMs by 30%. It supports any arbitrary rate between rate of the mother code and 1.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. Section II introduces the background of RC LDPC codes and a comparison of different puncturing schemes. The high-throughput RC LDPC decoder architecture is presented in Section III. Section IV gives the implementation results by comparing with some existing WiMax LDPC decoders. Conclusions and future work are discussed in Section VI.
II. PUNCTURING SCHEMES FOR RATE-COMPATIBLE LDPC CODES
A. Rate-Compatible LDPC Codes
A major advantage of using rate-compatible codes is to reduce the storage memory. For instance, the WiMax standard lists six different LDPC codes for four different rates (1/2,2/3A,2/3B,3/4A,3/4B,5/6). A typical WiMax LDPC encoder/decoder design [8] must store the parity-check matrices of all six codes. Rate-compatible codes avoid the storage overhead as well as the potential latency problem caused by the large switching network. In communication systems, rate-compatible codes are also suitable for the hybrid ARQ protocols [9], because the transmitter can add redundancies progressively by puncturing the mother code based on the CSI.
Since rate-compatible codes have many advantages listed above, a prominent question is: can they provide comparable error performance with the unpunctured codes? For fair comparison, the unpunctured codes are referred to dedicated codes with the same length of the punctured codes. Before presenting the detail puncturing schemes, we ſrst introduce the deſnition of the k-SR variable node [4].

B. Selected Puncturing Scheme
Since a k1-SR node is more reliable than a k2-SR node if k1 < k2, we ſrst maximize the size of the 1-SR group G1 in order to minimize the error performance loss of the punctured codes. Then try to maximize the size of the 2-SR group G2, and so on. In other words, for the groups {G1, G2 · · · Gn} the punctured scheme will puncture the low indexed group ſrst.
The puncturing procedure can be divided into two steps: (1) the punctured block selection and (2) the punctured bits selection inside a block.

III. LOW-COMPLEXITY RATE-COMPATIBLE LDPC DECODER ARCHITECTURE
A. Parallel Layered Decoding Architecture
Traditional layered decoding algorithm processes layers in sequential order, which results in a large decoding latency per iteration. A method of increasing parallelism inside a layer is proposed in [12], [8], but all layers are still processed in series. Although decoding throughput can be improved, this method introduces crossbar-based interconnection networks that increase the hardware complexity. Let us take vertical block 1 of H base matrix as an example. Fig. 3(a) shows the traditional layered decoding procedure. Renewed APP messages from one row are only delivered to the next row serially within a same layer. For a vertical block, no APP message delivery happens among different layers except at the boundary.
In [7], a parallel layered decoding architecture (PLDA) is proposed which allows all layers to be processed concurrently. Each layer has a single check node processing unit (CNU) which generates and sends updated messages and at the same time also receives the updated messages from other layers. Unlike the method proposed in [12], PLDA uses parallel processing among all layers and serial processing within each layer. Critical path splitting technique [7] is also incorporated to reduce the latency of the critical path and thus improve the throughput.
In contrast to traditional layered decoding, in PLDA the message passing routes among layers are based on the their permutation values [7], as shown in Fig. 3(b). For each vertical block, we can sort the permutation values of all layers in descending order. Each layer then passes messages to the next layer with a smaller permutation value. The layer with the smallest permutation value loops back and connects to the layer with the largest value. This message passing scheme guarantees the updated messages among all layered are processed progressively within each iteration.

According to the message passing rules described above, the APP messages are passed following the route: Layer 5 →Layer 10→Layer 7. The numbers along the route denote how many clock cycles are left for the delivery of APP messages, which are actually the differences of the permutation values. Here we assume that each CNU starts from the row 1 of each layer. Then, we discover that message delivery from Layer 5 to Layer 10 must be ſnished within 2 cycles, which is too stringent. In other words, the CNU must ſnish the following tasks in 2 clock cycles, including message reading, check node processing, APP message updating and message writing. Otherwise, the renewed APP messages from Layer 5 would not be applied by Layer 10.

B. RC LDPC Decoder Design
The overall architecture of the RC LDPC decoder is shown in Fig. 5. It consists of CNUs, LLR initialization block, APP memory banks, CTV memory banks and bit decision units. It is similar to a regular PLDA design except for the initialization of the APP memory. Using the rate-1/2 WiMax code as an example, the entire Hb matrix is divided into 12 layers and each layer has a dedicated CNU. APP memory banks and CTV memory banks are used to store APP messages and CTV messages. Each nonzero elements in the base parity check matrix corresponds to one APP memory unit and one CTV memory unit. Each APP memory exports APP messages to the CNU and each CTV memory also exports CTV messages to the CNU. The CNU ſrst calculates the VTC messages, then calculate the updated set of CTV and APP messages, and ſnally imports updated CTV message to CTV memory banks and updated APP messages to APP memory banks. Therefore, the CNU becomes the critical path of the PLDA which limits the maximum frequency.
However, as indicated in Section III-A, an interval of 5 clock cycles is available for the APP message passing from one layer to another. Considering the memory writing operation which will cost one clock cycle, a split CNU with 4 pipeline stages can be designed by inserting some registers to the original CNU, bringing in reduced critical path delay and improved maximum frequency.
The puncturing scheme is implemented by patching the LLRs of the punctured parity bits to be 0’s, which is called LLR initialization. The length of the actual received codeword is smaller than that of the original mother code because of the punctured bits. For the selected puncturing scheme, the punctured blocks and bits listed in Table II should be initialized to zeros.
IV. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
For experimental study, we implement the selected puncturing scheme for the WiMax LDPC codes. We choose the rate-1/2 LDPC code in the WiMax standard as the mother code. Numerical simulations are performed to verify the BER performance between the punctured codes and the dedicated codes at three different rates. Furthermore, the rate compatible LDPC decoder are developed based on the PLDA architecture and then implemented using standard cell ASIC design ƀow.

Figure 6. BERs of the punctured LDPC codes
A. Simulation Results for Punctured WiMax Codes
The BER performance of a group of punctured LDPC codes are presented in Fig. 6. The rate of mother code is 1/2 and the code length is 2304 bits. Five different rates are generated using the selected punctured schemes, i.e., 3/5, 2/3, 3/4, 5/6 and 6/7. The corresponding number of punctured bits are 384, 576, 768, 922 and 960. Thus the code length of the punctured codes are 1920, 1728, 1536, 1382 and 1344, respectively.
To verify the selected puncturing scheme, dedicated LDPC codes at rate 2/3, 3/4 and 5/6 from WiMax standard are simulated to compare their performance with the punctured codes, also shown in Fig. 6. At each rate, a speciſc mode is selected from the 19 modes of each WiMax code to make the selected code lengths of the dedicated codes equal or similar to those of the punctured codes. The code lengths of the dedicated codes at rate 2/3, 3/4 and 5/6 are adjusted to 1728, 1536 and 1344, equivalent or similar to those of the corresponding punctured codes whose code lengths are 1728, 1536 and 1382. Fig. 6 shows that the BER of the punctured code is very close to the dedicated code, with less than 0.2dB performance loss at BER of 10−5.
B. Hardware Implementation Results
To demonstrate the effectiveness of our schemes, the proposed LDPC decoder, which supports rate 2/3, 3/5, 3/4 and 6/7 LDPC codes with puncturing, is synthesized and implemented on FPGA. Implemented by Xilinx XC2V8000 device, the maximum frequency is 61.3 MHz, corresponding to an input throughput of 28Mbps with 25 iterations. The throughput can be improved to 70Mbps if the number of iteration is set to be 10. Table III shows the comparison of our proposed decoder with a previous multi-rate WiMax decoder in [8].
V. CONCLUSIONS
This paper presents the algorithm, design and implementation of a rate-compatible LDPC decoder. Using the selected puncturing scheme, the BER performances of the punctured codes are comparable with the dedicated codes with less than 0.2dB performance degradation in simulation results.
