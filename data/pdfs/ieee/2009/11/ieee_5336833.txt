Waterfall Performance Analysis of
Finite-Length LDPC Codes on Symmetric Channels
Raman Yazdani, Student Member, IEEE, and Masoud Ardakani, Member, IEEE
Abstract—An efficient method for analyzing the performance
of finite-length low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes in the
waterfall region, when transmission takes place on a memoryless
binary-input output-symmetric channel is proposed. This method
is based on studying the variations of the channel quality around
its expected value when observed during the transmission of
a finite-length codeword. We model these variations with a
single parameter. This parameter is then viewed as a random
variable and its probability distribution function is obtained.
Assuming that a decoding failure is the result of an observed
channel worse than the code’s decoding threshold, the block
error probability of finite-length LDPC codes under different
decoding algorithms is estimated. Using an extrinsic information
transfer chart analysis, the bit error probability is obtained from
the block error probability. Different parameters can be used
for modeling the channel variations. In this work, two of such
parameters are studied. Through examples, it is shown that this
method can closely predict the performance of LDPC codes of
a few thousand bits or longer in the waterfall region.

I. INTRODUCTION
F
OR the asymptotic analysis of low-density parity-check
(LDPC) codes [1], when the block length tends to infinity,
efficient methods such as density evolution [2] or extrinsic
information transfer (EXIT) chart analysis [3] are widely used.
Such methods, however, are not accurate for finite-length
codes. Therefore, recently, there has been a growing interest
in the analysis of finite-length LDPC codes [4]–[10]. For
example, on the binary erasure channel (BEC), a combinatorial
analysis has been presented in [4], [5] and an upper bound on
the error floor is given in [6]. These methods give the average
performance of an ensemble using recursions. Unfortunately,
these recursions are computationally complex and even for
practical block lengths only very simple ensembles can be
analyzed.
For hard-decision decoding on the binary symmetric chan-
nel (BSC), a combinatorial analysis has been presented in
[9] which enumerates the error patterns uncorrectable by the
decoder. The complexity of this method grows exponentially

with the block length. Thus, an approximation method has
also been proposed to reduce the complexity. The approach of
[9] has also been extended to quantized soft-decision iterative
decoding algorithms in [10].
In another work [8], through a detailed analysis, it is
shown that the performance curves of LDPC codes in the
waterfall region, follow a scaling law. This scaling law is
proved for iteratively decoded LDPC ensembles over the BEC.
The scaling law consists of two extra parameters 𝛼and 𝛽
which depend on the code ensemble. Although the scaling
law predicts the performance very accurately, the procedure
of calculating 𝛼and 𝛽is only known for the BEC and can be
cumbersome especially for irregular codes [8]. Also, for other
channels such as the BSC and the binary-input additive white
Gaussian noise (BIAWGN) channel, the parameters are fitted
to the empirical data.
The scaling law is a consequence of the threshold behavior
of LDPC decoders. That is, successful decoding is possible
if and only if the channel quality is better than a threshold.
When the code length is finite, during the transmission of a
single codeword, the average channel behavior may not be
observed. For example, in a BSC with crossover probability
𝜖(BSC(𝜖)), after a finite number of channel uses, say 𝑁, the
actual number of flipped bits may be more than or less than
𝜖𝑁. The decoder is influenced by the actual number of flipped
bits or the observed channel quality during the transmission
of a codeword, not the average channel quality.
This view can be used to form a simplified and efficient
version of the scaling law without the extra parameters 𝛼
and 𝛽. The main idea is to estimate the block error rate
by assuming that a decoding failure is the result of an
observed channel worse than the decoding threshold of the
code. For some memoryless binary-input output-symmetric
(BIOS) channels, since the observed channel cannot be fully
modeled by a single variable, a direct comparison of the
observed channel with the decoding threshold is not possible.
We discuss modeling the channel variations with a single
parameter and briefly study and compare different parameters.
We then show that our approach provides a very good estimate
of the code’s block and bit error rate performance (typically
within 0.1 dB) in the waterfall region when the code length
is more than a few thousand bits (typically >2500 bits). For
codes of shorter length, our method loses its accuracy in the
low error rate regions. The method is applicable to both regular
and irregular codes under different decoding algorithms. Also,
no fitting of parameters is required.

IV. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
A. Examples
Example 1: Consider codes from the ensemble of (3,6)-
regular LDPC codes on the BSC(𝜖) with Gallager’s decoding
algorithm A. In Fig. 2, the actual performance of these codes
obtained from simulation are compared to our prediction
method and also the scaling law prediction method [8] for
different block lengths. The scaling law prediction method
parameters are fitted to simulation data as in [8]. It is seen that
our prediction method provides accurate enough prediction
results while it does not need any parameter fitting.
□
Example 2: Consider the BIAWGN(𝜎) channel with sum-
product decoding. In Fig. 3, the actual performance of the
codes from the ensemble of (3,6)-regular LDPC codes is
compared with our prediction methods for different 𝑁.
□
Example 3: Now consider a rate-1/2 irregular code on the
same channel. Fig. 4 compares the simulation results obtained
for different randomly constructed LDPC codes with our best
prediction method, i.e., the method based on 𝑃obs. Again, we
see that our prediction method closely approximates the actual
performance. It is worth mentioning that in both cases 𝑃obs
gives more accurate results than 𝐶obs.

B. Discussion
As the results show, by considering the channel variations
around its expected behavior, we are able to describe the
code’s performance in the waterfall region. In other words,
even by ignoring the presence of cycles in the code’s factor
graph, it is possible to predict the performance of LDPC codes
of a few thousand bits or longer in the waterfall region quite
accurately.

V. CONCLUSION
A simple method for analysis of finite-length LDPC codes
in the waterfall region is introduced. Compared to the scaling
law [8], our method gives slightly less accurate results. From
any practical point of view, however, our method gives accu-
rate enough estimation of the performance for codes of a few
thousand bits or longer. In addition, our method is simpler,
does not have any additional parameters, and does not require
any curve fitting. Our method is applicable to both regular and
irregular codes, different decoding algorithms and different
BIOS channels.
In this work we used 𝑃obs and 𝐶obs for modeling the
channel. Other parameters such as the mean of the observed
LLRs, or the observed noise power could also be used in
modeling the channel variations and predicting the perfor-
mance. Our investigations over various channels show that
𝑃obs consistently provides the most accurate results.
