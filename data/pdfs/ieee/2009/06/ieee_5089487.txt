Toward Low LDPC-Code Floors: A Case Study
Yifei Zhang, Member, IEEE, and William E. Ryan, Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—While LDPC codes have been widely acclaimed in recent years for their near-capacity performance, they have not found their way into many important applications. For some cases, this is due to their increased decoding complexity relative to the classical coding techniques. For other cases, this is due to their inability to reach very low bit error rates (e.g., 10−12) at low signal-to-noise ratios (SNRs), a consequence of the errorrate floor phenomenon associated with iterative LDPC decoders. In the present paper, we make strides in the low-floor problem by identifying the weaknesses of the code under study and applying compensatory counter-measures. These counter-measures include: modifying the code itself, modifying the decoder, or adding a properly designed outer algebraic code. Our results demonstrate that each of these techniques can successfully lower an LDPC code’s floor, and that, for the code under study, an outer BCH code appears to be particularly effective. All of our results are based on FPGA decoder simulations and so they are reliable and repeatable.
Index Terms—LDPC code, IRA code, error floor, trapping set, bit pinning.
I. INTRODUCTION
T HERE has been a great deal of research devoted to lowdensity parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] in the past decade, due to their near-Shannon limit performance when decoded with relatively low-complexity iterative decoders. However, they have not been employed in systems that require very low error rates, such as data storage devices, due to the errorrate floor phenomenon associated with iterative decoders. A large number of papers have been dedicated to the design of LDPC codes with low floors [2]-[12] and only a few have been devoted to the floor-lowering techniques at the decoder [13], [14], [36]-[40]. The present paper considers this latter approach.
Even if the floor phenomenon was fully understood, the design of low-floor LDPC code systems is very difficult because they are not amenable to analysis, and simulations down to the 10−10 bit error rate (BER) region can take several weeks on even the fastest computers. Further, unless the computer simulations were "bit-true" emulations of hardware decoders, results based on computer simulations would not be reliable because LDPC floors are highly dependent on decoder specifics, e.g., algorithm type and word size. An alternative to computer simulations are simulations based on a fieldprogrammable gate-array (FPGA) platform. Our study of an LDPC code’s floor relies on FPGA decoder simulations and, hence, can be considered to be reliable, and other hardware

It is well known that the error floor of LDPC codes under message-passing decoding schemes are usually due to lowweight near-codewords [15], or trapping sets [16], rather than low-weight codewords. In this paper, focusing on a (4544, 4096) structured irregular-repeat-accumulate (S-IRA) code [10], we describe techniques for finding the code’s dominant trapping sets and prescribe means for mitigating their impact. These mitigating counter-measures include: modifying the code itself, modifying the decoder, or adding a properly designed outer algebraic code. By focusing on a single code, our discussions can be more concrete and the results of our floor-lowering techniques may all be compared. However, these methods can be applied to other structured codes which have few dominant trapping sets. For random codes or codes with lots of dominant trapping sets, they are not very effective.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. In Section II, the definition and construction of S-IRA codes are reviewed. The performance results of an S-IRA(4544, 4096) code are presented. In Section III, algorithms for finding the trapping sets of this code are presented. In Section IV, we propose two decoder modification techniques for lowering the code’s floor and then we investigate the ability of outer algebraic codes to lower the floor.
II. STRUCTURED IRA CODES
The original LDPC codes of Gallager [1] and MacKay [17] were largely unstructured. LDPC codes with little structure suffer from higher encoder and decoder implementation complexities. More recent LDPC code designs possess varying degrees of structure. We divide the set of structured LDPC codes into two categories: deterministically structured codes and randomly structured codes. A deterministically structured code is a code for which the parity-check matrix is built according to a deterministic algorithm, based, for instance, on algebraic, combinatoric, or geometric principles. Examples are the finite geometry codes [2] and the array codes [18]. A randomly structured code is a code whose parity-check matrix possesses structure, e.g., a quasi-cyclic structure, but was constructed using a computer-based algorithm with a random search component. The code considered in this paper falls into the latter category.
Structured LDPC codes are usually cyclic or quasi-cyclic (QC). The parity-check matrix H for a QC code can be conveniently arranged into an M ×N array of Q×Q circulant matrices (some of which may be the Q×Q zero matrix). Often, all of the circulant matrices have unity weight, in which case we call them circulant permutation matrices (cpm’s). We will refer to the Q variable nodes (VNs) associated with a column of circulants as a VN group and the Q check nodes (CNs) associated with a row of circulant as a CN group. III. FINDING TRAPPING SETS
It is well known [17] that the SPA decoder does not accurately compute marginal probabilities on Tanner graphs with cycles. Unlike the maximum-likelihood decoder, the SPA decoder failures are usually not due to low-weight codewords. Rather, they are usually due to low-weight near-codewords [15]. A (ω, ν) near-codeword is an n-bit error pattern of weight ω which results in ν check failures (i.e., the syndrome weight is ν), where ω and ν are “small”. Near-codewords tend to lead to error situations from which the SPA decoder (and its approximations) cannot escape. An essentially equivalent notion is that of a trapping set [16]. In this section, we discuss how trapping sets may be determined, with particular focus on the 0.9(4544, 4096) S-IRA code described above. The search for trapping sets is divided into three steps: First, we catalog the dominant trapping sets observed in our FPGA simulations. Second, via computer search of the H matrix, we catalog all trapping sets which share the same structure as those found in the first step. Third, an importance sampling (IS) technique based on [31] is applied to find additional dominant trapping sets related to the ones found in the second step. These three steps are discussed in the three following subsections.

A. Initial Trapping Sets from Simulations
In SPA decoding of LDPC codes, dominant trapping sets are, in general, caused by a combination of multiple cycles, [4], [31], especially in highly optimized graphs. Since the trapping set definition depends on the decoder input space and the decoding algorithm [16], error floor behavior can depend strongly on the level of quantization used [32]. On the AWGN channel, given a code and a soft-decision decoding algorithm, it is very difficult to predict trapping sets without computer simulations.
We found two trapping set search techniques in the literature [16], [13]. In [16], the trapping sets are first located using hardware decoding simulations by observing decoding failures, and then additional trapping sets may be discovered while evaluating their contribution to the error floor using a type of IS technique. In [13], a graph search technique combined with IS simulations was proposed which exploits the fact that trapping sets are frequently formed by the overlap of multiple short cycles. The searching process includes two steps: (1) For each VN, find all of the short cycles up to some cycle length and list all of the other bits involved in those cycles. (2) For each VN, apply IS to identify the trapping sets associated with these cycles by biasing all of the cycle-associated bits.

The goal of the algorithm which searches for the (4, 8) trapping set is to find a 14 × 4 submatrix of Hs having the characteristics of the 14 × 4 submatrix in Fig. 3(a). For example, any candidate submatrix must have four weight-five columns, eight weight-one rows (unsatisfied checks) and six weight-two rows (mis-satisfied checks). The algorithm starts from column 0 (the leftmost column) of Hs and proceeds rightward, seeking three more columns, one at a time, which satisfy the characteristics of the Fig. 3(a) submatrix. The algorithm then starts with the VN group unused in the previous step and proceeds rightward, seeking an appropriate 14 × 4 submatrix; and so on. Each trapping set representative found will have a multiplicity of 64, which reduces the search time.
Fig. 3(b) presents the generalized submatrices for the (5, 5) and (5, 7) trapping sets. Observe that both the (5, 5) and (5, 7) trapping sets can be found by continuing the above procedure to find the fifth column because the first four columns of the (5, 5) and (5, 7) submatrices are identical to the (4,8) submatrix of Fig. 3(a).

The errors observed in the error floor region do not necessarily appear exactly in one of the above three trapping set forms we have discussed so far. Many error patterns are variations of these. The most dominant variation of the (5, 5) trapping set is a (6, 5) trapping set, which has a parity bit VN attached to one of the five unsatisfied CNs in Fig. 2(a), without changing the syndrome weight. For the (5, 7) trapping set, the most dominant variation is another (6, 5) trapping set. In fact, this (6, 5) variation is more significant than the (5, 7) trapping set, since the trapping set with the larger ω (more bit errors) and smaller ν (fewer unsatisfied checks) is more dominant, as noted in [16], [13]. For the (6, 2) trapping set class of Fig. 2(b), many relevant error blocks observed contain only a subset of bit errors of the (6, 2) trapping sets. These relevant errors are oscillating errors, that is, bit error patterns which change in an oscillatory fashion from iteration to iteration. They contribute nearly all error blocks with only one or two systematic bit errors.
The IS technique provides a very simple and efficient way to find the variations by biasing the bit nodes in the trapping sets. We simply inserted errors into transmitted codewords by changing the all-zero transmitted codeword to a near-codeword corresponding to one of the three trapping set classes, and then ran the software decoder emulating the hardware one. We collected the variations from decoding failures. Rather than listing all the variations of the dominant trapping sets introduced in the above subsections, we only focus on the most dominant (6, 5) trapping set. The search results for this (6, 5) trapping set are: v˜p = [3 10 18 33 52 70], v˜o = [0 9 23 9 10 34]; v˜p = [8 30 43 52 58 67], v˜o = [0 49 32 14 10 30]; and v˜p = [30 42 43 52 58 67], v˜o = [0 17 47 29 25 45].
IV. FLOOR LOWERING TECHNIQUES
There exist few results in the literature on decoder improvements for lower LDPC code error floors. We divide the proposed approaches into two categories: decoder modification and outer code addition. In [14], an averaged decoding algorithm was proposed to reduce the number of incorrectly decoded frames in the error floor region of the Margulis code. This algorithm is a modified SPA decoding algorithm which averages messages over several iterations to “slow down” the convergence rate of certain variable values, because a sudden magnitude change in the values of certain variable messages or fast convergence to an unreliable estimate is a possible indicator of the emergence of an error trap. The authors of [40] suggested an efficient approach based on loop calculus that generalizes the BP algorithm and which is capable of reducing the error floor effect. In [13], the authors propose a post-processing technique to lower the error floor by using a look-up table of known trapping sets. After conventional SPA decoding, this table is used to process the residual error blocks much like a syndrome decoder. Another postprocessing method was introduced in [36], [37], where bitguessing is used whenever decoder fails. The other category

C. Outer Algebraic Code
As discussed in Section III, the error floor of the SIRA(4544, 4096) code is a consequence of frequently occurring error blocks, with a small number of systematic bit errors in each error block. A natural solution to clean up these residual errors and lower the floor is the addition of an outer error correction code. A BCH outer code is already employed in the DVB-S2 standard [33]. In this section, we first consider an outer Reed-Solomon (RS) code and then an outer BCH code.
