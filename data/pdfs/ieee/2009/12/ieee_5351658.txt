Iterative Decoding using Eigenmessages
Todd K. Moon, John S. Crockett, Jacob H. Gunther, and Ojas S. Chauhan
Abstract—The eigenmessage approach to iterative decoding
introduces a degree of nonlocality into a belief propagation
decoder by representing an entire set of messages around a cycle
of the Tanner graph as a linear operator. The eigenvector for
the operator represents a fixed point of the belief propagation
algorithm around a cycle, with incident messages fixed. A
multiple eigenmessage approach is also presented, in which
messages around several cycles are simultaneously expressed.
The eigenmessage approach may be applied to any graph with
cycles, but we demonstrate its use on LDPC decoding. In this
setting, computational results compare eigenmessage methods
with conventional belief propagation decoding showing, using
simulation and EXIT charts, that the eigenmessage approaches
slightly reduce the number of decoder iterations compared to
belief propagation decoding while preserving the probability of
error of conventional decoding.
Index Terms—Error correction coding, iterative methods, de-
coding, eigenvalues and eigenfunctions.
I. INTRODUCTION AND SUMMARY
M
AXIMUM posterior marginal (MPM) or maximum
likelihood decoding of a code on a graph is a global
discrete optimization problem and suffers from computational
complexity. Belief propagation (BP) decoding algorithms (or
the sum-product or message passing (MP) algorithms) [1], [2],
[3] reduce the global problem to a set of local problems which
have lower complexity while finding a solution frequently
equal to the global solution. It is well known [2] that for
a graph without cycles, the BP algorithm terminates in a
number of iterations proportional to the diameter of the graph.
However, graphs associated with iteratively decoded codes
such as turbo codes, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes,
or repeat-accumulate codes, do have cycles. In such cases,
the performance the BP algorithm provides is nearly optimal,
but the decoding may take many iterations. This high number
of iterations diminishes the potential utility of such codes
by increasing decoding complexity and decoding latency. In
using more than strictly local information, the problem, of
course, is to use the nonlocal information in a way that does
not significantly increase the computational complexity per
iteration. It is well known that it is possible to reformulate a
graph to eliminate cycles, but the conventional approach [2]
results in nodes taking on a number of values exponential
in the number of nodes that were in the cycle removed.
Removing all the cycles in the graph in the conventional way
would reduce the problem to simple BP on a tree, having a

strict bound on the the number of required iterations, but the

significantly. We have been exploring algorithms on graphs
with cycles which make use of more nonlocal information than
conventional BP algorithms, so that the message passed along
an edge is a function of information that is not strictly incident
to the nodes on the edge. The intent of this exploration has
been to determine the performance of such algorithms, and to
determine if they can reduce the number of decoding iterations
[4], [5], [6].
Conventional (local) BP can be expressed as a linear
operation on the messages. The linear operation for a BP
step between two nodes depends upon the messages that are
incident upon the nodes at the time the message is sent. Our
approach is to express the BP rules as linear operators (given
that other edges incident upon a cycle are fixed), then compose
the linear operators around a cycle into a single normalized
linear operator that describes passing a message around the
entire cycle. The passage of a message around the cycle
corresponds to applying this linear operator to the message.
The fixed point of this belief propagation around the cycle
could be obtained (in principle) by applying the linear operator
an infinite number of times. Alternatively, the limiting message
could be obtained simply by solving an eigenequation. The
message vector obtained from solving such an eigenequation
is referred to as the eigenmessage of the cycle. The decoding
algorithm proceeds by solving an eigenequation for each cycle
in the graph, with edges incident to the cycle fixed as if the
decoding for other nodes were in fact at a fixed point. The
eigenmessage thus takes advantage of information at all nodes
around the cycle, and behaves as if an infinite number of
iterations had been computed on that cycle. This technique is
referred to as the “single-cycle eigenmessage” method, since
the eigenequation is established for each cycle separately.
It is also possible to stack up multiple belief propagation
steps to establish multiple constraints among the messages
circulating around several cycles. This is called the “multiple-
cycle eigenmessage” method, although computationally it is
handled as a least-squares problem. This viewpoint has the
interesting feature that, by converting the decoding problem
into a series of least-squares problems, it may be suitable for
solution in hardware by any number of efficient hardware-
oriented algorithms, such as pipelined CORDIC rotations [7].
The conventional decoding schedule, in which every node
sends a message to its neighbors at each decoding iteration,
is referred to here as a flooding schedule. Other researchers
have also successfully examined decoding schedules (see, e.g.,
[8], [9], [10]) and shown that changing the decoding schedule
or message values results in reduced decoding complexity or
reduced error probability, or both. For example, [11] shows

the number of iterations decreases, the probability of error

Behavior of BP algorithms on graphs with cycles has been
examined in some simplified cases. In [17], very small turbo
codes with block lengths ≤4 were examined, demonstrating
that a variety of convergence behaviors could be obtained even
for very short codes. In [18], a graph with a single cycle
was examined, where the simplified structure of the graph
led to an explicit eigenvalue problem whose solution exactly
characterized the fixed point of the iterated BP; the analysis
compared the “true” solution to the BP solution obtained
using the eigenvalue problem. An analysis was reported in
[19] which examined the parameter estimates for Gaussian
random variables for data on graphs with cycles. Similarly,
the results for max-product belief propagation on graphs with
cycles was examined in [20] by “unrolling” the cycles. In
[21], BP on graphs with cycles is accelerated by representing
these graphs by a set of overlapping minimal spanning trees.
A BP round then consists of propagating messages entirely
throughout each tree. This results in a reduction in the number
of iterations. A high-level analysis of the convergence behavior
is presented in [22], [23].

C. Scheduling
Just as there is some flexibility in the scheduling of mes-
sages in a conventional belief propagation sweep, so there is
some flexibility in the manner in which eigenmessage steps
are interlaced with the conventional steps. We present here
one method for such scheduling, without making any claims
to optimality.
A code of even moderate length may have a very large
number of cycles in it, and many of these cycles may share
several edges. Computing an eigenmessage for each cycle in
the graph could thus result in a large number of computations,
and would result in many messages being recomputed. Rather
than considering all the cycles of a graph, we have chosen
to use only the shortest cycle through each bit node. A bit
node with more than one cycle of shortest length through it
retains all of the shortest cycles. Cycles which are duplicates
(shared by more than one bit node) are eliminated. These
preprocessing steps are summarized in the following.

Since the eigenmessage represents the fixed point for an
infinite number of belief propagation operations around a
cycle, if the cycle matrix represented the actual cycle matrix
that exists at convergence for the entire graph, it would
only be necessary to compute eigenmessages on each cycle
once. However, since each cycle matrix depends on messages
incident from outside the cycle its eigenmessage is at best an
approximation to the final fixed point of the entire graph. It
is therefore necessary to iterate over the entire graph several
times, recomputing the cycle matrices each time.
The overall algorithm, which we call the Eigenmessage
Algorithm, is summarized in Algorithm 2 and in Fig. 2. For
each block of input data to the decoder, initialization of the
decoder is done by standard BP once on each edge. After
this, the algorithm consists of computing eigenmessages on
each of the cycles in turn in both the forward and backward
direction around the cycle. For each cycle the eigenmessage
is pushed around the cycle so that every edge in the cycle has
its eigenmessage. (This is simple 2 × 2 matrix multiplication
if the BP matrices for the cycle are saved.) Propagation of
information between cycles is done by computing a regular BP
step. (It was found that this overall BP step was necessary to
ensure good convergence.) In the figure, it is shown that (after
the initial BP step) the computations for the BP are computed
twice for each iteration: once to build the cycle matrices,
and once again to propagate the information found throughout
the graph. Thus the overall cost for an eigenmessage step is
somewhat more than the cost of two conventional BP steps.
Because of the extra computations involved, it is difficult
to know exactly how to compare the computational complex-
ity of conventional belief propagation and the eigenmessage
approach. We have chosen as our bases of comparison the
number of decoding iterations and the probability of error.
However, in interpreting these results, it should be kept in
mind that the eigenmessage approach requires two BP steps
(plus some extra computations) for every iteration.

D. Computational Considerations
The multiple-cycle eigenmessage approach adds significant
computational complexity to the single cycle algorithm. While
in the single cycle case the eigenmessages can be read directly
from the cycle matrix, there is no shortcut in the multiple-
cycle case that can circumvent the QR factorization. We can
exploit known zero locations to speed the factorization, but
this does not even achieve a factor-of-two speedup. Thus the
native computational complexity of multiple-cycle decoding is
greater than single cycle decoding.
A reduction in computational complexity can be gained
by noting that the solution to the optimization problem
minw ∥AZw −b∥2 is not changed by only using half of the
probabilities. That is, since each 𝐴and ℒmatrix in (21) is
column stochastic, the second row is completely determined
by the first row. If we include only the first rows of the 𝐴s
and ℒs, the dimension of the matrix in the QR factorization
is halved, but the solution w obtained is not changed. (The 𝒪
matrix is halved as well so the dimensions fit.)
The number of iterations required to decode for single cycle
and multiple-cycle are approximately equal, as will be demon-
strated by simulation below. The computational simplifications
discussed benefit multiple-cycle eigenmessage decoding more
than single cycle eigenmessage decoding, bringing the two
roughly on par in terms of overall decoding time using reduced
cycle lists.

These results must be interpreted in light of information
about the girth distribution of the code. Figure 9 shows
the girth distribution for the codes examined. What these
distributions show is that for codes of small girth there are few
cycles at the shorter length, while all codes show a tendency
to peak around girth 12. These distributions explain, in part,
why the decoding is largely invariant to girth for the codes
examined: there are only a small fraction of the cycles at girth.

provide better advantage on graphs with long cycles. Con-

D. Using a Subset of the Cycles
The eigenmessage algorithms rely on a preprocessed list
of the shortest cycles in the graph. This has been done
by including the shortest cycles through every node (i.e. if
the shortest cycle through a given node has length 8, then
every cycle of length 8 through that node is included in the
list). However, since the decoding time for the eigenmessage
algorithm is proportional to the number of cycles it processes,
a reduction in the number of cycles processed would result
in a reduction in computation time. We pose the question: Is
there some subset of the cycles we can use for the processing
while still maintaining the performance of the complete set of
cycles?

We tried two subsets: We reduced the list of cycles to the
fewest possible while still ensuring each node appeared at least
once in the list (“repeatfactor=1”). Then we made sure each
node appeared at least twice in the list (“repeatfactor=2”). For
large matrices these resulted in approximately 75% and 60%
reductions in cycles, respectively. (Notice that in choosing a
subset of the cycles to process in the algorithm we do not
eliminate the other cycles from the graph, but simply ignore
them.)
The results can be described as follows. For each of the
eigenmessage algorithms, the time to decode dropped drasti-
cally when using repeatfactor=2 and more so for repeatfac-
tor=1. An interesting side effect of using only a subset of the
cycles was that the number of iterations required to decode
increased slightly. This was not big enough to offset the drastic
decrease in computations per iteration. The probabilities of
error were comparable, suggesting that when using only a
subset of the cycles the algorithm would still decode correctly.
It seems unlikely that any smaller set would be better, since
it must leave out nodes, but this could be an area for further
research.
V. CONCLUSIONS
We have presented the eigenmessage method for belief
propagation on graphs with cycles, applying it (by way of
example and verification) to the problem of LDPC decoding.
We have presented both single cycle and multiple cycle
methods, showing how to construct the cycle matrices from
a succession of matrices derived from the belief propagation
steps. Each eigenmessage step contains within it two BP steps,
plus some additional lower complexity operations to propagate
messages around the cycles. In the application to LDPC
decoding, we have shown that, even taking into account the
double BP step, the eigenmessage methods require fewer BP
steps than conventional BP (flooding). Some evidence for this
is supplied by the EXIT charts, for which the eigenmessage
method has a wider channel, even when double-stepping
occurs on the conventional BP EXIT chart. When taking
into account the appropriate number of BP iterations, BP and
eigenmessage methods have essentially equivalent probability
of error performance, illustrating that the eigenvector approach
does not introduce biases into the operation.
In
general,
the
multiple-cycle
eigenmessage
methods
yielded little if any additional benefit compared to single cycle
methods, particularly when the computational complexity is
taken into account. Research is ongoing into how multiple
cycles should be selected to minimize computational com-
plexity while achieving good performance in terms of number
of iterations and probability of error. Designing LDPC codes
that can be very effectively decoded using the eigenmessage
step is also ongoing.
As mentioned in the introduction, several other methods
have been investigated in the literature to address the presence
of cycles in BP algorithms for LDPC decoding. Interesting
future research could involve hybridization of these ideas.
For example, the eigenmessage concept applied to normalized
[11], [12], [26] or offset [11] belief propagation would be
straightforward and could yield improved results.
While applied to the LDPC decoding problem, the method
is general, and may be applied wherever BP algorithms are
employed on graphs with cycles. It is a topic of future
investigation to determine performance in other settings.
