On the Dynamics of Analog Min-Sum
Iterative Decoders: An Analytical Approach
Saied Hemati, Senior Member, IEEE, and Abbas Yongaçoglu, Senior Member, IEEE
Abstract—In this paper, we show an ideal analog min-sum
decoder, in the log-likelihood ratio domain, can be considered
as a piecewise linear system. Many theoretical aspects of these
decoders, thus, can be studied analytically. It is also shown that
the dynamic equations can become singular for codes with cycles.
When it is non-singular, the corresponding dynamic equations
can be solved analytically to derive outputs of the decoder.
We study the relationship between singularity and error floor
and prove that absorption sets with degree two check nodes
are singular graphs and under specific conditions the dynamic
equations of an analog min-sum decoder can be reduced to that
of an absorption set. The proposed approach paves the way for
further analytical analysis on the dynamics of analog min-sum
decoders and error floor in low-density parity-check codes.
Index Terms—Iterative decoding, analog decoding, min-sum
decoding, LDPC codes, singularity, error floor, absorption set.
I. INTRODUCTION
F
OR many communication channels, error-correcting per-
formance of iterative decoding algorithms, applied to low-
density parity-check (LDPC) codes and turbo codes, is close
to optimum decoding [1], [2]. As the decoding complexity is
affordable, these decoding algorithms are now used in many
applications [3]. Iterative decoding algorithms are defined in
discrete time domain and are associated by a synchronous
message passing scheme and thus can be implemented by
digital circuits [4] , [5]. However, improving speed/power ratio
in these decoders motivated several groups to design analog
circuits for performing the necessary operations [6]-[22]. In
the so-called analog iterative decoders, iteration disappears
and is replaced by a transient response that would be expected
to converge to the most likely transmitted codeword. Several

In [22] a simple model for ideal analog iterative decoding
was introduced and its dynamic equations, which are nonlin-
ear differential equations, were derived. By using numerical
techniques, it was shown that the dynamics of analog iterative
decoders is different from that of the conventional iterative
decoders. While the former is based on application of succes-
sive relaxation (SR) technique to the fixed point problem of
decoding, the latter is based on the application of successive
substitution (SS) method. It was also observed that due to
higher chance of convergence for SR compared to SS, analog
decoding can provide some coding gain for codes with cycles.
In this paper, an analytical approach is used for studying the
dynamics of ideal analog min-sum decoders. This study sheds
more light on theoretical aspects of decoding in continuous
time domain and provides a better understanding about time
response of ideal analog min-sum decoders. We show that an
ideal analog min-sum decoder in log-likelihood ratio (𝐿𝐿𝑅)
domain can be considered as a piecewise linear system; that is,
for any time interval [𝑡1, 𝑡2], this system can be partitioned into
finitely many sub-intervals, such that on each such sub-interval
𝐼, it is equal to a linear system. We also show that outputs of
the decoder can be derived analytically by having the parity-
check matrix of the code and the received information from
the channel.
We consider the model introduced for analog decoding in
[22], and write the dynamic equation in a matrix form. We then
study many important aspects of this system, such as bounds
on eigenvalues and stability of the dynamic equations. We
show the dynamic equations can become singular and under
specific conditions we relate them to absorption sets that are
responsible for error floors in LDPC codes. We also show
dithering that models implementation imperfections such as
mismatch can help in tackling singularity problem.

III. ANALOG MIN-SUM DECODING
While conventional iterative decoding is defined in the
discrete-time domain, analog iterative decoding clearly works
in the continuous-time domain. The requirement of continuity
of messages in the time domain in analog iterative decoding
makes it different from the conventional iterative decoding
algorithms. To accommodate the continuity requirement in
a simple model for analog decoding, we may use different
approaches. It is possible to consider continuity for variable
nodes’ outgoing messages, for variable node’s incoming mes-

filter (LPF) can ideally remove discontinuities in the required
messages and thus we consider LPF modules for messages that
are assumed to be continuous in the time domain, as shown
in Fig. 1.

IV. SINGULARITY AND ERROR FLOOR
A singularity is a problematic state in dynamic systems that
indicates lack of enough consistent equations for predicting the
behavior of the system and that a unique solution cannot be
found. The singularity of the characteristic matrix is caused by
the structure of the code. Similarly, error floors in LDPC codes
are often linked to the structure of the code, and in particular,
to its poorly connected sub-graphs, that cause the iterative
decoding algorithm to fail when a matching error pattern is
received [27]. This is often the dominant failure mechanism
of iterative decoders in the high signal to noise ratio region.
These sub-graphs are called absorption sets when Gallager’s
bit-flipping algorithms and binary symmetric channels are con-
sidered [28]. When Gaussian channels are considered, these
sub-graphs are called trapping sets [27]. Empirical results
show that for many LDPC codes of interest, absorption sets,
which are more convenient for analytical analysis, are indeed
the dominant trapping sets [28]-[29]. We will show that error
patterns similar to absorption sets can make the characteristic
matrix of an analog min-sum decoder singular.
In the following, we will focus on the most common
absorption sets, where each wrongly satisfied check node is
connected to two variable nodes in the set and each unsatisfied
check node is connected to one variable node in the set,
whereas in general it could be any even and odd number,
respectively [29]. These absorption sets are often smaller and
thus are more dominant as they are more likely to match the
error pattern. Fig. 2 shows three absorption sets in [29] where
degree two check nodes are not shown between the connecting
variable nodes.
Absorption sets with degree two check nodes have singular
characteristic matrices as the following theorem states. An

V. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, a theoretical approach was adopted for study-
ing the analog min-sum decoder as a piecewise linear system
that sheds more light on the dynamics of these decoders. We
studied eigenvalues of the system and found upper bounds for
them that correspond to the maximum possible rate of change
in analog min-sum decoders. Furthermore, we derived a closed
form expression for the decoder’s outputs in time domain. One
of the important consequences of this study is in showing that
the dynamic equations of the analog min-sum decoder can
become singular. The singularity issue degrades the decoding
performance of the ideal analog min-sum decoder. It was
also shown that error patterns similar to absorption sets can
make the analog min-sum decoder singular that implies there
are relationships between singularity issue and error floor in
LDPC codes. Furthermore, our simulation results for a code
showed that by using the dithering method, which mimics the
effects of implementation imperfections such as mismatch in
practical analog decoders, singularity issues can be resolved.
This approach may also be useful in tackling the error floor
issue in LDPC codes.
