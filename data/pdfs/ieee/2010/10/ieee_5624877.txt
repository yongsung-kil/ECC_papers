ERROR PERFORMANCE AND DECODER HARDWARE COMPARISON BETWEEN EG-LDPC AND BCH CODES

Jonghong Kim, Junho Cho, and Wonyong Sung

Department of Electrical Engineering and Computer Sciences, Seoul National University, Seoul, 151-744 Korea

ABSTRACT
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes are promising for low code rate applications, however its competitiveness over BCH codes in the high code rate region is not well studied. In this work, we compare the Euclidean geometry (EG) LDPC and BCH codes of the length 1,023, 4,095, and 16,383 that have the code rates of 0.75~0.85. Hard-decision input data are applied to both decoders, and the EG-LDPC codes are decoded using the layered bit-flipping (BF) algorithm. Since the number of needed iterations for LDPC decoding depends on the channel condition, both decoders are designed to have the similar minimum throughput. Not only error performance but also hardware complexity and power consumption of these decoders are compared.
Index Terms— Low-density parity-check codes, BCH codes, layered decoding, bit-flipping
1. INTRODUCTION
LDPC codes were chosen for many applications such as the Mobile WiMAX and DVB-S2 systems because of their excellent decoding performance. However, their implementtation complexity is usually considered very high when employing soft-decision decoding algorithms such as the sum-product algorithm (SPA). The restriction due to high implementation complexity limits LDPC codes to fairly low code rate applications, such as 1/2~2/3. Meanwhile BCH codes are one of the popular forward error correcting codes and they also have been used for many applications ranging from memory storage devices to long-haul optical communication systems. The BCH codes render fairly simple implementation when the error correcting capability is small, which corresponds to the high code rate region. Therefore, it is of considerable interest to analyze the competitiveness between LDPC and BCH codes in the code rate region of 3/4~7/8.
Most works for improving the error performance of LDPC codes have been focused on the soft-decision decoding due to its superior error correcting performance.

In this work, LDPC decoding with hard-decision input data is considered because of fair comparison with BCH decoding as well as low implementation complexity. Although high-rate LDPC codes can be generated using other construction methods, we use Euclidean geometry (EG)-LDPC codes [4] since it shows good error performance with bit-flipping (BF) decoding [5] and has good algebraic structures, such as cyclic or quasi-cyclic structures. The layered BF algorithm is employed for implementing LDPC decoders.
Some papers reported the error performance comparisons of LDPC and BCH codes [1, 2]. The LDPC codes outperform the BCH codes when decoded by the SPA [1]. Furthermore, [2] shows that the error performance of selected LDPC codes is still better than that of BCH codes when decoded by the BF algorithm. However, no studies have presented the comparison between these decoders in terms of hardware complexity and power consumption. In this paper, we compare the error performance of BCH and LDPC codes of various code lengths, and also present their implementation complexities. VLSI design for these decoders is conducted to assess the chip area, decoding time, and power consumption.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 introduces decoding algorithms of LDPC and BCH codes, and gives a comparison of their error performance. In Section 3, the architectures of LDPC and BCH decoders are presented. The hardware complexity comparisons of LDPC and BCH decoders are provided in Section 4. Finally, Section 5 concludes this paper.

2. ALGORITHM COMPARISON OF BCH AND LDPC CODES

2.1. Layered bit-flipping decoding of LDPC codes

EG-LDPC codes form a class of geometric LDPC codes that show good error performance with the BF decoding [3]. Various code-length EG-LDPC codes with the code rates of 0.75~0.87 are generated for this study as shown in Table 1. Note that the construction of EG-LDPC codes is very restricted in the choice of the code length and code rate. The way to construct EG-LDPC codes is described in [4] in detail. Table 1 shows that these codes have quite high row and column weights, which makes it very difficult to employ soft-decoding algorithms. The simple BF algorithm is employed, and layered mode is used for BF decoding to reduce the number of clock cycles for iteration. A brief review of the BF algorithm is introduced in advance of discussing the layered mode.
Consider a regular LDPC code that has the parity-check matrix with ‫ ܯ‬rows and ܰ columns. Assume binary phase shift keying (BPSK) modulation over an additive white Gaussian (AWGN) channel with zero mean and variance ܰ଴Τʹ . The BF decoding algorithm [5] is described as follows:

Initialization: Assign received word ‫ݕ‬௡ to the bit node ‫ݖ‬௡.

Step 1:

Compute the check-sums. If all the syndrome

bits are zero, stop decoding.

Step 2:

Find the number of non-zero check-sums for

each variable node. If the summation is larger

than the predefined threshold, flip the

corresponding variable.

Step 3:

Repeat Steps 1 and 2 until all the check-sums

are zero or the predefined maximum number

of iterations is reached

Due to the nature of the message-passing algorithm, it requires iterations to enhance the decoding performance. In conventional two-phase message-passing algorithm, variable node update and check node update operations are repeated iteratively until the decoding criterion is satisfied. To achieve higher decoding throughput, the layered decoding scheme [6] can be applied to the conventional message-passing algorithms. Furthermore, to achieve better error correcting performance, threshold adaptation [7] is applied to this layered BF.
In the layered BF algorithm, the parity-check matrix can be viewed as vertical layers. At the first iteration, all check nodes are updated before proceeding to the variable node update. Then, from the following iteration, variable and check nodes are updated simultaneously in a layered manner; i.e., if a variable node is flipped by checking the number of unsatisfied check nodes, the check nodes participating in the flipped variable node are also immediately flipped. The vertical layers are processed sequentially from the left-most to the right-most column in an iteration.

Therefore, the layered decoding can reduce the decoding time by overlapping the variable and check node processing.

2.2. Decoding of BCH codes

BCH codes are also generated for comparisons as shown in Table 1. The decoding procedure of BCH codes consists of three steps: syndrome computation, error locator polynomial computation, and finding error locations by the Chien search algorithm. At the beginning of BCH decoding, ʹ‫ ݐ‬syndromes are generated by the syndrome computation block, where ‫ ݐ‬stands for the error correcting capability. To solve the key equation, the Berlekamp-Massey algorithm (BMA) is widely used, which computes the error-locator polynomial from ʹ‫ ݐ‬syndromes. Due to the speed bottleneck in BMA, many studies have been conducted to improve the performance of error-locator polynomial computation through algorithmic transformation or architecture exploration. The reformulated inversion-less BMA (RiBMA) [8] and the simplified inversion-less BMA (SiBMA) [9] have quite regular structures, thus they are suitable for VLSI implementation. The SiBMA, which is specialized for binary BCH codes, is employed for this design. Finally, Chien search finds the error locations.

Architecture of layered BF decoder

For designing LDPC decoders, the decoding termination method is important since it affects the decoding throughput. Traditionally, there are two types of termination methods. The first one is to delay the output of the decoded word until the iteration limit even if the decoding succeeds beforehand (which we call the scheme A). The stopping criterion ࡴ‫ ்ݖ‬ൌ Ͳ is tested at every iteration and if the criterion is satisfied, the decoder remains in the idle state. In this case, the decoder produces a decoded word at every constant time interval, and hence the throughput does not vary with the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). Detailed structure of a check node processing unit is depicted in Fig. 5. The CPU consists of Ȗ-input 1-bit adder to count the number of non-zero check-sums and DETECT_FLIP block to detect whether the flipping operations occur or not. To minimize the critical path delay and area, Wallace tree and carry look-ahead adders are employed as illustrated Fig. 5. The VPU computes the check-sum by modulo-2 summation; hence it is implemented with an XOR gate tree. The check-sum is stored to the corresponding check node and is used to test the stopping criterion in the control logic; i.e., the control logic has a flipflop which keeps an intermediate result of testing the criterion, and it is initialized to zero before starting each iteration. The flip-flop is OR-ed with the check-sum and stores the result during the decoding process; hence, at the end of each iteration, we can readily know the result of testing stopping criterion. Therefore, no additional cycle is required to test the criterion.
3.2. Architecture of BCH decoder
During the BCH decoding process, many constant Galois field multiplications (CGFMs) are needed, especially in the syndrome computation and the Chien search process. In order to reduce the chip area for these blocks, we apply the iterative matching algorithm (IMA) and group matching algorithm (GMA) [10]. To further reduce the chip area, we use resource sharing between the syndrome generator and the Chien search block as described in [9]. However, when we use the resource sharing scheme, the syndrome generation and the Chien search process cannot operate simultaneously. To solve this problem and increase the decoding throughput, we employ the encoder-assisted syndrome generation scheme. The encoder is used to generate the syndrome polynomial so that consecutive BCH decoding can be achieved by overlapping syndrome generation and Chien search processes. The encoder, which is based on the linear feedback shift-register (LFSR) structure, is to compute the syndrome polynomial as described in [9]. A detailed mathematical analysis is shown in [11]. This syndrome polynomial is used to compute ʹ‫ ݐ‬syndromes.

Note that since BCH codes are systematically encoded, the error occurrence can be detected during encoding process by identifying whether the parities generated from the encoder are all zeros or not. Therefore, if there is no erroneous bit, the decoding process can be completed at the end of the encoding process.
Finally, we adopt the SiBMA for the key-equation solver block to eliminate redundant computation of RiBMA.
3.3. Parallel and time-multiplexing factors
The decision of parallel and time-multiplexing factors is important to perform hardware complexity comparisons between LDPC and BCH decoders. To achieve almost similar decoding throughput, first of all, we need to determine the minimum throughput requirement. Here, we set the requirement to 1 Gbps at the clock frequency of 100 MHz for all LDPC and BCH decoders. Moreover, we set the requirement to 2 and 3 Gbps for the (4095,3367) EG-LDPC and (4095,3375,60) BCH codes to estimate the hardware complexity for various decoding throughput. 4. CONCLUDING REMARKS
In this paper, we compared high-throughput VLSI circuits for BCH and EG-LDPC decoding. The EG-LDPC codes show similar or better error performance than the equivalent BCH codes. The VLSI design results reveal that these two decoding methods also demand comparable chip areas and

power consumption. However, we demonstrated that, if the decoding throughput is fixed, the LDPC decoders consume much smaller power (~34%) than the equivalent BCH decoders in the reasonably high SNR region where most decoders are normally operating. The reduced power consumption is due to the early termination (scheme B) of LDPC decoding in the case of a small number of errors. The advantage of the early termination can also be utilized to increase the maximum throughput of LDPC decoders in good channel conditions. Although EG-LDPC codes have restrictions in code design, this study shows that EG-LDPC decoders with the code rate of 0.75~0.85 seem very competitive with BCH algorithm based ones.
