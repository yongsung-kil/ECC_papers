Constructing Short-Length
Irregular LDPC Codes with Low Error Floor
Xia Zheng, Francis C. M. Lau, Senior Member, IEEE, and Chi K. Tse, Fellow, IEEE
Abstract—Trapping sets (TSs) are known to cause error floors
in regular and irregular low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes.
By avoiding major error-contributing TSs during the code
construction process, codes with low error floors can effectively
be built. In [14], it has been shown that TSs labeled as [𝑤; 𝑢]
are considered as being equivalent under the automorphism of
the graph and are therefore contributing equally to the error
floor. However, TSs with the same label [𝑤; 𝑢] are not identical
in general, particularly for the case of irregular LDPC codes.
In
this
paper,
we
introduce
a
parameter
𝑒
that
can
identify the number of “distinguishable” cycles in the con-
nected
subgraph
induced by
an
elementary
trapping set.
Further, we propose a code construction algorithm, namely
the Progressive-Edge-Growth Approximate-minimum-Cycle-Set-
Extrinsic-message-degree (PEG-ACSE) method, that aims to
avoid small elementary trapping sets (ETSs), particularly detri-
mental ETSs. We also develop theorems evaluating the mini-
mum possible ETSs formed by PEG construction algorithms
in general. We compare the characteristics of the codes built
using the proposed method and those built using PEG-only
or PEG-Approximate-minimum-Cycle-Extrinsic-message-degree
(PEG-ACE) methods. Results from simulations show that the
codes constructed using the proposed PEG-ACSE method pro-
duce lower error rates, particularly at the high signal-to-noise
(SNR) region, compared with codes constructed using other PEG-
based algorithms.
Index Terms—Cycle indicator, distinguishable cycles, error
floor, irregular LDPC codes, PEG-ACSE algorithm, trapping set.

Long codes with length larger than 106 are of asymptotically
good performance because there is a small chance that their
graphical representations contain many short cycles. Neverthe-
less, it is not very practical to implement such long codes in
many applications due to hardware complexity as well as the
incurred time-delay problems [5]. Short-length codes (codes
with length of several hundreds to around a few thousands) in
this case are of special interest to many applications, but their
performance may degrade a lot compared with the asymptotic
values if there exist a large number of short cycles in the
associated bipartite graphs. To construct short-length LDPC
codes with large girth (shortest cycle length), a broad class
of methods has been proposed [6], [7], among which the
progressive edge growth (PEG) [7] is one of the most effective
algorithms. However, such codes may still suffer from the
existence of error floors. Such error floors pose a great concern
for potential applications of LDPC codes such as data storage
and deep-space communications, which require bit error rate
(BER) as low as 10−15[8].
There have been prior works addressing the error-floor issue
of short-length LDPC codes [9]–[11]. For example, Di et al.
[9] have thoroughly investigated the error floor of LDPC codes
over a binary erasure channel (BEC). Moreover, they have
proposed a concept known as “stopping set”, which defines
the set of variable nodes causing the decoder to fail if all bits
corresponding to the set are lost during the transmission. The
stopping set is also characterized by each of its neighboring
check nodes having more than one connections to the set
itself. In addition, Tian et al. [10] have pointed out that some
stopping sets with small sizes will degrade the performance
of short-length LDPC codes over the AWGN channel at high
SNR. They have further discovered that short cycles with few
connections to other nodes are likely contributors to small-size
stopping sets. With the use of “approximate cycle extrinsic
message degree (ACE)” that measures the connectivity of a
cycle to all other nodes, Tian et al. have proposed an algorithm
to generate code matrices with low error floor by avoiding
short cycles with ACE below a given threshold. To design
codes with good performance at both the waterfall region
and the high SNR region, studies have been carried out on
a series of PEG-based methods [12], [13]. In [12], a PEG-
ACE construction algorithm has been proposed by using a

In a similar time frame, another concept, known as the
“trapping set (TS)”, has been proposed and exploited as an
effective tool to evaluate the error performance of regular
LDPC codes at high SNR [14], [15]. A trapping set with
the label [𝑤; 𝑢] is defined as a set of 𝑤variable nodes with
exactly 𝑢of the neighboring check nodes having odd number
of connections to the (trapping) set. Moreover, it is well known
that TSs without cycles in the induced connected subgraph do
not contribute very much to the error floor. Thus, such TSs are
not considered in the definition of TSs by some researchers
[22], [24], [26]. The value of 𝑤determines how likely all
nodes in the [𝑤; 𝑢] TS are in error1. If the value of 𝑤is
smaller, the scenario that all the 𝑤variables in the [𝑤; 𝑢] TS
are erroneous (an “error pattern”) will become more likely
to occur during the LDPC decoding process. When an error
pattern occurs, the amount of valid information that can flow
into the [𝑤; 𝑢] TS and help improving the soft values in the
TS towards the correct decision is affected by the value of 𝑢.
A small value of 𝑢will limit the amount of valid information
that flows into the TS, jeopardizing the capability of the TS
to improve the soft values towards the correct decision. As a
consequence, [𝑤; 𝑢] TSs with small 𝑤and 𝑢values, named
as the dominant (or small) TSs, may cause the decoder to
oscillate and fail to converge at the high SNR region [14],
[16].
To help predicting the error floor of regular LDPC codes
due to TSs at the high SNR region, a two-stage mechanism
has been proposed [27]–[29]. The first stage aims to identify
as many dominant TSs as possible using a heuristic search
method. During the second stage, all the TSs found are further
classified according to the label [𝑤; 𝑢] because TSs labeled
as [𝑤; 𝑢] have been considered as being equivalent under the
automorphism of the graph [14] and are therefore contributing
equally to the error floor. Afterwards, the error probability
associated with an individual representative from each [𝑤; 𝑢]-
TS class is approximated by importance sampling [27]. Then
the overall error performance of the LDPC code is derived
based on the individual error probabilities as well as the sizes
of the [𝑤; 𝑢]-TS classes.
In [17], efficient algorithms have been proposed to enumer-
ate exhaustively all stopping sets and TSs up to certain sizes.
Using the searched results as a cost function, the error floor
of any arbitrary, finite-length codes can be further optimized.
However, the algorithms are only computationally affordable
for code lengths up to around 500. If the code length is much
larger than 500, the method will become too complex. In [18],
Ivkovi´c et al. have constructed codes by taking two (or more)

In addition, it has been found that check nodes of odd
degree larger than 3 are possible but not likely to exist
in small TSs [14], [16]. In [8], [16], [19], an elementary
trapping set (ETS) has been defined as a TS in which all
check nodes are of degree one or two, and extensive computer
simulations have shown that most TSs contributing to the error
floors are elementary TSs. Moreover, Landner and Milenkovic
[16] have proposed an averaged decoding technique and have
demonstrated that the proposed technique can significantly
reduce the number of incorrectly decoded blocks in the error-
floor region of the Margulis code. However, it takes more
time for the technique to converge in the case of a correct
solution. In [8], [19], the asymptotic distributions of ETS, and
TSs in general, in random, regular and irregular binary LDPC
code ensembles have been computed. If the exact relationship
between the distribution of trapping sets and block error rate
can be found analytically, the BLER can be determined.
The error floors of structured LDPC codes have also been
investigated recently. In [20], a hardware emulation platform
has been built to decode structured LDPC codes. Using a
Reed-Solomon based LDPC code (RS-LDPC) as an example,
the emulation results have shown that when the implementa-
tion precision is low, the error floors are caused mainly by the
fixed-point decoding effects; when higher precisions are used,
the error floor is due to a class of combinatorial structures
defined as absorbing sets (ASs). In fact, an absorbing set is a
particular type of TS. In the subgraph induced by a TS, when
each variable node is connected to more check nodes with
even degrees than those with odd degrees, the TS is also an
AS [25]. Subsequently, a postprocessing approach has been
applied to the decoding of the RS-LDPC code with an aim to
eliminate the AS errors [21]. In [22], the quantization effects
on the performance of array-based LDPC codes (a class of
regular, structured LDPC codes [23]) have been analyzed. An
improved quantization scheme has been proposed to reduce
the effects of “weak” ASs, hence lowering the error floor
and allowing only “strong” ASs to dominate the error floor.
A detailed theoretical analysis of ASs for array-based LDPC
codes is further given in [24], [26], and the theoretical results
are found to be in good agreement with the experimental
results.

gorithm, namely the PEG-Approximate-minimum-Cycle-Set-
Extrinsic-message-degree (PEG-ACSE) method, that aims to
avoid small ETSs, particularly detrimental ETSs (small ETS
with 𝑢= 0, 1 and 𝑒> 1). We also develop theorems evaluating

II. REFINED [𝑤; 𝑢; 𝑒] ELEMENTARY TRAPPING SET
A. Trapping Sets with the Label [𝑤; 𝑢]
Given a trapping set (TS) labeled as [𝑤; 𝑢], recall that
𝑤denotes the number of variable nodes in the TS and 𝑢
represents the number of neighboring check nodes having odd
number of connections to the TS. As in [22], [24], [26], we do
not consider cases where the TS-induced connected subgraphs
(TS-ICSs) possess variable nodes that are not involved in the
cycles. For these cases, we shall discard the variable nodes that
are not involved in the cycle(s) and investigate only the TS-
ICSs formed by the variable nodes involved in the cycle(s). We
further categorize the TSs according to the number of cycles
contained in their induced connected subgraphs (ICSs).
Definition 1: (T-type node) In a TS-ICS, a T-type node is
defined as a node connected to two or more different cycles.
A T-type node can be a variable node or a check node.
Furthermore, research reports [8], [14], [16] and our own
simulation results have indicated that the decoding errors at
the high SNR region are caused mainly by TSs with their
ICSs containing T-type variable nodes but no T-type check
nodes. In contrast, TSs with their ICSs containing T-type check
nodes have not been found contributing significantly to the
error floor. In order to focus our attention to TSs that are
contributing to the error floor, we will only consider the ICSs
of elementary TSs (ETSs), which have been defined as [𝑤; 𝑢]
TSs with check nodes having either one or two connections
[8], [16], [19]. In other words, the ICS of an ETS cannot have
any T-type check nodes.
Also, it is known that ETSs with the same label [𝑤; 𝑢] but
with different ETS-ICS configurations can contribute very dif-
ferent error rates to the overall performance of the code at the
high SNR region. Figure 1 shows four different configurations
of the ICSs of a [9; 1] ETS. It is understood that each of these
configurations will contribute differently to the error floor.
Our simulation results have shown that for ETS-ICSs with
the same label [𝑤; 𝑢], those with more than one cycle are the
main contributors to the error floor compared with those with
only one cycle. However, determining the number of cycles
in an ETS-ICS can be very complicated and time consuming.
It will be very useful if there is a simple way of evaluating
the configuration of an ETS-ICS. In the following section, we
will introduce a new parameter 𝑒to indicate the number of
“distinguishable” cycles in an ETS-ICS.

C. Harmfulness of [𝑤; 𝑢; 𝑒] ETS-ICSs
We describe a [𝑤; 𝑢; 𝑒] ETS-ICS as “harmful” if it is likely
to give rise to errors at the high SNR region. In the following,
we compare the “harmfulness” of [𝑤; 𝑢; 𝑒] ETS-ICSs under
different scenarios. Without loss of generality, we assume that
the all-zero codeword has been transmitted. Moreover, we
consider the scenario when most, if not all, of the variable
nodes in a [𝑤; 𝑢; 𝑒] ETS-ICS are erroneously decoded (as “1”)
while most of the variable nodes external to the [𝑤; 𝑢; 𝑒] ETS-
ICS are correctly decoded. We also assume that a BP iterative
decoder is used.
First, if 𝑤increases, it will be less and less likely that all
the 𝑤variable nodes in a [𝑤; 𝑢; 𝑒] ETS-ICS are erroneously
decoded (see also footnote 1). Thus, a [𝑤1; 𝑢; 𝑒] ETS-ICS is
less harmful than a [𝑤2; 𝑢; 𝑒] if 𝑤1 > 𝑤2. Second, we consider
the scenario when 𝑢varies. With a larger value of 𝑢, there is
a larger number of external “reliable messages” coming into
the [𝑤; 𝑢; 𝑒] ETS-ICS via the singly-connected check nodes,
making it more likely that the errors in the ETS-ICS can be
corrected. Thus, a [𝑤; 𝑢1; 𝑒] ETS-ICS is less harmful than
a [𝑤; 𝑢2; 𝑒] if 𝑢1 > 𝑢2 ≥0. Third, we compare the cases
when there are more distinguishable cycles in an ETS-ICS.
In general, more distinguishable cycles in a [𝑤; 𝑢; 𝑒] ETS-ICS
implies more T-type variable nodes. Since the messages are
enhanced at the T-type variable nodes, erroneous messages
are magnified. As a consequence, the erroneous messages
in a multiple-cycle ETS-ICS will have more chances to get
enhanced as the number of distinguishable cycles increases.
Furthermore, more distinguishable cycles provide more possi-
ble paths for the messages to flow, implying that the erroneous
messages can pass within the ETS-ICS more efficiently and
making the decoder harder to converge to the correct value.
Thus, in general, a [𝑤; 𝑢; 𝑒1] ETS-ICS is more harmful than
a [𝑤; 𝑢; 𝑒2] if 𝑒1 > 𝑒2 ≥1. Figure 1 presents examples of
[9; 1; 3], [9; 1; 2] and [9; 1; 1] ETS-ICSs. It is anticipated that
the [9; 1; 3] ETS-ICS will contribute more errors to the error
floor, followed by the [9; 1; 2] ETS-ICS and then the [9; 1; 1]
ETS-ICS.
To summarize, [𝑤; 𝑢; 𝑒] ETS-ICS with small 𝑤, small 𝑢
and large 𝑒should be avoided. Moreover, for a fixed 𝑤,
[𝑤; 0; 𝑒] ETS-ICSs and [𝑤; 1; 𝑒] ETS-ICSs with 𝑒> 1 are
comparatively more harmful. In [8], small ETS-ICSs have
been referred to those ETS-ICS with small 𝑤and 𝑢. Here,
we further defined “detrimental” ETS-ICS as follows.
Definition 4: (“Detrimental” ETS-ICS) We define “detri-
mental” ETS-ICSs as [𝑤; 0; 𝑒] ETS-ICSs and [𝑤; 1; 𝑒] ETS-
ICSs with small 𝑤and 𝑒> 1.
Therefore, to build codes with low error floor, an effective
way is to constuct codes by avoiding small ETS-ICSs, par-
ticularly detrimental ETS-ICSs. In the next section, we will
propose and present a code construction method, called PEG-
ACSE algorithm, that aims to achieve this goal.

A. Cycle Set EMD (CSE)
In [10], “extrinsic message degree” (EMD) and “approxi-
mate cycle EMD” (ACE) have been defined as follows.
Definition 5: (Extrinsic message degree (EMD)) An extrin-
sic check node of a variable-node set is a check node that is
singly connected to this set, and the EMD of a variable-node
set is the number of extrinsic check nodes of the variable-node

C. PEG-ACSE Code Construction Algorithm
In the following, we present the PEG-ACSE code construc-
tion algorithm, in which cycle sets with small ACSE values
are avoided. Suppose we are given the length of an LDPC
code and also the degree distributions of the variable nodes.
We then assign each variable node with a degree randomly
picked from the variable-node degree distribution. Next, we
sort the variable nodes according to their degrees in a non-
decreasing manner, i.e., 𝑑𝑖≤𝑑𝑗for 𝑖< 𝑗. To design codes
with low error floor, variable nodes with small degrees should
be involved in cycles which are as large as possible because
these variable nodes contribute few extrinsic connections. It is
particularly crucial for degree-2 variable nodes because they
do not give rise to any extrinsic connections in cycles.

E. Examples
In this part, we give an example on estimating the smallest
possible detrimental ETS-ICSs in a PEG-only constructed
code. Furthermore, we use it to illustrate how we should set
the parameter 𝐷in our proposed PEG-ACSE construction
algorithm so as to avoid such detrimental ETS-ICSs.
Consider the case when a code with length 1008, rate
0.5 and variable-node degree distribution given by (14) is
used. We observe that during the PEG-only code-construction
process, the smallest cycle length incurred by a degree-3
variable node in ˆ𝐺3 equals 12, i.e., ˆ𝑠3 = 12. Based on
Corollary 1, the smallest possible cycle set involving all the
edges of a degree-3 variable node is given by ∣𝕊(𝑅3, 3)∣=
12/2 + (⌈12/4⌉−1) (3 −2) = 8. Applying ∣𝕊(𝑅3, 3)∣= 8 to
Corollary 2 and Corollary 4, we can further conclude that
[8; 0; 𝑒] ETS-ICSs and [8; 1; 𝑒] ETS-ICSs with 𝑒> 1 are
possible ETS-ICSs residing in the code graph.
Next, we construct the code using the proposed PEG-
ACSE (and PEG-ACE) algorithms. In the PEG-ACSE/ACE
algorithm, two parameters, namely the depth threshold 𝐷and
the ACSE/ACE-value threshold 𝜁have to be set. When we
need to evaluate cycle lengths of 𝑙cycle, 𝐷should be set to no
less than half of 𝑙cycle, i.e., 𝐷≥𝑙cycle/2. For example, if we
intend to remove the aforementioned [8; 0; 𝑒] ETS-ICSs and
[8; 1; 𝑒] ETS-ICSs with 𝑒> 1, cycle lengths up to 12 should
be considered and hence we must set 𝐷to no less than 6.
Moreover, to reduce as many small ETS-ICSs and detrimental
ETS-ICSs as possible, 𝐷and 𝜁should be set to very large
values. However, setting a larger 𝐷implies cycle sets with
larger sizes will have to be searched, resulting a higher chance
that cycles/cycle sets not being able to meet the ACSE/ACE-
value threshold 𝜁. Alternatively, 𝜁has to be reduced or else
no codes fulfilling the requirements can be found. For the
case being considered, when the depth threshold 𝐷= 10,
the largest ACE/ACSE-value threshold 𝜁that can be achieved
equals 3 if the LDPC codes are to be constructed successfully.
When 𝐷= 8, the corresponding value of 𝜁equals 4.
We denote the five codes constructed using the aforemen-
tioned algorithms and parameters by the abbreviations “PEG-
only”, “PEG-ACE-10-3”, “PEG-ACE-8-4”, “PEG-ACSE-10-
3” and “PEG-ACSE-8-4”, respectively. The code “PEG-only”,
which is exactly the same as the particularly good code
“PEGirReg504x1008” in [31], is used as a reference here for
performance comparison. For the other codes, the first two
blocks of alphabets in the abbreviations represent the code-
construction method being used; the third block denotes the
value of 𝐷; and the last digit gives the value of 𝜁. For example,
the code “PEG-ACE-10-3” is constructed based on the PEG-
ACE mechanism with 𝐷= 10 and 𝜁= 3. In Table I, the
fifth column lists some of the ETS-ICSs found in the different
codes that are actually contributing to the error floor at an
SNR of 2.8 dB. For instance, for the “PEG-only” code, it
can be seen that the smallest [𝑤; 0; 𝑒] ETS-ICS found has a
size of 13 ([13; 0; 2] ETS-ICS), which is much larger than the
possible size of the [𝑤; 0; 𝑒] ETS-ICS evaluated above, i.e.,
[8; 0; 𝑒] ETS-ICSs. On the other hand, the smallest [𝑤; 1; 𝑒]
ETS-ICSs found have sizes of 9 and 10 ([9; 1; 2] and [10; 1; 2]
ETS-ICSs), which are comparable with the possible size of
the [𝑤; 1; 𝑒] ETS-ICS evaluated above, i.e., [8; 1; 𝑒] ETS-ICSs.
As a comparison, for the “PEG-ACSE-8-4” code, the smallest
[𝑤; 0; 𝑒] ETS-ICSs found have sizes of 16 and 17 ([16; 0; 2]
and [17; 0; 3] ETS-ICSs) while the smallest [𝑤; 1; 𝑒] ETS-ICS
found has a size of 12 ([12; 1; 2] ETS-ICS). Based on the
findings, it is expected that the “PEG-ACSE-8-4” code will
achieve a lower error rate at the high SNR region compared
with the “PEG-only” code.

V. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we have refined the label of an elemen-
tary trapping sets (ETS) to [𝑤; 𝑢; 𝑒], in which 𝑒represents
the number of “distinguishable” cycles in the ETS-induced
connected subgraph (ETS-ICS). We have further proposed
a new code construction algorithm, namely the PEG-ACSE
algorithm, the parameters of which can be tuned to avoid
certain harmful ETSs. Simulation results have shown that the
codes constructed using the proposed PEG-ACSE method,
compared with those built using other PEG-based methods,
can provide the lowest error floors at the high SNR region.
A further look into the block errors at the high SNR region
reviews that almost all errors are caused by ETSs. In addition,
approximately 90% of the errors are caused by multiple-cycle
[𝑤; 𝑢; 𝑒] ETSs, i.e., [𝑤; 𝑢; 𝑒] ETSs with 𝑒> 1. We have also
found that detrimental [𝑤; 𝑢; 𝑒] ETS-ICSs can be successfully
avoided by using the proposed PEG-ACSE method.
Finally, tens of millions of received codes have to be
decoded in order to arrive at a reasonably reliable BER/BLER
performance at the high SNR region (no larger than 3 dB).
If the SNR is to be increased further, we will not able
to evaluate the code performance using Monte Carlo (MC)
simulation due to the extremely low error rate and hence
the prohibitive amount of simulation time needed to arrive
at a meaningful error rate. To address the aforementioned
issue, we are attempting to evaluate irregular LDPC code
performance at the high SNR region using the importance
sampling approach in conjunction with ETSs identification,
and we will disseminate the results in another publication.
