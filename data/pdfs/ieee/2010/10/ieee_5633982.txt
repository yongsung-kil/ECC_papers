An Improved Variable Length Coding Scheme Using Structured LDPC Codes
Ming Jiang, Chen Wang, Yuan Zhang and Chunming Zhao National Mobile Communications Research Laboratory, Southeast University, Nanjing, P. R. China
Email: {jiang ming, wangchen, y.zhang, cmzhao}@seu.edu.cn

Abstract— This paper proposes an improved coding scheme for various transmission lengths by combining shortening and puncturing with the conventional lifting method. Considering both of cycle conditions and ACE spectrum, we design the basematrix in the framework of PEG construction. Only one basematrix is required to support a wide rage of coding lengths for our proposed scheme, where the lifting factors are set in nonuniformly distributed values. The transmission configurations with arbitrary lengths within the coding range can be achieved by shortening and puncturing. Moreover, our proposed scheme provides better error performance than the traditional structured LDPC codes lifted by many different factors, when the shortening and puncturing patters are optimally selected by numerical methods.
I. INTRODUCTION
The family of structured low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes has been an important focus in the design and application of LDPC codes in recent years. Well-structured LDPC codes are able to achieve the near Shannon-limit performance with low encoding/decoding complexity. Therefore, these structured LDPC codes have already been widely utilized in current communications standards, such as IEEE 802.16e [1] and IEEE 802.11n [2]. With the purpose of supporting various types of service in wireless communications systems, the structured LDPC codes should be flexibly configured with different coding lengths and rates. There have been some literatures, i.e. [3] and [4], regarding the construction methods of rate-compatible structured LDPC codes with variable lengths. In this paper, we propose an improved coding scheme for the structured LDPC codes to construct codes with flexible lengths.
A structured LDPC code is usually defined by a base-matrix of a relatively small size. The entries of a base-matrix are mapped to all-zero matrices or circulant permutation matrices of size Z × Z respectively [5], [6]. The mapping procedure from base-matrix to parity-check matrix of structured LDPC codes is called expansion or lifting and the parameter Z is defined as expansion factor or lifting factor. In order to generate a sequence of structured LDPC codes with different coding lengths, the base-matrix should be designed to be adaptable to different lifting factors. Floor-lifting [6] and module-lifting [7] procedures are usually used to guarantee the girths of the base-matrices lifted by a series of lifting factors. For a much wider range of block lengths, there is an obvious increase in the number of lifting factors, so called lifting degree [6]. Then

it actually becomes a difficult issue to design a single basematrix associated with a considerable number of lifting factors. Besides, the shortening and puncturing processes are always employed to support the transmission of an arbitrary length between the discretized coding lengths lifted by successive factors. Hence, another important problem to be solved is the optimization of the shortening and puncturing patterns for the structured LDPC codes.
In this paper, we present a construction method of structured LDPC codes, where a variable step-size strategy is adopted in the intervals of lifting factors for a wider coding range and smaller lifting degrees. Due to fewer lifting factors considered, the construction of the base-matrix is effectively simplified. Moreover, we also optimize the shortening and puncturing patterns for the structured LDPC codes. The transmission length can be continuously configured in the coding range without noticeable performance losses.
This paper is organized as follows. Section II describes the proposed construction of base-matrix associated with different lifting factors. The optimization of the transmission configuration with an arbitrary length by shortening and puncturing will be presented in section III. In section IV, based on the coding parameters of LDPC codes in [1], an example of the structured LDPC codes is presented, which can support a much wider coding range and achieve arbitrary transmission lengths. Section V provides comparison results between our proposed coding scheme and the LDPC codes used in 802.16e system. Section VI concludes this paper.

In order to maintain low encoding complexity, the dual-diagonal and “a-0-a” structures [1] [2] are also utilized for the parity-bits.
III. SHORTENING AND PUNCTURING OF BASE-MATRIX
Due to the difficulties in the optimization on the basis of lifting with a large number of different factors, we assume that one base-matrix only supports a finite number of lifting factors within a relatively limited range of coding lengths from nb × Z1 to nb × ZL. Further requirements for transmission with arbitrary lengths can not be supported simply by lifting from base-matrix. Shortening or puncturing methods, that are usually used to construct rate compatible LDPC codes [3] [4], can also change the transmission lengths of LDPC codes from the original coding length. To deal with this problem, we resort to the shortening and puncturing methods to achieve an arbitrary transmission length between the coding lengths lifted by two successive lifting factors Zl and Zl−1.
Shortening is in fact also a form of data puncturing, which blocks out some information bits before transmission. The values and positions of the shortened bits, which are known to both transmitter and receiver, can be set to 0 or 1 before encoding. At the receiver, the shortened information bits are decoded as the variable nodes with infinite reliabilities on the tanner graph [4] [11]. Because of the omission of the shortened bits, there will be both rate reduction and length decrease during the transmission. On the other hand, puncturing is performed both on the information bits and the parity bits after encoding. The values of the punctured bits are unknown to the receiver and are decoded as erasure errors, although the punctured positions are known to both sides. Since the transmission length is smaller than that of the original codeword, a higher rate can be achieved by puncturing. As far as we can see, with the utilization of shortening and puncturing, we can flexibly configure the lengths for the transmission of LDPC codes, while the original coding rates remain the same.
For structured LDPC codes, the variable nodes belonging to the same column in the base-matrix show similar behaviors in iterative decoding. As a result, we only need to consider a specified pattern of columns in the base-matrix, which indicate the bits to be shortened or punctured. Assume that nb subblocks of bits, V1, V2, ..., Vnb , correspond to all columns in the base-matrix, where the first kb sub-blocks correspond to the kb × Zl information bits. At least ns = Ns/Zl and np = Np/Zl sub-blocks should be selected, if there are Ns and Np bits to be shortened and punctured, respectively. All bits in the first ns − 1 and np − 1 sub-blocks are shortened and punctured, while the Ns − (ns − 1) × Zl and Np − (np − 1) × Zl bits will be selected from the ns-th and np-th sub-block, respectively. Since different bits in the same sub-block are equivalent in shortening or puncturing, we just sequentially select the remaining bits in the ns-th and np-th sub-blocks. Arbitrary transmission lengths W ∈ (nb × Zl−1, nb × Zl) can be achieved by shortening and puncturing on the LDPC codes with the base-matrix Hlb. Firstly, Ns bits to be shortened are selected from the information bits, which are specified by the ns sub-blocks from V1, V2, ..., Vkb . Then, we determine the Np = W − Ns bits to be punctured from the remaining nb − ns sub-blocks. The priority sequence of the sub-blocks selected to be shortened and punctured can be described by S = (si, i = 1, 2, ..., ns) and P = (pi, i = 1, 2, ..., np), where 1 ≤ si ≤ kb and 1 ≤ pi ≤ nb, pi ∈/ S are the indices of the sub-blocks. Since matrices Hbl are lifted from the same base-matrix HbL with different lifting factors, the degree distributions and the positions of nonnegative entries in the base-matrices are the same. Hence, we can use a fixed shortening and puncturing patterns S and P for all the lifted base-matrices.
After shortening and puncturing, not only will the original optimized degree distribution for the matrix HLb be changed, but also there will be trapping sets caused by the arbitrary puncturing. We should optimize the patterns S and P based on the modified degree distribution in order to guarantee the error performance in the water-fall region. Moreover, the k-step (k > 1) recoverable variable nodes [12] by puncturing should also be avoided as far as possible to keep the performance in the error-floor region. In the traditional density evolution (DE) [13], only the degree distributions of LDPC code ensembles are considered, while the detailed connection patterns between the variable nodes with different weights are ignored due to the large size of the parity-check matrix H. Fortunately, there are only nb columns and mb rows in the base-matrix where nb and mb are relative small. Thus, we can utilize a more precise method to analyze the base-matrices of the structured LDPC codes.
The basic idea of the analysis based on the structured LDPC codes is to map the base-matrix into a small Tanner graph or a simple protograph [14] without multi-edges. The variable nodes and the check nodes in protograph correspond to the nb columns and mb rows in the base-matrix, respectively. Based on the mapping of protograph, the PEXIT analysis [15] can be utilized to calculate the approximate decoding thresholds of the structured LDPC codes with different shortening and puncturing patterns. The PEXIT analysis is not only limited simply to the degree distributions but also the detailed connections between the variable nodes to the check nodes in protograph. Moreover, the punctured nodes are naturally included in the PEXIT. Since the shortened nodes are known and make no contributions to the iterative decoding, they can be directly removed from the protograph. For a small protograph, the complexity of the PEXIT is much lower than the traditional DE, so we can obtain the optimum patterns of shortening and puncturing without many difficulties.

IV. A CONSTRUCTION EXAMPLE
In order to verify the effectiveness of our proposed coding scheme, this section presents an example of constructing LDPC codes with optimized shortening and puncturing patterns. The coding lengths of the LDPC codes in IEEE 802.16e [1] cover the range from 576 to 2304, where nb = 24 and Zl = 24 + 4(l − 1), l = 1, 2, ..., L, L = 19. For each coding rate, the base-matrix corresponding to the lifting factor ZL is specified in the protocol and the other base-matrices can be generated according to (3). Aiming at supporting a wide range of coding lengths and many lifting factors, it is hard to perform the optimization of base matrix HbL. For some coding lengths and rates, there even exist cycles of length four in the lifted matrix Hbl , which may cause considerable performance degradations.
We propose to construct the base-matrix in terms of a series of lifting factors separated by non-uniform intervals, which can cover a wider range of coding lengths and take less lifting factors into consideration. Based on the same parameter nb = 24, the coding lengths cover the range from 576 to 4608 according to 7 lifting factors chosen as follows
(Zi, i = 1, 2, ..., 7) = (24, 36, 48, 72, 96, 144, 192). (6)
Notice that 4608 is twice the longest length 2304 in IEEE 802.16e. The intervals between the different lifting factors increase from 12 to 48. Since there are only 7 different lifting factors, we can easily construct the base-matrix by the optimized criterion [9]. The gaps between the coding lengths of different base-matrices increase with the increasing values of the lifting factors. However, we are still able to achieve an arbitrary length of transmission by using puncturing/shortening technology, since the pruned length of each sub-block also increases. For example, the maximum gap of 24 × 48 bits between the coding length 24 × 96 and 24 × 144 can be covered by shortening and puncturing 4 sub-blocks, respectively.
Since only 7 lifting factors are considered in the optimized constructions, we can easily design the base-matrix supporting 7 different coding lengths in terms of the joint criterion of girth conditions and ACE spectrum [9]. In order to be compatible with the 802.16e standard, we design the base-matrix according to the same parameters and degree distributions of the LDPC codes in [1]. VI. CONCLUSION
We propose an improved coding scheme for the structured LDPC codes, which can support various coding lengths by different lifting factors and pruning methods. Using the modified PEG construction and taking both the cycle conditions and the ACE spectrum into consideration, we design a unique basematrix according to only a few lifting factors separated by nonuniform intervals. The dynamic range of coding lengths from 576 to 4608 is twice as wide as that of the conventional LDPC codes in IEEE 802.16e. Arbitrary transmission length between 576 and 4608 can be achieved by shortening and puncturing. Based on the shortening and puncturing pattern optimized by the PEXIT analysis, the proposed coding scheme obtains a noticeable coding gain over the traditional structured LDPC codes with different lifting factors.
