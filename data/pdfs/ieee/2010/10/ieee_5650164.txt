Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes were first invented by Gallager in early 1960s [1]. Although they were ignored for about thirty years, probably except the graph representation by Tanner in 1981 [2], the rediscovery of LDPC codes in 1990s [3] has sparkled a flurry of interests in the related research. LDPC codes can achieve near-shannon-limit performance with iterative massage-passing decoding [4] and sufficiently long block length. However, most of the LDPC codes are of no specific construction. Quasi-cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes [5]–[11], whose parity-check matrices are composed of permutation matrices, can be encoded efficiently with simple feedback shift-registers. The algebraic structure of QC-LDPC codes allows for an efficient high-speed very-large-scaleintegration (VLSI) implementation. With QC-LDPC codes as the counterpart, LDPC convolutional codes (LDPC-CCs) [10]– [17] are convolutional codes with sparse parity-check matrices. By using a modified iterative message-passing algorithm,

parity-check matrices for general LDPC-CCs may cause some performance loss. Therefore, we also propose a systematic way of removing this kind of length-8 cycles. From simulation results, the codes obtained by our construction have better BER performance and a lower error-floor than previously constructed codes at high SNRs. By applying the cycleremoval procedure, we can further improve the performance.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Basics of LDPC-CCs together with the previous construction are described in Section II. Section III presents the new construction of irregular LDPC-CCs, where both upper and lower bounds on the free distance are derived as well. We also provide conditions for the constructed codes to have a girth of at least 8. Section IV gives a method to remove length-8 cycles coming from the pairs of binomial terms in the parity-check matrices. Simulation results are given in Section V for performance verification. Finally, the work is concluded in Section VI.

Similar to LDPC block codes, a Tanner graph based on the parity-check matrix can be obtained for LDPC-CCs. It is also a bipartite graph with two sets of nodes representing check nodes and variable nodes. Since the scalar parity-check matrix of an LDPC-CC is infinite and repeated, the corresponding Tanner graph extends infinitely with repeated nodes in the same structure.
The previous algebraic construction of (s, t)-irregular LDPC-CCs in [9], [10] is given as follows. For a prime m, the integers in {0, 1, . . . , m−1} form a field GF(m) under the modulo-m addition and multiplication. Given nonzero integers a, b ∈ GF(m), with the order of a equal to t and the order of b equal to s, the s × t parity-check matrix of the construction is given in (1).
For the (s, t)-irregular LDPC-CCs obtained by (1), the upper bound on the free distance is limited. For example, the upper bound on the free distance for the previously constructed (5, t)-irregular LDCP-CCs in [10] is only equal to 48. Besides, the lower bound on the free distance has not been discussed. Therefore, it is possible to obtain codes with small free distance based on the previous construction. Also there is no guarantee that the codes based on the construction in (1) always have a girth of at least 8, and hence they may have cycles of length 6.

We then discuss lower bounds on the free distance. The free distance is equal to the smallest number of dependent columns of the scalar matrix H corresponding to H(D). Due to the property of binomials in (2), the 1’s of the dependent columns in the last two row-blocks of H must form disjoint cycles. And the corresponding 1’s in the other row-blocks must be cancelled out in order to make these columns dependent. So we can first find the position of cycles in the last two row-blocks and then check the corresponding 1’s in the other row-blocks.

VI. CONCLUSION In this paper, we have proposed a new construction of (s, t)-irregular LDPC-CCs, which is done by replacing some monomials in the parity-check matrices by binomial terms. Based on our construction, not only the resulting LDPCCCs can have a larger upper bound on the free distance, but also a lower bound on the free distance can be obtained. Furthermore, codes of girth at least 8 can be guaranteed. Simulation results show that our new codes can have a larger coding gain and a lower error-floor than previously constructed codes. Furthermore, we can improve the performance of our irregular codes by the proposed procedure to remove length-8 cycles coming from the pairs of binomial terms.
