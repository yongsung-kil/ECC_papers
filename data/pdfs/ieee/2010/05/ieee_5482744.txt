Low-Power Embedded LDPC-H.264 Joint Decoding Architecture Based on Unequal Error Protection
Yoon Seok Yang and Gwan Choi Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering Texas A&M University, College Station, Texas 77840
Email: ysyang14@tamu.edu,gchoi@ece.tamu.edu

Abstract—This paper presents a low-power embedded LDPCH.264 decoding architecture to lower the baseband energy consumption of a channel decoder using joint source decoding and dynamic voltage and frequency scaling (DVFS). This method is developed for H.264 layered (data partitioned) video transmission over Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) codec. We exploit the fact that not all transmitted data require the same level of error protection; this is known as unequal error protection (UEP). In particular, we use variable iteration LDPC decoding along with H.264 data partitioning (DP). In this scheme, we determine and use the lowest values of iterations that are needed by the decoder to achieve pre-specified image qualities at the receiver, and apply DVFS to minimize power.
I. INTRODUCTION
The increasing capabilities of mobile multimedia devices and services demand improved energy efficiency and error tolerance. We address this using UEP which changes the distribution of errors without requiring additional hardware resources. A UEP scheme uses prioritized data information; the aim is to reduce errors in more important data. This is in contrast to a equal error protection (EEP) scheme that all received frames are treated equally. Unequal error protection based on low-density parity-check (LDPC) code has been proposed in [1] and [2].
Currently, layered video coding [3] is supported by most video compression standards, such as MPEG-2, MPEG-4 and H.264 [4]. Data partitioning (DP) is one of the techniques for layered coding. DP partitions network abstraction layer (NAL) units into three priority groups (A, B, and C) and each priority group contains certain coded video elements of similar importance to the reconstructed video quality. In [5], the use of DP in H.264/AVC mobile communication yields a lower percentage of lost frames. An unequal error protection algorithm based on LDPC forward error correcting (FEC) and H.264/AVC data partitioning has been proposed in [6]. In that paper, high priority data coded with lower rate to reduce the chances of frame errors.
LDPC code is an error correcting block code originally proposed by Gallager [7] in the 1960s and rediscovered in late 1990s [8]. Typically its decoding involves an iterative two-phase message passing algorithm (TPMP), known as the belief-propagation (BP) algorithm or min-sum algorithm [9]. This algorithm is composed of iteration updates of the parity and data information defined as check-nodes and variablenodes in a two phase schedule. The min-sum algorithm can

reduce decoding complexity in implementation; however, it degrades the coding performance. The improved BP based algorithm, offset-min-sum [10], alleviates this performance degradation. This paper is based on the VLSI design of an LDPC decoder using the offset-min-sum algorithm [11].
We compose a UEP-LDPC decoding architecture using DVFS technique, a method through which a variable amount of energy is allocated to perform each task. It is employed for adjusting frequency (F reqLDP C ) and voltage (VDDL) to supply optimal power to the LDPC decoder. Power consumption of a digital CMOS circuit is

C. Energy Minimization by using DVFS
Fig. 3 represents the hardware implementation of the LDPC decoder and DVFS controller. The offset-min-sum LDPC decoder that we use in this paper is based on [11]. The check node units (CNU1-61) take the variable-node message associated with each check-node serially and compute the least magnitude of all variable-node messages. They are stored in Final States (FS) buffers. Check-node messages to each associated variable-node are sent out serially again, and they are selected by index and sign comparison, where the signs of R messages are stored in a sign FIFO. As soon as checknode messages to the first connected variable-node are ready, the corresponding P sum message can be computed. The Q message is then ready for check-node message processing of the next iteration.
The searched sets of minimum iterations for priority frame A, B, and C are stored in a look-up table (priority information

block in Fig. 3) for the energy minimization scheme in a realtime system. Frequency information is calculated from the table and stored into a frequency selector register in the DVFS controller. The frequency selector generates a Freq sel signal based on the priority information. When the selection is done, the clock divider, such as a phase-loop locker (PLL) [13], divides the fast system clock (f sctr) into slower clock signals (fdec,fctr) according to the frequency selection register. fdec clocks the decoder for the current frame, and fctr is sent to the variable supply voltage logic, as shown in Fig. 1 [12].

IV. CONCLUSIONS
This paper described a low-power embedded LDPC-H.264 joint decoding method using an unequal error protection (UEP) and dynamic voltage frequency scaling (DVFS). The design is developed for video mobile applications over an LDPC decoder and is configured to search optimal tradeoffs between energy consumption and performance. As a result, our design minimizes overall energy dissipation and compromises picture quality negligibly.
