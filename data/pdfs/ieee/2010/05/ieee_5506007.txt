A Novel Partially Parallel Architecture for High-throughput LDPC Decoder for DVB-S2

Seok-Min Kim, Chang-Soo Park, and Sun-Young Hwang, Member, IEEE

Abstract — This paper proposes a high-throughput LDPC (Low-Density Parity-Check) decoder architecture for DVB-S2, the second generation standard for European satellite digital video broadcasting system adopted for HDTV services.
In the proposed decoder architecture, a unified processing module, B/CFM (Bitnode/Checknode Functional Module), is designed, which performs the computations both at bitnodes and at checknodes for maximal sharing of hardware resource. It performs the computations at bitnodes clustered into a group and at checknodes clustered also into a group sequentially. The B/CFMs replace the BFMs (Bitnode Functional Module) and CFMs (Checknode Functional Module) used in previous architecture, thus resulting to an increase in throughput. To support parallel data accesses by these B/CFMs, data aligner is designed and placed in front of input port of each memory bank. Experimental results of the proposed architecture exhibit the throughput of 1,020 Mbps, 95.8% improvements over previous architecture, with 25.2% increase in area1.
Index Terms — LDPC (Low-Density Parity-Check) codes, DVB-S2, HDTV, bitnodes, checknodes.
I. INTRODUCTION
ETSI (European Telecommunications Standards Institute) introduced DVB-S2 standard, the second generation standard for European satellite broadcasting services, in 2003 [1], [2]. Many HDTV cannels are served worldwide by DVB-S2 standard. LDPC codes which are concatenated with BCH (Bose-Chaudhuri-Hocquenghem) codes are adopted with high-order modulation scheme as a powerful FEC (Forward Error Correcting) structure of DVB-S2. Gallager proposed LDPC codes in 1962. They have remarkable BER (Bit Error Rate) performance that is close to Shannon limit [3]. LDPC code is defined by a sparse parity check matrix H, which can be modeled as a Tanner graph [4] where n bitnodes and (n – k) checknodes are connected by edges. The bitnodes (checknodes) in a Tanner graph are mapped to columns (rows) of matrix H. A column and a row of element 1 in matrix H are connected by an edge in a Tanner graph. Fig. 1 shows a Tanner graph for DVB-S2 LDPC codes. Bitnodes consist of information

and parity bitnodes; information bitnodes are randomly connected by edges with checknodes by features of matrix H specified in DVB-S2 standard [1]. Depending on coderate, representing the ratio of information bitnode size to codeword size, sizes of information bitnodes, parity bitnodes and checknodes vary. LDPC codes can be decoded by implementing a message passing algorithm such as belief propagation [5], and messages transmitted iteratively between bitnodes and checknodes contain the probabilities corresponding to node values 0 and 1. A message expressed in a LLR (Log-Likelihood Ratio) value contains both probabilities. Use of LLR values reduces the number of overall computations and minimizes the memories for storing messages.
LDPC decoder architectures are generally classified into serial, parallel and partially parallel architectures [6]. As computations at bitnodes or checknodes of a Tanner graph are performed sequentially in serial decoder architecture, minimal number of functional modules is required. This architecture occupies the least area, but has low-throughput by nature [7]. Owing to such low-throughput property, serial decoder architecture cannot satisfy minimum throughput for high-definition broadcasting serviced in DVB-S2, therefore may not be suitable for DVB-S2 decoder architecture. Parallel decoder architecture has functional modules as the same numbers of bitnodes and checknodes, and message computations at bitnodes or checknodes are performed simultaneously. Such an architecture performs decoding in shorter time, but occupies huge area [8], [9]. A partially parallel architecture is therefore the reasonable choice in terms of area and throughput. The architectures proposed by Dielissen [10] and Kienle [11] belong to this class.
DVB-S2 can provide users with interactive environment through full-duplex data communication. For satisfying latency requirements, fast data processing must be performed in receiver. We propose a novel partially parallel decoder architecture suited to DVB-S2 LDPC codes. The proposed architecture exhibits much higher throughput when compared with previous architectures.
This paper is organized as follows: The properties of parity check matrix H for DVB-S2 LDPC codes are described in Section II. The proposed decoder architecture is presented in Section III. Section IV shows experimental results acquired by measuring synthesized results of the proposed architecture. Section V draws conclusion of this paper.

II. DVB-S2 LDPC CODES
LDPC codes adopted as an inner coding scheme of DVBS2 are capable of transmitting data closely to theoretical limit (Shannon limit). DVB-S2 LDPC codes belong to the class of IRA (Irregular Repeat-Accumulate) codes, since matrix H of the LDPC codes consists of sub-matrices whose elements are shifted cyclically. DVB-S2 LDPC encoder generates a parity block p = (p0, p1, … , pn-k-1) of size (n – k) from an information block i = (i0, i1, … , ik-1) of size k and constructs c = (i0, i1, … , ik-1, p0, p1, … , pn-k-1) of size n by concatenating these information and parity blocks. III. PROPOSED DECODER ARCHITECTURE
This section presents the structures of the B/CFM and memory bank designed to implement a high-throughput LDPC decoder in small area. The computations required at the B/CFM are performed using cascaded adders operating in a pipelined fashion. The number of required adders is variable depending on whether for bitnode or checknode. For maximal sharing of these adders, B/CFM that performs computations both at bitnodes and at checknodes is designed. To get higher throughput, more processing modules in decoder are required. These processing modules have to access memories to get messages at the same time, which may cause memory conflicts. To resolve memory access conflicts, we propose a new memory bank structure together with data aligner placed in front of each input port.
A. Node Clustering Scheme Exploiting the Properties of Matrix H
Matrix H consists of two sub-matrices A and B. Columns of matrix A are mapped to information bitnodes and columns of matrix B are mapped to parity bitnodes. Matrix A has elements 1 at the rows specified by xt introduced in Section II. The elements on xt -th rows, (360 × t)-th columns in matrix A are 1, where t = 0, 1, 2, … , (k / 360) – 1. The next 359 columns from (360 × t)-th column have elements 1 at {xt + m mod 360 × q} mod (n – k)-th rows, where m represents the column number. The values of q and xt are determined by the coderate of the LDPC codes. For the code whose coderate is 1/2, q is 90 and the values of xt are shown in Fig. 3.

B. Proposed Processing Module for Low-Area Implementation
Bitnodes and checknodes on the Tanner graph are clustered into 180 and 90 node groups, respectively. Each of these groups consists of a bunch of bitnodes and checknodes. The number of constituent bitnodes and checknodes in a group is determined by connecting edges of the Tanner graph. In previous architectures, a bitnode and a checknode groups are assigned to a BFM and a CFM, respectively [13]. A BFM is connected to 2, 3, or 8 CFMs, and a CFM is connected to 7 BFMs according to the clustered Tanner graph. There are 3 types of BFMs, each of which has different numbers of adders depending on the connections to CFMs.
Fig. 5 shows the structure of the cascaded adders of the largest BFM. Receiving messages which contain LLR values computed at CFMs as input values, the cascaded adders in a BFM compute new LLR values using a channel LLR value stored at a memory bank. A CFM also contains cascaded adders, which are different number from that of a BFM. These cascaded adders occupy dominant area both in the BFM and in the CFM.

We designed a unified processing module B/CFM by merging a BFM and a CFM so that they can share the cascaded adders. This reduces chip area without performance degradation. Fig. 6 shows the designed B/CFM. The B/CFM has separate input ports for bitnodes and checknodes, and has also separate ports for outputs. To compute new LLR values as bitnodes (checknodes) at a B/CFM, it receives messages through input ports for bitnodes (checknodes). The proper LLR messages are selected through multiplexers placed in front of the cascaded adders. The B/CFM takes larger cascaded adders between those in a BFM and in a CFM to guarantee correct results when the B/CFM performs computations both for bitnodes and for checknodes.
C. Memory Access Scheme for High-throughput
In a LDPC decoder, messages are transmitted repeatedly between bitnode and checknode groups that are connected by edges on clustered Tanner graph. Edges on the clustered Tanner graph can be implemented as routing logic and memory banks where LLR values are stored. When computations at bitnodes are performed, B/CFMs read LLR values that are stored at memory banks at rising edge of a clock cycle, and compute new LLR values. The values are transmitted to selection network, which distributes the messages to memory banks. The selection network chooses different paths depending on whether messages are sent from bitnodes to checknodes or from checknodes to bitnodes. Messages arrived at the memory banks are stored sequentially at falling edges of 180 consecutive clock cycles.
Fig. 7 shows message flow when B/CFMs operate as bitnodes. It is a key block of the proposed decoder architecture. Data aligners are placed in front of input ports of memory banks for proper message transmission. In the figure, numbers in the memory banks represent indices of the B/CFMs computing the LLR values as checknodes in previous step.

As shown in the figure, two B/CFMs are computing concurrently, while only one module is computing in the previous architecture [13]. Thus throughput can be increased. To support this concurrent computation, memory banks are designed for two B/CFMs to access two LLR values at a memory bank simultaneously. Each entry in the memory banks holds two LLR values, it makes possible for B/CFMs to access two LLR values concurrently using 2-port memories instead of 4-port memories.
On the clustered Tanner graph bitnodes belonging to a bitnode group send messages to the checknodes belonging to a checknode group connected by an edge according to the offsets derived by matrix H. The memory banks are used to store messages transmitted from bitnodes to checknodes and from checknodes to bitnodes, alternatingly. There are differences in the derived offsets between the addresses where the LLR values are stored at a memory bank depending on whether computations are performed at bitnodes or at checknodes. Data aligner is employed to store the received messages at the correct locations when the offset is odd. Fig. 8 shows the structure of the data aligner.

D. Overall Decoder Architecture
Fig. 9 shows the overall architecture of the proposed decoder. This figure explicitly shows the main constituent block (hereinafter will be refered to as a B/CFM plane), where two B/CFMs compute for one bitnode group and one checknode group. The proposed decoder contains 180 B/CFM planes. LLR values computed at B/CFMs are transmitted to selection network, which route the received messages to other planes for computation in next step. The decoder for LDPC codes of 1/2 coderate contains 180 B/CFMs together with 180 BFMs. The B/CFMs compute LLR values for 90 information bitnode groups and 90 checknode groups, and 180 BFMs compute LLR values for 90 parity bitnode groups. Computations at a B/CFM as a BFM are as follows: In the first clock cycle for decoding LDPC codes, the 0-th B/CFM receives LLR values which were computed at the 54-th, 9318th, 14392-nd, 27561-st, 26909-th, 10219-th, 2534-th, and 8597-th checknodes according to x values specified in the DVB-S2 standard, and computes new LLR values as must be done at the 0-th bitnode of the Tanner graph. Because all the checknodes are clustered into 90 checknode groups, these received values are the results computed by the 54-th, 48-th, 82-nd, 21-st, 89-th, 49-th, 14-th and 47-th B/CFMs in previous step. The 1-st B/CFM computes new LLR values as must be done at the 1-st bitnode using the results computed at the 144-th, 9408-th, 14482-nd, 27651-st, 26999-th, 10309-th, 2624-th, and 8687-th checknodes. These computations can be done concurrently at the two B/CFMs. In the second clock cycle, the 0-th B/CFM computes LLR values as must be done

V. CONCLUSION
This paper proposes a high-throughput LDPC decoder architecture for DVB-S2. Exploiting the similarity of the computations performed in a BFM and in a CFM, a BFM and a CFM were merged into a B/CFM in the proposed decoder for area reduction. By designing and placing data aligner in front of write port of a 2-port memory, two concatenated messages can be written on memory. These concatenated messages are read and fed to two B/CFMs where computations as a BFM or a CFM are performed. This feature makes it possible to process two messages at the same time and to improve throughput without significant area increase.
