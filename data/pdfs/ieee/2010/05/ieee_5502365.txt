Design of Efficiently-Encodable Generalized LDPC Codes

Tingjun Xie, Stephen G. Wilson Charles L. Brown Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering,
University of Virginia, Charlottesville, VA, U.S.A E-mail: {tx4c, sgw}@virginia.edu

Abstract—We propose two classes of efficiently-encodable GLDPC (EE-GLDPC) codes by generalizing the ideas of IRA and EERC codes. These GLDPC codes have performance as good as ordinary GLDPC codes. Moreover, another advantage of these EE-GLDPC codes is that they are naturally suitable for puncturing. Simulation shows that, at code length around 600, the punctured EE-GLDPC codes can outperform optimized irregular LDPC codes by 0.4-0.5 dB at BER=10−6, making them good candidates when designing error correcting codes at shortto-medium code lengths.
Keywords: Efficient encoding, generalized LDPC (GLDPC) code, puncturing
I. INTRODUCTION
The rediscovery of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] is one of the most significant events in modern coding era. As a subclass of linear block codes, LDPC codes can be specified either by a sparse parity-check matrix H or the Tanner graph. In LDPC codes, the bits involved in the each row of H should satisfy a single-parity check (SPC) constraint, which means in the Tanner graph each check node is imposing the SPC constraint on the adjacent variable nodes. LDPC codes have shown their remarkable performance under iterative decoding algorithms: when carefully designed, LDPC codes can exhibit capacity-approaching performance on the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel at very long code length [2].
In the Tanner graph, if some linear code C(n, k) other than a SPC code is used as the check node constraint, we obtain Generalized LDPC (GLDPC) codes [3], and the C(n, k) is usually called the component code or constituent code. Reflected in the H matrix, H should have a row-weight n, and the vector formed by all the bits in each row should be a codeword of C(n, k). From the definition we know that LDPC codes can be regarded as a special class of GLDPC codes. Well-designed GLDPC codes have many merits such as good minimum distance [4], low error floor [5], and they can show equivalent or better performance than LDPC codes under certain circumstances.
Finding efficiently-encodable codes is also an important issue in practice. The brute-force encoding method has the complexity O(N 2), where N is the code length, while some carefully designed codes enjoy linear-time encoding complexity. For GLDPC codes, some earlier papers on GLDPC codes [4][6] do not focus on efficient encoding. In order to encode these ordinary GLDPC codes, after the column-expansions

of adjacency matrix of GLDPC code to single parity-check matrix, the technique in [7] can be used, which can be tricky for short and moderate-length codes. Based on the principle in [7], Zhang et al [8] proposed a class of GLDPC codes with approximate upper triangular structure, which can be easily encoded using the technique in [7]. Quasi-cyclic GLDPC codes are also proposed in [5], where both adjacency matrix and expanded single parity-check matrix have quasi-cyclic structure, and quasi-cyclic GLDPC codes can be efficiently encoded using shift registers. Also in [9] the authors devised very low-rate GLDPC codes using first-order Reed-Muller (RM) codes; a very large portion of degree-1 parity bits in the code make encoding much simpler.
In wireless communications, the time-varying channel makes rate-compatible (RC) data transmission desirable. In RC codes, the bits of higher-rate codes are contained in lowerrate codes. When the channel condition becomes worse and the receiver fails to decode, the additional redundant bits can be sent incrementally to the receiver until the whole codeword can be recovered. Due to these reasons, RC codes are very flexible and efficient in wireless environments. The RC-LDPC codes are widely studied in existing literature, whereas there are are few studies on RC-GLDPC codes.
In this paper, inspired by the structures of irregular repeataccumulate (IRA) codes [10] and efficiently-encodable ratecompatible (EERC) codes [11], we propose two classes of efficiently-encodable GLDPC (EE-GLDPC) codes: IRA-like GLDPC codes and EERC-like GLDPC codes. The principles of IRA and EERC codes are generalized and adapted to design these novel GLDPC codes. Simulation results indicate that these new GLDPC codes have good performance and they are naturally suitable for puncturing and rate-compatible transmission, in addition to the advantage of efficient encoding.
The paper is outlined as follows. In section II we briefly review the principles of IRA and EERC codes, and discuss how these principles can be generalized. We describe the code structures of IRA-like GLDPC codes and EERC-like GLDPC codes in section III. Section IV provides the design method for these GLDPC codes. In section V, we give simulation results and section VI is the conclusion of paper.

C. Puncturing of IRA-like GLDPC codes and EERC-like GLDPC codes
Research on puncturing of IRA codes and EERC codes can be found in [13] and [11], respectively. Though the methods for puncturing IRA codes and EERC codes are slightly different in form, the principle behind them is the same: puncture in the way that an iterative decoder can recover all the punctured bits in as few steps as possible. When puncturing the GLDPC codes, we should also observe this principle.
Another issue is unique to the puncturing of GLDPC codes. For GLDPC codes, we can puncture up to (n − k) paritybits while at the same time are still able to recover them in one decoding step. Thus we are facing the choice between concentrated puncturing on as few check nodes as possible and decimating the punctured bits from various check nodes as uniformly as possible. Simulation shows that the latter scheme achieves a better performance over the former one. Therefore, when puncturing the GLDPC codes, both principles should be considered.

Given the code length N and rate R, the algorithm is outlined as follows:
Step 1) Find appropriate component code (n, k) and variable node degree, according to design rate R, puncturing can be done at later stage if necessary. Determine the dimension M × N of adjacency matrix H, where M is unknown.
Step 2) Find out n using M , and place the deterministic part {Ini }, i = 1, 2, ..., D in H2
Step 3) According to n, determine which rows contain degree-1 nodes and the number of degree-1 nodes in each row, generate the second edge of degree-2 nodes in these rows either using PEG algorithm or manually.
Step 4) Calculate Θj for the remaining check nodes, use PEG algorithm, in decreasing order of parity-bits, to complete H2. Note for parity-bit j, the candidate nodes can only be from Θj.
Step 5) Run standard PEG algorithm in decreasing order of information bits to complete H1.
Step 6) Do puncturing to match R if necessary.
The above-mentioned algorithm is generic for both IRA-like GLDPC codes and EERC-like GLDPC codes. One remark is that the PEG algorithm does not specify the check node degree, whereas the H matrix of GLDPC codes should have a fixed row weight. It could happen that at final stages a very few shorter cycles may be formed due to the lack of available sockets in check nodes, slightly affecting the cycle spectrum of code.
V. SIMULATION RESULTS
Many algorithms exist for iterative decoding of GLDPC codes. We can use either a maximum-a-posteriori (MAP) decoding algorithm based on the component code trellis [15], or a fast decoding algorithm for some special classes of codes [14]. In the following experiments, the trellis decoding algorithm [15] is used.
In the first experiment, we construct a (1024, 640, 0.625) IRA-like GLDPC code Γ1 using RM(3,5) code as the component code, so the adjacency matrix H is 64 × 1024. The edge degree distributions of Γ1 are:
λ(x) = 0.0103 + 0.9590x + 0.0308x2, ρ(x) = x31
The PEG algorithm can avoid length-4 cycles in H so the girth of Γ1 is 6. Fig. 1 presents the performance of Γ1 on the AWGN channel, where the maximum number of iterations is set to 200.
From Fig. 1 it can be observed that EERC LDPC code performs slightly better at medium SNR due to its lower rate, whereas at medium-to-high SNRs (≥ 2.5 dB). Γ1 shows a steeper error rate curve over EERC codes. From this evidence

we conjecture that Γ1 could offer a better performance at even higher SNRs (> 2.8 dB).
The EERC-like GLDPC code is studied in the second experiment: a (645, 301, 0.467) code Γ2 with (15, 11) Hamming code as component code is devised. The H of Γ2 is 86 × 645 and contains no length-4 cycles, and n is chosen as [43, 21, 11, 5, 2, 2, 1, 1]. The edge degree distributions of Γ2 are:
λ(x) = 0.0085 + 0.9659x + 0.0256x2, ρ(x) = x14
Fig. 2 compares the performance of Γ2 with ordinary GLDPC codes [4]; Γ2 and these ordinary GLDPC codes are of different lengths but use the same component code. From Fig. 2, after taking the code-length difference into account, we conclude that, as a special class of GLDPC codes, Γ2 offers a similar or slightly better performance over the ordinary GLDPC codes. Moreover, Γ2 exhibits a good error floor behavior at high SNRs, both for BER and FER.
Investigations on puncturing of Γ2 is done in the next experiment: Following the two principles in section III, we puncture 42 parity bits from Γ2 to obtain Γ3, a (603, 301, 0.499) code. In order to illustrate the principle of uniform puncturing, we give the indices of first ten punctured bits: [2,8,11,14,17,21,27,29,33,39]. Note that the punctured bits are uniformly distributed among the check nodes.
The comparison of Γ3 with the irregular LDPC code of same length and rate is made in Fig. 3. The irregular LDPC code devised in [16] has regular check degree 9 and the highest variable node degree is 20. At low SNR, the irregular LDPC code outperforms Γ3 a little because it enjoys a lower decoding threshold. However, Γ3 behaves better after SNR passes through 2.2 dB. At BER = 10−6, Γ3 has an improvement of 0.4-0.5 dB over irregular LDPC codes.
Finally we compare the performance of different puncturing schemes. Instead of using uniform puncturing, we puncture the first 42 parity-bits in Γ2. Let Γ3 denote the code obtained after the concentrated puncturing. Fig. 4 compares the performance of Γ3 with Γ3, we find that Γ3 always offers a performance no worse than Γ3, verifying the suggestions on puncturing in section III.
VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, two classes of GLDPC codes suitable for efficient encoding and puncturing are proposed. When the iterative decoder is employed, IRA-like GLDPC codes have the linear-time encoding complexity O(N ) whereas EERClike GLDPC codes have a even better encoding complexity O(log2 N ). These EE-GLDPC codes also exhibit the performance as good as the ordinary GLDPC codes and show their advantages over LDPC codes when the code length is short. Moreover, their good error floor behavior and easy-puncturing property make them competitive candidates for forward error correcting schemes at short-to-medium code lengths. One open problem for these EE-GLDPC codes is designing optimal puncturing schemes. Another issue is that n can have forms
