Performance Evaluation of Channel Coding for Gbps 60-GHz OFDM-based Wireless Communications
Miroslav Marinkovic, Maxim Piz, Chang-Soon Choi, Goran Panic, Marcus Ehrig and Eckhard Grass
IHP Microelectronics, Im Technologiepark 25, 15236, Frankfurt (Oder), Germany
marinkovic, piz choi, panic, ehrig, grass @ihp-microelectronics.com

Abstract- In this paper, we undertake a comparison of LDPC (768,384) and convolutional (171,133) codes for 60-GHz OFDMbased wireless communication systems. The comparison is based on two parameters: Frame Error Rate (FER) for 60-GHz channels and decoding hardware complexity for a required throughput of more than 1 Gbps. Additionally, we provide performance parameters of a fully parallel LDPC decoder chip, fabricated in our IHP in-house 0.13 μm CMOS technology, achieving a throughput of more than 1 Gbps.
Keywords: 60-GHz wireless communication, OFDM, LDPC code, convolutional code
I. INTRODUCTION
Recently, 60-GHz wireless communication systems have attracted much interest for commercial applications because of their capability to provide extremely high data rates beyond 1Gbps over a short distance. The basis for these applications is 7 GHz of unlicensed spectrum which has been allocated by the FCC at 57 to 64 GHz.
In earlier work [1], we developed a 60-GHz wireless system, mainly for WPAN and video distribution applications. This wireless system includes SiGe-based analog frontend chips, as well as digital baseband- and MAC-processors. The baseband processor was implemented on an FPGA platform and processes an analog signal bandwidth of 400 MHz. Algorithms and architecture of the baseband processor have been presented in [2], [3]. In some respects, our OFDM based transmission scheme can be considered as an up-scaled version of the 802.11a standard. For channel coding, the same convolutional code (171,133) was used with a fixed transmission scheme ranging from BPSK to 64-QAM. Furthermore, a number of parallel banks of convolutional encoders/interleavers at the transmitter side, and Viterbi decoders/deinterleavers at the receiver side of the baseband processor were employed. Due to the high level of parallelization, a peak PHY throughput of 1.08 Gbps was achieved.
Within the EASY-A project [4], we have developed an OFDM system providing higher throughput using a much wider bandwidth of 2.16-GHz.. Additionally, we want to implement an advanced coding scheme based on LDPC coding in order to obtain higher coding gain. It is well-known that LDPC codes besides their excellent error-correction performance, offer a potential for high-throughput and lowlatency decoding.
This paper deals with performance evaluation and implementation complexity of convolutional and LDPC

coding schemes for 60-GHz OFDM-based wireless systems. In Section II, we present our OFDM physical layer parameters. Then, in Section III, after presenting two different 60-GHz wireless channels, we evaluate the performance of the convolutional as well as LDPC codes in the system. Sections IV and V describe the hardware architecture of the convolutional (171,133) and LDPC(768,384) decoders, respectively. In Section VI, we undertake a hardware complexity comparison of Viterbi and LDPC decoders implemented in our in-house 0.13 μm CMOS technology. We also provide LDPC decoder chip parameters . Conclusions are drawn in Section VII.
II. OFDM PHYSICAL LAYER
For our system development, we have adopted the channel plan of task group IEEE 802.15.3c. A channel bandwidth of 2160 MHz is specified. Compared to our earlier developed 60-GHz demonstrator, we have now redefined our OFDM parameters in order to satisfy the requirements outlined in the following paragraph [5].
To come up with frequency selective channels over 2-GHz bandwidth, the FFT size has been chosen as 1024 with the sampling frequency of 2.16-GHz. This gives a FFT symbol time of T = 474 ns. To limit the overhead loss to 1 dB, we have set the guard length to a quarter of the FFT symbol time, i.e. Tg = 118.5 ns. Compared to the PHY parameters in the previous OFDM system, T = 640 ns, Tg = 160 ns, new PHY is more immune to phase noise but less tolerable channel delay spread. The number of subcarriers has been chosen to be equal to the block size of an irregular LDPC (768, 384) code taken from the IEEE 802.16e standard, [6]. The unused guard subcarriers simplify channel filtering in the analog domain without oversampling. Table I summarizes timing parameters for the wideband OFDM-mode.

A. 60-GHz Channel
We have made use of a subset of channel models provided by the IEEE 802.15.3c task group. The channel model is based on an extension of the the well-known SalehValenzuela cluster model. Parameters of the model like cluster arrival rate or arrival rate of rays within each cluster had been extracted from real measurements conducted at NICT, Japan [7]. The full set of channel models is divided into different scenarios including a residential, office and library model. Each model is split into a line-of sight (LOS) case and nonline-of-sight case (NLOS). Since transmitter antenna beamwidth has strong impact on channel characteristics, different channel models were made for omni-directional, 30 degree and 60 degree HPBW transmitter antenna.
For performance evaluation in this work, we have chosen the NLOS residential channel model CM2.3 (with 30-degree antenna beamwidth) and the LOS residential model CM1.2 (with 60-degree antenna beamwidth). NLOS mode CM2.3 l shows significant multipath propagation leading to a frequency selective channel. The rms channel delay spread is 10.7 ns. From Fig. 1, it can be seen that the received power level for CM2.3 is subject to very high fluctuations. To obtain meaningful results, we therefore decided to normalize every impulse response to unity gain. In this way, we can derive the system performance depending on the received power level. On the other hand, LOS model CM1.2 shows hardly any power fluctuations and has a strong LOS component.

IV. VITERBI DECODER ARCHITECTURE
The Viterbi algorithm is known as an optimal decoding approach for convolutional codes. In principal, the implementation of the Viterbi algorithm requires three main blocks namely, branch metric unit (BNU), add-compare-select unit (ACSU), and traceback unit (TBU). The path decisions are stored simultaneously in two dual port block rams, which are denoted as trellis memory. On the opposite side, two logical blocks perform the tracebacks. In the normal case, a traceback is accomplished over 96 steps to deliver 48 output bits. The design does not require any wait cycles. Hence, the throughput is half a source bit per cycle per traceback unit and the total throughput is one bit per cycle.
A significant reduction in complexity comes from the fact that no maximum path metric has to be calculated and tracebacks always can be performed starting from the zero state. This can be achieved when sufficiently long traceback length is used. In this case the path from any initial state will converge with very high probability to the correct path. On the other hand, termination bits at the end of the stream ensure that the zero state is the final state, and with this a priori knowledge, the last traceback can correctly start from this state.
The output bits during one traceback are assembled in reversed order. Therefore they are stored in a shift register and copied to another shift register when all bits are read. Afterwards, they are read out by the output arbiter with a speed of 8 bits per cycle. The output arbiter reads the output of the buffers one after the other. It operates burst-wise in that all bits belonging to a particular traceback have to be read out in conjunction before moving on to the next unit.

V. LDPC DECODER ARCHITECTURE
Having in mind high-throughput applications (> 1 Gbps), we have decided to develop a fully-parallel LDPC decoder architecture [9]. In fully-parallel architectures, each node in the LDPC code’s Tanner graph is assigned to dedicated hardware processing units (check and variable nodes) and messages are exchanged between nodes by wires. A common challenge for fully-parallel architecture implementation is the complex and congested global interconnect network. To mitigate this problem, several techniques have been recently introduced [10], [11].
As an LDPC decoding algorithm, an approximation of belief-propagation (BP) (or message-passing) decoding, which is also known as the min-sum (MS) algorithm, has been

employed. Although the MS algorithm causes a small errorperformance loss compared to the BP algorithm, its implementation is much simpler due to lower complexity in the calculation at the check nodes. VII. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we have provided a comparison of convolutional (171,133) and LDPC (768,384) codes based on two main criteria, FER performance for a 60-GHz wireless channel and hardware complexity. First, we have shown that an LDPC-coded 60-GHz OFDM-based system gives improved FER performance improvement compared to an equivalent system supported by convolutional (171,133) code. Then, implementation details of the Viterbi decoder as well as our fully-parallel LDPC decoder architecture are discussed. Performance parameters of a LDPC decoder chip implemented in our in-house IHP 0.13 μm technology are presented. The cell area of this decoder chip is equivalent to 669.2 K inverter gates, and the total chip area is 10.4 mm2. The comparison with three other chips is very favourable for our design. The chip is under fabrication and expected to be ready for testing.
