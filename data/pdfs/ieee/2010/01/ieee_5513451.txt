Bilayer Protograph Codes for Half-Duplex Relay Channels
Thuy Van Nguyen†, Aria Nosratinia† and Dariush Divsalar‡ †Department of Electrical Engineering, The University of Texas at Dallas, Richardson, TX 75083-0688 USA
‡Jet Propulsion Laboratory, California Institute of Technology, Pasadena, CA 91109-8099 USA Email: nvanthuy@utdallas.edu, aria@utdallas.edu, divsalar@jpl.nasa.gov

Abstract— This paper presents a high-performing LDPC code for the relay channel that addresses simultaneously two important issues: a code structure that allows low encoding complexity, and a flexible rate-compatible code that allows matching to various channel conditions. Most of the previous high-performance LDPC codes for the relay channel are tightly optimized for a given channel quality and are not easily adapted, without extensive reoptimization, for various channel conditions. This paper presents a code for the relay channel that combines structured design and easy encoding with rate compatibility to allow adaptation to the three links involved in the relay channel, and furthermore offers very good performance. The proposed code is constructed by synthesizing a bilayer structure with a protograph. In addition to the contribution to relay encoding, we also produce an improved family of protograph codes for the point-to-point AWGN channel whose high-rate members enjoy thresholds that are within 0.07 dB of capacity.
I. INTRODUCTION
It has been known that the basic problem of coding for the Gaussian relay channel1 can be reduced to the following: a code and its sub-code must be designed simultaneously that operate with two different rates at two different SNRs. This problem is nontrivial, however, significant progress has been made on it and a subset of past work is mentioned below.
Although significant progress has been made in the relay coding problem, existing codes must be painstakingly optimized to match to a single set of channel conditions, many of them do not offer easy encoding, and most of them do not have structured design. This paper proposes a class of LDPC relay codes that address three important issues in an integrative manner: low encoding complexity, modular structure allowing for easy design, and rate-compatibility so that the code can be easily matched to a variety of channel conditions without extensive re-optimization. In addition, the proposed codes offer excellent performance.
We briefly review a subset of past work in this area. The recent work most relevant to this paper is [1], which produces bilayer LDPC code structures for the decode-and-forward (DF) scheme. This approach has been refined in other works including [2], [3]. Other examples of LDPC codes for the halfduplex relay channel include [4], [5], [6], [7], among others. They design the code and sub-code mentioned above in various
1This refers to the half-duplex decode-forward relay in the power-limited regime, where much of the recent work has been done. The general relay coding problem can be more complicated.

manners via joint optimization. Although LDPC codes with easy encoding has been attempted in [8] (limited to symmetric cooperation in low rates), to our knowledge no previous work has produced rate-compatible LDPC relay codes that can be matched to varying set of channel conditions.
The main contribution of this paper is a coding scheme for the relay channel using protographs [9] that are built with a bilayer-lengthened (BL) structure [1]. A protograph code [9] is an LDPC code that can be constructed from a small base matrix with a few elements. Protograph codes can achieve very good thresholds with low encoder complexity as well as fast decoding [10], [11]. The proposed approach offers flexibility in designing a family of rate-compatible embedded codes for relay channels. In the process of designing our codes for the relay channel, we also produce improved high-rate protograph codes for the ordinary (point-to-point) AWGN channel with thresholds within 0.07 dB of capacity.
The organization of this paper is as follows. Section II briefly explains the system model. In Section III we describe the basic structure of our codes for the relay channel. Section IV is dedicated to designing improved protograph codes that are useful not only for the relay channel, but also in the point-to-point AWGN channel. The improved protographs allow us to construct more powerful codes for the relay channel. Section V applies the approach of Section IV to the design high-performing codes for the relay channel. Finally, Section VII concludes the paper.

B. Proposed Code
Recall that the main problem of half-duplex relay coding can be reduced to the simultaneous design of two codes at two rates and two SNRs, such that one is a subset of the other. This problem can be addressed by forceful optimization, but a clever method of addressing this problem is via the bilayer lengthened (BL) LDPC structure [1, Figure 5]. This method uses a bilayer Tanner graph to make the two codes, while using a concept of “parity forwarding” with subsequent successive decoding that removes the need to directly address the issue of uneven SNRs among the symbols of a given codeword. This method is attractive in that it addresses head-on some of the main issues in the design of relay codes, but it does not by itself give rise to highly structured codes with simple encoding, nor does it give rate-compatible codes. The main contribution of this work is to construct a class of codes that simultaneously possess a bilayer parity-forwarding mechanism, while also benefiting from the properties of protograph codes (easy encoding, modular design, rate-compatible).
Our coding strategy consists of designing a protograph code and then splitting its variable nodes into two layers. The design of good protograph codes for our purposes is addressed in Section IV and V. The codeword (both layers) is broadcast from the source (Figure 1). In the example of this figure, Layer 1 consists of a rate-1/2 code (seen again on the right side of the figure entitled “Step 2: Decoding Layer 1” ) and Layer 2 consists of up to 2n variable nodes. The relay receives this code and since this overall code is designed for the S-R channel, will be able to decode it. Then, the relay will generate an extra set of parity bits only for Layer 2, in a manner similar to [1], and transmits it to the destination. The extra parity bits, together with their systematic bits that are already received from the source, will be decoded at the destination.2 Once the destination decodes this secondary code, its bits can be eliminated from the Tanner graph of the first code to give rise to a lower-rate code that can be directly decoded at the S-D SNR.3 The operations are depicted in Figure 1.

The design process for a variety of channel conditions is now very easy. We assume the mother protograph code (highest rate) is designed and is available. From this mother code we start by choosing the code with rate necessary to support transmission in the S-R link. This is the code demonstrated at the left side of Figure 1. We then split this code into two layers by considering the SNR of the S-D link: Layer 1 will consist of a sub-code with a rate that is supported by the SNR of the S-D link. The remaining nodes are in Layer 2. Finally, another code is used to forward the Layer 2 variables from relay to destination. For this secondary code, we will use the systematic version of (another) rate-compatible protograph codebook, which can be matched to the R-D channel quality.
IV. IMPROVED PROTOGRAPH CODES FOR THE AWGN
CHANNEL
A. Protograph Codes
A protograph [9] is a Tanner graph with a relatively small number of nodes. A protograph code (an equivalent LDPC code) is a larger derived graph constructed by applying a “copy-and-permutation” operation on a protograph. The protograph code is the graph of a code N (copies) times as large as the code corresponding to the protograph, with the same rate and the same distribution of variable and check node degrees. Several capacity-approaching protograph code designs which support a large range of rates and have simple structure have been proposed in [11], [15]. A simple example of a protograph is shown in Figure 2. This graph consists of 3 variable nodes and 2 check nodes that are interconnected by 5 different edge (variable-to-check) types. The derived graph is constructed by replicating the protograph 3 times and permuting variable-tocheck pairings within the same edge type of the protograph.
An iterative decoding threshold of a protograph is the minimum channel quality that supports reliably iterative decoding of asymptotically large LDPC codes built from the protograph. Thresholds of LDPC codes can be computed by using density evolution (DE) [16] and extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) chart [17] techniques. However, the general EXIT chart cannot be applied to compute the thresholds of protograph codes due to its inability of computing thresholds of graphs that have degree-1 variable, punctured variable nodes or protographs with same degree distributions and different thresholds [18]. To solve these problems, the authors in [18] proposed PEXIT method that is simple and allows to compute thresholds of

VI. NUMERICAL RESULTS
In this section we present numerical results representing the codes for the relay channel and compare them with other relay codes from the literature.
We designed rate-compatible bilayer-lengthened protograph codes that are suitable for the half-duplex relay channel as described in Section III-B. The proposed bilayer codes can be designed for any two rates ≥ 1/2. A class of these codes have been designed with very good thresholds, as shown in Table II. We compare the performance of our relay code with the bilayer-lengthened and bilayer-expurgated irregular LDPC codes proposed in the literature, as given in Table III. All codes use rate pair (RSD1 0.5, RSR1 0.7). Our rate1/2 code is better than the code proposed in [7]. Although our rate-1/2 code has a threshold 0.088 dB above that in [1] and 0.148 dB above that in [2], this is offset by a lower threshold for the higher-rate code of the pair.4 Our codes have in general better thresholds compared with bilayerexpurgated codes. Thus, a simple design approach has yielded a family of codes that offer a performance as good as or even better than other highly-optimized bilayer LDPC codes, while offering important advantages in terms of rate-compatibility, low encoding complexity, and the fast decoding properties of protograph codes.
VII. CONCLUSION
This paper presents a rate-compatible bilayer protograph code for the half-duplex relay channel. The proposed code allows easy design, great flexibility in matching to various

relay channel conditions, and offers low encoding complexity. The heart of this method is a simple approach to design bilayer lengthened protograph codes of various rates. In the process of this work, in addition to the relay channel codes, an improved family of AWGN point-to-point protograph codes is produced whose high-rate members enjoy a gap of only 0.07 dB to AWGN capacity.
