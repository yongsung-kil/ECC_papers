On the Scalability of SIMD Processing for Software Defined Radio Algorithms

Abstract—Software defined radio (SDR) systems require programmable architectures that provide high performance combined with high energy efficiency. Wide single instruction, multiple data (SIMD) architectures could potentially satisfy these demands, but only few publications examine the interdependence between the SIMD width and the performance of typical SDR algorithms. In this paper, we present a detailed analysis of three SDR algorithm classes on a newly developed scalable SIMD architecture, which combines SIMD processing with long instruction word (LIW) execution. Although radix-2 and mixed-radix fast Fourier transform, sphere decoding, and low-density parity-check decoding all benefit from SIMD processing, our results show that there are different constraints on the performance.
I. INTRODUCTION
The software defined radio (SDR) technology has gained significant interest for mobile devices as well as base stations. SDR promises a cost-effective and flexible solution, potentially supporting multiple wireless standards on a common platform. Yet, the computation and power requirements of future fourth generation (4G) cellular wireless standards, as proposed by the ITU [1], are steep. For example, a downlink peak data rate of 600 MBit/s is required for a 4G compliant 40 MHz channel.
Single instruction multiple data (SIMD) processing is considered as a key technology for SDR systems, offering high computing power with low energy consumption. Future SDR systems might consist of multiple SIMD cores and some dedicated accelerators. This paper aims at providing insight for the challenge of choosing an appropriate SIMD width for such a SIMD core: small SIMD widths provide less computing power than wide SIMD widths, but some SDR algorithms may not feature enough data parallelism to fully utilize wide SIMD data paths [2]. In our study, we discuss three different classes of SDR algorithms and analyze the scalability of SIMD processing on a newly developed scalable SIMD architecture with long instruction word (LIW) support. The algorithm classes are introduced in section III. The contributions of this paper are as follows:
• We introduce a newly developed scalable SIMD processor architecture with LIW support and different permutation network topologies (section IV).
• We discuss the SIMD scalability of radix-2 and mixedradix fast Fourier transforms (FFTs), sphere decoding using the fixed-complexity sphere decoder, and decoding

for quasi-cyclic low density parity-check codes (section V). Different network topologies are also considered. The results demonstrate that the algorithms have different scalability requirements on the relation between algorithm parameters and SIMD width.
Related research is discussed in section II. We draw conclusions from our findings in section VI.
II. RELATED WORK
The Advanced Computer Architecture Lab at the University of Michigan at Ann Arbor has done a lot of work on SIMD processor architectures for SDR [2]–[5]. The issue of the scalability of SIMD processing has been examined in [2], [4].
In [2], the scalability of a 1024-point FFT, two MIMO algorithms (space time block coding and V-BLAST), and an LDPC decoder for WiMAX (z = 96, rate 5/6) on the Signalprocessing On Demand Architecture (SODA) is analyzed. The results show good scalability for FFT and MIMO processing, while parallelism in LDPC decoding is limited by the expansion factor z. Our research complements this study by considering a LIW SIMD architecture and by analyzing the effects of different FFT sizes and LDPC expansion factors. We also consider sphere decoding for MIMO detection.
In [4], the development of the ARM Ardbeg processor from SODA is discussed. During the development, different SIMD widths have been analyzed for some third generation (3G) wireless algorithms. The results show that energy and delay can both be reduced by increasing the SIMD width. Furthermore, four different permutation network topologies have been analyzed for a 32-way SIMD processor. We adopted the analysis of different network topologies.

A. OFDM-based (De-)Modulation
OFDM is a frequency-division multiplexing (FDM) method for multi-carrier transmission. The incoming data stream is divided into multiple parallel sub-carriers, which are modulated by conventional methods such a quadrature amplitude modulation (QAM). The sub-carriers are defined in frequency domain and represent orthogonal frequencies. On the transmitter side, the sub-carriers are transformed into a time-domain OFDM symbol, using the inverse fast Fourier transform (IFFT), and a guard interval (usually as cyclic prefix) is added to prevent intersymbol interference. On the receiver side, the guard interval is removed and the sub-carriers are demodulated by an FFT.
Orthogonal frequency division multiple access (OFDM-A) is a multi-user extension of an OFDM system. Here different users are assigned different subsets of sub-carriers. The user data may be defined in frequency domain — as in the singleuser OFDM case — or in time domain. The second case is called single carrier frequency division multiple access (SCFDMA). Each user’s data is defined as a time domain signal, transformed into frequency domain using a short FFT, and mapped into a subset of OFDM sub-carriers. The UMTS long term evolution (LTE) standard [6] applies OFDM-A for downlink transmission and SC-FDMA for uplink transmission. Both downlink and uplink use a 2048-point IFFT (transmitter) for 20 MHz bandwidth and smaller IFFTs for smaller bandwidths (e.g. 512-point for 5 MHz). In the uplink, each user is assigned multiple resource blocks, consisting of 12 sub-carriers, with a maximum number of 1200 sub-carriers and a restriction of the FFT sizes to a product of powers of two, three, and five. Hence, SC-FDMA requires support for mixed-radix FFTs. Both pure radix-2 and mixed-radix FFTs are considered in the SIMD scalability analysis.

B. Sphere Decoding for MIMO Detection
MIMO systems utilize multiple transmit and receive antennas to improve signal quality or data rate. In the latter case — denoted as spatial multiplexing — the independent transmit antennas are used for multiple parallel data streams. Systems typically utilize 2 × 2 or 4 × 4 antennas. Sphere decoding is a decoding method for spatial multiplexing, which allows maximum-likelihood detection with reduced complexity. The sphere decoder first transforms the channel matrix into upper triangular form and then applies a depth-first tree search algorithm on the symbol space.
The major draw backs of sphere decoding are as follows: the number of required searched symbol vectors fluctuates and the decoding cannot be easily parallelized [7]. Hence, different modified algorithms have been designed, which apply breadth-first search and limit the number of searched paths at the cost of decreased performance. Examples are the Kbest sphere decoder (e.g. [8]) and selective spanning with fast enumeration (SSFE) [9]. We apply the fixed-complexity sphere decoder (FSD) [10], due to its low computational complexity with quasi-maximum likelihood performance [11].
C. LDPC Codes
LDPC codes are forward error correction (FEC) codes based on sparse parity-check matrices. They have been discovered by Gallager in 1960 [12], but have only gained consideration for implementation after the advent of turbo codes. LDPC codes can achieve performance close to the Shannon limit with relatively low decoder complexity — and don’t require licensing. Hence, LDPC codes have been considered for many wireless systems, such as WiMAX [13] and CMMB [14].
Due to the regularity of the LDPC matrix, quasi-cyclic LDPC codes, defined by a base matrix Hb, are used in most standards. Each element of Hb defines a z-by-z submatrix of the LDPC matrix. The submatrices are either permutation matrices or zero matrices. WiMAX defines multiple LDPC codes with different expansion factors (z-factors) from one common Hb.
LDPC decoders operate iteratively using message passing algorithms. The LDPC code is described by a set of bit nodes (representing the code word) and a set of check nodes (representing parity check conditions). During the decoding process, messages are sent from bit nodes to check nodes and vice versa. Most implementations use the min-sum algorithm for decoding, which requires only a minimum operation and sign products for the check node processing.

1) Architecture Overview: The proposed scalable SIMD processor architecture was developed using the LISA language [15], which was extended with macros for scalability support [16]. LISA was chosen as it enables the automatic generation of a fast, cycle-accurate instruction set simulator (ISS), synthesizable register transfer level (RTL) code, and necessary software development tools, such as assembler, disassembler, and linker, from one common description.
A block diagram of the developed processor architecture is shown in Fig. 2. The data path consists of a scalar 16-bit data path and a scalable SIMD data path with multiple 16bit lanes. For the scalability analysis we considered SIMD widths ranging from 8 to 64 16-bit lanes (128 to 1024 bits). The processor contains two separate local memories for scalar and vector data with separate load/store units and address generators. Each SIMD lane contains a local 16-bit 16-entry register file, eight 1-bit mask registers, and two 40-bit accumulator registers for intermediate results of MAC operations. The SIMD data path supports masked execution and single cycle multiplication. As many baseband algorithms operate on complex data, instructions for complex multiplication and MAC operations have been added. Complex operations treat a pair of consecutive vector elements as imaginary and real part of a complex number; the multiplication is split into two real-valued multiplications and takes two clock cycles.
2) Permutation Network Support: As part of the scalability analysis, we decided to consider multiple permutation networks with different network topologies. This approach enables analyzing the benefit of more flexible permutations on

the algorithm performance. Existing SIMD architectures apply different types of networks ranging from single stage shuffle networks [3] to full crossbar networks [17] and multi-stage networks on two input vectors [4].
Multi-stage inverse butterfly and crossbar network topologies have been implemented. As algorithms, such as the FFT, require permutations on pairs of vectors, networks with different widths (one vector or two vectors) have been considered. Hence, overall four network topologies have been realized. In the following, permutation networks with two input vectors will be abbreviated as double-vector networks and permutation networks with one input vector will be denoted as single-vector networks. All network topologies directly support rotation and butterfly permutations through special instructions, as these operations are necessary for FFT and LDPC decoding (sections V-A respectively V-C). Other permutations can be defined using shuffle pattern registers.
3) Instruction Level Parallelism: Architectural support for instruction level parallelism (ILP) can improve the utilization of SIMD units and reduce the execution time. Hence, LIW support was considered for the scalable SIMD processor architecture. However, LIW requires additional register file ports, which increase the area and power demands of the register file. Therefore, implementations of W-CDMA [18] and FFT [19] algorithms on the Embedded Vector Processor (EVP) [17] first have been analyzed in order to decide on the number of LIW slots. An average number of 3.53 parallel instructions per cycle was measured for a radix-2 FFT loop, which contains three FFT stages and vector permutations. Most algorithms achieved average numbers of between 2.4 and 2.6 parallel instructions (e.g. radix-3 FFT stage: 2.47; channel estimation: 2.6; tap solver: 2.46). Based on these results, we decided to implement the processor architecture with variable-length LIW support with up to four parallel SIMD or scalar instructions.
The architectural features are summarized in Table I. The term SIMD width is used for the number of elements in a vector and SIMD bit width denotes the corresponding width in bits.
4) Analysis Methodology: Sixteen different instances of the scalable SIMD processor architecture, with four different SIMD widths and four network topologies, have been implemented using LISA tools (Synopsys Processor Designer) and register transfer level (RTL) code has been generated. Synopsys Design Compiler in topographical mode has been used for synthesizing the processors in a 90 nm process with a target clock frequency of 300 MHz. Assembly code for all processor configurations has been hand-coded and optimized for LIW execution and run times have been measured during simulation. The energy consumption was extracted from the synthesized processors based on the execution of the algorithms. The energy consumption includes leakage and the estimated energy of the clock tree. Memories were not synthesized and are excluded from the energy and area results, but included in the performance analysis. V. ALGORITHMS: RESULTS AND ANALYSIS
In the following, results for radix-2 and mixed-radix FFT, fixed-complexity sphere decoding, and LDPC decoding will be presented and analyzed. Both FFT and sphere decoding operate on complex 16+16 bit vector elements. Hence, the term SIMD width will be used to describe the number of 32bit elements for FFT and sphere decoding and the number of 16-bit elements for LDPC decoding.
Next to the available data parallelism, there are other factors that can influence the performance gain through a wider SIMD width. An increased overhead due to required additional — or more complex — permutations limits the achievable speedup. On the other hand, a greater SIMD width may require less registers for storing necessary data. Hence, if memory access is a limiting factor, the speedup with a doubled SIMD width may be even greater than two. These effects are demonstrated below.

A. Radix-2 and Mixed-Radix FFT
In prior work, radix-2 and mixed-radix FFT have been implemented on the EVP [17] and SIMD algorithms have been developed [19], [20]. The algorithms have been designed with the goals of minimizing the number of vector permutations and guaranteeing that all stages of the FFT operate on full vectors. The resulting requirements on the ratio between the SIMD width NSIMD and the FFT length differ for the mixedradix and the pure radix-2 case:

B. Fixed-Complexity Sphere Decoder
The fixed-complexity sphere decoder [10] has been implemented for a 4 × 4 antenna system with quadrature phaseshift keying (QPSK) and 16-QAM support. The decoder operates in three steps: reordering of the channel matrix, matrix decomposition into triangular form, and reduced tree search. The first two stages consist of matrix transformations of the channel matrix. These stages can be either parallelized by processing one matrix row/column in parallel or by processing multiple OFDM sub-carriers in parallel. The first approach requires intra-vector operations (e.g. dot product) and offers only limited parallelism. The second approach allows parallel processing of sub-carriers up to the size of the FFT. The tree search can be parallelized by processing multiple possible transmit symbols in parallel or again by processing multiple sub-carriers. The latter approach offers more parallelism and, due to the orthogonality of sub-carriers, requires no communication between vector elements.
The implemented sphere decoder processes multiple subcarriers in parallel in all stages of the algorithm. Hence, parallelism is only limited by the length of the OFDM symbol. The implementation is easily scalable for different SIMD widths. The baseline implementation with a 128 bit SIMD bit width and a single-vector butterfly network achieves a throughput of 54.86 Mbps for QPSK modulation and 34.85 Mbps for 16QAM. The obtained speedups are shown in Fig. 3 (as 4x4 FSD). For both QPSK and 16-QAM a linear speedup is achieved, i.e. a doubling of the SIMD width results in exactly doubled throughput. The selected permutation network does not influence the performance.
C. Decoder for Quasi-cyclic LDPC Codes
We implemented LDPC decoders for WiMAX codes [13] with code rates 2/3 and 5/6 with three different expansion factors for z-by-z submatrices (z = 32, z = 64 and z = 96). The expansion factor z = 96 defines the biggest and hence

most challenging LDPC matrices. The decoders use the minsum algorithm with a serial schedule [21], i.e. one row of the matrix Hb is processed at a time. Ten decoding iterations per code word have been done for the measurements.
The processing consists of two parts: check node update and bit node update. During the check node update, all non-zero zby-z submatrices in a row are processed. First, the data vectors corresponding to the submatrix are loaded and rotated. Next, a message to the check node is calculated by subtracting the old check node data for this bit node. Then, the min-sum algorithm updates the minimum of all messages to the check node and the message signs. During the bit node update, the new check node information (based on the min-sum algorithm) is added to the messages from bit to check nodes to get the new bit node information. As a last step, the bit node rotation has to be reversed. The required processing can be realized by two loops on all bit nodes connected to the check nodes. The check node processing requires to store the minimum, 2nd minimum, and minimum position of all incoming messages and the sign bits of these messages for each node. The serial schedule enables to overwrite the bit node data with the messages to the check nodes, further reducing the required memory.
Woh et al. [2] already showed that LDPC decoding can be vectorized by processing one z-by-z submatrix in parallel. Our results for SIMD bit widths less than 1024 bit — and z = 64 with a SIMD bit width of 1024 bit — support this conclusion (see Fig. 3 on the right-hand side). The code rate has no significant impact on the speedup. For comparison purposes, the achieved throughputs of the baseline implementation (128 bit SIMD bit width, single vector butterfly network) are displayed in Table III.
Seo et al. [22] presented an optimized implementation of a WiMAX LDPC decoder for z = 96 and R = 5/6 on a customized variant of the 512-bit SODA processor clocked at 400 MHz. On the original SODA processor, a throughput of 18.3 Mbps is achieved. The optimized implementation uses accelerator units and customized instructions and achieves an improved throughput of 30.4 Mbps. Our implementation for the same LDPC code on a 512-bit SIMD processor has a throughput of 18.2 Mbps at a clock frequency of 300 MHz, which is comparable to the results on SODA for 400 MHz.
Surprisingly, Fig. 3 shows that speedups better than linear speedup are obtainable for z-factors 32 and 64. The reason for this behavior is the limited number of registers in combination with the number of needed data vectors. If at most two vectors are needed for z bit nodes, all required data for the check and bit node updates (the old values of minimum, 2nd minimum, minimum position and signs at the check node and the updated values) can be kept in registers. Hence, the rotation of data vectors and the check node respectively bit node update can be done in one step. If more vectors are needed for z values, the processing has to be split into separate rotation and node update loops. This results in a degraded performance. Hence, bigger SIMD widths that only require one or two data vectors per submatrix achieve an increased speedup.

Firstly, due to the presence of zero submatrices, the processed submatrices do not always occupy consecutive memory locations. This issue can be overcome by reordering bit positions before the decoding, but it is not possible to guarantee consecutive submatrices for all LDPC codes. Secondly, parallel processing of multiple submatrices in one vector requires support for vector element rotations on segments of a vector. These rotations can be realized on all considered permutation networks, but come with a significant memory overhead: rotations on complete vectors can be defined by a scalar rotation distance, rotations on segments of a vector have to be specified by permutation patterns that require significantly more memory.
The implementations for 1024 bit wide SIMD processors with z = 32 and z = 96, which require parallel processing of multiple submatrices, have been realized by interleaving two consecutive code words instead of reordering submatrices in a code word. The overhead for the interleaving and deinterleaving is low as these steps are required only once per pair of code words and not in each decoding iteration. Furthermore, by choosing an appropriate interleaving pattern, partial rotations defined by permutation patterns can be avoided for crossbar network topologies. A different interleaving pattern had to be used for inverse butterfly network topologies1 and permutation patterns had to be used to define the permutations in this case. With these modifications, approximately linear speedup can be achieved even for SIMD widths greater than the expansion factor z.

Fig. 4 shows mostly normalized energy figures smaller than one. This can be explained by the energy consumption of the LIW instruction decoder and the scalar data path. Both do not depend on the SIMD width. Hence, the required energy does not scale with the SIMD width.
Besides this effect, the results are consistent with the obtained speedups. Short FFTs, with less than linear speedup due to the initialization overhead, achieve the worst energy figures.

The lowest normalized energy consumption is obtained for the LDPC decoders with z-factors 32 and 64, which have speedups above linear speedup.
Double-vector networks require more area and more energy than single-vector networks, unless the increased network width translates into better speedup (e.g. small radix-2 FFTs). Even the fixed-complexity sphere decoder, which only uses permutations to rotate candidate symbols, requires more energy on double-vector network due to the increased number of register file ports.
E. LIW performance analysis
One of the major features of the proposed SIMD processor architecture is the support of 4-way LIW execution. LIW support has been realized with the intention of reducing the time overhead for non-computational operations (i. e. memory access, data movement, and vector permutations).
The benefit of LIW processing can be assessed by analyzing the resource utilization of the computational SIMD vector processing units — the vector ALU (VALU) and the vector MAC unit (VMAC). The resource utilization RVALU of the VALU respectively RVMAC of the VMAC describes the relative amount of time that the unit has been active for useful computations. A resource utilization value close to 100 percent for either of these processing units indicates that the overhead for non-computational operations can be hidden by LIW execution. For most of the inner loops, the LIW performance does not depend on the SIMD width or the type of permutation network. VI. CONCLUSION
SIMD based processor architectures offer good scalability for typical SDR algorithms. The results for all implemented

algorithms show approximately linear speedup for all considered SIMD widths. Yet, the results also unveil different constraints for optimal performance gains based on the ratio of algorithm parameters and SIMD width: the FFT can be done with minimum overhead for data reordering if the FFT size is a multiple of the SIMD width (radix-2 case) or the squared SIMD width (mixed-radix case), SIMD parallelization of the fixed-complexity sphere decoder is only limited by the length of an OFDM symbol, and LDPC decoding is limited by the expansion factor, but the results also show that the z factor is no hard limit.
The obtained speedups also demonstrate that a LIW architecture can potentially hide the overhead of necessary vector permutations (and memory access). For example, on a SIMD

The speedups for different network topologies indicate that the choice of the permutation network has negligible impact for small SIMD widths. Yet, the differences in speedup grow with increasing SIMD widths — especially for the FFT. The results show a significant improvement in performance from a single-vector network to a double-vector network and a smaller improvement from inverse butterfly network to crossbar network.
Short mixed-radix FFTs and LDPC decoders with SIMD widths greater than the expansion factor have performance limitations due to memory access patterns, i.e. data from different memory locations is potentially needed for one data vector. An architecture that supports single instruction multiple disjoint data (SIMdD) processing [23] can potentially overcome these limitations. A SIMdD architecture provides multiple address generators for different sub-vectors [5]. Furthermore, the LDPC decoder benefits from specialized instructions for rotation operations on sub-vectors (without the need for storing permutation patterns in memory). The impact of these modifications on the performance is a possible topic for future work.
