A 10.37 mm2 675 mW reconfigurable LDPC and Turbo encoder and decoder for 802.11n, 802.16e and 3GPP-LTE

F. Naessens, V. Derudder, H. Cappelle, L. Hollevoet, P. Raghavan, M. Desmet, A.M. AbdelHamid, I. Vos, L. Folens, S. O'Loughlin, S. Singirikonda, S. Dupont,
J.-W. Weijers, A. Dejonghe, L. Van der Perre

IMEC vzw, Kapeldreef 75, B3001 Leuven, Belgium E-mail: Frederik.Naessens@imec.be

Abstract This paper describes the implementation of a flexible Turbo and LDPC outer modem engine which is capable of supporting the WiFi(802.11n), WiMax(802.16e) and 3GPPLTE standard on the same hardware resources. The chip is implemented in a 65nm CMOS technology and occupies 10.37 mm2. The decoder flexibility is offered by means of an application-specific instruction-set processor (ASIP), with full datapath reuse between Turbo and LDPC decoding. The encoders are dedicated ASIC datapaths. The maximum clock speed can be set to 320 MHz allowing a decoder output rate for a single iteration in excess of 140 Mbps for Turbo and 640 Mbps for LDPC with a maximum power consumption of 675 mW. The architecture template has been extended to support other standards like the DVB-S2/T2 LDPC decoding as well. Keywords: outer modem, Turbo, LDPC
Introduction With the growing number of wireless standards and the different variations and modes within a standard, there is a need for a flexible channel coding solution. LDPC standards are needed in WiMAX and are optional in WLAN. Turbo decoding is needed in the 3GPP-LTE outer modem. A flexible solution provides an area- and cost-efficient solution to accommodate these different standards. However there is a need to balance the flexibility to ensure energy efficiency.
Architecture In this paper, we introduce the FlexFEC architecture. The chip consists of an ASIP capable of both LDPC and Turbo decoding, as well as of dedicated LDPC and Turbo encoders. We have also introduced monitors to monitor chip degradation and the process variation over different chips using an automated methodology of [1]. The ASIP decoder architecture consists out of three major parts: a configuration, an encoder and a decoder block. The configuration block offers quick reconfiguration between different modes through the use a local parameter storage memory. The Turbo and LDPC encoders, are made up with separate parametrizable hardware blocks. Besides memory, the FlexFEC decoder consists of three different parts, depicted in Figure 1, that are specialized for each of the different types of operations: vector, scalar/control, read/write crossbars and address generation. The vector datapath is an N=96 way SIMD datapath that is specialized for a high throughput and optimized decoding process for both Turbo and LDPC decoding. Besides load/store and basic arithmetic instructions, the processor also has support for max* calculations [2]. A detailed view on this ASIP core including its pipeline stages, from which the length is determined by the read latency from the background memory, is depicted on Figure 2. The address generation unit is responsible for generating the address for

the 96 parallel memory banks of the processor. Additionally, it controls the shuffler to perform the appropriate collision free memory access in case of Turbo decoding and in case of LDPC to perform the rotation. The architecture also consists of two sets of 96 memory banks. One set is used for input/output while the processor is working on the other, thereby performing double-buffered low-latency decoding.
Figure 1: FlexFEC decoder architecture The choice for the parallelization is driven by the standards in scope. As for the layered LDPC modes, the maximum parallelization is equal to the size of the parity check submatrices. In case of Turbo, the parallelization is obtained by performing the maximum a posteriori (MAP) decoding in parallel multiple “windows” [2]. Besides the MAP decoding, interleaving operations must also be parallelized. In [3] it is proven that any interleaving law can be mapped onto any parallel multi-banked architecture without collisions. In addition, an annealing algorithm is proposed that is proven to always converge to a valid mapping function.

Besides a separate voltage for the IO, the active area is divided into three distinct voltage domains: always-on (containing the shared blocks), encoder and decoder. On the test PCB, each voltage domain has a low-resistance current sensing resistor. This allows monitoring the supply. Figure 5 shows the measured decoder power dissipation and timing for the largest 802.16e LDPC mode (Z=96, coderate = 5/6). Table 1 shows the measurement results for a selected LDPC and turbo mode. The conversion ratio between current sensing voltage and power consumption is equal to 0.15. In order to measure the peak power accurately, a lot of decoding iterations were selected. The decoding time can be derived through the dec_in_full and dec_out_empty chip interface signals. In cases when not all SIMD slots are needed, they are de-activated, resulting in a power reduction.

Comparison to State of the art

Table 2 gives an overview of the state-of–the-art FEC

solutions with the associated power and throughput. A first

important conclusion is that these are dedicated non-flexible

implementations, tailored for a specific Turbo or LDPC

decoder. To the best of our knowledge, this work is the first chip realization that provides a flexible solution capable of

supporting 3GPP-LTE Turbo, 802.11n and 802.16e LDPC

decoding on the same hardware resources. Secondly, the

proposed solution compares favorably to the equivalent set of

dedicated implementations, while offering the advantages of a

flexible solution (lower time-to-market, reduced development

cost, support for future standards). As an example, if we add

up both Turbo as LDPC solution [6] and [7] we can reach a

performance-competitive solution that offers flexibility at a

reasonable penalty in area and power consumption (see also

possible additional optimizations listed in the next section).

Future optimizations for lower power and area Further optimizations can be applied on the design resulting in improved area and power figures. With little impact an 7 bit word length can be selected, instead of the current 10 bit, resulting in linear decrease in area and power. With an LDPC only FlexFEC decoder architecture following optimizations are possible: ASIP core tuning, single instance background memory and barrel shifter instead of full cross bars. According to our synthesis experiments, power and area gains of 45% and 70% can be envisaged, respectively.
Conclusions This paper presents the implementation of a unified domainspecific instruction-set programmable architecture capable of performing both Turbo and LDPC decoding both through parallel processing. An ultra-wide SIMD approach is chosen and combined with an innovative transparent collisionavoiding parallel interleaving/scrambling managed through distributed memory hierarchy and reconfigurable address generation units. Its flexibility is proven for multi-standard decoding and could offer support for other existing or emerging standards. Implementation in a 65nm process led to a chip which offers combined LDPC and Turbo decoding which is not present in other state-of-the-art work.
