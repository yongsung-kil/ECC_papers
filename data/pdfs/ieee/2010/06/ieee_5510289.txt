Abstract— This paper aims at introducing a safe voltage scaling and body biasing methodology for Low-Density Parity Check (LDPC) hard-wired IP. The proposed methodology allows an efficient post-silicon tuning of the LDPC, and the performances can be adapted to High Speed mode, or Low Operating Power mode, or Low Standby Power mode requirements. Concrete 45nm silicon results are introduced in this paper to demonstrate the added value of the methodology. More precisely, it is shown that running the High Performance mode leads to +24% on circuit maximum operating frequency. And the Low Standby Power mode results on x0.73 leakage minimization. The proposed adaptive LDPC encoder/decoder can remove some barriers to the adoption of long LDPC codes on portable devices.
Index Terms– Voltage Scaling, Body Biasing, Performance Tuning, Algorithms
I. INTRODUCTION
Energy consumption, not only the one of integrated systems, has become one of the most widely discussed topics in recent years because of the global warming effects. Since semiconductor electronic devices are increasingly used, different eco-labels have appeared, and the customer demand on green-products is increasing continuously. In this way, designing products with the environmental impacts in mind through their entire life cycle becomes one of the major challenges.
To achieve sustainable products, the complementary postsilicon tuning techniques can be used to adapt circuit performances to the application requirements as summarized in figure 1.

Up to now, many articles were published on the autoadaptive circuits combining both voltage scaling and body biasing tuning techniques to reduce efficiently dynamic power and leakage [1-5]. The best way to reduce the overall circuit power consumption is to reduce the supply voltage (VDD). However, as the optimum VDD can be less than the transistor threshold voltage (Vth), a

complementary performance tuning techniques such as the body biasing are a major area of interest in low power research. Body biasing relies on the threshold voltage (Vth) tuning [6] by controlling the body-source voltage (Vbs). Two types of body biasing techniques are identified: Forward Body Biasing (FBB) and Reverse Body Biasing (RBB). On one hand, FBB reduces Vth and results on an increased speed at the cost of an increased leakage. On the other hand, RBB reduces leakage while slowing down the circuit speed. The body biasing has a linear impact on speed performance, and an exponential impact on circuit leakage.
I.1. Hard-wired LDPC Challenges
The Low-Density Parity-Check Code (LDPC code) is an error correcting code used to transmit a message over a noisy channel. The LDPC code is present on different high volumes communication chips, and targets applications as the Digital Video Broadcasting (DVB-S2), or the Worldwide Interoperability for Microwave Access (WiMAX). The benefits of the LDPC code are the small implementation complexity and the ability to encode large quantities of information and large codeword length. Furthermore, the emergence of high level synthesis tools has allowed the implementation of hard-wired LDPC code with minimal area. Also, the use of advanced process technologies will allow shrinking the implementation area, and reducing cost.
Today’s the LDPC code supports a wide range of portable communication applications. And the chip must achieve appropriate power consumption for each application over a wide range of performance. Within this context, this paper addresses two key points summarized by the following questions. Can we implement an adaptive hard-wired LDPC reused for different applications, even within same product? How can we perform a safe and optimal LDPC post-silicon performance tuning combining both voltage scaling and body biasing techniques to achieve Low Power LDPC, or Low Operating Power LDPC, or High Performance LDPC?

III. METHODOLOGY IMPLEMENTATION
III.1. The ITRS product classification
According to the ITRS (International Technology Roadmap for Semiconductors), three types of products are identified: High Performance (HP), Low Operating Power (LOP), and Low Standby Power (LSTP) devices integrating different circuit level power optimization techniques such as multi-Vt [6], multi-Tox [6] and multiVdd [6]. This roadmap also suggests some additional power optimization techniques to be applied at architecture, and operating systems levels but also software level.
As a result, each circuit can be classified under one of the three performance categories (HP, LOP, LSTP) according to its speed and power requirements. On the other hand, can we adapt the product performances post-manufacturing to target either High Performance applications or Low Standby Power applications?
Also, as the same speed performance can be reached using different combinations of Voltage Scaling (VS) and Body Biasing (BB), how can we identify the operating point [VS, BB] allowing the best trade-off between processing delay and static/dynamic power?
III.2. Methodology Overview
This work aims to perform a post-silicon tuning of the hard-wired LDPC performances using a safe methodology. The need to minimize power has been the driver

influencing the methodology development and the algorithmic choices. As the ITRS product classification, we have considered three performance tuning modes. First, the High Performance (HP) mode gives the priority to processing delay minimization. Then, the Low Operating Power (LOP) mode enhances circuit speed, while maintaining a minimal dynamic power. Meanwhile, the Low Standby Power (LSTP) mode targets a reduced leakage power.
III.2.1. Setting Regulation Rules
First of all a complete study of the performance requirements for each mode is needed. Thus the speed and leakage targets for each mode could be identified. Also, in order to perform a safe post-silicon tuning, some additional tuning constraints are considered. Among these regulation rules, the minimal speed performance allowed while decreasing performances and saving power. This ‘SPEEDmin’ parameter will ensure the LDPC functionality under tuning. Furthermore, the maximum leakage authorized value ‘LEAKAGEmax’ is needed to avoid an important leakage increase while speeding up the circuit. With respect of the 45nm Low Power technology properties, additional constraints on the safe supply voltage (VDD), the Reverse Body Biasing (RBB), and the Forward Body Bias are identified. These limit values ‘VDDmin’, ‘VDDmax’, ‘RBBmin’, and ‘FBBmax’ will ensure the functionality of both logic and memories.
III.2.2. Speed & Power Characterization
The most straightforward method for saving both dynamic and static energy is lowering the voltage supply. However, the voltage scaling technique reduces power at the cost of a strong delay decrease. In this way, a complementary body biasing tuning should be adapted. The body biasing is expected to be less effective for speed and leakage tuning with technology scaling because of thinner gate oxide and worsening short channel effect. On the other hand, rather than considering it as an obstacle, the reduction of body effect can be an opportunity to perform a complementary fine tuning while using the voltage scaling for large tuning. Moreover, applying the voltage scaling technique only can be limited by the granularity and resolution of the supply voltage generators.
As a result, the three performance tuning modes HP, LOP and LSTP use efficiently the voltage scaling and body biasing techniques to adapt the hard-wired LDPC performances. In this way, at the circuit silicon characterization step, the LDPC maximum operating frequency and the leakage power are measured for different operating points. Each operating point relates to a defined supply voltage (VDD) and body bias (BIAS) values. The used triple well process allows a body biasing on both N-well and P-well, and a simple body biasing policy was adopted. It consists in applying the same body bias simultaneously to both NMOS and PMOS transistors as defined by the expression (1).
BIAS (mV) = VDD-VDDS = GNDS-GND (1)

The High Performance (HP) mode aims to decrease the LDPC processing latency. In order to perform a large delay tuning, priority is given to voltage supply increase till reaching the speed target. In case one of the regulation rules is not met while increasing the supply voltage, a complementary body biasing is used as detailed in figure 3. In this way, the HP algorithm iterates increasing the voltage supply (VDD) or the body bias (BIAS) as long as the regulation rules [VDDmin, VDDmax, RBBmin, FBBmax, SPEEDmin, LEAKAGEmax] are respected.

When, neither voltage scaling, nor body biasing are authorized, the HP algorithm stops and identifies the Valid Operating Point [VDD, BIAS]. In the same way, the Valid Operating Points at different process and temperature limit conditions will be computed and stored in a Look Up Table (LUT). As a final step, only the worst case Valid Operating Point will be considered. In this way, the hardwired LDPC on HP mode will maintain its functionality, and will respect all the regulation rules in spite of process/temperature fluctuations.
The same tuning strategy is applied for the Low Operating Power (LOP) mode. However, for LOP mode the priority is given to Forward Body Biasing (FBB) first. In this way circuit speed is enhanced without compromising dynamic power consumption.
On the other hand, the Low Standby Power (LSTP) mode gives the priority to leakage minimization. The most straightforward method for reducing both dynamic power and leakage is the decrease of the supply voltage. However, in order to maintain an acceptable circuit speed, the ‘SPEEDmin’ regulation parameter should be respected. As a result, a complementary Reverse Body Biasing (RBB) is used to allow further leakage minimization while maintaining an acceptable maximum operating frequency. RBB has an exponential impact on leakage and moderate impact on circuit speed.

The tuning modes [HP – LOP – LSTP] have been computed for different process and temperature limit conditions. As an example, at nominal temperature 30°C, the operating point obtained using the HP mode algorithm is [VDD=1.2V, BIAS=300mV]. Meanwhile, for worst case process and worst case temperature 125°C, the HP operating point is [VDD=1.2V, BIAS=100mV] as the

In this way, in order to guarantee a safe functionality of the hardwired LDPC under tuning, the worst case operating point [VDD=1.2V, BIAS=100mV] is considered for the HP mode as listed in Table 1. This worst case operating point reflects a trade off between performance enhancement and safety under process/temperature fluctuations. The same worst case policy was adapted to identify the operating points [VDD, BIAS] for both LOP and LSTP modes.
As a result, the implemented algorithms help identifying the adapted [VDD, BIAS] operating points requirement for each mode (HP, LOP, or LSTP) without compromising the LDPC functionality. Consequently, the presented adaptive LDPC may target different application requirements.
Finally, in order to achieve an objective evaluation of the methodology, a study of its implementation cost should be performed. This study will address a key point summarized by the following question. Does this adaptive hard-wired LDPC costs less than the development of three independent LDPC circuits with three different performances?
V. CONCLUSION
The main contribution of this paper is to introduce a safe post-silicon tuning methodology for Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) hard-wired IP. The proposed methodology combines both voltage scaling and body biasing techniques to run the LDPC either on High Performance mode (HP), or Low Operating Power mode (LOP), or Low Standby Power mode (LSTP). Concrete silicon results related to the speed and leakage performances of a 45nm chip have been presented. The leakage savings reach x0.73 for LSTP mode and the speed enhancement rises to 24% on HP mode. In this way, the same adaptive hard-wired LDPC encoder/decoder can be adapted to different product requirements in terms of processing latency and power.
