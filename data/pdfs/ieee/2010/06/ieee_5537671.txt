Low Power Decoder Design for QC-LDPC Codes

Kai He, Jin Sha, Li Li
Institute of VLSI Design Nanjing University
Nanjing, Jiangsu 210093, China Email: shajin@nju.edu.cn

Zhongfeng Wang

Abstract—This paper presents a low-power decoder design approach for generic quasi-cyclic low-density parity-check (QC-LDPC) codes based on the layered min-sum decoding algorithm. To reduce the energy consumption, a novel message length-shortening scheme is explored. The check node processing unit (CNU) is accordingly optimized using bit-serial architecture. This low cost design scheme can greatly lower the power consumption while maintaining the necessary throughput required by mobile applications. We further demonstrate the benefits of the proposed techniques by applying the new architecture to the QC-LDPC code in CMMB standard.
Index Terms—Decoder, error correction codes, low-density parity-check (LDPC), VLSI architecture.
I. INTRODUCTION
Proposed by Gallager in 1962 [1], and rediscovered by MacKay in 1996 [2], Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes have attracted much attention due to their near Shannon limit performance. Recently, LDPC codes are widely adopted by many industrial standards such as IEEE 802.11n, DVB-S2, and 802.16e.
The belief-propagation (BP) algorithm, also called sum-product algorithm (SPA) is typically used for decoding LDPC codes. There are many algorithm variations and approximations that have been studied based on SPA. To simplify the BP algorithm, min-sum algorithm [3] is introduced to reduce the complexity of the check node operations. Also, layered decoding approach [4] has been of great interest in LDPC decoder design because it not only converges much faster but also reduces the storage memories for message passing.
In practice, Quasi-Cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes have gained the most attention due to the regularity in their parity check matrices which is well suited for VLSI implementation.[you may cite my 2007 TVLSI paper here] Moreover, QC-LDPC codes can provide comparable error correction performance compared with random LDPC codes [5].
In general, LDPC decoder architecture can be classified into three types: fully parallel, serial and partially parallel. In

fully parallel architecture, every check node or variable node corresponds to an individual node processor unit, which usually leads to large hardware cost, complicated routing, and less flexible. The serial architecture applies just one check node processor unit to perform the computation of all check nodes serially. As a result, it will be too slow for most practical applications. For partially parallel architectures, multiple processing units are used to allow proper trade-off between hardware cost and throughput. Also, partially parallel method can exploit the matrix regularity of specially constructed LDPC codes, such as QC-LDPC codes.
Besides hardware cost and speed requirement, power consumption is another challenge of LDPC decoder design, especially for mobile applications. In this paper, a hardware efficient low-power LDPC decoder design for QC-LDPC codes based on layered min-sum algorithm is presented. The partially parallel architecture efficiently reduces the hardware cost and leads to energy-efficient design compared with other architectures. A bit-serial architecture for check node processing unit (CNU) is explored to support the message length-shortening scheme. It can further reduce the energy consumption by adaptively adjusting the length of messages with ongoing iterative decoding. This architecture is preferable for applications which require low power and moderate throughput.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows: Section II presents the structure of QC-LDPC codes as well as the decoding algorithm. The low-power decoder architecture is presented in Section III. Section IV gives the implementation results and the conclusion is drawn in Section V.

A. Quasi-Cyclic LDPC Codes
The LDPC codes can be defined by a M × N sparse parity check matrix H, in which most of the elements are 0’s and only several elements are 1’s. The parity check matrix HM*N has M rows and N columns, where M and N also represents the number of parity check equations and block length.

B. Layered Min-Sum Algorithm
Min-sum decoding algorithm is a low complexity approximation of SPA and it is also computed in two phases: the check node processing and the variable node processing.

The layered decoding approach improves the convergence speed and thus reduces the number of iterations by dividing the parity check matrix into L layers. Each layer is processed independently and the updated check-to-variable messages can be used immediately. The layers are normally chosen such that the column weight of each layer is no more than 1. C. Length-shortening Scheme

In the implementation of layered min-sum LDPC decoder, the energy consumption in soft message passing is linearly proportional to the word-length of soft messages. Using less number of quantization bits for each soft message can be an effective approach to further reduce energy consumption. However, simply reducing the word length of soft messages usually leads to significant performance degradation. In this section, a length-shortening scheme which uses fewer bits per soft message with the ongoing of iterative decoding is proposed to achieve comparable decoding performance to that using 6 bits per soft message. In order to minimize the performance degradation, the length of variable-to-check message is kept as 6 bits in the first and second iterations. In the following iterations, the message length is shortened for energy saving. This operation is supported by the bit-serial architecture of the CNU. Two length-shortening schemes are proposed. D. Simulation Results and Comparisons
A (9216, 4608) (3, 6) QC-LDPC code in the China Multimedia Mobile Broadcasting (CMMB) standard [8] is chosen as the code example in this work. Fig. 1 illustrates the bit error rate (BER) and frame error rate (FER) performance comparisons of the discussed decoding schemes for an AWGN channel with various Eb / N0 and Imax = 10, where Imax is the maximum allowed decoding iteration times. Note that, at FER=10-3, scheme 1 suffers only 0.03 dB performance loss compared to the normal 6-bit quantization scheme. Scheme2 shows about 0.1 dB performance loss due to the further length-shortening. Similar simulation results are achieved for some other LDPC codes including high rate regular or irregular codes. It should be noted that, by delaying the shorten operation, the performance gap decreases while power increases. By selecting scheme 1 for implementation, assuming 10 iterations are all conducted, 28% of the message passing operations can be saved in total.

III. LOW-POWER DECODER ARCHITECTURE WITH LAYERED
DECODING APPROACH
In this section, based on the layered min-sum algorithm and the proposed length-shortening scheme, a novel QC-LDPC decoder architecture is discussed.
A. Decoding Schedule
For a (N, M) (c, t) QC-LDPC code, the parity check matrix can be partitioned into L layers with each column weight being at most 1. Cn CNUs and Vn VNUs are instantiated in the decoder, with Cn = 5*M/L and Vn = M*t/L. First, Vn columns are processed concurrently in one clock cycle. Then Cn/5 CNUs received Vn variable-to-check messages. Because of the bit-serial architecture, they need 5 clock cycles to generate Cn/5 check-to-variable messages. In the second clock cycle, the Vn VNUs process another Vn columns and send the messages to next Cn/5 CNUs through the Distributer module, and so on. After 5 clock cycles, the VNUs send messages to the first Cn/5 CNUs again. With this decoding schedule, we can finish one iteration in L clock cycles.
B. Overall Decoder Architecture
Fig. 2 shows the decoder architecture from the top level， which mainly consists of an array of Cn VNUs, an array of Vn CNUs, a Channel RAM and an R Storage RAM. Each block of the decoder specifies a particular function being executed. In this decoder, the Channel RAM is used to store the input LLR value of the received data initially. During decoding, it is used to store the posterior reliability values of variable nodes. VNU is used for computing the variable-to-check messages. Each VNU is composed of two adders. CNU is used for generating the Rcv messages for one row of the R Storage RAM.
C. Architecture of the Check Node Processing Unit
To support the message length-shorting scheme, the CNU is accordingly using bit-serial architecture.

Figure 3. Architecture of CNU design
Fig. 3 shows the CNU architecture. All the input Lvc messages are stored in shift registers first so that the CNU can read one message bit each clock cycle. In the first two iterations, the length of variable-to-check messages are 6 bits, including one sign bit. Therefore, a CNU needs 5 clock cycles to finish this row process. The sign bits of the messages are processed independently. The comparator compares the magnitude of the input messages serially in 5 cycles. In practice, the comparator is implemented to hardware with simple “0-selecting” logic. In each clock cycle, if the incoming bit is “1” and there are more than one “0” bits in other messages, the corresponding message passing path will be disabled to reduce unnecessary operation and save power. The shift register which gets the disable signal will stop shifting. If there is only one “0” found in the messages bits, it can be claimed that the minimum or the 2nd-minimum value is found. As soon as the 2nd-minimum value is found, the row operation is finished.

D. Architecture of the Variable Node Processing Unit
The architecture of the variable node processing unit is shown in Fig. 4. The StoT converts the input from the sign-magnitude format to two’s complement. The TtoS is for the reverse conversion. In this design, one VNU is connected with 5 CNUs. The variable-to-check message is generated in one clock cycle and is sent to one of the 5 CNUs. The CNUs receive the new soft messages every 5 clock cycles. As mentioned previously, they need 5 clock cycles to process the messages. A distributor is used to send the message to the corresponding CNU.
IV. IMPLEMENTATION RESULTS
The QC-LDPC codes defined in CMMB standard are chosen as a design example in this work. The decoder supports both rate-1/2 and rate-3/4 regular LDPC codes with the same codeword length of 9216 bits. Table I gives the synthesis result. The number of CNUs and VNUs are 45 and 54, respectively. The clock speed is set to 50MHz as for the low power requirement. The decoding throughput achieved with 10 iterations is 90 Mb/s. The gate count of CNU (12-input messages to support rate 3/4) and VNU are 864 and 337, respectively. In comparison with traditional partially parallel decoder architectures, about 20% energy saving can be achieved by estimation. It should be pointed out that the presented design can run at much higher clock speed with regular supply voltage. In other words, we can operate the design at 50MHz using significantly lowered supply voltage, which will lead to further power reduction. V. CONCLUSION In this paper, a low-cost low-power decoder architecture for generic QC-LDPC codes has been proposed. To enable the length-shortening scheme, a novel bit-serial CNU architecture has been presented. The architecture can reduce energy consumption significantly by reducing the length of variable-to-check messages dynamically. A (9216, 4608) (3, 6) LDPC code in CMMB standard is used as a design example to demonstrate the decoding performance and estimate the decoding throughput and hardware cost.
