An Adaptable Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) Engine for Space Based Communication Systems
Gregory M. Striemer and Ali Akoglu University of Arizona, Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering,
Tucson, AZ 85721, USA gmstrie@email.arizona.edu, akoglu@ece.arizona.edu

Abstract
Space communication systems are characterized by the severe limitations to the on-board computational power and the tight constraints of received signal strengths. Also, these systems observe degradation in signals caused by large propagation latencies, extreme distances traveled, as well as data corruption causing high biterror rates. LDPC codes provide powerful error correction capability where signal power is very low, making them an ideal candidate for space based applications. A hardware architecture that is configurable to dynamic changes in channel conditions is a necessity for error resilient communication systems. In this study we demonstrate the feasibility of designing an FPGA based adaptable LDPC decoder architecture that also matches the throughput demand of current space based communications requirements. We design an LDPC engine that is adaptable to three code rates by taking advantage of the partial reconfiguration technology and parallel nature of the FPGA architecture. We evaluate the tradeoff between the level of parallelism to exploit on the FPGA when implementing LDPC codes and resource demand for each code rate under the constraints of delivering a partially reconfigurable and adaptable solution. Based on the implementation using a Xilinx Virtex-5 FPGA, our design handles context switching between the codes on board in 92μs.
1. Introduction
Compared with common terra and satellite communications, deep-space communications presents challenging constraints for data communications, such as long distance, very low signal to noise ratio, high signal propagation delays and data corruption rates and so on [8]. Therefore, ensuring that low bit error rates are achieved is highly important.
Forward Error Correcting (FEC) codes are used to ensure the reliability and correctness of communications in space, including communication between landers, rovers, and orbiters working in proximity [9]. FECs are utilized to find any errors present in a transmission, as

well as to correct them. This is highly valuable because the retransmission of signals can be very costly if not impossible. The benefit of having FECs become even more apparent as the demand for increasing data rates continues to expand for applications such as hyperspectral imaging, high-resolution stereo cameras, as well as other instruments [16].
The Shannon limit, states that noisy channels have a maximum rate at which data can be transferred through them, and that it is possible for error correcting codes to approach this limit. Because of the losses in signal power over interplanetary distances, codes which can still approach the Shannon limit in these conditions are valuable. Low-complexity encoders and decoders for FECs are necessary, both to manage high data rates and to deal with the limited resources available on space probes [12].
One of the error correcting codes capable of such requirements is the low density parity check (LDPC). LDPC codes were developed by Gallager in 1960 [17], and have become very popular over the past several years for ensuring reliable data transmissions over noisy channels as a mechanism for forward error correction. LDPC codes have become very popular because of their high error-correction performance. One of the primary advantages of LDPC is that it uses an iterative decoding process which can be easily parallelized to match the ever increasing throughput demand. FECs are currently being utilized for deep-space missions with throughput demands in the range of 150Mb/s-1GB/s [10], and are capable of approaching within 1dB of the Shannon limit. These FECs are also being utilized in satellite systems that provide broadcasting services to fixed terminals [13]. LDPC codes provide low decoding complexity with low bit-error rate and superior coding gain over traditional codes [12], making them an ideal candidate for highbandwidth communication systems in space [13].
As the reliability of a transmission channel degrades, or the channel becomes noisier, an LDPC decoder with stronger code rate needs to be employed. However, higher code rate indicates more redundancy in the received data which demands more hardware resources during the decoding process. Therefore it is critical to employ the

An LDPC decoder architecture relies heavily on bitwise operations, similar to cryptography applications. These intensive bitwise computations make Field Programmable Gate Arrays (FPGAs) a suitable architectural match for LDPC applications, because they employ fine grained look up table (LUT) based processing elements. Modern FPGAs are capable of partial reconfiguration (PR) which means parts of the device can be configured to run different code rates. This property allows an FPGA switch between the on-board tasks in near real-time as it eliminates full reconfiguration. Therefore, depending on the bit error rate or other properties, an FPGA can adjust its decoders onthe-fly. By pairing the LDPC decoder technology with FPGAs, a highly powerful and adaptive decoder system can be created. It is also possible for these devices to be reprogrammed remotely, providing the ability to conform to new mission requirements as well as adapt to new technologies without the need for new specialized hardware. The ability of partial reconfiguration also provides FPGAs with a higher level of robustness, as they can be utilized to self-configure themselves if errors should occur [1] . FPGAs also have a very low power consumption, compared to general purpose processors, making them suitable for applications where available power is limited, but high performance is still required.
Our objective in this work is to design an adaptive solution to respond to dynamic channel conditions which takes advantage of the PR capabilities of the Virtex-5 FPGA. This paper proposes a runtime adaptable partially reconfigurable LDPC decoder using a min-sum algorithm, which accommodates three different code-rates. We explore the level of parallelism in our design to exceed a throughput of 1GB/s. We find that we can dynamically switch between decoders in near real-time. Switching between on-board decoders takes up to 92μs, however replacing a decoder with a new one through PR takes up to 3.3 seconds. To our knowledge this is the only PR based LDPC implementation which is adaptable to three different code-rates. The rest of the paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we describe the LDPC algorithm and its mapping on to the FPGA. The partial reconfiguration methodology is discussed in Section 3.

We present our discussion of results in Section 4, and our conclusions and future work in Section 5.

3. Partial Reconfiguration Methodology

In our design we utilize the module-based PR feature of the Virtex-5. A module based approach is well suited for LDPC decoders, because we can simply exchange decoders with ones containing the desired properties. We have designed our PR system to have 2 PR regions, each having the ability to reconfigure to a different decoder. This was done so that one decoder could continue its error correction while the other module reconfigures to another decoder when necessary. Bit streams for decoders (2,3) and (3,4) are initially loaded on the PR regions. This allows for task switching in near real-time. Since the (3,5) has a lower throughput due to its higher complexity, the bit stream for it is stored in a compact flash card connected to the FPGA so that it can be read and dynamically, and configured to the first region when needed. To create a PR design for the Virtex-5, we use Xilinx Platform Studio (XPS), Xilinx ISE 9.2 (Service Pack 4, IP Update 2), Xilinx ISE 10.1 and PlanAhead 10.1.03 software flow.
We use XPS to build the soft processor core, MicroBlaze. This core runs software which notifies the modules when they need to reconfigure to a different decoder. This core will be mapped to the FPGA as a static region. We also use XPS to connect peripherals with the on-chip peripheral bus (OPB), and connect the MicroBlaze processor to on-chip BRAM memories. Each LDPC module is coded as an OPB peripheral, and is considered a slave device. Connections made between MicroBlaze and peripherals are through bus macros, ensuring signals are not lost during reconfiguration. The HWICAP core is used to read and write configuration

memory as well as load PR bit-streams through the ICAP. The user constraints file is also generated by XPS.
We use ISE 9.2 to design and synthesize each of our PR modules (decoders). After synthesis of each PR module, the top module is synthesized in ISE 10.1. The top module includes the processor, all PR modules, as well as the bus macros. A high level view of our defined architecture is illustrated in Fig. 7. We should note that the most stable versions of Xilinx ISE for PR are 9.2 and 10.1. Because of this we do not use the successive releases.
PlanAhead is used to physically define the regions of the FPGA which will be used for each PR module, as well as the static MicroBlaze system. The size of each is determined by the number of slices required for the largest decoder. This is necessary, because all three decoders must be able to fit within each region. Having two reconfigurable modules allows for seamless switching between LDPC decoders since we can pre-fetch and load the bit-streams as channel conditions change. An example of the regions which we define in PlanAhead can be seen in Fig. 9.
4. Results and Discussion
Our design decisions are influenced by two main factors. The first is maximizing the throughput of the decoders, and the second is having the ability to partially reconfigure each of the two PR regions to suit the needs of incoming channel data. As discussed in Section 2, one design choice is to have a PR region which can contain all three decoders, and the second region can contain the (2,3) and (3,4) decoders. With this design, the (3,5) decoder contains 9 check nodes, 15 variable nodes, and has a throughput of 481MB/s. The second option discussed in Section 2 reduces the (3,5) decoder to 6 check nodes and 10 variable nodes, but we can fit all three decoders in either PR region. However, if this is done the throughput is reduced from 481MB/s to 321MB/s as shown in Table 4. This is due to the fact that as the number of nodes decrease, more iterations are required to process the channel data. For example, with 6 check nodes and 10 variable nodes, (3,5) decoder, an LDPC code of size 900x1500 would require 150 iterations, while 9 check nodes and 15 variable nodes requires 100 iterations. 5. Conclusion and Future Work
In this study we demonstrate the feasibility of designing an FPGA based adaptable LDPC decoder architecture that is suitable for space based applications. We take advantage of the partial reconfiguration technology and the parallel nature of the FPGA architecture for designing the decoders for three different code rates. We evaluated the design choices we made during the implementation of these code rates We studied the tradeoff between the throughput and resource demand under the constraints of delivering a partially reconfigurable and adoptable solution. We showed that reconfiguration time is the limiting factor for real time adaptation. We have already started studying fast bitstream relocation mechanisms to accelerate the reconfiguration process [14][15]. With faster relocation mechanisms, overhead associated could be less substantial making PR more feasible for real-time adaptation scenarios. In the near future, we plan to incorporate these mechanisms to our LDPC engine.
