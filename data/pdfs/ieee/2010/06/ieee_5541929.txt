A Quantization Schema with Negligible Degradation for LDPC Decoder
Ming-Qiu Wang, Shu-Tao Xia, He-Guang Su, Lai-Zhong Cui Graduate School at Shenzhen, Tsinghua University Guangdong, China email:xiast@sz.tsinghua.edu.cn

Abstract-A quantization schema with negligible degradation for LDPC decoder is proposed. It's based on the decoding algorithm deduced from Belief Propagation algorithm on LLR. The schema offers a quantization criterion for iteration decoder, based on the study of channel outputs. The schema is adaptable for different channel status and quantization scale. Static step lengths for hardware implementation are selected according to the proposed criterion. The performance of the proposed schema is illustrated by both structured and random codes. In simulations for 6-bits quantization, performance loss is negligible. And the schema produces less than O.01dE degradation for 5-bits quantization above 10-5 BER and about O.ldE for 4-bits quantization. There is no error floor of BER even around 10-7. Simulations show the coverage plays an important role in quantization process. The results of static step lengths are as good as or even better than those of the variable step lengths.
Keywords-channel coding, decoding algorithm, quantization, LDPC.
I. INTRODUCTION
L Ow-density parity-check (LDPC) codes were proposed by Gallager[l], and was almost forgotten due to lack of practical decoder. Only Tanner[2] introduced a graphical representation of LDPC codes, now called Tanner graphs, which is helpful for the decoding of LDPC codes. LDPC codes gained much attention since Mackey's feasible decoder[3], which shows the performance of the codes with very sparse matrices is close to Shannon limit. One of decoders for LDPC codes is Belief Propagation algorithm, which can be described on a Tanner graph. For binary codes, Belief Propagation algorithm processes messages from the channel, which represent the probabilities of a bit being 0 and 1. Other researchers have proposed decoding algorithms with lower computational complexity and better performance. For instance, Belief Propagation algorithm on logarithm reduces the complexity, while Min Sum algorithm gets much lower complexity, but holds higher bits error rate (BER).
For LDPC codes, besides their outstanding performance, efficient quantization of decoding algorithms is the prerequisite for their wide use. For Hardware implementation of decoding algorithm, fix-point computation cell is much cheaper than that of float-point computation. Quantization turns decoding algorithm from float-point compute to fix-point computation.
The quantization schema reported by Li[4] was based on Parity Likelihood Ratio Algorithm (PLRA) which worked on

The plot of his simulation showed the performance loss was about 0.15dB around 10-4 BER adopting 6-bits quantization. And there were obvious error floors around 10-5 BER.
Oh[6] applied quantization on the Min Sum algorithm. So his quantization schema was of low computational complexity. With the offset technique, the schema gained 0.2dB perfor­ mance loss around 10-5 BER for 6-bits quantization. There were error floors started from 10-6 BER.
Kim[7] also applied quantization on the Min Sum algorithm. His work was based on Oh[6], with special adaptive quanti­ zation skills. His quantization schema made a progress on the performance loss, reducing to O.OldB for 6-bits quantization. But there are still error floors around 10-7 BER.
The paper is organized as follows. In section II, Belief Prop­ agation algorithm on Log-Likelihood-Ratio (LLR) is given, and another decoding algorithm is deduced. In section III, we investigated the effects of the modular and the channel noise, applying BPSK and AWGN channel as a typical case. The quantization schema is derived in this section. Simulation results and discussions on implementations of hardware are presented in section IV. Section V serves as a conclusion.

IV. SIMULATION AND RESULTS
Simulation is based on two kinds of binary LDPC codes. One is constructed by Ge[12], with length of 2016, and 1692 information bits. It is structured codes. The other one is constructed by Progressive Edge-Growth (PEG), proposed by Hu[9]. Its block length is 2016, with the same density distribution as the codes constructed by Ge[12]. It is random codes. The maximum iteration is 50.
A. Performance of Quantization
Figure 2 shows the performances of 6-bits, 5-bits, and 4-bits quantization. With criterion (20), 6-bits quantization schema results in negligible loss of performance in forms of BER versus SNR. The performance loss of 5-bits quantization is less than O.OIdB above 10-5 BER, and that for 4-bits quantization is about O.ldB. There is no error floor of BER even under 10-7.
Figure 3 gives the results of the random codes. The perfor­ mance losses of the structured and random codes are just the same. Quantization schema is even better than the continuous one in high SNR for structured codes, while for the random codes, it is not.
B. On Implementation of Hardware
The step length depends on 0"2, which implies the channel
status. The search table is decided by the number of quanti­ zation bits and the step length. The hardware implementation of this schema has to keep a lot of search tables for different channel variances, which is expensive and impossible. This problem can be solved by selecting a static step length for

all channel variances. The criterion is that the static step length should ensure quantization value scale around (20). To simulate hardware implementation, static step length is selected as Table I.
Figure 4 plots the results of 4-bits quantization with a static step length of 0.75 and 6-bits quantization with static step length � = 0.1875. The two step lengths both ensure that the quantization value scales are around criterion (20). The performances of static step length and variable step length are almost the same above 10-5. And for 4-bits quantization, the results of static step length are even better than the variable step length.
C. Criterion for Quantization
Figure 5 explains the quantization criterion. Adopting the same step length, �8 = 0.75, the quantization set for 4-bits is a subset of that for 5-bits, but the result of 5-bits is not better than that of 4-bits. Because the 5-bits quantization extends the quantization scale, which leads to difficult convergence. The decoder will fail when it can not converge before the maximum iteration. So the quantization value scale should not be too large.
For 5-bits quantization with static step length �8 = 0.375, this schema leads to less than O.01dB performance loss above 10-5 BER. But the result is not ideal for 4-bits adopting static step length �8 = 0.375. It is because the static step length �8 = 0.375 for 4-bits quantization causes too much more messages lost. So the quantization value scale, on the other hand, should be large enough.
