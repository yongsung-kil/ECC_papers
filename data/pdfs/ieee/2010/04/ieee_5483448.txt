Irregular M-SC-MPC Codes for Wireless Applications
Marco Baldi, Giovanni Cancellieri, Franco Chiaraluce and Amedeo De Amicis Universita` Politecnica delle Marche Ancona, Italy
E-mail: {m.baldi; g.cancellieri; f.chiaraluce; a.deamicis}@univpm.it

Abstract—In this paper, we elaborate on our previous proposal of Multiple Serially-Concatenated Multiple-Parity-Check (M-SCMPC) codes, that are a class of structured LDPC codes obtained as the serial concatenation of M very simple codes. They have been shown able to achieve good error correction performance while allowing the usage of a very simple encoder architecture, and to compare favorably with the previously proposed M-SCSPC codes, based on Single Parity-Check components. However, we have observed that, in order to avoid error floors in the original M-SC-MPC codes, rather high values of M are needed, that may produce non-optimal performance in the waterfall region. So, we propose a solution to improve the error rate of M-SC-MPC codes without renouncing their structural simplicity. The proposed technique, based on the cancellation of some parity checks within the MPC component codes, permits us to design irregular codes and, in several cases, to reduce the number of nodes having short local cycles in their Tanner graph. We design codes compliant with the IEEE 802.16e specification, and compare them with the codes included in the standard.
I. INTRODUCTION
Modern Forward Error Correction (FEC) schemes are able to approach the fundamental capacity limits by exploiting the so called “turbo principle” [1], which consists in an iterated exchange and updating of reliability values for each received bit. More generally, iterative soft decoding algorithms use, as input, the soft information from the channel in the form of a priori probabilities on the status of each received bit, and iteratively update such values based on the parity-check constraints imposed by the code. Hence, the algorithm can produce extrinsic messages, to be used as starting values for the next iteration, and a posteriori probabilities, that represent the updated reliability values [2]. Such principle is at the basis of turbo codes [1] and Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes [3], [4], that are the state of the art in modern error correction. Such codes have been included in a variety of applications and standards, and they have come to common use in many telecommunication systems.
LDPC codes, in particular, have been included in several standards for wired and wireless communications. Among the latter, we can mention the DVB-S2 [5] and the IEEE 802.16e (or Mobile WiMax) standards [6]. In both cases, the LDPC codes have structured parity-check matrices, that is, matrices with a simple and repetitive inner structure. Opposite to them, very good LDPC codes can be obtained through a constrained random design of the parity-check matrix, but this, together with the need to adopt large codes for achieving

good performance, can yield difficulties in their hardware implementation.
An important class of structured LDPC codes are QuasiCyclic (QC) LDPC codes, having parity-check and generator matrices formed by circulant blocks [7]. Such form of the matrices allows efficient encoding and decoding, often without any loss in the code performance. However, the design of QCLDPC codes is block oriented, thus yielding some constraints on the choice of the code parameters.
We have recently proposed an alternative class of structured LDPC codes, named Multiple Serially-Concatenated MultipleParity-Check (M-SC-MPC) codes [8]. The M-SC-MPC design technique permits us to obtain codes with very simple encoder and decoder structures without losing flexibility in the choice of their parameters. We have shown that M-SC-MPC codes can be designed for wireless applications, by referring to the IEEE 802.16e standard. In particular, in [9], the performance of some M-SC-MPC codes with moderate/high rate has been assessed, and we concluded that they can achieve performance comparable with that of the standard codes.
On the contrary, for moderate/low code rates, QC-LDPC standard codes are able to outperform classic M-SC-MPC codes, especially in the waterfall region. This can be mainly attributed to two facts: i) QC-LDPC standard codes are irregular, while M-SC-MPC are almost regular (except for the triangular part of the parity-check matrix) and ii) QC-LDPC standard codes include null blocks in their matrices, that help to increase the length of local cycles, while M-SC-MPC codes often have a high number of local cycles with length 6. It is known that irregular codes can perform better than regular ones, especially for low code rates [10]. Increasing the length of local cycles in the Tanner graph also improves performance of the belief propagation decoder, that works in conditions closer to the optimal.
Such observations motivate this work, in which we propose a solution for improving the waterfall performance of M-SCMPC codes. The proposed approach exploits the introduction of null blocks in the parity-check matrix of an M-SC-MPC code to design irregular codes with possibly increased cycle length, without compromising the structural simplicity of MSC-MPC encoders and decoders.

II. M-SC-MPC CODES
M-SC-MPC codes are a family of structured LDPC codes we have recently proposed [8]. They represent a generalization of Multiple Serially Concatenated SPC (M-SC-SPC) codes [11], that cannot be defined by LDPC matrices. MSC-MPC codes are characterized by good performance, very good flexibility in the code design and very low encoder and decoder complexity. Each M-SC-MPC code can be seen as the serial concatenation of very simple component codes that are a generalization of Single Parity-Check (SPC) codes. This provides a very simple inner structure of each component code, that facilitates the codecs implementation.
Fig. 1 shows the scheme of an M-SC-MPC code, consisting in the serial concatenation of M component codes, each with length ni, dimension ki and redundancy ri = ni − ki, i = 1 . . . M . As it is evident from the figure, the serial concatenation is systematic, and each component code appends its ri redundancy bits to the input codeword. III. IRREGULAR M-SC-MPC CODES
Based on the description given in the previous section, we observe that M-SC-MPC codes have parity-check matrices that are almost regular. In fact, as it is also evident from Fig. 4, the parity-check matrix of an M-SC-MPC code, in its nontriangular part (that is, the first n1 columns), has column weight constant and equal to M .
Having almost regular parity-check matrices has been proved to be non-optimal [10]; so, the performance of MSC-MPC codes could be improved by introducing some form of irregularity in the non-triangular part of the matrix. The approach we propose aims at increasing irregularity of the parity-check matrix though preserving its structured character and the very simple encoder implementation described in Section II. Other solutions could be adopted, also aimed at optimizing the degree distributions of irregular matrices, but they are out of the scope of this paper and will be considered in future works.
Our proposal is to replace some of the identity blocks included in one or more matrix layers with null blocks. This is equivalent to switch off some columns in the encoder shown in Fig. 2, in such a way that the input bits in those columns do not participate to any parity-check equation. The proposed technique reduces the weight of some columns within the matrix, so introducing irregular weights. As a counterpart, the cancellation of some blocks reduces the code minimum distance, since the corresponding MPC encoder has no more effect on the input bits corresponding to such blocks. For this reason, in order to obtain codes with good minimum distance, when the proposed block nulling is performed on the paritycheck matrix, it can be necessary to increase the value of M (i.e. the number of concatenated codes) with respect to the case without block nulling. These aspects will be further investigated in Section V.

IV. LDPC CODES IN IEEE 802.16E
We focus on the design of codes for application in wireless systems, as mobile wireless Metropolitan Area Networks (MAN) compliant with the IEEE 802.16e standard [6]. We consider LDPC codes already included in the standard, and design M-SC-MPC codes with the same parameters, in order to compare their characteristics and performance.
The IEEE 802.16e standard, approved in 2005, includes, as an option, the possibility of adopting LDPC codes for forward error correction. As common in wireless applications, the FEC scheme must be able to adapt itself against variable channel conditions, providing different solutions as tradeoffs between error correcting capability and spectral efficiency. For this reason, a family of channel codes with different rates must be provided. In addition, all standards in the IEEE 802 family deal with packet switched networks having variable length blocks; so, flexibility in the block length is a mandatory requirement for channel codes to be used in these systems. In order to fulfill such needs, the standard adopts a family of QC-LDPC codes having variable code rate (R = 1/2, 2/3, 3/4, 5/6) and length ranging between 576 and 2304 bits by steps of 96 bits. These codes are used in conjunction with QPSK, 16-QAM and 64-QAM modulation. Standard QC-LDPC codes have paritycheck matrices formed by z × z circulant permutation blocks or null blocks. V. CODE EXAMPLES
In this section, we give some examples of code design with the goal to compare M-SC-MPC codes and their irregular versions with IEEE 802.16e standard QC-LDPC codes. For this purpose, we consider codes having three values of rate (R = 1/2, 2/3, 3/4) among those specified by the standard and length n = 1632. We have designed some M-SC-MPC codes and irregular M-SC-MPC codes with exactly the same length and rate; so, we can compare their performance over the AWGN channel, under the hypothesis of using BPSK modulation.
Table II shows the parameters of the codes considered in our examples, that are of three types: QC-LDPC codes compliant with the IEEE 802.16e standard, classic M-SC-MPC codes and irregular M-SC-MPC (i-M-SC-MPC) codes. In the latter case, the table also reports the number (cj) of identity blocks nulled in the j-th matrix layer (that is, the number of columns switched off in the j-th component encoder). Such values, that form what we call a nulling pattern, have been found on a heuristic basis; so, margins exist for further optimization.
More precisely, for a given nulling pattern, the choice of the blocks to cancel in each layer has been made through a

constrained random search, based on two criteria: i) avoiding the appearance of very low weight columns in the nontriangular part of the matrix (denoted by dv(i) the weight of the i-th column, we fixed dv(i) ≥ 3, i ∈ [1; n1]) and ii) optimizing the length of local cycles. Table II also reports the average column weight of each matrix, dv , and the number of variable nodes having local cycles with length 6, L{6}. Since length-4 cycles are avoided by design, the remaining n − L{6} variable nodes have local cycles with length ≥ 8. It should be noted that, differently from standard codes, MSC-MPC and i-M-SC-MPC codes have weight-1 columns in their parity-check matrices. More precisely, the columns with weight 1 are those corresponding to the last component code; so, their number is rM . For this reason, M-SC-MPC and i-MSC-MPC codes have rM variable nodes without local cycles, and the maximum number of nodes with length 6 is reduced to n − rM . As a counterpart, weight-1 columns correspond to as many variable nodes that are not affected by the decoding algorithm, so they may deteriorate the decoder performance, similarly to what happens in Low-Density Generator-Matrix (LDGM) codes [14].
The simulation results and the parameters of the considered codes are compared in the following subsections.
A. Codes with rate 3/4
The first four codes we have considered in our examples have rate 3/4, and their simulated performance is shown in Fig. 7. Code C1 is the rate 3/4 “B” QC-LDPC code recommended in the IEEE 802.16e standard [6]. As we notice from Table II, C1 has average column weight 3.67, that is comprised between the average column weight of the two MSC-MPC codes we have designed with M = 4 and M = 5, respectively. They are denoted by C2 and C3 in the figure. We also have designed an i-M-SC-MPC code (C4), obtained by applying the nulling pattern [4, 0, 3, 2, 0] to the parity-check matrix of code C3. This way, the average column weight is reduced, and approaches that of the standard code.
In the case of C4, the cancellation of some blocks within the parity-check matrix did not reduce the number of variable nodes with length-6 local cycles. Despite this, block nulling is still able to improve the performance of C3, by lowering the average column weight and increasing the parity-check matrix irregularity. We notice that, for rate 3/4, the standard code has length-6 local cycles for all variable nodes.
As we observe from Fig. 7, the M-SC-MPC code with M = 5 has good error floor performance, but its waterfall begins at a rather high signal-to-noise ratio. On the other hand, the M-SC-MPC code with M = 4 has a good waterfall, but it is counterbalanced by a high error floor. The i-M-SCMPC code C4 outperforms the M-SC-MPC codes and exhibits better curves both in the waterfall and in the error floor region. Its performance approaches that of the standard code, that is able to obtain the lowest error rates in the waterfall region. However, the error rate curves of code C4 exhibit a more favorable slope with respect to those of the standard code, and intersect them in the error floor region.

B. Codes with rate 2/3
Similarly to what done for rate 3/4 codes, we have considered four codes with rate 2/3. Code C5 is the rate 2/3 “B” QC-LDPC code recommended in the IEEE 802.16e standard [6]. It has the lowest average column weight and only 1292 variable nodes with length-6 local cycles (the remaining 340 nodes have length-8 cycles).
Also in this case, we have designed two M-SC-MPC codes with M = 4 and M = 5, denoted by C6 and C7, respectively. Compared to C5, they have a larger dv and a greater number of variable nodes with length-6 local cycles. This number can be reduced through the block nulling approach, by adopting, for the parity-check matrix of code C7, the same nulling pattern used for rate 3/4 codes. This way, we have obtained the i-M-SC-MPC code C8, having a parity-check matrix with a lower column weight than for C7, and 84 nodes with local cycles length 8 instead of 6.
The simulated performance, plotted in Fig. 8, confirms that C8 has better performance than C7, due to the block nulling technique. Its performance is also better than that of C6, that exhibits a high error floor. Even more noticeably, code C8 is also able to outperform the standard code, especially at high signal-to-noise ratios, where the latter exhibits a change in the slope of its error rate curves.

VI. CONCLUSION
We have considered our previous proposal of a class of structured LDPC codes, named M-SC-MPC codes, and we have proposed a possible improvement of their design technique. The main idea consists in canceling some blocks within their parity-check matrices, in order to design codes with more irregular matrices and a potentially smaller number of variable nodes with length-6 cycles. Both such characteristics facilitate the convergence of the belief propagation decoder, so improving its performance in the waterfall region. On the

other hand, the proposed technique does not compromise the regular structure of M-SC-MPC matrices, thus preserving the possibility of using very simple encoders.
We have applied the proposed technique to the design of some codes compliant with the Mobile WiMax standard, and compared their simulated performance with that of QC-LDPC codes already included in the same standard. Our results show that the proposed technique is effective in improving the performance of original M-SC-MPC codes, by producing better waterfall behavior without causing the occurrence of high error floors. The designed codes have performance comparable with that of standard codes and even better, for some choices of their parameters.
The selection of the parity-checks canceled in each component encoder has been done on a heuristic basis, and with the constraint of canceling entire blocks to facilitate implementation. As a further work, more elaborate criteria could be developed, that may allow further control over the matrix irregularity and optimization of the node degree distributions.
