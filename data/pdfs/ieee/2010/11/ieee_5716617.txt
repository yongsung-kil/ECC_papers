A 5.7Gbps Row-Based Layered Scheduling LDPC

Decoder for IEEE 802.15.3c Applications

Shiang-Yu Hung, Shao-Wei Yen, Chih-Lung Chen, Hsie-Chia Chang, Shyh-Jye Jou, and Chen-Yi Lee
Department of Electronics Engineering National Chiao Tung University
Hsinchu, Taiwan 12

Abstract—A LDPC decoder chip supporting four code rates of IEEE 802.15.3c applications is presented. The row-based layered scheduling with normalized min-sum algorithm is proposed to reduce the iteration number while maintaining similar performance. In addition, the reconfigurable 8/16/32-input sorter is designed to deal with four LDPC codes. In order to alleviate routing complexity, both of the reallocation of sorter inputs and pre-coding routing network are proposed to reduce the input numbers of multiplexers by 64%. Fabricated in 65-nm 1P10M CMOS process, this test chip can achieve maximum 5.79Gbps throughput for the highest code rate code while the hardware efficiency and energy efficiency are 3.7Gbps/mm2 and 62.4pJ/bit, respectively.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) code, a famous error control code with near Shannon limit performance, was firstly introduced by Gallager [1] and has attracted many research interests after rediscovery [2] by MacKay. The LDPC code is based on the iterative belief propagation (BP) algorithm and is capable of parallel implementation for higher decoding speed than turbo decoders. Several LDPC designs [3] have been demonstrated due to the advance of CMOS process. Newly high-speed communication systems such as IEEE 802.15.3c [4] and IEEE 802.11VHT have also adopted LDPC codes to enhance performance and data rate.
In LDPC codes, the rows and columns of parity-check matrix H are mapped to check nodes and variable nodes in the Tanner graph. A Tanner graph is also called a bipartite graph, which is analogous to the trellis of convolution codes. The decoding algorithms of LDPC codes iteratively exchange messages between the two types of node to update the posteriori probabilities of variable nodes. The order of exchanging messages between nodes is called scheduling, which will influence the convergence speed of the decoding algorithm. The layered scheduling and shuffle scheduling have been proposed in [5] and [6] to increase the convergence speed. The former performs message update along different check node groups while the latter performs along different variable node groups. Experiments show that the two scheduling methods could reduce about half maximum iterations compared to the standard BP algorithm with similar performance.
In this paper, four irregular LDPC codes are provided by IEEE 802.15.3c standard. The supported code rates, information block lengths, and codeword block lengths are

listed in TABLE I. These parity-check matrices can be horizontally and equally decomposed into 4 sub-matrices (H0 to H3). Each sub-matrix is called a layer, and it is composed of several z×zʳ (z =21 in our work)ʳ circulant permutation matrices and all-zero matrices as shown in Fig. 1. The number in each grid indicates the shift amount and “-” represents all-zero matrix.
According to the parity-check matrices, the column degree of each layer is at most one. Thus, the corresponding layers (e.g. HA3, HB3, HC3, and HD3) of the four LDPC codes could be merged vertically, leading to convenience for design. As shown in Fig. 1, the first and the second sub-rows of HA3 are combined and form the first sub-row of HB3. Step by step, all the four sub-rows in HA3 are merged into a single sub-row in HD3. However, the row degrees of the four parity-check matrices are 8, 16, 16, and 32, respectively. This will cause the incompatibility of check node size and require suitable decoder architecture to avoid additional hardware cost.
There are several challenges in architecture design for 802.15.3c standard include high throughput requirement, complicated signal routing of irregular parity-check matrices and hardware overhead to support four code rates. In this paper, the proposed decoder aims at providing an area-efficient and energy-efficient approach to multiple LDPC codes of IEEE 802.15.3c applications. In order to achieve the throughput requirement, row-based scheduling and normalized min-sum (NMS) decoding algorithm [7] are employed to accelerate the decoding convergence speed. Based on the structure of parity-check matrices, a sorter input reallocation technique and routing pre-coding network are proposed to support multiple code rates in shared hardware architecture. Besides, the two proposed techniques also mitigate routing complexity of message passing between the two types of node.
This paper is organized as follows. The decoding algorithm and row-based layered scheduling are introduced in Section II. The proposed hardware architecture is demonstrated in detail in Section III. Based on the proposed architecture, Section IV shows the simulation and the implementation results. Finally, a conclusion is given in Section V.

II. ROW-BASED LAYERED SCHEDULING WITH NMS ALGORITHM
In order to speed up the convergence of decoding, row-based layered scheduling with NMS algorithm is exploited to enhance throughput. The initialization, stopping criterion test, and output steps remain the same as the BP algorithm with flooding schedule. The only difference between the two algorithms lies in the message updating procedure. Row-based layered scheduling allows the updated information to be utilized more quickly, so the error-rate can reach the same level as the normal flooding schedule in less iteration number. In addition, the NMS algorithm which compensates the approximation error by a normalized factor ȕ to improve performance can be applied in our approach.
In the row-based layered scheduling, the rows of parity-check matrix H are horizontally divided into G layers (G=4 in our work). The message updating between check nodes and variable nodes within a layer would be done in one sub-iteration where one iteration indicates the process of completing every layers of H sequentially. The log-likelihood ratio (LLR) of intrinsic information of n-th variable node is denoted by Pn. The message from m-th check node to n-th variable node and the message from n-th variable node to m-th check node are defined as rmn and qnm, respectively. The posteriori LLR of n-th bit is denoted by Zn. The updating procedure of NMS algorithm with row-based layered scheduling is carried out as follows.

III. PROPOSED DECODER ARCHITECTURE
In this section, the proposed decoder architecture will be presented. The entire decoder architecture depicted in Fig. 2 is composed of fully-parallel variable node groups (VNG) and proposed check node unit (CNU). Each CNU consists of three blocks, including sorter inputs reallocation, reconfigurable 8/16/32-input sorter, and pre-coding routing network. Early termination block is applied to avoid redundant decoding time and power as soon as the valid codeword is detected.
A. Reconfigurable 8/16/32-input sorter
For each CNU, a sorter is needed to sort out the first and second minimum magnitude in (1), where the size of sorter is decided by row degree. However, the incompatibility of row degrees among the four codes leads to the mismatch of sorter size. For example, the row degrees of HA and HD are 8 and 32 at most, corresponding to an 8-input sorter and a 32-input sorter, respectively. Since a 32-input sorter is composed of four 8-input sorters, one CNU in code D is equivalent to four CNUs in code A. Similarly, the other two LDPC codes can also be mapped by the same rule. Consequently, the incompatible check node degrees can be unified. Fig. 3 shows the architecture of a reconfigurable 8/16/32-input sorter. The intermediate results of 8-input and 16-input sorters should be preserved for output.
B. Sorter inputs reallocation
In the V-to-C routing network, the adjacent variable nodes of each sorter input depend on the four LDPC codes and different updating layers, so the straightforward implementation makes the routing network inefficient and impractical. In TABLE II, the Original part shows an example of the connected variable nodes for a sorter input. It implies that a 12-to-1 multiplexer is needed to connect all neighboring variable nodes. Totally, 672 (=21ͪ32) 12-to-1 multiplexers are required for all 21 32-input sorters. Undoubtedly, such complicated interconnection will cause routing congestion and increase delay time of critical paths. To solve this problem, the sorter inputs reallocation is proposed to alleviate the routing complexity. The main concept is to reallocate the order of sorter inputs considering the code features without affecting the result of 8/16/32-input sorting.

C. Pre-coding routing network
In the C-to-V routing network, there are 3 kinds of message passing to VNGs, including minimum, second minimum value and minimum index. Besides, due to the same condition described in Section III-B, 12-to-1 multiplexers are also needed for each kind of messages. The complexity of C-to-V routing becomes more critical as compared with V-to-C routing.
Therefore, the pre-coding routing network is proposed to lessen the routing overhead of C-to-V. Based on the code structure of H matrices, only one kind of sorter outputs (8-sorter, 16-sorter, or 32-sorter) would be selected in the network during decoding process, the other sorter outputs shall not be chosen in the network. For example, as shown in Fig. 3, only the sorter outputs represented by bold line shall be selected in the network for the decoding process of code D. In conclusion, the pre-coding routing network does the coarse selection and diminishes the outputs of sorter, so the messages from CNUs can be reduced.

D. Summary Due to the proposed sorter inputs reallocation and pre-coding routing network, the routing complexity between the check nodes and variable nodes is substantially reduced. The following gives the comparison for the input numbers of multiplexers before (Mbit) and after (MbitR) reduction. Let the bit-width w of the messages to be 6-bit, and dc and z denote the maximum row degree of the four codes and the size of circulant permutation matrix. Mbit = V-to-C Routing + C-to-V Routing = z × dc × w ×12 + N × (Min + 2ndMin + Index + Sign) ×12 = z × dc × w ×12 + N × (2(w − 1) + log2 dc + 1) ×12 = 21× 32 × 6 ×12 + 672 ×16 ×12 = 177408
MbitR = V-to-C Routing + C-to-V Routing + Pre-coding Network = z × dc × w × 4 + N × (Min + 2ndMin + Index + Sign) × 4
+ 4 × z × (Min + 2ndMin + Index + Sign) × 3 = 21× 32 × 6 × 4 + 672 ×16 × 4 + 4 × 21×16 × 3 = 63168
The overall reduction of multiplexer inputs for our proposed architecture is 64%, thereby leading to lower routing overhead and higher hardware utilization. It is noteworthy that the overhead of hardware cost by accommodating the four LDPC codes is only the pre-coding routing network.
IV. SIMULATION AND IMPLEMENTATION RESULT
The performance is simulated to determine the normalized factor of NMS algorithm and the required bit-width under AWGN channel with 16-QAM modulation. Based on the simulation results, 6-bit input (4-bit integer and 2-bit decimal fraction) and the normalized factor of 0.75 are chosen as design parameters. Fig.5 shows the bit-error-rate (BER) curves for the proposed LDPC decoder, and the BER curves indicate that 5 iterations for the proposed decoder can achieve almost similar performance of standard BP algorithm with 15 iterations.

The proposed LDPC decoder is implemented by standard cell design flow and has been fabricated in 65-nm 1P10M CMOS technology. The die photo and the distribution of key modules are shown in Fig. 6. The chip size is 5.3mm2 while the core occupies 1.562mm2 with 73.3% utilization density. The total gate count is 723k, including testing module of 76k (encoder and AWGN modules). According to the measurement results, this work can achieves 5.79Gbps throughput with 5 decoding iterations under 1V supply voltage. The average power dissipation is 361mW (code D at SNR 9dB).
Compared with the state-of-the-art listed in Table III, the proposed LDPC decoder outperforms the others in the aspects of hardware efficiency and energy efficiency. This work has at least 5 times and 30% improvements in energy efficiency and hardware efficiency, respectively. Moreover, the proposed decoder can support four LDPC codes while the others only support one specified code type.
V. CONCLUSION
An area-efficient and energy-efficient LDPC decoder is presented that supports the four LDPC codes of IEEE 802.15.3c applications. Row-based layered scheduling with NMS algorithm is employed to enhance throughput. Based on the special code structure in each layer, the proposed reconfigurable 8/16/32-input sorter architecture could support four LDPC codes with negligible hardware overhead. Furthermore, the interconnection between check nodes and variable nodes can be simplified with the proposed reallocation sorter inputs scheme and pre-coding routing network. The multiplexer inputs can be reduced up to 64%, leading to an area-efficient design. Moreover, early termination scheme can further avoid redundant decoding time and power. Fabricated in 65-nm CMOS process, the chip occupies 1.562mm2 core area and achieves maximum 5.79Gbps data rate at 1V.
