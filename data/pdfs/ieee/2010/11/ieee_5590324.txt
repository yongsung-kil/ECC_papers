Quasi-Cyclic LDPC Codes:
An Algebraic Construction, Rank Analysis, and
Codes on Latin Squares
Li Zhang, Qin Huang, Shu Lin, Life Fellow, IEEE,
Khaled Abdel-Ghaffar, Member, IEEE, and Ian F. Blake, Fellow, IEEE
AbstractвҖ”Quasi-cyclic LDPC codes are the most promising
class of structured LDPC codes due to their ease of implementa-
tion and excellent performance over noisy channels when decoded
with message-passing algorithms as extensive simulation studies
have shown. In this paper, an approach for constructing quasi-
cyclic LDPC codes based on Latin squares over finite fields is
presented. By analyzing the parity-check matrices of these codes,
combinatorial expressions for their ranks and dimensions are
derived. Experimental results show that, with iterative decoding
algorithms, the constructed codes perform very well over the
AWGN and the binary erasure channels.
Index TermsвҖ”Latin square, row-column constraint, row-
distance constraint, quasi-cyclic LDPC code.
I. INTRODUCTION
L
DPC codes have attracted widespread interest because
of their remarkable performance that can be achieved
by efficient decoding algorithms. These codes, first discov-
ered by Gallager in 1962 [1], laid dormant for about 35
years until their rediscovery in the late 1990s [2], [3]. Ever
since their rediscovery, a great deal of research effort has
been expended in design, construction, structural analysis,
encoding, decoding, performance analysis, generalizations and
applications of LDPC codes. Many LDPC codes have been
chosen as the standard codes for various next generations
of communication systems. Applications of these codes for
storage systems to achieve very low error rates are now being
seriously investigated.
A regular binary LDPC code рқ’һ[1] is given by the null
space of a sparse parity-check matrix H over GF(2) that has
constant column weight рқӣҫand constant row weight рқңҢ, where рқӣҫ
and рқңҢare small compared to the code length. Such an LDPC
code is said to be (рқӣҫ, рқңҢ)-regular. If the columns and/or rows

In almost all of the proposed constructions of LDPC codes,
the following constraint on the rows and columns of the parity-
check matrix H is imposed: no two rows (or two columns)
can have more than one position where they both have 1-
components. This constraint on the rows and the columns of
H is referred to as the row-column (RC)-constraint. The RC-
constraint on H ensures that the Tanner graph [4] of the LDPC
code given by the null space of H is free of cycles of length
4 and hence has a girth of at least 6 [5], [6], [7]. Let рқӣҫрқ‘ҡрқ‘–рқ‘ӣ
be the minimum column weight of H. The RC-constraint on
H also ensures that the minimum distance of the LDPC code
given by the null space of H is lower bounded by рқӣҫрқ‘ҡрқ‘–рқ‘ӣ+ 1
[5], [6], [7]. This lower bound on the minimum distance of
an RC-constrained LDPC code is poor for small рқӣҫрқ‘ҡрқ‘–рқ‘ӣand
irregular LDPC codes. However, it is tight for regular LDPC
codes whose parity-check matrices have large column weights,
such as finite geometry LDPC codes [5] and finite field LDPC
codes constructed in [8] and this paper.
If the parity-check matrix H of an LDPC code is an array
(or a block) of sparse circulants of the same size over GF(2),
then the null space of H gives a binary quasi-cyclic (QC)-
LDPC codes. Many of the algebraic constructions of LDPC
codes result in QC-LDPC codes [8]-[28]. Extensive simulation
studies have shown that algebraic QC-LDPC codes have very
good performance over noisy channels when decoded with
message-passing algorithms, see [6], [7] and the references
therein. Well designed algebraic QC-LDPC codes can perform
close to the Shannon limit and just as well as (or even better
than) their corresponding random or pseudo-random QC-
LDPC codes constructed using computer-based methods over
the AWGN and the binary erasure channels, as demonstrated
in [8], [17], [21], [24], [28]. In addition, QC-LDPC codes
also enjoy advantages in hardware implementation of their
encoding and decoding. Their encoding can be implemented
with simple shift registers with linear complexity [19]. For
hardware decoder implementation, their QC-structure simpli-
fies wire routing [29] and allows partially parallel decoding
[29], [30] which offers a trade-off between decoding com-
plexity and decoding speed. The good performance and the
ease of implementation of QC-LDPC codes continue to attract
widespread interest among coding researchers in their design,
construction and structural properties.

This paper is concerned with algebraic constructions of QC-
LDPC codes and rank analysis of their parity-check matrices.
We first consider a general class of algebraic QC-LDPC codes
and then a specific class constructed based on Latin squares
over finite fields. Construction based on Latin squares over
finite fields results in a class of QC-LDPC codes much larger
than any class of algebraic QC-LDPC codes constructed in
[8].
The paper is organized as follows. Section II presents a
general algebraic method for constructing QC-LDPC codes. A
general framework to determine the rank of the parity-check
matrices is developed in Section III. Although the general
algebraic method for constructing QC-LDPC codes presented
in Section II was presented in [8], [17], [24], these papers
did not provide any analysis of the ranks of the parity-check
matrices of the constructed codes. In this paper, such an
analysis is provided. This analysis generalizes and simplifies
the rank analysis of a class of QC-LDPC codes constructed
based on cyclic MDS (or RS) codes [27] with two information
symbols. In Section IV, we present a large class of algebraic
QC-LDPC codes constructed based on Latin squares over
finite fields, called QC-LDPC codes on Latin squares, and
analyze the minimum distances of codes in this class. The
construction of QC-LDPC codes on Latin squares follows
the general method explained in Section II. (The construction
methods as well as the constructed codes significantly differ
from those based on BIBDs devised using Latin squares
[31], [32], [33]). In Section V, we analyze the ranks of
the parity-check matrices of several subclasses of QC-LDPC
codes on Latin squares and derive combinatorial expressions
for these ranks. Section VI considers a special subclass of
QC-LDPC codes on Latin squares and presents an extension
of this subclass. In Section VII, a known technique, called
masking [6], is used to construct irregular QC-LDPC codes
on Latin squares. Section VIII presents another construction
of RC-constrained Latin squares based on RS codes with two
information symbols. We show that the major construction of
QC-LDPC codes based on finite fields (method-1) presented
in [8] is actually a construction based on a special subclass of
Latin squares over finite field constructed based on minimum
weight codewords of RS codes with two information symbols.
Therefore, the construction based on Latin squares presented
in this paper gives a much larger class of QC-LDPC codes
than the first method presented in [8]. Section IX concludes
the paper with some remarks.

VI. A SPECIAL SUBCLASS OF QC-LDPC CODES ON
LATIN SQUARES AND AN EXTENSION
In this section, we consider a special subclass of QC-LDPC
codes on Latin squares and an extension of codes in this
subclass. We show that codes in this special subclass not
only perform well with fully soft-input soft-output iterative
decoding based on belief propagation using SPA but also
perform well with a reliability-based iterative binary message-
passing decoding algorithm (RB-IBMPDA) with reduced de-
coding complexity which was recently proposed in [41].
The RB-IBMPDA requires only integer additions and binary
logical operations. The number of integer additions required
per iteration is equal to the number of 1-entries in the parity-
check matrix of the code to be decoded.

Masking a рқӣҫГ— рқңҢarray H(рқӣҫ, рқңҢ) = [Bрқ‘–,рқ‘—] of CPMs is done by
replacing a set of CPMs in H(рқӣҫ, рқңҢ) = [Bрқ‘–,рқ‘—] by a set of ZMs.
The masking process begins with designing a low-density рқӣҫГ—рқңҢ
matrix Z(рқӣҫ, рқңҢ) = [рқ‘§рқ‘–,рқ‘—] over GF(2). Then, take the following
matrix product: M(рқӣҫ, рқңҢ) = Z(рқӣҫ, рқңҢ) вҠ—H(рқӣҫ, рқңҢ) = [рқ‘§рқ‘–,рқ‘—Bрқ‘–,рқ‘—],
where рқ‘§рқ‘–,рқ‘—Bрқ‘–,рқ‘—= Bрқ‘–,рқ‘—for рқ‘§рқ‘–,рқ‘—= 1 and рқ‘§рқ‘–,рқ‘—Bрқ‘–,рқ‘—= 0 (a
(рқ‘һвҲ’1) Г— (рқ‘һвҲ’1) ZM) for рқ‘§рқ‘–,рқ‘—= 0. Z(рқӣҫ, рқңҢ) is called the
masking matrix, H(рқӣҫ, рқңҢ) the base array and M(рқӣҫ, рқңҢ) the
masked array. Since the base array H(рқӣҫ, рқңҢ) satisfies the RC-
constraint, the masked array M(рқӣҫ, рқңҢ) also satisfies the RC-
constraint, regardless of the masking matrix. It is clear that
the density of the masked array is lower than the base array.
The null space of the masked array gives a QC-LDPC code
which is different from the code given by the null space
of the base array. If both the masking matrix and the base
array are regular, the masked array is also regular and its null
space gives a regular QC-LDPC code. However, if the masking
matrix is irregular and the base array is regular, the masked
array is irregular and its null space gives an irregular code.
To construct an irregular code to achieve good performance,
the masking matrix Z(рқӣҫ, рқңҢ) is constructed with column and
row weight distributions identical or close to the variable-
and check-node degree distributions of a Tanner graph that
are designed based on density evolution to optimize the
performance of a code (asymptotically) for a specific rate [7],
[45]. After masking, the column and row weight distributions
of the masked array, as a рқӣҫ(рқ‘һвҲ’1)Г—рқңҢ(рқ‘һвҲ’1) matrix over GF(2),
are identical or close to the designed variable- and check-node
degree distributions. Then, the null space of the masked array
would give a close to optimal performance if the code is long
enough.

IX. CONCLUSION AND REMARKS
In this paper, we first analyzed the rank of the parity-check
matrix of a QC-LDPC code constructed based on the array
dispersion of an RD-constrained matrix over a finite field.
Then, we presented a class of RD-constrained Latin squares
over finite fields. Based on this class of RD-constrained Latin-
squares over finite fields, we constructed a large class of
QC-LDPC codes, called QC-LDPC codes on Latin squares.
The ranks of the parity check matrices of several subclasses
of QC-LDPC codes on Latin squares were analyzed and
combinatorial expressions were derived. Several subclasses of
QC-LDPC codes on Latin squares were shown to have large
minimum distances. Some QC-LPDC codes on Latin squares
were constructed. Computer and FPGA-decoder simulations
showed that the constructed codes perform very well over
