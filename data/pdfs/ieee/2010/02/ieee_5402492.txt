Low-Floor Detection/Decoding of LDPC-Coded Partial Response Channels

Yang Han and William E. Ryan

Abstract—The error-rate floor phenomenon associated with iterative LDPC decoders has delayed the use of LDPC codes in certain communication and storage systems. Error floors are known to generally be caused by so-called trapping sets which have the effect of confounding the decoder. In this paper, we introduce two techniques that lower the error-rate floors for LDPC-coded partial response (PR) channels which are applicable to magnetic and optical storage. The techniques involve, via external measures, “pinning” one of the bits in each problematic trapping set and then letting the iterative decoder proceed to correct the rest of the bits. We also extend our earlier work on generalized-LDPC (G-LDPC) decoders for error-floor mitigation on the AWGN channel to partial response channels. Our simulations on PR1 and EPR4 channels demonstrate that the floor for the code chosen for this study, a 0.78(2048,1600) quasicyclic LDPC code, is lowered by orders of magnitude, beyond the reach of simulations. Because simulation in the floor region is so time-consuming, a method for accelerating such simulations is essential for research in this area. In this paper, we present an extension of Richardson’s importance sampling technique for estimating the level of error floors.
Index Terms—error floor, LDPC codes, partial response channels, bit pinning, trapping sets
I. INTRODUCTION
T HE ERROR(-RATE) floor phenomenon for LDPC codes under iterative decoding can be characterized as an abrupt decrease in the slope of a code’s performance curve from the moderate-SNR water-fall region to the high-SNR floor region [1]. It is well known that the error floors are usually due to low-weight near-codewords [2], or trapping sets [1], rather than low-weight codewords. A (w, v) trapping set is a set of w variable nodes which induce a subgraph with v odd-degree check nodes (unsatisfied check nodes, USC nodes) and an arbitrary number, usually substantially larger than v, of even-degree check nodes (mis-satisfied check nodes, MSC nodes). Throughout the paper, to simplify the language, we shall often use “trapping set” when we are referring to the induced subgraph. When w and v are relatively small, errors in each of the w variable nodes create v parity check failures and tend to lead to situations from which the iterative decoder cannot escape.
Due to the presence of unions of cycles in trapping sets, conflicting decisions from different locally operating check nodes are introduced. Such conflicts often lead the iterative decoder away from the maximum-likelihood codeword. MSC nodes and 5 USC nodes, and each (5,7) trapping set has 9 MSC nodes and 7 USC nodes. The dimension (w, v) of a trapping set roughly indicates the relative importance of this trapping set among others for a given LDPC code: w is related to how likely the detector/decoder receives a particular channel noise realization that lead to such a trapping set; v indicates to some extent how difficult it is for the decoder to escape because the smaller the v is, the less extrinsic information is available to break the decoder from its trapping state. A more accurate ranking of the trapping sets of a given code in terms of how they dominant the floor can be evaluated using direct simulations and importance evaluation.
For most practical codes, because the trapping set-induced subgraphs associated with the error floor are relatively small, it is feasible to discover, enumerate, and evaluate all of the dominant trapping sets with the help of a computer. Once we obtain the dominant trapping sets for an LDPC code by simulation and by some graph-search techniques (such as in [3], [4], [5], [6]), we can deliberately target the known trapping sets with novel, custom-tailored iterative decoder designs.
Much research has been dedicated to solving or mitigating the floor problem by attempting to design LDPC codes with low floors (for example, [7], [8], [9]). However, the problem is usually the decoder, not the code, and only a few papers focus on decoder-based strategies for lowering floors. For example, [10] and [11] attempt to lower the floors of LDPC codes by modifying the scheduling of the decoders. Reference [12] proposes an averaged decoding algorithm which decreases the frequency of trapping set error events by averaging the messages over several iterations. In [13], the floors are reduced by utilizing linear programming-based decoding. Also, in [3] and [17] a post-processor is proposed which, after the decoder becomes trapped, exploits the knowledge of the dominant trapping sets by inverting the bits of the trapping set associated with the decoder’s failed parity checks .
One solution for freeing an iterative decoder from a trapping set is to disturb the stabilized subgraph structure. As shown in Fig. 1(a-b), when one of the variable nodes in a (5, 5) trapping set is “pinned” by setting its bit value to 0 (or 1) prior to encoding and correspondingly setting to the maximum (or minimum) value the log-likelihood ratio input to the decoder, the entire trapping set is corrected with two additional LDPC decoder iterations. Similarly, a (5, 7) trapping set needs at most two additional iterations as in Fig. 1(c-d). In this paper, we apply and extend such ideas to partial response channels.

Before we introduce our floor-lowering techniques, we first emphasize that the trapping sets indicated above for the (4544, 4096) code and its derivative (2048, 1600) code were found via simulations on the binary-input AWGN channel. In general, when one applies a code to a different channel, or even a different decoder, the trapping sets can change. Or, more accurately, since the graphical configuration of trapping sets remain unchanged for the same code, the relative importance of each trapping set may vary on different channels. We have observed that the most dominant trapping sets for the binary-input AWGN channel are also the dominant trapping sets for the PR1 and EPR4 channels. This is understandable for the case when the PR BCJR detector output is approximately conditionally Gaussian, i.e., is much like the binary-input AWGN channel. Thus, our pinning techniques for these two PR channels will target the aforementioned classes of (5, 5) and (5, 7) trapping sets and our results support this approach.

When the LDPC decoder fails to get past a trapping set, the BCH decoder corrects the bit error due to the trapping set and then feeds back all 256 correct bits value to the channel detector, effectively pruning its trellis. Eventually, the residual errors in the trapping set will be corrected by additional LDPC decoder iterations.
For the post-pinning solution, it is important to handle the case when the BCH code has mis-correction. When miscorrection happens, the outer BCH decoder will feed wrong information to the LDPC decoder, making it pin the wrong variable nodes or pin the correct variable nodes with wrong values. We call these two cases “mis-pinning”. As discussed in [27] for BCH and RS codes, the probability of mis-correction is zero if the number of errors does not exceed the designed correction capability t. When the number of errors is well above the code’s capability, the mis-correction rate increases. Mis-pinning can be prevented with high probability by the following: (1) After the first round of LDPC decoding before BCH decoding, check the syndrome weight of the LDPC decoder hard-decision output. If the syndrome weight is larger than a predetermined threshold θ, which indicates the LDPC decoder converges to an error event that does not belong to the targeted trapping set list, the BCH decoder is by-passed and no pinning is performed. (2) If the LDPC decoder syndrome weight is less than θ, BCH decoding is performed. Then we check how many bits are flipped by the BCH decoder through comparing the BCH decoder input with its output; if the number is more than designed correction number t (normally

essentially zero, and so BCH decoding and pinning will be bypassed. Thus, the error-rate curve in the waterfall region will not be degraded. On the other hand, because the floor region of the error-rate curve is attributable entirely to trapping set events, the BCH decoding and pinning scheme will perform in exactly the fashion for which it was designed. That is, one bit in the failed trapping set will be pinned to its correct value after BCH decoding, allowing the LDPC decoder to correct the remaining trapping set errors. In this way, the level of the floor will be vastly reduced while not impacting the waterfall region.

is possible in principle to group all of the SPC constraints into one combined global constraint and design a decoder for that graph. But that would be an ML decoder (for example), which has unacceptable complexity. Our strategy is to instead take one step toward that ideal and cleverly combine only a few SPC constraints at a time. Specifically, we combine SPC node processors corresponding to unsatisfied checks in the problematic trapping sets and call the combination a generalized-constraint processor (GCP). Observe that an advantage of combining SPC nodes is that cycles and other deleterious graphical structures (from the perspective of an iterative decoder) may be removed.
Such a G-LDPC decoder can be applied to LDPC-coded PR channels. We show how this is done on the QC-LDPC code under study. As listed in Table I, the code has 10 dominant trapping set classes (640 trapping sets). By examining the list of unsatisfied checks of these trapping sets, we found that combining 128 SPCs into 64 GCPs, pairwise, assures each trapping set has at least one GCP bridging it to another trapping set. The rationale is when the decoder becomes trapped in one trapping set, reliable information from its companion trapping set will be passed along the GCP, allowing recovery from the trapping set error event.
The constituent decoder for the GCP can be any softinput/soft-output decoder. We use a locally optimal decoder which employs the BCJR algorithm designed to the “BCJR trellis” [28] [29] for the linear code represented by the GCP. The G-LDPC decoder, essentially the SPA with GCP’s (denoted by SPA-GCP) passes soft information iteratively between VN processors and GCP’s in the same manner as the SPA decoder. See [15] for details.
The performance of this G-LDPC decoder on the PR1 channel is shown in Fig. 11. We have simulated the case of no global iterations and five global iterations between the detector and the decoder, respectively. As shown in the figure, for both the G-LDPC decoder and the conventional SPA decoder, performing global iterations offers around 0.5 dB FER improvement in the waterfall region. For both the G= 0 and G= 5 cases, the conventional SPA decoder curves quickly run into error floors. We remark that it is a general observation that an increased number of iterations does not help the error

floor performance. Fig. 11 also shows that the error floors for both cases (G= 0 and G= 5) are substantially reduced by the G-LDPC decoder, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the G-LDPC decoders on the PR channel. Moreover, unlike the pinning solutions, the G-LDPC decoder has no performance loss at medium SNR region. In consideration of complexity, the technique techniques require no additional computation than the non-pinning detector/decoder solution. The G-LDPC decoder’s complexity largely depends on the constituent decoder for GCPs. While the BCJR GCP decoder is optimal, it is possible to design lower complexity GCP decoders.
VII. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
This paper examined bit-pinning, trellis-pruning techniques and the G-LDPC decoder for mitigating the error floors on partial response channels. Given the knowledge of the dominant trapping sets of an LDPC code of interest, trellis-pruning/bitpinning can be applied to the detector/decoder by directly pinning certain bits prior to transmission or utilizing outer BCH codes; G-LDPC decoders can be designed to connect dominant trapping sets to prevent the decoder from converging the trapping sets. We demonstrated that these techniques lower the error floor of our (2048, 1600) LDPC code on the PR1 and EPR4 channels efficiently. A power transfer gain which offsets any code rate loss was observed for the trellis-pruning case.
The pinning and G-LDPC solutions can be easily generalized to other practical LDPC codes. For example, in an earlier work [15], we demonstrated the success on the Margulis code on the AWGN channel. Based on our observations on various high-rate QC-LDPC codes suitable for applications such as magnetic recording, where low error-floors are required, there typically exists a few (usually less than 10) trapping set classes that dominate the error floor performance. The elimination of these trapping sets may provide as much as 6 orders of magnitude improvement in error floor region. Another observation is that the variable nodes of dominant trapping sets are very likely overlapped, making it possible to select a relatively small pinning set.
