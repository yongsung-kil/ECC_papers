The Effect of Cycles on Binary Message-Passing Decoding of LDPC Codes
Gottfried Lechner
Institute for Telecommunications Research University of South Australia Adelaide, Australia gottfried.lechner@unisa.edu.au

Abstract—We study the error-floor behavior of binary message-passing decoders for low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. We find that the stability condition is independent of the quality of the channel messages (i.e. soft or hard decision). Furthermore, we identify a structure in the graph where the involved variable nodes cannot be corrected by the binary message-passing decoder. This leads to an error floor for regular LDPC codes with variable node degree smaller than four. An additional constraint on the degree distribution is derived which allows to avoid this structure, leading to optimized LDPC codes without error floors.
I. INTRODUCTION
When Gallager introduced low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1], [2], he also presented binary message-passing decoding algorithms that exchange only binary messages between the variable and check node decoder. These algorithms are referred to as Gallager A and Gallager B [3]. The advantages of these algorithms are the reduced memory requirements and the low complexity implementation, especially of the check node decoder, making them promising candidates for high-speed applications, e.g. optical transmission systems [4]. However, these advantages come at the cost of a significant loss in performance.
Binary message-passing algorithms were studied in [5], [6] where the authors proved that optimum algorithms must satisfy certain symmetry and isotropy conditions. In [7], the authors extended binary message-passing algorithms by assuming that the variable node decoder converts all incoming messages to L-values [8], performs decoding in the L-value domain and applies a hard decision on the result. This general approach assures that the symmetry and isotropy conditions are satisfied and the algorithms can be extended for systems where the channel provides more information than hard decisions, while the variable and check node decoder still exchange binary messages only. This reduces the gap between optimum decoding and binary message-passing decoding, while the complexity is kept low.
Richardson and Urbanke showed in [9] that the degree distribution of an LDPC code has to satisfy a stability condition to enable the decoder to converge to zero error probability. If the stability condition is not satisfied, then the performance of the decoder is dominated by an error floor at good channel conditions (e.g. high signal-to-noise ratio, low error probability). In [6], the stability condition for binary message-passing

decoders in combination with the binary symmetric channel (BSC) has been derived.
The main contributions of this paper are
• We derive the stability condition for binary messagepassing decoders and soft-decision channels.
• We show that for finite length codes, there is an additional constraint on the fraction of degree two and three variable nodes that has to be satisfied to avoid an error floor.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. In Section II we derive the transfer functions for variable and check node decoders. These functions are used in Section III to derive the stability condition for binary message-passing decoders. In Section IV we show that the existence of cycles leads to an additional constraint on the variable node degree distribution. Finally, simulation results are presented in Section V.

IV. EFFECT OF CYCLES

Analysis tools like density evolution and EXIT chart assume

that the decoding neighborhood is a tree. Therefore, the factor

graph of the LDPC code has to be cycle-free to allow for an

infinite number of iterations. It has been shown in [12] that

finite length codes which can be represented by cycle-free

graphs have poor properties in terms of minimum distance.

Therefore, when constructing finite length LDPC codes, a

common approach is to maximize the girth of the graph while

avoiding some specific constellations (e.g. cycles of length g

including only variable nodes of degree two lead to a minimum

distance

For binary message-passing decoders, we identify a con-

stellation that leads to error floors which are caused by the

decoding algorithm and not by the properties of the code.

Theorem 2. Assume a cycle formed by variable nodes of degree two and three only. Furthermore, assume that the channel messages associated with the nodes in the cycle are in error. If all other incoming messages at the involved check nodes are correct, then the variable nodes building the cycle cannot be corrected by the binary message-passing decoder.

Proof: An example of such a cycle is shown in Figure 1, where the left edges correspond to the channel messages which are all assumed to be in error. In the first iteration the variable nodes send out their received messages. Since every check node in the cycle is connected twice to the set of variable nodes, the outgoing messages from the check nodes are also in error.
In the following iterations, the outgoing messages at the variable nodes are computed according to (8). For a degree two variable node, the extrinsic L-value is the sum of the channel L-value and the L-value of the other incoming message. Since both messages are in error, the outgoing message is also in error. For a variable node of degree three, one message is not involved in the cycle (as shown in Figure 1). Assume that this message is correct. The messages sent out on the other two edges of that variable node are then computed as the sum of the channel L-value and two L-values from the check nodes (a correct and an incorrect one). Since the messages from the check nodes have the same magnitude they cancel out and the extrinsic message is in error due to the erroneous channel message. Since this is the same state as in the first iteration, the decoder can not correct the nodes forming the cycle.
If the factor graph contains cycles consisting of variable nodes of degree two and three only, there is a nonzero probability that the involved channel messages are in error leading to a decoding failure. Similar to stopping sets [13], this situation leads to error floors. According to Theorem 2, to avoid an error floor, the factor graph of an LDPC code must not have such cycles. VI. CONCLUSION
We studied binary message-passing algorithms for LDPC codes with soft and hard decisions from the channel. The stability condition for soft channel messages has been derived and the result is the same as for hard decisions from the channel. We showed that cycles in the factor graph (which are unavoidable for finite block lengths) lead to error floors under binary message-passing decoding for regular LDPC codes with dv < 4. Furthermore, we derived a condition under which this error floor can be avoided.
