A class of generalized quasi-cyclic LDPC codes: high-rate and low-complexity encoder for data storage devices
Vo Tam Van, Hajime Matsui, Seiichi Mita Dept. Electronics and Information Science Toyota Technological Institute
2–12–1 Hisakata, Tenpaku, Nagoya, 468–8511, Japan Email: {sd07501,hmatsui,smita}@toyota-ti.ac.jp

Abstract—In this paper, we study no 4-cycle, high-rate LDPC codes based on finite geometries for use in data storage devices and prove that these codes cannot be classified as quasi-cyclic (QC) codes but should be considered as broader generalized quasi-cyclic (GQC) codes. Because of the GQC structure of such codes, they can be systematically encoded using Gröbner bases and their encoder can be implemented using simple feedbackshift registers. In order to demonstrate the efficiency of the encoder, we show that the hardware complexity of the serial-in serial-out encoder architecture of these codes is of linear order O(n). To encode a binary codeword of length n, less than 2n adders and 3n memory elements are required. Furthermore, we evaluated the error performances of these codes with sum product algorithm (SPA) decoding over additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels. At a bit error rate (BER) of 10−5, they perform 1-dB away from the Shannon limit after 10 decoding iterations.
Index Terms— generalized quasi-cyclic (GQC) codes, finite geometry low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, circulant matrix, serial-in serial-out systematic encoder.
I. INTRODUCTION
In addition to turbo codes, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes form another class of Shannon limit (or channel capacity) approaching codes. LDPC codes were first discovered by Gallager [5] in 1962 and were rediscovered and generalized by MacKay [13] in 1999. The methods for constructing LDPC codes can be classified as random and algebraic construction methods. It has been observed that the performance of irregular LDPC codes with code lengths longer than 107 bits constructed by random construction methods [13] is close to the Shannon limit. However, the encoding of these LDPC codes is quite complex because their code structure is not cyclic or quasi-cyclic. Therefore, researchers concentrate on algebraic constructions of LDPC codes. LDPC codes can be algebraically constructed in several ways [3], [18] based on circulant matrices; these codes belong to a class of quasi-cyclic (QC) codes. In accordance with their quasi-cyclic structure, some efficient encoding methods [4],[9] that involve circulant matrices and division techniques have been proposed. Another interesting method for algebraic construction of LDPC codes is based on finite geometry (FG) [7],[10]. These codes, divided

into Euclidean (or affine) geometry (EG) and projective geometry (PG) codes, have received considerable attention from coding researchers owing to their large minimum distances and very good error performance up to 0.3-dB of the Shannon limit. However, most of finite geometry LDPC codes proposed in [7] cannot be presented in cyclic or quasi-cyclic form, and hence, their encoding cannot be realized using linear shift registers.
In this study, we prove that no 4-cycle, high-rate LDPC codes based on finite geometries which are included not in QC codes but in broader generalized quasi-cyclic (GQC) codes (cf. Fig. 1) [17],[19]. It can be stated briefly that GQC codes have a higher randomness than QC codes as they are formed by varying the lengths of cyclic parts of QC codes. In addition, GQC codes form the largest algebraic class of linear codes with a compact encoder architecture. Therefore, we can select more appropriate high-performance codes from among GQC finite geometry codes than from among QC finite geometry codes. In [15], we evaluated the performance of finite geometry LDPC codes on magnetic recording channels in the presence of pattern-dependent noise and found that they performed well with iterative decoding using sum-product algorithms (SPA). These results motivated us to continue with the investigation of systematic encoders of finite geometry codes. Owing to the GQC structure of such codes, they can be systematically encoded and their encoder can be implemented using simple feedback-shift registers. In this paper, we also attempted to prove that the hardware complexity of the serialin serial-out encoder architecture of the codes is of linear order O(n). To encode a binary codeword of length n, less than 2n adders and 3n memory elements are required.
This paper is organized as follows. Section 2 provides the definition of GQC codes and a brief review of finite geometry LDPC codes. Section 3 proves the GQC structure of finite geometry LDPC codes. In Section 4, we prove the linearity of the circuit scale of the systematic encoder architecture for FG-LDPC codes and show the simulation results for finite geometry LDPC codes. Finally, this paper is concluded in Section 5.

Example 3: Let m = 4 and q = 22. PG(4, 22) over F2 consists of 341 points and 5797 lines. All the lines in PG(4, 22) can be partitioned into 17 cyclic classes of size l1 = l2 = · · · = l17 = 341. To construct the 4-D type-II PG-LDPC code, we form the parity-check matrix HP(2G) (4, 22) using the incidence vectors of all the 5797 lines over 341 points as columns. We obtain a 341 × 5797 matrix with row weight = 7 and column weight = 57. The null space of this matrix provides the type-II PG-LDPC code, which is a (5797,5507) code with a rate of 0.95 and a minimum distance of at least 8. This code exhibits a very good bit error performance for SPA decoding in comparison with random (computer generated) LDPC code of the same length and code rate. At a bit error rate (BER) of 10−5, the performance of this code over AWGN channels is 1-dB away from the Shannon limit after 10 decoding iterations. Moreover, we evaluated the performance of this code by retrieving a real data series from a perpendicular magnetic recording channel at a BER of approximately 10−3. In Fig. 3, we use several decoding methods in combination with SPA method such as PR3 (partial response class 3), PR1 with post processor [14], and the hyper-plan method [15]. We can observe that this PG–LDPC code can correct all the errors after several iterations and that the number of iterations for HP1 (the best case) can be reduced by approximately half, as compared to that for PR3. This observation indicates the effectiveness of finite geometry LDPC codes in data storage channels. In the next section, we will show that finite geometry LDPC codes

also have an efficient systematic encoder with linear hardware

complexity and their encoders can be implemented using only

linear feedback-shift register circuits.

V. CONCLUSIONS
In this study, we first proved that LDPC codes based on finite geometries (Euclidean and projective geometry) are generalized quasi-cyclic (GQC) codes. Due to their GQC structure, we can construct an efficient systematic encoder for finite geometry LDPC codes based on the Gröbner bases theory. Furthermore, we also showed that the hardware complexity of the serial-in-serial-out systematic encoder is in the linear order of code-length for FG-LDPC codes. To encode a binary codeword of length n, less than 2n adders and 3n memory elements are required. Because of their very good error performance, we believe that they can be used in future magnetic recording channels.
