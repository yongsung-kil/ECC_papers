An Improvement on the Soft Reliability-Based Iterative Majority-Logic Decoding Algorithm for
LDPC Codes
T. M. N. Ngatched, Attahiru S. Alfa, and Jun Cai Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering
University of Manitoba Winnipeg, MB R3T 5V6
Canada Email: {ngatched, alfa, jcai}@ee.umanitoba.ca

Abstract—This paper presents an improvement of the reliability-based iterative majority-logic decoding algorithms for regular low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes proposed by Huang et al. We improve the computation of the extrinsic information that is used to update the reliability measure of each received bit in each iteration with some kind of reliability measures of the check-sums that are orthogonal on the considered bit. The improved algorithm achieves a significant gain over the standard one with only a modest increase in computational complexity.
I. INTRODUCTION
First introduced by Gallager [1] in 1962, low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes were recently brought back into prominence by MacKay and Neal [2] and shown to form a class of Shannon limit approaching codes for a wide range of channels when decoded with iterative decoding algorithms also known as message-passing algorithms [3]. Since their rediscovery, design, construction, encoding, decoding, generalizations, performance analysis and applications of LDPC codes have been attracting a great deal of research interest. Many classes of LDPC codes have been constructed, and a wide spectrum of decoding algorithms, ranging from low to high complexity and from reasonably good to very good performance, have been designed.
The algorithms for decoding LDPC codes can be classified into three general categories: hard-decision decoding( [1, p. 39], [4, pp. 273-304], [5]), soft-decision decoding( [1, pp. 40-48], [3], [6]–[9]), and hybrid decoding (also known as reliability-based decoding) ( [10]–[17]). From an implementation point of view, hard-decision decoding is the simplest in complexity; however, its simplicity results in a relatively poor performance that can be as far away as a few decibels from that of soft-decision decoding. Soft-decision decoding provides the best performance but requires the highest computational complexity. Hybrid decoding is in between the two

extremes and provides a good trade-off between performance and complexity.
Among hybrid decoding algorithms, the soft reliabilitybased iterative majority-logic decoding (SRBI-MLGD) algorithm recently introduced in [17] offers one of the best tradeoff between performance and complexity. The algorithm is designed based on the orthogonal check-sums concept used in the one-step majority-logic-decoding (OSMLGD) algorithm ( [4, pp. 273-304], [10]). The decoding function of the SRBI-MLGD algorithm includes some kind of soft reliability measures of received symbols which are improved through decoding iterations. Simulation results show that SRBI-MLGD performs just as well as many variants of weighted bit-flipping (WBF) algorithms and the differential binary message-passing decoding (DBMPD) algorithm [16], with much less decoding complexity. Furthermore, it has a faster rate of decoding convergence. An important feature of SRBI-MLGD is that it is a binary message-passing algorithm and requires only logical operations and integer additions. This feature significantly simplifies the decoder implementation, as it can be achieved using simple combinational logic circuits. Another feature of SRBI-MLGD is that it allows parallel decoding of all the received symbols in each decoding iteration, which is important to achieve a very high decoding speed.
This paper presents a further improvement of the SRBIMLGD algorithm. The improvement is achieved by introducing a reliability measure for each check-sum of the paritycheck matrix, and taking it into account in the computation of the extrinsic information that is used to update the reliability measure of each received bit in each iteration. Then the total extrinsic information contributed to each received bit is appropriately scaled to update its reliability measure. This improvement, while requiring very little additional computational complexity, results in a significant performance improvement over the SRBI-MLGD algorithm.

II. SRBI-MLGD ALGORITHM [17]
A binary LDPC code is completely described by its sparse binary parity-check matrix H. For an (N, K) LDPC code, H has N columns and M ≥ N − K rows. For a regular
LDPC code, H has the following structural properties: (i) every row has weight ρ; (ii) every column has weight γ; (iii) no
two rows (or two columns) have more than one 1-component in common; and (iv) both ρ and γ are small compared to the length N of the code. The set of bits that participate in check m is denoted as N (m) {n : Hmn = 1}. Similarly, the set of checks in which bit n participates is denoted as M(n) {m : Hmn = 1}.
Suppose a binary (N, K) LDPC code is used for error
control over a binary-input additive white Gaussian noise (BI-
AWGN) channel with zero mean and power spectral density N0/2. Assume binary phase-shift-keying (BPSK) signaling with unit energy. A codeword c = (c0, c1, . . . , cN−1) ∈ {GF (2)}N is mapped into the bipolar sequence x = (x0, x1, . . . , xN−1) before its transmission, where xn = (2cn − 1) with 0 ≤ n ≤ N − 1. Let y = (y0, y1, . . . , yN−1) be the soft-decision received sequence at the output of the receiver matched filter. For 0 ≤ n ≤ N − 1, yn = xn + vn where vn is a Gaussian random variable with zero mean and variance N0/2. Let the corresponding binary hard-decision of the received sequence be z(0) = z0(0), z1(0), . . . , zN(0−) 1 . For any tentative binary hard-decision z made at the end of
each decoding iteration, the syndrome vector is computed as s = z · HT . The log-likelihood ratio (LLR) for each channel output yn, 0 ≤ n < N , is denoted as Ln. Its absolute value, |Ln|, is called the reliability of the initial decision zn(0). For the AWGN channel, a simple measure of the reliability, |Ln|, of a received symbol yn is its magnitude, |yn|. The larger the magnitude |yn| is, the larger the reliability of the hard-decision digit zn(0) is.
Suppose the samples of the received sequence y are symmetrically clipped at a threshold yth, and then uniformly quantized in the range [−yth, yth]. There are 2b−1 quantization intervals, symmetric with respect to the origin. Each quantization interval has a length Δ = 2yth/ 2b − 1 and is represented by b bits. For 0 ≤ n ≤ N −1, let qn = Q (yn) denote the quantized value of the sample yn which is an integer representation of one of the 2b − 1 quantization intervals. The range of qn is therefore − 2b − 1 , + 2b − 1 . If the magnitude |yn| is larger than the quantization range 2b − 1 Δ, then qn is set at − 2b − 1 or 2b − 1 , depending on the sign of yn. The magnitude |qn| of the quantized sample qn gives a soft measure of the reliability of the hard-decision received bit zn. The SRBI-MLGD uses qn as the initial reliability measure of the bit zn at the beginning of the decoding process and improves it through the decoding iterations. III. IMPROVED ALGORITHM
A. Algorithm
In the SRBI-MLGD algorithm, the check-sums in M(n) orthogonal on zn are treated equally. This implicitly assumes that these check-sums have the same reliability. This could be true only if the reliabilities of the received symbols were equal. However, since the reliabilities of the received symbols are different, the reliabilities of different check-sums are also different. Introducing a reliability measure for each check-sum in M(n), and taking it into account in the computation of the total extrinsic information, En(k), can improve the accuracy of

B. Computational Complexity
From the above description, one can find that compared with the standard algorithm, the modified one requires more 3M ρ − 3M real-number comparisons to compute the weighting coefficients wm,n in the initialization part, and (γ − 1) N real number additions to compute the extrinsic information. It should be noted that, since the scaling factor α is a real number, the updated reliability measure of each received bit is also a real number. Ignoring the cost of the computation of the weighting coefficients wm,n, since it is done only once at the initialization, one iteration of the proposed algorithm requires a total of 2δ + N − M logical operations, (δ − 1) N integer additions, and N real additions. Thus the cost per iteration of the modified algorithm is slightly higher than that of the standard algorithm. The slight increase in complexity of ISRBI-MLGD however is counter-balanced by a significant improvement in performance as will be illustrated in the next section.
IV. SIMULATION RESULTS
In this section, using Monte Carlo simulations, the error performance in terms of bit-error rate (BER) and frame-error rate (FER) as a function of the rate-normalized SNR per information bit, of the improved algorithm is compared with that of the standard SRBI-MLGD on a number of high-rate FG-LDPC [10] codes of various length. The following Type-I 2-D Euclidean geometry (EG)-LDPC codes are simulated: the (255, 175) code, the (1023, 781) code, and the (4095, 3367) code. The codes are labeled as C1, C2, and C3, respectively. The rates of these codes are 0.686, 0.763, and 0.822, respectively. The performance of the codes using iterative decoding based on belief propagation (IDBP) and the improved modified weighted bit-flipping (IMWBF) of [13] is also included. The IMWBF is one of the best performing variant of the weighted bit-flipping (WBF) algorithm for FG-LDPC codes in terms of error performance. For the proposed algorithm and the standard SRBI-MLGD, we use 8-bit uniform quantization with 255 levels; the quantization step Δ, which is obtained through simulations, is 0.0787, 0.0308, and 0.015 for C1, C2, and C3, respectively. For the proposed algorithm, the optimal value of α, which is also obtained through simulations, is 0.1, 0.12, and 0.1 for C1, C2, and C3, respectively. The maximum number of iterations is set to 50 for both the proposed and the standard algorithm, and 200 for IDBP and IMWBF; for C1 it is however set to 100 for the IDBP and IMWBF. At each SNR value, at least 200 codewords errors are detected for C1, and 100 codewords errors for C2 and C3.
Figs. 1 - 3 show the BER and FER performances of C1, C2, and C3, respectively. It can be observed that, at the BER of 10−5, the improved algorithm outperforms the standard one by about 0.5 dB, 0.25 dB, and 0.25 dB for C1, C2, and C3, respectively. The same observation can be made as far as the FER is concerned. The proposed algorithm performs practically the same as IMWBF for all the three codes, and is only 0.6 dB, 0.55 dB, and 0.5 dB away from IDBP for C1, C2, and C3, respectively. V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, an improvement of the SRBI-MLGD algorithm for regular LDPC codes has been proposed. Simulations results performed on a number of high-rate FG-LDPC codes of various length show that this improvement, while requiring very little additional computational complexity, results in a significant performance improvement over the SRBI-MLGD algorithm.
