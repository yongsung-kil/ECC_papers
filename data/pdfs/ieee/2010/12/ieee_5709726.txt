Analog Realization of Iterative Threshold Decoding
based on High-Order Recurrent Neural Networks
Mohamad Mostafa, Werner G. Teich, J¨urgen Lindner
Institute of Information Technology, University of Ulm
Albert-Einstein-Allee 43, D-89081 Ulm, Germany
Email: mohamad.mostafa@uni-ulm.de
Abstract—Artificial neural networks (ANN) are known for
their ability to solve classification and optimization tasks and
have been applied in many fields of communications such as
equalization and multiuser detection, among others. In this paper
an analog realization of iterative threshold decoding for binary
linear codes is presented. It is shown that the iterative threshold
decoding algorithm matches well with the structure of a con-
tinuous high-order recurrent neural network. The performance
of the analog realization has been evaluated by simulation and
is compared with the corresponding digital realisation. The
motivation of this work is that analog decoding improves the
power/speed ratio and minimizes the area consumption on the
very large scale integration (VLSI) chip.
Index Terms—Iterative threshold decoding, high-order recur-
rent neural networks, analog signal processing.
I. INTRODUCTION
In channel coding, to achieve low error rate, long codes are
required. On the other hand, the optimum decoding complexity
of many good codes grows exponentially with the length of
the code. Using long codes but still with feasible decoding
complexity has driven the idea of iterative decoding. An ex-
ample of such long codes with iterative decoding are the low-
density parity-check codes (LDPC) and turbo codes [1]. Those
powerful error correcting codes were developed to satisfy the
increasing demand for efficient and reliable communication
systems. Nowadays turbo codes are the standard forward error
correction scheme for the Universal Mobile Telecommuni-
cation System (UMTS) [2] and for Long Term Evolution
(LTE) [3]. Iterative decoding algorithms require floating point-
computations because of the exchange of soft information.
Therefore they are computationally very demanding for high
data rates. They need a large computation power to deliver
real-time results, which is in contrast to the mobile users
requirement of long battery live time. Doing the decoding in an
analog way improves the power/speed ratio and minimizes the
area consumption on the very large scale integration (VLSI)
chip. Teich et al. [4], [5] presented a convolutional code
decoder based on a multilayer perceptron. This structure can
be understood as analog decoding, if the multilayer perceptron
is realized in an analog way. Hagenauer et al. [6] designed an
analog decoder for a tail-biting convolutional code as well as
for a turbo code with (7,4) Hamming codes as component
codes. In addition to discrete-time simulation results, the au-
thors provide continuous-time simulation results, which follow
closely the first one. Winstead et al. [7] investigated an analog
MAP decoder for the (8,4) Hamming code. It was reported
that this analog decoder achieves an order of magnitude better
power consumption than a comparable digital implementation
and consumes less silicon area.
A new concept for analog iterative decoding was suggested
by Hemati et al. in [8], [9]. In this case the dynamics of
analog iterative decoding is modeled by a system of first-
order nonlinear differential equations. This system can be
solved numerically by application of successive relaxation to
a fixed point problem. This model assumes, that the analog
circuits are ideal. It can be used to efficiently study the
behaviour of an analog iterative decoder once an estimate of
the delay distribution between the variable and check nodes is
achievable. Hemati & Banihashemi [10] have shown that the
near-steady-state performance of the analog iterative decoder
is achievable if the decoder is allowed to operate on a given
input for a certain multiple of the time constant of the circuit.
The obtained error rate results based on this model have
been compared with measured results for an analog iterative
decoder chip in [11]. The results are consistent particulary
for low signal-to-noise ratios (SNR), where the channel noise
dominates the imperfections due to nonideal circuitry. De-
varakonda & Winstead [12] investigated the accuracy of this
abstract dynamical model. The difference between the outputs
of this abstract model and a corresponding SPICE model
is calculated for randomly constructed codes and random
stimuli. It has been reported that the calculated difference
increases when the input is currupted with single or multiple
errors. However, in most of the simulations both models were
able to correct the error(s) in the input code. This holds for
codes of increasing block length. This shows the importance
of the system of first order nonlinear differential equations.
In contrast to the complex and costly transistor-level circuit
simulations, this model provides a good approximation for the
performance of practically analog-realized iterative decoders
with low complexity.
The parity check equations of binary linear codes can be
visualized in a very effective way using Tanner graphs [13].
Furthermore, Tanner graphs give good insight into the code
structure and the structure of iterative decoding algorithms.

III. HIGH-ORDER RECURRENT NEURAL NETWORK
Trained neural networks have successfully been applied
for channel equalization [20]–[22] and decoding [4], [5].
The structure of some dynamic neural networks is especially
suitable for certain tasks, like using Hopfield networks as
multiuser detectors [23], [24]. In this case no training is
required because of the identical structure of the network and
the task. High-order neural networks have been successfully
applied in pattern classification [25]–[27], usually producing
better performance, both in approximation accuracy and com-
putational cost, than conventional neural architectures such as
the multilayer perceptron (MLP). Hopfield [28] has shown that
a system of highly interconnected “neurons” has useful col-
lective computational properties. The mathematical modelling
has been based on “neurons” that are different from a realistic
functioning of a simple electronic circuit. Hopfield proved
that a modified model [29], built of operational amplifiers and
resistors, has the same collective properties like the original
model. The new model “the analog model” has a quadratic
energy function and has been applied to many optimization
problems like the travelling salesman problem [30] and the
analog to digital conversion [31], among others. Researchers
aim to avoide the limitation of this model by increasing the
order of the energy function and thus allowing nonlinear
combination of the outputs as feedback inputs of the neuron. In
[32] the asymptotical stability of a high-order recurrent neural
network with delay has been proved. The input of each neuron
is a sum of weighted nonlinear combinations of the outputs of
the network and the same nonlinear combinations delayed by
𝜏. External inputs are assumed to be zero. Constraints have
been derived for this model, which guarantee its asymptotic
stability. For details we refer to [32]. A modified high-order
recurrent neural network can be derived from the model in
[32], when we assume that there are no feedback inputs with
delay and the external inputs are not zero, Fig. (2). Analog
with the stability proof in [32], constraints on the modified
model can be derived, which guarantee the asymptotic stability
of the modified model. The inner state of the neurons 𝑢𝑖(𝑡) in
this case are described by the following differential equation:

also in its analog realization. The future work will focus on
longer codes and joint equalization and decoding based on
analog neural networks.
