On the Decomposition Method for
Linear Programming Decoding of LDPC Codes
Haiyang Liu, Wenze Qu, Bin Liu, and Jie Chen, Member, IEEE
Abstract—In this paper, we focus on solving the linear pro-
gramming (LP) problem that arises in the decoding of low-
density parity-check (LDPC) codes by means of the revised
simplex method. In order to take advantage of the structure
of the LP problem, we reformulate the dual LP and apply the
idea of Dantzig-Wolfe (D-W) decomposition method to solve the
problem. Each subproblem in the D-W decomposition method
is an LP over a convex polyhedral cone. We define the convex
polyhedral cone as local parity-check cone and discuss its special
structures. Then, we enumerate its extreme rays and use these
extreme rays to design an efficient method for the general LP
decoding problem. The proposed method exhibits advantages in
reducing both the storage and computational requirements.
Index Terms—Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, linear
programming (LP) decoding, revised simplex method, Dantzig-
Wolfe (D-W) decomposition, local parity-check cone.
I. INTRODUCTION
I
N the last few years, low-density parity-check (LDPC)
codes [1], [2] have been widely studied due to their power-
ful error-control capacities. It has been demonstrated that long
block length random LDPC codes can achieve near Shannon
limit performance under belief-propagation based decoding
algorithms (such as sum-product algorithm) [3], which are
collectively called message passing iterative (MPI) algorithms
because the decoding process can be viewed as iterative
message-exchanging in the Tanner graph (or factor graph)
of the code. The asymptotic behavior of an MPI decoder
for infinite-length LDPC codes on a given symmetric binary-
input channel is studied using probabilistic approaches, such as
the density evolution method [4] and Gaussian approximation
method [5] etc. The finite-length behavior of the decoder (e.g.
error floor), however, is often complicated to analyze because
of the cycles in the Tanner graph.
Recently, linear programming (LP) based decoding of
LDPC codes (in fact, any binary linear codes) proposed by
Feldman et al. has received a lot of interest [6]-[10], [15]-
[21]. In fact, LP decoding relaxes the maximum-likelihood
(ML) decoding problem into an LP problem [6], which can
be efficiently solved by certain algorithms, such as the simplex
algorithm or the interior-point algorithm [11]. Many analytical
and simulation results suggest that the performance of LP

that the MPI algorithms are related to a nonlinear optimization
problem: the minimization of the Bethe Free Energy (BFE)
function over the same feasible region as LP decoding [12]-
[14]. As the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) goes to infinity, the
BFE function converges pointwise to the linear objective
function in LP decoding [14]. All these results indicate that
there are close connections between MPI decoding and LP
decoding.
Furthermore, LP decoding has several advantages over
MPI decoding. Firstly, an LP decoder has the ML certificate
property [6], i.e., the decoder outputs either an ML codeword
or a non-integral pseudocodeword. When the decoder output
is nonintegral, it immediately detects a decoding failure.
Secondly, since an LP decoding problem can be completely
characterized by the vertices of its feasible region (pseu-
docodewords here), it is easier to theoretically analyze the
LP decoder (especially the finite-length behavior) than the
MPI decoder. Thirdly, the performance of LP decoding can be
improved by adding redundant checks to the original parity-
check matrix [6], which allows us to balance a trade-off
between the decoding performance and complexity.
While these results suggest that LP decoding may be a
potential alternative to MPI decoding, the daunting complexity
of the general LP decoder (i.e., the number of constraints or
variables) makes LP decoding practically infeasible. However,
LP decoding problems often possess certain special structures,
which may be exploited to circumvent this difficulty. Yang et
al. [15] introduced a box-constrained quadratic programming
form as an approximation of the LP decoding problem, which
is especially suitable for LDPC codes with certain regular
structures (product LDPC codes). In [16], Taghavi et al. pre-
sented an adaptive method for LP decoding, which reduces the
LP decoding problem to iteratively solving a series of small-
scale LP problems. More recently, Taghavi et al. [17], [18]
further proposed modified versions of adaptive LP decoding,
in which the constraint matrices of the LP problems involved
have a structure similar to that of the parity-check matrix with
respect to the positions of the entries 1s. By exploiting this
property, the authors presented an efficient algorithm for these
LP problems based on the interior-point method and designed
a preconditioner for the most complex part of the algorithm.
In an independent work, Vontobel [19] also studied the imple-
mentation of the interior-point algorithm for LP decoding and
discussed some possible approaches of reducing the decoding
complexity. It is also interesting to note that a different stream
of work has focused on the message-passing-based methods

In this paper, we take a different approach on the efficient
implementation of LP decoding of LDPC codes. We propose
an algorithm for the general LP decoding problem based on
the revised simplex method, one of the most widely used
methods for LP problems. There are two main reasons for
us to use this method. First, the revised simplex method
is in general efficient [11], despite the fact that it is not
polynomial in the worst case. Second, this algorithm can
easily enable the application of the Dantzig-Wolfe (D-W)
decomposition method [22], a method suitable for large-scale
LP problems that have certain ‘block angular’ structure. More
concretely, the D-W decomposition method is devised to take
advantage of this special structure to solve the original LP
problem by iteratively solving one master problem and a series
of independent subproblems. At each iteration, the master
problem provides an updated set of cost coefficients for the
subproblems and receives from the subproblems a new column
based on these cost coefficients. Information is passed back
and forth between the master problem and the subproblems
until an optimal solution to the original problem is achieved.
We introduce an equivalent form of the dual LP decoding
problem and show that the result of general LP decoder can be
obtained iteratively from our form using the idea of the D-W
decomposition method. There is a one-to-one correspondence
between the subproblems and the rows of the parity-check
matrix. Besides, each subproblem is a special LP problem, i.e.,
an LP over a convex polyhedral cone. We define the convex
polyhedral cone as the local parity-check cone and discuss
its special structures, which can facilitate the enumeration
of all its extreme rays. Then we use the extreme rays of
the local parity-check cones to design an efficient algorithm
for the general LP decoding problem, in which explicitly
solving for LP subproblems is avoided. The advantages of
the decomposition-based method are observed by considering
both the storage and computational requirements.

presents a brief overview of LDPC codes and LP decoding.
We formulate the (primal) LP decoding problem and its dual
problem in Section III. In Section IV, we reformulate the dual
LP decoding problem and propose an efficient algorithm based
on the revised simplex method and the D-W decomposition

rays of the local parity-check cone are discussed in Section V.
Some simulation results and analysis are presented in Section
VI. Section VII concludes the paper.

IV. DECOMPOSITION-BASED ALGORITHM FOR LP
DECODING
In this section, we introduce an efficient algorithm that gives
the exact output of the general LP decoder (8). The two main
practical methods for LP problems are the simplex algorithm
and the interior-point algorithm.
The simplex algorithm is the first practical approach for LP
problems and remains being used most commonly in practice.
Roughly speaking, the simplex algorithm proceeds by moving
from one vertex of the polytope to another which has a lower
cost with respect to the objective function at each iteration,
until an optimal vertex is reached. For each vertex, there is a
corresponding basis matrix (not necessarily unique) that can
be constructed by the columns of the constraint matrix of the
LP problem. The crucial step of the algorithm is the update
computation of the (inverse) basis matrix.
In contrast, the interior-point algorithm moves along an
interior path of the polytope (As expected from the word ‘in-
terior’). At each iteration, this algorithm minimizes objection
function over an approximate region that is easy to solve,
and outputs a new point, until it reaches the optimal point.
The crucial step of this algorithm is to solve a system of
linear equations by certain iterative gradient-type methods. In
practice, the interior-point algorithm can be competitive with
the simplex algorithm, especially for large-scale sparse LP
problems.
The polynomial-time convergence of the interior-point algo-
rithm has been theoretically demonstrated. However, the direct
implementation of a general LP decoder by this algorithm may
be challenging in real-time decoding, especially with long
code lengths, because the linear systems in this algorithm
are often ill-conditioned and the convergence speed of the
gradient-type methods might be very slow when the algorithm
approaches the optimal point. Several effective methods have
been proposed to address this problem. For example, Vontobel
[19] presented the idea of finding the solutions of the linear
systems using graphical models. Taghavi et al. [17], [18]
proposed a number of approaches for designing suitable pre-
conditioners for solving such systems taking advantage of the
special structure of the constraint matrices of the LP problems
and demonstrated the effectiveness of the preconditioners.
Although the implementation of the LP decoder using the
interior-point method seems to be promising, we aim to design
efficient algorithms for LP decoding based on the simplex
method. This is because the simplex algorithm has been
proved to be efficient in general, despite the fact that it is not
polynomial in the worst case. Recently, this phenomenon has
been reasonably explained to some extent in [24]. Moreover,
the simplex algorithm can easily enable the application of the
D-W decomposition method, which is suitable for large-scale
LP problems whose constraint matrices have ‘block angular’
structure as in (14). There are various implementations of the
simplex algorithm, and the most practical one is the revised
simplex method because the explicit computation of the in-
verse matrix can be avoided in this method [11]. Consequently,
we choose this method for solving the general LP decoding
problem.

than 100. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed
decomposition method from another aspect.
Finally, it is worth mentioning that although the total
complexity of the decomposition-based method is not a linear
function of the code length, the operations in this method are
much simpler than the sum-product algorithm, in which the
computations of the complicated hyperbolic tangent function
are needed.
In conclusion, the decomposition method that takes the full
advantages of the special structure of the LP decoder is more
efficient.

VII. CONCLUSION
We discussed the solving of general LP decoding problem
by the revised simplex method in this work. To make use of
the special properties of LP decoding, we reformulated the
dual problem and applied the idea of the D-W decomposition
method to solve the problem. There is a one-to-one correspon-
dence between the check nodes and the subproblems in the
D-W decomposition method. The feasible region of each sub-
problem was defined as a local parity-check cone, which can
be determined by the degree of the corresponding check node.
We proved the extreme ray set of the local parity-check cone
has symmetry property, which can be used to enumerate all the
extreme rays. Then, we proposed an efficient decomposition-
based method for general LP decoding problem using these
extreme rays. The effectiveness of the proposed method can be
observed by considering both the storage and computational
requirements. One potential direction for future study is to
suitably apply the symmetry properties of the local parity-
check cone and its extreme rays to further simplify the general
LP decoding problem.
