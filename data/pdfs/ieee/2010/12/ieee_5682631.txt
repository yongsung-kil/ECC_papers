On the Reduced-Complexity of LDPC Decoders for Beyond

400 Gb/s Serial Optical Transmission

Ivan B. Djordjevic1, Lei Xu2, and Ting Wang2
1University of Arizona, Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, 1230 E. Speedway Blvd., Tucson, AZ 85721, USA 2NEC Laboratories America, Princeton, NJ 08540, USA
ivan@email.arizona.edu

Abstract: Two reduced-complexity (RC) LDPC decoders, which can be used in combination with

large-girth LDPC codes to enable beyond 400 Gb/s serial optical transmission, are proposed. We

show that optimally attenuated RC min-sum sum algorithm performs only 0.45 dB worse than

conventional sum-product algorithm, while having lower storage memory requirements and much

lower latency.

In order to reduce the complexity of sum-product algorithm (SPA), commonly used in decoding of low-density

parity-check (LDPC) codes, a number of different approximate algorithms were proposed [1],[2], and these are

reviewed in [3]. The main focus was to reduce the complexity of check-node (c-node) update rule. Because c-node

update rule is the key step in SPA, imperfect approximations lead to significant BER performance degradation.

In this paper we follow different strategy. Instead of trying to reduce the complexity of c-node update rule, we

try to reduce the complexity of variable-node (v-node) update rule. Two reduced complexity (RC) LDPC decoding

algorithms are introduced: (i) RC min-sum algorithm and (ii) RC a posteriori probability (APP) algorithm. We show

that even complete elimination of v-node update rule leads to only 0.45 dB degradation when large-girth LDPC

codes are used. We further study the use of RC decoding algorithm in polarization multiplexed (PolMUX) 32-IPQ

(see [4] for more details of this modulation format) of aggregate date rate 400 Gb/s and show that that net effective coding gains (NECGs) beyond 11.4 dB (at BER=10-9) are possible.

Before we fully describe the proposed RC algorithms, we introduce several definitions that will facilitate their

description. Decoding of LDPC codes is based on SPA, which is an iterative decoding algorithm where extrinsic

probabilities are iterated forward and back between variable and check nodes of bipartite (Tanner) graph

representation of a parity-check matrix H. The Tanner graph of an LDPC code is drawn according to the following

rule: check node c is connected to variable node v whenever element hcv in H is a 1. For v-node v (c-node) c we define its neighborhood N(v) (N(c)) as the set of c-nodes (v-nodes) connected to it. Because the c-node update rule

involves log and tanh functions, it is computationally intensive, and there exist many approximations. The very-

popular is the min-sum-plus-correction-term approximation [1]. Namely, it can be shown that “box-plus” operator

The attenuations can be implemented by appropriate register shifts (e.g., 0.5 corresponds to one register shift to the

right). We turn our attention now to the evaluation of proposed reduced complexity algorithms. In Fig. 1, we show

the results of Monte Carlo simulations for different LDPC decoding algorithms and optimum attenuation factors.

The large-girth LDPC(16935, 13550) code, designed as described in [3], is used in simulations. It is interesting to

notice that min-sum-plus-correction-term algorithm faces negligible performance loss, which is in agreement with

[1]. The min-sum algorithm with optimum attenuation factor α=0.8 performs the same as SPA, while RC-min-sum with optimum attenuation factor of 0.44 faces only 0.45 dB degradation at BER of 10-9. The storage complexity and

latency of optimally attenuated RC-min-sum algorithm are much lower, which selects this algorithm as an excellent

candidate for 40 Gb/s implementation. The optimally attenuated RC-APP algorithm performs 0.2 dB worse than

optimally attenuated RC-min-sum algorithm. Given this description of proposed RC LDPC decoding algorithms, we

further study their use for beyond 400 Gb/s serial optical transmission.

The results of simulations of PolMUX based LDPC-coded 32-IPQ scheme with 400 Gb/s in aggregate data rate (determined as 2×5×0.8×50 Gb/s) are shown in Fig. 2. The net effective coding gain (NECG) at BER of 10-9, when

min-sum-with-correction-term algorithm is used, is about 12.1 dB. The optimally attenuated min-sum algorithm

performs comparable. The RC-min-sum provides the NECG of about 11.4 dB. This RC decoding based scheme,

therefore, represents an excellent candidate to be used for future 400G Ethernet technologies.

In conclusion, we proposed two RC LDPC decoders, suitable for use in beyond 400 Gb/s serial optical

transmission: (i) attenuated RC min-sum algorithm and (ii) RC APP algorithm. We show that optimally attenuated RC min-sum sum algorithm performs 0.45 dB (at BER=10-9) worse than conventional SPA, when used in

combination with large-girth LDPC codes. We further evaluate the proposed algorithms for use in beyond 400 Gb/s

serial optical transmission in combination with PolMUX 32-IPQ-based signal constellation. We show that low BERs

can be achieved for medium optical SNRs, while achieving the NECG above 11.4 dB.
