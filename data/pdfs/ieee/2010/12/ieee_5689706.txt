Improved Method for Detecting the Short Cycles of LDPC Codes
Pan Hu, Hongyu Zhao
Key Lab of Information Coding and Transmission Southwest Jiao tong University Chengdu, China
E-mail: hp_tongxin@126.com, hyzhao@swjtu.edu.cn

Abstract-It is well known that the short cycles in Tanner Graph (TG) greatly impact on the iterative decoding performance of low density parity check (LDPC) codes. In previous literature two methods can be found for detecting such short cycles. This paper presents an improved version of previous Jun Fan’s method, which is based on combinational analysis and enumeration of the check matrix patterns caused by cycles of length of 4, 6, 8 and 10 in the corresponding TG. The improved method can more exactly detect the cycles of length greater than 4 in comparison with Jun Fan’s method. The validation of the proposed method can be verified by the detection results of short cycles for some LPDC codes.
Keywords-LDPC codes; cycle detection; check matrix
I. INTRODUCTION
The Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes were introduced firstly by Gallager [1] in 1962. Since 1996, LDPC codes have shown excellent performance near the Shannon limit [2, 3] due to the iterative decoding algorithm as well as the specifically designed sparse check matrix. The iterative decoding algorithm of LDPC codes can be well described by bipartite graphs, i.e. the so-called Tanner graphs (TG) [4]. If the TG of a LDPC code has short cycles, then the decoding performance will be degraded, since the short cycles will reduce the independence of the extrinsic information which is passed between the variable nodes and check nodes of TG [5]. As a result, on one hand, these short cycles should be avoided as far as possible in the code design, and on the other hand, given a LDPC code, short cycle detection of the corresponding TG is particular of interest for the evaluation of the code design. So far, two methods have been proposed to get around the latter problem. In [6], Jun Fan et al. present a method based on combinational analysis and enumeration of the check matrix patterns caused by possible cycles of length of 4, 6, 8 and 10 in the corresponding TG. Their method can detect most of short cycles in LDPC codes, however, it cannot find out all patterns of short cycles in some cases. Recently, Bo Li et al. present a method in [7] based on logic algebraic calculation over the check matrix for detecting the short cycles of LDPC codes. However, no detection results are given in [7].
The sequel of this paper is organized as follows. In secĊ we analysis the definition of 4,6,8,10 cycles in TG of LDPC codes and give a brief introduction for Jun Fan’s method. Then, secċ presents the improved method of short cycle detection in details, which is followed by secČ, where some short cycle detection results are presented and compared with Jun Fan’s method [6]. II. DEFINITION OF CYCLES AND JUN FAN’S METHOD
The check matrix of a LDPC code is a sparse matrix containing few ‘1’s and majority of ‘0’s. Supposing H denotes such a check matrix of m rows and n columns, H can be equivalently represented by a TG composed of n variable nodes and m check nodes. If and only if the element of i'th row
and j'th column of H is 1, i.e. H (i, j) 1, the i'th check node
and j'th variable node connected with each other through an edge. Any close loop in the TG is called a cycle, and the length of cycle is defined by the number of edges in the cycle. Apparently, the length of any cycle in a TG must be an even integer since TG is bipartite. For convenience, we use k-cycle to represent a cycle of length of k.
In [6], Jun Fan et al. have found out the check matrix patterns caused by short cycles in the corresponding TG. For instance, any 4-cycle shape in the check matrix is consist of 4 ‘1’s belonging to 2 rows and 2 columns equally, which is shown as Fig.1. Similarly, a 6-cycle shape in the check matrix is consist of 6 ‘1’s belonging to 3 rows and 3 columns equally. And, an 8-cycle of shape is consist of 8 ‘1’s belonging to 4 rows and 4 columns equally. Also a 10-cycle shape is consist of 10 ‘1’s belonging to 5 rows and 5 columns equally. For simplicity, we do not give any figure for showing the patterns of cycle of length greater than 6.

V. CONCLUSION
This paper presents an improved method for detecting short cycles of LDPC codes. The improved method is based on non-zero element column index vectors which differ from previous Jun Fan’s method. In comparison with Jun Fan’s algorithm [6], the proposed method can more exactly estimate the number of short cycles of length greater than 4. Using three different LDPC constructions, i.e. Mackay’s construction, random construction and PP construction, the validation of the improved method has been verified. The algorithm presented in this paper can be readily applied to detect all short cycles with length less than or equals to 10 and thus evaluate the design of LDPC codes.
