Quantifying the Wave-Effect of Irregular LDPC
codes based on Majority-Based Hard-Decoding
Asad Mahmood
Department ECE,
Center for Advanced Studies in Engineering,
19 Ataturk Av. - 44000 Islamabad, Pakistan.
Email: asad.mahmood@case.edu.pk
Abstract— Irregular LDPC codes have shown to give perfor-
mances closer to capacity than their regular counteparts. The
intuitive reasoning backed by experimental evidence given by
its founder (Luby et al.) was the presence of a wave-effect.
It was shown that only with the presence of a carefully opti-
mized irregularity degree profile, comes the better performance.
Conventionally this irregularity profile is optimized employing
asymptotic assumptions which studies have shown result in
poor performance measures in finite-length codes. Also, the
conventional techniques are computationally expensive which
makes their use inappropriate in cases, e.g. multicarrier [1],
where run-time optimization of the irregularity profile may be
needed. This paper proposes a simplistic technique for precise
quantification of the wave-effect phenomenon in irregular codes
and hence can be used for run-time optimization of irregularity
in finite length codes which may not be possible with classical
methods.
I. INTRODUCTION
The works of Mackay [2] re-discovered the capacity ap-
proaching behavior of Low Density parity Check (LDPC)
Codes that were initially proposed by Gallager [3]. This not
only led to define LDPC codes as channel codes in some recent
wireless standards [4] but also made LDPC codes as one of
the most researched areas in channel coding in recent years.
The performance of LDPC codes rely on the number of
statistical independent iterations of the probabilistic decoding
algorithm which is defined by the sparsity of the parity check
matrix H of dimension (𝑚× 𝑛) with 𝑚and 𝑛representing
the amount of redundancy added and the code-word length
respectively. In other words each column of the H matrix
represents a bit of the code-word to be transmitted, whereas
each row represents a parity-check constraint involving some
bits of the code-word. This matrix is also conventionally
represented by a bi-partite graph [5] consisting of two types
of nodes, ‘variable-nodes’ (one for each bit/message in the
code-word) and ‘check-nodes’ (one for each parity-check
constraint).
The sparsity of the parity check matrix is classically repre-
sented by the parameters of ‘j’ and ‘k’, each representing the
number of 1’s in the columns and rows of H respectively.
Gallager [3] initially proposed codes based on small fixed
values of ‘j’ and ‘k’(also termed as degree) for all columns
and rows to define a ‘low-density’ H matrix. Such codes are
commonly referred to as the ‘regular’ LDPC codes. However,
it was first demonstrated by Luby et al. [6] that if one or
both of these parameters (degrees) are allowed to vary for
different bit/check nodes, hence calling them Irregular codes,
then the asymptotic Bit Error Rate (BER) performance of
irregular LDPC was found to be nearer to the channel capacity
limits than its ‘regular’ counterpart. Luby et al. first gave the
intuitive reasoning of this better performance of irregular codes
based upon what they observed experimenally. He inferred
that instead of a constant value of degree, a wide spread of
degrees lead to a phenomenon of what he called as the wave-
effect, where the variable nodes with high degrees tend to
correct their value quickly thereby providing good information
to the associated lower degree message nodes leading to
improvement in overall performance.
Optimization of Irregular LDPC codes generally makes use
of complex optimization techniques like variants of Genetic
Algorithm [7] or Linear Programming [7] in order to search
for the optimal or best-possible distribution of the total number
of edges over bit-nodes, while generally assuming that the
check-node degree remains the same. This approach, however,
makes use of the asymptotic assumption for the code-length
and decoding iterations, thereby leading to a profile that may
not be the best irregularity profile for short block-lengths.
Hard-Decoding algorithm, originally proposed by Gallager
in his thesis, has recently again gained popularity in the form
of Majority-Based (MB) algorithms [8] because of their fast
convergence properties and ease of analytical performance
evaluation. By making use of the combinatorial analysis for
MB hard decoding, our goal in this work is to propose a
computationally feasible method for quantifying the wave-
effect where we can compare the effect by different nodes
with different degrees. State of the art in Irregularity profile
construction and MB-Hard Decoding will be given in section
II. Gallager’s hard decoding bound modified for irregular
LDPC codes alongwith introduction to the Majority-Based
(MB) hard-decoding algorithms will be given in section III.
Section IV will give the conventional method for calcu-
lating the evolution in probability of error alongwith our
reduced complexity Sum-of-Products-of-Combinations (SPC)
based method. The comparisons in simulation time for the
two methods will be given in the same section. Finally we
will conclude in section V.

II. STATE OF THE ART
Design process of Irregular LDPC codes is divided into
two steps of 1. Optimization of Degree Distribution and 2.
Placement of Edges between different variable-nodes for a
given Degree Distribution. In literature, both, the optimization
methodologies for irregularity profile optimization as well as
the techniques for construction of good finite-length codes for
a given degree distribution are present separately and both
combine to define the complete design of irregular codes.
Two algorithms have generally been employed to design
a class of irregular LDPC codes under some channel con-
straints: The Density Evolution algorithm [7] and the Extrinsic
Information Transfer (EXIT) charts [9]. Richardson et al.
[7] designed capacity approaching irregular codes with the
Density Evolution (DE) algorithm. This algorithm tracks the
probability density function (pdf) of the messages through the
graph nodes under the asymptotic assumption i.e. infinite code-
length. Differential Evolution [10] algorithm and the Gaussian
Approximation [11] are two enabling techniques for feasible
implementation of Density Evolution algorithm.
Similarly, extensive literature is also present on the con-
struction of practical short block-length LDPC codes. No-
table contributions include those techniques that are based
on completely-random [2] or semi-random methods [3] for
allocating edges. More deterministic approaches which tend
to improve the girth characteristics of the underlying graph
are more popular because of their better performances. Bit-
Filling [12] and Progressive Edge Growth (PEG) [13] are
two mostly commonly employed techniques in this regard.
Similarly in [14], the authors study the histogram of cycles
length of randomly generated LDPC codes, based on MacKays
construction 2A and they select the best cycle-length histogram
shape and show that increasing the mean girth of the Tanner
Graph results in giving better performance than maximizing
the local girth. Similarly it was shown in [15], that not
all short-length cycles have equally deteriotive impact on
the performance and hence the selective avoidance of cycles
was proposed. Gallager [3] developed a weak-bound based
on Hard-Decoding/ Bit-Flipping [16] decoding algorithms,
later known as the Gallager A and Gallager B decoding
algorithms. These decoding algorithms were later extended for
the irregular [6] case and are the subject of analysis in this
paper. Bit flipping usually operates on hard decisions. The
same bit-flipping approach was modified to form Majority-
Based (MB) hard-decoding algorithms whose properties were
extensively studied in [8]. The convergence properties of MB-
hard-decoding algorithm showed that they converge to zero
super-exponentially to the number of iterations as compared
to the exponentional convergence of Gallager-A algorithm to
zero.
III. MAJORITY-BASED (MB) HARD-DECODING
ALGORITHM FOR IRREGULAR LDPC CODES
The basic underlying concept of MB-decoding is evident
from its name i.e. the decision on whether the flipping of a
variable-node’s value at each iteration is taken based upon
the consensus over the majority of incoming messages. First
detailed works on the MB-based algorithms were performed
by Zarrinkhat et al. [8] where he defined MB hard-decoding
algorithms of varying order (𝑤). The order 𝑤defines the
strength of the majority i.e. how many more than atleast half of
the incoming messages are in agreement with each other where
0 ≤w ≤⌊(𝑗−1)/2⌋. The basic per iteration computations
for an order 𝑤MB hard-decoder can be summarised as:
∙Messages are from the set 0, 1 and represent the current
estimate of the decoder of a particular bit.
∙At Check Nodes : The outgoing message from a check-
node results from the computation of the XOR sum of
the incoming (extrinsic) messages
∙At Variable Nodes : The outgoing message equals the
originally received message, except if a ⌈(𝑗−1)/2⌉+ 𝑤
or more of the incoming messages disagree, in which
case this common value is sent
The value of 𝑤determines the order and hence the strength
of the disagreement required for the change. Extreme cases
are standard majority-decoding algorithm (order 0) i.e. 𝑤= 0
, and the case where all the incoming messages unanimously
agree on a value (maximum order and same as Gallager A
algorithm) i.e.𝑤= ⌊(𝑗−1)/2⌋.

V. CONCLUSION
Irregular LDPC codes are the capacity-achieving codes that
have shown better performances than their regular counter-
parts. This better performance is attributed to the wave-effect,
inherent in their decoding. Quantification of this wave-effect
can lead to many useful applications e.g. for the multicarrier
case where run-time optimization of the irregularity profile is
needed. Whereas classical methods for the calculation of the
error-probability evolution in majority-based hard-decoding
are computationally prohibitive, a novel simplistic method for
quantification of this wave-effect phenomenon is proposed
in this paper. Simulation results have shown large reduction
in computational complexity with no compromise in perfor-
mance.
