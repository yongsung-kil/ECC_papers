Connections Between Problematic Trapping Sets and Deviations on the Computation Trees of LDPC Codes

Eric T. Psota and Lance C. Perez
Department of Electrical Engineering University of Nebraska Lincoln, NE 68588
Email: epsota24@huskers.unl.edu, lperez@unl.edu

Ahstract-The error mechanisms of iterative message-passing de­ coders with low-density parity-check codes are studied. The connec­ tions between trapping sets and deviations on computation trees are explored in depth in an attempt to bridge experimental and analytic approaches to these error mechanisms. An iterative algorithm for finding low-weight trapping sets is presented along with a method for characterizing the weight of deviations that result from trapping sets.
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have been shown to exhibit an error floor when using iterative decoders [10]. In many cases, error floors occur when decoding performance becomes dominated by small-weight errors and the error rate decreases more slowly with increasing channel signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). Often, the very low error rates at which the error floors of LDPC codes occur makes it computationally intractable to study them using Monte Carlo simulations. There are also no known practical upper bounds for iterative decoding of LDPC codes that can accurately determine whether or not a particular code will have a significant error floor. The inability to predict the error floor behavior of LDPC codes has inhibited their deployment in high-throughput applications requiring near error-free performance [8].
LDPC codes are most commonly decoded using iterative de­ coders such as the min-sum (MS) [9] and sum-product (SP) decoders [5]. Several methods have been introduced to estimate the performance of these decoders by characterizing their errors, including stopping sets [2], trapping sets [4], and absorbing sets [10]. When the weight of one of these sets is less than the weight of the minimum-weight codeword, it will be referred to as a problematic trapping set. Unfortunately, none of these sets provides an upper bound on the performance of iterative decoding over the additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel. The notion of deviations, introduced by [9], can be used to derive upper bounds on the performance of iterative decoders, but they are computationally intractable after even a small number of iterations [6]. This paper examines connections between deviations and problematic trapping sets, with the goal of using the precise analytical properties of deviations to study problematic trapping sets.

The notions of trapping sets, stopping sets, absorbing sets and deviations are all attempts to accurately characterize the error mechanisms that determine the performance of LDPC codes with iterative decoders.
A. Stopping Sets
The notion of stopping sets was first introduced by Forney et at. [2] in 2001. It has been shown that the error rates of iteratively decoded LDPC codes on the binary erasure channel (BEC) can be determined exactly from stopping sets.
Definition 11.1 (Stopping Sets [2]). A set S S;; V is a stopping set if all check nodes connected to S are connected to at least two variable nodes in S.
During iterative message-passing decoding, information passed from a check node to a variable node is derived from all other variable nodes connected to that check node. If two or more of the variable nodes connected to a check node contain erasures, the check node will send erasure messages to each of its neighbors. If all nodes in S begin with erasures, the check nodes connected to S will not be capable of resolving them.

The significance of a trapping set to iterative decoding depends upon the values of t and Wt. Much like low-weight codewords, a smaller t results in a higher probability that T will cause an error. Additionally, a small Wt means that only a small number of parity­ checks is capable of detecting the error. Thus, trapping sets with small t and Wt are considered problematic to iterative decoders.
In [7], trapping sets are examined for different decoders on the binary erasure channel, binary symmetric channel (BSC), and the additive white Gaussian noise channel. Whereas stopping sets can be used to precisely determine the probability of error on the BEC, trapping sets appear to cause errors on the AWGN channel [7]. Unfortunately, the somewhat vague definition of trapping sets makes it difficult to use them for performance analysis.

In simulations the majority of errors encountered in the error floor region during sum-product decoding of the IEEE 802.3an low-density parity-check code can be attributed to absorbing sets [10]. Although absorbing sets show potential for estimating the performance of iterative message-passing decoding, they do not directly lead to upper bounds. For upper bounds, one can use the concept of deviations on the computation tree.
D. Computation Trees and Deviations
In his 1996 dissertation, Wiberg [9] presented groundbreaking analytical results with respect to iterative decoding of low-density parity-check codes. He provided a precise analysis of both the MS and SP decoders by introducing a model of iterative decoding known as the computation tree and the concept of deviations on the computation tree.
Definition 11.4 (Deviation [9]). A deviation is any set of variable nodes on the computation tree satisfying the following conditions.
1) Each check in the computation tree is adjacent to either two or zero variable nodes in the deviation set.
2) A deviation contains the root node of the computation tree. 3) No proper and non-empty subset of variable nodes in the
deviation form a valid configuration on the computation tree.

III. DEVI ATIONS FROM PROBLEM ATIC TRAPPING SETS
Deviations can be used to define a necessary condition for an error to occur during iterative decoding. At high SNR, low­ weight deviations become much more likely to have negative cost than high-weight deviations. Since low-weight stopping sets are precisely the cause of errors for iterative decoding over the BEC [2], it is reasonable to suggest that low-weight deviations on the BSC can be created from the variable nodes in low-weight stopping sets. It is also reasonable to suggest that absorbing sets coincide with low-weight deviations over the AWGN channel, since they have been frequently observed to cause errors at high SNR during iterative decoding over the AWGN channel [7] [10]. The connections between stopping/absorbing sets and deviations are examined in the following.

A. Stopping Sets as Deviations

Stopping sets consist of a subset S of the variable nodes, such that each check node connected to S is connected at least twice. According to the definition of a deviation given in Definition 11.4,
each check node connected to the variable nodes in a deviation is
connected exactly two times. These two properties will be used to
study the relationship between stopping sets and their corresponding
deviations. First, it is shown how a deviation can be created using
only the variable nodes in a stopping set.
Consider a computation tree where each of the variable nodes
begins with an assignment of a binary O. Then, for each variable node in S, assign their copies in the computation tree to a binary 1. The resulting configuration will not, in general, be a valid configuration, because check nodes connected to the set S might be connected an odd number of times greater than two. For each copy of a check node connected to S let only one of its child variable nodes be equal to a binary 1, and set all other child variable nodes
and their descendants to a binary O. The resulting configuration is a deviation that contains only variable nodes in S.
Figure 4(a) illustrates a portion of a configuration on the compu­ tation tree induced by a stopping set, where Vl, V2, V3 E S. Because only one of the child variable nodes below It can be included in a deviation, it is sufficient to arbitrarily choose to include V2 and exclude V3. The effect of the resulting deviation may be different
on the BEC than on the BSC when using iterative decoding. The
reason that stopping sets cause errors over the BEC is illustrated in Figure 4(b). When each variable node in S is received over the channel as an erasure c, an erasure is sent from It to Vl. The root node is similarly decoded as an erasure if it is also in S.

V I. CONCLUSION

The connections between problematic trapping sets and their

corresponding deviations have been examined. It was shown that

variable nodes in a stopping set can be used to form a deviation,

while trapping sets and absorbing sets form a deviation only if a

subset of their variable nodes forms a stopping set. Otherwise, the

trapping sets and absorbing sets define deviations that are required

to include variable nodes outside the of set. It was also shown

that the number and proportion of the variable nodes outside the

set can be found experimentally using the MS decoder. This led to

an iterative method for identifying low-weight problematic trapping

sets in LDPC codes. Simulation results demonstrate that this method

is capable of finding many low-weight problematic trapping sets.

Finally, a novel method for determining the weight of deviations

induced by trapping sets on the computation tree was introduced.

This approach involves determining the minimum-weight stopping

set which contains the trapping set, and then creating a directed

Tanner graph from the stopping set which favors variable nodes

within the trapping set. It is possible to compute the weight of the

deviation using a recursive method for computing the multiplicity of

variable nodes within the deviation. In one example, it was shown

that a deviation exists on the computation tree with weight less

than the minimum-weight codeword. Thus, there exists a necessary

condition for an MS decoding error that is more likely than an ML

codeword error.
