Abstract- T he error-floor performance of low-density parity­ check (LDPC) codes under iterative decoding is governed by combinatorial configurations in the Tanner graph of the code termed trapping sets. Finding the smallest trapping set in a Tan­ ner graph is an NP-hard problem. However, for codes constructed from designs one can partially characterize trapping sets and enumerate them efficiently. We focus on LDPC codes based on Steiner triple systems (STS), and quantify small trapping sets for bit-flipping decoding over the BSC and small trapping sets for the AWGN channel. Furthermore , we provide simulation results that show that the enumeration scheme at hand provides good estimates for the error-floor behavior of STS LDPC codes.
I. INTRODUCT ION
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1] are a family of linear error-correcting codes with capacity-approaching performance under iterative decoding, used in a variety of applications. One of the outstanding problems in the analysis of LDPC code performance is the error-floor phenomenon. The error floor represents a change in the slope of bit and frame error rate curves at high signal-to-noise ratios. For the binary erasure channel (BEC), the error vectors responsible for the onset of the error floor can be completely characterized via stopping sets [2], which prohibit the successful completion of the decoding process. For additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels and binary symmetric channels (BSC), error floors arise due to combinatorial configurations in the parity-check matrix of the code, generally known as trapping sets, but also frequently referred to as pseudocodewords or near-codewords [3], [4]. Trapping sets are hard to characterize, identify or approximate in general [5].
We focus on structured LDPC codes designed from Steiner triple systems (STS) [6]. These codes are henceforth referred to as STS LDPC codes, as their parity-check matrix is defined as the point-line incidence matrix of the underlying STS, which determines the code properties and its error-rate characteris­ tics. Kashyap and Vardy [7] and, more recently, Colbourn and Fujiwara [8] investigated STS LDPC codes for signaling over the BEC and identified stopping sets to be equivalent to the well-studied class of full configurations in STSs. The authors of [8] characterized all configurations corresponding to small stopping sets and determined their number. Furthermore, they showed that the smallest size of a stopping set must be six or seven, and that no STS LDPC codes with smallest stopping set of size larger than seven exist.

Very little is known about trapping sets of structured LDPC codes. For designs based on Latin squares constructed accord­ ing to the the Bose method, the authors provided a partial characterization of small trapping sets in [9]. Special types of trapping sets were also described for the class of array codes by Dolecek et al. [10] and for projective geometry codes by the the authors in [11].
We extend the analysis of [8] to trapping sets of STS LDPC codes, by focusing on configurations that cause communica­ tion errors over the BSC and AWGN channels. Unfortunately, unlike for the case of stopping sets [2], not all error patterns leading to decoding failure can be simply described via well­ studied configurations in designs, such as full configurations. Nevertheless, using special types of configurations, we show that all STS LDPC codes have to contain certain small trapping sets, and provide the means for enumerating trapping sets of size up to five and classifying trapping sets of size up to eight. In addition, for the BSC we describe how one can dynamically track changes in the error configurations during iterative bit flipping decoding. We also present simulation results that illustrate the contributions of different trapping set classes to the error rate of STS LDPC codes in the error-floor regime.
The paper is organized as follows. Section II introduces rele­ vant definitions and terminology regarding STSs and trapping sets. Sections III and IV provide an overview of analytical and simulation-based results for the AWGN channel. An investigation of the bit-flipping algorithm for the BSC and accompanying simulation results are given in Section V.

A configuration is called constant if its number of occur­ rences in an STS is independent of other configurations, oth­ erwise it is called variable. Two configurations are isomorphic if one can be transformed into the other by re-Iabelling point and line indices.
An important sub-configuration of STS(7) is the (614)­ configuration. It is known as a Pasch configuration, referred to as C16 according to the standard notation in [6]. This is the smallest Erdos configuration, the smallest full configuration, and the only full four-line configuration. The only full config­ uration on five lines is the Mitre configuration Dl. The Mitre configuration is an Erdos (7I5)-configuration. STSs without Pasch and Mitre configurations are called anti-Pasch and anti­ Mitre, respectively.
The number of (kl€)-configurations in an STS can be enumerated in a constructive manner. In this setting, one starts by enumerating a set of basic configurations and by counting in how many ways lines can be added to such configurations to form larger configurations. One can in principle enumerate all possible configurations in an STS, based on the number of initial configurations and their possible extensions. One such approach was provided by Horak et al. [12], who showed that any constant configuration, together with all full configurations on at most € lines, forms a generating set for all i-line configurations. Also, it is known that the number of any (kl€)­ configuration in a given STS is determined by the number of its Erdos configurations with up to € lines and by the number of constant i-line configurations [13].
A complete enumeration of small configurations of up to four lines in STSs was performed by Grannell et al. [13]. Forbes [14], [15] created a compilation of all configurations of size up to eight, which we use of in our subsequent analysis. For consistency, we denote all configurations of at most four lines following the standard notation AO-C16 from [6]. For configurations of five or more lines, we use the canonical ordering, with generators taken from [16].
STS LDPC codes can be formally defined as follows [9]. Definition 2.5: An STS LDPC code is an LDPC code whose parity-check matrix H = (Hi,j) represents the point­ line incidence matrix of an STS, i.e., the points of the STS index the rows of the parity-check matrix while the lines index its columns. A point with index i lying on a line with

index j corresponds to a one-entry in the parity-check matrix, Hi,j = 1. Clearly, H has dimensions v x v(v - 1) /6. Since every line contains three points and every point lies on (v - 1)/2 lines, STS LDPC codes are (3,(v - 1)/2)­ regular codes, i.e. codes with parity-check matrices of column weight three and row weight (v - 1)/2. We illustrate all our experimental findings on the example of a [1080,999] STS LDPC code constructed from an anti-Pasch STS(v) with v = 81 via the Bose method [9].
B. Trapping sets in STS LDPC codes for the BSC and AWGN channels
In order to characterize the decoding failures of iterative de­ coders of STS LDPC codes for the BSC and AWGN channels, we restrict our attention to (a, b) trapping sets defined in [11].
When interpreted in the context of STSs (V, 13), an (a, b) trapping set is a set T(a, b) S;; 13 of a = IT(a, b)I lines with the property that of all points x lying on at least one line B S;; T(a,b) exactly b points are contained in an odd number of lines, i.e., I{x : I{B: x E B E T(a,b)}1 = 1 (mod 2)}I = b. For elementary trapping sets T(e)(a, b), the points x lying on at least one line B S;; T(e)(a,b) obey the conditions I{B: x E B E T(e)(a,b)}1 ::; 2 and I{x I{B : x E B E T(e)(a,b)}1 = 1}1 = b. The size of a trapping set is the number of lines of its corresponding STS configuration.
As parity-check matrices of STS LDPC codes have column weight three, all (a, b) trapping sets have a == b (mod2). Furthermore, all (a, b) elementary trapping sets correspond to ((3a + b) /2Ia)-configurations, with a + b even, and 0 ::; b ::; 3a. Using code ensemble analysis, elementary trapping sets were shown to lead to the majority of decoding errors [17]. We therefore focus on trapping sets with configurations that have the following properties, termed property set Pl:
- All points in the configuration are connected, i.e. there are no isolated sub-configurations.
- At most one point per line has odd degree. - Not every line in the configuration contains a point of
odd degree.
Note that due to the constant variable-node degree (equal to three), trapping set configurations in STS LDPC codes obeying property Pl correspond to absorbing sets, described in [10].
The most common decoding method of LDPC codes for the BSC is the serial bit flipping algorithm, described in detail in [18]. This decoding algorithm searches in every iteration for bits connected to more unsatisfied than satisfied check nodes and flips one randomly chosen bit from this set. Consequently, the number of unsatisfied check nodes strictly decreases at each iteration. The decoder stops if no more bits can be flipped. Trapping sets for serial bit flipping correspond to absorbing sets and are also trapping sets for the Gallager A (or B) decoding algorithm [3]. On the other hand, elementary trapping sets [3] were shown to be dominating error events for message passing decoders of codes over AWGN channels. Therefore, configurations obeying property P1 correspond to dominant error configurations for both BSC and AWGN communication systems and iterative decoding methods.

IV. S IMULATION RESULTS FOR T HE AWGN CHANNEL
Analysis of belief propagation decoding at high signal-to­ noise ratios around 6.75 dB for the [1080,999] STS LDPC code confirms that an overwhelming number of decoding errors correspond to small trapping sets. During 250 iterations of belief-propagation (BP) decoding, the STS configurations in error after each iteration were recorded. Unlike typical decoding failures due to trapping sets [3] characterized by a fixed set of bits in error starting from a specific iteration number up to the end of the decoding process, the observed STS configurations formed by erroneous bits were found to change periodically during the course of BP decoding, remaining transient for at most four consecutive iterations. The most frequently encountered error patterns include small (5,1), (4,2), (5,3), (6,2), (6,4), and (7,1) trapping sets. Furthermore, (6,0) and (8,0) elementary trapping sets corre­ sponding to wrong codewords accounted for a large portion of

V. ANALY SIS OF TRAPPING S ETS OF B I T-FUPPING DECOD ING FOR T HE BSC
Error events for the BSC are more simple to characterize than error events for the AWGN channel, due to their combi­ natorial nature. In the low-error regime, when Pssc < 10-6 and for codelengths n not exceeding 104, one expects to see not more than three bits in error after hard thresholding, as the probability of encountering at least four bits in error is two orders of magnitude smaller than the probability of encountering three bits in error. Hence, we focus on error events involving up to three bit errors only.
Serial bit-flipping decoding of STS LDPC codes can be described in terms of error configurations formed by lines corresponding to erroneous bits. Let us refer to the lines corresponding to the erroneous bits at the decoder input as the initial error configuration. The points of odd degree in the configuration represent unsatisfied checks visible to the decoder. STS LDPC codes have constant variable node degree three, so that at every decoder iteration, a randomly chosen bit connected to at least two unsatisfied checks is flipped. Conse­ quently, the error configuration is updated at each iteration by adding or removing a line connected to at least two odd-degree points. Bit flipping can either result in correcting an erroneous bit, which means removing the corresponding line from the error configuration, or flipping a correct bit, which corresponds to enlarging the error configuration by adding a new line and connecting odd-degree points of the existing configuration. The number of odd-degree points in the error configuration decreases at every iteration by one or three, while the number of lines may increase or decrease by one. As there exists a line between every pair of points in an STS, the decoder stops if and only if at most one odd-degree point is left in the terminal error configuration. Therefore, we have the following result.
Lemma 5.1: The terminal error configurations for bit flip­ ping decoding of STS LDPC codes are confined to (2a,0) and (2a + 1,1) trapping sets with a E N\{l}.
The number of iterations until decoding failure is declared or a codeword is found and the size of the terminal error configuration are limited by the number of odd-degree points in the initial error configuration, as for every decoder iteration, the number of odd-degree points is reduced by one or three and the number of lines is reduced or increased by one.
For the case of one bit error, the initial error configuration is an isolated line, AO, containing three odd-degree points, so the bit-flipping algorithm removes this line leaving an empty configuration, i.e. a codeword. Initial error configurations of two bit errors form either the configuration A2, if both bits share a check node, or the configuration AI, cf. Fig. 3. The bit flipping decoder may randomly choose to flip an erroneous bit corresponding to one of the two lines in the initial error configuration, thus leaving the line AO unchanged.

This line is then corrected in the following iteration. On the other hand, if the decoder flips a correct bit, the error configuration is expanded by a line passing through one odd­ degree check node of each line in the configuration. Hence, the configuration Al is transformed into a B4 configuration and the A2 configuration is transformed into a B5 configuration. These error configurations are further updated until either all lines are removed or at most one odd-degree point remains. As Al (A2) contains six (four) odd-degree points, at most five (three) bit-flipping iterations may be performed before the decoder stops, resulting in terminal error configurations with at most seven (five) lines.
