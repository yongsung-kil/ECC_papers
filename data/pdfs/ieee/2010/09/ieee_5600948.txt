BP-Maxwell Decoding Algorithm for LDPC Codes over AWGN Channels

Yi Fang1, Jianwen Zhang1, Lin Wang1, Francis C.M.Lau2
1Dept. of Communication Engineering, Xiamen University, Fujian 361005, P.R.China 2Dept. of Electronic and Information Engineering, Hong Kong Polytechnic University, Hong Kong
Email: wanglin@xmu.edu.cn

Abstract—Designing realizable Maximum Likelihood (ML) decoders for low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes has always been a challenging problem. Maxwell decoder is a well-known ML decoder for LDPC codes over a binary erasure channel (BEC). Based on the concept used in the Maxwell decoder, it may be possible to design near-ML decoders for LDPC codes over an additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel. In this paper, we introduce a novel idea called channel transformation, which will be used as a bridge linking a BEC channel and an AWGN channel. With the transformation, we further propose a near-ML decoder for LDPC codes sent over an AWGN channel. The proposed near-ML decoder, combining a Belief Propagation (BP) decoder and a Maxwell decoder, is called the BP-Maxwell (BM) decoder. Simulation results show that compared with a BP decoder, the BM decoding algorithm can accomplish better frame error rates (FERs) by eliminating many of the error frames with a smaller percentage of error bits. Thus, the BM decoder is a step closer to the ML decoder compared with the BP decoder.
Keywords- AWGN channel; LDPC code; BM decoder; frame error rates
I. INTRODUCTION
LDPC codes [1] have been demonstrated to perform very close to the Shannon limit under iterative decoding algorithms such as the Belief Propagation (BP) algorithm. However, for short- and moderate-length LDPC codes sent over AWGN channel, cycles exist in the corresponding Tanner graphs of the LDPC codes, rendering the BP algorithm becoming a sub-optimized decoding method. For example, the BP algorithm is found to be stuck in the trapping sets [2] and cannot converge, resulting block errors even at a high signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). Therefore, finding a realizable, ML approaching decoder for short- and moderate-length LDPC codes that can effectively overcome these trapping sets has always been a challenging problem. Researchers have solved the issue to some extent by improving the BP decoding algorithm itself [2]-[4] or by post-processing the signals obtained from the BP decoders [5]-[8]. Recently, extensive efforts have also been spent on improving the performance of LDPC codes over a binary erasure channel (BEC). For example, in [9]-[14], some good-performance decoding algorithms have been proposed. In particular, the Maxwell decoder accomplishes a ML-approaching performance for a BEC channel [14].
Inspired by the post-processing technique used for the BP decoders in the case of an AWGN channel and by the ML-approaching performance of the Maxwell decoder in the case of a BEC channel, we propose a joint decoding algorithm,

called the BM decoding algorithm, in this paper. First, after the BP decoding process, we will convert the AWGN channel into an effective BEC channel by post-processing the BP messages. Such a conversion is called a channel transformation. Together with this conversion, some trapping sets will be changed into stopping sets. Then, we exploit the Maxwell decoding algorithm to further overcome many of these small stopping sets such that the correct codeword can be found. In other words, the BM decoding algorithm consists of three major steps: (i) standard BP decoding, (ii) channel transformation, and (iii) Maxwell decoding. Simulation results show that the proposed decoding algorithm can outperform the BP decoding over an AWGN channel, particularly when the BP decoder output possesses a relatively small percentage of error bits. The BM decoder hence opens a new perspective toward finding ML-approaching decoders for LDPC codes over an AWGN channel.

II. THE BM DECODING ALGORITHM
The proposed BM decoding algorithm consists of three steps: standard BP decoding, channel transformation and Maxwell decoding. In this section we will first introduce some definitions and notation, and review the Maxwell decoder. Afterwards, we will describe the proposed BM decoding algorithm. In the following, the log-likelihood ratio (LLR) values are always referred to as the LLR values after a BP decoder.
A. Definitions and Notation
A LDPC code is a linear block code with a sparse parity-check matrix. Every parity-check matrix can be represented by a Tanner graph with two sets of nodes: variable nodes representing the encoded symbols and check nodes representing the parity-check constraints. Given a LDPC code, we denote V as the set of variable nodes; C as the set of check nodes; mvc as the variable-to-check message and mcv as the check-to-variable message. We also denote v(ci ) as the set of variable nodes connected to the check ci and nc as the number of unsatisfied check nodes. In the case of a BEC channel, we denote ε as the subset of V erased by the channel (the erasure symbol is denoted by “?”). Moreover, we denote some unknown variable nodes belonging to a set χ by xi (the value of xi is 0 or 1), with i being the index.

B. Maxwell Decoder
The Maxwell decoder was first proposed to analyze the relationship between the BP algorithm and the maximum a posteriori (MAP) decoding algorithm for a BEC channel [13]. In the initialization step, the cumulative sums of all the check nodes contained in the residual graph are computed. Then, the Maxwell decoder begins an iterative decoding process, the steps of which are shown as follows.
1) If the residual graph G is an empty graph, then go to 6).
2) If the number of check nodes of residual degree-1 is zero, in other word the decoder gets stuck in a non-empty stopping set. Then go to 5).
3) Choose a check node of residual degree-1 from the graph G at random. Forward the check-to-variable message mcv of the selected check node to its neighbor node and compute the variable-to-check message mvc of the connected variable node. If mvc = mcv , then delete the check node and its connected edge. If the variable node is connected to several degree-1 check nodes, choose the most reliable message of its connected check nodes as its final message. to some introduced variables xi and eliminate these variables. 4) Forward mvc of the selected variable node in the previous step to all its remaining neighbors and update mcv of its neighbors. III. SIMULATION RESULTS

In this section, we present the simulation results of the LDPC codes described in Section II. An AWGN channel and BPSK modulation are assumed. Moreover, the BM algorithm has been optimized. We first divide the error frames into three types according to the proportion of error bits: Type 1 error frames contain less than 1% error bits compared with the code length, i.e., less than 10 bits in our simulations after the BP decoding; Type 2 error frames contain 1% to 2% error bits compared with the code length, i.e. 10~20 bits; and Type 3 error frames contain more than 2% error bits compared with the code length, i.e., more than 20 bits.

We consider the (3,6) regular LDPC code and the irregular LDPC code described in Section II. Fig. 3 plots the frame error rates (FERs) of the BP and BM decoders for these two codes. The simulation results show that the BM algorithm outperforms the BP algorithm by about 0.1 dB to 0.2 dB for the range of Eb/N0 under study. Furthermore, the distribution of the error-frame types after the BP decoder and after the BM decoder for the irregular code is shown in Fig. 4. It can be seen from the figure that the BM decoding algorithm can dramatically reduce the number of Type 1 error frames when compared with the BP decoding algorithm. For example, at Eb/N0 = 2.5 dB, the BP decoder has 66 Type 1 error frames but the BM decoder produces only 22 such frames. In Fig. 5, we plot the total number of error bits resulting from Type 1 error frames for the irregular code. We observe that the number of such error bits lie between 110 and 170 for the BM decoder while the number of such error bits lie between 260 and 310 for the BP decoder. Similar observations are also obtained for the regular code but are not shown here due to shortage of space. In summary, the BM decoding algorithm outperforms the standard BP decoding algorithm by further correcting error frames with a small number error bits. The price to pay is an increase of 18% in computation time compared with the BP algorithm.

IV. CONCLUSION

The Maxwell decoding algorithm for LDPC codes has been well-known for its ML-approaching performance over BEC channels. In this paper, we have introduced the concept of channel transformation that converts a problem under an AWGN channel into one under a BEC channel. Furthermore, by incorporating a BP decoder, a channel transformation and a Maxwell decoder, we form a joint decoder named as BM decoder. Simulation results have shown that the BM decoding algorithm has a better FER performance than the BP decoder because the BM decoder can eliminate most of the Type 1 error frames (frames with a small proportion of error bits) which cannot be resolved by a BP decoder. Therefore, the BM decoder can be considered as a step closer to the ML decoder for LDPC codes over an AWGN channel. However, most Type 2 and Type 3 error frames (frames with a relatively larger proportion of error bits) remain unsolvable by the BM

algorithm because the probability of the channel transformation is much smaller than that of Type 1 frames. Thus, part of our future work includes further optimizing the BM decoding algorithm so as to improve the error rate. It is expected that an intensive optimization of the residual graph can lead to a larger performance gain compared with the BP algorithm.
Moreover, it has been reported that the error floor of LDPC codes is mainly due to low-weight near-codewords [15] or small trapping sets [16] rather than low-weight codewords, and both low-weight near-codewords and small trapping sets result in Type 1 error frames. Thus, the BM decoder, with a remarkable advantage in eliminating Type 1 error frames, may also be effective in alleviating the error-floor problem in LDPC codes after the parameters have been optimized.
