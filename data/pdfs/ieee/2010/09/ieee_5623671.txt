Design of Permuted Serially Concatenated Multiple Parity-Check Codes
Marco Baldi, Giovanni Cancellieri, Franco Chiaraluce and Amedeo De Amicis
Università Politecnica delle Marche Ancona, Italy
E-mail: {m.baldi; g.cancellieri; f.chiaraluce; a.deamicis}@univpm.it

Abstract: This paper elaborates on the design of Multiple Serially Concatenated Multiple Parity-Check (M-SC-MPC) codes, that are a class of structured Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes recently proposed by the authors. M-SC-MPC codes are obtained as the serial concatenation of simple component codes, and their associated Tanner graph can be free of short length cycles. In this paper, we adopt a modified version of the Progressive Edge Growth (PEG) algorithm to improve the design of M-SC-MPC codes in terms of local cycles length. The proposed codes can be seen as M-SC-MPC codes where an interleaver is added between each pair of component codes; so we denote them as Permuted Serially Concatenated Multiple Parity-Check (P-SC-MPC) codes. We assess their performance through numerical simulations and compare them with previous solutions. We show that the proposed codes perform comparably or even better than both regular and irregular MSC-MPC codes and Quasi-Cyclic (QC) codes included in the IEEE 802.16e standard.
Keywords: Channel coding, M-SC-MPC codes, PEG algorithm.
1. INTRODUCTION
In the context of soft-decision Forward Error Correcting (FEC) techniques, Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes [1] represent a state-of-art solution for obtaining capacity achieving performance with limited complexity [2].
Despite semi-random and irregular LDPC codes are known to asymptotically achieve the best performance [3], structured and regular LDPC codes are often preferred in recommendations and practical applications, due to the fact that they facilitate the codec implementation and, in many cases, are still able to achieve almost optimal performance.
A widespread class of structured LDPC codes are QuasiCyclic (QC) LDPC codes, that have both the parity-check and the generator matrices formed by circulant blocks [4]. For this reason, encoding can be accomplished through very simple circuits, based on barrel shift registers, that allow to significantly reduce the implementation complexity.
An alternative class of structured LDPC codes is represented by Multiple Serially Concatenated Multiple Parity-Check (M-SC-MPC) codes, that we have recently proposed [5]. M-SC-MPC codes are obtained as the serial concatenation of M very simple component codes we denote as Multiple Parity-Check (MPC) codes. M-SC-MPC codes have been proved to be able to approach and even outperform

other families of structured codes, like Multiple Serially Concatenated Single Parity-Check (M-SC-SPC) codes [6], of which they are a generalization. MPC codes have also been used as the starting point for designing new families of semirandom and QC-LDPC codes by exploiting the usage of interleavers in the serial concatenation [7].
The idea of introducing interleavers between each pair of serially concatenated components has also inspired this work, where we investigate the possibility of using interleavers for increasing the length of local cycles in the Tanner graph associated with an M-SC-MPC code. Similarly to [7], we use the Progressive Edge Growth (PEG) algorithm [8] to optimize the length of local cycles in the code design. However, contrary to [7], where non-structured semi-random codes are designed, we are interested in preserving the very simple structure of M-SC-MPC codes.
For such reason, we propose the usage of a modified version of the PEG algorithm that operates block-wise on the parity-check matrix in order to preserve its structured character. The modified PEG algorithm we adopt replaces identity blocks within the parity-check matrix of an M-SCMPC code with circulant permutation blocks or null blocks. This allows to maintain a very simple structured nature of the parity-check matrix (similar to that of QC codes), that greatly facilitates the hardware implementation. The LDPC codes so designed can still be seen as the serial concatenation of MPC components, but with the inclusion of an interleaver between each pair of them. So, we denote the new family of codes as Permuted Serially Concatenated Multiple Parity-Check codes or, briefly, P-SC-MPC codes. They have longer local cycles with respect to M-SC-MPC codes and can be irregular. Recently, we have proposed an alternative approach to design irregular M-SC-MPC codes and increase the length of local cycles in their Tanner graph [9], [10]. Such approach is based on the cancellation of some blocks in the parity-check matrix of an M-SC-MPC code and, in some cases, it is able to significantly improve the code features under the considered viewpoints. In this paper, we also compare P-SC-MPC codes with irregular M-SC-MPC codes proposed in [9] and [10].
The paper is organized as follows: in Section 2 we recall the definition of M-SC-MPC codes and their main properties; in Section 3 we introduce P-SC-MPC codes and the modified PEG algorithm we use for their design; in Section 4 we compare P-SC-MPC codes with regular and irregular M-SCMPC codes; finally, Section 5 concludes the paper.

An M-SC-MPC code is defined as the serial concatenation of M component codes, each with length ni, dimension ki and redundancy ri, i = 1…M. Serial concatenation is systematic, in the sense that each component code appends its ri redundancy bits to the input word; so, the serially concatenated code has dimension k (that coincides with the number of information bits given as input to the first component code) and redundancy:

The i-th component code is an MPC code that calculates its j-th redundancy bit as the parity-check of the codeword bits whose indexes are smaller than j by an integer multiple of ri. This gives, for each component code, a parity-check matrix formed by an array of ri×ri identity matrices. The component parity-check matrices can be combined to obtain a valid parity-check matrix for the serially concatenated code, that has lower triangular form due to the systematic character of the components and the overall serially concatenated code.

It can be proved that M-SC-MPC codes are also LowDensity Generator Matrix (LDGM) codes, and this allows to deduce some further properties of this class of codes. Due to the systematic character of the code, the generator matrix (G) is a k×n binary matrix whose leftmost part is formed by a k×k identity block. As concerns its remaining r columns, that permit to calculate the redundancy part of each codeword, they can be found through the following procedure.
Let us consider the row of G at position k – q, q ∈ {0, 1, …, k – 1} and limit the analysis to its first k elements. Due to the systematic form of G, only the element at column k – q is set to 1, while all the other elements are null. 3. P-SC-MPC CODES
The parity-check matrix of an M-SC-MPC code, described in the previous section, is free of length-4 cycles, under the condition (2), but often exhibits a high number of length-6 cycles. As short length cycles prevent the LDPC decoder from working in near-ideal conditions, it is worth to investigate how their length could be increased.
In [7], the authors start from M-SC-MPC codes and aim at increasing their local cycles length by including an interleaver between each pair of component MPC codes. Such approach exploits the PEG algorithm for optimizing the cycle length within each component parity-check matrix, and the result is a family of semi-random LDPC codes, with nonstructured parity-check matrices. Differently from such approach, we want to investigate the chance of increasing the length of local cycles without renouncing the structured nature of the parity-check matrices, that is an important feature for the decoder implementation. For this reason, we propose to adopt the PEG algorithm for the design of interleavers able to preserve the structured form of M-SCMPC matrices.
Inspired by the approach proposed in [11], we adopt a modified version of the PEG algorithm that operates blockwise, in such a way to preserve the block-based structure of the parity-check matrix. More specifically, our modified PEG algorithm assigns a whole ri×ri matrix at each iteration. In the original M-SC-MPC matrix, each ri×ri block is an identity matrix (or part of it, for the leftmost blocks). We remove such constraint and let each ri×ri block to be an arbitrary circulant permutation matrix. The choice of each circulant permutation matrix is the degree of freedom on which the PEG optimization acts, aiming at increasing the length of local cycles. The adoption of generic circulant permutation matrices in place of identity matrices (that are a special case of circulant permutation matrices) in each matrix layer (except for the last block in each layer, to preserve the systematic form) is equivalent to the introduction of a permutation of the information bits given as input to each MPC encoder. We denote these codes as P-SC-MPC codes.

4. PERFORMANCE COMPARISON
We provide results of numerical simulations of transmission over the Additive White Gaussian Noise (AWGN) channel, when using Binary Phase Shift Keying (BPSK) modulation. We adopt the standard Sum-Product Algorithm (SPA) with log-likelihood ratios (LLR) for LDPC decoding [12]. We also consider irregular M-SC-MPC (iMSC-MPC) codes designed through the approach proposed in [9] and [10], and compare their features with those of the new codes. For the choice of the code parameters, we refer to some of the QC codes included in the IEEE 802.16e standard for mobile wireless metropolitan area networks [13]. We consider codes with block length n = 1632 and three values of code rate: 1/2, 2/3 and 3/4. For each choice of the parameters, we have designed two M-SC-MPC codes with a different number of component codes. Starting from the MSC-MPC code with the highest M, we have also designed an irregular M-SC-MPC code, following the approach in [9] and [10], and a P-SC-MPC code based on the modified PEG algorithm.
The parameters of the considered codes are given in Table 1, where the average column weight (<dv>) and the number of nodes with length-6 cycles (L6) are also reported. As we observe from the table, the proposed P-SC-MPC design approach is really effective in optimizing the local cycles distribution. For all the considered code rates, the P-SC-MPC code has a lower number of length-6 cycles with respect to the M-SC-MPC and iM-SC-MPC codes with the same value of M (we remind that length-4 cycles are avoided by design; so, the cycles length is increased to 8 or more).
Simulation results are reported in Figs. 4, 5 and 6 in terms of bit error rate (BER) versus the energy per bit to noise power spectral density ratio (Eb/N0). Looking at the M-SCMPC codes performance, we observe that the choice of a low M is more favorable in the water-fall region, but can yield a significant error floor for high signal-to-noise ratio. To improve the error floor behavior, a higher M has to be adopted, that, however, gives worse performance in the water-fall region.

The design of irregular M-SC-MPC codes is able to join the advantages of both low and high M values: at all rates, the curve corresponding to the iM-SC-MPC code shows a gain with respect to the M-SC-MPC code with high M, and also keeps good performance in the error floor region. For all the considered values of code rate, iM-SC-MPC codes also achieve better performance with respect to the corresponding M-SC-MPC codes with low M.
The proposed P-SC-MPC codes are also able to achieve very good performance, that, for all the considered code rates, even improves that of the corresponding iM-SC-MPC code. P-SC-MPC codes also compare favorably with the QC codes included in the IEEE 802.16e standard,, thus confirming that they can be of interest for practical applications.

5. CONCLUSION
We have proposed a design technique for increasing the length of local cycles in M-SC-MPC codes. The proposed approach is based on the PEG algorithm, that we have modified in order to optimize the cycles length while preserving the structured nature of M-SC-MPC matrices.
The proposed solution is equivalent to the inclusion of an interleaver between each pair of component MPC codes in serial concatenation. Differently from previously proposed solutions based on interleavers, our aim was to preserve the structured character of the M-SC-MPC matrices.
The proposed P-SC-MPC codes are better than the original M-SC-MPC codes and comparable, or even better, than recently proposed variants, as irregular M-SC-MPC codes. PSC-MPC codes are also able to reach and, in some cases, improve the performance of IEEE 802.16e standard codes.
