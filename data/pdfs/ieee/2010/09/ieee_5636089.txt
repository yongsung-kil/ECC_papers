A Configurable FPGA Implementation of PEG-based PS-LDPC Decoder

Kun WANG,Ning-qing LIU,Bin SUN
Communication Research Center Harbin Institute of Technology
Harbin, China dulubleach@126.com

Hao-wei SUN
School of Mechanical Science and Engineering Daqing Petroleum Institute Daqing, China

Abstract—Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes form an important subclass of error correcting coding techniques, and its implementation has been hot spot of domains such as signal process, magnetic recording or next generation communication for years. This paper proposes a configurable FPGA implementation of Partition-and-Shift LDPC decoder based on Min-Sum algorithm. An MPEG algorithm is introduced to reduce the complexity of searching for closed paths in shift matrix. And a routing paths switch concept is proposed to realize the expected configurability. Benefits of the proposed technique are demonstrated with FPGA design for differently configured PS-LDPC decoders which all achieve a Gbps throughput, low hardware cost and excellent BER performance at 16 iterations.
Keywords- Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes; Error correcting coding technique; Signal process; Partition-and-Shift LDPC codes; Min-Sum algorithm
I. INTRODUCTION
Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes, invented by Gallager in 1962 [1], were rediscovered and proved to achieve near-Shannon capacity in the late 1990s [2,3]. Researchers have shown an increasing interest in developing implementation of LDPC codes [4-9]. Though the inherent parallel manner of LDPC codes brings great possibilities for its realization, there are still many challenges to implement LDPC decoders, while targeting good error correction performance, high throughput, low hardware complexity and cost. In this context, many decoder architectures have been well studied [4-8]. Nevertheless, none of those performs the desired high throughput. Then Radivoje Zarubica et al. [9] propose a novel fully-parallel FPGAbased LDPC decoder architecture which achieves high throughput (6 Gbps). But it also brings high hardware complexity and costs a mass of logic resource.
This paper will focus on FPGA implementation of a configurable low-cost and high throughput LDPC decoder. To guarantee good error correcting performance with low complexity, PS-LDPC (Partition-and-Shift LDPC) codes [10-12], which facilitate the systematic design of LDPC decoders, are used. And to improve the efficiency of “searching for closed paths” in shift matrix when constructing a PS-LDPC matrix, a novel algorithm based on the PEG (Progressive Edge-Growth) [13-15] principle is proposed in this paper. Additionally, to reduce hardware

complexity and cost, a “routing paths switching” concept is introduced. It allows the reuse of VPUs (Variable node Processor Unit) during the decoding iteration. In addition, the structure of PS-LDPC matrices of the proposed decoder can be configured according to different requirement of performance and cost.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. This paper first gives a brief overview of PS-LDPC codes in section II. Then we propose a construction method for PSLDPC codes in Section III. Section IV describes the MS decoding algorithm in a special mode to use it in our design. The decoder architecture is presented in Section V. Section VI reports a specific performance-analysis of the proposed PS-LDPC decoder. The last section assesses our works.

III. CONSTRUCTION OF PS-LDPC CODES PS-LDPC codes form a sort of LDPC codes with arbitrary large girth, arbitrary column weight, and flexible code rates. An elaborate algorithm for shift parameter choosing has been reported in [12]. However, to determine the shift parameter Sα,β in S in this algorithm, all the closed paths of length 4 to 2t that containing Sα,β should be found firstly. It’s a pretty hard work, especially when t is large. In order to reduce complexity of this work, a novel algorithm MPEG (Modified PEG) based on the progressive edgegrowth principle is proposed in this section.
Figure 2. Progressive edge-growth for closed paths
For the case of searching closed path of length 2t containing Sα,β, a tree of depth 2t (indexed 0 to 2t-1) is progressively grown as shown in Fig 2. In this tree each point denotes a row (colored gray, called row-point) or a column (colored black, called column-point). Firstly, the column-point β in depth-0 is branched to several row-points which are linked to column β in the shift matrix. Then each row-point in depth-1 is branched to several column-points which are linked to this row-point in the shift matrix, except for column β. By this way, the other row-points and column-points in the tree of 2t depth are generated alternately. Finally, each row-point α in depth 2t-1 which is not branched from β, is linked to a column-point β in the final step. As shown in Fig. 2, each column-point β in the final step has a unique path to the one in depth-0, which is the length 2t closed path expected. The algorithm described above is called MPEG method.
By employing this method, the closed paths are found efficiently. And based on these closed paths, 2t-CYCLE theorem [12] can be used as criterion to determine Sα,β. To verify the correctness of the approach above, BER of the constructed PS-LDPC codes with girth 12 is shown in Fig 3. The simulation results with maximum 16 iterations indicate an excellent error correcting capability of such LDPC codes.

V. DECODER ARCHITECTURE
The PS-LDPC decoder in this paper is composed of VPUs (Variable node Processor Unit), CPUs (Check node Processor Unit), RPUs (routing paths unit), IRPU (inverse routing paths uint) and an interface unit.
A. Overall working schedule
Different from fully parallel decoding schedule, in one clock cycle, only p variable nodes are processed simultaneously in this paper. The variable nodes indexed from 0 to p-1 are processed first, and then p to 2p-1, and so on. At the same time, all the Nc×p check nodes are processed concurrently. In each clock cycle, each VPU receives Nc L(rJ,I) messages and compute the Nc L(qI,J) messages. Each CPU receives one L(qI,J) message and accomplishes one step of the check node process in each clock cycle, so that after Nb clock cycles a CPU will output the L(rJ,I) messages transferred to the VPUs in the next iteration. With this working schedule, one iteration can be accomplished after at least Nb clock cycles.
B. Routing paths unit

enabled in turn according to the grouping structure of PSLDPC matrix.
In fully parallel architecture of LDPC decoder, thousands of VPUs may be required, which induces huge hardware consumption. However, by using this routing paths switching structure, we manage to divide the process of computing all the L(rJ,I) messages into Nb steps, so that VPU can be reused when completing the calculating work in different clock cycles. Therefore only p VPUs are required in this decoder, which is significant to cut down hardware cost. C. Architecture of CPU and VPU
Fig. 5 illuminates the structure of a CPU. According to the principle of CPU, magnitudes of the outputs should be the two minimal ones of the Nb inputs in each iteration. Firstly, |·| module transforms the input L(qI,J) messages into magnitude bits and sign bit, and then the COMP module is used to pick out the two minimal ones of all the Nb magnitudes in one iteration. Finally, in the last clock cycle in each iteration, the minimal two magnitudes and the index of the minimal one should be stored. In the next iteration, these data are transformed into L(rJ,I) messages which are transferred to VPUs cycle by cycle.
Figure 5. Architecture of Check node processor unit
The block diagram of a VPU of degree three is shown on Fig. 6. Targeting high throughput, VPU employs 2-stage pipeline design to increase the clock frequency. Although totally eight 2-input adders are used in each VPU, the hardware consumption is acceptable since the total number of VPUs has been reduced.

Figure 8. Interface uint and RAM address division
To transform the high speed serial data to the repeated and parallel L(c) messages required, two RAMs are employed in both input and output interface. The illustration of interface module and the address allocation of a read RAM are shown in Fig. 8. For the case of the input data interface, during the decoding process of a block of codes, the received symbols are written into one of the two RAMs,

and the other stores the L(c) messages which are read repeatedly by the current decoding process. During the decoding process of the next block of codes, functions of the two RAMs are interchanged. The output interface has similar structure and inversed function of the input data interface.
The frequency of writing the received symbols into the RAM is f1, and that of reading LLR messages is f2. f1 and f2 are determined by the throughput required and the working frequency of the decoding module.

VII. CONCLUSION
The work has been undertaken to design a sort of configurable PS-LDPC decoder. We first bring in the PSLDPC codes for implementation because of its good error correcting performance and low hardware designing complexity. The main contribute of this study is the routing paths switch concept which is essential for the configurability of the decoder architecture. Such PS-LDPC decoders differently configured have been implemented, which perform Gbps throughput, high error correcting capability and low hardware cost, as expected.
The performance comparison of the differently configured decoders verifies that the decoder design is configurable in terms of p, qN and Nb, to meet different requirements. In addition, such decoder architecture is also suitable for other subclasses of QC-LDPC codes.
