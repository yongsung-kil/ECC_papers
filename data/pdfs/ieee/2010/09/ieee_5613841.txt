Abstract-In this work, we propose a method specially suited for creating high rate LDPe codes. The technique employed adds the connections of the bipartite graph on an edge-by-edge basis. Unlike other progressive edge-growth methods however, we favor the use of a density metric over the conventional distance metric for the node selection process. As benchmark, the algorithm yield codes of higher rates than those obtained from bit-filling algorithms. Because of its efficient approach in filling edges on the bipartite graph however, the algorithm may also be used to produce codes (of various rates) that are very competitive in terms of error-correcting performance.
I. INTRODUCING THE BASIC CONCEPT
Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes were first intro­ duced by R. Gallager in 1962 [1] and rediscovered by MacKay and Neal in 1995 [2]. Edges, on a bipartite graph representing the code [3], connect two distinct sets of nodes (check nodes and bit nodes) based on the location of non-zero entries in the associated parity-check matrix. Conventionally, the decoding algorithm for LDPC codes makes use of an iterative message-passing schedule over the bipartite graph [4] and its performance is dependant upon the specific edge connections and local topologies of the graph [5], [6], [7]. It is known for example that graphs with cycles of length 4 lead to bad performance. In his design, MacKay [4] constrained his constructions to avoid such cycles. One may infer that removing larger cycles will lead to better performance. This is true and confirmed by the work of many, such as [8], [9].
As such, efficient construction of bipartite graphs is as im­ portant as the elaboration of appropriate decoding algorithms for LDPC codes. Indeed a good message-passing algorithm running over a poorly constructed graph will lead to bad per­ formance [4]. A great deal of research is therefore conducted for the elaboration of algorithms to create good LDPC graphs.
One branch focuses on algebraic constructions such as [10], [11], [12]. The graphs generated are usually quasi-cyclic and as such enable linear-time encoding. Another branch uses graph-theoretic approach such as [13], [14] with the objective of creating graphs with the largest girth possible. Note that the girth of a code is defined as the length of the smallest cycle in its bipartite graph. Since the number of independent iterations of the decoding algorithm is directly dependent upon the girth [15], maximizing the latter can indeed be an interesting

avenue. High girth alone however is not sufficient to ensure good performance [16].
In this work, a progressive edge-growth technique similar to those of [9], [13], [17], [18] is presented. Conventionally, with some slight variations, these techniques proceed by choosing nodes furthest along some paths on the graph. However, they do not efficiently discriminate among the final candidate nodes. The bit-filling algorithm [9], for example, proceeds by choosing the final candidate that has the least degree. If more than one node share this attribute, then the algorithm searches one level deeper for the number of connections of these nodes, as a means of further discrimination. The density-based PEG, on the other side, counts the number of nodes spanning the tree of final candidates up to a certain depth, and chooses the one with least count or density. Indeed, each count result relates to the "density" of the subgraph of a final candidate node. As such, while the distance metric is relatively short­ sighted and optimizes the selection as it progresses from one step to the next, the DPEG method optimizes (in a single run) the various subgraphs, ensuring that no one is carrying a disproportionate number of nodes. Results provided in this paper provides ample evidence of the efficacy of the technique demonstrated.
The paper is organized as follows: In Section II, the nota­ tions to be used are introduced. A description of the DPEG technique used to maximize rate is provided in Section III. Section N portrays a modification of the technique for girth­ maximization. Results are provided in Section V and we finally conclude with Section VI.

IV. MAXIMIZING THE GIRTH OF A GIVEN CODE WITH
FIXED RATE
We now turn our attention to a slightly different application of the DPEG technique. Assuming a quasi-regular LDPe code (with fix number of Is per column) and fixed rate needs to be created, how can the DPEG technique be adapted to maximize the girth of such code? Since harmonizing the density of subgraphs enhances the rate, it is reasonable to believe that girth can be maximized when rate is kept fixed.
To this end, a strategy similar to [13] is developed, with algorithm 3 and 4 providing the steps required. An upper and lower bound on the girth are first specified, denoted by 9max and 9min respectively. The basic idea is then to create an initial graph where the minimum girth acceptable is gmin + 2 (using algorithm 3). Once a graph is built with those characteristics, bit nodes with less than A edges are pruned, together with their connectivity information, and the graph building process continues. However, this time, the minimum girth allowable is set to 9min (refer to Fig. 4 which depicts how girth varies
when a (504, 252) code is created). This strategy serves to
compensate for the sub-optimality of the algorithms we use. In fact, these could also have been altered in a number of ways, each with different possible consequences. A few alternatives are presented below:
1) Start the graph with girth 9max, then prune rejected bit nodes, and lower the girth by two, until 9min is reached.
2) Instead of choosing check nodes with least density, first pre-screen check nodes with least degree, and only then select nodes in that group with least density. This would have the effect of creating concentrated (or perfectly regular) codes.

VI. CONCLUSION
In this work, we described a "density-based" progressive­ edge growth (DPEG) technique for the creation of LDPC codes. Contrary to the current trend, we make use of a density metric, rather than the conventional distance metric to discrim­ inate among nodes for the selection process. Results show that very high rate codes can be created. In addition, the technique also proves to be competitive in terms of error-correcting performance, outperfoming randomly generated codes and even PEG codes of [13]. Another interesting result is that the girth histogram of the (1008,504) code produced is better than the corresponding modified PEG code of [17].
