A Multi-Standard Flexible TurbolLDPC Decoder via ASIC Design

Abstract-This paper describes the first complete design of a single-core multi-standard flexible TurbolLDPC decoder using an ASIC approach. Such a solution outperforms other state-of-the­ art implementations based on application-specific instruction-set processors (ASIPs), which are shown to sufTer from impaired throughput and power consumption. In this paper, we describe in detail the VLSI flexible architecture of a decoder coping with all the moderu communication standards defining LDPC and Turbo codes, and provide a proof-of-concept implementation complaint with 3GPP-HSDPA, DVB-SH, IEEE 802.16e and IEEE 802.11n standards. T he decoder, running at only 150 MHz for a reduced power, occupies an area of 0.9mm2 with a maximum power consumption of only 86.1 mW.
I. INTRODUCTION
The integration of a wide range of different applications onto the same hand-held device is becoming a must for the worldwide users and the evolution of the mobile market represents a valuable example of this overwhelming trend. Initially conceived for voice traffic, mobile devices are indeed embracing advanced services such as fast Internet connection, satellite broadcast TV and positioning, to mention some.
The ever increasing spectrum of applications, featuring high data-rate and requiring enhanced quality-of-service (QoS), has fostered the adoption of channel coding as an effective method to improve the communication reliability. Among different techniques, low-density parity-check (LDPC) [1] and Turbo codes [2] are currently regarded as the most effective solutions for forward error correction (FEC). However, the design of an advanced FEC system is still very challenging since it is the most computationally demanding block of the whole transceiver and so the bottleneck in terms of hardware-complexity, achievable data-rate and power con­ sumption. Moreover, the channel decoder shall be compliant with many or even all the communication standards in use. In this sense, flexibility, i.e. the support of multiple standards with the minimum softwarelhardware overhead, represents the driven factor to keep complexity and power consumption under control.
To the best of our knowledge this work is the first doc­ umented design of a flexible multi-standard LDPCffurbo decoder using an ASIC approach. As shown in Sec. VII, this strategy overcomes other state-of-the-art solutions especially in terms of achievable data-rate and power consumption.

II. ANALY SIS OF THE STATE-OF-THE-ART AND
ENABLING TECHNOLOGIES
The most straightforward solution to attain multi-standard support is the integration of individual intellectual property (IP) cores. Many examples of ad-hoc, single-standard, channel decoder architectures can be found in literature, such as LDPC decoders for IEEE 802. lIn [3] and IEEE 802.16e [4], as well as Turbo decoders for 3GPP's [5] and IEEE 802.16e [6]. In most cases this solution is impractical since, although each core can be individually optimized, the integration of many IPs is clearly demanding in terms of area and power consumption.
Alternatively, it is possible to conceive a multi-standard decoder as made up of two different flexible cores, one specialized for Turbo and the other for LDPC decoding. A flexible LDPC decoder compliant with IEEE 802.IIn and IEEE 802.16e was proposed in [7] while a flexible binary/duo­ binary Turbo decoder architecture compliant with UMTS and 802.16e was described in [8]. Although considerable reduc­ tions of complexity and power consumption can be expected, this solution is still suboptimal in terms of area and power consumption especially when targeting mobile devices.
The most promising approach is the design of a single IP capable of supporting both Turbo and LDPC codes. Currently, application-specific instruction-set processor (ASIP) is the enabling technology for multi-standard decoders. Alles at al. proposed the so-called FlexiChaP decoder in [9], supporting both binary & duo-binary turbo codes as well as LDPC codes for IEEE 802.11n and IEEE 802.16e, with a chip area of only 0.622 mm2• Another interesting ASIP platform was proposed by Naessens et al. in [10], supporting all the codes defined by IEEE 802.16e and IEEE 802.11n with an area of 0.96 mm2. Typically an ASIP implementation features low hardware complexity with most of the decoding steps performed in a time division manner. Consequently, the increase of decoding time, also caused by the fetch/decode/execute/storage cycles typical of instruction-set processors, is usually compensated by the use of high operating clock frequencies, to the detriment of the overall power consumption. As an example in [9], although the operating clock frequency was pushed up to 400 MHz, the attained throughput was still inadequate for the IEEE 802.16e support.

Although the proposed VLSI architecture can in principle serve any communication standard implementing TurbolLDPC codes, we focused on mobile applications and considered as a benchmark scenario the support of UMTS/3GPP-HSDPA [12], IEEE 802.11n [13], IEEE 802.16e [14] and DVB-SH [15].

As a baseline, Turbo codes are all based on 8-state parallel concatenated convolutional codes (PCCCs) which yield a reduced-complexity design of the state­ metric recursion unit [16]. On the other hand LDPC codes fall into the class of architecture-aware (AA) codes [17] which can be exploited for a massive parallelization of the decoding operations.
IV. B CJR DECODING ALGORITHM SCHEDULE
The identification of a common decoding algorithm is a pivotal step toward the definition of a low-complexity flexible architecture, as it is at the base of the massive sharing of hardware resources. The BCJR algorithm [18] has been selected as the most appropriate solution since, besides the well-known use for turbo decoding, it can be also employed for LDPC decoding in the form of turbo-decoding message­ passing (TDMP) [17].

Specifically, the use of a single-processor (SP) in a time division manner to perform both the forward (0:) and backward ((3) recursion, increases the decoding time but only requires single port memories for extrinsic information. Conversely, the decoding time of a double-processor (DP) architecture is considerably smaller, but dual-port memories are required to sustain two concurrent accesses, one for each recursion.
Focusing on the initialization strategy, the use of a training­ sequence (TS) for the backward recursion increases the de­ coding time without requiring any initialization memory. On the other hand, the border-metric (BM) technique initializes the backward recursion with the final values at the previous iteration. Although this approach reduces the decoding time, it also requires an extra memory to store the border metrics. A. LDPC codes
The TDMP algorithm of LDPC codes is a particular form of layered decoding [19], where check-node operations are carried out via the BCJR algorithm. Indeed every check-node constraint defined by the parity check matrix can be viewed as a 2-state trellis [17]. Since the maximum check node degree of the supported codes is relatively small (22), the original formulation of the BCJR algorithm can be run disregarding solutions such as sliding window.

V. VLSI FLEXIBLE DECODER ARCHITECTURE
The architecture of the flexible TurbolLDPC decoder is shown in Fig. 3. Targeting high throughput applications, it is arranged into N data-paths which can either decode N codewords in parallel (inter-trellis mode) or be concurrently assigned to the decoding of a same codeword (intra-trellis mode). Each data-path represents a flexible BCJR-based soft­ in soft-out (SISO) decoder implementing the TS-SP schedule. Although designed to support the benchmark scenario detailed in Sec. III, the architecture can be easily generalized to support any other solutions (e.g. 16-state trellis).

A. Turbo decoding
Data from the LLR (Nc2v) memory feed the channel-metric
manager (CMM) which is in charge of computing the branch
metrics I associated to each edge in the trellis. Along with the
a-priori extrinsic information taken from the related memory, the branch metrics enter the state-metric array (SMA) which
performs the aj(3 recursions. According to the TS-SP schedule the SMA computation is arranged into three phases:
• forward recursion: a-metrics are computed and stored in
the state-metric memory;
• backward training: (3-metrics are not stored but the final
value of the recursion initializes the next phase;
• backward recursion: (3-metrics are computed and summed to the branch metrics «(3 + I = a') to feed the extrinsic processor together with a-metrics read from
memory.
The extrinsic processor updates the a-posteriori reliabilities �, finally stored into the �/SO memory. Note that in case

of a intra-trellis decoding mode, memory conflicts could arise due to the parallelization of the interleaving law. According to [20], the issue is solved by an ad-hoc memory management and by interfacing the memory with two permutation networks in order to properly route the extrinsic messages.
B. LDPC decoding
At the initialization stage, the �/SO memory is written with the channel reliabilities while the Nc2v memory is reset. In this case, the CMM is by-passed, while the permutation network on the �/SO memory output performs a differential cyclic rotation [3] according to the circulants in the parity­ check matrix. Since the SMA can process 8-state duo-binary trellises and the check-node processing involves only 2-state trellises, 8 check-nodes can be updated in parallel. In this case, the SMA performs the BCJR algorithm with the forward phase (and related storage in memory) directly followed by the backward recursion, without training. The extrinsic processor is then configured to update the soft-outputs (SOs), which are an updated estimation of the transmitted bits, and check-to­ variable messages (c2v), stored into the A/c2v memory. Note that in this case the permutation network on the memory input is configured to be transparent.
C. Flexibility Issues
A wide range of coding schemes can be efficiently sup­ ported by ad-hoc designing the processing units of the archi­ tecture as well as their interfaces. These last are structured as optimized multiplexing networks (adaptation networks in Fig. 3) that reconstruct the correct data-flow depending on the decoding scheme in use.
1) State-Metric Array: the architecture of the flexible SMA
is shown in Fig. 4. This is composed of 8 state-metric units (SMUs) plus a feedback network implementing the connec­ tions of the trellis. The latter can be highly optimized in terms of number and size of the constituent multiplexers thanks to an ad-hoc mapping of the trellis states onto the SMUs [16]. Each SMU updates a single state of the forwardlbackward recursion and supports binary as well duo-binary codes. Particularly, four edges are connected to each state of a duo-binary code, thus a maxlmax* operation among four metrics (sum of the recursion
metric at the previous step plus the branch metric and the
a-priori reliability) is needed. The output of the maxlmax* tree is then normalized w.r.t. to the value of the state SO, for optimal fixed-point performance [17]. The normalization value is computed by the processor associated to the state SO whose output is 0. 2) Extrinsic Processor: the extrinsic processor, whose ar­ chitecture is shown in Fig. 5, computes the a-posteriori extrinsic reliabilities. Focusing on 8-state turbo codes, the processor must calculate the max/max* of 8 inputs. Overall, it is equipped with four 8-input max/max* trees, one for each symbol of a duo-binary code. Then, in the case of binary codes (2 symbols), only the trees #0 and #1 are actually used. The inputs of the max/max* trees are provided by the stage of input adder and are properly routed according to the trellis structure through eight 4x4 permutation networks. Finally, the output of the trees are normalized w.r.t. the value associated to the symbol O. In the case of TDMP of LDPC, the max/max* of only two data must be considered. Consequently, the outputs of the first level of the max/max* tree are combined into two 8-adders stages to produce the updated check-to-variable and SO messages. According to the layered algorithm SOs are given by the sum of the updated c2v messages and the v2c metrics, also output by the SMA.

VII. COMPARISONS AND CONCLUSION
To the best of our knowledge, this work is the first attempt to design a flexible multi-standard channel decoding platform with an ASIC approach. For this reason, it is very difficult to make a fair comparison with similar state-of-the-art de­ signs. Nevertheless, the designs in [9], [10] and [11] have been selected as the most relevant implementations for the comparison, reported in Tab. IV.

The decoder in [11] is less complex than the proposed solution, but the reported area relates to a single standard support only. Consequently, a certain overhead must be added in view of the missing multi-standard flexibility. Furthermore, due to the very high clock frequency, the architecture is highly inefficient in terms of power consumption.
The ASIP design in [9] seems to be advantageous in terms of both complexity and power consumption. However, it can not sustain the data-rates of every communication standards: the authors declare a maximum achievable throughput (with only 5 iterations) for the duo-binary IEEE 802.16e Turbo codes of 37.2 Mbps, well below the required 70 Mbps.
A more complete and fair comparison can be done with the ASIP design in [10], a multi-standard decoder fully compliant in terms of throughput. As shown in Tab. IV, our solution outperforms such design both in terms of complexity and power consumption (saving of 57%).
