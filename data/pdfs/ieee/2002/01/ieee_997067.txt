I. INTRODUCTION
It has been shown [ 131 that in additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels long and random codes with sparse parity check matrices (low-density parity check codes) combined with iterative decoding can achieve nearoptimum performance [19]. In the past few years several LDPC codes have been designed with performances very close to the theoretical limit [18] [14]. Also, a significant insight into iterative decoding has been gained due to Kschischang et al. [lo] and McEliece et al. [15] interpretations of a message passing in terms of belief propagation in graphical models [171 [8] [20] [22].
In our recent paper [2 l] we presented a “deterministic” construction of very high rate regular LDPC codes (Gallager codes [9]) based on balanced incomplete block designs (BIBD) [2]. These codes belong to the class of one-step majority logic decodable codes, whose iterative decoding have been discussed recently by Lucas et al. [23]
More specifically, our codes are based on 2(v,k,l) BIBD[S], [12], where v is a number of parity bits, and k is a parity check matrix column weight. The deterministic construction has two major advantages: (i) the resulting codes are structured and can lend themselves to low complexity implementation, and (ii) the code parameters such as minimum distance vzmin)and the girth g) of the underlying bipartite graph are completely determined by the BIBD. The bipartite graphs of BIBD codes have girth g=6, which is acceptable in high speed-low complexity applications where the number of iterations is limited to five to six.
In this paper we introduce a novel construction of highrate codes. The main idea of the paper is the realization that

the minimum distance of codes proposed in [21] can be increased by ensuring that the underlying BIBD does not contain Pasch configurations (to be defined later). However, the anti-Pasch BIBBs are extremely rare, specially those that lead to high rate codes. In this paper a construction of antiPasch codes based on affine geometries is proposed. The main idea is to create a set of parallel lines in affine geometry to eliminate Pasch configurations. The result is a large class of Gallager codes with d,,,,,=6. Using anti-Pasch resolvable designs to construct regular LDPC has been independently proposed by Johnson and Weller [24].

The existence and construction of designs is an active area of research in combinatorics. An class of designs that leads to high-rate codes is due to Buratti [3]. The handbook of combinatorial designs [4] edited by Colbourn and Dinitz is an excellent reference. The Abel and Greg Table 2.3 in [4, pp. 41-421 gives the summary of known results in existence of short designs. However, very often the construction of these design is somewhat heuristic or works only for a given block size. In many cases such constructions give a very small set of design with parameters of practical interests. An important subclass of BIBDs are so called infinite families [4, p. 671, which, as the name indicates contain an infinite number of designs. The examples of these BIBDS include projective geometries, affine geometries, unitals and Denniston designs are some geometric equivalents of 2designs (see [4 VI. 7. 121). The known infinite families of BIBD are listed in TABLE II [4].
Notice that Kou, Lin and Fossorier construction of LDPC codes [l l] is based on projective geometry, a design from this class. Figure 2 shows a lower bound on code length as a function of a code rate for LDPC codes obtained by using a infinite families from Table II.

This motivates the introduction of 2 (v,k,l) designs that have flexible number of blocks h, and arbitrary block size k. The idea is to trade a code rate and number of blocks for the simplicity of construction and flexibility of choosing design parameters. Our construction is based on resolvable BIBD based on an affine geometry in which blocks are defined as lines connecting points of a rectangular integer lattice (more on the concept of affine geometry can be found in [ 11). More importantly, as it will be shown, this construction gives anti-Pasch designs.

Proof Consider a periodically extended lattice. The proof is based on the observation that in a the nzxk lattice it is not possible to create a quadrilateral such that each point occurs twice in it. Note that no sides with infinite slope are allowed The Figure 4 shows one such quadrilateral. Without loss of generality, we can assume that the starting point of the first pair of meeting lines is (0,O). The slopes of the four lines in Figure 4 can be chosen as: s, p, p+a/2 and s-a/2. The points (O,O), (O,a), (2,2s) and (2,a+2p) are all different and each of them is on two lines. As long as the remaining four points are concerned, they will be on two lines in one of these three cases: 1) s=p+a/2 and s+a/2=a+p; 2) s=s+a/2 and p+a/2=p+a and 3) s=p+a and p+a/2=s+a/2 (all operations are modulo nz). The case 1) implies s-p=a/2 which means that points (2,2s) and (2,a+2p) are identical, a contradiction. If both equalities in case 2) and 3) were satisfied, then a would have to be 0, which would mean that two leftmost points are identical, again a contradiction.
