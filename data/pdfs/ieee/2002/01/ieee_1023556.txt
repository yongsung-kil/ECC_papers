Abstract - It has been recognized that appropriatly chosen irregular low-density parity-check codes (LDPCCs) have a remarkable performance advantage compared to regular LDPCCs and that their threshold is much better [I].Starting with regular graphs, we construct codes consisting of more sophisticated subcodes (compared to simple XORs) and show that performance gains can be achieved by optimizing the proportions of the high-degree bit nodes and the different subcodes being used.
I. INTRODUCTION AND MOTIVATION
Let “strict-sense LDPC codes” (SS-LDPCCs) be LDPCCs where all check nodes are simple XORs and let “wide-sense LDPC codes” (WS-LDPCCs) consist of more complicated subcodes. In 1962, Gallager defined the class of SS-LDPCCs where additionally all bit nodes have the same degree and all check nodes have the same degree (regular SS-LDPCCs). Later, Tanner generalized this idea t o WS-LDPCCs. In the second half of the last decade, it was recognized that a sensitive choice of bit and check degrees of SS-LDPCCs can improve the performance quite markedly (see, e.g., [l]);the tool of density evolution for calculating performance thresholds proved t o be very valuable. The aim of this paper is t o show that irregularity helps also WS-LDPCCs; we will design codes that have optimized proportions of the high-degree bit nodes and the subcodes being used, respectively.
A second motivation was the fact that most graph families studied by mathematicians are regular (i.e. the degrees of all vertices are the same) or biregular. One approach to construct a regular SS-LDPCC is to take a biregular bipartite graph where the nodes of each class have the same degree. Other approaches to construct regular and irregular LDPC codes modify, and/or extend, and/or prune the underlying graph while maintaining at least some desirable properties of the graph (see, e.g., [2]). In this paper, we try to keep the whole structure of the underlying graph as far as possible.

We conclude with a few general remarks. The subcodes must be placed so as to avoid small-weight codewords; one can optimize such codes by calculating thresholds; codes with a better threshold usually also have a lower encoding complexity; if the underlying graph is a Cayley graph one can place the subcodes according t o the generators.
