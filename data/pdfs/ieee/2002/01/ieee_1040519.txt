Application of Gaussian Approximation to Iterative Decoding with Finite Blocklengths
A. O¨ zgu¨r Yılmaz and and Wayne E. Stark
University of Michigan, Ann Arbor Dept. of Electrical Engineering and Computer Science
{yilmaza,stark}@eecs.umich.edu

Abstract— In this paper, the convergence analysis based on Gaussian approximation will be extended to small and medium blocklengths. Gaussian approximation to extrinsic information has been instrumental in understanding and predicting the behavior of iterative decoding for codes that would allow iterative decoding such as turbo codes and LDPC codes. It enables a procedure to predict the ”waterfall” region behavior where a steep slope of error rate curves is observed. As well known, combination of transfer function and union bound techniques can be applied to predict the ”error floor” region performance with necessary assumptions. This paper provides a framework to distinguish the different regions of behavior through defining a decoding failure probability.
I. Introduction
Iterative techniques have been investigated for communication systems quite extensively in the last decade. This trend started with the introduction of parallel concatenated convolutional codes (turbo codes) [1] and generated much interest in soft information systems. Turbo codes is a family of codes whose encoders consist of at least two constituent encoders with interleavers in between them. Although maximum a posteriori (MAP) decoding these codes is not practical due to their structure, it is also this structure that enables a straightforward iterative decoding scheme as a suboptimal solution to MAP decoding. MAP decoders of turbo codes’ constituent codes exchange information on data bits (also parity bits if necessary). This overall decoding structure provides systems that performs quite well at rates close to channel capacity in many scenarios with reasonable complexity and interleaver lengths.
Density evolution method was proposed to understand and predict the behavior of iterative decoding of low density parity check codes in [2]. Gaussian approximation to extrinsic information was deployed to analyze turbo decoding in [3] for large blocklengths. El Gamal proved the existence of a convergence threshold for infinitely long blocklengths by regarding the constituent decoders as signal-tonoise ratio transformers [3]. Same approach was taken by ten Brink in [4] where he proposed a method to find convergence thresholds using a different measure to characterize the information generated by the decoders.
This work was supported in part by the Departent of Defense Research & Engineering (DDR&E) Multidisciplinary University Research Initiative (MURI) on “Low Energy Electronics Design for Mobile Platforms” managed by the Army Research Office (ARO) under Grant DAAH04-96-1-0377 aand by National Science Foundation under Grant ECS-9979347.

Current literature on analysis of iterative decoding concentrates on asymptotic behavior, i.e., large blocklengths. However, a wide range of applications mandate the use of small blocklengths in practice. In this paper, we will provide a framework to study the convergence characteristics of iterative decoding for small/medium blocklengths. This study, in no way, proposes a solution to instability problems shown to exist in iterative decoding [5], [6]. This work provides a framework to evaluate and compare various turbo codes for small to medium blocklengths both in the waterfall and error floor regions, where errors are due to convergence problems and code characteristics respectively.
The paper is organized as follows. Section II describes the constituent decoders and explains interleaving issues. In Section III, a probabilistic model of the information exchange will be explained and extended to finite blocklengths. Section IV explains the relation between the convergence characteristics and the probabilistic model of information exchange. In Section V convergence types and errors will be categorized. A new turbo coding scheme will be introduced in Section VI. The paper is concluded by Section VII.
II. Turbo Decoder and Interleaving
Throughout this work, a turbo code with two constituent encoders is employed. Extension of the results to more than two encoders is feasible. Data bits are encoded by the encoders and all the data and parity bits combined into a packet. This packet is BPSK modulated and transmitted over AWGN channel. Bitwise decoding is assumed on the receiver side and symbolwise decoding is similar [7].
A. Turbo Decoder
The turbo decoder is composed of two decoders each corresponding to one of the constituent encoders. Each constituent decoder produces bitwise MAP probabilities by employing BCJR algorithm [8]. Channel observations and a priori information of the data bits are the inputs of the decoder and the output is the bit likelihoods. After a decoder produces bit probabilities, referred to as soft information hereafter, it extracts the so-called extrinsic information in the soft information and passes this extrinsic information to the next decoder as the new a priori information (extrinsic and a priori information will be used interchangeably from this point on.) B. Interleaving
Two different interleavers are considered in this paper. The choice of interleaver has no effect on the probabilistic description of the decoders given the assumptions that will be explained in Section III. The first interleaver considered is the uniform interleaver which permutes a given sequence of bits randomly without any constraint. The second interleaver type is the S-random interleaver obtained with the constraint that no bits within distance S of each other in the input sequence can be mapped to positions within distance S in the interleaved sequence.
III. Probabilistic Model of the MAP Decoder
In this section, we will concentrate on the application of the Gaussian approximation to the extrinsic information for finite blocklengths rather than describing the empirical foundations of this assumption in detail. The Gaussian approximation to the extrinsic information implies that the extrinsic information of a particular bit produced by a decoder at a certain iteration can be approximated as a Gaussian random variable. Furthermore, these random variables are assumed to be independent of the bit’s channel observation and a priori probability at any given iteration [3].
In both [3] and [4], the MAP decoder is regarded as a probabilistic device that transforms the characteristics of the input information, which is extrinsic information from the other decoder, with respect to a parameterized measure. In this paper, we will take the mean of the extrinsic log-likelihood ratios as the choice of characteristic measure. This is due to the fact that assuming the extrinsic information holds the consistency condition (p(y|x) = p(−y|x)ex·y) [2], distribution of the extrinsic information can be described only by this parameter. In addition, this choice fits in well with the characteristics of the decoder for small/medium blocklengths. We will also present results with the mutual information measure (MI) [4] in some of the figures.
As mentioned before, all the previous research on convergence of iterative decoding is towards large blocklengths which can be considered as asymptotic performance. The extrinsic information produced is regarded as a random variable with a fixed parameter. If an input information with mean α is fed into a decoder, the extrinsic information generated always has a fixed mean β no matter what data is used or what disturbances occur in the channel. In this case, the input/output relation of the decoder is assumed to be deterministic.

Extrinsic information transfer charts (EXIT) has been

introduced to study and predict the convergence proper-

provide much information for the code and the iterative

V. Convergence Types and Errors Recently, there have been attempts to discover and classify different convergence types and errors in iterative decoding [9], [10]. Different modes of iterative decoding have been identified and ways to enhance performance were explored. In our work, extensive simulations were conducted to confirm various forms of convergence and errors with the goal of distilling a clear understanding of iterative decoding. In each simulation, 50 iterations were run and at least 50 packet errors were collected. Based on these observations and the classifications offered in [9], [10], we will first determine the basic behavior of iterative decoding and then put these behavior into categories. MAP decoders are capable of producing parity bit likelihoods along with data bit likelihoods. Combining these two, an iterative decoder’s estimate of transmitted codeword can be obtained at any iteration by hard decision. The behavior of iterative decoding will be associated with the form this estimate takes. This estimate can converge or oscillate where its data estimate can be correct, incorrect with few bits or off by around 10% error rate (large number of errors). Moreover, this estimate may be a vector inside or outside the code space. The below table summarizes the basic behavior.
TABLE I
Categories of Iterative Decoders’ Behavior
1. Estimate converges to a codeword with few or no errors in data bits. 2. Estimate converges to a vector that is not in the code space (noncodeword) with a lot of erroneous data bits. 3. Estimate oscillates with a lot of errors in data bits. 4. Estimate converges to a noncodeword with few errors in data bits. 5. Estimate oscillates with a few errors in data bits.
If we had a decoder which would provide the ML solution, only category 1 would likely exist: there would, with high probability, be either a correct decoding or a decoding error with convergence to a codeword nearby the transmitted codeword causing small number of errors. So the rest four are most likely due to the suboptimality of iterative decoding as a solution to ML decoding. All the categories except for the 1st, which is the desired operation of the de-

coder, provides a noncodeword estimate. This finding will be made use as a stopping criterion in the next section. Note that cases 4 and 5 (also pathological cases presented in [9], [10]) rarely occur in practice and their existence does not significantly affect the results drawn in this and next sections. Thus, last two cases will be neglected for the rest of the paper.
Category 2 is stuck at a point close to origin in the EXIT chart, i.e., at a fixed point [6]. The likelihoods in case 3 oscillate indefinitely without continuous improvement nearby the origin. In Section IV the probability of decoding failure was defined and evaluated. This probability corresponds to categories 2 and 3 since these two cause undesired extrinsic information evolutions between two encoders. This implies that obtaining the likelihood of being in either of these categories is possible using the EXIT chart method with percentiles (and in case percentiles are not available, by the use of standard deviations.)
A type of error called residual errors occurring with iterative decoding is mentioned in [9]. Neglecting category 4, these errors are classified under category 1. This type of error corresponds to errors that would even exist with an ML decoder. It is well known that ML decoding error probability can be bounded using the distance spectrum of the code used. For turbo codes, the distance spectrum of a particular code can be found by using the distance spectra of the constituent codes under the uniform interleaving assumption [11]. Another bound can be found by obtaining the free distance with utilization of constrained subcodes [12]. Uniform interleaving method will be made use of in this paper.
We can now differentiate between two modes of erroneous decoding: due to ML decoding errors (in category 1) and decoding failure (rest of the categories). The former mode draws on small number of errors whereas the latter causes a large number of errors. This statement is in agreement with other researchers’ observations that iterative decoders produce either no error or a large number of errors in the low-medium SNR ranges. ML decoding errors fail with a codeword estimate whereas decoding failure cases provide noncodeword estimates.

VI. A Stopping Criterion Scheme
In this section, a new stopping criterion for the iterative decoding and the related retransmission scheme will be explained based on the results of Section V. In Section V, it was pointed out that different types of decoding errors can easily be distinguished by checking iterative decoder’s estimate of the transmitted codeword. This check can be performed by turbo-encoding the hard-decision estimate of the data bits and matching the outcome with the codeword estimate. If the estimate is a codeword, the error is due to ML decoding. On the other hand, when the estimate is not a codeword the error is due to decoding failure.
These observations naturally lead us to the idea that using this codeword criterion, i.e., whether the estimate is in the code space or not, the decoder can be stopped. In case the estimate is a codeword, ML solution is achieved and obvious results will follow. In the other case, either the convergence has not happened yet or the packet will never converge to ML solution and ultimately cause a decoding failure. Specifying a maximum allowable number of iterations, the following rule is acquired: • Stopping rule: Whenever estimate is a codeword, stop. Continue decoding as long as estimate is not a codeword up to a prespecified number of iterations.
This rule obviously allows ML decoding errors. ML decoding errors cause, with high probability, small number of erroneous data bits and correcting them might not be of interest in many settings although doing so is possible by

using an inner code [9], [13]. However, decoding failures
cause a large number of errors and asking a retransmission
makes more sense in this case. We propose the following
automatic repeat request (ARQ) scheme: In case the de-
coder doesn’t stop until a specified number of iterations,
request a retransmission of the packet. This scheme in-
sures that only ML decoding errors occur. This approach
simply uses the intrinsic properties of iterative decoding
and can be further refined using the ideas in [9], [10], [13].
VII. Conclusions
In this paper, we have developed a framework to study
the behavior of iterative decoding with turbo codes of small
and medium blocklengths. The input/output relation of
a MAP decoder is found to be probabilistic. This find-
ing helps obtain a decoding failure probability for iterative
decoding. The behavior of iterative decoding was catego-
rized and details of different behavior was explained. A
new stopping criterion and an ARQ scheme with the stop-
ping criterion was proposed. We are currently investigat-
ing ways to reason and model the probabilistic nature of
the MAP decoder and to find out more accurate decoding
failure probability measures for iterative decoders.
