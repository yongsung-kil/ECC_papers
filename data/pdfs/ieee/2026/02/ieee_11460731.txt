Abstract—This paper investigates Graph Neural Network (GNN)–based decoding of Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes for fifth- and sixth-generation (5G/6G) communication systems. Classical decoding algorithms, such as Belief Propagation (BP) and Min-Sum (MS), perform well under ideal conditions but degrade in the presence of channel fading, noise, and hardware nonlinearities. GNN-based decoders exploit the Tanner graph structure of LDPC codes, replacing fixed message-passing rules with trainable neural layers, including Multilayer Perceptrons (MLPs), Gated Recurrent Units (GRUs), and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks. Simulation results obtained using benchmark and 5G New Radio (NR) LDPC codes over Additive White Gaussian Noise (AWGN) and Rayleigh fading channels demonstrate that GNN decoders achieve improved Bit Error Rate (BER) and Block Error Rate (BLER) performance, mitigate error floor effects, and reduce decoding latency and computational complexity compared with BP, MS, and Normalized Min-Sum (NMS) decoders. Consequently, GNN-based LDPC decoding emerges as an adaptive and efficient solution for ultra-reliable lowlatency communication in next-generation wireless networks.
Keywords—Low-Density Parity-Check Codes, Neural Networks for Communications, Graph Neural Networks, 5G/6G
I. INTRODUCTION
The rapid evolution of telecommunication networks, particularly with the rollout of 5G and the forthcoming 6G standards [1], has imposed highly demanding requirements on data throughput, ultra-low latency, and transmission reliability [2]. While traditional decoding strategies such as BP [3] and MS [4] are effective under nominal conditions, they often struggle in challenging environments characterized by strong noise, nonlinear distortions, multipath fading, or Doppler shifts. These limitations highlight the need for intelligent, adaptive decoders capable of learning from channel conditions and dynamically optimizing their decision processes.
Artificial Intelligence (AI), particularly deep learning techniques, has emerged as a promising tool to address these challenges. Unlike classical deterministic methods, AI-driven decoders can approximate highly complex decoding functions, adapt to varying channel conditions without explicit redesign, and leverage contextual information from the communication environment. Among these approaches, GNNs stand out for their ability to operate on structured graph data [5, 6], which aligns naturally with the Tanner graph representation of LDPC codes [7] commonly employed in 5G systems. By learning optimized message-passing operations between variable and check nodes [4], GNN-based decoders can achieve superior accuracy and faster convergence compared to conventional iterative algorithms [8].

Beyond error-correction performance, GNN architectures [9] provide scalability and flexibility, making them suitable for both high-demand communication scenarios—such as ultra-reliable low-latency communication (URLLC) in autonomous vehicles, immersive Augmented Reality, Virtual Reality (AR/VR) streaming, tactile Internet applications, and industrial Internet of Things (IoT)—and for deployment in resource-constrained edge devices. By incorporating learnable node- and message-update functions through MLPs, GRUs, or LSTMs, these decoders can significantly reduce the number of iterations needed to reach a target BER while balancing computational complexity, latency, and energy efficiency.
The benefits of GNN-based LDPC decoders extend to multiple practical aspects:
• Enhanced BER/BLER performance at low Signal-toNoise Ratio (SNRs) compared to conventional BP.
• Reduced decoding latency, enabling sub-millisecond performance critical for URLLC applications.
• Adaptivity to dynamic channel conditions without algorithmic redesign.
• Energy-aware implementation, suitable for embedded or IoT devices.
Consequently, leveraging GNNs for LDPC decoding represents more than a mere replacement of conventional algorithms [10-13] — it marks a paradigm shift toward cognitive, adaptive receivers capable of self-optimization in real-world 5G/6G environments. This paper investigates the theoretical foundations and experimental evaluation of GNNbased LDPC decoders, emphasizing their advantages in terms of error-correction performance, computational efficiency, and applicability to emerging next-generation [14] use cases.
II. GRAPH NEURAL NETWORK APPROACHES TO LDPC DECODING
A. GNN-Based Formulation for LDPC Decoding
Graph Neural Networks represent a modern class of artificial neural network architectures designed to efficiently process data with an inherent graph structure. Since LDPC codes, widely adopted in 5G NR systems, can be naturally described through a Tanner graph consisting of variable nodes and check nodes [15, 16], GNNs are particularly well suited for decoding tasks. In a Tanner graph, edges connect variable nodes to their corresponding check constraints as defined by the parity-check matrix. This structure provides a natural mapping for employing GNN-based learning strategies.
As outlined in [10], conventional BP decoding relies on iterative message-passing between variable and check nodes.

III. EXPERIMENTAL EVALUATION OF GNN-BASED LDPC DECODERS AND COMPARATIVE PERFORMANCE ANALYSIS
To validate the effectiveness of the proposed GNN-based LDPC decoding framework, we conducted a series of simulations in MATLAB under standardized 5G/6G communication scenarios. The evaluation considers both benchmark LDPC codes and practical 5G NR codes. Specifically, we employed the 3GPP TS 38.212 base graphs (BG1 and BG2), as well as smaller reference codes such as (7,4), (15,11), and (648,324), to provide a balanced perspective between classical short block codes and modern long block lengths. Code rates of 0.5 and 0.75 were selected, as they represent typical configurations for 5G NR transport blocks.
In terms of modulation formats, the experiments span from QPSK to high-order constellations including 16QAM, 32QAM, 64QAM, 256QAM, and 1024QAM, in order to capture performance trends across a broad SNR operating range. Simulations were performed over both the AWGN channel and a Rayleigh fading channel, reflecting realistic transmission conditions where fading and multipath effects are significant.

The proposed GNN-based decoder leverages the natural Tanner graph structure of LDPC codes. Each variable and check node is represented as a vertex, with message-passing implemented through trainable neural layers. Node update functions are parameterized using MLPs, GRUs, or LSTM cells (as shown in (5)–(12)), allowing the model to capture temporal dependencies in iterative decoding. Key hyperparameters include a learning rate of 0.001 with the Adam optimizer [18], a batch size of 64 codewords, 200–500 training episodes per simulation, hidden dimensions of 64– 128 per node update function, sigmoid and tanh as activation functions, and a binary cross-entropy loss computed between transmitted and decoded codewords. This configuration enables a holistic comparison between classical and intelligent decoders, covering error-correction performance, convergence behavior, computational efficiency, and latency — all of which are critical metrics for next-generation 5G/6G communication systems [19].

IV. CONCLUSION
This paper studied GNN-based LDPC decoding as an intelligent alternative to BP and MS. Simulations over AWGN and Rayleigh channels show that the proposed framework consistently outperforms classical iterative methods in BER/BLER, convergence speed, and error floor reduction. The novelty lies in trainable message-passing functions aligned with Tanner graph structure, enabling adaptive decoding under diverse modulation formats and code rates. Compared with BP and MS, the GNN decoder achieves nearly 1 dB gain for 5G NR BG2 codes while reducing FLOP and inference latency by up to 60%, offering a favorable trade-off between performance and efficiency. These results highlight the potential of GNNs as scalable, energy-aware decoders for URLLC, AR/VR, and IoT. The presented approach provides a strong foundation for practical integration in advanced wireless systems. Future work may extend this approach to hybrid schemes with GNNs, DRL, or transformers, enhancing robustness and adaptability in dynamic 5G/6G scenarios. In particular, transformer-based architectures offer a promising alternative to recurrent units such as LSTM and GRU, thanks to their self-attention mechanism and ability to capture longrange dependencies without sequential processing. For LDPC decoding, attention-based message aggregation may enable more effective exploitation of global graph dependencies, especially for long block lengths and high-order modulations. However, the increased computational and memory requirements of Transformers imply that their practical deployment in real-time 5G/6G receivers requires careful architectural optimization, which is left for future work.
