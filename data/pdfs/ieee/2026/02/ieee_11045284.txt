BayesBB: A 9.6-Gb/s 1.61-ms Configurable All-Message-Passing Baseband-Accelerator for
B5G/6G Cell-Free Massive-MIMO Systems

Yi Zhang, Wenyue Zhou , Yiwei Zhang, Houren Ji , Student Member, IEEE, Yongming Huang , Fellow, IEEE, Xiaohu You , Fellow, IEEE, and Chuan Zhang , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—Cell-free massive multi-input multi-output (CF-mMIMO) has emerged as a promising alternative for the forthcoming beyond 5G and 6G (B5G/6G) systems. As an imperative part of supporting B5G/6G applications, baseband (BB) chips encounter more challenging key performance indicator (KPI) requirements. To achieve the B5G/6G goal of higher than 8-Gb/s/user throughput, less than 2-ms latency, and configurable hardware, a Bayesian inference-based all-messagepassing baseband-accelerator (BayesBB) is presented in this article. Benefiting from the pipelined and fully unfolded allmessage-passing architecture, BayesBB delivers three unexplored merits: high throughput (9.6-Gb/s), low latency (1.61-ms), and good configurability. Specifically, a ten-gigabit ethernet (XGE) interface with 10.3125-Gb/s SerDes is used, resulting in 9.6-Gb/s throughput. A fully unfolded MIMO-BP detector achieves a detection latency of 1.44-ms, contributing to chip latency of 1.61-ms. A configurable architecture is designed, supporting various B5G/6G applications, for example, the decoding of both 3GPP low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes and polar codes. As a system-level implementation, tests have verified that 16 arrays of BayesBB can handle the BB processing of a 128 × 128 CF-mMIMO system with commercial setups, delivering 9.6-Gb/s throughput and >100-b/s/Hz spectrum efficiency (SE).
Index Terms—Baseband (BB) accelerator, Bayes’ theorem, beyond 5G and 6G (B5G/6G), cell-free, massive-multi-input multi-output (mMIMO).

A. Challenge 1 (High Throughput)
With the rapid development of wireless communication, mobile data traffic grows with an explosive trend, resulting in higher and higher throughput requirements. From the original 1G to the current 5G, the throughput KPI is increased from Kb/s to Gb/s. The forthcoming 6G further improves the throughput requirements to support various vertical applications. However, achieving high throughput is demanding for BB chips, as it requires not only a well-designed architecture but also a high-speed interface to handle the anticipated increase in B5G/6G data traffic.
B. Challenge 2 (Low Latency)
Through 4G LTE to the current 5G, vertical application scenarios propose more and more demanding requirements for user plane latency. For latency-sensitive B5G/6G application scenarios, such as ultra-reliable low-latency communication (uRLLC), the user plane latency is required to achieve less than 2-ms. However, ensuring low latency is tricky for BB chips because it requires incorporating various algorithms into a single BB chip, all while meeting the stringent time requirements of B5G/6G applications.
C. Challenge 3 (Configurability)
B5G/6G involves many detailed application scenarios such as uncrewed aerial vehicle (UAV) communications [22], [23], vehicle-to-everything (V2X) communications [24], [25], and satellite communications [26]. Hence, B5G/6G expects that BB chips can be configurable to support these various application scenarios. However, facilitating exceptional configurability is difficult, as it necessitates a flexible architecture that can cater to multiple applications in the B5G/6G realm.

Facing the challenges above, this work presents a 19.093-mm2 BB accelerator IC, which delivers a throughput of 9.6-Gb/s and a latency of 1.61-ms. In contrast to reported BB ICs [19], [20], [21], this chip integrates a comprehensive BB system encompassing key components such as a highspeed interface, a channel estimator, an MIMO detector, and a versatile channel decoder, alongside other essential modules. In designing this chip, named Bayesian inference-based allmessage-passing baseband-accelerator (BayesBB), we have first adopted a message-passing approach based on Bayesian inference, to implement all the BB modules. BayesBB leverages several advantages, including jointly optimized performance, high parallelism, efficient inter-module communication, and excellent configurability. BayesBB embodies the following unexplored features to target the three challenges.
1) For Challenge 1, we implement a ten-gigabit ethernet (XGE) interface with 10.3125-Gb/s SerDes and follows the IEEE 802.3 standard, which is widely adopted due to its high speed, low latency, and robustness. In addition, a 200-MHz-matched preprocessor together with a channel estimator is also implemented. This design enables a throughput of 9.6-Gb/s, which is 7.0× of the state-ofthe-art BB chip [21].
2) For Challenge 2, we design an 8 × 4 16-QAM MIMO belief propagation (BP) detector using a fully unfolded architecture with five iterations. This design delivers a latency of 1.61-ms, resulting in a 2.5× reduction compared to the user plane latency (4-ms) required by 5G [4].
3) For Challenge 3, we design a configurable architecture to handle various applications of B5G/6G (e.g., decoding both 3GPP LDPC and polar codes). Furthermore, BayesBB’s scalability enables it to effortlessly fashion into a 128 × 128 CF-mMIMO system with commercial B5G/6G configurations, achieving more than 100-b/s/Hz SE, which is 6.7× higher than the 15-b/s/Hz SE required in 5G [2].

II. ALL-MESSAGE-PASSING BB RECEIVER

Fig. 2 reports the system diagram of the BB receiver with BayesBB. After passing the wireless channel, the received (RX) signal is buffered into the RF chain module. The preprocessor and the FFT module demodulate the RX OFDM symbol and deliver the data of resource elements (REs) to BayesBBs. As BB accelerators, BayesBBs are utilized to recover the transmitted (TX) data stream from the frequencydomain data, enabling applications such as UAV and 4K HD video. Each BayesBB involves BB modules such as channel estimation, MIMO detection, soft modulation, and channel decoding. Actually, the RX signal of each BB module can be formulated as a linear MIMO system model, expressed as follows:

y = Hx + n

(1)

where x represents the TX signal, H denotes the transformation matrix, and n is the system noise for this BB module.
To recover the TX data, conventional BB accelerators or processors often utilize separate algorithms for different BB modules. For instance, the MMSE and BP algorithms are applied for MIMO detection and channel decoding, respectively. However, there are two main shortcomings for conventional BB accelerators. On the one hand, it is difficult to find the global optimal solution when applying the separated algorithms for different BB modules, resulting in suboptimal performance. On the other hand, the separated BB algorithms have divergent characteristics, leading to low hardware efficiency. For instance, it is intractable to design a jointly optimized unified architecture for both the MMSE detection and the BP decoding. To this end, the all-messagepassing algorithm based on factor graphs is utilized for our BayesBB, as shown in Fig. 3. Specifically, there are two types of nodes in factor graphs of each BB module: function node (FN) and variable node (VN). III. BAYESBB ACCELERATOR ARCHITECTURE
Fig. 6 shows the overall architecture of BayesBB. Faced with the aforementioned three challenges, the proposed BayesBB architecture primarily includes three unexplored

A. Challenge 1 (High Throughput)
To achieve the high-throughput requirements, BayesBB employs a high-speed XGE interface and pipeline-designed architectures involving an Ethernet parser, a preprocessor, and a channel estimator. In particular, the XGE interface integrates a 10.3125-Gb/s SerDes, a physical coding sublayer (PCS), and a media access control (MAC), which follows the IEEE 802.3 standard. After receiving the Ethernet packages, the payload data is drawn by the Ethernet parser first. Then, the preprocessor will filter the RX data by performing phase compensation and data descrambling. The channel estimator buffers the RX data and utilizes the Gaussian approximation message passing (GAMP) algorithm to evaluate the channel matrix. The Ethernet parser, the preprocessor, and the channel estimator all utilize a pipeline-designed architecture, benefiting from which maximum data throughput is achieved.
B. Challenge 2 (Low Latency)
To meet the low-latency requirements, BayesBB implements a fully unfolded architecture for the MIMO-BP detector with five iterations. This unfolded architecture enables pipelined data processing for each RE, thus resulting in a low detection latency. More specifically, the MIMO detector first converts the RX data from the complex domain to the real domain. Then, the posterior and a prior messages are iteratively updated

IV. DESIGNS FOR HIGH-THROUGHPUT ARCHITECTURE As highlighted in Challenge 1, for the forthcoming B5G/6G systems, designing a high-throughput architecture is crucial. In

this section, the high-throughput architecture of BayesBB is introduced, involving a high-speed Ethernet interface, a highthroughput pre-processor, and a pipeline-designed channel estimator. More details are introduced as follows.
Fig. 7 shows the high-speed interface of BayesBB, mainly comprised of three modules: SerDes, PCS, and MAC. The SerDes involve both analog and digital components to support high-speed serial communication. The physical medium attachment (PMA) serves as a key part of the SerDes, including SERializer/DESerializer, clock data recovery (CDR), and decision feedback equalizer (DFE) to ensure the high quality of TX/RX signal. The PCS facilitates data transmission between the SerDes and MAC, handling functions such as 64/66B encoding/decoding, scrambling/descrambling, pseudorandom sequence testing, and data synchronization link monitoring. When the predetermined bit error rate (BER) threshold is achieved, the RX data will be descrambled and decoded. The elastic FIFO stores the decoded results and compensates for minor frequency differences between the internal clocks of the two transmission devices. The MAC module manages data forwarding control, framing, and verification functions, communicating with the PCS via the 10-Gb media independent interface (XGMII). After receiving data, the MAC removes the preamble, the start frame delimiter, and the frame

The architectures of the Ethernet frame parser and the high-throughput preprocessor are detailed in Fig. 8. The Ethernet frame parser draws data information, for example, OFDM number, resource block (RB) number, and payload data with a fixed time expense of 158 clock cycles (CCs) for each Ethernet frame. The pre-processing module performs rate matching, phase compensation, and descrambling to ensure the stability and accuracy of end-to-end data transmission. Specifically, the MAC clock frequency is fixed at 156.25-MHz according to the IEEE 802.3 standard for 10Gb/s Ethernet. Therefore, there are 64-bit data processed by the MAC module for each CC. Since the RE data RX in each antenna is quantized with 48 bits, our BayesBB is designed to handle 48 bits per CC, resulting in a maximum system clock frequency of 10000/48 ≈ 208.33-MHz. Considering the trade-off between throughput and power, the system clock frequency of BayesBB is set as 200-MHz. Therefore, the clock frequency is transformed from 156.25- to 200-MHz here. In particular, the 156.25- and 200-MHz clocks are generated from two external crystal oscillators, providing differential clock signals. An on-chip clock module provided by the fabrication (FAB) vendor converts the different clocks into single-ended clocks, which are then used as the internal clocks for the MAC and system. In the module Input Buffer A, the bit-width of the parsed data will be converted from 64- to 48-bit, aligning with the rate-matching specifications. The control logic then reads the configuration vectors stored in RAMs and performs phase compensation and descrambling. Finally, the pilot signal is sent from the preprocessor to the channel estimator. BayesBB supports configuring the phase compensation value and scrambling vector, rendering it applicable for handling BB signal processing of different user equipment (UE).
Fig. 9 represents the architecture of the channel estimator, which involves three sub-modules: 1) input buffer B; 2) GAMP; and 3) output buffer. More specifically, the inputs of the GAMP module are the RE data stored in Input Buffer B, which can be categorized into two types: 1) “empty RE”

for noise computing and 2) demodulation reference signal (DMRS) RE for channel estimation. The empty RE is filled with all zeros at the TX side, which thus can be utilized to compute system noise. The DMRS RE carries pilots at the TX side, with which the GAMP algorithm [28] is adopted to estimate the channel matrix. It is noted that the GAMP channel estimator computes the channel coefficients in an antenna-byantenna data stream, whereas the forthcoming MIMO detector performs signal detection for the data stream per RE. Therefore, a data dimensional conversion is necessary here, which is achieved by the registers in the output buffer. Finally, for each RE, an 8×4 channel matrix is available after the channel estimation, and four consecutive REs share the same estimated channel matrix for MIMO detection.

VI. DESIGN FOR CONFIGURABLE ARCHITECTURE
To address the configurability issue mentioned in Challenge 3, BayesBB needs to provide satisfactory performance under diverse communication scenarios. Channel coding is essential for capacity-achieving transmission. LDPC codes and polar codes have been standardized by the 5G NR standard owing to their distinct advantages. To this end, BayesBB implements a

A. Configurable Architecture
To accommodate various data formats such as different data streams, encoding block lengths, and rates, it is crucial to ensure alignment between decoding block selection and sub-band data. The design of the interface for the channel decoding front-end is particularly important. Fig. 15 illustrates the format of the front-end LLR data stream and the corresponding FIFO design, which serves various functions including LLR data allocation for each RB, decoding rate matching, and decoding block data caching. To enhance the overall throughput and configurability of the decoding process, an LLR FIFO Stream with 32 FIFO arrays is designed for parallel data buffering. Each FIFO has a width of 10Q bit and a depth of 512 elements. The preceding MIMO detector outputs 2Q bit LLRs to the decoder every CC. After concatenating data from five cycles based on RB numbers, they are indexed to the corresponding FIFO for data buffering. By strategically caching the post-MIMO detection data across multiple FIFOs, we can align the output completion rate with various decoding configurations and data throughput requirements, thereby optimizing the decoding process.
In both decoding modes, the LLR FIFO stream module facilitates the provision of data from various RBs, enabling parallel input to the rate-matching module and data allocation. Specifically, when the FIFO data outputs 10Q bit LLRs per CC, the data is segmented based on the lifting value Z in the LDPC codeword construction during LDPC mode. Consider an example with an information bit length K = 1500 and a rate R = 0.5, corresponding to base matrix BG2 and Z = 80. The rate-matching process is depicted in Fig. 16. Once rate matching is completed, the data is divided into blocks of 80Q and stored in the LLR RAM. In contrast, polar code mode leverages the simple graph structure of polar codes, obviating the need for data segmentation. Consequently, the corresponding information can be directly stored in the LLR RAM without further segmentation.

D. Result Analysis
Table I demonstrates the key performance merits of BayesBB compared with prior art detectors (top) and decoders (bottom). Thanks to the soft output and fully unfolded features, the detector in BayesBB achieves a 7.0× higher throughput over [21] at the expense of a 41.3% reduction in AE. Compared to larger-scale MIMO detector ICs in [8] and [11], the proposed fully unfolded detector also achieves 5.3× and 3.5× throughput, respectively. The decoders demonstrate comprehensive superiority in both LDPC and polar decoding modes (bottom). The LDPC decoder boosts throughput by 3.2× with squeezing 41.1% energy and 34.1% area over [21]. The polar decoder improves both area and EE by an order of magnitude compared to previous work [19], [20], [21]. In LDPC and polar decoding modes, the decoders outperform the prior art [21] in terms of AE by 9.4× and 3.4× and EE by 10.9× and 7.8×, respectively. Table II summarizes the main highlights of BayesBB. BayesBB is the first all-message-passing full-BB-processing chip for the B5G/6G CF-mMIMO system. The full-BBprocessing capabilities of BayesBB are demonstrated through its extensive core features, excellent configurability and scalability, and abundant system interface resources. These characteristics ensure that BayesBB can perform full-chain BB data processing with high throughput, low latency, and flexible configurability. BayesBB adheres to communication protocols such as IEEE 802.3 standard, 5G LDPC/polar decoding, and 5G NR CRC check. The configurability of BayesBB lies in six main aspects: preprocessing unit, channel decoder, rate matching, CRC check, Ethernet interface, and system control. More specifically, several parameters such as the scrambling/phase-compensation vectors, the decoding modes, the maximum number of iterations, and so on, can be configured to support various scenarios, for example, 4K ultra-HD video streaming, UAV applications, and autonomous driving. Moreover, a scaled-up array of 16 BayesBB units can accommodate up to 128 × 128 antennas in actual system testing.

VIII. CONCLUSION
A 19.093-mm2 system-level all-message-passing configurable BB chip called BayesBB is proposed, delivering 9.6-Gb/s throughput and 1.61-ms latency. Three unexplored features: high-throughput pipeline-designed interface, lowlatency fully-unfolded MIMO detector, and multi-application configurable architecture are implemented to achieve the B5G/6G expectations. Results tested in a scalable B5G/6G 128 × 128 CF-mMIMO platform show that BayesBB can handle the BB signal processing of 4K HD video transmission. Compared with the reported BB ICs, BayesBB achieves up to 24.2× throughput for detection and 9.4× AE for decoding. Future research endeavors will focus on deepening the integration and enhancing the computational efficiency of this Bayesian framework. We plan to investigate its applications in emerging domains, particularly AI acceleration technologies and advanced digital signal processing implementations, while exploring its potential in edge computing scenarios and realtime system optimizations.
