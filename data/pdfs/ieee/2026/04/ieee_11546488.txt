Abstract—Quantum key distribution (QKD) is a secure communication technique that uses quantum mechanical principles. This method enables two trusted users to exchange a secret key or message securely by exploiting the fundamental properties of quantum mechanics. Strong long-distance secure communication is provided by QKD but its effectiveness is mainly limited by quantum bit error rate (QBER), finite-key effects, and the effectiveness of classical error-correction during information reconciliation. In this work, we proposes a neural-aided adaptive reconciliation (NAAR) framework based on artificial neural networks for QKD that integrates a lightweight neural belief propagation decoder with QBER-driven adaptive LDPC coderate selection. By lowering frame-error rate and information leakage, neural-assisted log-likelihood ratio calibration enhances belief-propagation convergence for binary symmetric channels. The result shows an improvement in secret key rates (SKR) and lower error rates over traditional LDPC-based reconciliation.
Index Terms—Quantum Key Distribution (QKD), Secret Key Rate, NAAR, BB84, Reconciliation,Neural Network
I. INTRODUCTION
Wireless communication has evolved from kilobit-persecond to gigabit-per-second data rates, with future systems targeting even higher capacities. However, Shannon’s theorem fundamentally limits achievable channel capacity under given bandwidth and noise conditions. Beyond capacity constraints, emerging quantum computing technologies threaten the security of classical cryptographic schemes that rely on computational hardness. In particular, Shor’s algorithm can efficiently break the Rivest–Shamir–Adleman (RSA) cryptosystem, exposing the long-term vulnerability of complexitybased encryption mechanisms [1].
Quantum Key Distribution (QKD) provides informationtheoretic security by exploiting quantum principles such as the no-cloning theorem and measurement disturbance. In a typical

QKD system, Alice encodes key information into quantum states and transmits them to Bob over an insecure quantum channel. After measurement, classical post-processing over an authenticated channel enables eavesdropping detection and the generation of a shared secret key [2]. The resulting key can be used in symmetric encryption schemes such as the one-time pad.
QKD protocols are broadly classified into discrete-variable (DV-QKD) and continuous-variable (CV-QKD) systems. DVQKD protocols, including BB84 and decoy-state BB84, generate correlated but imperfect binary keys due to channel noise and device imperfections. The reconciliation stage corrects mismatches between Alice’s and Bob’s sifted keys while minimizing information leakage, directly impacting the secret key rate (SKR) and achievable distance. Modern DVQKD systems predominantly employ Low-Density ParityCheck (LDPC) codes to approach the Shannon limit of the binary symmetric channel [1], [2].
In a QKD system, the secret key rate (SKR) denotes the rate at which two legitimate users, Alice and Bob, generate secure, error-corrected, and privacy-amplified bits per unit time, accounting for channel loss, excess noise, reconciliation efficiency, and information potentially accessible to an eavesdropper (Eve). SKR can be improved by increasing modulation variance for better signal distinguishability, reducing excess noise through low-noise sources and efficient homodyne/heterodyne detection, enhancing channel stabilization, and employing high-efficiency error-correction codes such as multi-dimensional LDPC schemes [3].

Motivated by the limitations of existing neural-assisted LDPC and threshold-based adaptive reconciliation schemes, this work proposes a novel reconciliation framework that jointly optimizes LDPC rate selection and neural-enhanced decoding to improve SKR. The proposed Neural-Aided Adaptive Reconciliation (NAAR) scheme integrates a lightweight multi-layer neural network for real-time LDPC rate prediction with a neural belief propagation (NBP) decoder that refines LLRs prior to conventional BP iterations, improving convergence and reducing BER. Unlike prior methods that address decoding and rate adaptation separately, a QBERdriven dynamic code-switching mechanism enables continuous rate optimization based on instantaneous channel conditions and decoder feedback, thereby reducing key leakage and enhancing reconciliation efficiency.

II. SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE
Fig. 1 illustrates the proposed neural-aided adaptive hybrid reconciliation framework, consisting of five blocks: codeswitching module, DV-QKD channel, LDPC encoder, BP decoder, and neural-adaptive reconciliation unit.
A. System Overview
The system first estimates channel SNR from QBER and detection statistics. Based on this estimate, a dynamic codeswitching module selects the LDPC rate: lower rates for highnoise conditions and higher rates for favorable channels to balance redundancy and information leakage.
The LDPC encoder generates syndrome bits from Alice’s sifted key, which are transmitted over the authenticated classical channel for secure reconciliation. The DV-QKD link

is modeled as a Binary Symmetric Channel (BSC). At the receiver, measurement outcomes are converted into refined log-likelihood ratios (LLRs) to enable reliable soft-decision decoding. A belief propagation (BP) decoder iteratively processes the LLRs and received syndrome over the LDPC Tanner graph until convergence or a maximum iteration limit. Information leakage is accounted for in secret key rate estimation. Finally, the neural-adaptive reconciliation unit monitors decoding performance and channel variations, dynamically adjusting decoding parameters or redundancy when required. The reconciled key is then subjected to privacy amplification to obtain the final secure secret key.

III. METHODOLOGY:DISCRETE-VARIABLE QKD PROTOCOL MODEL
Secure key generation follows the BB84 protocol, where Alice encodes random bits into non-orthogonal quantum states prepared in two mutually unbiased bases and transmits them to Bob over a quantum channel. Bob measures using randomly selected bases, followed by basis sifting and parameter estimation over an authenticated classical channel to obtain the sifted key and estimate the quantum bit error rate (QBER). Practical imperfections such as detector inefficiency, channel loss, and noise are incorporated through QBER estimation and finite-key analysis. Final security is ensured via information reconciliation and privacy amplification.

E. Optimization Criteria
The neural-aided adaptive hybrid reconciliation framework uses the Adam optimizer to train a neural network for channel reliability estimation and LLR refinement by minimizing prediction loss between estimated and true reliability metrics. Adam optimizer is chosen for its robustness to noisy, nonstationary gradients in quantum channels and its fast, stable convergence with limited data. Its adaptive learning-rate mechanism enables reliable training without extensive tuning,

producing more accurate soft information, reducing LDPC

decoding failures, and ultimately improving reconciliation

efficiency and secret key rate (SKR) in the DV-QKD system.

In this work we are presenting a sub-optimal reconciliation under fluctuating channel conditions. Normally QKD shall do the reconciliation with LDPC codes but the quality of reconciliation affects the performance matrices such as secret key rate (SKR) and the efficient key extraction even under low SNR conditions. Normally the channel SNR changes unpredictably and the system used fixed LDPC codes and the chosen code rate does not match with instantaneous channel conditions, But in our proposed model with the NAAR architecture, leverages machine learning for code rate adaptation and soft decision decoding using Neural BP, ensuring robust and efficient key extraction even under low SNR conditions.

B. Results and Discussions
Figure 2 illustrates the simulated relationship between the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) and the achievable Secret Key Rate (SKR) for two reconciliation strategies: Conventional LDPC-based error correction and the proposed Neural-Aided Adaptive Hybrid Reconciliation (NAAR) framework. The results clearly demonstrate that the NAAR method consistently outperforms the traditional LDPC approach across the entire SNR range from 0 to 20 dB. At low-SNR conditions (0–5 dB), both schemes exhibit reduced SKR due to the high quantum bit error rate, but NAAR still shows a noticeable advantage. This improvement arises from its neural predictor, which estimates channel noise more accurately and facilitates the selection of an LDPC code with an appropriate rate for harsh channel conditions. As a result, the system avoids excessive syndrome leakage and improves reconciliation efficiency even when operating near the noise threshold.
Figure 3 presents the variation of Block Error Rate (BLER) with respect to the number of LDPC decoding iterations for both the conventional LDPC algorithm and the proposed

Neural-Aided Adaptive Hybrid Reconciliation (NAAR) framework. As expected, BLER decreases for both schemes as the number of decoding iterations increases, demonstrating the iterative nature of belief-propagation decoding. However, a clear and consistent performance gap emerges across all iteration counts, with the NAAR framework exhibiting significantly lower BLER than the classical LDPC decoder. This improvement is particularly prominent at higher iteration levels, indicating that the neural-aided BER prediction and adaptive code-rate selection allow the LDPC decoder to operate closer to its optimal convergence conditions.
Figure 4 illustrates the impact of reinforcement-learningbased LDPC code selection on the Secret Key Rate (SKR) performance of the reconciliation process across varying Quantum Bit Error Rate (QBER) values. The baseline LDPC curve represents a fixed-rate code traditionally used in classical QKD post-processing, whereas the RL-optimized curve corresponds to the dynamic LDPC selection strategy employed in the NAAR framework. Across the entire QBER range from 0.01 to 0.12, the optimized LDPC consistently delivers higher SKR, demonstrating the effectiveness of reinforcement learning in adapting code parameters to the instantaneous error characteristics of the quantum channel. Figure 5 presents the variation of Block Error Rate (BLER) with respect to increasing Quantum Bit Error Rate (QBER) for both the baseline LDPC reconciliation scheme and the proposed optimized LDPC configuration used in the NAAR framework. As anticipated, BLER increases for both methods as QBER rises, since higher physical-layer noise directly increases the probability of decoding failures during information reconciliation. C. Comparison of Secret Key Rate (SKR) Performance of the proposed model with Existing Works
Table I compares the proposed Neural-Aided Adaptive Reconciliation (NAAAR) scheme with conventional and literaturereported reconciliation techniques for CV-QKD and DV-QKD systems. The evaluation considers reconciliation type, QBER operating range, normalized SKR, and qualitative advantages.
The proposed NAAAR framework, integrating adaptive LDPC, belief propagation (BP), and neural-assisted softinformation refinement, achieves a normalized SKR of approximately 0.85 at QBER < 2%, outperforming conventional fixed-rate LDPC methods that typically achieve 0.48–0.75 depending on channel conditions. At moderate QBER levels (5–8%), the proposed method maintains SKR values of 0.62 and 0.51, respectively, demonstrating enhanced noise robustness. In contrast, fixed-rate LDPC schemes exhibit faster SKR degradation due to the lack of adaptive redundancy and intelligent soft-information processing. For reference, the ideal reconciliation bound based on the Bennett–Brassard assumption (approximately 0.78) is also included. The proposed NAAAR approach closely approaches this theoretical limit while remaining practically implementable. Overall, the results confirm that combining adaptive coding with neural-aided decoding significantly improves SKR stability and reconciliation efficiency compared to classical LDPC-based methods. Table II presents a comparative analysis of AI-assisted LDPC reconciliation methods at different QBER levels (2%, 5%, 8%), highlighting decoder type, AI strategy, and achieved SKR. The proposed NAAR scheme achieves the highest SKR across all operating points (0.85, 0.65, 0.52), demonstrating superior adaptability and decoding efficiency. The gain results from neural-assisted soft-information refinement, adaptive code-rate control, and dynamic LLR optimization. Existing AI-based approaches, including neural BP decoding, regression-based LDPC tuning, and fixed-rate LDPC with predictive adjustments, provide moderate improvements over conventional LDPC. However, most methods either enhance decoding convergence without code-rate adaptation or perform offline parameter optimization without real-time QBER awareness. Consequently, their SKR degrades at higher QBER, where adaptive redundancy is essential. Overall, the results show that integrating neural assistance with adaptive coding inside the reconciliation loop enables improved SKR stability under varying channel conditions.
