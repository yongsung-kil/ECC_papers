HFMLLR: Heterogeneous Feature Mining for Low-Overhead Latency Reduction Scheme of LDPC Codes in 3-D TLC NAND Flash Memory

Yongchao Wang , Debao Wei , Member, IEEE, Dixin Ma, Huqi Xiang , and Liyan Qiao , Member, IEEE

Abstract—Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have been widely adopted in 3-D NAND flash memory systems to enhance data storage reliability. However, when the raw bit error rate (RBER) during data read operations is high, the LDPC decoding process often incurs significant iterative latency, which severely impacts system performance. To address this challenge, this article introduces a novel heterogeneous feature mining for lowoverhead latency reduction (HFMLLR) scheme. This scheme reconstructs data based on the distinct characteristics of LDPC codes and flash memory and then further leverages log-likelihood ratio (LLR) and interframe error properties to achieve an organic integration of these features. In detail, this scheme first systematically analyzes the divergent impact of information bits versus check bits on reliability within LDPC codes. By leveraging the inherent characteristic disparity between pages in 3-D TLC NAND flash, it proposes a disparity characteristic-based data reconstruction (DCDR) scheme. This approach naturally creates a reliability gradient within data frames without adding extra storage overhead. Furthermore, this article explores the mechanism by which the LLR influences decoding convergence. By taking advantage of the spatial correlation between multiframe errors within the same flash page, the HFMLLR scheme optimizes the decoding process, significantly reducing the number of iterations with minimal computational and storage overhead. Experimental results demonstrate that, compared to traditional schemes, the HFMLLR scheme achieves up to an 83.4% reduction in decoding iteration latency, highlighting its remarkable latency reduction capabilities and practical value.
Index Terms—Heterogeneous feature, iteration latency, loglikelihood ratio (LLR) values, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, triple-level cell (TLC) NAND flash memory.

This article aims to reduce the decoding latency of LDPC codes in 3-D TLC NAND flash memory. To minimize computational and storage overhead, the disparity between information bits and check bits within a frame of LDPC encoded data was first analyzed. Subsequently, the variations among page characteristics in 3-D TLC NAND flash memory were explored, leading to the proposal of a disparity characteristic-based data reconstruction (DCDR) scheme. This approach naturally generates reliable distinctions between information bits and check bits within a frame, without requiring auxiliary information, ensuring reliable LLR optimization. Subsequently, the influence of varying LLR values on decoding performance and the error characteristics between frames within each NAND flash page are explored, leading to the development of the heterogeneous feature mining for low-overhead latency reduction (HFMLLR) scheme. Building upon this foundation, it further explores LLR and interframe error characteristics, thereby enabling the organic integration of these various features. Since the proposed scheme reconstructs data via a single wordline, it is not suitable for operations involving the reading of only a single page. Instead, it is better adapted to scenarios that require continuous access to large volumes of data. Actual experimental results validate the advanced nature of the proposed scheme, demonstrating a significant reduction in decoding iteration latency up to 83.4%, with almost no computational or storage overhead.
The main contributions made in this article are as follows.
1) For the first time, the intrinsic disparity in reliability impact between information bits and check bits in LDPC codes is systematically revealed. By integrating the physical characteristic variations among pages within 3-D TLC NAND flash memory, the deep correlation

By incorporating the spatial correlation of multi-frame errors within the same flash page, the design space for decoding optimization is significantly expanded. 4) Building upon the DCDR scheme, this article proposed an HFMLLR decoding enhancement scheme with significant performance breakthroughs. Experimental validation confirms that the proposed scheme reduces decoding iteration latency by up to 83.4% while maintaining nearly identical computational and storage overhead, showcasing substantial potential for practical deployment and engineering adoption.

This architecture primarily relies on two distinct types of memory cells: the floating-gate (FG) type [23] and the charge-trap (CT) type [24], both of which store data by modulating the electrical charge within the cell. Fig. 1 depicts the architecture of 3-D TLC NAND flash memory, revealing a complex hierarchical structure. Each flash chip consists of numerous blocks, which represent the smallest units for erase operations. Within each block, multiple wordlines are integrated, and read or program operations are performed at the wordline level. In TLC NAND flash, every wordline accommodates three distinct page types: LSB, CSB, and MSB. The reliability of NAND flash memory is dynamic and deteriorates with increasing program/erase (P/E) cycles and retention time, directly leading to reduced reliability of read data [29]. To mitigate this, high-performance LDPC codes are widely adopted in the industry to enhance data reliability. However, the degraded reliability of flash memory necessitates more LDPC iterative decoding, which in turn increases decoding latency and prolongs overall read latency. Consequently, an in-depth exploration of the inherent characteristics of LDPC codes is essential for developing strategies to reduce read latency in NAND flash memory systems.
B. LDPC in 3-D NAND Flash Memory
LDPC codes, recognized for their capacity to approach the Shannon limit [9], have emerged as a pivotal technology for ensuring data reliability in high-density NAND flash memory, owing to their superior error correction capability and decoding flexibility. Their efficacy stems from the use of a sparse parity-check matrix, which enables robust performance even under long-code conditions [30]. For encoding, quasi-cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes are particularly advantageous; their structured regularity permits implementation via simple shift registers, resulting in low-complexity, high-speed hardware encoding [31]. This characteristic makes them well-suited for NAND flash controllers, which impose strict requirements on real-time operation and power efficiency. Accordingly, this article adopts QC-LDPC for the encoding process. On the

decoding side, a simplified min-sum algorithm, derived from belief propagation (BP) [32], is widely employed for its ability to markedly reduce computational complexity while preserving decoding reliability. The min-sum algorithm is therefore utilized for decoding in this work.
Fig. 2 depicts the application process of LDPC codes within TLC NAND flash memory. Upon acquisition of data designated for storage, the information first undergoes LDPC encoding. The resulting encoded sequence comprises both original information bits and algorithmically generated check bits. Information bits carry the substantive information to be preserved, whereas check bits provide redundant information essential for subsequent error detection and correction. The code rate, defined as the ratio of information bits to the total number of transmitted bits, represents a critical tradeoff: a lower code rate introduces more check bits, thereby enhancing error correction capability and decoding robustness at the cost of increased storage overhead [33]. Consequently, selecting an appropriate code rate tailored to specific application requirements and reliability constraints is imperative.
Following encoding, the data are assembled into frames, each containing a systematic arrangement of information bits and their associated check bits. The frames are sequentially allocated to the LSB, CSB, and MSB pages. Since the data for each page can only be determined after the allocation is complete, the order of allocation does not impact the performance of the proposed scheme. Depending on the configured page size, each page accommodates a predetermined number of such frames. The data contained in each page are then programmed into the memory chip. During data retrieval operations, the stored information is readback from the physical cells. Owing to various disturbance mechanisms inherent to NAND flash technology, the raw data read often contains a nonnegligible amount of erroneous bits. These retrieved data are then processed by the LDPC decoder, which utilizes the embedded check bits to detect and correct the errors, ultimately recovering the original information. This sequence of operations completes the application process of LDPC codes in TLC NAND flash memory systems.

C. Related Work and Research Motivation
The superior error-correction capability of LDPC codes significantly enhances the reliability of NAND flash memory, albeit at the expense of increased read latency. Strategies aimed at mitigating LDPC-induced read latency can be broadly classified into three categories: first, reducing the RBER of read data [34], [35] [36]; second, optimizing the decoding process through auxiliary information [18], [22] [37]; and third, refining read voltage placement strategies to minimize sensing overhead [20], [38] [39].
Reducing the RBER of the sensed data serves as a fundamental method for read latency mitigation [40]. By improving the accuracy of the read data, this approach enhances the reliability of the input to the LDPC decoder, thereby reducing the number of iterations required for successful decoding and shortening overall latency [36]. For instance, Li et al. [41] establish a more accurate threshold voltage distribution model and leverage variations in RBER across metadata pages within the same program state as feedback to estimate the optimal read reference voltage. While this method effectively lowers the RBER and thus decreases read latency, it inevitably introduces considerable storage and computational overhead.
The second strategy for latency reduction leverages auxiliary information to optimize the decoding process. By incorporating external data as prior information [42], this method enhances the accuracy of LLR estimates without modifying the underlying data read operations. Improved LLR precision facilitates faster convergence during iterative decoding [18], thereby reducing the total number of iterations and ultimately lowering read latency. As an example, Sun et al. [26] exploit correlations between data stored in different pages of the same wordline to refine the initial LLR values supplied to the decoder. While this approach effectively curtails decoding iterations, it relies on the availability of external data, inevitably introducing additional storage overhead.
A third category of methods focuses on read voltage placement optimization [43], a technique particularly suitable for the later stages of flash memory lifespan. Upon occurrence of a read failure, these strategies dynamically adjust the positioning of read reference voltages to improve data sensing accuracy without increasing the number of read operations [39]. This enables more reliable data retrieval with the same sensing budget, thereby reducing read latency. For instance, Wang et al. [38] employ entropy as a metric to quantify the nonuniformity of error distributions across different threshold voltage states and introduce an overlap region sensing voltage placement strategy. Although this method effectively mitigates read latency, it necessitates the storage of overlap region characteristics and is not applicable with hard decision decoding procedures typically employed during the initial lifetime phase of flash memory.
Based on the above analysis, it is clear that current strategies for reducing LDPC code read latency in NAND flash memory can be grouped into three primary approaches. While each approach helps mitigate read latency to some extent, they all introduce significant storage and computational overhead. Additionally, strategies that focus solely on optimizing read voltage placement cannot be applied consistently throughout

the entire lifespan of flash memory. Therefore, this work seeks to explore a latency reduction strategy that remains effective across the entire lifespan of flash memory, with minimal additional overhead.
Furthermore, most existing research on reducing LDPC code read latency in NAND flash memory has primarily focused on the inherent characteristics of the flash memory itself, without fully leveraging the interdependent properties that emerge when LDPC codes are integrated with NAND flash. As discussed earlier, TLC NAND flash memory consists of three distinct page types, MSB, CSB, and LSB, each with its own unique characteristics. In addition, LDPC-encoded data are composed of two distinct types: information bits and check bits. Therefore, we propose investigating the heterogeneous characteristics between LDPC-encoded data and TLC NAND flash in order to develop an optimization strategy that minimizes overhead while significantly reducing read latency.
III. DESIGN AND IMPLEMENTATION OF THE DCDR SCHEME
This section first examines the divergent characteristics between information bits and check bits in LDPC codes. It then explores the divergent characteristics among pages within 3-D TLC NAND flash memory. Finally, by strategically leveraging these divergent characteristics, the DCDR scheme is introduced.
A. Disparity Characteristics of LDPC Encoded Data
LDPC codes are widely used in NAND flash memory to improve its reliability. As shown in Fig. 2, data encoded with LDPC consist of two types: information bits and check bits. Both information bits and check bits are transmitted together over the channel and stored within the flash memory. Noise interference in both wireless channels and flash memory chips can lead to data corruption. These errors occur randomly, meaning that information bits and check bits have approximately equal probabilities of being corrupted. For decoding, both types of bits must be involved in the iterative verification process of the parity-check matrix. Therefore, it is essential to investigate the differing impacts of information bits and check bits on decoding performance.
In practical experiments, errors are randomly distributed, making it impossible to precisely control the number of errors in either the information bits or the check bits within a frame. In contrast, simulations allow for exact control over the error counts in both types of bits. These error counts were randomly chosen within the decoding threshold of the LDPC code, thereby ensuring that the experimental results remain consistent across any error count within this range. C. Design of DCDR Scheme
The information bits and check bits in LDPC codes exhibit distinct characteristics, with check bits having a more significant impact on decoding performance. Similarly, in 3-D TLC NAND flash memory, the LSB, CSB, and MSB pages also show differing characteristics, with the CSB page offering the highest reliability. Based on these observations, we propose an innovative approach: storing the check bits, which have a greater impact on decoding performance, within the CSB page of the same wordline. This approach is expected to reduce decoding iteration latency.
Fig. 5 presents the overall flowchart of the proposed DCDR scheme. In the diagram, IB represents information bits and CB represents check bits. L1 denotes the first frame of data from the LSB page, while C1 and M1 correspond to the first frames of data from the CSB and MSB pages, respectively. LSB, CSB, and MSB refer to the data stored after LDPC encoding, whereas LSB,’ CSB,’ and MSB’ represent the results after data reconstruction. The reconstruction process is nonsequential, allowing individual pages to be rebuilt concurrently. Moreover, as it involves no computation, the process incurs no additional latency penalty. IV. DESIGN AND IMPLEMENTATION OF THE HFMLLR SCHEME
This section begins by analyzing the influence of LLR values on decoding performance, followed by an exploration of frame error characteristics. Finally, the HFMLLR scheme is

A. Influence of the LLR Values
For LDPC decoding in NAND flash memory, one effective way to reduce decoding latency is to improve the accuracy of the input LLR. Traditional methods to enhance LLR accuracy often rely on external auxiliary information, resulting in substantial storage and computational overhead. The LLR optimization scheme proposed in this article, however, does not depend on external data. By cleverly reconstructing the information and check bits, it inherently creates a reliability distinction between these two bit types within a frame. As a result, different LLR values can be assigned to them without requiring any external information.
LLR quantifies data reliability, and its accuracy directly influences both decoding success and efficiency. Therefore, understanding the effect of LLR values on decoding performance is crucial. B. Mining the Frame Error Characteristics
In 3-D TLC NAND flash memory, multiple data frames are stored within each page. Due to the random nature of error distribution, the reliability of each frame varies. When a page contains one or more frames that fail to decode, a retry operation must be performed to obtain more accurate read data. Therefore, analyzing the characteristics of frame errors in flash memory is essential for developing effective latency reduction schemes.

In most cases, either all frames decode successfully or multiple frames fail simultaneously. This strong interframe correlation suggests that frames within the same page share similar error characteristics. Consequently, this property can be effectively exploited to optimize the read latency of LDPC decoding in NAND flash memory.
C. Design of the HFMLLR Scheme
The DCDR scheme proposed in this article effectively utilizes the disparity between information bits and check bits in LDPC codes, as well as the variations in error characteristics across flash memory pages. Through data reconstruction, it creates reliability distinctions among bits within a frame without relying on any auxiliary information. Analyzing the impact of LLR values on decoding results, alongside extracting frame error characteristics, offers additional means to optimize read latency. Based on these insights, this section introduces the HFMLLR scheme, which capitalizes on the inherent characteristics of LDPC codes and flash memory to significantly reduce read latency with minimal overhead.
Algorithm 2 presents the complete workflow of the proposed HFMLLR scheme. The process begins by determining the stored data information, followed by data reconstruction and storage as per the DCDR scheme. When data need to be read, the entire wordline is accessed. Since the DCDR scheme reconstructs data at the level of a single wordline unit, the HFMLLR scheme is particularly suitable for scenarios involving continuous reading of large datasets. After the data are read, the inverse process of the DCDR scheme is performed to recover data for each page, followed by decoding.
In the decoding process, the initial LLR values are assigned to the data stored in the LSB, CSB, and MSB pages, respectively. For check bits, the LLR values are scaled by a factor of 2.5, whereas those for information bits are kept unchanged. When both information bits and check bits reside in the CSB page, no LLR adjustment is applied. Decoding proceeds frame by frame. A. Test Platform of Flash Memory
All test data presented in this section were generated using our team’s custom-developed test platform [44]. Fig. 10 displays the physical layout of the test platform, which is based on the ZYNQ-7000 SOC. E. Analysis of Overhead
A key advantage of the proposed DCDR and HFMLLR schemes over others is their extremely low overhead while still achieving reduced latency. The DCDR scheme requires no additional storage overhead, with its computational overhead being limited to the reordering of data written to flash memory, which is negligible. The HFMLLR scheme optimizes LLR values by directly assigning specific initial values to the LLR of the check bit, thus incurring no overhead. The use of frame error characteristics only requires adding a single verification stepłchecking whether decoding was successful łresulting in minimal computational overhead. Additionally, when the flash page capacity cannot be matched to an integer multiple of the frame rate, both the proposed scheme and the traditional scheme require storing the extra data on the next wordline. Therefore, this approach does not incur additional overhead compared to the traditional scheme. Clearly, the proposed scheme achieves significant latency reduction with extremely low overhead.
VI. CONCLUSION
To effectively address the excessive LDPC decoding latency in 3-D TLC NAND flash memory, this article systematically proposes the HFMLLR scheme. It first reveals the divergent impact of information bits and check bits on reliability within LDPC codes. By combining this insight with the physical characteristic variations among pages in 3-D TLC NAND flash memory, an innovative DCDR scheme is introduced. This approach establishes a natural bit-level reliability gradient within data frames without introducing additional storage overhead, providing a solid foundation for subsequent decoding optimization. Building upon this, this article further investigates the mechanism by which the LLR influences decoding convergence during the iterative decoding process. It also explores the spatial correlation among multiframe errors within the same flash page, ultimately leading to the proposal of the HFMLLR scheme. This scheme organically integrates the distinct characteristics of LDPC codes and flash memory, along with LLR and interframe error features. Consequently, it offers comprehensive advantages, including low overhead, high compatibility, and applicability throughout the entire lifecycle. This approach achieves substantial acceleration of
