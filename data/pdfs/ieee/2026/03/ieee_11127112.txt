A Study on the Fitness of GA for Improving SP Decoding Performance
Madoka Nishikawa 1, Yasuaki Nakamura 1, Yasushi Kanai 2, and Yoshihiro Okamoto 1
1Graduate School of Science and Engineering, Ehime University, Matsuyama 790-8577, Japan 2Department of Engineering, Niigata Institute of Technology, Kashiwazaki 945-1195, Japan
We have studied low-density parity-check (LDPC) coding and iterative decoding methods for shingled magnetic recording (SMR) in high-capacity hard disk drives (HDDs), which is an areal recording density of 4 Tbits/inch2. Previously, we applied a neural network to evaluate the log-likelihood ratio (LLR) associated with row operations in the sum-product (SP) decoder for LDPC codes. Then, we emphasized the LLR considering the influence of noise depending on the recording pattern by providing the LLRs for the decoding target and its adjacent bits to the neural network in SP decoding. Furthermore, we explored the optimal parameters to emphasize the LLR by applying the genetic algorithm (GA). In this study, to explore more optimal parameters, we propose a fitness function to improve selection accuracy and increase the number of eligible LLRs. Then, we aim to improve the performance of SP decoding by the effect of GA. As a result, by applying the proposed fitness function to the GA, it is possible to maintain high selection accuracy and to increase the probability that LLRs are emphasized in SP decoding. Therefore, it achieves error-free performance with fewer iterations of turbo equalization compared to the case of a conventional fitness function.
Index Terms— Genetic algorithm (GA), low-density parity-check (LDPC) code, neural network, shingled magnetic recording (SMR), sum-product (SP) decoding.

I. INTRODUCTION
I N RECENT years, the explosive increase in data volume has required even higher capacity hard disk drives (HDDs). Therefore, we are focusing on the shingled magnetic recording (SMR) [1], which enhances perpendicular magnetic recording (PMR), and developing signal processing methods to achieve higher recording density. We studied the performance improvement of low-density parity-check (LDPC) coding and iterative decoding methods in SMR [2]. In the SMR, due to its narrow tracks, the signal processing methods are required to reduce the influence of intertrack interference (ITI) and signaldependent noise, such as transition jitter. We have achieved a reduction of the effects of ITI by applying a 2-D finite impulse response (TD-FIR) filter [3]. Also, to consider the influence of signal-dependent noise, we proposed the sum-product (SP) decoder, in which a neural network evaluates the log-likelihood ratio (LLR) related to row operations using the LLRs of the decoding target bit and its adjacent bits and emphasizes the LLR based on the neural network outputs [4]. Furthermore, we demonstrated that a genetic algorithm (GA) with a fitness function to minimize a bit error rate (BER) in the SP decoder is useful for determining parameters, such as thresholds and weights to emphasize LLRs. However, we believe that setting a fitness function so that more reliable LLRs are emphasized will contribute to improve decoding performance.

II. READ/WRITE SYSTEM

Fig. 1 shows a block diagram of an SMR read/write (R/W) system with LDPC coding and iterative decoding. The input sequence is encoded by a 128/130(0, 16/8) runlength limited (RLL) [5] encoder and a (30, 3)-regular LDPC encoder [2]. After that, the recording sequence is recorded on a granular medium model consisting of the discrete Voronoi diagram [6]. In this study, we assume the areal recording density of 4 Tbits/inch2. The model sets the average grain size, grain diameter dispersion, and average nonmagnetic grain boundary to 5.0, 1.0, and 1.0 nm, respectively. III. ITERATIVE DECODING IN THE SP DECODER
In this section, we explain the details of the iterative decoding in the SP decoder proposed in this article.
A. SP Decoding
Fig. 2 illustrates the block diagram of the SP decoder shown in Fig. 1. The LDPC code of the parity check matrix with a code length of 36 990 bits, a column weight of 3, and a row weight of 30 is employed in this study. As the parity

check matrix is composed of three submatrices, the LLRs are calculated for the three submatrices. The light-yellow, light-green, and pink blocks in the figure mean the LLR calculation for the 1st, 2nd, and 3rd submatrices, respectively. Furthermore, the extrinsic information as prior information for APP decoding is calculated from the LLRs for message nodes and SP decoder input and is returned to the APP decoder.
The LLR for the message node is calculated by the Maxlog-MAP algorithm [8] using ln γkn, ln αkn, and ln βkn (n = 1–3) based on the LDPC code constraints, where γkn means the transition probability from the previous point to the current point on the trellis diagram for the nth submatrix of the parity check matrix. Also, αkn and βkn mean the forward and backward probabilities on the trellis diagram for the nth submatrix, respectively. Using a method similar to [4], the neural network evaluates ln γkn related to the decoding target bit and its adjacent bits in row operations on the parity check matrix to consider the influence of noise depending on the recording patterns. Then, the reliable LLRs are emphasized using the parameters explored by the GA, as shown in Section III-B.
During the iterative decoding in the SP decoder, the LLR for the check node of each submatrix is obtained by adding the LLRs for the message node of other submatrices, and a priori information for each submatrix is obtained by multiplying Wpm based on the parity check information m (= 0–3). We discuss the details of the parity check information and the LLR calculation for prior information in [9], respectively.
