Research on QC-LDPC Decoding Method With Low Quantization Word Length Based on Adaptive Information Mapping in Passive Optical Network

Tao Zhang , Dehui Kong , Xinwei Fu, Yilin Zhong, Zhaosheng Liu, Renlin Dai, He Shi, and Junhui Song

Abstract—Passive Optical Networks (PON) have advantages such as stable performance, convenient installation, high bandwidth, and resource saving, making them a mainstream network access technology with broad application prospects. The development of such as 8K video, digital twins, VR and other technologies causes explosive growth of network traffic and drives the next generation of PON to evolve towards higher rates, which results in the use of forward error correction (FEC) coding with higher coding gain to improve the power budget of PON. Quasi-cyclic low density parity check (QC-LDPC) codes are widely utilized in PON systems due to high coding gain and parallel encoding and decoding capabilities. However, the application of turbodecode message passing (TDMP) decoding method is inevitably equated with high hardware complexity of the decoder caused by storing and processing massive information, which is one of the obstacles to PON evolution. Reducing the quantization word length is effective to reduce hardware complexity, but it also leads to saturation of decoding information, causing errors and affecting decoding performance. This work proposes an nonlinear mapping method which utilizes adaptive compression of decoding information through node saturation state monitoring during the decoding process, in order to ensure that the probability density of node information has a reasonable distribution. The simulation results indicate that the method can effectively mitigate the decline in decoding performance under low-word-length conditions.
Index Terms—Quasi-cyclic low density parity check codes turbo-decode message passing, quantization, nonlinear mapping.

Moore’s Law, the bandwidth demand of users increases by an order of magnitude every three years. In the field of access networks, the 10G EPON technology has matured and entered commercial deployment as a replacement for 1G EPON. Furthermore, as early as 2011, the ITU-T initiated research on NG-PON2 technology, indicating that PON standards have already entered the era of 40 Gbps [1]. In the face of the ever-increasing bandwidth demand, the capacity of PON systems has to be increased, resulting in higher noise power and subsequently a decrease in the SNR. This poses a significant challenge to the power budget of PON systems. FEC technology, as an essential component of PON, can effectively improve the bit error rate by correcting errors in the codewords using redundant information. It is an economical and effective technique to enhance the power budget of the link. In the early days of optical communication systems, FEC was not commonly employed. However, even the use of a simple FEC scheme can yield significant SNR gains, making FEC highly promising. In 1988, a fundamental FEC scheme specifically designed for optical communication was proposed, enhancing the transmission performance of the system [2].
The application of FEC in PON systems has always encountered challenges due to the inherent asymmetry of PON, where the Optical Network Unit (ONU) located at the end-user premises can only support limited complexity in hardware implementation. As a result, early deployed PON systems primarily relied on simplistic FEC schemes, most of which were based on Reed-Solomon (RS) codes proposed by Irving Reed and Gustave Solomon [3]. Currently, more complicated FEC schemes, such as LDPC codes or concatenated code [7], have been proposed in higher performance PON standards. These advanced FEC schemes provide superior error correction capabilities and enable higher transmission efficiency in PON systems.
LDPC codes have experienced remarkable development since their introduction in the 1960s by Robert Gallager [8]. Initially overshadowed by RS codes, LDPC codes gained significant attention in the late 1990s when efficient decoding algorithms were discovered [9]. An enormous amount of results [10], [11] illustrate that the performance of LDPC codes is close to the Shannon limit in AWGN channels. Besides, LDPC codes have low implementation complexity, as well as the ability to perform high-speed parallel encoding and decoding.

Subsequent research focused on improving LDPC code performance and decoding algorithms. Irregular LDPC codes were introduced, offering better error-correction capabilities compared to regular LDPC codes. The check matrix of QCLDPC codes exhibits quasi-cyclic characteristics, making them simple to construct and flexible in terms of encoding and decoding architectures [12], [13]. As a result, they are widely used in various engineering applications. The aforementioned super FEC scheme used in high-rate DWDM submarine optical cable transmission systems in 2004 is essentially a form of QC-LDPC codes.
In terms of decoding algorithms, LDPC soft-decision decoding schemes have become a research focus due to their generally superior performance compared to hard-decision decoding schemes. The belief propagation (BP) decoding algorithm is one of the most commonly used soft-decision algorithms. Among its derivatives based on the log-likelihood ratio (LLR), Normalized Min-Sum (NMS) and Offset MinSum (OMS) algorithms have been widely adopted due to their ease of hardware implementation [14], [15], [16]. Furthermore, various adaptive modified MS algorithms have been extensively studied in recent years [17].
In terms of LDPC decoding scheduling, there are two schemes based on the order of information updates. Two Phase Message Passing (TPMP) scheme involves completely independent sequences for updating check node information and variable node information [18]. On the other hand, in TDMP scheme, check nodes and variable nodes update alternately, resulting in significantly improved convergence speed compared to TPMP [19]. Additionally, TDMP can reduce the amount of stored information, effectively decreasing area and power.
Apart from these studies, quantization schemes also have a significant impact on chip area and power consumption. The letter [20] enhances the reconstruction of coarsely quantized check node messages by utilizing messages from the previous iteration as side-information. Non-uniform quantized decoding algorithm is proposed for LDPC in [21]. Simulation results provide coding gain increases closer to of 0.5 dB in higher SNR and has good error floor at 10−5. The paper [22] proposes and verifies a 5-bit non-uniform quantization (NUQ) LDPC code for LLR processing, and compares it with two other uniform quantization schemes. The work [23] supports the massive machine type communications by synergistically utilizing neural MS decoder to enhance decoding performance, and neural quantizer to reduce computational and memory demands. The bottlenecks of low-precision quantization schemes mainly exist in the following three aspects:
• Narrow quantization range, where the absolute values of probability information gradually increase during the decoding process [24]. Insufficient quantization bit width can cause a large amount of information to be quantified to saturation, thereby weakening the decoding performance.
• Low precision of quantization, leading to insufficient discrimination between the minimum and second minimum values in the MS algorithm, thereby reducing the decoding speed.

• The basic idea of traditional NUQ methods is based on the initial step size of quantization, introducing the concept of growth rate to exponentially increase the quantization interval, thereby increasing the quantization range at the cost of increasing complexity. This method is suitable for decoding message sequences with most values distributed in lower regions and a few values distributed in higher regions. The construction of NUQ algorithms is complex and there are certain difficulties in hardware implementation [25].
This paper addresses the phenomenon caused by low quantization bit width and proposes a nonlinear mapping scheme to effectively compress the decoding information, thereby avoiding the occurrence of quantization saturation in a large number of nodes and low discrimination between the minimum and second minimum values. By continuously monitoring the saturation status of each variable node, the external information transmitted between nodes is compressed to ensure it remains within the quantization range. The simulation results indicate that the method can significantly reduce the word length of the decoding information with small decoding performance loss.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. In section-II, BP decoding algorithms and related basic knowledge is introduced. Section-III discusses the impact of information quantization schemes on decoding and an nonlinear mapping method for low quantization word length. In section-IV, the effectiveness of the method is demonstrated through simulation. Section V concludes the papers.
II. BP DECODING ALGORITHMS
A. Motivation
In this section, we primarily introduce two forms of the BP decoding algorithm and the decoding scheme used in this article for testing. To enable LDPC codes to be effectively applied in PON systems, it is crucial to select efficient and hardwarefriendly decoding schemes. On the other hand, the decoding scheme is also a prerequisite for determining the quantization scheme. The NMS algorithm, as one of the most suitable and efficient LDPC decoding algorithms for hardware implementation, originates from the BP algorithm based probability. The analysis and improvement of subsequent quantization schemes are also based on the Gaussian evolution of the BP algorithm.

E. TDMP-NMS Algorithm
In the traditional BP algorithm, variable node i waits for all the check node in Ci to be updated before updating qi and qi j. For hardware, updating all check node information in Ci simultaneously is undoubtedly complex. To avoid the delay caused by waiting for all check node updates and to accelerate convergence by quickly utilizing the updated variable node information, the TDMP strategy can be adopted. In the TDMP strategy, the check node information in Ci is updated one by one. Once variable node i receives a specific r ji from Ci, it immediately updates the information using the following equation:

By using the NMS algorithm, this operation can be simplified to a hardware-friendly linear operation, significantly reducing the implementation cost. B. Gaussian Approximation
For LDPC codes using LLR-BP decoding algorithm on memoryless binary-input AWGN channels, Chung et al. [24] proposed Gaussian approximation to simplify the density evolution theory of multidimensional check node and variable node messages into one-dimensional mean evolution theory. This approximation facilitates the analysis of the asymptotic performance of LDPC codes. On the other hand, the Gaussian approximation also reveals the fundamental reasons for the impact of quantization schemes on decoding performance. First, we provide a definition:
Definition 1: Let f (x) represent the probability density of message in the form of LLR. The symmetry condition can

be expressed as f (x) = f (-x)ex. If f (x) ∼ N(µ, σ2), then the symmetry condition can be simplified to σ2 = 2µ. In this case, the Gaussian distribution is called the symmetric distribution.
Definition 2: Let f (x) represent the probability density of message in the form of LLR. The symmetry condition can be expressed as f (x) = f (-x)e−x. If f (x) ∼ N(µ , σ 2), then the symmetry condition can be simplified to σ 2 = −2µ . In this case, the Gaussian distribution is called the anti-symmetric distribution.
Definition 3: For a generalized symmetric distribution that includes both symmetric and anti-symmetric distributions, if µ = −µ , the distribution is called the isomorphic generalized symmetric distribution with respect to µ.

C. The Impact of Information Quantization Schemes on Decoding
In the case of using floating-point algorithms, LDPC codes exhibit excellent error correction performance. However, practical applications demand the use of quantization with minimal word length to meet performance requirements while satisfying constraints on system power consumption and area. A smaller quantization word length implies a limited quantization range for information without sacrificing quantization precision. When the quantization range of information is limited, the mean of the isomorphic generalized symmetric distribution saturates at the boundaries of the quantization range, leading to saturation quantization errors. If the quantization range is too small, it can severely impact decoding performance.
Similarly, for the aforementioned LDPC codes, different quantization word length are considered to investigate the variation of mean values for various messages during the decoding process. Q(qv, qc) denotes a quantization bit width of qv bits for variable messages and qc bits for check messages. Q(F, F) represents the floating-point algorithm.

Fig. 2 illustrates the variation of mean absolute values for decoding messages of LDPC (2304, 1920) under the conditions of BPSK modulation, AWGN channel, and a SNR of 3dB. It can be observed that the blue curve representing the floating-point algorithm continuously increases during the decoding process, while the remaining curves representing fixed-point algorithms tend to flatten near the boundaries of the quantization range. This indicates the presence of quantization saturation phenomena in the decoded information.
D. Decoding Information Mapping
Regarding the quantization scheme for decoding messages, the analysis above reveals that the low-word-length quantization scheme primarily affects the quantization range of the decoded information. When a majority of decoding messages reach a state of quantization saturation, there will be a lack of discrimination for the larger values of the decoding information, making it extremely challenging to correct the signs of error variable nodes that are in a saturated state. To achieve an expanded quantization range for decoding information under low-word-length condition, the most effective approach is to compress the decoding information.

However, an important issue that is still overlooked is that this processing method also leads to a lack of discrimination for small decoding information. The position and magnitude of the smallest and second smallest variable node information in the NMS algorithm are crucial factors affecting the decoding performance and speed. For instance, in the original variable message, the minimum and second minimum values before compression are 3 and 2, respectively. After applying information compression, both values become 1. As a result, the positions of the minimum and second minimum values become ambiguous in the next iteration. it is advisable to record the positions of the minimum values before compression, rather than during the iterative decoding process. Then, when correcting message after compression, two different factors αminand αmin2 can be used to respectively correct the minimum and second smallest values, which can compensate for the information loss caused by information compression process.
E. Adaptive Information Mapping Decoding (AIM) Scheme Based on TDMP-NMS Algorithms
In practical applications, the timing of information compression cannot be generalized due to variations in SNR and the specific choice of quantization word length. The frequency of information compression should match the rate of growth in the mean absolute value of the decoded messages. When the mean absolute value of the decoded messages increases rapidly, frequent information compression is necessary to prevent quantization saturation. Conversely, when the mean absolute value of the decoded messages increases slowly, infrequent information compression is preferred to avoid information loss caused by compression. Therefore, it is essential to observe the saturation state of each variable node to determine whether information compression should be performed.

By taking the quantization word length of the variable messages as 8, 7, 6, and 5 bit, a set of relationships between ηv and S NRv can be obtained, as shown in Fig. 5. The analysis of figure reveals that for the same ηv, curves with smaller quantization word length exhibit lower saturation S NRv. When ηv is small, the S NRv is high, indicating that only a small portion of variable nodes are in the quantization saturation state, resulting in minimal quantization saturation noise. If compression is applied to the decoding messages at this stage, the information that is not in the quantization saturation state will be lost. When ηv ≈0.1, the S NRv for each quantization word length has already dropped below the SNR threshold (SNR degradation slows down when it falls below 30dB, so 30 dB is chosen as the SNR threshold). At this point, if the compression information is not utilized, the decrease in S NRv will adversely affect the decoding performance.

As the quantization word length decreases from Q(8,6) to Q(5,3), the decoding performance gradually deteriorates, which is due to the increasing severity of quantization saturation phenomena generated by low-word-length schemes during the decoding process. When the AIM2 algorithm is used, the output bit error rate (BER) of Q(5,3) exhibits a noticeable improvement at low input BER levels, while the improvement is not significant at high input BER levels. The two factors correction in the AIM2 algorithm compensates for the information loss caused by information compression, resulting in further enhancement of the decoding performance. Compared to the TDMP-NMS algorithm, the decoding performance can be improved by approximately 1.5 to 2 orders of magnitude at low input BER levels with the Q(5,3) quantization scheme by using AIM2 algorithm. In addition, it can be observed that the AIM2 method performs about 0.5 order of magnitude better than traditional NUQ schemes.

V. CONCLUSION
The quantization scheme greatly impacts the performance and hardware complexity of LDPC. Through the Gaussian approximation theory analysis of irregular LDPC codes, it is observed that the mean of absolute value of the decoding message tends to increase with iterations. The primary cause for the deficiency in decoding performance under low quantization word length is the saturation of variable information due to insufficient quantization range.
This paper proposes an effective method to address the inadequate decoding performance caused by low-word-length quantization. By compressing the information, the occurrence of quantization saturation can be avoided. Based on this, we further investigate the hardware implementation scheme for information compression and suggest using a LUT to directly map decoding messages, thereby preventing information loss due to varying compression factors. Furthermore, to effectively counteract the information loss during the compression process, we introduce a two factors correction scheme to compensate for the information compression loss. Finally,
