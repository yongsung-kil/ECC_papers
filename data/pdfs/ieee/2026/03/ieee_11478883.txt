Noisy Soft Syndrome Decoding of Quantum LDPC Codes for the Quantum Erasure Channel
Gayathri R.†⋆, Shobhit Bhatnagar∧⋆ and P Vijay Kumar† †Department of Electrical Communication Engineering, IISc Bangalore ∧Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, The University of Arizona
{gayatr,shobhitb97,pvk1729}@gmail.com

Abstract—Erasure decoding of quantum low-density paritycheck (QLDPC) codes has recently received a lot of interest as qubit erasures occur in many physical systems such as neutralatom systems, trapped-ion systems etc. However, decoding of QLDPC codes heavily relies on syndrome information, which may be unreliable as syndrome extraction circuits are prone to errors in practice. While syndrome errors are usually modeled as flipping of a binary syndrome bit, typically, measurement outcomes yield analog voltage/current values, which are then discretized to obtain the binary syndrome. Prior work on correcting qubit errors in the presence of syndrome noise shows that this soft (i.e., analog) information can be exploited to achieve performance improvement. In this paper, we consider the case of qubit erasures and present an iterative decoding algorithm, based on the hardware-friendly min-sum algorithm, that incorporates this soft information to perform check node updates. We show via simulations on hypergraph product codes and lifted product codes that our decoder achieves performance very close to that in the case of noiseless syndrome for a certain noise regime.
I. INTRODUCTION
Quantum computation promises to provide an exponential advantage over classical computation, but quantum information is much more fragile than its classical counterpart. Quantum error correction (QEC) is therefore a necessary step in building a practical quantum computer. A promising class of quantum error-correcting codes to achieve practical fault tolerance is the family of quantum low density parity-check (QLDPC) codes, which are analogous to classical LDPC codes [1]. A significant amount of effort has been made over the last decade to construct QLDPC codes that have both high rate and large minimum distance. This effort has led to many non-trivial code constructions [2]–[9]. One such construction is the hypergraph product (HGP) construction by Tillich and Zémor [10], and its subsequent generalization, the lifted product (LP) construction by Pantaleev and Kalachev [11].
Analogous to classical LDPC codes, QLDPC codes are equipped with efficient message-passing decoding algorithms, such as the belief propagation algorithm and the hardware-friendly min-sum algorithm (MSA) [12]. However, contrary to the classical case, the input to the decoder of a QLDPC code is a syndrome vector instead of a received vector. The decoder is tasked with finding an error estimate that corresponds to this syndrome. An interesting and significant difference from the classical case here is the phenomenon of degeneracy, where the decoder may output any one among a set of error estimates rather than a single, unique error vector.

The syndrome extraction process, however, is itself prone to errors. Thus, the QLDPC decoder may receive as input an erroneous syndrome, in which case it will try to find an error estimate that corresponds to this erroneous syndrome. While syndrome measurements can be repeated, this process is inefficient and emphasis in the literature has been on singleshot error correction [13], where decoding is performed using only a single round of (noisy) syndrome measurements. Usually, syndrome errors are modeled as a flipping of the binary syndrome value with some probability [14]. For such a setting, one can employ quantum data-syndrome (QDS) codes [15], which recover from syndrome errors by coding across the syndrome bits. However, Pattison et al. in [14] point out that in practice, syndrome measurements yield analog voltage/current values which are then discretized to obtain the binary syndrome. Then in the case of surface codes they modify the Minimum Weight Perfect Matching and Union Find decoders to incorporate this analog soft information and achieve improved performance. In a followup work, Raveendran et al. [16] borrow the Gaussian noise model for syndrome errors from [14] and modify the MSA algorithm to incorporate the noisy soft syndrome. For a certain noise regime, this algorithm achieves performance almost identical to that in the case of noiseless syndrome, for various families of QLDPC codes.
The focus in both [14] and [16] is on recovering from qubit errors in the presence of a noisy soft syndrome. On the

Our Contributions: In this paper, we focus on erasure decoding of QLDPC codes in the presence of a noisy soft syndrome. We assume the Gaussian noise model for syndrome noise assumed in [14], [16] and present an iterative decoder that iterates between performing the MSA algorithm on a sub-Tanner graph consisting of check nodes whose associated reliability is larger than a suitable threshold and are therefore reliable, and updating the reliabilities of less reliable check nodes using final messages accumulated at variable nodes. As the algorithm proceeds, more check nodes become reliable and participate in the message-passing. We demonstrate the efficacy of our decoder by showing through simulations on HGP and LP codes that, for a certain noise regime, it achieves performance very close to that of the noiseless syndrome setting. Calderbank-Shor-Steane (CSS) codes [26], [27] are a popular class of stabilizer codes where each stabilizer is a tensor product of either only I2-type and X-type Pauli matrices, or only I2-type and Z-type Pauli matrices. Consider

SX = {Xa1 ⊗ Xa2 ⊗ · · · ⊗ Xan | [a1, a2, . . . , an] ∈ CX⊥},
and the Z-type stablizers are given by
SZ = {Zb1 ⊗ Zb2 ⊗ · · · ⊗ Zbn | [b1, b2, . . . , bn] ∈ CZ⊥}.
An X-type (Z-type) error is a Pauli error that consists of only I2-type and X-type (Z-type) Pauli matrices. Independent X-type and Z-type errors can be handled separately (and analogously) in the CSS framework. HX is used to correct Z-type errors while HZ is used to correct Xtype errors. The syndrome for X-type errors is given by sTX = HZ EXT , where EX is the corresponding error vector for the X-type Pauli error. EX contains I2-type and X-type Pauli matrices in its vector representation. In the remainder of this paper, we will deal with only X-type errors, and therefore use the less cumbersome notation s for sX , H for HZ , and E for EX , so that sT = HET . The i-th bit si of the syndrome s is 0 if E commutes with the i-th Z-type stabilizer generator, and 1 if E anti-commutes with it.

B. Quantum LDPC Codes
QLDPC codes are quantum analogues of classical LDPC codes and correspond to the case when the HX and HZ matrices mentioned before are sparse in nature. We briefly describe below two popular classes of QLDPC codes known as HGP codes [10] and LP codes [11].
For HGP codes, the matrices HX and HZ are given by
HX = (H1 ⊗ In2 Im1 ⊗ H2T ), HZ = (In1 ⊗ H2 H1T ⊗ Im2 ),
where H1 ∈ F2m1×n1 and H2 ∈ Fm 2 2×n2 are parity check matrices of two classical binary LDPC codes.
LP codes are generalizations of HGP codes, where each scalar entry in the matrices HX and HZ above is replaced by a circulant matrix. This is called lifting. Thus, the matrices HX and HZ for an LP code are given by
HX = (H˜1 ⊗ In2 Im1 ⊗ H˜2T ), HZ = (In1 ⊗ H˜2 H˜1T ⊗ Im2 ),
where H˜1 and H˜2 are (m1 × n1) and (m2 × n2) matrices, respectively, whose entries are (L × L) binary circulant matrices.

D. Noiseless Syndrome Decoding of QLDPC Codes
The MSA algorithm is a low-complexity message-passing algorithm that passes messages along the edges of the Tanner graph associated with a code. Let H ∈ Fm 2 ×n be a paritycheck matrix of a binary [n, k ≥ (n − m)] code C. The corresponding Tanner graph T (H) = (V, C, E) is a bipartite graph, where V = {1, 2, . . . , n} is the set of variable nodes corresponding to the n code symbols, C = {1, 2, . . . , m} is the set of check nodes corresponding to the rows of H, and E is the set of edges of T (H). Check node i is connected to variable node j iff Hi,j = 1, where Hi,j denotes the (i, j)th entry of H. The neighborhood N (v) of a variable node v ∈ V is the set of all the check nodes that are connected to it and similarly the neighborhood N (c) of a check node c ∈ C is the set of all the variable nodes connected to it.

E. Gaussian Syndrome Noise Model
We adopt the Gaussian syndrome noise model in [14], [16]. In case of perfect, noiseless syndrome measurements, the measurement outcome yields the real value s¯i = +1 if the Pauli error commutes with the i-th stabilizer, and s¯i = −1 if the Pauli error anti-commutes with it. III. SOFT SYNDROME ERASURE DECODING
We will now discuss our soft syndrome decoding algorithm, the pseudocode for which is presented in Algorithm 1. Consider the input Tanner graph T1(H) = (V1, C, E1). As in [28], we initialize the channel LLR of unerased variable nodes to llrmax = 25 (instead of +∞) and that of erased variable nodes to llrmin = 10−5 (instead of 0) for numerical stability. Let CE ⊆ C denote the set of check nodes that have at least one erased variable node in their neighborhood. It follows that the true (i.e., noiseless) syndrome bit corresponding to each of the remaining check nodes in C \ CE must be 0. Thus, we update γc to +∞ for each check node c ∈ C \ CE.
To perform message-passing, we use a modified Tanner graph (see Fig. 2) as in [16], where, to each check node c ∈ C, an additional type of variable node vc, which we will refer to as a virtual variable node, is attached, while retaining the existing variable nodes, check nodes, and edges of T1(H). We will denote the modified Tanner graph by T2(H) = (V1 ∪ V2, C, E2), where V2 denotes the set of virtual variable nodes. The channel LLR associated with virtual variable node vc is set to γc, and is denoted by γvc in lieu of λvc as a reminder that this variable node is a virtual variable node. Thus, there is a direct correspondence between reliabilities of check nodes of the original Tanner graph T1(H) and reliabilities of the corresponding virtual variable nodes of the modified Tanner graph T2(H). Informally, the syndrome values on check nodes of the original Tanner graph have been “pushed onto” the virtual variable nodes in the modified Tanner graph. Thus, in the modified Tanner graph, the sum of the variable node bit values in the neighborhood of a check node should match the bit value on the corresponding virtual variable node, which implies that the syndrome bit associated to each check node in the modified Tanner graph should be 0.
We now describe the message-passing schedule for T2(H). First, we choose a threshold Γ and denote by C(a) the set of all check nodes c for which |γvc | > Γ. We denote by T2(H)|C(a) the subgraph of T2(H) obtained by removing all the check nodes in C \ C(a) and all the edges incident to any such check node. We remark here that the main idea behind Algorithm 1 is the empirical observation that message-passing decoding is more robust to disregarding a few check nodes than to the presence of even a small number of erroneous syndromes. For example, for the [[882, 24, 18 ≤ d ≤ 24]] LP code in Fig. 1, at erasure rate 0.25, the nMSA algorithm with noiseless syndrome has failure rate 1.38 × 10−2. If 15 check nodes are removed randomly then the failure rate increases to around 1.76 × 10−2, whereas in the presence of 5 erroneous syndrome bits the failure rate is around 2.2 × 10−1. So our algorithm excludes a few lowreliability check nodes at each iteration and updates them eventually as per a schedule. In a nutshell, we will perform message passing only on T2(H)|C(a) for T iterations and use the messages accumulated at variable nodes to update the virtual variable nodes corresponding to check nodes in C(b) = C \ C(a) at the (T + 1)th iteration. thresholding the final messages at the virtual variable nodes and again perform the n-MSA algorithm on T2(H)|C(a) . As the algorithm proceeds, the size of C(b) reduces which helps the n-MSA algorithm to converge. Note that if Γ is too small,

We wish to emphasize here that the message-passing schedule of our algorithm as well as the syndrome update rules are significantly different from that in [16], where syndrome updates as well as halting condition check is performed after each message-passing iteration. The algorithm in [16] was proposed for qubit errors and performs close to the noiseless syndrome case. However, for the case of qubit erasures this algorithm does not perform as well. For example, for the [[882, 24, 18 ≤ d ≤ 24]] LP code in Fig. 1, at erasure rate 0.25, the algorithm in [16] has failure rate 1.7 × 10−1 for σ = 0.3, which is far from the noiseless syndrome case (1.38 × 10−2). However, as Fig. 1 shows, Algorithm 1 has performance very close to the noiseless syndrome case even when σ = 0.4.
We remark here that when the noise standard deviation is large, it is possible that check nodes with high reliabilities are also erroneous. In this case, one can employ an additional check if the halting condition is not met and the maximum number of iterations T ′ has been reached. One can check whether sˆT matches HEˆT for all virtual variable nodes that have absolute value of the final message greater than a suitably chosen threshold value Γ′ > Γ. In the simulation results presented in Fig. 1 and Fig. 3, we employ this additional check.

If this is the case, the decoder outputs Eˆ as the error estimate, otherwise, it outputs non-convergence.
We now discuss the complexity of Algorithm 1. The complexity of performing the n-MSA algorithm for T iterations is O(nT ), where a popular choice of T is log n [29]. Thus, the overall complexity of Algorithm 1 is O(nT ′ log n). As our simulations suggest, T ′ can be chosen to be a small number, and thus, in the typical case, the complexity of Algorithm 1 is similar to that of the n-MSA algorithm.
V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we present an erasure decoder for QLDPC codes for the setting of a noisy soft syndrome. The iterative decoder leverages the soft information from the noisy syndrome to classify the check nodes, enabling their selective participation in message-passing according to a defined schedule. The algorithm alternates between running the nMSA algorithm on the sub-Tanner graph and reclassifying the check nodes until the halting condition is met. We showed through simulations on HGP and LP codes that our decoding algorithm achieves performance close to the noiseless syndrome case for a certain noise regime. Handling degeneracy to further improve decoding performance is an interesting direction for future work.
