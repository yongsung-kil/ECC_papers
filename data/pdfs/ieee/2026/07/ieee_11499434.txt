From SMURF to HI-SMURF: Scalable Multivariate Nonlinear Function Approximation via Compact Stochastic Architectures
Xincheng Feng , Wenyong Zhou , Taiqiang Wu , Zhengwu Liu , Member, IEEE, Meng Li , and Ngai Wong , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—Multivariate nonlinear functions are fundamental to many tasks in artificial intelligence (AI) and communication systems but are difficult to implement efficiently in hardware due to high computational complexity and resource demands, especially on edge and embedded devices. Existing stochastic computing (SC) approaches are mostly limited to univariate cases and do not scale well for high-dimensional approximation. This paper presents SMURF (Stochastic Multivariate UniversalRadix Finite-State Machine) and its enhanced variant HISMURF (HIgh-Dimensional Stochastic Multivariate UniversalRadix Finite-State Machine) that uses tensor-train (TT) factorization to approximate high-dimensional functions without exponential state growth. SMURF analytically derives steady-state distributions and employs convex optimization for parameter synthesis, while HI-SMURF preserves accuracy under scalable input dimensionality by factorizing the parameter space. Both architectures use configurable state resolution and simple logic circuits driven by stochastic bitstreams. We evaluate SMURF and HI-SMURF on low-density parity-check (LDPC) decoding, image classification, image reconstruction, and high-dimensional nonlinear benchmarks against baselines including Taylor-series expansions, look-up table (LUT) methods, Bernstein polynomial SC, tensor-factorization methods, and quantized multilayer perceptrons (MLPs). Results show that our methods maintain comparable accuracy while dramatically reducing hardware area, power, and area-delay product (ADP), demonstrating superior scalability and efficiency for resource-constrained systems.
Index Terms—Nonlinear functions, stochastic computing, multivariate, high hardware efficiency.

I. INTRODUCTION
M ULTIVARIATE nonlinear functions are fundamental to a wide variety of computing tasks such as signal processing [1], [2], communications [3], [4], and artificial intelligence (AI) accelerators [5], [6], [7]. They enable accurate modeling of variable interactions that are critical for tasks like modulation and coding [8], [9], softmax and attention in neural networks [10], [11], and manifold-based transformations in data analytics [12], [13]. As embedded AI and edge computing workloads grow, the efficient hardware realization of these operators has become a central challenge [14], [15]. Such functions are often non-separable and require costly exponentiation, products, or complex activation profiles, which results in significant silicon area, power, and latency overheads under tight resource budgets. The rapid growth of deep learning [16], [17], vision transformers (ViTs) [18], [19], graph neural networks [20], and advanced signal processing pipelines has dramatically increased the volume of nonlinear computations performed in both training and inference. Modern AI models rely heavily on attention mechanisms [21], [22], normalization layers, and sophisticated activation functions, making the demand for efficient nonlinear operators especially acute in resource-constrained edge devices and large-scale data centers.
Traditional approximation strategies include Taylorseries expansions [23], coordinate rotation digital computer (CORDIC) algorithms [24], and look-up table (LUT) interpolation [25]. Despite their popularity, each has fundamental limitations: Taylor expansions often require high-order terms for complex or multivariate functions, increasing arithmetic depth and pipeline stages; CORDIC relies on iterative rotations, hindering efficient pipelining and low-latency design; and LUT interpolation scales exponentially in storage with dimensionality and resolution, becoming impractical for multivariate settings. These drawbacks motivate alternative approaches that better balance accuracy, scalability, and hardware efficiency.
Recent approximate computing methods include piecewiselinear arithmetic units [26], hybrid logic-table ensembles [27], Chebyshev polynomial evaluators [28], and analog or memristor based synthesizers [29]. Although they offer improved trade-offs in certain regimes, many are fixed-function,

hard to reconfigure, or rely on floating-point support and nontrivial control logic, and they are not intended for generalpurpose multivariate nonlinear approximation, limiting their use in reconfigurable accelerators and edge devices.
Stochastic computing (SC) has emerged as a promising paradigm for lightweight and noise tolerant arithmetic [30]. By encoding real values as unary bitstreams and processing them through simple logic gates such as AND, XOR, and MUX, SC enables ultra low cost computation. More recently, finite state machine (FSM) based SC architectures have extended these benefits to function approximation [31], [32], mapping bitstream statistics to state transitions to approximate functions such as exp(x), tanh(x), and ReLU(x). In parallel, Bernstein polynomial based SC realizes nonlinear mappings through stochastic evaluations of polynomial bases [33]. However, Bernstein approaches typically require long bitstreams to achieve high precision and become inefficient in high dimensional settings due to growth in required basis evaluations, which limits their practicality beyond univariate or low dimensional cases.
To address these challenges, we introduce the SMURF (Stochastic Multivariate Universal-Radix FSM) framework, which establishes a stochastic finite-state paradigm for approximating multivariate nonlinear functions in a compact and analytically tractable manner. SMURF models nonlinear mappings through probabilistic state transitions rather than explicit arithmetic operations, achieving substantially higher hardware efficiency than Taylor, LUT, Bernstein, or CORDIC baselines. By formulating nonlinear approximation in the probabilistic domain, SMURF enables flexible and hardware-friendly computation that inherently tolerates noise and quantization effects.
Building on this foundation, we present HI-SMURF (HighDimensional SMURF), a generalized framework that extends SMURF to high-dimensional nonlinear functions. HI-SMURF incorporates tensor-based function factorization, allowing complex multivariate mappings to be expressed as combinations of lower-order components. Through tensor training (TT), it effectively captures cross-dimensional dependencies while maintaining tractable state complexity and stochastic precision. This formulation provides a compact and scalable realization of highdimensional nonlinear operators without exponential growth in area or power. Together, SMURF and HI-SMURF form a unified SC framework that connects theoretical modeling with efficient hardware implementation for nonlinear function approximation.
The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
• SMURF introduces a probabilistic finite-state formulation that represents nonlinear functions through universal-radix transitions, providing a principled and compact alternative to arithmetic-based computation.
• HI-SMURF extends this formulation to high-dimensional domains by integrating TT with stochastic state modeling, enabling efficient synthesis of multivariate nonlinear operators with controllable accuracy and resource cost.
• Extensive software and hardware evaluations demonstrate that the proposed architectures achieve substantial

improvements in area, power, and area-delay product (ADP) efficiency while maintaining comparable accuracy to traditional approximation methods. • The proposed frameworks are applied to representative computing tasks, including low-density parity-check (LDPC) decoding and vision transformer (ViT) activation modeling, confirming their scalability and practical relevance across communication and AI hardware. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section II reviews the preliminaries, including SC fundamentals, sampling gates, FSM-based nonlinear modeling, and tensortrain decomposition. Section III presents the SMURF and HI-SMURF architectures, detailing their theoretical basis and tensor-based formulation. Section IV provides experimental and hardware results. Section V concludes the paper.

B. Random Sampling Gates
Random sampling gates [35] can be used to implement any function with known probability distributions. The logic gate used to map one probability distribution directly to another is called the θ-gate. The logic gate used to map one probability distribution to another under a condition is called the conditional probability table (CPT) gate.
θ-gate samples a random number set according to a certain probability to obtain a binary Bernoulli distribution. By comparing the value between a prescribed input value and a random entropy source, one obtains a binary (0, 1) output value. The gate can sample exactly from each given distribution and produce one sample per cycle, e.g., SNG in Fig. 1 is a simple θ-gate. As the sequence becomes sufficiently long, the output of a θ-gate approaches the set distribution. This is also equivalent to the Monte Carlo method. A θ-gate can also sample complex probability distributions such as the Sobol sequences. Henceforth, for convenience in further explanations, we refer to all gates that convert a full-precision value into an SN as θ-gates.
CPT-gate is a collection of θ-gates, together with a MUX to select one of the θ-gates as its output. This is equivalent to rolling a biased dice and choosing which dice value to take based on the input. In practice, we can adjust the distribution of the overall system output by modifying the threshold values of the θ-gates. The control of the MUX often involves the use of random devices such as an FSM whose state transitions follow specific probabilistic rules.

E. Discussion
SMURF is a stochastic, table-parameterized approximator with analytic mixing weights Ps and variance scaling O(1/L). HI-SMURF preserves the same Markov front-end and mixing law while tensorizing the CPT via TT cores, yielding linearin-M storage at fixed rank r and O(M r2) per-cycle compute.

IV. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
We evaluate SMURF and HI-SMURF across generic nonlinear operators, application tasks, and hardware benchmarks. SMURF exhibits clear 1/L-like error decay and outperforms LUT, Taylor, CORDIC, and Bernstein under comparable precision, while HI-SMURF scales to M =16 variables with accuracy competitive to a quantized multilayer perceptron (MLP). In applications, SMURF improves LDPC decoding bit error rate (BER), achieves competitive image reconstruction compared with traditional implicit neural representations (INRs), and HI-SMURF yields consistent gains when replacing softmax in a Tiny ViT. Standard fixed-point representation is employed for θ-gate inputs, whose quantization error is negligible compared to other sources of errors, such as the Markov process uncertainties and random bitstream averaging.
We first test the computing accuracy of SMURF in a 3-variate softmax function using 3-state, 4-state and 8-state FSMs for each input variable. The average absolute error with respect to the bitstream length is shown in Fig. 6, showing the fast decay and convergence of the average absolute errors. The average absolute errors are all around 0.15 near zero, and decrease to 0.02 at a bitstream length of 256. On the other hand, increasing the number of states in an FSM results in only small gains in output precision (≤ 10−2). Bitstream correlation can significantly bias gate-level SC arithmetic (e.g., AND/XNOR-based operations) because multiple stochastic streams are directly combined. In contrast, HISMURF uses stochastic streams primarily to drive FSM state transitions, and produces outputs via a table-based mapping

from FSM states; thus, correlation mainly affects variance rather than strongly biasing the expected output. Consistent with this, our correlation ablation (fixed L=128) shows only minor RMSE variation when sweeping the target correlation coefficient from ρ=1.0 to ρ=0.0, indicating low sensitivity to input-stream correlation under the evaluated setting.

C. Performance of SMURF in an LDPC Decoder

LDPC algorithms and SC share a common focus on stochastic methods to process information. SC represents data and performs operations using random bitstreams, which can be advantageous for hardware implementations due to its simplicity and fault tolerance. LDPC decoding can leverage SC by using these bitstreams to perform iterative stochastic calculations, potentially reducing complexity and power consumption in decoding processes.
In LDPC decoding algorithms, variable nodes (VNs) and check nodes (CNs) play a crucial role in the iterative process. VNs represent the bits of the codeword and are responsible for updating their values based on messages received from the connected CNs. CNs ensure parity constraints are satisfied by processing messages from VNs and sending feedback to guide corrections. This collaborative message-passing between nodes iteratively refines the probability estimates, improving the accuracy of decoded data.

D. Performance of SMURF in Fourier Decomposition and Image Reconstruction
To evaluate SMURF’s ability to approximate highly irregular image functions, we first decomposed target images into localized Fourier basis components. Directly training SMURF on pixel-wise grayscale values was found to be inefficient due to the non-smooth nature of high-frequency variations. Instead, by treating pixel coordinates (x1, x2) as input variables and approximating the image via Fourier basis functions, we transformed the reconstruction task into a set of smoother subproblems. Representative Fourier components with varying frequencies were fitted individually, as illustrated in Fig. 10. The results show that SMURF can accurately capture both low- and highfrequency Fourier bases, providing reliable building blocks for subsequent image synthesis.
After fitting the Fourier bases, the complete image was reconstructed by aggregating the individually approximated components. This two-stage process enables SMURF to handle both fine-grained details and global structural patterns. Fig. 11 reports the convergence behavior across different frequency terms, where lower-frequency functions converge faster while higher-frequency components require longer bitstreams and training epochs. Nevertheless, all tested functions eventually achieve stable approximation accuracy, confirming SMURF’s scalability to complex spectral features.

Fig. 12. Qualitative comparison between neural network-based INR, including SIREN [38] and WIRE [39], and our SMURF-based INR on gray-scale image reconstruction.
We then benchmarked SMURF against MLP-based INRs, including SIREN [38] and wavelet implicit neural representation (WIRE) [39]. As shown in Figs. 12, 13, and 14, SMURF achieves competitive or superior reconstruction quality across diverse scenarios. On a grayscale photograph, SMURF delivers a PSNR of 36.24dB, outperforming SIREN (34.13dB) and approaching WIRE (36.83dB). For computed tomography (CT) reconstruction, SMURF achieves 31.93dB, comparable to WIRE (32.37dB) and higher than SIREN (30.51dB), while preserving clinically relevant details. On color image reconstruction, SMURF attains 38.47dB, closely matching WIRE (38.53dB) and surpassing SIREN (38.26dB), faithfully capturing textures and subtle gradients. These results demonstrate that Fourier-decomposed training combined with stochastic FSM approximation provides an effective path for accurate image reconstruction, while maintaining substantial hardware efficiency advantages.

In particular, the compatibility scores produced by the querykey dot products were normalized using HI-SMURF rather than the conventional softmax function, while the remaining attention pipeline was left unchanged.

This design enables HI-SMURF to serve as a drop-in replacement for the softmax function, thereby directly validating its effectiveness within Transformer-based models. The baseline model follows a vanilla Tiny ViT configuration. For the MNIST dataset, each 28 × 28 image was partitioned into 7 × 7 patches, resulting in 16 patch tokens. Each token was linearly projected into a 128-dimensional embedding space. The Transformer encoder consisted of 4 attention heads, with a hidden layer dimension of 512 (corresponding to 128 × 4). Training was performed for 10 epochs without batch normalization.

For CIFAR-10, images were normalized to mean values of (0.4914, 0.4822, 0.4465) and standard deviations of (0.2470, 0.2435, 0.2616). Each 32 × 32 image was divided into 4 × 4 patches, resulting in 16 patch tokens projected to 128dimensional embeddings. The same encoder structure (4 heads, hidden dimension 512) was adopted, and training was conducted for 50 epochs with batch normalization enabled.
We further extended the evaluation to CIFAR-100 under the same training configuration. The experimental results are summarized in Table V. On MNIST, HI-SMURF slightly outperforms the baseline, achieving 98.4% validation accuracy compared to 98.2%. On CIFAR-10, HI-SMURF again yields a modest gain, reaching 76.9% versus 76.6% for the baseline. On the more challenging CIFAR-100 dataset, HI-SMURF maintains competitive convergence and surpasses the baseline, obtaining 47.2% validation accuracy compared to 46.9%. These results demonstrate that HI-SMURF can effectively replace the softmax operation in Transformer attention while delivering accuracy improvements across diverse datasets.

F. Hardware Implementation
To further validate the efficacy of SMURF and HI-SMURF, we benchmarked its hardware metrics using an RTL-verified, synthesis-based evaluation flow. The RTL implementation was verified in ModelSim, and the hardware metrics were obtained by mapping the design to an SMIC 65 nm standard-cell technology library under a 400 MHz clock constraint. Unless otherwise stated, the reported “SMURF core” area/power are measured with two SNGs: one for runtime inputs and one for trained parameters feeding the MUX; the RNG macro (1600 μm2) is included and dominates the SNG overhead. In our implementation, the RNG is realized by a lightweight tausURNG (combined Tausworthe PRNG), which generates one uniform random word per cycle to drive the SNG comparator (i.e., SNG = RNG + comparator); Box-Muller is only used when Gaussian/additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) samples are required in auxiliary experiments. All hardware evaluations were performed under consistent precision settings: SMURF and HISMURF used 256-bit bitstreams, Bernstein used the same 256bit configuration, and other baselines used 8-bit post-training quantization. The numerical accuracy (MAE/RMSE) has been reported in Subsections A and B. For higher-dimensional inputs, SNG cost can increase with parallel SNG replication; we use the two-SNG design as a practical latency-area trade-off. As an illustrative example, the bivariate Euclidean distance function was implemented, and a cubic Taylor-series polynomial was constructed to match SMURF’s approximation accuracy at a 256-bit bitstream length. SMURF employed two 4-state FSMs, consistent with the previous configuration.

G. Additional Remarks
The proposed SMURF architecture provides accurate and efficient approximation of diverse nonlinear functions through an SC-based FSM design, achieving substantial reductions in area and power compared with conventional methods such as Taylor and LUT implementations. Its parameterizable θ-gates enable flexible reconfiguration for different functions using the same hardware. Building on this foundation, HI-SMURF extends the approach to high-dimensional operators with only marginal hardware overhead, retaining SMURF’s compactness and energy efficiency. Together, SMURF and HI-SMURF offer a unified, reconfigurable, and resource-efficient framework

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we have presented SMURF, a stochastic computing architecture based on finite-state machines for efficient approximation of multivariate nonlinear functions, together with its high-dimensional extension, HI-SMURF. We have established a unified analytical framework for steady-state behavior, detailed hardware implementations, and tensor-trainbased parameterization to alleviate state-space explosion. Extensive evaluations across representative nonlinear functions and practical applications, including LDPC decoding and Vision Transformers, demonstrate that both SMURF and HISMURF achieve competitive numerical accuracy while consistently delivering superior hardware efficiency in terms of area, power, and area-delay product compared with conventional approximators such as Taylor, LUT, Bernstein, and quantized MLPs. These results highlight the scalability of HI-SMURF for high-dimensional operators and its potential as a flexible and reconfigurable coprocessor for resource-constrained edgeAI workloads.
