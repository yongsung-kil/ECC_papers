Hardware-Efficient QC-LDPC Decoding Architecture With Reduced Bit-Width
and Saturation Management

Hyeongseok Moon , Student Member, IEEE, and In-Cheol Park , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—In quantized layered low-density parity-check (LDPC) decoding, the saturated a posteriori probability (APP) value prevents extrinsic information from being correctly accumulated and overemphasizes certain error paths, making it necessary to use a larger APP representation. The proposed method compensates for the information loss caused by such saturation and eliminates the need for a larger APP bitwidth, reducing the APP bitwidth to the level of the check-to-variable message while preserving the decoding performance. This compensation is achieved by disabling APP updates when saturation occurs, starting from the iteration identified by the shortest cycles in the parity-check matrix. The proposed control preserves correct message passing and prevents the accumulation of errorpath weights in the APP. In the prototype LDPC decoder, the proposed method requires only a lightweight circuit to detect saturation, incurring a negligible area overhead and almost no degradation of processing delay. As a result, the proposed architecture reduces area and power by 15% and 25%, respectively, and achieves a throughput-to-area ratio of 25.08 Gbps/itr/mm2, while maintaining the FER performance of the 7-bit APP representation.
Index Terms—Low-density parity-check codes, normalized min-sum algorithm, VLSI hardware implementation, 5G new-radio standard, quasi-cyclic LDPC decoder.
I. INTRODUCTION
L OW-DENSITY parity-check (LDPC) codes were first proposed by Gallager [1] and later rediscovered in 1997 by MacKay and Neal [2]. The LDPC codes achieve near-capacity error-correcting performance when decoded by iterative message-passing algorithms and have been widely adopted in modern communication standards, including 5G new radio (NR) [3], [4], [5], [6], [7], [8]. Despite these techniques, LDPC decoders operating with fixed-point arithmetic still demand wide datapaths and substantial memory resources to support iterative message updates. To reduce this overhead, check-to-variable (C2V) messages, which generally exhibit a limited dynamic range, can be efficiently represented using only 5 or 6 bits. In contrast, a posteriori probability (APP) values require additional bits to reliably accumulate contributions across iterations [11], [12].
In this context, prior work has mitigated quantization effects by employing scaling or non-uniform quantization in the message update process [13], [14]. In this work, we keep the C2V message precision unchanged and focus on the APP accumulation process. Since the APP memory and the associated datapath occupy a large portion of the overall decoder, reducing the APP bitwidth provides significant benefits in hardware efficiency. However, in layered scheduling, if the APP bitwidth is aggressively reduced, the saturation can prevent sufficient reflection of valid extrinsic information, leading to decoding failures due to overemphasized errors [15]. As the magnitudes of messages and error components tend to grow along cycles in the parity-check matrix (PCM), this brief introduces a cycle-based saturation management mechanism.
In the proposed approach, the iteration to stop the APP update is determined based on the cycle structure of the PCM. From that iteration, the APP update is disabled once the value reaches its upper bound. This prevents the accumulation of error-path contributions while preserving correct message exchange at the beginning iterations. With the proposed approach, the APP bitwidth for all paths can be unified to that of the C2V messages, enabling a 27% reduction in APP memory size, an 18% reduction in logic area, and a 25% reduction in total decoder power consumption.

C. Hardware Implementation of QC-LDPC Decoders
Given the input LLRs, LDPC decoding exchanges messages between variable nodes and check nodes iteratively. A rowlayered, partially parallel architecture is commonly adopted, and a parallel factor of 32 is typically used to meet the 5G NR requirements [12], [18]. The overall data flow is illustrated in Fig. 1. First, during the initialization step (1), the input LLRs are written into the APP memory. As a 5G NR LDPC decoder employs QC-LDPC codes, the values in the APP memory must be cyclically shifted according to the shift values specified in the base graph. To support various lifting sizes in 5G NR,

this shifting is implemented using a QC-LDPC shift network (QSN) [19] consisting of two shifters and a merge network that combines their outputs. The data aligned by the QSN is then passed through a distributing network, which forwards the data from the APP memory to the 32 decoding function units (DFUs). Then, the node operations are performed in each DFU. The variable-node unit (VNU) generates V2C messages by combining the APP and C2V values according to (2). These messages are fed to the check-node update unit (CNU), which finds the two minima, MIN1 and MIN2, and the index of MIN1, IDX. The CNU then produces new C2V messages according to (3). Finally, the APP unit (APPU) uses the generated C2V and V2C values to compute updated APP values according to (4). A gathering network then returns the updated APP values to the corresponding APP memory.
III. PROPOSED METHOD
In a conventional LDPC decoder, a commonly used compromise for hardware-efficient implementations is to represent C2V messages with 5 bits and APP values with 7 bits [11], [12]. This configuration still incurs significant hardware overhead due to the wider APP bitwidth. In this section, we describe how to unify the datapath into a single 5-bit representation. In addition, we introduce a simple error-compensation method to suppress the resulting increase in decoding errors.
A. Saturation Management for APP Values
This brief proposes a novel node-processing method in which APP updates are disabled once saturation occurs, starting from a specific iteration. The proposed approach prevents the limited bitwidth from hindering the growth of correct information and from overemphasizing error components.
When the updated APP value is larger than the maximum APP value allowed in a specific representation, the correct C2V message is no longer reflected into the APP in (4).

In the subsequent iteration, the V2C message is computed according to (2) without accounting for saturation. In other words, the C2V message used in the previous iteration is simply subtracted, even though the C2V message has not been incorporated into the APP, leading to an incorrect message [15]. Such a loss of reliable messages facilitates the error amplification mechanism reported in [13]. In the mechanism, decoding failures in quantized LDPC decoders occur when certain small-magnitude LLR values repeatedly traverse specific node-processing cycles in the PCM and grow rapidly due to uncorrected sign errors. This phenomenon becomes more pronounced when the APP bitwidth is reduced, increasing the overall error floor. In this case, once the APP of a node becomes saturated at a certain iteration, disabling subsequent APP updates protects the correct information already embedded in the APP from being overdriven by errors. This disabling is implemented by forcing the C2V messages to zero in (2) and (4) whenever saturation is detected. Saturation is determined when the quantized APP value reaches the maximum or minimum value representable by the given APP bitwidth, which directly affects how early saturation occurs.
This effectively makes the saturated APP behave as if it had a larger representable dynamic range than that allowed by the limited bitwidth. Furthermore, this approach controls APP saturation without modifying C2V message update, making it applicable to different bitwidths and min-sum variants including the OMS algorithm.

B. Girth-Aware Update Termination

For a few beginning iterations, the APP values are usually updated with low reliability. Stopping the updates can degrade the decoding performance even when they reach their minimum or maximum in the early iterations. In case of error components, on the other hand, their values accumulate as they circulate along cycles in the PCM, and once they become sufficiently large, they begin to affect the APP values. Therefore, correct messages dominate before errors grow through the cycles, which means that applying the proposed method from the first iteration can degrade the decoding performance.
To overcome this issue, we determine iteration k based on the cycle structure of the PCM, from which the accumulation of errors is suppressed while allowing the APP values to be updated with sufficiently reliable messages. The update rules incorporating these stopping conditions are

PCM, where the girth represents the length of the shortest cycle in the PCM. Once an error enters the shortest cycle, it can immediately exert a harmful influence on the decoding process. Conservatively, correct messages can be reliably exchanged up to the iteration at which messages first propagate around the shortest cycle. Since a cycle consists of a sequence of edges and the flooding scheduling propagates messages across two edges per iteration, a message requires a number of iterations equal to half the girth to traverse the shortest cycle. In the layered scheduling, however, a message traverses a larger number of edges within a single iteration [20], which makes the shortest cycle form with fewer iterations than half the girth. In QC-LDPC codes, the PCM is generally designed with a girth of at least six [21]. Consequently, the shortest cycle is encountered at the third iteration under the flooding scheduling and typically at the first or second iteration under the layered scheduling.
In 5G NR LDPC codes, two columns in the core part are punctured, resulting in extremely small C2V values in the first iteration. This indicates that error propagation is negligible at this iteration, allowing the iteration from which the proposed method is applied to be relaxed. Therefore, since correct message exchange can generally be guaranteed for two or three iterations, it is reasonable to set k to 3 or 4.
Fig. 2 shows the FERs obtained by applying the proposed saturation method from a specific iteration, where the notation (a,b) represents the APP and C2V bitwidths, respectively. If the proposed saturation method is applied earlier than the fifth iteration, the FER improves. Applying it from the fifth iteration or later, however, leads to a sharp FER degradation at high Eb/N0, because larger input LLR magnitudes cause earlier APP saturation and more pronounced error propagation. This observation indicates that activating the proposed saturation mechanism from the fourth iteration is appropriate and consistent with the cycle analysis based on the girth.

An LDPC decoder is highly memory-dominated, and its memory is divided into the C2V memory and the APP memory. To support the diverse code rates defined in 5G NR, the APP memory consists of 26 memory banks for the core part and one shared memory bank for the extension part, denoted as APP0 to APP26 in Fig. 3. When a 7-bit APP bitwidth is used, the APP memory requires 182,784 bits, whereas reducing the APP bitwidth to 5 bits yields 130,560 bits, corresponding to roughly a 25% reduction in capacity.
IV. EVALUATION
The effectiveness of the proposed algorithm is evaluated by comparing the (7,5)-bit case [12], the (5,5)-bit case without the proposed method, and the (5,5)-bit case with the proposed method. This evaluation is performed for the 5G NR LDPC BG1 core part with a code rate of 11/12, a codeword length of 9216, and a message length of 8448. The normalization factor in the NMS algorithm is set to 0.8, and the maximum number of iterations is set to either 10 or 30. V. CONCLUSION This brief has proposed a new method to reduce the bitwidth of the datapath and APP memory in implementing a QC-LDPC decoder, which yields improvements in area and power efficiency. As an error-compensation mechanism, saturation management is incorporated by determining the starting iteration from which APP updates are disabled if the APP has become saturated. To determine the starting iteration, the girth of the PCM being considered is taken into account. The net effect of the proposed saturation method is to virtually increase the APP bitwidth, and thus to make the decoding performance remain almost identical to that of two-bit larger decoders. The additional circuits required to support the proposed saturation method are very small, so the bitwidth reduction pays off the additional overhead, leading to an 18% reduction in the logic area, a 27% reduction in the APP memory area and a 25% reduction in total power consumption.
