SPATIALLY-COUPLED OTFS SYSTEMS VIA BLOCK MARKOV SUPERPOSITION TRANSMISSION
Jiayi Yang1, Shuangyang Li2, Qianfan Wang3, Linqi Song3,4, Bo Ai5, Xiao Ma1, and Giuseppe Caire2

ABSTRACT
This paper investigates a novel spatially coupled orthogonal time frequency space (SC-OTFS) transmission scheme by integrating block Markov superposition transmission (BMST) with OTFS modulation. We first statistically analyze the distribution of detector mutual information across coded blocks and establish a decoding-threshold-based lower bound to characterize the asymptotic performance of conventional LDPC-coded OTFS systems. The analysis reveals that increasing the code length yields only marginal coding gains in typical doubly-selective channels with limited resolvable paths. Motivated by this observation, we propose an SCOTFS system in which a portion of each previously coded block is superimposed onto the current block and subsequently modulated using OTFS, thereby exploiting both coding gain and inter-block diversity gain in high-mobility channels. Furthermore, an extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) chart analysis is developed for the SC-OTFS system, which enables optimization of the critical superposition fraction based on decoding thresholds. Simulation results demonstrate that this optimization leads to a significant performance improvement, achieving more than 3 dB gain over conventional 5G LDPC-coded OTFS systems.
Index Terms— 5G, block Markov superposition transmission (BMST), LDPC codes, orthogonal time frequency space (OTFS), high-mobility channel.

Unlike OFDM, which operates in the time–frequency (TF) domain, OTFS modulation maps information symbols into the delay–Doppler (DD) domain. Through the inverse symplectic finite Fourier transform (ISFFT), each symbol is spread across the full bandwidth and frame duration, yielding potentially full diversity gain and improved error-correction performance [3].
To ensure reliable transmission, OTFS needs to be combined with powerful channel coding. Prior studies have explored several initial forms of integration. In [4], the performance of coded OTFS was analyzed, highlighting the tradeoff between diversity and coding gain. Space–time coding was introduced in OTFS [5], while a low-complexity detection algorithm for Polar-coded OTFS was proposed in [6]. Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, adopted in 5G [7], have also been incorporated into OTFS systems, and their error-correction performance has been evaluated in [8]. Despite these efforts, most existing works still adopt a separate design, applying block codes to OTFS without leveraging potential gains from joint design. Moreover, when the channel exhibits only a few resolvable paths, such as in mmWave transmissions [9], the inherent diversity of OTFS may be insufficient, necessitating retransmission. A practical approach to achieve efficient retransmission is block Markov superposition transmission (BMST) [10, 11, 12], which realizes retransmission through finite-field superposition, thereby providing coding gain while preserving spectral efficiency.

2. SPATIALLY COUPLED OTFS SYSTEMS WITH 5G LDPC CODES
2.1. Asymptotic Performance for LDPC Coded OTFS
Consider a coded OTFS system, as shown in Fig. 1. At the transmitter, the information bits u are first encoded by a channel encoder (e.g., LDPC), and then modulated, and reshaped into an M × N symbol matrix in the delay-Doppler (DD) domain. Through the inverse symplectic finite Fourier transform (ISFFT), the DD-domain symbols are mapped into the time-frequency (TF) domain, and the time-domain signal s(t) is generated via pulse shaping by gtx(t). The wireless channel is modeled in the DD domain as a sum of P resolvable paths with distinct delay and Doppler shifts.
At the receiver, the received signal passed through this channel is filtered and demodulated into TF-domain symbols,

algorithm (SPA) [18, 19, 20] as the OTFS detector. To cap-

evaluate the bit-wise MI between the transmitted bits and the

soft log-likelihood ratios (LLRs) generated by the SPA detec-

2.2. Transceiver Design of the SC-OTFS Systems

Consider an information sequence u = (u(0), . . . , u(L−1)) of L blocks, where each u(t) ∈ F2k. In the conventional coded OTFS system, codewords from different blocks are in-
dependent. If we further assume that the channel impulse re-
sponse is independent across blocks, consistent with the wide-

sense stationary uncorrelated scattering (WSSUS) assumption within the stationarity region [21], then, as indicated by our lower-bound analysis, the coding gain achievable by in-

dependent coded OTFS is quite limited.

imposed onto the codeword of the next time slot. Formally,

let α ∈ [0, 1] denote the superposition fraction. A selection

matrix S is constructed by masking n(1 − α) columns of a

random n × n permutation matrix, such that vS represents

an interleaved version of v with some bits set to zero. The

transmitted block at time t is given by

2.3. Design Optimization by EXIT Chart Analysis
To optimize the superposition fraction α of the proposed SCOTFS system, we adopt an EXIT chart analysis. However, due to the non-ergodic nature of OTFS-demodulated signals in high-mobility scenarios, the conventional EXIT analysis is not directly applicable. To address this, we estimate the PDF of the detector output MI (Fig. 2) and sample it to obtain the initial a priori MI. Specifically, L samples IE(i,)Det (0 ≤ i ≤ L − 1) are generated and assigned to the L layers as the initial messages IM→+, rather than feeding them directly to node LDPC . The subsequent protograph-based EXIT analysis follows the update schedule of the SWD over the d-layer normal graph in Fig. 4. 3. NUMERICAL RESULTS
Without loss of generality, we consider a BPSK modulation over doubly-selective channels with uniform power profile.
Example 2: Consider the SC-OTFS transmission scenario with a relative UE speed of 150 km/h and five independent resolvable paths (P = 5). We employ the 5G LDPC codes constructed from BG2 with a code length of 512 and a code rate of 1/2 as the component codes. The superposition fractions range from α = 0.3 to 0.7 with a coupling length of L = 100. At the receiver, the SWD with window size d = 4 is employed for recovery, with the SISO LDPC decoder limited to 50 iterations. Given a target FER = 10−3, we can obtain the required Eb/N0 by simulation. We can also obtain the decoding thresholds for different α by the proposed EXIT chart analysis. The comparison results are shown in Fig. 5, from which we observe that the trends of the simulation results are consistent with those of the analysis by the proposed EXIT chart for different superposition fractions α, and the optimized fraction is α = 0.6 for the decoding threshold and the required Eb/N0. The consistency between simulation and analysis confirms the effectiveness of the study.
Example 3: Consider the OTFS transmission scenario with a relative UE speed of 150 km/h and varying numbers of independent resolvable paths of P = 2, 3, and 4. For the proposed SC-OTFS system, we employ the 5G LDPC codes constructed from BG2 with a code length of 512 and a code rate of 1/2 as the component codes. The superposition fraction is α = 0.6 and the coupling length is L = 100. At the receiver, the SWD with d = 4 is performed for recovery. For comparison, the conventional coded OTFS employs the original 5G LDPC code with length 2080 and rate 1/2, so that the overall decoding latency is comparable to that of the SC-OTFS system. 4. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we proposed a novel SC-OTFS scheme by integrating the BMST framework with OTFS modulation. We analyzed the asymptotic performance of LDPC-coded OTFS and showed that increasing the code length yields only marginal gains in doubly-selective channels with limited resolvable paths. To overcome this, we designed an SC-OTFS scheme where part of each 5G LDPC-coded block is superimposed onto the subsequent block, enabling coding and inter-block diversity gains in high-mobility scenarios. An EXIT chart analysis was further developed to optimize the superposition fraction, and simulations demonstrated up to a 3 dB gain over conventional 5G LDPC-coded OTFS.
