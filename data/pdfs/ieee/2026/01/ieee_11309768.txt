BP-LCGCD: A Gaussian-Elimination-Free and High-Performance Decoder for Surface Codes
Qianfan Wang , Member, IEEE, Jifan Liang , Lvzhou Li , Linqi Song , Senior Member, IEEE, and Xiao Ma , Member, IEEE

Abstract— Belief propagation (BP) combined with ordered statistics decoding (OSD) can achieve near-optimal logical error rates for surface codes. However, OSD requires high-latency and unstable-complexity Gaussian elimination (GE), limiting its practicality. In this letter, we propose BP-LCGCD, a GEfree and high-performance decoder that replaces the GE-based OSD with the GE-free LC-GCD. Moreover, in contrast to the original BP-OSD, which adopts a single fixed normalization factor α, we further propose a list-parameterized variant, BP-LCGCD+α, which performs multiple BP decodings with different α to generate diverse posterior LLRs. We present complexity analysis, demonstrating that at low physical error rates, the average decoding complexity of the proposed algorithm approaches that of standard BP. Simulation results demonstrate that BP-LCGCD achieves logical error rates close to BP-OSD, while BP-LCGCD+α nearly matches the performance of the BPOSD. They also show that both proposed decoders significantly outperform standard BP and minimum-weight perfect matching (MWPM) in terms of logical error rate and threshold.
Index Terms— Locally constrained guessing codeword decoding (LC-GCD), logical error rate, OSD, surface codes.
I. INTRODUCTION
QUANTUM computing can transcend the informationprocessing limits of classical systems, offering exponential speedups for important tasks such as integer factorization and quantum simulation [1], [2], [3]. However, physical qubits, the core carriers of quantum computing, are highly susceptible to decoherence and operational errors. Quantum error-correction codes (QECCs) are thus essential for protecting physical qubits and realizing fault-tolerant quantum computation [4], [5].
Surface codes, a planar variant of Kitaev’s toric code, have emerged as one of the leading classes of QECCs due to

These results indicate that increasing the code distance systematically suppresses logical errors, reinforcing the promise of surface codes as a platform for fault-tolerant quantum computation.
For the decoding of the surface codes, the minimum-weight perfect matching (MWPM) is a widely used algorithm that solves a graph-matching problem to identify the error patterns, and achieves good performance [10], [11]. Other graphmatching-based decoders have also been explored, such as spanning tree matching decoders [12] or almost-linear-time (or linear-time) decoders [13], [14], which reduce complexity compared to MWPM at the cost of some performance loss. Alternatively, BP decoding, originally developed for classical LDPC codes, has also been adapted to surface codes [15], [16], [17]. While BP offers low complexity and low latency, it suffers from significant performance loss compared to MWPM.
To mitigate this loss, OSD has been applied as a post-processor to BP, resulting in the original BP-OSD algorithm (hereafter referred to as BP-OSD) [18]. In this framework, BP is performed at the pre-processing stage to provide soft information, enabling OSD to perform re-encoding based on the most reliable basis (MRB). Empirically, BP-OSD significantly outperforms both MWPM (as well as almostlinear-time decoders) and BP decoder under depolarizing channels, especially at low physical error rate [18], [19], [20], [21]. However, OSD requires Gaussian elimination (GE) to extract the MRB, which introduces unpredictable latency and incurs O(n3) time and O(n2) memory complexity [22], rendering it unsuitable for the low-latency requirements of quantum error correction systems.
A key question thus arises: can we eliminate GE yet retaining near-optimal decoding performance? The answer is positive and can be achieved by the LC-GCD algorithm [23], [24], which circumvents GE by a fixed basis and preserves decoding performance by the locally-constrained searches. In this work, we take the LC-GCD as the post-processor to BP, resulting in a GE-free decoder, called BP-LCGCD. Moreover, the normalization factor in NMS significantly affects the posterior LLRs and decoding performance, but original BP-OSD uses only a single fixed α, which constrains its search space. To address this, we further propose a parameterlistized enhancement, called BP-LCGCD+α, to improve the performance. II. PRELIMINARIES
A. Stabilizer Codes and Surface Codes
Let Gn = {±1, ±i} × {I , X, Y, Z}⊗n be the n-qubit Pauli group, where I , X, Y , and Z are the basic Pauli operators. Let C [[n, k, d]] denote a stabilizer code, which encodes k logical qubits into n physical qubits with distance d, correcting up to ⌊(d − 1)/2⌋ errors. Stabilizer code C is specified by an abelian subgroup S = ⟨S1, . . . , Sm⟩ ⊂ Gn, m ≥ n − k, called the stabilizer group [4]. Define the stabilizer matrix S of size m × n, where each row corresponds to a stabilizer generator Si. The code space is the 2k-dimensional subspace
C = {|ψ⟩ : Si|ψ⟩ = |ψ⟩, ∀Si ∈ S}.
For decoding, each Si is measured to obtain syndrome bits si ∈ F2, after which an error pattern is inferred from s = Fm 2 .
A planar surface code [[2d2 − 2d + 1, 1, d]] is realized on a d − 1 order square lattice (see Fig. 1(a)). Physical qubits reside on edges, and there are two types of stabilizer: i) Xtype on each face f : Xf = e∈∂f Xe, and ii) Z-type on each vertex v: Zv = e: v∈∂e Ze, where ∂f is the set of edges around face f , and ∂e is the two endpoints of edge e. All X- and Z-type stabilizers commute, i.e., [Xf , Xf′ ] = [Zv, Zv′ ] = [Xf , Zv] = 0. Hence, all generators can be measured simultaneously [25]. As a special class of quantum LDPC codes, the surface code can also be represented by a Tanner graph (I, J , E): variable nodes I (|I| = n) for qubits, check nodes J (|J | = m) for stabilizer generators, and an edge (i, j) ∈ E if stabilizer Sj acts nontrivially on qubit i (Fig. 1(b)).

In the low physical error-rate regime, Phase I succeeds with high probability, making η1 = Pr{Phase II is invoked} ≪ 1. Consequently, the average complexity is dominated by the single BP run in Phase I, approaching O(n T1,avg), while the contribution from Phase II becomes negligible. For example, at p ≈ 10−3 on the tested codes we observe η1 ≈ 10−4, i.e., Phase II (LC-GCD) is invoked roughly once in 104 trials. Note that the same conclusion applies to BP-OSD, whose post-processing is invoked only upon a Phase I failure. In the worst case, we have η1 = η2 = 1 and ℓ, T1, T2 taking their maximum values in (3), so that the total decoding complexity is governed by the corresponding worst-case bounds on the BP and LC-GCD stages. Also note that, because the worst-case complexity of the post-processing scales exponentially with the code dimension k, the proposed approach may become impractical for higher-rate QLDPC codes.

V. CONCLUSION
We have proposed BP-LCGCD, a GE-free list decoder for surface codes, which replaces the OSD module in BP-OSD with LC-GCD, thereby eliminating online GE. To further enhance decoding performance, we introduced BP-LCGCD+α, which performs multiple BP decodings with distinct normalization factors and applies LC-GCD to generate candidates. We also presented a complexity analysis, showing that the proposed decoding algorithm can achieve complexity comparable to standard BP decoding under low physical error rates. Numerical results show that the proposed decoders achieve logical error rates and thresholds nearly identical to those of BP-OSD, while significantly outperforming BP and MWPM. As a promising direction for future work, we plan to evaluate BP-LCGCD under more realistic noise models, including phenomenological and circuit-level models [13], [35], [36], to assess robustness to circuit-induced correlations and leakage.
