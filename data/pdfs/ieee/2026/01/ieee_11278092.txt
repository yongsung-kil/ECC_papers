ABSTRACT This article proposes a novel low-complexity syndrome-based linear programming (SB-LP) decoding algorithm for decoding quantum low-density parity-check codes. Under the code-capacity model, the SB-LP decoder can be used as a standalone decoder; however, it is particularly powerful when used as a postprocessing step following SB min-sum (SB-MS) decoding. In the latter case, the proposed decoder is shown to be capable of significantly reducing the error floor of the SB-MS decoder for both flooded and layered SB-MS scheduling. Also, an early stopping criterion is introduced to decide when to activate the SB-LP algorithm, avoiding executing a predefined maximum number of iterations for the SB-MS decoder. Simulation results show, for some example hypergraph and generalized bicycle (GB) codes, that the proposed decoder can lower the error floor by one to three orders of magnitude compared to SB-MS for the same total number of decoding iterations. Furthermore, for the class of GB codes, it is shown that as the minimum distance of the code increases, the logical error rate provided by the proposed decoder also improves, indicating that the solution is scalable. Under the circuit-level noise model, it is shown that while the SB-LP decoder does not fully replace the need for ordered statistics decoding (OSD) when flooded SB-MS is used as a preliminary step, it reduces the number of calls to the OSD postprocessor, which directly impacts the overall latency. In addition, the method offers a syndrome-matching decoder and improves the accuracy of the logical error rate for bivariate bicycle codes of distances 6 to 18, particularly at low error rates, when compared to the belief propagation+OSD benchmark.
INDEX TERMS Linear programming-based decoding, quantum low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, quantum error correction.

To reduce the effects of noise, many new codes and decoders for quantum error correction have been designed over the past few decades [5]. Surface codes represent one of the first approaches proposed for fault tolerance, thanks to their high error threshold and their implementation using simple 2-D connectivity. This makes them well-suited for a wide range of hardware platforms, and many small surface codes have been experimentally demonstrated [7].
While surface codes remain a central solution for faulttolerant quantum computing, they do have limitations when it comes to protecting a large number of logical qubits. Due to their low code rate, they require a significant number of physical qubits, leading to high overhead. However, other codes with lower redundancy and cost can be combined to offer more efficient solutions [8]. Surface codes also require complex decoding algorithms, such as the minimum-weight perfect matching algorithm of [9], which has a worst-case complexity of O(N3). Although this decoding complexity can be close to linear on average, matching-based decoders are only applicable to a small subset of stabilizer codes. This complexity can be reduced to O(N2) using local matching, but at the cost of reduced error correction performance [10], [11]. To achieve a shorter decoding time, lowcomplexity decoders, such as the weighted growth unionfind [12] and bubble clustering [13] algorithms have been proposed, trading off some logical error performance for improved speed. For more detailed information on surface code decoders, see [14], which provides an in-depth discussion of state-of-the-art decoders for this particular family of codes.
As an alternative with a higher code rate, quantum lowdensity parity-check (QLDPC) codes offer benefits by reducing the qubit overhead1 and also improving the minimum distance [16], [17], [18]. Despite their potential, there are still open problems, such as the fault-tolerant implementation of the encoding process on the quantum side and the design of efficient decoders that can obtain very low logical error rates [19]. This article will focus on addressing the latter limitation.
One of the main problems in performing efficient decoding for a QLDPC code is the existence of short cycles within the code’s Tanner graph, which result in an error floor when belief propagation (BP)-based decoders are applied. This error floor effect means that an improvement in the physical error rate of the qubits does not translate into an improvement in the logical error rate [20]. For the first category of approach, some more powerful decoding techniques, such as the syndrome-based (SB) generalized BP (GBP) algorithm [21] have been proposed to improve the decoding performance. Unfortunately, these require a high computational complexity, so additional simplifications are necessary to obtain practical real-time solutions. It has also been proposed to use different scheduling schemes within the message-passing algorithm to optimize the sequence of operations, as discussed in [22]. For instance, in layered scheduling the check nodes (CNs) are divided into groups (called layers) and each group is processed sequentially, allowing for more frequent updates and significantly speeding up the convergence of the algorithm. Some neural network-based decoders for QLDPC codes have also been explored [1], [2], [23]. However, training and deploying these algorithms for large QLDPC codes can be computationally intensive and resource-demanding when the number of qubits grows. It also suffers from early error floors and requires a large number of iterations.
For the second category, ordered statistics decoding (OSD) was proposed in [24] as a postprocessing technique for BP (the combination decoder is referred to as BP+OSD, or as BP+OSD-0 when the order-0 version of OSD with lowest complexity is implemented). By seamlessly integrating OSD when BP decoding fails, this technique can significantly enhance the error correction capability for some specific QLDPC codes, providing improved error rates after postprocessing. However, OSD decoders are computationally complex and introduce an extremely large latency due to the requirement, in one of the steps, of performing Gaussian elimination for the inversion of a dynamic submatrix of the code’s parity-check matrix; thus, an efficient hardware implementation that can finish decoding within the required time interval (of hundreds of nanoseconds to several microseconds) is not feasible [25].
Stabilizer inactivation (SI) decoding for QLDPC codes was also recently introduced in [26] as an alternative postprocessing method. When combined with min-sum (MS) decoding, a BP-based approach, it forms the MS-SI decoder. This was shown to outperform OSD in lowering the error floor while also reducing the decoding complexity. However, it is a list decoding method (the list being computed according to the BP output), the complexity of which grows with the length of the list. Serial processing of the list can lead to information loss due to the short decoherence time, while parallel processing would require high power consumption. Similar to the SI algorithm, proposed in [27], the checkagnosia algorithm functions as a postprocessing technique that identifies unreliable qubits without inactivating them. By keeping the qubits active, the decoder can iteratively refine its error correction strategy based on evolving reliability assessments. However, this requires multiple rounds of decoding attempts, increasing the overall computational

Another postprocessing technique, proposed in [28], involves the use of BP-guided decimation (BPGD). This strategy sequentially freezes the most reliable qubits, determined by their soft information, after a specific number of BP iterations. While this technique can improve convergence and decoding accuracy, the iterative freezing of qubits in BPGD requires additional iterations in order to determine which qubits to freeze and when to freeze them. The sequential freezing process also adds complexity to the decoding algorithm. This highlights the importance of continuing the search for alternative decoding techniques.
Besides these previous algorithms, linear programming (LP)-based decoding has also been recently explored to derive decoders for QLDPC codes. As LP decoding proved to be capable of reducing the error floor for classical LDPC codes [29], some works, such as [30] and [31] adapted classical LP decoding algorithms to QLDPC codes in order to obtain lower error floors. These quantum adaptations transform the decoding problem into an optimization problem where error patterns are evaluated based on their probabilities, subject to quantum stabilizer constraints. While these approaches offer some mathematical performance guarantees and demonstrate promise in error floor reduction, they introduce significant hurdles for practical implementation. The computational overhead associated with solving linear programs through methods, such as simplex or interior point algorithms results in a complexity that scales polynomially with the code length [32], [33]. For this reason, the latency introduced by solving the LP at each decoding iteration makes these algorithms impractical for real-time quantum error correction applications where rapid decoding is essential.
Against this background, the main contributions of this article are summarized as follows.
1) A low-complexity iterative SB-LP decoder is proposed for decoding QLDPC codes. For the code-capacity model, SB-LP can be used as a standalone decoding algorithm or as a postprocessing step after SB-MS decoding to improve the behavior in the error floor region and the threshold of the code.
2) We propose an early stopping criterion for the SB-MS decoder based on the syndrome Hamming distance. We provide insights into the operation of this early stopping criterion, which enables automatic detection of misconvergence in the SB-MS decoder and uses this to trigger the SB-LP post-processing.
3) We demonstrate through simulations that under the code-capacity model, the proposed combination of SBMS with SB-LP improves the error floor with lower complexity and without the need for list decoding as required by SI or solving a dynamic system of equations with Gaussian elimination as in OSD. This gain in performance is observed for both flooded-schedule and layered-schedule implementations of the SB-MS

II. BACKGROUND This section introduces stabilizer codes, Calderbank–Shor– Steane (CSS) codes, and QLDPC codes. It also summarizes the error models utilized in our work. It provides a brief overview of the SB-MS decoder, detailing the two relevant scheduling approaches; this is a de facto standard for lowcomplexity BP-based decoding. Throughout this discussion, all vectors are assumed to be row vectors.
A. STABILIZER CODES The n-fold Pauli group, denoted by Pn, is comprised of all n-fold tensor products of the single-qubit Pauli operators (I, X, Y, Z), which are essential in quantum computing as they represent fundamental quantum gates. Bit-flips are described by Pauli X operators, phase-flips are described by Pauli Z

operators, and combinations of bit-flip and phase-flip operations are described by Pauli Y operators [36].
Within this framework, an Abelian subgroup S of Pn is defined, called the stabilizer, together with a set of m = n − k independent generators of this subgroup. The corresponding [[n, k, d]] stabilizer code encodes k logical qubits into n physical qubits, where d represents the code’s minimum distance [37]; the code space consists of all quantum states that are (+1)-eigenstates of each stabilizer generator. Thus, the stabilizer generators maintain a specific 2k-dimensional subspace of the 2n-dimensional Hilbert space invariant.
The behavior of errors in a quantum processor can be described using a depolarizing noise model, where the error probabilities associated with Pauli X, Pauli Y, and Pauli Z operators are represented by pX, pY, and pZ, respectively. X and Z errors are represented by the length-n binary error vectors eX and eZ, respectively, in which the jth entry is equal to 1 in the presence of a corresponding error on the jth qubit, and is equal to 0 if there is no such error. Each stabilizer generator is capable of detecting a certain type of error, and if well-designed, they can together provide powerful error correction capabilities.
CSS codes are a subset of quantum stabilizer codes constructed by combining two classical linear codes, one designed to correct X errors, and the other to correct Z errors. These classical codes are utilized to create the stabilizer generators for the quantum code. A distinctive feature of CSS codes is that, in the tensor product representation of each stabilizer generator, the nonidentity components are always either all X or all Z. Thus, CSS codes can be characterized by two m × n binary parity-check matrices HX and HZ; the corresponding length-m binary syndrome vectors sX = eXHTZ and sZ = eZHTX are measured from the quantum system and can be used to correct X and Z errors, respectively [16]. In this work, we consider quantum low-density CSS codes, in which the HX and HZ matrices are sparse, thus allowing the use of iterative message-passing algorithms for decoding. To simplify the analysis, decoding using HX and HZ are treated separately as X and Z errors are considered to be independent.
In the context of the code-capacity model, we assume a simplified scenario in which only data qubits are subject to stochastic noise (i.e., depolarizing), while all other components (gates, measurements, ancilla states) are considered to be ideal. This abstraction facilitates theoretical analysis and decoder benchmarking, though it does not capture the full complexity of physical implementations.
By contrast, the circuit-level noise model provides a more realistic representation by introducing faults at all possible locations in the circuit, including gates, measurements, ancilla preparations, and idle qubits. Under this model, the same CSS decoding framework applies; however, as more noisy locations are considered, the effective error vector must be expanded to account for additional fault sources, and the associated parity-check matrices become more complex, reflecting the increased connectivity and correlations
2100319

introduced by the noise. To represent this type of noise, a detector error model is typically employed [38], capturing how physical faults propagate to detection events observed during syndrome extraction.
For ease of exposition, in the rest of this article, we will focus on the correction of only one type of error, and we will use the simpler notations H, e, and s in place of HZ, eX, and sX, respectively.
In all of the following sections except Sections III-D and V-G, we consider the code-capacity model, such that each qubit experiences an independent depolarizing error probability p and we also have pX = pY = pZ = p/3. For Sections III-D and V-G, we assume circuit-level noise with the parameters defined in [39].
B. TANNER GRAPH The Tanner graph serves as a graphical representation of the m × n parity-check matrix H = (Hi j ). This graph consists of n variable nodes (VNs) and m CNs, connected by a set of edges E. For each i ∈ {1, 2, . . . , m} and j ∈ {1, 2, . . . , n}, the ith CN is connected to the jth VN by an edge (i.e., (i, j) ∈ E) if and only if Hi j = 1. Ni denotes the set of neighbors of the ith CN, while N j denotes the set of neighbors of the jth VN. For ease of exposition, we assume that the Tanner graph is regular, i.e., all CNs have the same degree dc and all VNs have the same degree dv. In the QLDPC decoding context, VNs correspond to entries of the estimated error pattern e = (e1 e2 · · · en) while CNs correspond to the entries of the syndrome vector s = (s1 s2 · · · sm).
C. SYNDROME-BASED MIN-SUM (SB-MS) ALGORITHM BP decoding, first applied to quantum error correction by MacKay et al. [18], works as an iterative message-passing algorithm on the code’s Tanner graph. In this process, messages are iteratively exchanged between VNs and CNs to refine probability estimates. The key characteristic of the BP decoder is its use of soft decisions, which incorporate probabilistic information, as opposed to hard decisions that rely on binary information. However, the computational complexity of the BP decoder arises from its CN operations, which require hyperbolic tangent (tanh) and inverse hyperbolic tangent (tanh−1) operations [28]. To reduce this computational burden, the SB-MS algorithm removes these complex CN operations, replacing them with an approximation involving the minimum operation. While this approach reduces precision, it offers significant computational savings, making SB-MS an efficient alternative in resource-constrained scenarios [40], [41].
Algorithm 1 presents the SB-MS decoder with the flooded schedule, which enables a high degree of parallelism. Algorithm 2 describes the layered-schedule SB-MS decoder as an alternative, which can improve the convergence and decoding performance.

III. PROPOSED METHOD LP-based decoding, originally proposed for classical LDPC codes in [29], relies on a linear programming relaxation approach to achieve a high-performance decoding solution with theoretical guarantees on performance. In [43], Vontobel and Koetter proposed low-complexity iterative decoding algorithms that exploited the unique structure of the linear program to be solved (in particular, exploiting the sparsity of H). In the following, we propose an LP-based decoder which is based on modifying [43, Algorithm 1] to align with the unique features and requirements of QLDPC codes. The algorithm in [43] is based on formulating the decoding problem as an LP problem, and then solving a “softened” version of the dual LP via an iterative coordinate-ascentbased method. One key difference between this method and conventional message-passing algorithms is that while both methodologies have low complexity due to their use of local message updates on the code’s Tanner graph, in each iteration the algorithm in [38] directly updates the a posteriori LLRs, instead of passing extrinsic LLRs which, in the case of quantum codes, can cause oscillatory behavior in the decoder due to information feedback occurring through the many short cycles present in the Tanner graph.

Fig. 1 shows an example of the evolution of the number of unmatched syndrome elements through successive iterations for the SB-MS decoder (with both scheduling approaches) as well as for the SB-LP decoder. An oscillatory pattern is observed during SB-MS decoding when the decoder becomes stuck in a trapping set, indicating that it struggles to converge to an error pattern matching the measured syndrome, regardless of the scheduling approach used. In contrast, the proposed SB-LP decoder can converge in such cases; however, a high number of iterations is required. The oscillatory behavior exhibited by the SB-MS decoder is due to the propagation of extrinsic information around short cycles in the Tanner graph. This information feedback mechanism leads to quasi-periodic oscillations when many short cycles are present. On the other hand, the SB-LP algorithm mitigates this issue by simply refining the a posteriori LLR estimate in each iteration, avoiding such oscillatory effects.
From Fig. 1 we can conclude that operating the SB-MS decoder for a fixed number of iterations is not an efficient approach; instead, the SB-MS decoding procedure should terminate when such oscillatory behavior is detected. Note that while this approach is shown here to be effective for BP-based decoders with postprocessing, it may potentially be applicable to other decoding schemes as well. In Algorithm 4, we terminate the SB-MS decoding when the Hamming distance dH(sˆ, sˆpre) between the current syndrome estimate sˆ and the previous syndrome estimate sˆpre is less than or equal to the VN degree dv. The Hamming distance dH(sˆ, sˆpre) between consecutive estimated syndrome vectors reflects how much the decoder is altering the estimated syndrome vector on each iteration. When this metric becomes less than or equal to the variable node degree dv, it indicates that the decoder is making minimal changes in the number of unmatched syndrome elements. The reason for this choice of threshold is that the estimation of the error vector occurs at the VNs, while the syndrome vector is determined at the
2100319

CNs, which are directly influenced by the VNs (specifically, up to dv syndrome vector entries per VN). When only a single error vector element is flipped between consecutive iterations, its impact is restricted to the CNs connected to that particular VN, altering at most dv syndrome elements. By setting the stopping threshold to dv, the algorithm effectively detects when the decoder is making minimal changes (i.e., only flipping one error vector element per iteration), thus preventing the oscillations typically observed in SB-MS decoding. This threshold ensures a good balance between stopping the iterations early enough to avoid unnecessary computations while still providing the SB-LP decoder with a high-quality initialization. This, in turn, reduces the required number of decoding iterations and improves the logical error rate. Indeed, we found that, at p = 0.05, increasing the threshold value by 1 (i.e., to dv + 1) causes the decoder to fail 3.3 times more frequently compared to a threshold value of dv.
It is worth mentioning that this early stopping criterion can be applied when using the SB-MS decoder (with either scheduling approach) in conjunction with any other postprocessing technique, such as OSD or SI, to activate the postprocessing exactly when required and reduce the total number of average decoding iterations. Also note that increasing the maximum number of iterations remains compatible with our proposal and can indeed improve the number of convergent cases [44], as we can set a large maximum number of iterations (e.g., 10 000) while also implementing an oscillation detector to terminate SB-MS early when convergence is unlikely.
D. ADAPTATION OF THE SB-LP DECODER TO THE DETECTOR ERROR MODEL In this section, we describe how the SB-LP algorithm can be adapted to work with the detector error model of [38]. This adaptation is necessary because the degrees of the CNs are significantly larger in the detector error model compared to the code-capacity model. This increase in CN degrees would lead to an exponential increase in the complexity encountered in Steps 6 to 12 of Algorithm 3. In addition, we have introduced a modification of the soft a priori information provided to the SB-LP algorithm from the SB-MS algorithm, incorporating two new heuristic parameters (δ0 and δ1) to tailor the processing to the graph for the case of circuit-level noise. We also optimized the scaling factors for both algorithms, αBP and αLP, to minimize the logical error rate, as will be discussed in Section V-G.
Algorithm 3 was originally designed for decoding under the code-capacity model, which typically involves lowdegree CNs. In this context, the sets Bi and Ci contain a manageable number of elements, 2dc , making the maximization steps computationally feasible. E. IMPACT OF MINIMUM DISTANCE Typically, an increase in the minimum distance of a quantum error correction code enhances the error correction capabilities of the code. To explore whether this property can be exploited by our proposed decoder, we investigate its performance using GB codes with different minimum distances. Fig. 8 shows the performance of the SB-MS decoder, both alone and when combined with SB-LP, as the code’s minimum distance increases. It can be observed that the number of physical qubits used for encoding logical qubits increases, the SB-MS decoder fails to exhibit an improvement in error rate. This is due to the fact that codes with a high number of physical qubits have Tanner graphs with more short cycles, and thus a higher trapping set density. As the code dimensions increase and the number of trapping sets grows, the SB-MS algorithm encounters challenges in accurately converging to the correct syndrome. This is evident in the comparison between T4 and T5 codes, where T4 (smaller code length) has a logical error rate of 10−2, while T5 (greater code length) has a higher error rate of 10−1, at p = 0.05. In contrast, the SB-MS decoder combined with the SB-LP decoder showcases a significant improvement in the logical error rate as the block length of the code increases. At p = 0.05, this combination reduces the logical error rate from 10−2 to 10−4 for T4, and from 10−1 to 10−5 for T5. This improvement is due to the SB-LP decoder’s ability to avoid becoming stuck in the code’s trapping sets, effectively tackling the oscillation issues encountered in the SB-MS decoder.
F. COMPARISON WITH BENCHMARK DECODERS 1) PERFORMANCE In Figs. 9 and 10, we compare the performance of our proposed decoder with that of established benchmarks from literature, including the BP+OSD [24], MS-SI [26], and

BP+OSD executes the SB-MS algorithm for 100 iterations (with scaling factor αBP = 0.625) before OSD postprocessing. It is important to note that the OSD algorithm has a complexity of O(n3) in the worst case, where n represents the number of columns in H; this will limit the latency that can be achieved in hardware implementations and can become a bottleneck for certain quantum technologies. The MS-SI decoder is configured with λ = 10, where λ represents the number of inactivated stabilizers (here 1 ≤ λ ≤ m, where m represents the number of rows in H). It executes the SBMS algorithm with random layer ordering for 100 iterations (with scaling factor αBP = 0.9) before postprocessing (here λ = 10 is chosen since further increases do not significantly improve the logical error rate). The BPGD algorithm operates in r rounds (where 1 ≤ r ≤ n), executing a maximum of T iterations of BP decoding per VN in each round. In our simulations, T = 10 was chosen as higher values did not significantly reduce the logical error rate for p > 0.075 and r = n. This approach requires nT iterations in the worst case, resulting in 8820 iterations for the B1 code and 19 220 iterations for the C2 code. In contrast, our proposed decoder uses 100 iterations of layered SB-MS decoding (with random layer ordering to ensure a fair comparison) followed by 100 iterations of SB-LP decoding. As shown in Figs. 9 and 10, our proposed decoder shows an improvement in the logical error rate compared to the BP+OSD decoder, exhibiting a decrease by a factor of approximately 2.6 for the B1 code and 3.7 for the C2 code at a depolarizing error probability of 0.09. On the other hand, the performance of the MS-SI and BPGD decoders is slightly better than that of the proposed algorithm at lower depolarizing error probabilities. This new three-stage hierarchical architecture, SBMS+SB-LP+OSD-0, follows the workflow illustrated in Fig. 12. The process is executed as follows.
1) SB-MS Stage: The decoding process begins with a low-complexity BP algorithm, which is effective for resolving the most straightforward error cases. The BP decoder is initialized with the prior probabilities from the circuit-level noise model.
2) SB-LP Stage: If the SB-MS decoder fails to find a valid error vector or terminates due to the early stopping criterion described in Sections V-D and V-E, the SB-LP decoding stage is triggered. In this case, the a priori LLRs are initialized using a weighted combination of the LLRs derived from the error probabilities of the detector error model and the final LLRs output by the SB-MS stage, as detailed in Section III-D. To assess the impact of the proposed early stopping method (Section V-D), we compare the performance of SBMS + SB-LP decoding without early stopping (labeled “without ES” in the figure legends) and with early stopping enabled (“with ES”). In addition, to evaluate the effect of the proposed method, we compare decoding over the full detector error model graph to decoding over the graph after applying the graph sparsification techniques introduced in [47], denoted as “ST” in the legends. This is performed to show that even without sparsification, the early stopping mechanism is able to identify the loops and obtain a similar accuracy.
3) Postprocessing Stage: If the SB-LP decoder also fails to converge, a postprocessing algorithm with higher accuracy is invoked as the final decoding step. For our experiments, OSD-0 was implemented, but this approach can also be applied to any other postprocessing technique for circuit-level noise, such as ambiguity clustering [44], localized statistics decoding [48], or even decision-tree decoders combined with BP [49] to ensure that the final decoder will be syndrome

VI. CONCLUSION In this article, we have introduced a novel low-complexity SB-LP decoder for QLDPC codes. Under the code-capacity noise model, the proposed SB-LP decoder can operate either as a standalone decoder or as a postprocessing stage following SB-MS with either flooded or layered schedules. Under circuit-level noise, SB-LP is most effective when applied after SB-MS, where it provides a clear advantage by reducing the number of OSD calls and thereby lowering decoding complexity. For both noise models, we proposed an early-stopping criterion for SBMS based on the syndrome Hamming distance, enabling a more efficient transition between the MS and LP decoding stages.
Our complexity analysis shows that the proposed decoder achieves performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods at a significantly lower computational cost. In particular, unlike BP+OSD, the combined SB-MS+SB-LP+OSD approach is able to lower the error floor while maintaining reduced complexity. In the circuit-level noise setting, our primary goal has been to demonstrate how incorporating SB-LP improves OSD performance and reduces complexity; however, we expect similar benefits when combining SB-LP with other recently proposed postprocessing and graph-searchbased decoding techniques. Many such methods—e.g., those described in [44], [48], [51], and [47]—rely on BP as a preprocessor and can naturally integrate an LP stage for further gains. Even decision-tree decoders, such as [49] may benefit from LP to accelerate convergence.
Although our results for larger distance codes under circuit-level noise are encouraging, they are not yet competitive with the best existing decoders, indicating that further improvements are needed. Extending SB-LP techniques to higher distance QLDPC codes and achieving stronger performance in realistic noise settings therefore remains an important direction for future research. Promising avenues include exploring new SB-LP formulations, integrating LP
