ABSTRACT We investigate the design of two neural network (NN) architectures recently proposed as decoders for forward error correction: the so-called single-label NN (SLNN) and multi-label NN (MLNN) decoders. These decoders have been reported to achieve near-optimal codeword- and bit-wise performance, respectively. Results in the literature show near-optimality for a variety of short codes. In this paper, we analytically prove that certain SLNN and MLNN architectures can, in fact, always realize optimal decoding, regardless of the code. These optimal architectures and their binary weights are shown to be defined by the codebook, i.e., no training or network optimization is required. Our proposed architectures are in fact not NNs, but implement the maximum likelihood decoding rule differently. Optimal performance is numerically demonstrated for Hamming (7, 4), Polar (16, 8), and BCH (31, 21) codes. The results show that our optimal architectures (which are not NNs) are less complex than the SLNN and MLNN architectures proposed in the literature, which in fact only achieve near-optimal performance. Extension to longer codes is still hindered by the curse of dimensionality. Therefore, even though SLNN and MLNN can perform maximum likelihood decoding, such architectures cannot be used for medium and long codes.

Historically, NNs were first considered for decoding FEC codes in the early 1990s, e.g., in [24]. NN decoders were deemed suitable only for very short codes (n < 100) due to the curse of dimensionality [24], [25]. Even considering the recent surge in NN decoders proposed in the literature, starting with [26], we see from the comprehensive survey [17] and references therein that the codes considered for NN-based decoding are almost always limited to lengths below n = 512.
Recently, several decoder designs based on transformer architectures have been proposed. The latest examples of these decoders are the ones in [27], [28], [29], [30], [31], [32], [33], and [34]. While these works report promising performance, a careful examination reveals that their evaluations are often limited to comparisons against other transformerbased decoders. Moreover, in cases where classical decoders are included, widely adopted algorithms for specific code families are frequently omitted. For example, BP is used to decode BCH codes in [31], [32], and [34] instead of BDD or ordered-statistics decoding (OSD) [35]. Likewise, BP is employed for polar codes in [34] in place of SC or SC list decoding. Consequently, based on comparisons against classical decoders, we concluded in our earlier work [36] that transformer-based decoders generally remain less competitive than classical decoders, except in scenarios involving ultra-short blocklengths [33] or unconventional channel models [30].
More recently, single-label NN (SLNN) and multi-label NN (MLNN) decoders have been introduced [37], [38], [39], [40]. These decoders have a fully-connected architecture, with a few hidden layers. SLNN and MLNN decoders have been shown in [40] to provide codeword- and bit-wise near-optimal performance, respectively, for a variety of short (n < 34) FEC codes. The results in [40] were obtained by training the NNs at a fixed signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), and then, using the same NN for a wide range of SNRs. The NN architecture of these decoders does not rely on the knowledge of the specific code structure, i.e., they are model-free, and therefore, in principle, applicable to any code. Furthermore, these decoders are noniterative.
The computational complexity of the SLNN and MLNN decoders becomes infeasible for large input lengths k. To alleviate this problem, the iterative network pruning approach proposed in [41] has been used to prune weights in the SLNN and MLNN architectures in [42]. With this approach, the complexity of the SLNN and MLNN decoders has been reduced by up to 97 % without compromising the decoding performance [42, Sec. 6]. The reduction in complexity has been demonstrated for the Hamming (7, 4) and Polar (16, 8) codes. SLNN and MLNN with weight

pruning have the potential to be scalable for longer codes, without significantly sacrificing performance [42].
For the aforementioned reasons, at first glance, SLNN and MLNN decoders seem to be a very interesting alternative for decoding short codes. However, only heuristic rules for the design of these decoders exist in the literature. For example, it is unclear how many hidden layers or neurons per layer these decoders should use for different codes. Furthermore, to the best of our knowledge, the performance of SLNN and MLNN has not been rigorously analyzed in the literature, and no comparisons to traditional decoders exist.
In our earlier work [36], we studied the design and performance of SLNN and MLNN decoders. We proved analytically in [36, Theorem 1] and [36, Theorem 2], and that for certain non-fully-connected architectures with up to a single hidden layer, SLNN and MLNN decoders realize codeword- and bit-wise optimal decoding, respectively. The weights of these optimal architectures are binary and fully described by the codebook, i.e., no training is in fact required. Thus, these optimal architectures are not actually NNs but graphical representations of maximum likelihood decoding. Finally, we showed that already for blocklength n = 31, the NNs previously proposed in the literature are not competitive with respect to traditional (e.g., BDD) decoders. In this paper, we extend our study of SLNN and MLNN decoders in [36]. Our first contribution is to provide a more detailed discussion around the optimality analysis based on [36, Theorem 1] and [36, Theorem 2]. The second contribution is to extend our investigations to include a simulation study using polar codes which are among the most popular nowadays. Finally, our third contribution is a quantitative complexity analysis (see Table 1, ahead) to demonstrate that our optimal non-NN architectures are significantly less complex than the nonoptimal SLNN and MLNN decoders used in [37], [38], [39], and [40]. These optimal architectures might find applications in machine-type and ultra-low-latency communications to decode short blocklength codes [43], [44].
This paper is organized as follows. We provide preliminary information in Sec. II. SLNN and MLNN decoders are described and studied in Sec. III. Conclusions are given in Sec. IV.

C. SCALABILITY TO LONGER CODES As we discussed in Sec. I, scalability of NN decoders for longer FEC codes is hindered by the curse of dimensionality, i.e., the size of networks that provide adequate performance increases exponentially with k. This is indeed the case for the SLNN and MLNN decoders as they both have at least one layer where the number of neurons grows exponentially with k. Here, we study the performance of NNs defined in Theorems 1 and 2 for the (16, 8) polar code considered in [26] and for the (31, 21) BCH code considered in [40]. The properties of the corresponding NNs are given in Table 1. We see from Fig. 8 again that the NNs defined in Theorems 1 and 2 realize codeword- and bit-wise maximum likelihood decoding, respectively, for both codes.
The results for the (16, 8) polar code are shown in Fig. 8a and Fig. 8b. We note that the MLNN used in [26] was able to obtain bit-wise optimal performance. We also note that the MLNN used in [42, Fig. 4] was unable to achieve optimal performance for a (16, 8) polar code, although the objective there was to reduce complexity by pruning rather than to obtain the best performance. We see from Table 1 that both these MLNNs have a larger size than the optimal NNs described in Theorems 1 and 2, and require training. As expected, the results from Theorems 1 and 2 match those of maximum likelihood. However, as shown in the second to last column of Table 1, this comes at the cost of significant complexity. We note that optimal performance demanding significant complexity is in line with our expectations: maximum likelihood decoding is a difficult problem. Thus, we expect optimum decoding will only be realized for complexity-unconstrained applications or for ultra-short blocklengths, while practical suboptimal decoders such as SC and BDD will still be used when low-complexity solutions are required, e.g., in low-power edge devices.

IV. CONCLUSION We provided a formal analysis of two neural network decoders for error-correcting codes: SLNN and MLNN decoders. These decoders have been shown in the literature to achieve codeword- and bit-wise near-optimal decoding performance, respectively. The designs in the literature are based on heuristic rules. In this paper, we showed analytically that these decoders, in fact, always realize optimal decoding for certain network architectures, without even requiring any training. This optimality was also numerically demonstrated for the Hamming (7, 4), polar (16, 8), and BCH (31, 21) codes. Moreover, these optimal architectures, which are not NNs but a way to realize maximum likelihood decoding, have binary weight matrices and are more sparsely connected than the ones achieving near-optimal performance. Our optimal decoders require at least one layer with size 2k . Thus, our overall conclusion is that it is possible to achieve optimal decoding for codes with k < 32 with a ‘‘trivial’’ architecture that does not require training. Moreover, the complexity of these trivial architectures is smaller than that of ‘‘actual’’ suboptimal NNs that require training of real-valued weight matrices and bias vectors. On the other hand, for longer codes (k > 32), NNs that can achieve optimal performance quickly grow to impractical sizes, whereas smaller networks provide significantly poor performance.
