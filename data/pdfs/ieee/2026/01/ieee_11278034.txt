Optimization of the Bit Mapping for LDPC-Coded Faster-Than-Nyquist Systems
Jiayi Yang , Shuangyang Li , Member, IEEE, Qianfan Wang , Member, IEEE, Xiao Ma , Member, IEEE, and Giuseppe Caire , Fellow, IEEE

Abstract— This letter focuses on the analysis and optimization of the bit mapping for low-density parity-check (LDPC) coded faster-than-Nyquist (FTN) systems with high-order modulation. We propose the extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) chart analysis for LDPC-coded FTN systems based on the Ungerboeck observation model, where the vector-input and vector-output mutual information of the FTN detector is approximated using a suitably trained neural network (NN). Leveraging the EXIT chart, we optimize the bit mapping, i.e., the assignment of coded bits to the binary labeled constellation points, specifically for FTN systems. Through threshold analysis and simulations, it is shown that for an FTN system using a variant of the LDPC codes specified in the 5G standard, coded bits with highly reliable positions (information bits) in the Tanner graph preferentially to be transmitted over bit-wise sub-channels with higher capacity, while low-degree bits preferentially to be transmitted over bit-wise sub-channels with lower capacity. Numerical results show that: 1) the LDPC-coded FTN system with the optimized mapping outperforms those with random mappings, consistent with the proposed EXIT chart analysis; 2) under the same spectral efficiency, the LDPC-coded FTN system with optimized mapping also outperforms its 5G LDPC-coded Nyquist counterpart.
Index Terms— Extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) chart, faster-than-Nyquist (FTN), neural network (NN).

Applying FTN acceleration to other practical pulse shapes or combining it with modern coding techniques appeared roughly two to three decades later, marking the beginning of the modern development of coded FTN systems [2]. Owing to these advantages, FTN signaling has since attracted significant interest in various communication scenarios, including satellite [3], optical [4], and wireless communications [5].
However, FTN signaling inherently introduces inter-symbol interference (ISI). To alleviate the impact of ISI in iterativerecovery systems, two effective solutions are tailored to FTN signaling, the design of dedicated channel codes and the optimization of bit mapping. For channel codes, a variety of low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have been proposed, including quasi-cyclic (QC) LDPC codes with increased girth [6] and uniformly shortened LDPC codes that better accommodate the ISI [7]. More recently, a class of improved 5G LDPC codes optimized for FTN signaling has been proposed in [8] based on extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) chart analysis, where the mutual information (MI) function of the FTN detector is characterized using polynomial fitting techniques.
For high-order modulation, bit-mapping optimization can provide notable gains in conventional Nyquist systems. However, extending such optimization to FTN signaling is challenging, as the detector’s input–output space grows exponentially with modulation order, making polynomial fitting impractical. To address this, we employ a neural network (NN) to approximate the vector MI of the FTN detector, enabling EXIT chart analysis under different bit-mapping strategies. The main contributions of this letter are listed as follows: Distinguished from [8], we use an NN to approximate the vector-input and vector-output MI function of the FTN detector to characterize the MI of the bit-wise sub-channels induced by bit-interleaved coded modulation (BICM) with high-order modulations. This allows us to develop the EXIT chart analysis tailored for FTN signaling with high-order modulation. By the EXIT chart analysis, we optimize the bit mapping of LDPC coded FTN system. Decoding thresholds and results reveal that, for the LDPC coded FTN system, the coded bits with a lower degree in the Tanner graph are preferentially placed to be transmitted over sub-channels with lower capacity. Although demonstrated for FTN signaling, the NN-assisted EXIT framework is detector-agnostic and can be applied to other BICM systems with soft-in/soft-out detection.

C. EXIT Analysis for LDPC-Coded High-Order FTN System
Based on the vector-input and vector-output MI updates for the detector and decoder, the EXIT chart analysis for LDPC-coded FTN system with high-order modulation can be established, see Fig. 3 for reference. Given the channel message and IE,Dec of length nb produced by the decoder, the detector is invoked nb/m times. At the ℓ-th invocation, the input MI of length m to the detector is constructed as
IA(0,,Dℓ)et, · · · , IA(m,D−e1t,ℓ) = π IE(0,,Dℓ)ec, · · · , IE(m,D−e1c,ℓ) , (8)
where π denotes the block-wise interleaver defined in Sec. II. Similarly, at the ℓ-th invocation, the output MI of the detector is deinterleaved to produce the input for the decoder, which is denoted as
IA(0,,Dℓ)ec, · · · , IA(m,D−e1c,ℓ) = π−1 IE(0,,Dℓ)et, · · · , IE(m,D−e1t,ℓ) . (9)
After completing all nb/m detector invocations, the a priori MI IA,Dec for the decoder is obtained. For clarity, the overall EXIT chart procedure is summarized in Algorithm 1. Finally, a bisection search is performed to find the minimum Eb/N0 required for successful decoding, which is referred to as the decoding threshold of the LDPC-coded FTN system. Note that the proposed scheme appears only in the offline design, where the NN-assisted EXIT analysis selects a fixed mapping for each base graph and modulation. This mapping is implemented as a simple interleaver/deinterleaver, so the online complexity and impact on the 5G NR specification are negligible.

Example 2 (Performance Comparison with Nyquist Signaling): Consider the LDPC-coded FTN system with τ = 2/3 and 16-QAM modulation. For comparison, we also evaluate an LDPC-coded Nyquist system with the same modulation. In this example, three bit mappings are considered for both systems: i) Nyquist-optimized mapping (1, 0, 2, 3)/(0, 3, 1, 2) obtained via one-dimensional, ii) FTN-optimized mapping (0, 3, 1, 2) by the proposed EXIT chart, iii) randomly interleaved mapping. To ensure the same SE of 3 bits per channel use, the FTN system employs a rate-1/2 improved 5G LDPC code [16] with n = 11264, while the Nyquist system uses a rate-3/4 standard 5G LDPC code with the same n = 11264. The performance comparison is shown in Fig. 6. It is observed that the FTN system with the optimized mapping (0, 3, 1, 2) outperforms that with Nyquist-optimized mapping. Compared to random interleaving, mapping optimization of the FTN system provides larger gains than that in Nyquist systems. Notably, under the same SE, the optimized FTN system achieves a gain of about 0.7 dB over all Nyquist counterparts at BER = 10−3. At a rate of 3 bits/s with a 30% roll-off RRC pulse (about 2.3 bits/s/Hz), our proposed FTN signaling combined with LDPC coding operates about 1.5 dB away from the Shannon limit at BER = 10−4 [17], while the Nyquist-based 5G LDPC scheme remains around 2.2 dB away. These observations also confirm that our approach achieves about 0.5 dB improvement over the simple-coding-plus-FTN schemes reported in [18].
Remark: For fair comparison, we fix the total transmitted energy per codeword. Both FTN and Nyquist signals use sequences of comparable length with equal average symbol energy, ensuring identical total energy. Under this setup, FTN signaling can yield an improved spectral efficiency as demonstrated in [2], because of its improved constrained capacity compared to the Nyquist counterpart. Similar fairness criteria—same block length and equal spectral efficiency— have also been adopted in [6] and [19]. Moreover, with the same shaping pulse h(t), their power spectral densities are proportional to |H(f )|2, occupying the same bandwidth— clarifying that FTN does not inherently expand bandwidth.
