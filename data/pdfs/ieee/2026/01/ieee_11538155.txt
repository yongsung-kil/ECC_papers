ABSTRACT Neural decoders have recently advanced error correction performance, with transformerbased architectures in the error correction code transformer (ECCT) framework achieving state-of-the-art results among neural decoders. However, the multiple-masks ECCT suffers from suboptimal mask selection, which impacts decoding performance. In this work, we propose spectral-guided ECCT, leveraging the Laplacian matrix and its eigenvectors from the variable-variable graph to identify weakly connected node clusters for optimized mask generation. By embedding spectral properties into the decoding process, our method constructs more effective masks that improve information flow across variable and check nodes. Experimental results demonstrate consistent gains over existing ECCT-based models, achieving superior decoding accuracy and reliability while significantly improving training efficiency. This work highlights the potential of spectral graph theory to address structural challenges in neural decoders and provides a principled direction for future error correction design.
INDEX TERMS Neural decoders, ECCT, Laplacian matrix, eigenvectors, spectral-guided mask generation.

I. INTRODUCTION
E RROR correction codes (ECCs) are integral to ensuring data integrity across a multitude of modern communication and storage systems, including wireless networks, data storage devices, and emerging sixth-generation (6G) infrastructures [1], [2]. While conventional iterative decoding algorithms, such as belief propagation (BP) and the min-sum algorithm, have demonstrated considerable efficacy, they exhibit inherent limitations in approaching the Shannon capacity under practical complexity constraints [3], [4].
The error floor phenomenon represents a formidable and long-standing obstacle in the design of high-performance communication systems [5], [6], [7]. In the high signal-tonoise ratio (SNR) regime, where error rates are expected to decrease exponentially with increasing signal power, many advanced ECCs exhibit a sudden and problematic flattening of their performance curve. This behavior signifies that further increases in power yield diminishing, or

even negligible, improvements in the bit error rate (BER), rendering the system highly inefficient. The root cause of this limitation is overwhelmingly attributed to the presence of small, topologically detrimental subgraphs within the code’s Tanner graph, commonly known as trapping sets or absorbing sets [6], [8]. These structures act as local traps for iterative decoding algorithms like BP, causing incorrect messages to be endlessly circulated and amplified within the set, thereby preventing the decoder from converging to the correct codeword.
Mitigating the impact of these structures has been a central focus of coding theory research. Foundational work has included algorithmic approaches to enumerate and classify dominant trapping sets based on graphical properties like cycle length and node degree [6], as well as sophisticated algebraic tools such as the cycle consistency matrix for verifying the existence of absorbing sets in structured codes like quasi-cyclic (QC) low-density paritycheck (LDPC) codes [9]. Recent investigations have definitively shown that even the most advanced deep learning-based decoders, including those built on transformer architectures, still suffer from an error floor when decoding powerful algebraic codes like Bose–Chaudhuri–Hocquenghem (BCH) and sparse-graph LDPC codes [10], [11], [12], [13], [14]. The universality of this problem is further highlighted by its direct analog in quantum computing, where quantum trapping sets (QTSs) have been identified as a key factor limiting the performance of quantum ECCs [15], [16], [17].
The advent of neural network (NN)-based decoders marks a paradigm shift from traditional model-based algorithms to data-driven, learned decoding strategies. By leveraging deep learning, these decoders can approximate near-optimal maximum a posteriori (MAP) decoding with a manageable level of complexity, often outperforming their classical counterparts [18], [19], [20], [21]. Recent theoretical works have further solidified this progress by establishing generalization bounds specifically for transformer-based channel decoders, providing critical insights into their reliability [22]. Within this domain, architectures based on the transformer model have emerged as particularly potent, largely due to their selfattention mechanism, which enables the network to learn and model complex, long-range dependencies between all variable nodes (VNs) in the code graph simultaneously [23], [24]. The error correction code transformer (ECCT) stands as the current state-of-the-art, having set new performance benchmarks by surpassing prior recurrent and convolutional neural decoder architectures across a wide spectrum of codes [10], [11], [24].
Despite its success, ECCT still faces key limitations. The self-attention mechanism scales quadratically with the block length, making it computationally burdensome for large codes [13], [14]. While recent unified frameworks have attempted to reduce this complexity [25], the structural coarseness of the standard PCM-derived mask remains a challenge, as it captures only high-level Tanner graph connectivity without addressing specific topological weaknesses. The multiple-masks ECCT (MM-ECCT) was introduced in [23] as a way to mitigate these computational burdens. MM-ECCT converts the PCM to a systematic form and generates complementary masks via cyclic shifts of the identity submatrix, enabling parallel masked attention branches that prune redundant attention paths. Results in [23] show that using two such masks yields strong performance for short block length codes. However, the mask selection strategy in MM-ECCT remains largely empirical. Although many valid masks can be derived from systematic and shifted identity structures, the framework does not offer criteria for choosing among them. When more than two masks are available, selecting a pair at random, as done in MM-ECCT, may overlook structurally important regions of the code, thus limiting achievable decoding performance. This reveals a methodological gap: while computational

efficiency is improved, mask design is not yet guided by a principled understanding of the code’s intrinsic structure.
Addressing this limitation requires a mechanism that links mask construction directly to the topology of the code. We therefore propose a principled spectral-guided (SG) mask generation method. Rather than relying on parity check matrix transformations, our approach examines the topology formed by variable node interactions via the construction of a variable-variable (V × V) graph and its normalized Laplacian. Our central premise is that nodes involved in trapping-set related failures tend to reside in regions of the V × V graph with weak connectivity, a structural characteristic strongly associated with error floor behavior. The spectral decomposition of the normalized Laplacian exposes these regions by separating the graph according to variations in connectivity strength [26], [27], [28]. This decomposition reveals the multi-scale clustering structure inherent in the code’s topology, where eigenvectors corresponding to smaller eigenvalues capture increasingly fine-grained partitions of the variable node set. In particular, the Fiedler vector, corresponding to the smallest non-zero Laplacian eigenvalue [26], provides a natural one-dimensional embedding that delineates boundaries between strongly and weakly connected groups of VNs. By projecting the graph onto this spectral axis, we obtain a continuous measure of structural vulnerability for each variable node relative to connectivity bottlenecks. These spectrally identified clusters form the basis for constructing topology-informed attention masks that direct the Transformer toward vulnerable regions of the code.
To address this limitation, we propose SG-ECCT, a principled framework that leverages spectral graph theory to construct topology-informed attention masks. By analyzing the normalized Laplacian of the V × V graph, our method systematically identifies weakly connected node clusters associated with trapping sets and directs the transformer’s attention toward these vulnerable regions. The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
• We introduce a principled SG mask construction method that analyzes the Laplacian of the V × V graph to identify weakly connected node clusters associated with trapping-set structures.
• We establish a spectral interpretation linking graph connectivity properties to structural vulnerabilities in the code topology, explaining how spectral embeddings reveal regions prone to decoding failures.
• We integrate SG analysis with transformer-based decoding by constructing topology-informed attention masks that guide the self-attention mechanism toward vulnerable regions of the code graph.
• We demonstrate through extensive simulations across BCH, Polar, and LDPC codes that the proposed method consistently improves decoding accuracy compared to baseline ECCT models. Furthermore, by providing a

SG MASK DESIGN FOR ECCT Based on the properties of the eigenvectors and eigenvalues of the Laplacian matrix, we propose the SG-ECCT method, which aims to construct masks by identifying and emphasizing error-prone VNs in the code graph. We first present the theoretical motivation behind the proposed approach, followed by a detailed description of the methodology and implementation steps of SG-ECCT.

A. SPECTRAL GRAPH FORMULATION FOR MASK GENERATION 1) MOTIVATION FOR V ×V GRAPH MODELING
Although it is possible to construct the Laplacian matrix directly from the Tanner graph, this approach is not wellsuited for our objective of identifying weak or error-prone VNs. The Tanner graph is inherently a bipartite structure, where edges exist only between VNs and CNs. Performing spectral analysis directly on this graph blends the internal structural relationships among VNs with the check constraints, making it difficult to distinctly isolate weakly connected clusters or error-prone nodes, thereby reducing both the clarity and interpretability of the spectral insights.
To analyze relationships within the variable-node set, we project the bipartite Tanner graph onto a single-partite, weighted V × V graph, denoted by GV = (V, EV , w). In this graph, an edge with weight wi j exists between two VNs vi and v j if and only if they share at least one common CN. This projection preserves the structural correlations among VNs while removing the explicit influence of the check constraints, allowing the subsequent spectral analysis on the Laplacian of GV to more accurately reveal communities of weakly connected or topologically vulnerable nodes.

IV. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS A. EXPERIMENTAL SETUP
As discussed, the maximum number of complementary masks that can be constructed is upper bounded by Nmask ≤ n/m . While employing a larger number of masks allows for broader coverage of the PCM and may potentially enhance decoding performance, each additional mask necessitates the introduction of a parallel self-attention branch. This leads to a linear increase in both the number of trainable parameters and the memory footprint required during the training and inference phases. Furthermore, for many highrate codes in which the block length n is not significantly larger than the number of parity checks m, selecting an appropriate number of masks becomes crucial for balancing representational diversity with architectural complexity. As demonstrated in [12], a dual-mask configuration (b = 2) represents an effective trade-off between decoding accuracy and computational overhead.
We compare the proposed SG-ECCT framework with the original ECCT and MM-ECCT models under an identical training protocol as in [10] and [12] to ensure fairness. Two configurations of SG-ECCT are evaluated
• Single-mask (b = 1): operates analogously to the ECCT but incorporates one SG attention mask; and
• Dual-mask (b = 2): extends the MM-ECCT architecture by employing two parallel masked self-attention branches, each guided by a distinct spectral mask derived from the Laplacian spectrum.
This comparative framework establishes a consistent experimental basis across all models and motivates the architectural configuration described below.
To comprehensively assess decoding accuracy and generality, we evaluate on three representative code families: BCH, Polar, and LDPC. Each code employs its standard algebraic or quasi-cyclic PCM, which is normalized prior to Laplacian construction for spectral analysis.
During training, SNR = Eb/N0 is sampled uniformly from [1, 6] dB for each sample. A total of 105 training samples and 104 validation samples are generated per epoch. For performance evaluation, SNR is swept from 1 dB to 7 dB in increments of 1 dB, with 104 Monte Carlo samples per SNR point. Each training sample corresponds to a randomly selected message encoded by the generator matrix and transmitted through an AWGN channel.
All models share an identical transformer backbone consisting of Nlayer = 4 with multi-head self-attention h = {4, 8}, a feed-forward network. The embedding dimension is tested with d ∈ {32, 64, 128, 256} to examine model scalability. Training runs for up to 1000 epochs with early stopping (patience = 30) using the Adam optimizer with an initial learning rate of 5 × 10−4, which is scheduled using cosine annealing with warm restarts. The mean squared error loss is used with a batch size of 256.

B. TRAINING EFFICIENCY AND CONVERGENCE We evaluate the training efficiency of the proposed SGECCT against the baseline ECCT and MM-ECCT models. The key metrics, presented in Table I, include the number of epochs required to reach a convergence threshold, the average computation time per epoch, and the resulting total training time in seconds.
A primary finding is that our proposed SG masks, for both b = 1 and b = 2 configurations, lead to significantly faster convergence. For instance, in the BCH code scenario, SG-ECCT with b = 1 and b = 2 models converge in 341 and 321 epochs, respectively. This is a nearly 3-fold reduction compared to the ECCT (979 epochs) and MM-ECCT (956 epochs) baselines. This trend holds true across all tested code families, with our models for Polar codes converging in approximately 355-372 epochs (vs. 966-981) and for LDPC codes in 467-531 epochs (vs. 782-891). This demonstrates that the SG masks provide a more effective learning signal, enabling the transformer to learn the code’s structural properties more rapidly.
Our method also reduces the computational overhead per epoch. The time per epoch for our models is consistently lower than that of the baselines. For the LDPC code, SGECCT with b = 1 model requires only 3.37 seconds per epoch, whereas ECCT and MM-ECCT require 6.12s and 6.57s, respectively. This suggests that the mask structure generated by our spectral method is more computationally efficient, likely due to a different sparsity pattern than the masks from the baseline methods.
The combination of faster convergence and lower perepoch cost results in a substantial reduction in the total training time. Our framework achieves speedups ranging from 2.67× to 4.53× over the baseline ECCT. Notably, the MM-ECCT method, while improving decoding performance, offers no training speedup and, in some cases (e.g., LDPC), is slower to train than the original ECCT. This twofold advantage—converging in fewer steps and executing each step more quickly—highlights that our SG approach not only enhances decoder accuracy but also significantly reduces the computational burden of training. This efficiency gain is critical, as the high computational cost of training is a primary barrier to the adoption of large-scale transformer-based decoders. By providing a more direct and principled learning signal derived from the code’s core graph structure, our spectral method avoids the lengthy, computationally intensive exploration phase that baseline models require to learn these same properties from scratch,

V. CONCLUSION In this paper, we present the SG-ECCT framework, which overcomes the empirical mask selection limitations of ECCT and MM-ECCT decoders. By leveraging spectral graph theory, the proposed method analyzes the Laplacian eigenvectors of the V × V graph to identify structurally weak node clusters associated with trapping sets, from

which principled attention masks are derived. Experimental results show that SG-ECCT achieves up to 4.5× faster convergence and consistently surpasses baseline decoders across BCH, Polar, and LDPC codes. The improvements are most evident in the high-SNR region, demonstrating effective error-floor mitigation and highlighting the benefit of integrating graph-theoretic priors into neural decoder architectures. Future work will aim to enhance the scalability of the SG framework by developing sparse or structured attention mechanisms to reduce quadratic complexity for large block lengths.
