Turbo Equalization for Flexible PAM-2/4 Passive

Optical Networks

Tianyuan Kong , Nebojsˇa Stojanovic´ , Member, IEEE, Tom Jonas Wettlin , Giuseppe Caruso , Stefano Calabro` , Balazs Matuz, Emna Ben Yacoub , Xianbo Dai, Marvin Xhemrishi , Maxim Kuschnerov , Member, IEEE, and Stephan Pachnicke , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—We investigate a turbo equalization (TE) scheme combining soft-input soft-output (SISO) Bahl–Cocke–Jelinek–Raviv (BCJR) detection with low-density parity-check (LDPC) decoding based on the standardized (17280, 14592) LDPC code for passive optical networks. For a two-tap intersymbol interference (ISI) channel, the maximal achievable information rates (AIRs) are analytically derived to quantify the performance gap between separate detection and decoding (SDD) and joint detection and decoding (JDD) under different ISI levels. This analysis reveals the potential gain achievable by JDD and motivates the use of TE as a practical approach to approach JDD performance. Experimental results obtained in a 50-GBd pulse-amplitude modulation (PAM) 2/4 passive optical network (PON) system demonstrate that TE provides received optical power (ROP) gains of 0.33 dB for PAM-2 and 0.56 dB for PAM-4, confirming its effectiveness for bandwidth-limited receivers.
Index Terms—Optical communication, turbo equalization, BCJR, LDPC, IM/DD, PON.
I. INTRODUCTION
T O MEET the rapidly increasing capacity demands in optical access networks, 50G passive optical networks (50G-PON) have been standardized as a key technology for next-generation access systems [1], [2], [3]. In cost-sensitive PON deployments, optical network units (ONUs) are typically equipped with low-cost avalanche photodiodes (APDs), which may result in bandwidth-limited receiver front-ends. When operating at symbol rates as high as 50 GBd, such receivers experience severe ISI due to the combined effects of limited device bandwidth and fiber chromatic dispersion (CD), which significantly degrades receiver sensitivity and constrains the achievable power budget.
To mitigate ISI under bandwidth-limited conditions, various digital signal processing (DSP) techniques have been extensively investigated [3], [4]. At the receiver side,

low-complexity linear and nonlinear equalizers, such as feedforward equalizers (FFE) and decision feedback equalizers (DFE), are widely adopted in practical PON systems owing to their implementation simplicity and low latency [5]. However, under strong ISI conditions, especially in high-speed PAM systems, FFE causes noise enhancement, while DFE suffers from error propagation, leading to limited bit error rate (BER) performance improvements.
To further enhance ISI mitigation capability, sequence-based detection techniques such as maximum likelihood sequence estimation (MLSE) and maximum a posteriori (MAP) detection have been proposed for bandwidth-limited optical links [6], [7], [8], [9]. These techniques can effectively handle severe ISI, offering significant performance gains over conventional FFE/DFE-based solutions.
In current PON systems, SDD, where equalization and forward error correction (FEC) decoding are performed independently, represents the state-of-the-art solution [3], [4], [10]. While SDD offers a favorable complexity–performance tradeoff, it is inherently suboptimal in the presence of strong ISI, since the soft information generated during symbol detection does not fully capture the residual ISI. This limitation becomes increasingly pronounced for higher-order modulation formats. Although PAM-4 is not currently adopted in 50G PON standards, it has attracted significant research interest as a potential option for boosting downstream data rates without increasing the symbol rate [11].
TE, which iteratively exchanges soft information between a SISO equalizer and a channel decoder, provides an effective means to approach JDD performance with manageable complexity [12], [13], [14]. The remainder of this letter is organized as follows. Section II introduces the principle of TE. Section III presents the simulation setup and results, while Section IV details the experimental setup and corresponding results. Finally, Section V concludes the letter.
II. PRINCIPLE OF TURBO EQUALIZATION
In the SISO TE framework [15], as depicted on the righthand side of Fig. 1, the BCJR-based equalizer iteratively operates on the received noisy signal and computes a posteriori symbol-wise log-likelihood values (L-values). These symbollevel likelihood metrics are subsequently transformed into bit-level L-values through a soft demapping operation based on marginalization, denoted as Lp,bc jr.

Le,bc jr = Lp,bc jr − La,bc jr,

(1)

where, in the initial TE iteration, the a priori information is initialized as La,bc jr = 0. The resulting extrinsic information Le,bc jr is then fed into the LDPC decoder.
The (17280,14592) LDPC code is decoded using the min-sum algorithm [16]. In this work, the baseline SDD performance is defined as BCJR detection followed by LDPC decoding with I = 20 iterations. The LDPC decoder produces extrinsic bit-wise L-values Le,LDPC, computed as

Le,ldpc = Lp,ldpc − La,ldpc,

(2)

where Lp,ldpc and La,ldpc denote the a posteriori and a priori L-values, respectively. These extrinsic L-values serve as a priori information for the next TE iteration and must be converted back to symbol-wise L-values for the BCJR detector. Ultimately, the iterative loop progressively refines the reliability of the estimated bits, analogous to the iterative decoding principle underlying turbo codes.

When a BCJR detector is cascaded with the channel, the achievable rate is evaluated using the generalized mutual information (GMI) [18], which quantifies performance under SDD. The blue and red dashed curves represent the baseline performance, i.e., a BCJR detector with I = 20 LDPC decoding iterations without TE, reaching 5.9 dB and 12.7 dB for PAM-2 and PAM-4, respectively. The circle and square markers indicate the performance with TE, where circles correspond to the number of LDPC decoding inner iterations I = 1 and squares to I = 2. As the TE iteration index K increases from 1 to 10, TE surpasses the baseline at K = 4 for I = 1 and at K = 3 for I = 2. IV. EXPERIMENTAL SETUP AND RESULTS
Fig. 4 shows the experimental setup of a flexible PAM-2/4 PON system. A pseudorandom bit sequence (PRBS) is generated and mapped to a PAM-2/4 signal, which is upsampled to 120 GSa/s, filtered by a root-raised cosine filter with a 0.1 rolloff, and output through a 120 GSa/s digital-to-analog converter (DAC, Micram). After amplification by an electrical amplifier (EA), the signal drives a 35 GHz O-band electro-absorption modulated laser (EML). A variable optical attenuator (VOA) controls the received optical power (ROP). The optical signal is detected by an APD with a transimpedance amplifier (TIA), whose combined bandwidth is 17 GHz, and the signal is captured by a 256 GSa/s digital sampling oscilloscope (DSO) for offline DSP. In this work, the sample data length is set to 5 million.

PAM-4, on the other hand, shows a slower ROP increase and outperforms PAM-2 above 70Gbps, making it more suitable for ≥ 100Gbps short-reach optical links with better scalability and power margin.
V. CONCLUSION
An analytical framework based on AIRs has been presented to estimate the theoretical maximal TE gain for a unit-memory ISI channel, clearly revealing the performance gap between SDD and JDD. Based on this analysis, TE combining a BCJR detector with the standardized (17280, 14592) LDPC code was investigated for a 50 GBd PAM-2/4 PON system.
