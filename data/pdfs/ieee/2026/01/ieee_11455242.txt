MrcPunc: Modulation and Rate Compatible Puncturing for Few-Iteration 5G LDPC Decoding

Qiushi Xu , Graduate Student Member, IEEE, Huayi Zhou , Member, IEEE, Mingyang Zhu , Member, IEEE, Ming Jiang , Member, IEEE, and Chuan Zhang , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—To meet the stringent high-throughput demands of enhanced mobile broadband (eMBB), low-density parity-check (LDPC) decoding is limited to few iterations. This constraint usually degrades error-correcting performance, rousing a critical need for puncturing towards algorithmic enhancements. However, existing puncturing methods lack modulation and rate compatibility, converging to local optima due to greedy search strategies. To this end, this paper proposes a modulation and rate compatible puncturing (MrcPunc) for 5G LDPC codes. It incorporates three key techniques: 1) a revised protograph-based extrinsic information transfer (PEXIT) analysis for accurate performance prediction, 2) a progressive multi-path search (PMPS) algorithm to avoid local optima, and 3) a compatibility optimization supporting various modulations and code rates. The MrcPunc yields up to 0.6 dB gain over the 5G standard puncturing under few-iteration condition without extra decoding complexity. It is noted that MrcPunc guarantees robustness against both larger iteration counts and mismatched modulations, alongside reducing the average number of decoding iterations.
Index Terms—Puncturing, compatibility, low-density paritycheck codes, few-iteration decoding.

the decoding latency increases linearly with iteration counts. Moreover, both URLLC and eMBB require 5G LDPC codes to maintain robust error-correcting capabilities [2], [3], [4], [5]. Although simplifying decoding and refining codes can mitigate issues related to latency, throughput, and reliability, respectively, a reduction in iterations inevitably leads to significant performance degradation.
To address the trade-off between low-latency, highthroughput, and ultra-reliability, a series of low-complexity LDPC decoding have been proposed, maintaining comparable performance via parameter optimization [6], [7], [8], [9]. However, these algorithms are inherently limited by the performance bounds of belief-propagation (BP) decoding, making LDPC design a pivotal alternative to enhance error-correcting capabilities through the optimization of code ensembles. Conventional LDPC design relies on density evolution (DE) analysis for evaluation [10]. Chung et al. optimized an LDPC ensemble using discrete DE, achieving near-Shannon-limit performance but requiring extensive iterations [11]. The studies, such as [11], [12], [13], and [14], assume near-infinite iterations, focusing only on large-iteration decoding and resulting in the suboptimal few-iteration decoding.
The concept of few-iteration decoding was first introduced in [15], which highlighted shortcomings of the 5G standard puncturing. Through direct simulations, the authors optimized the puncturing for moderate-rate and short 5G LDPC codes with 5 ∼ 10 iterations, achieving performance gains of up to 0.55 dB. Similarly, Koike-Akino et al. [16], [17] concentrated on iteration-aware decoding, developing LDPC codes with better performance compared to conventional ones by leveraging extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) analysis, an approximation of DE. However, these approaches present inherent limitations: the simulation-based optimization in [15] encounters a significant computational bottleneck due to the vast combinatorial space involving code rates, lengths, and modulations; although the EXIT analysis in [16] and [17] accelerates performance evaluation, it is confined to the LDPC design for specific code rates and low-order modulations. This lack of modulation and rate compatibility severely limits practical applicability in the future evolution of 5G new radio (NR) systems, where adaptive code rates and modulations are essential to accommodate dynamically varying channel conditions.

In contrast to [15], the DE-like analysis is utilized for puncturing optimization across different channels and decoding in [18], [19], [20], [21], and [22], while a range of rate compatible puncturing methods have been introduced in [23], [24], [25], [26], and [27]. These methods focus solely on the node perspective and neglect the protograph-based characteristic of 5G LDPC codes, similar to EXIT analysis. Although the protograph-based EXIT (PEXIT) analysis [28] acknowledges this aspect, it fails to account for the influence of few-iteration decoding and overlooks differences in information propagation paths between flooding and layered decoding. This oversight results in puncturing with poor performance being evaluated as optimal. Consequently, optimizations employing the traditional PEXIT analysis fail to achieve optimal puncturing, particularly in the context of layered few-iteration decoding, which is inevitable in URLLC and eMBB scenarios with more stringent requirements. Motivated by these considerations, this paper seeks to utilize the revised PEXIT analysis to enhance the efficiency of puncturing evaluation, while ensuring that the puncturing aligns with modulation and rate compatibility requirements, thereby addressing the challenges of adaptability in 5G and beyond 5G systems.
The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows.
• As the introduction of a layered schedule alters information propagation paths, the traditional PEXIT analysis fails to evaluate the puncturing for layered few-iteration decoding. Thus, we develop a revised PEXIT analysis for puncturing evaluation in bit-interleaved coded modulation (BICM) systems.
• To address the local optimum issue of the greedy search strategies in [15], we propose a progressive multi-path search (PMPS) algorithm to enhance the puncturing optimization, especially under high-order modulations, while ensuring acceptable computational complexity.
• To meet the compatibility requirement of 5G NR systems, we develop modulation and rate compatible puncturing (MrcPunc) for 5G LDPC decoding with different iteration counts, significantly enhancing error-correcting performance across different code rates, lengths, and modulations.
• We propose a block interleaver to implement MrcPunc, which avoids significant modifications to the existing 5G NR system and enables MrcPunc to be directly integrated with incremental redundancy hybrid automatic repeat request (IR-HARQ) transmission.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section II overviews the characteristics of 5G LDPC codes and puncturing, modulation and coding scheme (MCS) tables, as well as bit-interleavers in 5G NR systems. Section III develops the layered PEXIT analysis and proves its validity. Section IV details the design of MrcPunc. Section V presents the implementation of MrcPunc and its combination with IR-HARQ transmission. Section VI thoroughly analyzes the advantages of the PMPS algorithm and MrcPunc. Finally, Section VII concludes this paper with key findings and insights.

II. PRELIMINARIES
In this section, we introduce the parity-check matrices of 5G LDPC codes, the 5G standard puncturing, the characteristics of BICM systems, and the MCS tables.

A. LDPC Codes and Puncturing in 5G Standard
5G LDPC codes are a class of protograph-based LDPC codes [29], [30], where the parity-check matrices all can be structured as an array of Z × Z submatrices, with Z denoting the lifting factor. The base matrix Hb = [bh,s]1≤h≤m,1≤s≤n specifies the array structure, where bh,s ∈ N corresponds to a Z × Z permutation matrix or all-zero matrix, m and n represent the numbers of rows and columns in the base matrix respectively. Thus, the Tanner graph of a 5G LDPC code consists of m × Z check nodes (CNs) and n × Z variable nodes (VNs), where the i-th CN and j-th VN are denoted as ci and vj, respectively. According to these characteristics of 5G LDPC codes, CNs and VNs can be grouped into CN blocks and VN blocks, each containing Z nodes. The base matrices of 5G LDPC codes are derived from the base graph (BG) 1 and BG2, enabling the construction of codes with flexible code lengths and a wide range of code rates.
For information bits of length k, if bluek ∈/ {iZ | i ∈ N}, zero-filler is applied to the end of these bits before channel encoding, and the set of filler VNs is denoted as S. These filler bits are not transmitted since they are known a priori at the receiver ends. During decoding, these VNs are either assigned the maximum log-likelihood ratio (LLR) or completely removed from the Tanner graph. After encoding, 5G NR systems perform puncturing on VNs to achieve the target code rate R. As illustrated in Fig. 1, the 5G standard prioritizes the first two VN blocks for puncturing, followed by sequential puncturing from the last VN backward. The set of punctured VNs, defined as P, corresponds to the bits that are not transmitted and unknown at the receiver. After zerofilling and puncturing, the communication system can achieve

III. LAYERED PEXIT ANALYSIS FOR BICM SYSTEMS
In this section, a layered PEXIT analysis is proposed to assess the performance of the layered few-iteration decoding. When the iteration counts are small, the traditional PEXIT analysis [28], [36] cannot capture the convergence of layered decoding. Furthermore, changes in the decoding neighborhood induced by layered scheduling are considered. Before deriving the layered PEXIT analysis, we prove the validity of the fundamental assumption in DE analysis: the decoding neighborhood is a tree for infinite-length codes.

A. Flooding and Layered Decoding

The conventional decoding of LDPC codes employs BP

algorithm [37], which iteratively exchanges messages between

VNs and CNs to update LLRs of bits in the codeword. Demod-

ulated and zero-filled noisy channel information, denoted as lj(0), is directly fed into the VN vj. During the t-th iteration,
each VN passes information to all connected CNs (the edge information from vj to ci is denoted as lj(t→) i), while each
CN updates and passes information to all connected VN (the message from ci to vj is denoted as li(→ t) j). After each iteration, the updated LLR, denoted as lj(t), is output by the VN vj to
check whether to early terminate the decoding process. The

flooding BP (FBP) decoding is updated iteratively through

flooding scheduling. The layered BP decoding (LBP) updates

a subset of CNs layer by layer in each iteration to achieve

a faster decoding convergence speed and better performance

[38], [39].

B. Layered Decoding Neighborhood

nodes, these duplicates are treated as distinct entities. When
referring to a node o in the neighborhood, we specifically
mean the instance in the dependency path, not all its possible duplicates. In a layered decoding neighborhood, let N˜ (o) be
the neighbor set of o, which contains the nodes connected to o in the graph. N˜ (o) ⊂ N (o) will be guaranteed, where N (o)
is the neighbor set of o in the Tanner graph. The rigorous definition of L(tM )(vj) is given as follows.

iteration. The temporary neighborhood at step κ is denoted by L(tM )(vj; κ), and the final neighborhood is L(tM )(vj) = L(tM )(vj; mtM ). Prior to the recursive expansion, the temporary neighborhood L(tM )(vj; 0) is initialized to consist solely of the root node vj. C. Derivation of Layered PEXIT Analysis
We aim to develop a layered PEXIT analysis, which overcomes the mismatch between traditional PEXIT analysis and layered decoding. The PEXIT analysis evaluates decoding performance especially for punctured codes by virtue of accounting for differences and connection specifics between VN and CN blocks [28], [36]. This approach determines whether the decoder can converge at a certain signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) by calculating the mutual information, which is denoted by IA(tP) P,s, between the soft outputs of each VN block and the transmitted codewords in the t-th iteration. The mutual information between the edge information from the s-th VN block to the h-th CN block (or vice versa) and the transmitted codewords in the t-th iteration is define as IV(tN) ,s→h (and IC(tN) ,h→s), where the s-th VN block and the h-th CN block are the sets Vs = v(s−1)Z+1, . . ., vsZ and Ch = c(h−1)Z+1, . . ., chZ respectively.

IV. DESIGN OF MODULATION AND RATE COMPATIBLE PUNCTURING
In this section, we first develop a PMPS algorithm based on PEXIT analysis to design the optimal puncturing for 5G LDPC codes in the BICM systems under specific code rates and modulations. Secondly, according to the MCS tables in the 5G NR standard, we set multiple optimization objectives to be compatible with multiple modulations at a specific code rate. Finally, based on the characteristics of 5G LDPC codes, we integrate PMPS and multi-objective optimization to design the MrcPunc for few-iteration decoding.

A. Progressive Multi-Path Search
In the 5G NR systems, |P| bits are punctured from the LDPC codes to achieve a target code rate R. Directly optimizing these |P| positions is unfeasible due to the excessively large search space dimension. Thus, instead of individual bit puncturing, we consider puncturing entire VN blocks. This strategy aligns with [15], simplifies the optimization dimension of the puncturing process to n, and thereby renders the optimization feasible. C. Combining MrcPunc With IR-HARQ Transmission
The role of IR-HARQ is to adapt to time-varying channels and provide efficient incremental redundancy transmission for 5G LDPC codes. Except for bits corresponding to the first two VN blocks, all other information bits and paritycheck bits are stored in the IR-HARQ buffer. During initial transmission, high-rate 5G LDPC codes are sent starting from the beginning of the buffer. If decoding fails, untransmitted parity-check bits are continuously sent from the buffer; if decoding remains unsuccessful after all bits in the buffer are transmitted, cyclical retransmission is performed from the start. Decoding is performed after combining the soft information from all previous transmissions, thereby improving the reliability [22]. Punctured VNs correspond to untransmitted bits in the buffer, and the vector θ specifies the ordering of bits in the IR-HARQ buffer. When combining MrcPunc with IRHARQ transmission, the block interleaver and deinterleaver are deployed before and after the IR-HARQ buffer, respectively. At this point, the bits in the IR-HARQ buffer are sorted in a specific order, and the output of the IR-HARQ buffer is directly fed into the BICM system after deinterleaving. Through block interleaving and deinterleaving, MrcPunc is implemented without altering the buffer management, cyclical transmission, and decoding of IR-HARQ transmission, enabling performance gains while retaining the 5G standard procedure.

VII. CONCLUSION
This paper proposes MrcPunc to address performance degradation for few-iteration 5G LDPC decoding in URLLC and eMBB. A revised PEXIT analysis, which accounts for the distinct information propagation paths in layered decoding, enables precise puncturing evaluation. By integrating this with the PMPS algorithm and compatibility optimization, MrcPunc is designed to support code rate and modulation combinations in MCS tables while achieving robust decoding performance.
