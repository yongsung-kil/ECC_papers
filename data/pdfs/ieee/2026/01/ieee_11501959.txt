Spatially Coupled Convolutional Codes via Block-Oriented Accumulation: Analysis and Quantized Decoding

Shancheng Zhao

Abstract—Construction of high-performance spatially coupled codes has received considerable attention recently. This paper presents a new construction of spatially coupled convolutional codes (SC-CC), in which the codewords of the underlying convolutional codes are coupled together with the block-oriented accumulator (BA). The resulting codes are referred to as the BA-based SC-CCs (BA-SC-CCs). First, we employ the extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) chart analysis to study the iterative decoding thresholds of BA-SC-CCs. Our analysis indicates that, with appropriate component convolutional codes, the iterative decoding thresholds of BA-SC-CCs can be within 0.1 dB of the channel capacities. Second, we prove that the free distance of a BA-SC-CC is greater than that of the underlying convolutional code. Third, we present a method to analyze the decoding error propagation phenomenon of BA-SC-CCs. To enhance the decoding performance, we finally introduce a quantized threshold-based Max-Log-MAP (T-Max-Log-MAP) decoder for CCs, which can be incorporated into the decoder of BA-SCCCs. Extensive numerical results demonstrate the advantages of the proposed codes and the new quantized decoder. Specifically, terminated BA-SC-CCs perform comparably to the braided convolutional codes (BCC) and outperform regular spatially coupled low-density parity-check (SC-LDPC) codes and spatially coupled repeater-convolutional-combiner (SC-RCC) codes. Furthermore, the proposed quantized decoder performs better than the conventional quantized decoder.
Index Terms—Block-oriented accumulator, convolutional codes, spatial coupling.
I. INTRODUCTION
C ONSTRUCTION and analysis of high-performance spatially coupled codes for merging applications has received considerable attention [1], [2], [3]. Spatially coupled codes are constructed by interconnecting a series of disjoint graph-based codes into a single graph-based code.

The first class of spatially coupled codes is that of spatially coupled low-density parity-check (SC-LDPC) codes [1], [4], [5], [6], [7], [8]. Analytical results showed that the iterative decoding threshold of an SC-LDPC ensemble achieves the maximum a posteriori (MAP) threshold of the underlying LDPC block ensemble over binary-input memoryless outputsymmetric channels [4], [9]. This phenomenon, known as threshold saturation, makes spatial coupling an attractive technique for constructing capacity-achieving codes.
Since the advent of SC-LDPC codes, various types of spatially coupled codes have been introduced, including spatially coupled turbo-like (SC-TL) codes [10], [11], [12], block Markov superposition transmission (BMST) [13], [14], [15], and spatially coupled repeater-convolutional-combiner (SC-RCC) codes [16]. These works typically focused on illustrating the merits of spatial coupling in asymptotic latency regime.
In addition to theoretical advancements, significant efforts have been made to construct high-performance SC-LDPC codes. Spatially coupled repeat-accumulate (SC-RA) codes were studied in [17]. Algebraic constructions of time-invariant SC-LDPC codes were presented in [7]. In [18], the authors proposed to design structured SC-LDPC codes by maximizing the girth and minimizing the number of short cycles. In [19], the authors studied the short cycles of periodically timevarying SC-LDPC codes for improved code construction. The construction of short-length SC-LDPC codes by eliminating small trapping sets was investigated in [20]. Constructions of rate-compatible LDPC and SC-LDPC codes were investigated in [21], [22], and [23]. Graph extension was use in [21] to construct rate-compatible LDPC codes. Results in [22] showed that iterative decoding thresholds of randomly punctured SC-LDPC codes on additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channel can be estimated from the iterative decoding threshold of the un-punctured code and the design rate. Edge reallocation was used in [23] to construct high-performance rate-compatible SC-LDPC codes. Algebraic constructions of globally coupled LDPC codes with tail-biting structure were proposed in [24]. In [25], applications of SC-LDPC codes in joint source-channel coding were investigated. A novel technique based on partial spatial coupling was introduced in [26] to reduce the rate loss of SC-LDPC codes. The recently introduced free-ride codes are used in [27] to construct globally coupled LDPC codes.

More recently, attention has shifted to constructing spatially coupled codes based on strong basic codes, including turbo code, Hadamard code, convolutional codes, polar codes, and algebraic codes. The resulting codes include spatially coupled product-like codes [28], [29], [30], [31], SC-TL codes [10], [32], partially coupled codes [33], [34], [35], and spatially coupled generalized LDPC codes [36], [37], [38]. This paper is intended to construct high-performance terminated spatially coupled codes via coupling strong basic codes.
Designing low-complexity decoding algorithms for highperformance spatially coupled codes based on strong component codes is of practical interest, especially when these codes are used in applications with stringent complexity constraints. The min-sum algorithm (MSA) is the first class of lowcomplexity decoder for LDPC codes. Since the invention of MSA, various low-complexity decoders had been proposed in the literature, including attenuated MSA (AMSA) [39], weight bit-flip decoder [40], reliability-based decoder [41], and finite alphabet iterative decoder [42]. To lower the error floor of quantized MSA, a threshold-based attenuated MSA (TAMSA) was proposed in [43]. In [44], Ghassemi showed that non-uniform quantization performs better than uniform quantization. Based on the principle of maximizing mutual information (MMI), non-uniform quantization was designed for LDPC codes in [45]. A novel decoding paradigm for LDPC codes based on reconstruction-computation-quantization was proposed in [46].
Analyzing the free distance of spatially coupled codes remains a significant challenge [3], [47]. While existence-type lower bounds had been established for SC-LDPC codes with large constraint lengths [48], further results in [49] extended these bounds to periodically time-varying SC-LDPC codes. Asymptotic analyses have also shown that the free distance of SC-LDPC codes constructed from protograph-based LDPC block codes increases linearly with the constraint length [50]. More recently, new upper bounds on the free distance for periodically time-varying SC-LDPC codes were derived in [51], highlighting their advantage over their time-invariant counterparts. However, for SC-LDPC codes and SC-TL codes, the only general lower bound currently known is that spatial coupling does not reduce the minimum distance of the underlying uncoupled codes [47], [51]. These observations motivate further investigation into the free distance properties of terminated spatially coupled codes.
In this paper, we propose to use block-oriented accumulator (BA) to construct high-performance terminated spatially coupled convolutional codes (SC-CCs). The resulting codes are referred to as BA-SC-CCs. In BA-SC-CCs, the codewords of the underlying convolutional code (CC) are taken as the input of a block-oriented accumulator, which introduces memory among these codewords. The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows.
• First, the convergence behaviors of BA-SC-CCs can be analyzed by the extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) chart. The analysis shows that, with appropriate component convolutional code, the thresholds of BA-SC-CCs can be within 0.1 dB of the channel capacity.

Fig. 1. Encoder of a BA-SC-CC, where the node D is a length-N register and Π is an interleaver of size N .
• Second, we prove that the free distance of the proposed BA-SC-CC is about three times as large as the free distance of the underlying convolutional code. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first explicit and nontrivial lower bound reported for terminated spatially coupled codes.
• Third, we introduce the concept of the self-recovery threshold (SRT) to enable asymptotic worst-case analysis of error propagation of BA-SC-CCs. We show that stronger component CCs exacerbate error propagation. Self-recovery threshold is general and can be used to investigate error propagation of other spatially coupled codes.
• Fourth, we introduce a threshold-based Max-Log-MAP (T-Max-Log-MAP) decoder for CCs. We then propose a decoder for BA-SC-CCs based on non-uniform quantization (NUQ), T-Max-Log-MAP decoder, and the TAMSA. The new decoder is referred to as the quantized T-MaxLog-MAP-TAMSA.
We present extensive numerical results to demonstrate the advantages of the BA-SC-CCs and the new decoder. Comparisons show that the terminated BA-SC-CCs perform as well as the sparsely braided convolutional code and perform better than the SC-LDPC codes, existing BMST codes, and the SCRCC codes. In particular, for a decoding latency of 6,000 bits, the BA-SC-CC performs 0.5 dB better than the (3,6)-regular SC-LDPC code. The numerical results also show that, for lowresolution quantization, performance gains about 0.2 dB can be obtained with the proposed quantized T-Max-Log-MAPTAMSA and 5-bits non-uniform quantization is sufficient for the quantized T-Max-Log-MAP-TAMSA to approach the performance of the floating-point based decoder.

Remark 2: In BA-SC-CCs, a block-oriented accumulator

is used to introduce coupling across multiple codewords of

a terminated CC. This structure can be interpreted as a

coupled extension of convolutional accumulate codes [55],

where the bit-wise accumulator is replaced with a block-

wise accumulator. It may also be viewed as a special case of

BMST codes [13], in which the terminated CC acts as the

component code. However, in this work, we do not frame

BA-SC-CC within the BMST paradigm, as our objective is

to emphasize its unique role in coupling convolutional codes. In particular, the proposed block-oriented accumulation of a terminated convolutional code admits a structural interpretation as a periodic SC-LDPC code. Specifically, the terminated convolutional code first applies a linear transformation to the input information blocks, after which a memory-one, blockoriented accumulator creates a periodic syndrome-former. Terminated convolutional codes are considered because their trellis structure is essential for the free distance analysis, a property not shared by many alternative block codes. They also admit efficient MAP decoding, whereas optimal decoding for many powerful block codes, such as BCH or LDPC codes, is typically impractical. The proposed threshold-based Max-Log-MAP decoder in Sec. V is built directly upon this MAP decoding framework. We emphasize that different component codes may be preferable in different parameter regimes. The purpose of this work is not to claim universal superiority of BA-SC-CCs, but to rigorously analyze a class of spatially coupled convolutional codes. This serves as an important step toward a rigorous structural understanding of BMST codes.
B. Iterative Sliding Window Decoder
Let y(t) denote the received signal sequence corresponding to c(t). Given y = (y(0), y(1), · · · , y(L+T −1)), the receiver attempts to recover u. Similar to the decoding of SC-LDPC codes, this recovery process can be achieved with a sliding window decoder over the normal graph of the BA-SC-CCs. We present in Fig. 2 the normal graphical realization of a BASC-CC. This normal graph consists of four types of constraint nodes, which are node CC, node =, node Π, and node +. The normal graph is described in detail in the following.
• Node =, which has degree three, represents the constraint that all connecting variables must take the same value. Nodes = are referred to as variable nodes.
• Node +, which has degree three, represents the constraint of the superposition relationship among the connecting variables. Nodes + are referred to as check nodes.
• Node Π, which has degree two, interleaves or deinterleaves the input messages.
• Node CC, which has degree two, represents the constraint specified by the CC.
• The normal graph can be divided into layers, where each layer typically consists of a CC node, a check node, a Π node, and a variable node.
Let d denote the decoding delay and W denote the decoding window size. For a decoding delay of d, the sliding window decoder works iteratively over a subgraph consisting of W = d+1 consecutive layers of the normal graph. An example of the sliding window decoder with d = 2 and W = 3 is illustrated in Fig. 2. We call the first layer in a decoding window the target layer.
For the sliding window decoder, the messages are updated in a layer-by-layer manner. An iteration of the sliding window decoder consists of a forward iteration and a backward iteration with the schedule shown in Fig. 3. Here, the t-th layer is the target layer. III. ANALYSIS OF BA-SC-CCS For ease of presentation, we only consider 1-input, n-output terminated convolutional codes with memory order m in this section. For such codes, the overall constraint length v is equal to m [56] and termination can be achieved by appending an appropriately selected length-m bit sequence at the end of the information sequence. For feedforward CC, the appended termination sequence is the length-m all-zero sequence. For feedback CC, the appended termination sequence depends on the final state after encoding the K information bits. For convenience, we use Ct[(K + m)n, K, m] to denote the terminated convolutional code used in BA-SC-CCs, where m denotes the memory order of the CC. The unterminated convolutional code corresponding to Ct[(K + m)n, K, m] is denoted as C. Note that our analytical results are applicable for any terminated convolutional codes since the analyses do not rely on their input numbers k. For convenience, we assume that L length-K sub-blocks are encoded jointly and T all-zero length-K sub-blocks are used for termination.

A. Threshold Analysis of BA-SC-CCs

For a fixed K, we obtain a BA-SC-CC ensemble by selecting the interleaver in each transmission uniformly at random. We call this ensemble the (C, K, L, T )-BA-SC-CC ensemble. By increasing the information length K, we can construct a sequence of (C, K, L, T )-BA-SC-CC ensembles. To analyze the asymptotic performance of (C, K, L, T )-BASC-CC ensembles when K → ∞, we use the extrinsic information transfer (EXIT) chart.
Consider binary phase-shift keying (BPSK) modulation and additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) channels. Without loss of generality, we assume that the messages are represented in the form of log-likelihood ratios (LLRs). C. Asymptotic Analysis of Error Propagation
Similar to braided convolutional codes, the BA-SC-CCs may experience decoder error propagation. That is, the decoding error of one layer may cause the decoding failures of its subsequent layers. The error propagation is rooted in the fact the current output sub-block depends on the previous output sub-blocks. In this subsection, we intend to analyze the influence of error propagation in long length regime.
Let the ρ-th layer be the first layer decoded in error. Since layers 0 through ρ − 1 are decoded correctly, they can be removed from the normal graph, resulting in the ρ-th layer having the same graphical structure as the 0-th layer. Therefore, without loss of generality, we assume ρ = 0. Due to block-oriented accumulation, the decoding performances of its subsequent layers will be affected. We are interested in the question that is it possible for the iterative sliding window decoder to successfully recover the t-th layer for some t > 0. Once the t-th layer is recovered, the harmful influence of the failed decoding of the 0-th layer is fully eliminated. To be specific, we analyze the minimum required SNR for the iterative sliding window decoder to recover from decoding failure. This minimum required SNR is referred to as the selfrecovery threshold of a BA-SC-CC ensemble.
In this paper, the worst-case analysis of the self-recovery threshold is carried out. Specifically, we consider the selfrecovery threshold of a (C, K, L, T )-BA-SC-CC ensemble under the assumption that the 0-th layer is erased. For simplicity, we restrict the decoder for recovery to be the iterative sliding window decoder, as opposed to the maximum likelihood decoder. Hence, this self-recovery threshold is referred to as constrained self-recovery threshold (CSRT). In practice, it is preferred that a decoding failure only affects the recovery of a finite number of layers. To be more specific, we consider the constrained self-recovery threshold with a window size

C. Simulation Results and Complexity Analysis
We present in Fig. 12 the BERs of a BA-SC-CC when decoded with the quantized T-Max-Log-MAP-TAMSA under different quantization schemes. Both uniform quantization and non-uniform quantization are considered. The terminated convolutional code C1[2604, 1300, 2] is taken as the basic code. For comparison, we also present in Fig. 12 the error performances of this BA-SC-CC when decoded with different decoders. For the non-quantized T-Max-Log-MAP-TAMSA, the parameters are set to τ0 = 0.75, τ1 = 4, and λ = 0.75. For -bit uniform quantization, the interval [−5, 5] is uniformly divided into 2 levels, resulting in a quantization step size of ∆ = 5/2 −1. The corresponding thresholding parameters are selected as follows. For = 3, we set τ0 = 3.125 and τ1 = 4.375. For = 4, we set τ0 = 1.563 and τ1 = 4.063. For = 5, we set τ0 = 1.094 and τ1 = 4.219. From Fig. 12, we have the following observations.
1) The T-Max-Log-MAP-TAMSA with 5 bits non-uniform quantization is sufficient to approach the performance of threshold-based decoder with floating-point operations.
2) For the proposed decoder, 4 bits non-uniform quantization performs 0.15 dB better than the 4 bits uniform quantization and the 3 bits non-uniform quantization performs 0.2 dB better than the 3 bits uniform quantization.
3) The T-Max-Log-MAP-TAMSA with 4 bits non-uniform quantization performs 0.3 dB better than the Max-LogMAP-AMSA with 4 bits non-uniform quantization and 0.5 dB better than the Max-Log-MAP-AMSA with 4 bits uniform quantization. These results confirm the effectiveness of the proposed threshold-based decoder. Note that, in Max-Log-MAP-AMSA, the CCs are decoded with the scaled Max-Log-MAP decoder and the check nodes are decoded with the AMSA.
For iterative decoders, decoding complexity is determined by both the computational cost per iteration and the average number of iterations required. In general, non-quantized decoders incur higher complexity than quantized decoders. For quantized decoders, the per-iteration complexity increases with the number of quantization levels, but remains comparable between threshold-based and conventional decoders when the same quantization resolution is used. This suggests that the overall decoding complexity is primarily governed by the average number of iterations. We consider a concrete example with 4 bits quantization for illustration. To achieve a BER of 10−5, the average iteration numbers of the T-Max-Log-MAP-TAMSA with non-uniform and uniform quantization are both approximately 2.7, demonstrating that these two decoding schemes yield nearly identical decoding complexity.
