A High-Rate-Compatible Algebraic GC-LDPC Code for 3-D TLC NAND Flash Memory

Linxin Yin , Xiongfei Zhai , Member, IEEE, Yi Fang , Senior Member, IEEE, Qi’ao Zhu, and Guojun Han , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—The escalating storage density of three-dimensional (3D) NAND flash memory introduces heightened channel noise, significantly degrading channel quality and exacerbating the raw bit error rate (RBER). The fixed and unchangeable code rate of the traditional global coupled low-density-parity-check (GC-LDPC) codes fails to address the performance mismatch arising from the stochastic temporal and spatial fluctuations in 3D NAND flash channels. To address the above issue efficiently, in this paper we investigate high-rate-compatible LDPC codes in 3D NAND flash memory. Firstly, we analyze the flash memory channel characteristics from the FPGA test platform and model the 3D triple-level cell (TLC) NAND flash channel. Subsequently, we propose a novel High-Rate-Compatible Algebraic GC-LDPC (HRC-A-GC-LDPC) code and conduct a comprehensive theoretical analysis of its structure and performance. Specifically, the HRC-A-GC-LDPC code features an information bit length of 4 KB and supports dynamic adjustment to multiple code rates, enabling it to match the varying characteristics of 3D NAND flash channels. This paper also examines and analyzes various construction methods for the HRC-A-GC-LDPC code corresponding to different column weights (CWs), providing insights into its design flexibility. Simulation results demonstrate that, in 3D TLC NAND flash memory, the proposed HRC-A-GC-LDPC code outperforms conventional GC-LDPC codes in both error correction capability and extended memory lifetime, validating its suitability for high-density 3D NAND flash applications.
Index Terms—3D TLC NAND flash memory, error correction code, HRC-A-GC-LDPC code, lifetime.

Advances in semiconductor storage technology have significantly improved both capacity and performance, enabling its widespread adoption in consumer electronics (e.g., smartphones and personal computers) and enterpriselevel servers. To reduce the per-bit storage cost of semiconductor memory, three-dimensional (3D) NAND flash memory has gradually superseded two-dimensional (2D) planar NAND flash, becoming the dominant storage medium in contemporary systems [2]. Over the past decade, the continuous evolution of NAND flash technology has driven a steady increase in the storage density of individual cells: from Single-Level Cells (SLCs) with 1 bit per cell, to Multi-Level Cells (MLCs, 2 bits per cell), Triple-Level Cells (TLCs, 3 bits per cell), Quad-Level Cells (QLCs, 4 bits per cell), and even emerging Penta-Level Cells (PLCs, 5 bits per cell). To meet the ever-growing demand for higher storage density, the manufacturing process of NAND flash has fully transitioned from 2D planar architectures to 3D vertical stacking technologies (e.g., 3D TLC/QLC NAND). However, this increase in storage density exacerbates issues such as charge leakage, read disturbance, and oxide layer degradation, rendering flash cells more susceptible to various types of noise, including Program/Erase (P/E) cycle noise, data retention noise, program disturbance noise, inter-cell interference, random telegraph noise (RTN), and read disturbance noise. These factors contribute to an exponential rise in the raw bit error rate (RBER), presenting significant challenges to the reliability of NAND flash memory [3].
Solid-State Drives (SSDs) widely employ error correction code (ECC) technologies during data read / write operations, significantly improving data reliability and extending the device lifetime [4]. The Bose-Chaudhuri-Hocquenghem (BCH) code was the first ECC scheme adopted in SSDs, utilizing hard decision decoding to correct bit errors in storage [5]. However, traditional BCH codes suffer from poor performance when faced with high RBER, especially as the cell density and P/E cycles increase. In contrast, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes have garnered considerable attention in recent years due to their superior error correction capabilities, particularly in enhancing data reliability within 3D NAND flash memory [6].

In the context of 3D NAND flash memory, employing a high code rate with insufficient error correction performance may lead to frequent read retry operations, adversely impacting I/O latency and overall throughput. Conversely, lowering the code rate enhances error correction strength but increases the parity bit overhead, thereby reducing the effective storage capacity available for user data. To maximize storage efficiency, 3D NAND flash devices typically employ high-rate LDPC codes in early life stages when the RBER remains low. However, as the number of P/E cycles and data retention time (RT) increase, the RBER rises progressively. Under these conditions, transitioning to a lower code rate becomes necessary to maintain the reliability of the ECC, despite the associated storage overhead.
To address the above issues, numerous studies have been reported in the literature. For instance, the authors in [7] proposed a lifetime-adaptive coding scheme. Specifically, their designed flash translation layer leverages weak ECC during the early operational stage of the memory and switches to strong ECC in the late stage. In [8], the authors classified the bit error rates (BERs) of distinct pages within the same wordline (WL) of 3D TLC NAND flash memory into different categories using a clustering algorithm. This classification enables flexible selection of varying code rates, thereby reducing storage overhead associated with ECC. In [9], an optimized accumulate-repeat-accumulate scheme was proposed for constructing high-rate protograph codes. However, a critical limitation is that the information bit length of this scheme scales with the code length, which fails to meet the requirement of fixed information bit length in 3D NAND flash memory systems. In [10], the authors constructed a rate-adaptive protograph LDPC code with multiple code rates to dynamically adapt to different P/E cycles. However, all the aforementioned works target MLC NAND flash channels. Consequently, they may not be extended to the currently mainstream TLC NAND flash channels. Besides, a prevalent industry consensus is to leverage the gradual transition to 4 KB LDPC codes as the core ECC technique for NAND flash memory. The 4 KB code length matches the size of the flash page. By optimizing the alignment, it enhances the read/write efficiency and reduces the extra wear caused by cross-sector writes, which in turn extends the service life of the flash memory. In [11], the authors proposed globally coupled LDPC (GC-LDPC) codes. Specifically, they employed full-valued matrices to construct both the global matrix of GC-LDPC codes and the base matrices of local matrices, while utilizing finite fields for constructing the shift value matrix. Owing to their flexible decoding schemes, GC-LDPC codes were soon applied to flash memory systems. In [12], the authors constructed a new GC-LDPC code tailored to the application requirements of NAND flash memory, enabling efficient decoder implementation and flexible decoding flow control. In [13], the authors proposed an improved adaptive granularity progressive LDPC decoding scheme that leverages the proposed GC-LDPC code, enabling significant reduction in flash memory read latency. However, existing technologies primarily target 2 KB GC-LDPC codes for 3D NAND

II. BACKGROUND
In this section, an overview of the basic principles of 3D NAND flash memory is presented and the factors that affect the data reliability are discussed. Then, we collect experimental data under different P/E cycles and RT using the non-volatile memory test device. Based on these experimental results, a mathematical model is established to verify the designed LDPC codes. Finally, the construction and encoding of GC-LDPC codes are briefly introduced.
A. Basic Principles of 3D NAND Flash Memory
The cells of 3D stacked NAND flash memory can be arranged in the vertical direction as 3D stacking. Besides, the 3D architecture enables the larger storage capacity by increasing the number of stacked tiers without scaling down the cell sizes [15].
As shown in Fig. 2, a 3D stacked flash memory block consists of multiple storage layers. Each layer is constructed by multiple WLs with each containing several logical pages. Source selective line (SSL) and drain selective line (DSL) are used to control data programming, reading, and erasing operations.
According to the number of bits stored in each memory cell, 3D stacked NAND flash memory could be categorized as the SLC type, the MLC type, and the TLC type [16].

Fig. 2. Structure of a 3D NAND flash memory block.
Fig. 3. TVD of 3D TLC NAND flash memory.
For the TLC type, each WL consists of three logical pages: the most significant bit (MSB) page, the central significant bit (CSB) page, and the least significant bit (LSB) page. As shown in Fig. 3, the threshold voltage distribution (TVD) of 3D TLC NAND flash memories can be divided into 8 parts corresponding to 8 states, respectively.
The minimum erasable unit is a block, while the minimum readable and programmable unit is a page. From Fig. 3, the voltage distributions are encoded in Gray code, where we consider [LSB, CSB, M SB] ∈ {111, 101, 100, 110, 010, 000, 001, 011} in this work. When reading the stored data, the read reference voltages (RRVs), i.e., Vref1 . . . Vref7, are harnessed to restore data. For LSB, if the detected voltage Vread is greater than Vref4, the stored data is 0, otherwise it is 1; For CSB, if Vref1 < Vread < Vref3 or Vref5 < Vread < Vref7, the stored data is 0, otherwise it is read as 1; For MSB, if Vref2 < Vread < Vref6, the stored data is 0, otherwise it is read as 1. The numbers of the RRVs required for LSB, CSB and MSB are 1, 4 and 2, respectively. When the TVD shifts due to some physical reasons, the BER of CSB will be higher than those of MSB and LSB.

C. Construction and Encoding of GC-LDPC Codes

The parity-check matrices of the traditional LDPC codes are usually the single integral structures, and the encoding/decoding complexity grows exponentially when designing ultra-long codewords. To address this issue, the GC-LDPC codes split the information sequence into multiple local code blocks (LCBs), each capable of independent encoding and decoding. Then, a global code block (GCB) is introduced to couple all the LCBs together. The GC-LDPC codes can be ultra-long by replicating and expanding more LCBs, which significantly reduces the encoding and decoding complexity. Due to this coupled design, the sparsity of parity-check matrix is improved and the coding/decoding latency can be reduced by parallel processing. Besides, the decoding performance can be further optimized by exchanging the correct soft information among LCBs through the GCB.
The LCBs and GCBs typically exhibit the quasi-cyclic (QC) structure, leading to parity-check matrices consisting of cyclic permutation matrices (CPMs). This structured design can significantly simplify the hardware implementation [22], [23], [24], [25]. III. PROPOSED HRC-A-GC-LDPC CODES
In NAND flash memory applications, codeword lengths are typically selected from industry-standard specifications (e.g., 1 KB, 2 KB, 4 KB) to ensure optimal alignment with the physical page sizes [31]. Meanwhile, the ECC for NAND flash memory requires an ultra-high code rate (≥ 0.85) to minimize redundant overhead and maximize storage density [10].

In addition, the information bits and the parity bits are interleaved, resulting in more complexity when separating them after decoding. In our proposed encoding scheme, the swap of rows and columns is eliminated and the parity bits can be obtained by matrix multiplication, where the encoding complexity is reduced to O(n) [37]. The information bits and parity bits after direct encoding can be distinguished, facilitating the construction of LDPC parity-check matrices with different code rates and longer code lengths [38].
For GC-LDPC codes, the LCBs are decoupled, they can be decoded independently. Although the decoding process of

our proposed codes can be flexible, we mainly consider an efficient two stage decoding algorithm (TSDA), which consists of the LCB decoding stage and the entire parity-check matrix (EPCM) decoding stage, as shown in Fig. 17. Specifically, each stage adopts the Normalized Min-Sum (NMS) decoding algorithm. We set the maximum number of iterations for the LCB decoding stage and the EPCM decoding stage to TL and TG, respectively. When the iteration number of LCB decoding stage reaches TL, the EPCM decoding stage starts. If the result of each LCB is correct, the decoding information bits are output. Otherwise, the EPCM decoding stage is initiated. If the EPCM decoding stage fails when IG = TG, the decoding process is terminated and the information bits are output (decoding failure).
For 3D NAND flash channels, the page serves as the basic unit for data access. Due to differences in manufacturing processes, the error characteristics vary among different pages. Hence, TL and TG in the TSDA can be optimized according to the channel characteristics of different pages. For instance, TL and TG can be increased to ensure the reliability when the pages suffer from the serious RBER. Also, we can reduce TL and TG to improve the throughput for the reliable pages.
Thus TSDA can dynamically adapt to 3D NAND flash channels. To better compare the performance of the considered GC-LDPC codes, the decoding scheme with a fixed number of iterations is still adopted.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we first introduced the error characteristics of 3D NAND flash memory, the 3D TLC NAND flash channel model based on the test data from the Nanocycler platform and the traditional GC-LDPC codes. Then, we proposed several HRC-A-GC-LDPC codes with two construction methods: one aims at offering higher reliability, while the other one can achieve a higher maximum code rate. In particular, according to the varying RBER of LSB page which suffers from the most unreliability in 3D TLC NAND flash memory, the proposed coding schemes can dynamically adjust the code rate. In addition, the corresponding low-complexity encoding algorithm and TSDA were proposed. Finally, in AWGN channels, we conducted a theoretical analysis of submatrices and EPCMs to demonstrate that the proposed LDPC codes exhibit excellent performance. Besides, in 3D TLC NAND flash channels, the results verify that the proposed codes demonstrate superior error-correction threshold and extended endurance compared to state-of-the-art GC-LDPC designs in terms of performance, while also offering more flexibility of selecting the code rates. Therefore, the proposed HRC-A-GC-LDPC codes are expected to improve the data reliability of 3D TLC NAND flash memory and extend the lifetime of flash memory. Our future work will focus on adapting the proposed HRC-AGC-LDPC codes to higher-density storage technologies, such as QLC and PLC NAND flash, to combat their even more stringent reliability constraints.
