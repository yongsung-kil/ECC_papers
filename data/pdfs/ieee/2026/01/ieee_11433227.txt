1Samsung Research America, Plano, TX 75024, USA 2Samsung Research, Seoul 06765, South Korea

ABSTRACT Two methods are proposed to reduce the complexity of the belief propagation (BP) decoding for low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, with the focus on the layered decoding for quasi-cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes. The two methods can be separately incorporated into the layered decoding procedures and combined seamlessly to provide the two-fold complexity reduction. In the first method, the message updates regarding a subset of variable nodes are omitted. To construct the subset, the most reliable bit-level channels, derived from M -ary input channels, are identified. Then, we select the variable nodes with initial log-likelihood ratios (LLRs) exceeding a threshold among the ones assigned to the most reliable bit-level channels to form the subset. The threshold is designed and adjusted based on characteristics of the most reliable bit-level channels. Our numerical results demonstrate that the first method significantly reduces the complexity with negligibly impacting the error-correcting performance. In the second method, each layer of base graphs of QC-LDPC codes uses either sum-product algorithm (SPA) or min-sum algorithm (MSA) to update LLRs. A degree-based algorithm is presented to determine the layers employing either SPA or MSA. We compare with two intuitive methods: 1) applying SPA to randomly selected layers and MSA to the remaining layers, and 2) applying SPA to the core layers of 5G NR LDPC codes and MSA to the remaining layers. According to the numerical results, the degree-based algorithm maximally reduces the complexity while maintaining the same performance. To evaluate the joint method, the first method is applied on top of the second method. Further complexity reduction is achieved without degrading the performance of the second method.

I. INTRODUCTION LDPC codes [1] are known for approaching channel capacity asymptotically, by using the BP decoding which allows for practical implementation and delivers high decoding throughput. As a result, they are adopted by industrial communication systems such as digital television broadcast via satellites (DVB-S2) and Wi-Fi, and are also employed for the data transmission in the 5th Generation (5G) New Radio (NR) standard [2], achieving a throughput of tens of gigabits per second.
The associate editor coordinating the review of this manuscript and approving it for publication was Pietro Savazzi .

The BP decoding is essential for the successful implementation of LDPC codes. It is performed by iteratively passing messages within a Tanner graph representing LDPC codes. The graph consists of check nodes (CNs), variable nodes (VNs) and edges connecting them. The iterative message passing occurs between CNs and VNs which are neighbors, and continues until either the decoding converges or the maximum number of iterations is reached. As the convergence speed of the BP decoding increases, the number of iterations needed to obtain a valid codeword decreases, and vice versa.
Conventionally, the arrangement of updating messages depends on the flooding schedule, which is fulfilled by iterating the following three main steps: VNs report their

knowledge of bit variables to CNs in parallel; CNs combine the received information and pass back the extrinsic information in parallel; VNs update their knowledge in parallel, based on the extrinsic information from CNs. Although benefiting from full parallelism, the flooding schedule suffers from a slow convergence speed, because the updated knowledge are not utilized until next iteration. To speed up convergence, the BP decoding with the (row) layered schedule, commonly known as the layered decoding, was introduced in [3] and [4]. During each iteration, CNs are processed sequentially. When processing each CN, its neighboring VNs report their knowledge to the CN. Then, the CN returns new insights to the neighboring VNs for updating the knowledge. The sequential processing ensures the latest knowledge from VNs to be immediately exploited by the succeeding CNs. Sharon et al. [5] proved that the layered decoding converges twice as fast as the BP decoding with the flooding schedule, therefore reducing the necessary number of iterations by half [3].
In a Tanner graph, two CNs are considered disjoint if they do not share any common VNs. This property facilitates the partial parallelism in the layered decoding. Specifically, the CNs, which are mutually disjoint, can be processed in parallel without read-write conflicts. This partial parallelism can be efficiently realized for QC-LDPC codes [6]. CNs in a QC-LDPC Tanner graph can be divided into small number of groups and each group contains disjoint CNs. These groups are referred to as layers. In each layered decoding iteration, the layers are updated in order while the CNs of the same layer can be updated simultaneously.
The near-optimal performance of LDPC codes is guaranteed by SPA [1] which specifies the computations needed for updating messages. The accurate computations of the hyperbolic functions provided by SPA incur a significant increase in the computational complexity. By using its approximated versions, such as MSA [7], the normalized MSA [8] and the offset MSA [9], the computations are simplified at the cost of degradation in performance.
Other attempts to reduce the decoding complexity include skipping certain message calculations [10], [11], [12], [13], [14], [15]. Zimmermann et al. [10] proposed to detect the reliability of the VNs in each iteration and stop updating the reliable VNs in the subsequent iterations. The reliability depends on the comparison between the a posteriori LLRs (AP-LLRs) of the VNs and a threshold. This method is enhanced by employing an iteratively updated threshold [11], skipping LLR updates for the reliable CNs [12], [13], flipping wrong decisions [14] and deleting already converged VNs from the Tanner graph [15]. However, the above methods introduce extra computational complexity mainly due to the detection of the reliable VNs in each iteration.
This paper presents methods to reduce the complexity of the BP decoding. In the 5G NR and Wi-Fi standards, QC-LDPC codes are adopted to ensure the system reliability, with layered decoding commonly employed for implementation. Their superiority results from the aforementioned fast
39808

convergence and (partially) parallel processing, alongside additional advantages like efficient encoding and flexible rate matching. This paper elaborates and validates the proposed methods based on the layered decoding for QC-LDPC codes.
The first method skips the LLR updates for a subset of VNs, referred to as the deactivated VNs (dVNs). The non-uniform reliability of bit-level channels in bit-interleaved coded modulation (BICM) systems is utilized to identify dVN. The identification depends on the characteristics of the initial LLRs. Therefore, the set of dVNs can be determined prior to performing the layered decoding and remain unchanged throughout the decoding iterations. An LLR threshold, obtained from the analysis of the probability density function (PDF) of the initial LLRs, is adjusted to manage the trade-off between the complexity and the errorcorrecting performance.
The second method benefits from both the precise LLR calculations and the low complexity, provided by SPA and MSA, respectively. Hybrid sum-product and min-sum (hSPMSA), where each layer employs either SPA or MSA, is proposed to achieve this goal. By analyzing the contributions of SPA and MSA to the complexity of hSPMSA, an algorithm for selecting the layers employing SPA and MSA is proposed to optimize the performance-complexity trade-off. Shortly, SPA is preferentially employed by the layers with less number of neighbors. Furthermore, the two methods can be integrated into the layered decoding together without any conflict, thereby obtaining the combined complexity reduction.
5G NR LDPC codes at a wide range of code rates and lengths are simulated to evaluate the proposed methods. The error-correcting performance and the decoding complexity are measured by the block error rate (BLER) and the number of float-point operations (FLOPs), respectively. By deactivating VNs, up to 33% of FLOPs can be saved with negligible impact on performance. In hSPMSA, the proposed algorithm for selecting the layers employing SPA and MSA achieves a 20% reduction in FLOPs compared to the random selection, while preserving the same level of performance.
II. PRELIMINARIES A. NOTATION Zi:j ≜ {i, i + 1, · · · , j} represents the set of integers from i to j, with the abbreviation Zj ≜ Z0:j for convenience. |A| denotes the number of elements in a set A. The inclusion and exclusion of an element a for the set A are represented as A + a and A − a, respectively. A\B denotes the set of elements that are in the set A but not in the set B. Given a matrix M of dimensions m × n, we use Mj,[0:n−1], M[0:m−1],i and Mj,i to represent the row j, the column i and the entries positioned at the row j and the column i, respectively.
B. LDPC CODES An (N , K ) LDPC code is defined by a (binary) parity check matrix H of dimensions (N − K ) × N , where N and K denote the code length and the information size, respectively. Each row in the parity check matrix represents a parity check

constraint. A valid codewrod, denoted v ≜ {v0, · · · , vN−1}, must satisfy all parity check constraints, that says H ×v⊺ = 0.
The parity check matrix can be mapped to a bipartite graph, namely Tanner graph. It consists of N VNs and (N − K ) CNs. Additionally, there exists an edge connecting the CN j and the VN i if Hj,i is non-zero. A CN and a VN are neighbors if they are connected. The degree of a CN is defined as the number of its neighboring VNs. The VN i corresponds to the codeword bit vi, i.e., the column i of the parity check matrix. The CN j corresponds to the parity check constraint Hj,[0:N−1] × v⊺ = 0, i.e., the row j of the parity check matrix. Messages (usually in the form of LLRs) are iteratively exchanged in a scheduled order between the neighboring nodes for the decoding purpose.

III. REDUCED-COMPLEXITY LAYERED DECODING A. DEACTIVATING VARIABLE NODES As illustrated by Step 10 of Algorithm 1, hard decisions are made based on AP-LLRs. A bit is decoded correctly with a high probability if the absolute value of its AP-LLR is large enough. For convenience, when referring to a ‘‘large’’ LLR, we are implying that the absolute value of the LLR is significant.
With the decoding iterations, AP-LLRs increase and eventually converge, indicating their stability in the subsequent iterations and resulting in stable decisions. Therefore, when an AP-LLR is large enough, it can be considered to deactivate the corresponding VN by skipping the relevant LLR updates subsequently, to reduce the decoding complexity. AP-LLRs converge at different rates across individual VNs, and some of them become large in just a few iterations while others stay small. Monitoring their convergence and deactivating VNs individually in each iteration causes additional complexity. In this paper, we construct the set of dVNs solely based on initial LLRs. The construction is completed before the decoding iterations start and remains unchanged during the decoding iterations.

4) SHORTENED VARIABLE NODES
To accommodate rate and length flexibility, the shortening technique may be applied to LDPC codes as in the case of 5G NR LDPC codes described in Section II-B2. The shortening is realized by freezing certain systematic bits to zeros. Because the positions and the values of the shortened bits are revealed to the decoder, the corresponding initial LLRs are set to infinity. In the proposed method, we also characterize the shortened VNs as the set D of dVNs.

B. HYBRID SUM-PRODUCT AND MIN-SUM ALGORITHM
The primary advantage of SPA is its ability to achieve near-optimal performance by accurately computing the C2V LLRs. However, the involved hyperbolic tangent operations require a significant amount of decoding time and resources, which limits the decoding throughput. MSA, which approximates the hyperbolic tangent operations, can enhance the decoding throughput at the expense of the performance.
Based on the fact that each layer in the layered decoding are updated independently, our hSPMSA applies either SPA or MSA to each respective layer. The layers employing SPA and MSA are referred to as the SPA and MSA layers, respectively. By carefully determining the SPA and MSA layers, hSPMSA can exploit both the high computation accuracy offered by SPA and the reduced complexity offered by MSA. SIMULATIONS AND DISCUSSIONS The encoding procedures for the evaluations of the proposed methods comply with the 5G NR standard [2], which incorporates CRC encoding, systematic puncturing,4 rate matching and rectangular interleaving. The proposed methods are applicable to any code rate. However, the decoding procedures for high-rate codes subtly impact the system throughput, since the number of effective layers is small, as outlined in Section II-B2. This paper focuses on evaluating 5G NR LDPC codes with low-tomoderate code rates {0.3, 0.4, 0.5} across information sizes {200, 300, · · · , 1000} ∪ {1200, 1400, · · · , 8400}. QPSK, QAM-64 and QAM-256 modulations, whose mapping rules follow the 5G NR standard, are employed.
During the layered decoding, the sequence π({0, 1, · · · , n − k − 1}) utilized to determine the order of updating layers refers to [21] Section III-B. The syndrome check is conducted by using the sub-matrix [A, B] instead of the entire parity check matrix. Specifically, a decoding success is declared if [A, B] × vˆT = 0 is satisfied. The maximum number of iterations is set as 10.
The FLOP analysis in Section IV is utilized to evaluate the complexity of the layered decoding. The number of FLOPs naturally grow with the information size. To illustrate the reduction in FLOPs across information sizes ranging from 200 to 8400 on a consistent scale, the ratio of the reduced FLOPs relative to a baseline is presented. To assess the impact of the proposed methods on the error-correcting performance, the SNRs required to attain a target BLER are also presented. Following the requirement of the 3GPP NR enhanced mobile broadband (eMBB) [22], we set the target BLER as 10−2. ANI t¯ is equal to the empirical mean of the number of iterations at the corresponding desired SNR. In every instance of MSA, the offset MSA with the offset value 0.5 is utilized.

VI. CONCLUSION We introduce two methods, namely dVNs and hSPMSA, to reduce the complexity of the layered decoding for QCLDPC codes. dVNs can be enhanced by utilizing the unequal-error-protection provided by the bit-level channels in BICM systems. A variable node is deactivated when it is assigned to the most reliable bit-level channels and its initial LLR is larger than a threshold. The threshold is also carefully designed on the basis of the PDF of the initial LLRs.
VOLUME 14, 2026

The LLR updates regarding dVNs are omitted. In hSPMSA, we combine SPA and MSA by implementing them across different layers. A degree-based method is proposed to determine the layers employing SPA and MSA. Specifically, SPA tends to be employed by the low-degree layers, while MSA is applied to the remaining layers. In addition, we can incorporate dVNs and hSPMSA into the layered decoding together without any conflict to provide joint reduction in complexity. Through comprehensively evaluating 5G NR LDPC codes, the proposed methods demonstrate outstanding complexity-performance trade-off.
