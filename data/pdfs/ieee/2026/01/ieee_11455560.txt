Perturbed Derivative Decoding Based on Ordered Statistics for Cyclic Codes

Shuyan Yu , Tianyu Li , and Qin Huang , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—It has been shown that perturbations can improve the performance of ordered statistics decoding (OSD). In this paper, we focus on perturbations for derivative decoding based on OSD (DD-OSD) for cyclic codes, where the results of OSD in different derivative directions vote for the estimate of codewords. It reveals that the local maximum likelihood decoding (l-MLD) errors in derivative directions may trap DD-OSD. It proves that such traps can be detected by the null space of the derivative ascendants of cyclic codes, thereby guiding us to perform perturbations to avoid the traps. Simulation results show that perturbed DD-OSD with order-1 can achieve 1.3 dB gain over OSD with order-4 for extended BCH codes, and perform closely to maximum likelihood decoding with moderate complexity.
Index Terms—Ordered statistics decoding, derivative decoding, perturbation.
I. INTRODUCTION
O RDERED statistics decoding (OSD) is a general decoding algorithm that offers asymptotically optimal performance for linear codes of short to medium length [1]. In OSD, the received bits and generator matrix are re-ordered according to reliability. Then the re-ordered generator matrix is transformed into the systematic form using Gaussian elimination. This places the most reliable independent positions (MRIPs) into the information positions. The union of MRIPs and the most reliable positions beside MRIPs is referred to as the control band. The information bits and test error patterns (TEPs) are added to be re-encoded, and the most likely candidate codeword is output as the decoding result.
For OSD, errors in MRIPs and maximum likelihood decoding (MLD) errors determine the performance. On the one hand, the errors in MRIPs are exhaustively tested using TEPs across various orders. As the decoding order increases, the performance of OSD improves. For the linear code C(n, k) with length n, dimension k and minimum distance d, OSD with order l = d/4 − 1 can asymptotically approach the performance of MLD. Thus, many important studies focus on reducing the complexity of high-order OSD or achieving

It shows in [8] that perturbations or multiple random bases on MRIPs can enhance the performance of OSD by generating multiple information sets, thereby increasing the likelihood of selecting one with fewer errors for correct re-encoding. On the other hand, when MLD errors occur, it leads directly to the decoding failure.
Recently, derivative decoding based on ordered statistics decoding (DD-OSD) has been proposed to significantly enhance OSD for extended cyclic codes [18], [19]. It consists of three steps and runs in an iterative manner. First, it calculates the log-likelihood-ratio (LLR) of minimal derivative descendants (mDDs) in various directions. Then, it decodes these mDDs by OSD. Finally, the mDD decoding estimates are used to vote for the estimate of the original cyclic code. Due to the small dimension of mDDs and the voting scheme, it can outperform OSD with higher-order for extended cyclic codes.
As MLD errors lead to the failure of OSD, this paper demonstrates that the local MLD (l-MLD) errors in derivative directions may trap the decoder of DD-OSD. In particular, we prove that l-MLD errors in derivative directions can result from a wrong estimate in DD-OSD that is not a legal codeword of the original cyclic code, but belongs to its cyclic derivative ascendant (cDA). In other words, we can detect such decoding traps by the null space of the original code and its cDA. Once a trap is detected, we propose to escape from the current trapped decoding and restart the decoding with perturbations on the control band. It may move errors out of MRIPs in mDDs, thus helping to avoid the trap.
In order to make perturbations efficient, OSD usually selects bits with higher reliabilities from the control band for perturbations. However, we propose perturbing bits with lower reliabilities in the control band for derivative decoding. Since a derivative bit in an mDD depends on two bits of the original codeword, its reliability is largely determined by the original bit with lower reliability. Perturbing the bits with lower reliabilities is more likely to cause the derivative bits to be perturbed, thereby reducing the reliabilities of errors. As a result, such perturbations can effectively move errors out of MRIPs in mDDs, significantly improving the performance of these perturbed mDDs.

B. DD-OSD
The derivative decoding scheme for cyclic codes and extended cyclic codes is proposed in [18] and [19], which improves the performance of OSD. Cyclic codes form a wellknown class of linear codes with a cyclic structure. This class includes BCH codes [21], [22], Reed-Muller (RM) codes [23], [24], and others. The extended cyclic codes are formed by adding an overall parity-check bit to the codewords of cyclic codes.
Consider the extended cyclic codes C(n, k) with length n and dimension k. The derivative decoding scheme proposes to decode C(n, k) based on their mDDs in different directions. The mDDs are defined as the linear subspace containing the derivatives of all codewords in different directions with the smallest dimension. Their dimension is the same in different directions and much smaller than that of the original codes [18], [25]. For example, for the (128, 36) eBCH code, its mDDs in different directions are a (64, 14) linear code. Moreover, by analyzing the Mattson-Solomon polynomials and their exponent sets [26], [27], it has been proven that the mDDs in different directions are equivalent codes. As a result, the derivative decoding is performed using a single decoder for the mDDs in different directions.
Specifically, we first introduce the notation used in the derivative decoding. Let F2m denote the finite field of 2m elements. Let α denote a primitive element of F2m . Let α∞ denote the additive identity in F2m . The codeword coordinates are indexed by the elements of F2m . Let c = [cαi , i ∈ I] denote a codeword of C(n, k), where I = {[n − 1] {∞}}. Each derivative direction is represented by an element β ∈ F2m , and the set of all derivative directions is denoted by B. The derivative of a given codeword c = [cαi , i ∈ I] in the direction β is defined as
cβ = [cβαi , i ∈ I] = [cαi+β + cαi , i ∈ I].
Instead of directly decoding c, the derivative decoding applies effective decoders to the derivative codewords cβ for β ∈ B.

III. PERTURBATIONS FOR DD-OSD
In this section, we first investigate the occurrence and detection of decoding traps that result from l-MLD errors in DD-OSD. Such traps can be detected by the null space of the original code and its cDA. For the detectable decoding traps, we propose to escape from the current trapped decoding and restart the decoding with perturbations on the control band to avoid the traps. We present a perturbation method that effectively moves errors out of MRIPs in mDDs, thereby significantly improving their performance. Finally, the proposed PDD-OSD algorithm is presented based on the detection of decoding traps and perturbations.

A. Decoding Trap in DD-OSD
Recall that an MLD error directly leads to OSD decoding failure, even when the number of errors does not exceed the order l. Moreover, such errors cannot be mitigated by applying perturbations or by refining the information set in other ways. In the following, we investigate the occurrence of decoding traps caused by l-MLD errors in DD-OSD. We regard the MLD error that occurs in a single OSD of DD-OSD, as an l-MLD error. We prove that the sufficient conditions for this decoding trap are as follows: the voted estimate of DD-OSD is not a legal codeword of the original cyclic code, but belongs to its cDA. This enables the detection of decoding traps during the decoding process.
Before presenting the next theorem that investigates the occurrence of such decoding traps, we briefly recall key definitions from [18] and [19]. The cyclic derivative descendant (cDD), denoted by D(C), is the extended cyclic code with the smallest dimension which contains all the derivative codewords of C and consists of mDDs. The cDA of cDDs, denoted by A(D(C)), is the extended cyclic code with the largest dimension such that its derivative codewords belong to cDDs. Considering the w-th decoding iteration of DD-OSD, let L(w) = [L(αwi ), i ∈ I] denote the updated LLR vector and c(w) = [cα(wi ), i ∈ I] denote its hard decision, respectively.

C. Effective Perturbations
In this subsection, we first present the lemma for selecting the index sets of perturbations to effectively move errors out of MRIPs for mDDs. It reveals that within the specified range, a lower reliability of the perturbed bit leads to a greater number of perturbed mDDs, whose reliabilities of erroneous bits are reduced. Then, we further analyze the error probability of mDDs with effective perturbations.
Consider the direction β and OSD with order l in an mDD. Let kM denote the dimension of mDDs. Let {ie0 , · · · , ieh } denote the perturbed errors index set with coordinates {αj0 , · · · , αjh } in L. Note that {ie0 , · · · , ieh } ⊂ I. If the corresponding bits in I are all correct, then {ie0 , · · · , ieh } is an empty set.
Lemma 1: Consider the index set of perturbation I = {i0, i1, · · · , is−1}. To move the error bits out of MRIPs via a perturbation, it is required to satisfy that
max{i0, i1, · · · , is−1} ≤ n/2 + kM.
Among the perturbed bits corresponding to I, the error bits with a non-empty index subset {ie0 , · · · , ieh } lead to a reduction of the error reliabilities in at least max{ie0 , · · · , ieh } − 1 mDDs.
Proof: In this proof, we first show the valid range for selecting the index set to move errors out of MRIPs of mDDs. Then, we present perturbing low-reliability error bits within this range leads to a larger number of mDDs to be perturbed.
Consider an arbitrary perturbed bit with index iq ∈ I and coordinate αjq . Suppose that iq > n/2 + kM. In this case, at most n/2 − kM − 1 bits have smaller amplitude values. Equation (2) can be simplified based on min-sum approximation [28], i.e.,

where sign(·) denotes the sign function and αj = αi + β. Under this approximation, the reliability of a derivative bit is determined by the original bit with a lower reliability.
Thus, the number of bits in mDDs whose amplitudes are smaller than |Lαβjq | is less than n/2 − kM. In other words, the perturbed bit is already outside MRIPs in mDDs even before introducing perturbations, and the perturbations do not affect the errors in MRIPs of mDDs. As a result, to move the error bits out of MRIPs via a perturbation, the index set must satisfy max{i0, i1, · · · , is−1} ≤ n/2 + kM.

D. Perturbed DD-OSD
In this subsection, the details of PDD-OSD are presented, and its flowchart is shown in Fig. 2.
Consider transmitting a codeword c of the extended cyclic code C(n, k) over an AWGN channel using BPSK modulation. PDD-OSD is performed based on mDDs, while the decoding traps are detected based on cDA. The PDD-OSD decoding process consists of the following phases:
1) Perturbation: Given the perturbed index set I and the perturbed factor λ, perturbations are introduced to the original input LLR vector L according to Function 1, Perturbation, and obtain the perturbed LLR vector L¯ .
2) DD-OSD: The derivatives of C in different directions β ∈ B are decoded by OSD. The decoding estimates of mDDs are used for voting the estimated LLR of C, i.e., L˜ . The hard
decision estimate cˆ = [cˆαi , i ∈ I] is given by cˆαi = 1[L˜αi <
0]. 3) Detection of codeword: Let H denote the parity-check
matrix of C. If HcˆT = 0 is satisfied, it outputs the hard decision estimate cˆ which is a codeword in C. Here, T denotes the transpose. Otherwise, the process proceeds to the detection of decoding trap.
4) Detection of decoding trap: Let HA denote the paritycheck matrix of cDA. If HAcˆT = 0 but HcˆT = 0, it indicates that the hard decision estimate cˆ belongs to its cDA A(D(C)) but not to the original code C. In this case, the decoding process has fallen into a decoding trap, and the original LLR vector needs to be perturbed.
In particular, PDD-OSD takes the LLR vector L as input and operates iteratively. It contains two layers of loop: an inner loop and an outer loop. In the decoding process, the inner loop is executed to perform DD-OSD, the detection of legal codeword and the detection of decoding traps, while the outer loop handles the introduction of perturbations. If DD-OSD votes for a non-legal codeword and converges to a decoding trap, the decoding process moves to the outer loop, where perturbations are introduced to the original LLR vector L.

At the end of the subsection, we further extend our investigation to other state-of-the-art decoding algorithms with perturbation schemes [31], [32], [33], [34]. These perturbation schemes have been shown to be effective for low-density parity-check codes and polar codes. Specifically, an impulsive perturbation scheme for belief propagation (BP) decoding [31] perturbs a small number of the least reliable received symbols in the opposite direction with a large fixed magnitude. In [32] and [33], stochastic perturbation methods are proposed for successive cancellation (SC) decoding, either by injecting Gaussian noise or by exploiting the sequential intermediate computations and decision updates inherent in SC decoding. In [34], a hybrid perturbation-based SC decoding scheme for long polar codes is proposed, which first applies random

perturbation and then triggers biased perturbation on disagree bits according to cyclic redundancy check (CRC) feedback. This scheme achieves excellent performance comparable to that of CRC-aided SC list decoding. As a result, we further investigate three adapted perturbation schemes within the proposed framework, including impulsive perturbation, stochastic perturbation, and hybrid perturbation. We denote them as PImpulsive, PStochastic and PHybrid, respectively.
Consider the (128, 29) eBCH code. The LLRs of the perturbed bits are forced to 20 with reversed signs. For the perturbation scheme PStochastic, we introduce 5 different perturbations with tmax = 5, where the standard deviations of the Gaussian noise are set to 0.0125, 0.0250, 0.0375, 0.0500 and 0.0625, respectively. For the perturbation scheme PHybrid, we also introduce 5 different perturbations with tmax = 5. Once a decoding trap is detected, Gaussian noise is first injected to escape the local optimum; if two consecutive detected codewords are identical in the subsequent decoding attempts, biased perturbation is then applied based on the disagree bits. The disagree bits are selected according to the method in [34]. The simulation results show that PDD-OSD (1) outperforms DD-OSD (1) with the other three perturbation schemes.

V. CONCLUSION
To conclude, the main contributions of the paper are
• The l-MLD errors in derivative directions may trap DDOSD, and such decoding traps are detected based on cDA.
• Perturbations help escape from the current trapped decoding and further avoid the traps by moving errors out of MRIPs in mDDs.
• A probability analysis is provided to theoretically evaluate the error probability of perturbed mDD.
Furthermore, more advanced perturbation strategies and parameter selection methods may further improve the performance of PDD-OSD, which we will leave for future investigation.
