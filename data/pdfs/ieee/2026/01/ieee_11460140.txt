Offset Gradient Descent Bit-Flipping Decoding for Irregular Parity-Check Codes
WOOHONG MIN AND KYEONGCHEOL YANG , (Senior Member, IEEE)
Department of Electrical Engineering, Pohang University of Science and Technology (POSTECH), Pohang-si, Gyeongsangbuk-do 37673, Republic of Korea
Corresponding author: Kyeongcheol Yang (kcyang@postech.ac.kr)

ABSTRACT A key challenge for decoding irregular parity-check codes under the gradient descent bitflipping (GDBF) algorithm is that the irregularity in column degrees reduces the accuracy of the reliability measurements obtained through a flipping function (FF). To address the challenge, we introduce a novel approach based on the gradient descent bit-flipping with momentum (GDBF-w/M) algorithm. We first propose an offset GDBF-w/M (OGDBF-w/M) algorithm for decoding column-irregular codes over the additive white Gaussian noise channel, where the FF includes an offset term depending on column degrees. We then propose a diversity-based offset GDBF-w/M (D-OGDBF-w/M) algorithm which employs multiple OGDBF-w/M decoders with distinct offset values. Furthermore, we extend the proposed algorithms to the binary symmetric channel, although they are initially intended for the additive white Gaussian noise channel. Numerical results demonstrate that they achieve significant improvements in decoding performance for irregular parity-check codes, particularly for column-irregular codes.

I. INTRODUCTION Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes [1], renowned for their excellent error-correcting capability, are channel codes adopted in various standards including fifthgeneration (5G) wireless communications, Wi-Fi, and DVB-S2. When decoded by message-passing (MP) algorithms, they exhibit capacity-approaching performance over various channels such as the additive white Gaussian noise channel (AWGNC) and the Rayleigh fading channel [2], [3], [4]. Herein, MP algorithms generally refer to iterative decoding schemes that exchange and update the messages of variable nodes (VNs) and check nodes (CNs) along the edges of a Tanner graph representing the parity-check matrix (PCM) of an LDPC code. Among them, the sum-product (SP) algorithm as a prominent example demonstrates suboptimal decoding performance for LDPC codes. The min-sum (MS) algorithm is a simplified version of the SP algorithm and has a slight performance degradation. This degradation may be reduced considerably by the offset min-sum (OMS) and

normalized min-sum (NMS) algorithms which introduce an offset and a normalization factor, respectively, to the CN message update of the MS algorithm. In most cases, the computational complexity of an MP algorithm is heavily dependent on the number of quantization bits for the messages. While nonuniform quantization can improve decoding performance [5], [6], it also significantly increases implementation complexity, which motivates the search for simpler decoding schemes.
As a low-complexity alternative, bit-flipping (BF)-based algorithms represent a family of decoders that avoid the need for quantization. These algorithms operate by passing singlebit messages along the edges and flip a set of estimated bits under certain conditions. While MP algorithms update the messages on an edge basis, BF-based algorithms deal with one-bit messages on a node-basis. Despite the significant reduction in computational complexity, BF-based algorithms typically exhibit inferior decoding performance relative to MP algorithms.
The gradient descent BF (GDBF) algorithm [7] formulates the decoding problem as an optimization problem and employs the gradient descent (GD) method to define

a flipping function (FF). It shows better decoding performance than the conventional BF-based algorithms, but fails to achieve theoretically optimal performance due to the inherent nonlinearity of the problem. This behavior is similar to the situation where the GD method does not guarantee optimal solutions to nonconvex optimization problems. In fact, the decoder under the GDBF algorithm may be trapped in certain states, leading to a decoding failure despite a sufficient number of decoding iterations. To address such a limitation, the probabilistic GDBF (PGDBF) and noisy GDBF (NGDBF) algorithms incorporate randomness into the GDBF algorithm. In the PGDBF algorithm, a group of bits based on the FF is selected, and each bit in the group is flipped with a specific probability [8]. On the other hand, the NGDBF algorithm introduces randomness by adding Gaussian noise to the FF [9].
Another approach to overcome the limit of the GDBF algorithm is to utilize optimization strategies such as variants of tabu search or the momentum method. The tabu-list aided PGDBF (TPGDBF) algorithm combines tabu search with the PGDBF algorithm so that the bits flipped in the current iteration cannot be flipped again in the next iteration [10]. The tabu-list random-penalty GDBF (TRGDBF) algorithm applies a random penalty term to the FF of the GDBF algorithm in conjunction with tabu search [11]. The GDBF-w/M and PGDBF-w/M algorithms incorporate the momentum method into the GDBF and PGDBF algorithms, respectively, by introducing a momentum term into their FFs [12]. The momentum term is a pre-defined penalty applied to the currently flipped bits for a specific number of subsequent iterations. While the deterministic nature of the GDBF-w/M algorithm is advantageous for hardware implementation as it avoids random number generators, it may suffer from an errorfloor phenomenon. Some related works have proposed methods to overcome this limitation [13], [14]. Furthermore, the adaptive diversity GDBF (AD-GDBF) algorithm extends the GDBF-w/M algorithm by leveraging decoder diversity [15].
BF-based algorithms work well for column-regular codes whose VNs have a uniform degree, but not for columnirregular codes. Several attempts have been made to mitigate the performance degradation caused by the irregularity in column degrees. In [16], for the binary symmetric channel (BSC), an adaptive probabilistic flipping scheme is utilized, and the VNs of a column-irregular code are classified into two sets based on their degrees, with each set then assigned a distinct weight of the FF. Similarly, in the AD-GDBF algorithm [15], the weights of the FF are optimized in accordance with the column degrees. A generalized AD-GDBF (gAD-GDBF) algorithm for the AWGNC was recently proposed in [17] by introducing an extra reliability measure for each VN. However, these algorithms are not sufficient to fully address the imbalance in which the mean and variance of the FF vary with column degrees. Due to this phenomenon, their decoding

performance for codes with a wide range of column degrees is far from optimal.
To overcome the limitation of the existing algorithms for column-irregular codes, we propose a novel FF that incorporates an offset term based on column degrees, in addition to assigning the weights of the FF according to column degrees. The offset adjusts the mean of the FF, thereby enabling a more effective solution to the imbalance issue associated with the irregularity of column degrees. Furthermore, we present FFs tailored for both the AWGNC and the BSC to ensure their applicability across different channel conditions. The contributions of this paper are summarized as follows.
• We propose an offset GDBF-w/M (OGDBF-w/M) algorithm to enhance decoding performance for columnirregular codes. Existing compensation methods are insufficient to correct the degree-dependent bias in the mean of the FF. Therefore, our proposed OGDBF-w/M algorithm introduces an offset to provide a direct and stable mechanism for adjusting this mean.
• We propose a diversity-based offset GDBF-w/M (D-OGDBF-w/M) algorithm which employs multiple OGDBF-w/M decoders with distinct parameters. The decoder diversity technique provides an additional performance gain.
• We extend the proposed algorithms for the AWGNC to those for the BSC. Our analysis of the extension reveals that while most components of the FF transform antisymmetrically between the two channel models, the offset term behaves symmetrically.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. In Section II, we describe our system model and review the GDBF algorithm and its variants. The proposed OGDBF-w/M and D-OGDBF-w/M algorithms are presented in Section III. They are then extended to the BSC in Section IV. Numerical results on their decoding performance for irregular paritycheck codes are provided in Section V. Finally, concluding remarks are given in Section VI.

III. OGDBF-w/M AND D-OGDBF-w/M ALGORITHMS Although the GDBF and GDBF-w/M algorithms can be employed for decoding parity-check codes regardless of the code structure, their decoding performance degrades significantly for column-irregular codes. In this section, we propose an OGDBF-w/M algorithm that leverages an offset term to enhance decoding performance for columnirregular codes. It is based on a newly formulated FF. Its diversity-based variant, named D-OGDBF-w/M, is also proposed.

A. COLUMN-IRREGULARITY IN PRACTICAL CODES
Column-irregularity is a key feature of many highperformance codes used in communication systems. This feature is prominently observed in modern codes such as quasi-cyclic LDPC (QC-LDPC) codes as well as in classical codes like Bose–Chaudhuri–Hocquenghem (BCH) codes.
While the LDPC codes initially proposed by Gallager are regular, it was demonstrated in [18] that irregular LDPC codes might achieve much better error-correction performance. Subsequently, QC-LDPC codes with a dualdiagonal structure [19], which were proposed to lower encoding complexity, have been adopted in the standards of Wi-Fi and 5G. The PCM H of a QC-LDPC code in the IEEE 802.11n standard is partitioned into H = [HI HP] where HI and HP correspond to the information bits and the parity bits, respectively [20]. Specifically, HP is designed to have a dualdiagonal structure. Fig. 1 illustrates an example of HP with a dual-diagonal structure for QC-LDPC codes when the lifting size is Z .
As HP is irregular, H becomes irregular. Since HP has 2 or 3 as its column degree, HI contains columns with relatively higher degrees to achieve excellent error-correction performance. C. OGDBF-w/M DECODING
As remarked in the previous subsection, the proposed OGDBF-w/M algorithm is based on a newly formulated FF that introduces a degree-dependent offset term to compensate for column-irregularity. The specific design of this offset scheme is motivated by the empirical behavior of the bipolar syndrome sum Sn(ℓ). The degree-dependent behavior of Sn(ℓ) is confirmed experimentally, although a theoretical analysis in the previous subsection relies on the five simple assumptions.
Fig. 2 shows how the empirical average of Sn(ℓ) taken over the VNs with column degree d(n) varies with the number of iterations. The (1944, 972) 802.11n LDPC code and the (63, 45) BCH code are tested over the AWGNC using the GDBF-w/M algorithm (parameters are detailed in Section V), when Eb/N0 is 5.0 dB and 6.0 dB, respectively. As predicted theoretically, it is confirmed empirically that E Sn(ℓ) is heavily dependent on d(n). In particular, the
numerical results for the (1944, 972) 802.11n LDPC code in Fig. 2(a) align well with our theoretical analysis since the PCM of the code is so sparse that Assumption 3) is almost satisfied. At initial iterations, p(nℓ) is so high that the differences among the averages for distinct values of d(n) are not particularly apparent. As ℓ increases, the decrease in p(nℓ) causes q(ℓ) to approach zero, which in turn leads the theoretical mean E Sn(ℓ) = d(n) 1 − 2q(ℓ) to converge
toward d(n), as clearly shown in Fig. 2(a). A similar spreading phenomenon is manifestly shown in Fig. 2(b) for the (63, 45) BCH code. Specifically, the averages of Sn(ℓ) for distinct values of d(n) remain relatively close when ℓ is small. Although the dense PCM of the (63, 45) BCH code prevents these averages from fully reaching the limit d(n), a spreading phenomenon appears clear as ℓ increases. This observation confirms that the degree-dependent disparity in Sn(ℓ) is an apparent phenomenon even when the PCM is not sparse, thereby justifying the use of degree-dependent offsets to compensate for the disparity.

This is because we choose αn = α and βn = β for all n, and we set the offset δn normally to zero for any n.
The proposed OGDBF-w/M algorithm may be implemented so that an increase in the number of parameters is minimized, compared to the GDBF-w/M algorithm. For this purpose, we consider a simple parameter sharing approach to αn and βn, given by αn = α and βn = β for all n. The same value of the offset δn is shared with the VNs of the same column degree, that is, δn1 = δn2 if d(n1) = d(n2). Additionally, δn is chosen to be nonnegative and increases monotonically with d(n), i.e., δn1 ≤ δn2 if d(n1) ≤ d(n2). This is because E Sn(ℓ) is positive and proportional to d(n). Specifically, such monotonicity is expected when α and β are fixed as constants. However, it is not necessarily required when δn is jointly optimized with other degree-dependent parameters such as αn or βn.
It is noteworthy that the proposed OGDBF-w/M algorithm has the same flipping process as that of the GDBF-w/M algorithm. Furthermore, the OGDBF-w/M algorithm is distinguished from the gAD-GDBF algorithm in the sense that the former does not require additional computation of variable potentials while the latter does. The incremental memory overhead for the proposed algorithms relative to the GDBF-w/M algorithm is primarily determined by the storage for the offset values under the given parameter settings. The OGDBF-w/M algorithm uses one offset value per each distinct value of d(n) and thus requires storing at most D values, where D denotes the number of distinct values of d(n). For the D-OGDBF-w/M algorithm, the total number of offset values is up to D(T − 1) via the zero-offset setting of Decoder 1. The overhead for storing the offsets in the D-OGDBF-w/M algorithm is practically marginal within our simulation scope and becomes less significant for long codes, where D(T − 1) ≪ N is satisfied. VI. CONCLUSION We presented the OGDBF-w/M algorithm to enhance the decoding performance of irregular parity-check codes, especially column-irregular codes. It introduces an offset term to the FF and effectively adjusts the mean of the FF. This enhances the accuracy of reliability measurements and significantly improves decoding performance. In addition, the proposed D-OGDBF-w/M algorithm further improves decoding performance by leveraging decoder diversity through parameter variation. Numerical results demonstrate that the proposed algorithms achieve significant improvements in decoding performance for irregular parity-check codes, compared to existing algorithms.
